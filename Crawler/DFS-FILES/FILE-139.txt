      <p>
       <b>
        Mineralogy
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [n 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a subject of
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       specializing in the scientific study of
       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
        chemistry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">
        crystal structure
       </a>
       , and physical (including
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_mineralogy" title="Optical mineralogy">
        optical
       </a>
       ) properties of
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral" title="Mineral">
        minerals
       </a>
       and mineralized
       <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(archaeology)" title="Artifact (archaeology)">
        artifacts
       </a>
       . Specific studies within mineralogy include the processes of mineral origin and formation, classification of minerals, their geographical distribution, as well as their utilization.
      </p>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_mineralogy" title="History of mineralogy">
        History of mineralogy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Early writing on mineralogy, especially on gemstones, comes from ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonia
       </a>
       , the ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">
        Greco-Roman
       </a>
       world, ancient and medieval
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">
        China
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       texts from
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">
        ancient India
       </a>
       and the ancient Islamic World.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Needham-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Books on the subject included the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalis_Historia" title="Naturalis Historia">
         Naturalis Historia
        </a>
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
        Pliny the Elder
       </a>
       , which not only described many different minerals but also explained many of their properties, and Kitab al Jawahir (Book of Precious Stones) by Persian scientist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Biruni" title="Al Biruni">
        Al Biruni
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/German_Renaissance" title="German Renaissance">
        German Renaissance
       </a>
       specialist
       <a href="/wiki/Georgius_Agricola" title="Georgius Agricola">
        Georgius Agricola
       </a>
       wrote works such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_re_metallica" title="De re metallica">
         De re metallica
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        On Metals
       </i>
       , 1556) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_Natura_Fossilium" title="De Natura Fossilium">
         De Natura Fossilium
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        On the Nature of Rocks
       </i>
       , 1546) which began the scientific approach to the subject. Systematic scientific studies of minerals and rocks developed in post-
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Needham-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The modern study of mineralogy was founded on the principles of
       <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">
        crystallography
       </a>
       (the origins of geometric crystallography, itself, can be traced back to the mineralogy practiced in the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries) and to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microscopic" title="Microscopic">
        microscopic
       </a>
       study of rock sections with the invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Microscope" title="Microscope">
        microscope
       </a>
       in the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Needham_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Needham-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicholas_Steno" title="Nicholas Steno">
        Nicholas Steno
       </a>
       first observed the law of constancy of interfacial angles (also known as the first law of crystallography) in quartz crystals in 1669.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
       This was later generalized and established experimentally by
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_L._Rom%C3%A9_de_l%27Isle" title="Jean-Baptiste L. Romé de l'Isle">
        Jean-Baptiste L. Romé de l'Islee
       </a>
       in 1783.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Just_Ha%C3%BCy" title="René Just Haüy">
        René Just Haüy
       </a>
       , the "father of modern crystallography", showed that crystals are periodic and established that the orientations of crystal faces can be expressed in terms of rational numbers, as later encoded in the Miller indices.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
       In 1814,
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius" title="Jöns Jacob Berzelius">
        Jöns Jacob Berzelius
       </a>
       introduced a classification of minerals based on their chemistry rather than their crystal structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Nicol_(geologist)" title="William Nicol (geologist)">
        William Nicol
       </a>
       developed the
       <a href="/wiki/Nicol_prism" title="Nicol prism">
        Nicol prism
       </a>
       , which polarizes light, in 1827–1828 while studying fossilized wood;
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Clifton_Sorby" title="Henry Clifton Sorby">
        Henry Clifton Sorby
       </a>
       showed that thin sections of minerals could be identified by their optical properties using a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polarizing_microscope" title="Polarizing microscope">
        polarizing microscope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         15
        </span>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/James_D._Dana" title="James D. Dana">
        James D. Dana
       </a>
       published his first edition of
       <i>
        A System of Mineralogy
       </i>
       in 1837, and in a later edition introduced a chemical classification that is still the standard.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rafferty_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rafferty-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         15
        </span>
       </sup>
       X-ray diffraction was demonstrated by
       <a href="/wiki/Max_von_Laue" title="Max von Laue">
        Max von Laue
       </a>
       in 1912, and developed into a tool for analyzing the crystal structure of minerals by the father/son team of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Bragg" title="William Henry Bragg">
        William Henry Bragg
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Lawrence_Bragg" title="William Lawrence Bragg">
        William Lawrence Bragg
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       More recently, driven by advances in experimental technique (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_diffraction" title="Neutron diffraction">
        neutron diffraction
       </a>
       ) and available computational power, the latter of which has enabled extremely accurate atomic-scale simulations of the behaviour of crystals, the science has branched out to consider more general problems in the fields of
       <a href="/wiki/Inorganic_chemistry" title="Inorganic chemistry">
        inorganic chemistry
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_physics" title="Solid-state physics">
        solid-state physics
       </a>
       . It, however, retains a focus on the crystal structures commonly encountered in rock-forming minerals (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Perovskite" title="Perovskite">
        perovskites
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clay_minerals" title="Clay minerals">
        clay minerals
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectosilicate" title="Tectosilicate">
        framework silicates
       </a>
       ). In particular, the field has made great advances in the understanding of the relationship between the atomic-scale structure of minerals and their function; in nature, prominent examples would be accurate measurement and prediction of the elastic properties of minerals, which has led to new insight into
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismological
       </a>
       behaviour of rocks and depth-related discontinuities in seismograms of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">
        Earth's mantle
       </a>
       . To this end, in their focus on the connection between atomic-scale phenomena and macroscopic properties, the
       <i>
        mineral sciences
       </i>
       (as they are now commonly known) display perhaps more of an overlap with
       <a href="/wiki/Materials_science" title="Materials science">
        materials science
       </a>
       than any other discipline.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Calcit_Scalenoeder_-_Egremont,_England.jpg">

        <div class="thumbcaption">

         </div>

         </a>
         is a
         <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">

         <sub>

         crystal structure.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>

        </a>

         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Aragonite" title="Aragonite">

         </a>
         is an
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthorhombic" title="Orthorhombic">

         </a>
         polymorph of calcite.

      <p>
       An initial step in identifying a mineral is to examine its physical properties, many of which can be measured on a hand sample. These can be classified into
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       (often given as
       <a href="/wiki/Specific_gravity" title="Specific gravity">
        specific gravity
       </a>
       ); measures of mechanical cohesion (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mohs_scale" title="Mohs scale">
        hardness
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tenacity_(mineralogy)" title="Tenacity (mineralogy)">
        tenacity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)" title="Cleavage (crystal)">
        cleavage
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fracture_(mineralogy)" title="Fracture (mineralogy)">
        fracture
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cleavage_(crystal)#Parting" title="Cleavage (crystal)">
        parting
       </a>
       ); macroscopic visual properties (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luster_(mineralogy)" title="Luster (mineralogy)">
        luster
       </a>
       , color,
       <a href="/wiki/Streak_(mineralogy)" title="Streak (mineralogy)">
        streak
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Luminescence" title="Luminescence">
        luminescence
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Transparency_and_translucency" title="Transparency and translucency">
        diaphaneity
       </a>
       ); magnetic and electric properties; radioactivity and solubility in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride" title="Hydrogen chloride">
        hydrogen chloride
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        H
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
        Cl
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         97–113
        </span>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         39–53
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Hardness
       </i>
       is determined by comparison with other minerals. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Mohs_scale_of_mineral_hardness" title="Mohs scale of mineral hardness">
        Mohs scale
       </a>
       , a standard set of minerals are numbered in order of increasing hardness from 1 (talc) to 10 (diamond). A harder mineral will scratch a softer, so an unknown mineral can be placed in this scale by which minerals it scratches and which scratch it. A few minerals such as
       <a href="/wiki/Calcite" title="Calcite">
        calcite
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kyanite" title="Kyanite">
        kyanite
       </a>
       have a hardness that depends significantly on direction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         254–255
        </span>
       </sup>
       Hardness can also be measured on an absolute scale using a
       <a href="/wiki/Sclerometer" title="Sclerometer">
        sclerometer
       </a>
       ; compared to the absolute scale, the Mohs scale is nonlinear.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         52
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Tenacity
       </i>
       refers to the way a mineral behaves when it is broken, crushed, bent or torn. A mineral can be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittle" title="Brittle">
        brittle
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malleable" title="Malleable">
        malleable
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sectile" title="Sectile">
        sectile
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ductile" title="Ductile">
        ductile
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stiffness" title="Stiffness">
        flexible
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_deformation" title="Elastic deformation">
        elastic
       </a>
       . An important influence on tenacity is the type of chemical bond (
       <i>
        e.g.,
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Ionic_bonding" title="Ionic bonding">
        ionic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Metallic_bonding" title="Metallic bonding">
        metallic
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         255–256
        </span>
       </sup>
       Of the other measures of mechanical cohesion,
       <i>
        cleavage
       </i>
       is the tendency to break along certain crystallographic planes. It is described by the quality (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , perfect or fair) and the orientation of the plane in crystallographic nomenclature.
       <i>
        Parting
       </i>
       is the tendency to break along planes of weakness due to pressure, twinning or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exsolution" title="Exsolution">
        exsolution
       </a>
       . Where these two kinds of break do not occur,
       <i>
        fracture
       </i>
       is a less orderly form that may be
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Conchoidal_fracture" title="Conchoidal fracture">
         conchoidal
        </a>
       </i>
       (having smooth curves resembling the interior of a shell),
       <i>
        fibrous
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        splintery
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hackly
       </i>
       (jagged with sharp edges), or
       <i>
        uneven
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         253–254
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       If the mineral is well crystallized, it will also have a distinctive
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_habit" title="Crystal habit">
        crystal habit
       </a>
       (for example, hexagonal, columnar,
       <a href="/wiki/Botryoidal" title="Botryoidal">
        botryoidal
       </a>
       ) that reflects the
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">
        crystal structure
       </a>
       or internal arrangement of atoms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         40–41
        </span>
       </sup>
       It is also affected by crystal defects and
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_twinning" title="Crystal twinning">
        twinning
       </a>
       . Many crystals are
       <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">
        polymorphic
       </a>
       , having more than one possible crystal structure depending on factors such as pressure and temperature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_6-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         66–68
        </span>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         126
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Crystal_structure">
        Crystal structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mineralogy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Crystal structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Perovskite.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="726" data-file-width="755" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Perovskite.jpg/220px-Perovskite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Perovskite.jpg/330px-Perovskite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Perovskite.jpg/440px-Perovskite.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Perovskite.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perovskite_structure" title="Perovskite structure">
          perovskite crystal structure
         </a>
         . The most abundant mineral in the Earth,
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bridgmanite" title="Bridgmanite">
          bridgmanite
         </a>
         , has this structure.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharp_11-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Sharp-11">
           [10]
          </a>
         </sup>
         Its chemical formula is (Mg,Fe)SiO
         <sub>
          3
         </sub>
         ; the red spheres are oxygen, the blue spheres silicon and the green spheres magnesium or iron.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_structure" title="Crystal structure">
        Crystal structure
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Crystallography" title="Crystallography">
        Crystallography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crystal structure is the arrangement of atoms in a crystal. It is represented by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystal_lattice" title="Crystal lattice">
        lattice
       </a>
       of points which repeats a basic pattern, called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unit_cell" title="Unit cell">
        unit cell
       </a>
       , in three dimensions. The lattice can be characterized by its symmetries and by the dimensions of the unit cell. These dimensions are represented by three
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Miller_index" title="Miller index">
         Miller indices
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashcroft_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ashcroft-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         91–92
        </span>
       </sup>
       The lattice remains unchanged by certain symmetry operations about any given point in the lattice:
       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_symmetry" title="Reflection symmetry">
        reflection
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">
        rotation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Point_reflection" title="Point reflection">
        inversion
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Improper_rotation" title="Improper rotation">
        rotary inversion
       </a>
       , a combination of rotation and reflection. Together, they make up a mathematical object called a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Crystallographic_point_group" title="Crystallographic point group">
         crystallographic point group
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        crystal class
       </i>
       . There are 32 possible crystal classes. In addition, there are operations that displace all the points:
       <a href="/wiki/Translational_symmetry" title="Translational symmetry">
        translation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Screw_axis" title="Screw axis">
        screw axis
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Glide_plane" title="Glide plane">
        glide plane
       </a>
       . In combination with the point symmetries, they form 230 possible
       <a href="/wiki/Space_group" title="Space group">
        space groups
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ashcroft_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ashcroft-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         125–126
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Most geology departments have
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Powder_diffraction" title="Powder diffraction">
        powder diffraction
       </a>
       equipment to analyze the crystal structures of minerals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         54–55
        </span>
       </sup>
       X-rays have wavelengths that are the same order of magnitude as the distances between atoms.
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        Diffraction
       </a>
       , the constructive and destructive interference between waves scattered at different atoms, leads to distinctive patterns of high and low intensity that depend on the geometry of the crystal. In a sample that is ground to a powder, the X-rays sample a random distribution of all crystal orientations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dinnebier_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dinnebier-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Powder diffraction can distinguish between minerals that may appear the same in a hand sample, for example
       <a href="/wiki/Quartz" title="Quartz">
        quartz
       </a>
       and its polymorphs
       <a href="/wiki/Tridymite" title="Tridymite">
        tridymite
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cristobalite" title="Cristobalite">
        cristobalite
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klein_9-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klein-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         54
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Isomorphism_(crystallography)" title="Isomorphism (crystallography)">
        Isomorphous
       </a>
       minerals of different compositions have similar powder diffraction patterns, the main difference being in spacing and intensity of lines. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
        Na
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
        Cl
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Halite" title="Halite">
        halite
       </a>
       ) crystal structure is space group
       <i>
        Fm3m
       </i>
       ; this structure is shared by
       <a href="/wiki/Sylvite" title="Sylvite">
        sylvite
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        K
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
        Cl
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Periclase" title="Periclase">
        periclase
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">
        Mg
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        O
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Bunsenite" title="Bunsenite">
        bunsenite
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">
        Ni
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        O
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Galena" title="Galena">
        galena
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
        Pb
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        S
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Alabandite" title="Alabandite">
        alabandite
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Manganese" title="Manganese">
        Mn
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        S
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorargyrite" title="Chlorargyrite">
        chlorargyrite
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        Ag

       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">

       ), and

       ).

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chemical_elements">

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

      </div>

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg/170px-Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg/255px-Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg/340px-Portable_Micro-X-ray_fluorescence_machine.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>

      <p>
       A few minerals are
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical elements
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
        copper
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Silver" title="Silver">
        silver
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">
        gold
       </a>
       , but the vast majority are
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_compound" title="Chemical compound">
        compounds
       </a>
       . The classical method for identifying composition is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wet_chemistry" title="Wet chemistry">
         wet chemical analysis
        </a>
       </i>
       , which involves dissolving a mineral in an acid such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid" title="Hydrochloric acid">
        hydrochloric acid
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        H
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chlorine" title="Chlorine">
        Cl
       </a>
       ). The elements in solution are then identified using
       <a href="/wiki/Colorimetry_(chemical_method)" title="Colorimetry (chemical method)">
        colorimetry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Titration" title="Titration">
        volumetric analysis
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Gravimetric_analysis" title="Gravimetric analysis">
        gravimetric analysis
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         224–225
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since 1960, most chemistry analysis is done using instruments. One of these,
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_absorption_spectroscopy" title="Atomic absorption spectroscopy">
        atomic absorption spectroscopy
       </a>
       , is similar to wet chemistry in that the sample must still be dissolved, but it is much faster and cheaper. The solution is vaporized and its absorption spectrum is measured in the visible and ultraviolet range.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         225–226
        </span>
       </sup>
       Other techniques are
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray_fluorescence" title="X-ray fluorescence">
        X-ray fluorescence
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Electron_microprobe" title="Electron microprobe">
        electron microprobe
       </a>
       analysis and
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_emission_spectroscopy" title="Atomic emission spectroscopy">
        optical emission spectrography
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         227–232
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       In addition to macroscopic properties such as color or lustre, minerals have properties that require a polarizing microscope to observe.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmitted_light">
        Transmitted light
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mineralogy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Transmitted light">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When light passes from air or a
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       into a transparent crystal, some of it is
       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">
        reflected
       </a>
       at the surface and some
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refracted
       </a>
       . The latter is a bending of the light path that occurs because the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       changes as it goes into the crystal;
       <a href="/wiki/Snell%27s_law" title="Snell's law">
        Snell's law
       </a>
       relates the bending
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angle
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">
        Refractive index
       </a>
       , the ratio of speed in a vacuum to speed in the crystal. Crystals whose point symmetry group falls in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system" title="Cubic crystal system">
        cubic system
       </a>
       are
       <i>
        isotropic
       </i>
       : the index does not depend on direction. All other crystals are
       <i>
        anisotropic
       </i>
       : light passing through them is broken up into two plane
       <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">
        polarized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_(optics)" title="Ray (optics)">
        rays
       </a>
       that travel at different speeds and refract at different angles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         289–291
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A polarizing microscope is similar to an ordinary microscope, but it has two plane-polarized filters, a (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Polarizer" title="Polarizer">
         polarizer
        </a>
       </i>
       ) below the sample and an analyzer above it, polarized perpendicular to each other. Light passes successively through the polarizer, the sample and the analyzer. If there is no sample, the analyzer blocks all the light from the polarizer. However, an anisotropic sample will generally change the polarization so some of the light can pass through. Thin sections and powders can be used as samples.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         293–294
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When an isotropic crystal is viewed, it appears dark because it does not change the polarization of the light. However, when it is
       <a href="/wiki/Oil_immersion" title="Oil immersion">
        immersed in a calibrated liquid
       </a>
       with a lower index of refraction and the microscope is thrown out of focus, a bright line called a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Becke_line_test" title="Becke line test">
         Becke line
        </a>
       </i>
       appears around the perimeter of the crystal. By observing the presence or absence of such lines in liquids with different indices, the index of the crystal can be estimated, usually to within
       <span class="texhtml">
        ± 0.003
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         294–295
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>

         <sub>
          22

         <sub>

         <sub>

         </sub>
         (CO
         <sub>

      <p>
       Systematic mineralogy is the identification and classification of minerals by their properties. Historically, mineralogy was heavily concerned with
       <a href="/wiki/Taxonomy_(general)" title="Taxonomy (general)">
        taxonomy
       </a>
       of the rock-forming minerals. In 1959, the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Mineralogical_Association" title="International Mineralogical Association">
        International Mineralogical Association
       </a>
       formed the Commission of New Minerals and Mineral Names to rationalize the nomenclature and regulate the introduction of new names. In July 2006, it was merged with the Commission on Classification of Minerals to form the Commission on New Minerals, Nomenclature, and Classification.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are over 6,000 named and unnamed minerals, and about 100 are discovered each year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Manual of Mineralogy
       </i>
       places minerals in the following classes:
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral#Element_class" title="Mineral">
        native elements
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sulfide_mineral" title="Sulfide mineral">
        sulfides
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfosalt_minerals" title="Sulfosalt minerals">
        sulfosalts
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral#Oxide_class" title="Mineral">
        oxides and hydroxides
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral#Halide_class" title="Mineral">
        halides
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonate_minerals" title="Carbonate minerals">
        carbonates, nitrates and borates
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral#Sulfate_class" title="Mineral">
        sulfates, chromates, molybdates and tungstates
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phosphate_mineral" title="Phosphate mineral">
        phosphates, arsenates and vanadates
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Silicate_minerals" title="Silicate minerals">
        silicates
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manual_10-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manual-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_environments">
        Formation environments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mineralogy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Formation environments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The environments of mineral formation and growth are highly varied, ranging from slow crystallization at the high temperatures and pressures of
       <a href="/wiki/Igneous_petrology" title="Igneous petrology">
        igneous
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
        melts
       </a>
       deep within the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       to the low temperature precipitation from a saline brine at the Earth's surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Various possible methods of formation include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ramsdell_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ramsdell-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Volcanic_sublimate" title="Volcanic sublimate">
         sublimation
        </a>
        from
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">
         volcanic
        </a>
        gases
       </li>
       <li>
        deposition from
        <a href="/wiki/Aqueous_solution" title="Aqueous solution">
         aqueous solutions
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal" title="Hydrothermal">
         hydrothermal
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Brine" title="Brine">
         brines
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        crystallization from an
        <a href="/wiki/Igneous_petrology" title="Igneous petrology">
         igneous
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
         magma
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
         lava
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        recrystallization due to
        <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
         metamorphic
        </a>
        processes and
        <a href="/wiki/Metasomatism" title="Metasomatism">
         metasomatism
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        crystallization during
        <a href="/wiki/Diagenesis" title="Diagenesis">
         diagenesis
        </a>
        of sediments
       </li>
       <li>
        formation by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">
         oxidation
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Weathering" title="Weathering">
         weathering
        </a>
        of rocks exposed to the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
         atmosphere
        </a>
        or within the
        <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
         soil
        </a>
        environment.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biomineralogy">
        Biomineralogy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mineralogy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Biomineralogy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Biomineralogy is a cross-over field between mineralogy,
       <a href="/wiki/Paleontology" title="Paleontology">
        paleontology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">
        biology
       </a>
       . It is the study of how plants and animals stabilize minerals under biological control, and the sequencing of mineral replacement of those minerals after deposition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It uses techniques from chemical mineralogy, especially isotopic studies, to determine such things as growth forms in living plants and animals
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as things like the original mineral content of fossils.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A new approach to mineralogy called "mineral evolution" explores the co-evolution of the geosphere and biosphere, including the role of minerals in the origin of life and processes as mineral-catalyzed organic synthesis and the selective adsorption of organic molecules on mineral surfaces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbc_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bbc-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Uses">
        Uses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mineralogy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Uses">

        </a>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Minerals are essential to various needs within human society, such as minerals used as
       <a href="/wiki/Ore" title="Ore">
        ores
       </a>
       for essential components of metal products used in various
       <a href="/wiki/Commodity" title="Commodity">

        machinery
       </a>
       , essential components to building materials such as
       <a href="/wiki/Limestone" title="Limestone">
        limestone
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Marble" title="Marble">
        marble
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">
        granite
       </a>

       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plaster" title="Plaster">

       <a href="/wiki/Fertilizer" title="Fertilizer">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collecting">
        Collecting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mineral_collecting" title="Mineral collecting">
        Mineral collecting
       </a>
       is also a recreational study and collection
       <a href="/wiki/Hobby" title="Hobby">
        hobby
       </a>
       , with clubs and societies representing the field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Museums, such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Smithsonian" title="Smithsonian">
        Smithsonian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/National_Museum_of_Natural_History#Hall_of_Geology,_Gems,_and_Minerals" title="National Museum of Natural History">
        National Museum of Natural History Hall of Geology, Gems, and Minerals
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum_of_Los_Angeles_County" title="Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County">
        Natural History Museum of Los Angeles County
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_Museum,_London" title="Natural History Museum, London">
        Natural History Museum, London
       </a>
       , and the private
       <a href="/wiki/Mim_Museum" title="Mim Museum">
        Mim Mineral Museum
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Beirut" title="Beirut">
        Beirut
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">
        Lebanon
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       have popular collections of mineral specimens on permanent display.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
