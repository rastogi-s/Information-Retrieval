      <p>
       <b>
        Greater Orlando
       </b>
       , commonly referred to as the
       <b>
        Orlando metropolitan area
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        Metro Orlando
       </b>
       , and for
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census" title="United States Census">
        U.S. Census
       </a>
       purposes as the
       <b>
        Orlando–Kissimmee–Sanford, Florida Metropolitan Statistical Area
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_metropolitan_area" title="United States metropolitan area">
        metropolitan area
       </a>
       in the central region of the U.S. state of
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMB_10-02_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-OMB_10-02-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its principal cities are
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando,_Florida" title="Orlando, Florida">
        Orlando
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee,_Florida" title="Kissimmee, Florida">
        Kissimmee
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sanford,_Florida" title="Sanford, Florida">
        Sanford
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMB_10-02_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-OMB_10-02-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The U.S.
       <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Management_and_Budget" title="Office of Management and Budget">
        Office of Management and Budget
       </a>
       defines it as consisting of the counties of
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_County,_Florida" title="Lake County, Florida">
        Lake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Orange_County,_Florida" title="Orange County, Florida">
        Orange
       </a>
       (including Orlando),
       <a href="/wiki/Osceola_County,_Florida" title="Osceola County, Florida">
        Osceola
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Seminole_County,_Florida" title="Seminole County, Florida">
        Seminole
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMB_10-02_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-OMB_10-02-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_U.S._Census" title="2010 U.S. Census">
        2010 U.S. Census
       </a>
       , the population of Greater Orlando is 2,134,411, an increase of 51,990 new residents between 2009 and 2010. By population, it is the third-largest metropolitan area in Florida, the fifth-largest in the
       <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_United_States" title="Southeastern United States">
        southeastern United States
       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Table_of_United_States_Metropolitan_Statistical_Areas" title="Table of United States Metropolitan Statistical Areas">
        23rd largest
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . The MSA encompasses 4,012 square miles (10,400 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ) of total area (both land and water areas).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Orlando–Kissimmee–Sanford MSA is further listed by the U.S. Office of Management and Budget as part of the
       <b>
        Orlando–Deltona–Daytona Beach, Florida Combined Statistical Area
       </b>
       . This includes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deltona%E2%80%93Daytona_Beach%E2%80%93Ormond_Beach,_FL_metropolitan_statistical_area" title="Deltona–Daytona Beach–Ormond Beach, FL metropolitan statistical area">
        Deltona–Daytona Beach–Ormond Beach Metropolitan Statistical Area
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Volusia_County,_Florida" title="Volusia County, Florida">
        Volusia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Flagler_County,_Florida" title="Flagler County, Florida">
        Flagler
       </a>
       counties), as well as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_micropolitan_area" title="United States micropolitan area">
        micropolitan area
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/The_Villages,_Florida" title="The Villages, Florida">
        The Villages
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Sumter_County,_Florida" title="Sumter County, Florida">
        Sumter County
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OMB_10-02_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-OMB_10-02-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Combined_Statistical_Area" title="Combined Statistical Area">
        Combined Statistical Area
       </a>
       was estimated to have a population of 2,818,120 in 2010.
      </p>

      </p>

      <p>
       Greater Orlando is a popular tourist destination thanks to the many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_parks" title="Theme parks">
        theme parks
       </a>
       in the area. Famous attractions include
       <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">
        Walt Disney World
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/SeaWorld_Orlando" title="SeaWorld Orlando">
        SeaWorld Orlando
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Orlando" title="Universal Orlando">
        Universal Orlando
       </a>
       . Millions of tourists visit these and other attractions every year. In 2015, the Orlando area attracted 68 million people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Citrus" title="Citrus">
        citrus
       </a>
       industry historically dominated the Orlando area economy, but has declined over the past 100 years. The Christmas 1989 impact freeze proved particularly damaging to commercial citrus farming within Greater Orlando.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are still three major
       <a href="/wiki/Orange_juice" title="Orange juice">
        orange juice
       </a>
       plants remaining in the area: Cutrale Citrus Juices in
       <a href="/wiki/Leesburg,_Florida" title="Leesburg, Florida">
        Leesburg
       </a>
       ; Florida's Natural Growers in
       <a href="/wiki/Umatilla,_Florida" title="Umatilla, Florida">
        Umatilla
       </a>
       ; and Silver Springs Citrus in
       <a href="/wiki/Howey-in-the-Hills,_Florida" title="Howey-in-the-Hills, Florida">
        Howey-in-the-Hills
       </a>
       . Minute Maid maintains a major juice flavoring plant in
       <a href="/wiki/Apopka,_Florida" title="Apopka, Florida">
        Apopka
       </a>
       . Other agricultural pursuits, particularly cattle farming, remain important parts of the Central Florida economy, but are now all located on the outer fringes of the metro area. Orlando is also a major food processing center.
      </p>
      <p>
       Metro Orlando has served as a major
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_defense" title="Military defense">
        military defense
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aerospace" title="Aerospace">
        aerospace
       </a>
       center since World War II. The most prominent defense contractor in the area is
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">
        Lockheed Martin
       </a>
       , which operates both a laboratory and a manufacturing facility in Orlando. Military presence began in the 1940s, with the opening of
       <a href="/wiki/McCoy_Air_Force_Base" title="McCoy Air Force Base">
        McCoy Air Force Base
       </a>
       and the Orlando Naval Training Center. McCoy AFB was a major hub of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress" title="B-52 Stratofortress">
        B-52 Stratofortress
       </a>
       operations. McCoy AFB was split between the city and NTC Orlando in 1974, and NTC Orlando closed in the mid-1990s. McCoy AFB is now the location of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando_International_Airport" title="Orlando International Airport">
        Orlando International Airport
       </a>
       . Farther north in Sanford, the
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sanford_International_Airport" title="Orlando Sanford International Airport">
        Orlando Sanford International Airport
       </a>
       was originally Naval Air Station Sanford.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">
        Rail
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/SunRail" title="SunRail">
        SunRail
       </a>
       (formerly referred to as Central Florida Commuter Rail) is a commuter rail system under construction in the Greater Orlando, Florida area, linking Poinciana to DeLand through Downtown Orlando. When Phase 1 is completed in May 2014, it will run for 31 miles (50 km) with 17 stations along the CSX Transportation "A" Line (former Atlantic Coast Line Railroad main line). Service is scheduled to begin from Volusia County through Orlando with eventual connectivity to south Osceola County under Phase 2.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Church_Street_Station_(Orlando)" title="Church Street Station (Orlando)">
        Church Street Station
       </a>
       , once a stop along the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Coast_Line_Railroad" title="Atlantic Coast Line Railroad">
        Atlantic Coast Line Railroad
       </a>
       , has since been redeveloped as an urban night life center, while the station itself will serve as Downtown Orlando's centerpiece SunRail stop.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">
        Amtrak
       </a>
       serves stations in the area in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kissimmee_(Amtrak_station)" title="Kissimmee (Amtrak station)">
        Kissimmee
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando_(Amtrak_station)" title="Orlando (Amtrak station)">
        Orlando
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Park_(Amtrak_station)" title="Winter Park (Amtrak station)">
        Winter Park
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Auto_Train#Sanford_Terminal" title="Auto Train">
        Sanford
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DeLand_(Amtrak_station)" title="DeLand (Amtrak station)">
        DeLand
       </a>
       . The Sanford station is the southern terminus for the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Auto_Train" title="Auto Train">
         Auto Train
        </a>
       </i>
       , which transports people and their vehicles directly to Washington, D.C., via
       <a href="/wiki/Auto_Train#Lorton_Terminal" title="Auto Train">
        Lorton, Virginia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The other stations are served by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Silver_Meteor" title="Silver Meteor">
         Silver Meteor
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Silver_Star_(Amtrak_train)" title="Silver Star (Amtrak train)">

        </i>
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/South_Carolina" title="South Carolina">

       <a href="/wiki/North_Carolina" title="North Carolina">
        North Carolina

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charleston,_South_Carolina_(Amtrak_station)" title="Charleston, South Carolina (Amtrak station)">

       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fayetteville_(Amtrak_station)" title="Fayetteville (Amtrak station)">
        Fayetteville, North Carolina
       </a>

       <i>
        Silver Star
       </i>

       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_station_(South_Carolina)" title="Columbia station (South Carolina)">

       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raleigh_(Amtrak_station)" title="Raleigh (Amtrak station)">
        Raleigh, North Carolina
       </a>

      </p>
      <p>
       Orlando Sanford International (SFB) is generally served by charter flights from Europe, though it is also a hub for national small-city carrier
       <a href="/wiki/Allegiant_Air" title="Allegiant Air">
        Allegiant Air
       </a>
       and home to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_Connection_Academy" title="Delta Connection Academy">
        Delta Connection Academy
       </a>
       , a pilot training school.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Combined Statistical Area,
       <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach_International_Airport" title="Daytona Beach International Airport">
        Daytona Beach International Airport
       </a>
       also serves the area, and is used by many tourists seeking to directly connect to Daytona Beach's many local offerings, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Daytona_Beach_Bike_Week" title="Daytona Beach Bike Week">
        Daytona Beach Bike Week
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Speedweeks" title="Speedweeks">
        Speedweeks
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_Break" title="Spring Break">
        Spring Break
       </a>
       . It is located so its runways cradle
       <a href="/wiki/Daytona_International_Speedway" title="Daytona International Speedway">
        Daytona International Speedway
       </a>
       , making it convenient for some fans to arrive in Daytona, watch the
       <a href="/wiki/Daytona_500" title="Daytona 500">
        Daytona 500
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Coke_Zero_400" title="Coke Zero 400">
        Coke Zero 400
       </a>
       , and then return home the same day. Daytona Beach International also serves as the main airport for pilot training at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embry-Riddle_Aeronautical_University,_Daytona_Beach" title="Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University, Daytona Beach">
        Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Municipal airports in the region include
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Executive_Airport" title="Orlando Executive Airport">
        Orlando Executive Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee_Gateway_Airport" title="Kissimmee Gateway Airport">
        Kissimmee Gateway Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ormond_Beach_Municipal_Airport" title="Ormond Beach Municipal Airport">
        Ormond Beach Municipal Airport
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/DeLand_Municipal_Airport" title="DeLand Municipal Airport">
        DeLand Municipal Airport
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orlando_Chinatown">
        Orlando Chinatown
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Orlando&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Orlando Chinatown">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <span id="Orlando_Chinatown">
       </span>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Chinatown" title="Chinatown">
        Chinatown
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="zh" xml:lang="zh">
        奥兰多唐人街
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
        pinyin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="zh-Latn-pinyin" xml:lang="zh-Latn-pinyin">
         Àolánduō táng rén jiē
        </span>
       </i>
       ) as of 2002 at 5060 West Colonial Drive (located outside city limits). According to the West Orlando News, the Chinatown features a monument of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_Yat_Sen" title="Sun Yat Sen">
        Sun Yat Sen
       </a>
       , a donation from his granddaughter Dr. Lily Sun who unveiled it on the 87th anniversary of his death in 2012, making this the first commercial location to hold such a monument.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In March 2013, a
       <a href="/wiki/Paifang" title="Paifang">
        paifang
       </a>
       was unveiled at the entrance to the Chinatown plaza, "... helping legitimize the plaza as a center for Chinese commerce."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-daily_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-daily-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Chinatown features an eclectic blend of Chinese, Korean, Filipino, Vietnamese and Indian cultures through its numerous pan-Asian businesses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to an article by the Orlando Weekly, the location of Orlando's Chinatown was once the Westside Crossing Plaza, which was a
       <a href="/wiki/Walmart" title="Walmart">
        Walmart
       </a>
       shopping center with a
       <a href="/wiki/Publix" title="Publix">
        Publix
       </a>
       supermarket. In 2003, the old shopping center was converted to house "... 60 pan-Asian businesses and restaurants." Financing for the project came from Chinese investors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Orlando Sentinel further states that "... by retrofitting the mostly vacant strip center, which includes a former Wal-Mart discount store and Publix supermarket, a group of out-of-state Chinese investors are hoping to draw more than 60 Asian-owned businesses to the site by the end of the year. " The article states that this is "... creating what the project's developers are calling the region's first Chinatown." The amenities include bakeries, restaurants, and an Asian grocery store. So the article further elaborates by saying "... finally, there's a place to buy cuttlefish and black chicken."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the project was a success, its report on its conceptualization and development is used as a reference for the real estate and tourism industries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Media">
        Media
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Greater_Orlando&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Media">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The primary newspaper of the area is the daily
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Sentinel" title="Orlando Sentinel">
         Orlando Sentinel
        </a>
       </i>
       , owned by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tribune_Company" title="Tribune Company">
        Tribune Company
       </a>
       . It was created as the
       <i>
        Orlando Sentinel-Star
       </i>
       in 1973 when the
       <i>
        Orlando Morning Sentinel
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        Orlando Evening Star
       </i>
       were merged. It dropped "Star" from the name in 1982. It is also served by various weekly and semi-weekly papers, including
       <i>
        Orlando Weekly
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        The West Orange Times
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        The East Orlando Sun
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Osceola_News-Gazette" title="The Osceola News-Gazette">
         The Osceola News-Gazette
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Kissimmee,_Florida" title="Kissimmee, Florida">
        Kissimmee
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The extended area is also covered by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daytona_Beach_News-Journal" title="The Daytona Beach News-Journal">
         The Daytona Beach News-Journal
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Florida_Today" title="Florida Today">
         Florida Today
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Greater Orlando makes up a large portion of the "Orlando–Ocala–Daytona Beach, FL"
       <a href="/wiki/Media_market" title="Media market">
        DMA
       </a>
       , which ranks No. 19 in size with 1,466,420 households in 2007–08 according to
       <a href="/wiki/Nielsen_Media_Research" title="Nielsen Media Research">
        Nielsen Media Research
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       All six major broadcast networks are represented in Orlando with their own channels. WESH brought NBC to Orlando when it moved its main operations from Daytona Beach to Eatonville in 1991.
      </p>
