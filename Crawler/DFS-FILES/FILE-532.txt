      <p>
       <b>
        Peninsular Spanish
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        European Spanish
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        Iberian Spanish
       </b>
       , refers to the varieties of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       , as opposed to the Spanish spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       and in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . The related term
       <a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">
        Castilian Spanish
       </a>
       is often applied to formal varieties of Spanish as spoken in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       , the most prominent distinguishing element of Peninsular Spanish varieties, except for the southernmost ones, is the preservation of a distinction between the phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         θ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , represented respectively with the letters ⟨s⟩ on one hand and ⟨z⟩, or
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_C" title="Hard and soft C">
        ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e / i⟩
       </a>
       , on the other. This is usually called
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       in Spanish, while the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merger_(phonology)#Merger" title="Merger (phonology)">
        merger
       </a>
       of both phonemes into /s/ is called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       and the merger into /θ/ is called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
         ceceo
        </a>
       </i>
       . On one hand, in the Spanish of the Americas and in parts of southern Spain, words spelled with ⟨z⟩, with ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩, or with ⟨s⟩ are all typically pronounced with a sound similar to the English /s/; on the other hand, in the Peninsular dialects with
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       , words with ⟨z⟩ or with ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩ are pronounced with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         θ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       —that is, the initial sound of the English word
       <i>
        think
       </i>
       . However, many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusian_dialect" title="Andalusian dialect">
        Andalusian dialects
       </a>
       and the Spanish spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       do not use
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       as a general rule, but rather use either
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       , the most notable distinguishing feature of Peninsular Spanish is the use of the pronoun
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vosotros" title="Vosotros">
         vosotros
        </a>
       </i>
       (along with its oblique form
       <i>
        os
       </i>
       ) and its corresponding verb forms for the second person plural familiar. In virtually all other varieties of Modern Spanish, for the second person plural, the familiar and the formal are merged in
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       , with its verb forms. Again, the use of
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is uncommon in the Canary Islands and only partially introduced in Western Andalusia.
      </p>
