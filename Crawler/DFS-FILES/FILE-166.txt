      <p>
       An
       <b>
        ellipsoid
       </b>
       is a surface that may be obtained from a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       by deforming it by means of directional
       <a href="/wiki/Scaling_(geometry)" title="Scaling (geometry)">
        scalings
       </a>
       , or more generally, of an
       <a href="/wiki/Affine_transformation" title="Affine transformation">
        affine transformation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       An ellipsoid is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadric_surface" title="Quadric surface">
        quadric surface
       </a>
       , that is a
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_(mathematics)" title="Surface (mathematics)">
        surface
       </a>
       that may be defined as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_set" title="Zero set">
        zero set
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Polynomial" title="Polynomial">
        polynomial
       </a>
       of degree two in three variables. Among quadric surfaces, an ellipsoid is characterized by either of the two following properties. Every planar
       <a href="/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry)" title="Cross section (geometry)">
        cross section
       </a>
       is either an
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       , or is empty, or is reduced to a single point (this explains the name, meaning "ellipse like"). It is
       <a href="/wiki/Bounded_set" title="Bounded set">
        bounded
       </a>
       , which means that it may be enclosed in a sufficiently large
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       An ellipsoid has three pairwise
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_symmetry" title="Rotational symmetry">
        axes of symmetry
       </a>
       which intersect at a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_symmetry" title="Central symmetry">
        center of symmetry
       </a>
       , called the
       <b>
        center
       </b>
       of the ellipsoid. The
       <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">
        line segments
       </a>
       that are delimited on the axes of symmetry by the ellipsoid are called the
       <i>
        principal axes
       </i>
       , or simply axes of the ellipsoid. If the three axes have different lengths, the ellipsoid is said to be
       <b>
        tri-axial
       </b>
       or rarely
       <b>
        scalene
       </b>
       , and the axes are uniquely defined.
      </p>
      <p>
       If two of the axes have the same length, then the ellipsoid is an "ellipsoid of
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_of_revolution" title="Surface of revolution">
        revolution
       </a>
       ", also called a
       <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
        spheroid
       </a>
       . In this case, the ellipsoid is invariant under a
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">
        rotation
       </a>
       around the third axis, and there are thus infinitely many ways of choosing the two perpendicular axes of the same length. If the third axis is shorter, the ellipsoid is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate spheroid
       </a>
       ; if it is longer, it is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">
        prolate spheroid
       </a>
       . If the three axes have the same length, the ellipsoid is a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

         plane section of an ellipsoid
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The intersection of a plane and a sphere is a circle (or is reduced to a single point, or is empty). Any ellipsoid is the image of the unit sphere under some affine transformation, and any plane is the image of some other plane under the same transformation. So, because affine transformations map circles to ellipses, the intersection of a plane with an ellipsoid is an ellipse or a single point, or is empty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Obviously, spheroids contain circles. This is also true, but less obvious, for triaxial ellipsoids (see
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_section" title="Circular section">
        Circular section
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <p>
       The ellipsoidal shape finds many practical applications:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Geodesy
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid" title="Earth ellipsoid">
         Earth ellipsoid
        </a>
        , a mathematical figure approximating the shape of the Earth.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
         Reference ellipsoid
        </a>
        , a mathematical figure approximating the shape planetary bodies in general.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Mechanics
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Poinsot%27s_ellipsoid" title="Poinsot's ellipsoid">
         Poinsot's ellipsoid
        </a>
        , a geometrical method for visualizing the torque-free motion of a rotating
        <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">
         rigid body
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lam%C3%A9%27s_stress_ellipsoid" title="Lamé's stress ellipsoid">
         Lamé's stress ellipsoid
        </a>
        , an alternative to Mohr's circle for the graphical representation of the stress state at a point.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Manipulability_ellipsoid" title="Manipulability ellipsoid">
         Manipulability ellipsoid
        </a>
        , used to describe a robot's freedom of motion.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Crystallography
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Index_ellipsoid" title="Index ellipsoid">
         Index ellipsoid
        </a>
        , a diagram of an ellipsoid that depicts the orientation and relative magnitude of refractive indices in a crystal.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Thermal_ellipsoid" title="Thermal ellipsoid">
         Thermal ellipsoid
        </a>
        , ellipsoids used in crystallography to indicate the magnitudes and directions of the thermal vibration of atoms in crystal structures.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <dt>
        Lighting

      </dl>

      <p>
       Ellipsoids and
       <a href="/wiki/Cuboid" title="Cuboid">
        cuboids
       </a>
       rotate stably along their major or minor axes, but not along their median axis. This can be seen experimentally by throwing an eraser with some spin. In addition,
       <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">
        moment of inertia
       </a>
       considerations mean that rotation along the major axis is more easily perturbed than rotation along the minor axis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One practical effect of this is that scalene astronomical bodies such as
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       generally rotate along their minor axes (as does Earth, which is merely oblate); in addition, because of
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       , moons in
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       orbit with their major axis aligned radially to their planet.
      </p>
      <p>
       A spinning body of homogeneous self-gravitating fluid will assume the form of either a
       <a href="/wiki/Maclaurin_spheroid" title="Maclaurin spheroid">
        Maclaurin spheroid
       </a>
       (oblate spheroid) or
       <a href="/wiki/Jacobi_ellipsoid" title="Jacobi ellipsoid">
        Jacobi ellipsoid
       </a>
       (scalene ellipsoid) when in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       , and for moderate rates of rotation. At faster rotations, non-ellipsoidal
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pyriform" title="wikt:pyriform">
        piriform
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oviform" title="Oviform">
        oviform
       </a>
       shapes can be expected, but these are not stable.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fluid_dynamics">
        Fluid dynamics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The ellipsoid is the most general shape for which it has been possible to calculate the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creeping_flow" title="Creeping flow">
        creeping flow
       </a>
       of fluid around the solid shape. The calculations include the force required to translate through a fluid and to rotate within it. Applications include determining the size and shape of large molecules, the sinking rate of small particles, and the swimming abilities of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microorganisms" title="Microorganisms">
        microorganisms
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus the density function is a scalar-to-scalar transformation of a quadric expression. Moreover, the equation for any
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
        iso-density surface
       </a>
       states that the quadric expression equals some constant specific to that value of the density, and the iso-density surface is an ellipsoid.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_higher_dimensions">
        In higher dimensions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: In higher dimensions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The volume of a higher-dimensional ellipsoid (a
       <i>
        hyperellipsoid
       </i>
       ) can be calculated using the dimensional constant given for the
       <a href="/wiki/Volume_of_an_n-ball" title="Volume of an n-ball">
        volume of a hypersphere
       </a>
       . One can also define hyperellipsoids as the images of spheres under invertible linear transformations. The spectral theorem can again be used to obtain a standard equation akin to the one given above.
      </p>
