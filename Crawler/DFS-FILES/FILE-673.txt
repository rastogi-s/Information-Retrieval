      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        grammar
       </b>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γραμματική
       </span>
       ) is the set of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structural" title="Structural">
        structural
       </a>
       rules governing the composition of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clause_(linguistics)" title="Clause (linguistics)">
        clauses
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrases
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Words" title="Words">
        words
       </a>
       in any given
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">
        natural language
       </a>
       . The term refers also to the study of such rules, and this field includes
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       , often complemented by
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantics
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">
        pragmatics
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_of_the_term">
        Use of the term
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use of the term">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For linguists, grammar refers to cognitive information underlying language use. Speakers of a language have a set of internalized rules
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for using that language. These rules constitute grammar, and the vast majority of the information in the grammar is—at least in the case of one's
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        native language
       </a>
       —
       <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">
        acquired
       </a>
       not by conscious study or instruction, but by observing other speakers. Much of this work is done during early childhood; learning a language later in life usually involves a greater degree of explicit instruction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term "grammar" can also be used to describe the rules that govern the linguistic behaviour of a group of speakers. The term "English grammar", therefore, may have several meanings. It may refer to the whole of English grammar, that is, to the grammars of all the speakers of the language, in which case, the term encompasses a great deal of variation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Alternatively, it may refer only to what is common to the grammars of all, or of the vast majority of English speakers (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subject–verb–object">
        subject–verb–object
       </a>
       word order in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sentence" title="Simple sentence">
        simple declarative sentences
       </a>
       ). Or it may refer to the rules of a particular, relatively well-defined variety of English (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">
        standard English
       </a>
       for a particular region).
      </p>
      <p>
       A specific description, study or analysis of such rules may also be referred to as a grammar. A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reference_book" title="Reference book">
        reference book
       </a>
       describing the grammar of a language is called a "reference grammar" or simply "a grammar" (see
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_English_grammars" title="History of English grammars">
        History of English grammars
       </a>
       ). A fully explicit grammar that exhaustively describes the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammaticality" title="Grammaticality">
        grammatical
       </a>
       constructions of a language is called a descriptive grammar. This kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_description" title="Linguistic description">
        linguistic description
       </a>
       contrasts with
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        linguistic prescription
       </a>
       , an attempt to discourage or suppress some grammatical constructions, while promoting others. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Preposition_stranding" title="Preposition stranding">
        preposition stranding
       </a>
       occurs widely in
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       and has a long history in English.
       <a href="/wiki/John_Dryden" title="John Dryden">
        John Dryden
       </a>
       , however, objected to it (without explanation),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       leading other English speakers to avoid the construction and discourage its use.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CB1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CB1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Outside linguistics the term
       <i>
        grammar
       </i>
       is often used in a rather different sense. In some respects, it may be used more broadly, including rules of
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        spelling and punctuation
       </a>
       , which linguists would not typically consider to form part of grammar, but rather as a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       , the set of conventions used for writing a language. In other respects, it may be used more narrowly, to refer to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptive_grammar" title="Prescriptive grammar">
        prescriptive grammar
       </a>
       only and excluding those aspects of a language's grammar that are not subject to variation or debate. Jeremy Butterfield claimed that, for non-linguists, "Grammar is often a generic way of referring to any aspect of English that people object to."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        grammar
       </i>
       is derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γραμματικὴ τέχνη
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        grammatikē technē
       </i>
       ), which means "art of letters", from
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γράμμα
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        gramma
       </i>
       ), "letter", itself from
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γράφειν
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        graphein
       </i>
       ), "to draw, to write".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The same Greek root also appears in
       <a href="/wiki/Graphics" title="Graphics">
        graphics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        grapheme
       </a>
       , and photograph.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_linguistics" title="History of linguistics">
        History of linguistics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       made some early attempts at language description,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the first systematic grammars, of
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       , originated in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iron_Age_India" title="Iron Age India">
        Iron Age India
       </a>
       , with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaska" title="Yaska">
        Yaska
       </a>
       (6th century BC),
       <a href="/wiki/P%C4%81%E1%B9%87ini" title="Pāṇini">
        Pāṇini
       </a>
       (4th century BC) and his commentators
       <a href="/wiki/Pingala" title="Pingala">
        Pingala
       </a>
       (c. 200 BC),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Katyayana" title="Katyayana">
        Katyayana
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Patanjali" title="Patanjali">
        Patanjali
       </a>
       (2nd century BC).
       <a href="/wiki/Tolk%C4%81ppiyam" title="Tolkāppiyam">
        Tolkāppiyam
       </a>
       , the earliest
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       grammar, is mostly dated to before the 5th century AD.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the West, grammar emerged as a discipline in
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_period" title="Hellenistic period">
        Hellenism
       </a>
       from the 3rd century BC forward with authors like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhyanus" title="Rhyanus">
        Rhyanus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samothrace" title="Aristarchus of Samothrace">
        Aristarchus of Samothrace
       </a>
       , the oldest extant work being the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_of_Grammar" title="Art of Grammar">
         Art of Grammar
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Τέχνη Γραμματική
       </span>
       ), attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Thrax" title="Dionysius Thrax">
        Dionysius Thrax
       </a>
       (c. 100 BC).
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">
        Latin grammar
       </a>
       developed by following Greek models from the 1st century BC, due to the work of authors such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbilius_Pupillus" title="Orbilius Pupillus">
        Orbilius Pupillus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Remmius_Palaemon" title="Remmius Palaemon">
        Remmius Palaemon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Valerius_Probus" title="Marcus Valerius Probus">
        Marcus Valerius Probus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Verrius_Flaccus" title="Verrius Flaccus">
        Verrius Flaccus
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Aemilius_Asper" title="Aemilius Asper">
        Aemilius Asper
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A grammar of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">
        Irish
       </a>
       originated in the 7th century with the
       <a href="/wiki/Auraicept_na_n-%C3%89ces" title="Auraicept na n-Éces">
        Auraicept na n-Éces
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_grammar" title="Arabic grammar">
        Arabic grammar
       </a>
       emerged with
       <a href="/wiki/Abu_al-Aswad_al-Du%27ali" title="Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali">
        Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali
       </a>
       in the 7th century. The first treatises on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_grammar" title="Hebrew grammar">
        Hebrew grammar
       </a>
       appeared in the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       , in the context of
       <a href="/wiki/Mishnah" title="Mishnah">
        Mishnah
       </a>
       (exegesis of the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
       ). The
       <a href="/wiki/Karaite_Judaism" title="Karaite Judaism">
        Karaite
       </a>
       tradition originated in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abbasid" title="Abbasid">
        Abbasid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">
        Baghdad
       </a>
       . The
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diqduq" title="Diqduq">
         Diqduq
        </a>
       </i>
       (10th century) is one of the earliest grammatical commentaries on the Hebrew Bible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibn_Barun" title="Ibn Barun">
        Ibn Barun
       </a>
       in the 12th century compares the Hebrew language with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_grammatical_tradition" title="Islamic grammatical tradition">
        Islamic grammatical tradition
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Belonging to the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Trivium" title="Trivium">
         trivium
        </a>
       </i>
       of the seven
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberal_arts" title="Liberal arts">
        liberal arts
       </a>
       , grammar was taught as a core discipline throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       , following the influence of authors from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">
        Late Antiquity
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Priscian" title="Priscian">
        Priscian
       </a>
       . Treatment of vernaculars began gradually during the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       , with isolated works such as the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Grammatical_Treatise" title="First Grammatical Treatise">
        First Grammatical Treatise
       </a>
       , but became influential only in the
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">
        Baroque
       </a>
       periods. In 1486,
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">
        Antonio de Nebrija
       </a>
       published
       <i>
        Las introduciones Latinas contrapuesto el romance al Latin
       </i>
       , and the first
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">
        Spanish grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">
         Gramática de la lengua castellana
        </a>
       </i>
       , in 1492. During the 16th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">
        Italian Renaissance
       </a>
       , the
       <i>
        Questione della lingua
       </i>
       was the discussion on the status and ideal form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       , initiated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">
        Dante
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_vulgari_eloquentia" title="De vulgari eloquentia">
         de vulgari eloquentia
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">
        Pietro Bembo
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Prose della volgar lingua
       </i>
       Venice 1525). The first grammar of
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene language
       </a>
       was written in 1583 by
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_Bohori%C4%8D" title="Adam Bohorič">
        Adam Bohorič
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Grammars of non-European languages began to be compiled for the purposes of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelization" title="Evangelization">
        evangelization
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bible_translation" title="Bible translation">
        Bible translation
       </a>
       from the 16th century onward, such as
       <i>
        Grammatica o Arte de la Lengua General de los Indios de los Reynos del Perú
       </i>
       (1560), and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">
        Quechua
       </a>
       grammar by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fray_Domingo_de_Santo_Tom%C3%A1s" title="Fray Domingo de Santo Tomás">
        Fray Domingo de Santo Tomás
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1643 there appeared
       <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Uzhevych" title="Ivan Uzhevych">
        Ivan Uzhevych
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Grammatica sclavonica
       </i>
       and, in 1762, the
       <i>
        Short Introduction to English Grammar
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Lowth" title="Robert Lowth">
        Robert Lowth
       </a>
       was also published. The
       <i>
        Grammatisch-Kritisches Wörterbuch der hochdeutschen Mundart
       </i>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_German" title="High German">
        High German
       </a>
       grammar in five volumes by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Christoph_Adelung" title="Johann Christoph Adelung">
        Johann Christoph Adelung
       </a>
       , appeared as early as 1774.
      </p>
      <p>
       From the latter part of the 18th century, grammar came to be understood as a subfield of the emerging discipline of modern
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       . The Serbian grammar by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vuk_Stefanovi%C4%87_Karad%C5%BEi%C4%87" title="Vuk Stefanović Karadžić">
        Vuk Stefanović Karadžić
       </a>
       arrived in 1814, while the
       <i>
        Deutsche Grammatik
       </i>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Brothers_Grimm" title="Brothers Grimm">
        Brothers Grimm
       </a>
       was first published in 1818. The
       <i>
        Comparative Grammar
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Franz_Bopp" title="Franz Bopp">
        Franz Bopp
       </a>
       , the starting point of modern
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative_linguistics" title="Comparative linguistics">
        comparative linguistics
       </a>
       , came out in 1833.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development_of_grammars">
        Development of grammars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Development of grammars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        Historical linguistics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_English_grammars" title="History of English grammars">
        History of English grammars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Grammars evolve through usage and also due to separations of the human population. With the advent of written
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Knowledge_representation" title="Knowledge representation">
        representations
       </a>
       , formal rules about language usage tend to appear also.
       <a href="/wiki/Formal_grammar" title="Formal grammar">
        Formal grammars
       </a>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Codification_(linguistics)" title="Codification (linguistics)">
        codifications
       </a>
       of usage that are developed by repeated documentation over time, and by
       <a href="/wiki/Observation" title="Observation">
        observation
       </a>
       as well. As the rules become established and developed, the prescriptive concept of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_correctness" title="Grammatical correctness">
        grammatical correctness
       </a>
       can arise. This often creates a discrepancy between contemporary usage and that which has been accepted, over time, as being correct. Linguists tend to view prescriptive grammars as having little justification beyond their authors' aesthetic tastes, although style guides may give useful advice about
       <i>
        standard language employment
       </i>
       , based on descriptions of usage in contemporary writings of the same language. Linguistic prescriptions also form part of the explanation for variation in speech, particularly variation in the speech of an individual speaker (an explanation, for example, for why some people say "I didn't do nothing", some say "I didn't do anything", and some say one or the other depending on social context).
      </p>
      <p>
       The formal study of grammar is an important part of education for children from a young age through advanced
       <a href="/wiki/Learning" title="Learning">
        learning
       </a>
       , though the rules taught in schools are not a "grammar" in the sense most
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguists
       </a>
       use the term, particularly as they are often
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptive" title="Prescriptive">
        prescriptive
       </a>
       rather than
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive" title="Descriptive">
        descriptive
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">
        Constructed languages
       </a>
       (also called
       <i>
        planned languages
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        conlangs
       </i>
       ) are more common in the modern day than they used to be, although still extremely uncommon compared to natural languages. Many have been designed to aid human communication (for example, naturalistic
       <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </a>
       , schematic
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       , and the highly logic-compatible
       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_language" title="Artificial language">
        artificial language
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lojban" title="Lojban">
        Lojban
       </a>
       ). Each of these languages has its own grammar.
      </p>
      <p>
       Syntax refers to the linguistic structure above the word level (e.g. how sentences are formed)—though without taking into account
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       , which is the domain of phonology. Morphology, by contrast, refers to structure at and below the word level (e.g. how compound words are formed), but above the level of individual sounds, which, like intonation, are in the domain of phonology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No clear line can be drawn, however, between syntax and morphology.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Analytic_languages" title="Analytic languages">
        Analytic languages
       </a>
       use
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       to convey information that is encoded via
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic languages
       </a>
       . In other words, word order is not significant and
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       is highly significant in a purely synthetic language, whereas morphology is not significant and syntax is highly significant in an analytic language. Chinese and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afrikaans_language" title="Afrikaans language">
        Afrikaans
       </a>
       , for example, are highly analytic, and meaning is therefore very context-dependent. (Both do have some inflections, and have had more in the past; thus, they are becoming even less synthetic and more "purely" analytic over time.)
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       , which is highly
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic
       </a>
       , uses
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affixes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflections
       </a>
       to convey the same information that Chinese does with
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       . Because Latin words are quite (though not completely) self-contained, an intelligible Latin
       <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">
        sentence
       </a>
       can be made from elements that are placed in a largely arbitrary order. Latin has a complex affixation and simple syntax, while Chinese has the opposite.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar_frameworks">
        Grammar frameworks
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Grammar frameworks">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammar_framework" title="Grammar framework">
        Grammar framework
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Various "grammar frameworks" have been developed in
       <a href="/wiki/Theoretical_linguistics" title="Theoretical linguistics">
        theoretical linguistics
       </a>
       since the mid-20th century, in particular under the influence of the idea of a "
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_grammar" title="Universal grammar">
        universal grammar
       </a>
       " in the United States. Of these, the main divisions are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Transformational_grammar" title="Transformational grammar">
         Transformational grammar
        </a>
        (TG)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Systemic_functional_grammar" title="Systemic functional grammar">
         Systemic functional grammar
        </a>
        (SFG)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principles_and_Parameters" title="Principles and Parameters">
         Principles and Parameters Theory
        </a>
        (P&amp;P)
       </li>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Education">
        Education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptive" title="Prescriptive">
        Prescriptive
       </a>
       grammar is taught in primary and secondary school. The term "
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">
        grammar school
       </a>
       " historically refers to a school teaching
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">
        Latin grammar
       </a>
       to future Roman citizens, orators, and, later, Catholic priests. In its earliest form, "grammar school" referred to a school that taught students to read, scan, interpret, and declaim Greek and Latin poets (including Homer, Virgil, Euripides, Ennius, and others). These should not be confused with the related, albeit distinct, modern British grammar schools.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard language
       </a>
       is a particular dialect of a language that is promoted above other dialects in writing, education, and broadly speaking in the public sphere; it contrasts with
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       dialects, which may be the objects of study in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Descriptive_grammar" title="Descriptive grammar">
        descriptive grammar
       </a>
       but which are rarely taught prescriptively. The standardized "
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first language
       </a>
       " taught in primary education may be subject to
       <a href="/wiki/Language_politics" title="Language politics">
        political
       </a>
       controversy, because it may sometimes establish a standard defining nationality or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">
        ethnicity
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Recently, efforts have begun to update
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistics_in_Education" title="Linguistics in Education">
        grammar instruction
       </a>
       in primary and secondary education. The primary focus has been to prevent the use of outdated prescriptive rules in favour of more accurate descriptive ones and to change perceptions about relative "correctness" of standard forms in comparison to non-standard dialects.
      </p>
      <p>
       The pre-eminence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parisian_French" title="Parisian French">
        Parisian French
       </a>
       has reigned largely unchallenged throughout the history of modern French literature. Standard Italian is not based on the speech of the capital, Rome, but on the speech of
       <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
        Florence
       </a>
       because of the influence, Florentines had on early
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_literature" title="Italian literature">
        Italian literature
       </a>
       . Similarly, standard Spanish is not based on the speech of
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , but on that of educated speakers from more northerly areas like
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
        Castile and León
       </a>
       (e.g. see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">
         Gramática de la lengua castellana
        </a>
       </i>
       ). In
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       the Spanish standard is based on the local dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">
        Montevideo
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       has, for now, two official standards, respectively
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian language
       </a>
       is divided in a similar way;
       <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
        Serbia
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Republika_Srpska" title="Republika Srpska">
        Republika Srpska
       </a>
       use their own separate standards. The existence of a third standard is a matter of controversy, some consider
       <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">
        Montenegrin
       </a>
       as a separate language, and some think it is merely another variety of Serbian.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       has two standards,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">
         Bokmål
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">
         Nynorsk
        </a>
       </i>
       , the choice between which is subject to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_language_struggle" title="Norwegian language struggle">
        controversy
       </a>
       : Each Norwegian municipality can declare one of the two its official language, or it can remain "language neutral". Nynorsk is endorsed by a minority of 27 percent of the municipalities. The main language used in primary schools normally follows the official language of its municipality and is decided by referendum within the local school district.
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">
        Standard German
       </a>
       emerged from the standardized chancellery use of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_New_High_German_language" title="Early New High German language">
        High German
       </a>
       in the 16th and 17th centuries. Until about 1800, it was almost entirely a written language, but now it is so widely spoken that most of the former
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_dialect" title="German dialect">
        German dialects
       </a>
       are nearly extinct.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">
        Standard Chinese
       </a>
       has official status as the standard spoken form of the Chinese language in the People's Republic of China (PRC), the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_China" title="Republic of China">
        Republic of China
       </a>
       (ROC) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Republic of Singapore
       </a>
       . Pronunciation of Standard Chinese is based on the local accent of
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       from Luanping, Chengde in Hebei Province near Beijing, while grammar and syntax are based on modern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernacular_written_Chinese" title="Vernacular written Chinese">
        vernacular written Chinese
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Literary_Arabic" title="Literary Arabic">
        Modern Standard Arabic
       </a>
       is directly based on
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Classical Arabic
       </a>
       , the language of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qur%27an" title="Qur'an">
        Qur'an
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">
        Hindustani language
       </a>
       has two standards,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">
        Urdu
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United States, the Society for the Promotion of Good Grammar designated 4 March as
       <a href="/wiki/National_Grammar_Day" title="National Grammar Day">
        National Grammar Day
       </a>
       in 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
