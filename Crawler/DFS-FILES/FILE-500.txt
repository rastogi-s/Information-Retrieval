      <p>
       <b>
        Velars
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       articulated with the back part of the tongue (the dorsum) against the soft palate, the back part of the roof of the mouth (known also as the
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">
         velum
        </a>
       </b>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the velar region of the roof of the mouth is relatively extensive and the movements of the dorsum are not very precise, velars easily undergo
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       , shifting their articulation back or to the front depending on the quality of adjacent vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stroud,_2013_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stroud,_2013-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They often become automatically
       <i>
        fronted
       </i>
       , that is partly or completely
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       before a following front vowel, and
       <i>
        retracted
       </i>
       , that is partly or completely
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">
        uvular
       </a>
       before back vowels.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
        Palatalised
       </a>
       velars (like English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        keen
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        cube
       </i>
       ) are sometimes referred to as
       <b>
        palatovelars
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (December 2015)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Many languages also have
       <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">
        labialized
       </a>
       velars, such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʷ]
       </span>
       , in which the articulation is accompanied by rounding of the lips. There are also
       <a href="/wiki/Labial%E2%80%93velar_consonant" title="Labial–velar consonant">
        labial–velar consonants
       </a>
       , which are doubly articulated at the velum and at the lips, such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k͡p]
       </span>
       . This distinction disappears with the
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximant consonant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       since labialization involves adding of a labial approximant articulation to a sound, and this ambiguous situation is often called
       <b>
        labiovelar
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A velar
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trill
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" title="Flap consonant">
        tap
       </a>
       is not possible: see the shaded boxes on the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Consonants" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        table of pulmonic consonants
       </a>
       . In the velar position, the tongue has an extremely restricted ability to carry out the type of motion associated with trills or taps, and the body of the tongue has no freedom to move quickly enough to produce a velar trill or flap.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_velars">
        Lack of velars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Velar_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lack of velars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The velar consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       is the most common consonant in human languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only languages recorded to lack velars (and any dorsal consonant at all) may be
       <a href="/wiki/Xavante_language" title="Xavante language">
        Xavante
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_language" title="Tahitian language">
        Tahitian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wutung_language" title="Wutung language">
        Wutung
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vanimo_language" title="Vanimo language">
        Vanimo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nori_language" title="Nori language">
        Nori
       </a>
       , and Waimiri-Atroarí.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other languages lack simple velars. An areal feature of the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       of the coastal regions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
        Pacific Northwest
       </a>
       is that historical *k was palatalized. When such sounds remained stops, they were transcribed ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        kʸ
       </span>
       ⟩ in
       <a href="/wiki/Americanist_phonetic_notation" title="Americanist phonetic notation">
        Americanist phonetic notation
       </a>
       , presumably corresponding to IPA ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        c
       </span>
       ⟩, but in others, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Saanich_dialect" title="Saanich dialect">
        Saanich dialect
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Coast_Salish_languages" title="Coast Salish languages">
        Coastal Salish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Salish-Spokane-Kalispel_language" title="Salish-Spokane-Kalispel language">
        Salish-Spokane-Kalispel
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Chemakum_language" title="Chemakum language">
        Chemakum
       </a>
       , *k went further and affricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       . Likewise, historical *k’ has become
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃʼ]
       </span>
       and historical *x has become
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       ; there was no *g or *ŋ. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Caucasian_languages" title="Northwest Caucasian languages">
        Northwest Caucasian languages
       </a>
       , historical *
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       has also become palatalized, becoming
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kʲ/
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh
       </a>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       in most
       <a href="/wiki/Circassian_languages" title="Circassian languages">
        Circassian
       </a>
       varieties. In both regions the languages retain a
       <a href="/wiki/Labialized_velar_consonant" title="Labialized velar consonant">
        labialized velar series
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʷ], [kʼʷ], [xʷ], [w]
       </span>
       in the Pacific Northwest) as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">
        uvular consonants
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the languages of those families that retain plain velars, both the plain and labialized velars are
       <i>
        pre-velar
       </i>
       , perhaps to make them more distinct from the uvulars which may be
       <i>
        post-velar
       </i>
       . Prevelar consonants are susceptible to palatalization. A similar system, contrasting
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *kʲ
       </span>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *kʷ
       </span>
       and leaving
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *k
       </span>
       marginal at best, is reconstructed for
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Apart from the voiced stop
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       , no other velar consonant is particularly common, even the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       that occur in English. Of course, there can be no phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       in a language that lacks voiced stops, like
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but it is sporadically missing elsewhere. Of the languages surveyed in the
       <i>
        World Atlas of Language Structures
       </i>
       , about 10% of languages that otherwise have
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p b t d k/
       </span>
       are missing
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">
        Pirahã
       </a>
       has both a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       and a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       phonetically. However, the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       does not behave as other consonants, and the argument has been made that it is phonemically
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /hi/
       </span>
       , leaving Pirahã with only
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       as an underlyingly velar consonant.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       does not distinguish
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       ; ⟨k⟩ tends toward
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       at the beginning of utterances,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       , and is variable elsewhere, especially in the dialect of Ni
       <span class="unicode">
        ʻ
       </span>
       ihau and Kaua
       <span class="unicode">
        ʻ
       </span>
       i. Since Hawaiian has no
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       , and ⟨w⟩ varies between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       , it is not clearly meaningful to say that Hawaiian has phonemic velar consonants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several
       <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">
        Khoisan languages
       </a>
       have limited numbers or distributions of pulmonic velar consonants. (Their click consonants are articulated in the uvular or possibly velar region, but that occlusion is part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
        airstream mechanism
       </a>
       rather than the place of articulation of the consonant.)
       <a href="/wiki/Khoekhoe_language" title="Khoekhoe language">
        Khoekhoe
       </a>
       , for example, does not allow velars in medial or final position, but in
       <a href="/wiki/Ju%C7%80%27hoan_dialect" title="Juǀ'hoan dialect">
        Juǀ'hoan
       </a>
       velars are rare even in initial position.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Velodorsal_consonants">
        <span id="Velodorsal">
         Velodorsal
        </span>
        consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Velar_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Velodorsal consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Normal velar consonants are
       <i>
        dorso-velar
       </i>
       : The dorsum (body) of the tongue rises to contact the velum (soft palate) of the roof of the mouth. In disordered speech there are also
       <i>
        velo-dorsal
       </i>
       stops, with the opposite articulation: The velum lowers to contact the tongue, which remains static. In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensions_to_the_IPA" title="Extensions to the IPA">
        extensions to the IPA
       </a>
       for disordered speech, these are transcribed by reversing the IPA letter for a velar consonant, e.g. ⟨
       <span style="display: inline-block; -moz-transform: scaleX(-1); -ms-transform: scaleX(-1); -o-transform: scaleX(-1); -webkit-transform: scaleX(-1); transform: scaleX(-1);">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         k
        </span>
       </span>
       ⟩ for a voiceless velodorsal stop.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [note 3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
