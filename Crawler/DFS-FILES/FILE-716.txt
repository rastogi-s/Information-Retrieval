      <p>
       <b>
        Loísmo
       </b>
       , with its feminine counterpart
       <b>
        laísmo
       </b>
       , is a feature of certain dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       consisting of the use of the pronouns
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lo#Spanish" title="wikt:lo">
          lo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/la#Spanish" title="wikt:la">
          la
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (which are normally used for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_object" title="Direct object">
        direct objects
       </a>
       ) in place of the pronoun
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       (which is used for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indirect_object" title="Indirect object">
        indirect objects
       </a>
       ). Loísmo and laísmo are almost entirely restricted to some dialects in central Spain; they are virtually absent from formal and written language. In practice
       <i>
        laísmo
       </i>
       is more frequent than
       <i>
        loísmo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A simple example of loísmo and laísmo would be saying
       <i>
        lo hablé
       </i>
       (lit. "I spoke him") or
       <i>
        la hablé
       </i>
       (lit. "I spoke her") where a speaker of a dialect without loísmo would say
       <i>
        le hablé
       </i>
       (lit. "I spoke to him/her").
      </p>
      <p>
       This effectively means the loss of a
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declensional
       </a>
       case marker. The difference between
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative case
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
        dative case
       </a>
       ) are holdovers from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        Latin declension
       </a>
       . The general trend in the evolution of Spanish has been to drop such declensions, but most dialects of Spanish have preserved this feature for object pronouns. It just happens that speakers with loísmo have further lost this distinction, replacing it with a different distinction of a semantic kind.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another effect of loísmo and laísmo is that the gender of the indirect object is clearer than it would be using
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       . One issue with non-loísmo dialects is that the
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       pronoun is ambiguous, as it does not specify gender. For example,
       <i>
        le doy un beso
       </i>
       can mean "I give him a kiss", "I give her a kiss", or even "I give you (formal) a kiss". One way around this ambiguity is to clarify the pronoun with a prepositional phrase; for our example, this would mean
       <i>
        le doy un beso a él
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        le doy un beso a ella
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        le doy un beso a usted
       </i>
       , respectively. Since
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       indicates masculine and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       indicates feminine, using loísmo and laísmo means that this clarification is not necessary.
      </p>
      <p>
       Loísmo can also seemingly change the meaning of certain phrases, since some verbs take on a different meaning based on the case of their objects. For example,
       <i>
        le pegué
       </i>
       means "I struck him", but a speaker with loísmo would say
       <i>
        lo pegué
       </i>
       , which literally means "I pasted/stuck him (onto something)" in dialects without loísmo.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Lo.C3.ADsmo_and_the_Real_Academia_Espa.C3.B1ola">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loísmo_and_the_Real_Academia_Española">
        Loísmo and the Real Academia Española
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lo%C3%ADsmo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Loísmo and the Real Academia Española">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       listed loísmo and laísmo as correct in 1771; however, it condemned their use in 1796. The
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.rae.es/recursos/diccionarios/dpd" rel="nofollow">
        Academy's online grammar guide
       </a>
       currently labels them as "inappropriate".
      </p>
      <p>
       The lack of acceptance from the RAE has caused a certain classist or social stigma to be attached to loísmo and those who use it. This often leads to
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       , with loístas choosing the
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       pronoun even for direct objects as a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
        leísmo
       </a>
       . It has also been a source for some criticism of the RAE itself.
      </p>
