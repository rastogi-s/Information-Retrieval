      <p>
       <b>
        Peruvian Ribereño Spanish
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Peruvian Coastal Spanish
       </b>
       is the form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken in the coastal region of
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       . The Spanish spoken in Coastal Peru has four characteristic forms today: the original one, that of the inhabitants of
       <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">
        Lima
       </a>
       (known as
       <i>
        limeños
       </i>
       ) near the Pacific coast and parts south, (formerly from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_center_of_Lima" title="Historical center of Lima">
        old section of the city
       </a>
       from where it spread to the entire coastal region); the inland immigrant
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolect" title="Sociolect">
        sociolect
       </a>
       (more influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andean
       </a>
       languages); the Northern, in
       <a href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Peru" title="Trujillo, Peru">
        Trujillo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chiclayo" title="Chiclayo">
        Chiclayo
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Piura" title="Piura">
        Piura
       </a>
       ; and the Southern. The majority of Peruvians speak Peruvian Coast Spanish, as Peruvian Coast Spanish is the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard dialect
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Spanish" title="Peruvian Spanish">
        Spanish in Peru
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Between 1535 and 1739, Lima was the capital of the
       <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       in South America, from where Hispanic culture spread, and its speech became the "purest"
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (August 2016)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       since it was the home of the famous University of San Marcos of Lima.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also, it was the city that had the highest number of titles of nobility from
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Castile
       </a>
       outside of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       . Colonial people in Lima became used to living an ostentatious and courtly life style that people in the other capital cities of Spanish America did not experience, with the exception of
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       and later the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">
        Bogotá
       </a>
       . On the other hand, they mostly lived from the riches extracted from the inland mines by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">
        Indians
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_subject">
        Grammatical subject
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Grammatical subject">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        'vos'
       </a>
       instead of 'tú' as a familiar form of address was a marker of low social class in post-medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , it exists throughout contemporary Latin America but it was never used in the capitals of the viceroyalties, such as Lima or Mexico City.
      </p>
      <p>
       Prescriptive Limeño Spanish has adjusted considerably to more closely resemble the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        standard Spanish
       </a>
       linguistic model, because of the city's disdain of the contact with the Andean world and
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        autochthonous languages
       </a>
       for centuries.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, until the beginning of the 20th century, speech on the Northern Peruvian coast was similar in many ways with how individuals spoke on the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
        Ecuadorian
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombian
       </a>
       coast. The most remarkable variation from the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       norm was the presence of 'vos', which was used to refer to one's family and is completely missing today. This part of Northern Peru also had a strong influence on the extinct Muchik or
       <a href="/wiki/Mochica_language" title="Mochica language">
        Mochica
       </a>
       language.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inland_immigrants_variation">
        Inland immigrants variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inland immigrants variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The other main variety of Spanish from the coast of Peru is that which appeared after the linguistic influence from the Sierra and of the rural environment into the coastal cities and the former
       <a href="/wiki/Lima" title="Lima">
        'Garden City'
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Urbanization" title="Urbanization">
        Great Andean Migration
       </a>
       (1940–1980).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its main characteristics are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The strong use of diminutives, double possessives and the routine use of 'pues' or 'pe' and 'nomás' in postverbal position.
       </li>
       <li>
        The redundant use of verbal clitics, particularly 'lo' (the so-called loismo)
       </li>
       <li>
        The bilabization of /f/
       </li>
       <li>
        Closed timbre
       </li>
       <li>
        Andean tone
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_changes">
        Recent changes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Recent changes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This popular variety of Coastal Peruvian dialect is not only contributed to by Andean influences but also, of course, by foreign ones: Anglicisms and
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentinisms
       </a>
       are all very present in the lexicon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, young people from Lima's higher socioeconomic strata have also developed a peculiar and mannered form of speaking, noticeable particularly in the way that they alter their tone of speaking.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Some_common_expressions">
        Some common expressions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Some common expressions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Agarrar y + to do something
        <i>
         (Agarré y le dije...)
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Parar (en) = to frequently be somewhere or to frequently do something
        <i>
         (Paras en la cabina)
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Pasar la voz = to inform (e.g. "spread the word")
       </li>
       <li>
        De repente = perhaps, suddenly (depending on context)
       </li>
       <li>
        Ni a palos = no way (literally "not even clubbed")
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contributions_by_other_ethnic_groups">
        Contributions by other ethnic groups
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Contributions by other ethnic groups">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Peruvians of foreign blood, especially of Chinese and Japanese descent, from first and second generations have a tinge of their native languages' rhythm and intonation to Lima accent, but majority of younger generations have no trace of their languages' accent, if they speak their native languages.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slang">
        Slang
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Slang">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some Peruvian slang comes from inverting the syllables of a word. This can be seen in words like 'fercho', which comes from the word 'chofer', driver, the word 'tolaca', which comes from 'calato'. Slang words do not always have to be the exact inverse of the original word: for example 'mica' comes from the word 'camisa', which means shirt. Or 'jerma' which comes from 'mujer' meaning woman.
      </p>
      <p>
       Peruvian slang originally developed in the 1970s and 1980s with the experience of military dictatorships and the ever-present threat of terrorist activities from Maoist groups such as the MRTA and Sendero Luminoso.
