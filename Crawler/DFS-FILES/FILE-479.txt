      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       , an
       <b>
        allophone
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'f' in 'find'">
            f
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ; from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        ἄλλος
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        állos
       </i>
       , "other" and φωνή,
       <i>
        phōnē
       </i>
       , "voice, sound") is one of a set of multiple possible spoken sounds (or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
         phones
        </a>
       </i>
       ) or signs used to pronounce a single
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       in a particular language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref00qacid_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ref00qacid-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [pʰ]
        </span>
       </a>
       (as in
       <i>
        pin
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        spin
       </i>
       which is less aspirated) are allophones for the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       . The specific allophone selected in a given situation is often predictable from the phonetic context (such allophones are called
       <b>
        positional variants
       </b>
       ), but sometimes allophones occur in
       <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">
        free variation
       </a>
       . Replacing a sound by another allophone of the same phoneme will usually not change the meaning of a word, although sometimes the result may sound non-native or even unintelligible. Native speakers of a given language usually perceive one phoneme in that language as a single distinctive sound, and are "
       <i>
        both unaware of and even shocked by
       </i>
       " the allophone variations used to pronounce single phonemes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref89rahec_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ref89rahec-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref06hozep_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ref06hozep-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_concept">
        History of concept
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of concept">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term "allophone" was coined by
       <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Lee_Whorf" title="Benjamin Lee Whorf">
        Benjamin Lee Whorf
       </a>
       in the 1940s. In doing so, he placed a cornerstone in consolidating early
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       theory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term was popularized by G. L. Trager and
       <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Bloch_(linguist)" title="Bernard Bloch (linguist)">
        Bernard Bloch
       </a>
       in a 1941 paper on English phonology
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and went on to become part of standard usage within the American structuralist tradition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Complementary_and_free-variant_allophones_and_assimilation">
        Complementary and free-variant allophones and assimilation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Complementary and free-variant allophones and assimilation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Every time a user's speech is vocalized for a given phoneme, it will be slightly different from other utterances, even for the same speaker. This has led to some debate over how real, and how universal, phonemes really are (see
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       for details). Only some of the variation is significant (i.e., detectable or perceivable) to speakers. There are two types of allophones, based on whether a phoneme must be pronounced using a specific allophone in a specific situation, or whether the speaker has freedom to (unconsciously) choose which allophone to use.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a specific allophone (from a set of allophones that correspond to a phoneme)
       <i>
        must
       </i>
       be selected in a given context (i.e., using a different allophone for a phoneme will cause confusion or make the speaker sound non-native), the allophones are said to be 'complementary' (i.e., the allophones complement each other, and one is not used in a situation where the usage of another is standard). In the case of complementary allophones, each allophone is used in a specific phonetic context and may be involved in a
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ref47qoref_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ref47qoref-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In other cases, the speaker is able to select freely from
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">
         free variant
        </a>
       </b>
       allophones, based on personal habit or preference.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example of an allophone is
       <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(phonology)" title="Assimilation (phonology)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       , wherein a phoneme is to sound more like the other phoneme. One example of assimilation is
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_voicing_and_devoicing" title="Consonant voicing and devoicing">
        consonant voicing and devoicing
       </a>
       , wherein voiceless consonants are voiced before and after voiced consonants and voiced consonants are devoiced before and after voiceless consonants.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Allotone">
        Allotone
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Allotone">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tonic
       </a>
       allophone is sometimes called an
       <b>
        allotone
       </b>
       , for example in the neutral tone of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin" title="Standard Mandarin">
        Mandarin
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/English_phonology#Allophones_of_consonants" title="English phonology">
        English phonology § Allophones of consonants
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/English_phonology#Allophones_of_vowels" title="English phonology">
        English phonology § Allophones of vowels
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There are many allophonic processes in English, like lack of plosion, nasal plosion, partial devoicing of sonorants, complete devoicing of sonorants, partial devoicing of obstruents, lengthening and shortening vowels, and retraction.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">
         Aspiration
        </a>
        – strong explosion of breath. In English a voiceless plosive (that is p, t or k) is aspirated whenever it stands as the consonant at the beginning of the stressed syllable or of the first, stressed or unstressed, syllable in a word. For example,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [pʰ]
        </span>
        as in
        <i>
         pin
        </i>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [p]
        </span>
        as in
        <i>
         spin
        </i>
        are allophones for the phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p/
        </span>
        because they cannot distinguish words (in fact, they occur in
        <a href="/wiki/Complementary_distribution" title="Complementary distribution">
         complementary distribution
        </a>
        ). English speakers treat them as the same sound, but they are different: the first is aspirated and the second is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unaspirated" title="Unaspirated">
         unaspirated
        </a>
        (plain). Many languages treat these two phones differently; see
        <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant#Usage_patterns" title="Aspirated consonant">
         Aspirated consonant, section Usage patterns
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Nasal plosion – In English a plosive (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p, t, k, b, d, ɡ/
        </span>
        ) has nasal plosion when it is followed by a nasal, inside a word or across word boundary.
       </li>
       <li>
        Partial devoicing of
        <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">
         sonorants
        </a>
        – In English sonorants (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j, w, l, r, m, n, ŋ/
        </span>
        ) are partially devoiced when they follow a voiceless sound within the same syllable.
       </li>
       <li>
        Complete devoicing of sonorants – In English a sonorant is completely devoiced when it follows an aspirated plosive (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p, t, k/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Partial devoicing of
        <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">
         obstruents
        </a>
        – In English, a voiced obstruent is partially devoiced next to a pause or next to a voiceless sound, inside a word or across its boundary.
       </li>
       <li>
        Retraction – in English
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t, d, n, l/
        </span>
        are retracted before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Because the choice of allophone is seldom under conscious control, people may not realize they exist. English speakers may be unaware of the differences among six allophones of the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       , namely unreleased
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ t̚]
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        cat
       </i>
       , aspirated
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʰ]
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        top
       </i>
       , glottalized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʔ]
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        button
       </i>
       , flapped
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       as in American English
       <i>
        water
       </i>
       , nasalized flapped as in
       <i>
        winter
       </i>
       , and none of the above
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        stop
       </i>
       . However, they may become aware of the differences if, for example, they contrast the pronunciations of the following words:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Night rate
        </i>
        : unreleased
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnʌɪt̚.ɹʷeɪt̚]
        </span>
        (without
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_space" title="Word space">
         word space
        </a>
        between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         .
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         ɹ
        </span>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nitrate
        </i>
        : aspirated
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnaɪ.tʰɹ̥eɪt̚]
        </span>
        or retracted
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnaɪ.tʃɹʷeɪt̚]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If a flame is held before the lips while these words are spoken, it flickers more during aspirated
       <i>
        nitrate
       </i>
       than during unaspirated
       <i>
        night rate.
       </i>
       The difference can also be felt by holding the hand in front of the lips. For a Mandarin speaker, to whom
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʰ/
       </span>
       are separate phonemes, the English distinction is much more obvious than it is to the English speaker who has learned since childhood to ignore it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Allophones of English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       may be noticed if the 'light'
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       of
       <i>
        leaf
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈliːf]
       </span>
       is contrasted with the 'dark'
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɫ]
       </span>
       of
       <i>
        feel
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈfiːɫ]
       </span>
       . Again, this difference is much more obvious to a Turkish speaker, for whom
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɫ/
       </span>
       are separate phonemes, than to an English speaker, for whom they are allophones of a single phoneme.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Representing_a_phoneme_with_an_allophone">
        Representing a phoneme with an allophone
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Allophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Representing a phoneme with an allophone">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since phonemes are abstractions of speech sounds, not the sounds themselves, they have no direct
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_transcription" title="Phonetic transcription">
        phonetic transcription
       </a>
       . When they are realized without much allophonic variation, a simple
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broad_transcription" title="Broad transcription">
         broad transcription
        </a>
       </i>
       is used. However, when there are complementary allophones of a phoneme, so that the allophony is significant, things become more complicated. Often, if only one of the allophones is simple to transcribe, in the sense of not requiring diacritics, then that representation is chosen for the phoneme.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, there may be several such allophones, or the linguist may prefer greater precision than this allows. In such cases a common convention is to use the "elsewhere condition" to decide which allophone will stand for the phoneme. The "elsewhere" allophone is the one that remains once the conditions for the others are described by phonological rules. For example, English has both oral and nasal allophones of its vowels. The pattern is that vowels are nasal only when preceding a nasal consonant within the same syllable; elsewhere they are oral. Therefore, by the "elsewhere" convention, the oral allophones are considered basic; nasal vowels in English are considered to be allophones of oral phonemes.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other cases, an allophone may be chosen to represent its phoneme because it is more common in the world's languages than the other allophones, because it reflects the historical origin of the phoneme, or because it gives a more balanced look to a chart of the phonemic inventory. Another alternative, commonly employed for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archiphoneme" title="Archiphoneme">
        archiphonemes
       </a>
       , is the use of a capital letter, such as /N/ for [m], [n], [ŋ].
      </p>
      <p>
       In rare cases a linguist may represent phonemes with abstract symbols, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Dingbat" title="Dingbat">
        dingbats
       </a>
       , so as not to privilege any one allophone.

        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
