      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Spanish Constitution
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Constitución Española
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="eu">
         Espainiako Konstituzioa
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="ca">
         Constitució Espanyola
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="gl">
         Constitución Española
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supreme_law" title="Supreme law">
        supreme law
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Kingdom of Spain
       </a>
       . It was enacted after a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_constitutional_referendum,_1978" title="Spanish constitutional referendum, 1978">
        constitutional referendum
       </a>
       , and it is the culmination of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">
        Spanish transition to democracy
       </a>
       . The Constitution of 1978 is one of about a dozen of other historical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutions_of_Spain" title="Constitutions of Spain">
        Spanish constitutions and constitution-like documents
       </a>
       ; however, it is one of two fully democratic constitutions (the other being the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1931" title="Spanish Constitution of 1931">
        Spanish Constitution of 1931
       </a>
       ). It was sanctioned by King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I" title="Juan Carlos I">
        Juan Carlos I
       </a>
       on 27 December, and published in the
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Bolet%C3%ADn_Oficial_del_Estado" title="Boletín Oficial del Estado">
          Boletín Oficial del Estado
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Government_gazette" title="Government gazette">
        government gazette
       </a>
       of Spain) on 29 December, the date in which it became effective. The promulgation of the constitution marked the culmination of the Spanish transition to democracy after the death of general
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Francisco Franco
       </a>
       , on 20 November 1975, who
       <a href="/wiki/Francoist_Spain" title="Francoist Spain">
        ruled over Spain as a military dictator
       </a>
       for nearly 40 years. This led to the country undergoing a series of political, social and historical changes that transformed the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francoist" title="Francoist">
        Francoist
       </a>
       regime into a democratic state.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish transition to democracy was a complex process that gradually transformed the legal framework of the Francoist regime into a democratic state. The Spanish state didn't "abolish" the Francoist regime, but rather slowly transformed the institutions and approved and/or derogated laws so as to establish a democratic nation and approve the Constitution, all under the guidance of King
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">
        Juan Carlos I of Spain
       </a>
       . The Constitution was redacted, debated and approved by the
       <a href="/wiki/Constituent_assembly" title="Constituent assembly">
        constituent assembly
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Cortes Constituyentes
        </span>
       </i>
       ) that emerged from the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_1977" title="Spanish general election, 1977">
        1977 general election
       </a>
       . The Constitution then repealed all the
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_Laws_of_the_Realm" title="Fundamental Laws of the Realm">
        Fundamental Laws of the Realm
       </a>
       (the pseudo-constitution of the Francoist regime), as well as other major historical laws and every pre-existing law that contradicted what the Constitution establishes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Article 1 of the Constitution defines the Spanish state. Article 1.1 states that "Spain is established as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_state" title="Social state">
        social
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Democracy" title="Democracy">
        democratic
       </a>
       State, subject to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">
        rule of law
       </a>
       , which advocates as the highest values of its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legal_order" title="Legal order">
        legal order
       </a>
       the following:
       <a href="/wiki/Liberty" title="Liberty">
        liberty
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Justice" title="Justice">
        justice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Social_equality" title="Social equality">
        equality
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Political_pluralism" title="Political pluralism">
        political pluralism
       </a>
       . Article 1.2 refers to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_sovereignty" title="National sovereignty">
        national sovereignty
       </a>
       , which is vested in the Spanish people, "from whom the powers of the State emanate". Article 1.3 establishes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parliamentary_monarchy" title="Parliamentary monarchy">
        parliamentary monarchy
       </a>
       as the "political form of the Spanish state".
      </p>
      <p>
       The Constitution is organized in ten parts (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Títulos
        </span>
       </i>
       ) and an additional introduction (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Título Preliminar
        </span>
       </i>
       ), as well as a preamble, several additional and interim provisions and a series of repeals, and it ends with a final provision. Part I refers to fundamental rights and duties, which receive special treatment and protection under Spanish law. Part II refers to the regulation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monarchy_in_Spain" title="Monarchy in Spain">
        the Crown
       </a>
       and lays out the King's role in the Spanish state. Part III elaborates on Spain's legislature, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">
        Cortes Generales
       </a>
       . Part IV refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Spain" title="Government of Spain">
        Government of Spain
       </a>
       , the executive power, and the Public Administration, which is managed by the executive. Part V refers to the relations between the Government and the Cortes Generales; as a parliamentary monarchy, the Prime Minister (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Presidente del Gobierno
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is invested by the legislature and the Government is responsible before the legislature. Part VI refers to the organization of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judicial_power" title="Judicial power">
        judicial power
       </a>
       , establishing that justice emanates from the people and is administered on behalf of the King by
       <a href="/wiki/Judge" title="Judge">
        judges
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magistrate" title="Magistrate">
        magistrates
       </a>
       who are independent, irrevocable, liable and subject to the rule of law only. Part VII refers to the principles that shall guide the
       <a href="/wiki/Economy_of_Spain" title="Economy of Spain">
        economy
       </a>
       and the finances of the Spanish state, subjecting all the wealth in the country to the general interest and recognizing public initiative in the economy, while also protecting
       <a href="/wiki/Private_property" title="Private property">
        private property
       </a>
       in the framework of a
       <a href="/wiki/Market_economy" title="Market economy">
        market economy
       </a>
       . It also establishes the
       <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Accounts_(Spain)" title="Court of Accounts (Spain)">
        Court of Accounts
       </a>
       and the principles that shall guide the approval of the state budget. Part VIII refers to the "territorial organization of the State" and establishes a unitary state that is nevertheless heavily decentralized through delegation and transfer of powers. The result is a
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">
        federal
       </a>
       model, with some differences from federal states. This is referred to as an autonomous state (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Estado Autonómico
        </span>
       </i>
       ) or state of the autonomies (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Estado de las Autonomías
        </span>
       </i>
       ). Part IX refers to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_(Spain)" title="Constitutional Court (Spain)">
        Constitutional Court
       </a>
       , which oversees the constitutionality of all laws and protects the fundamental rights enshrined in Part I. Finally, Part X refers to constitutional amendments, of which there have been only two since 1978 (in 1995 and 2011).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutions_of_Spain" title="Constitutions of Spain">
        Constitutions of Spain
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The constitutional history of Spain dates back to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Constitution_of_1812" title="Spanish Constitution of 1812">
        Constitution of 1812
       </a>
       . After the death of dictator
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Francisco Franco
       </a>
       in 1975, a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_general_election,_1977" title="Spanish general election, 1977">
        general election
       </a>
       in 1977 convened the
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Constituent_Cortes" title="Constituent Cortes">
          Constituent Cortes
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (the Spanish Parliament, in its capacity as a constitutional assembly) for the purpose of drafting and approving the constitution.
      </p>
      <p>
       A seven-member panel was selected among the elected members of the
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Cortes
        </span>
       </i>
       to work on a draft of the Constitution to be submitted to the body. These came to be known, as the media put it, as the
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         padres de la Constitución
        </span>
       </i>
       or "fathers of the Constitution". These seven people were chosen to represent the wide (and often, deeply divided) political spectrum within the Spanish Parliament, while the leading role was given to then ruling party and now defunct
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Center_Union_(Spain)" title="Democratic Center Union (Spain)">
          Unión de Centro Democrático
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (UCD).
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>

      <p>
       The writer (and Senator by Royal appointment)
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Jos%C3%A9_Cela" title="Camilo José Cela">
          Camilo José Cela
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       later polished the draft Constitution's wording. However, since much of the consensus depended on keeping the wording ambiguous, few of Cela's proposed re-wordings were approved. One of those accepted was the substitution of the archaic
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         gualda
        </span>
       </i>
       ("
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mignonette_(Reseda)" title="Mignonette (Reseda)">
        weld
       </a>
       -colored") for the plain
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         amarillo
        </span>
       </i>
       (yellow) in the description of the
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_Spain" title="Flag of Spain">
        flag of Spain
       </a>
       .

           citation needed

        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The constitution was approved by the
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">
          Cortes Generales
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       on 31 October 1978, and by the Spanish people in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_constitutional_referendum,_1978" title="Spanish constitutional referendum, 1978">
        a referendum
       </a>
       on 6 December 1978. 91.81% of voters supported the new constitution. Finally, it was sanctioned by King Juan Carlos on 27 December in a ceremony in the presence of parliamentarians. It came into effect on 29 December, the day it was published in the Official Gazette.
       <a href="/wiki/Constitution_Day" title="Constitution Day">
        Constitution Day
       </a>
       on 6 December has since been a
       <a href="/wiki/Public_holidays_in_Spain" title="Public holidays in Spain">
        national holiday in Spain
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">
        Structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Constitution recognizes the existence of
       <a href="/wiki/Nationalities_and_regions_of_Spain" title="Nationalities and regions of Spain">
        nationalities and regions
       </a>
       (Preliminary Title).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Preamble">
        Preamble
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Preamble">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Traditionally, writing the preamble to the constitution was considered an honour, and a task requiring great literary ability. The person chosen for this purpose was
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Tierno_Galv%C3%A1n" title="Enrique Tierno Galván">
          Enrique Tierno Galván
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       . The full text of the preamble may be translated as follows:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <dl>
        <dd>
         The Spanish Nation, wishing to establish justice, liberty and security, and to promote the welfare of all who make part of it, in use of her sovereignty, proclaims its will to:
         <dl>
          <dd>
           Guarantee democratic life within the Constitution and the laws according to a just economic and social order.
          </dd>
          <dd>
           Consolidate a State ensuring the
           <a href="/wiki/Rule_of_law" title="Rule of law">
            rule of law
           </a>
           as an expression of the will of the people.
          </dd>
          <dd>
           Protect all Spaniards and all the peoples of Spain in the exercise of
           <a href="/wiki/Human_rights" title="Human rights">
            human rights
           </a>
           , their cultures and traditions, languages and institutions.
          </dd>
          <dd>
           Promote the progress of culture and the economy to ensure a dignified quality of life for all
          </dd>
          <dd>
           Establish an advanced democratic society, and
          </dd>
          <dd>
           Collaborate in the strengthening of peaceful and efficient cooperation among all the peoples of the Earth.
          </dd>
         </dl>
        </dd>
        <dd>
         Consequently, the
         <i>
          <span lang="es">
           Cortes
          </span>
         </i>
         approve and the Spanish people ratify the following Constitution.
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Preliminary_Title">
        Preliminary Title
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preliminary Title">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Section 2. The Constitution is based on the indissoluble unity of the Spanish Nation, the common and indivisible homeland of all Spaniards; it recognizes and guarantees the right to self-government of the nationalities and regions of which it is composed and the solidarity among them all.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       As a result, Spain is now composed entirely of 17 Autonomous Communities and two autonomous cities with varying degrees of autonomy, to the extent that, even though the Constitution does not formally state that Spain is a federation (nor a unitary state), actual power shows, depending on the issue considered, widely varying grades of decentralization, ranging from the quasi-confederal status of tax management in Navarre and the Basque Country to the total centralization in airport management.
      </p>

       The Constitution dedicates its Part II to the regulation of the monarchy, which is referred to as
       <i>
        The Crown
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         La Corona
        </span>
       </i>
       ). Article 56 of the Constitution establishes that the monarchy is the
       <a href="/wiki/Head_of_state" title="Head of state">
        head of state
       </a>
       and symbolizes the unity of the Spanish state. It refers to the monarch's role as a "moderator" whose main role is to oversee and ensure the regular functioning of the institutions. The King is also the highest-ranked representative of the Spanish state in
       <a href="/wiki/International_relations" title="International relations">
        international relations
       </a>
       and only exercises the functions that are explicitly attributed to him by the Constitution and the laws. The King's official title is "King of Spain" (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Rey de España
        </span>
       </i>
       ), but he is allowed to use any
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_titles_and_honours_of_the_Spanish_Crown" title="List of titles and honours of the Spanish Crown">
        other titles that are associated to the Spanish Crown
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The King of Spain enjoys immunity and is not subject to legal responsibility. In a broad sense, this means that the King cannot be legally prosecuted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some jurists say that this only refers to criminal procedures, while others claim this immunity is also present in civil procedures; in practice, the King has never been prosecuted and it is unlikely that he would be prosecuted even if it was proven that the monarch had committed a crime. The legal justification for royal immunity is that the King is mandated by the Constitution to fulfill several roles as the head of state; thus, the King is obligated to perform his actions and fulfill his duties, so the King cannot be judged for actions that he is constitutionally obligated to perform.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_refrendo">
        The refrendo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The refrendo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The fact that the King is not personally responsible for his actions does not mean that his actions are not free of responsibility. The responsibility for the King's actions falls into the persons who hold actual political power and who actually take political decisions, which the King only formally and symbolically ratifies. This is done through a procedure or institution called the refrendo.
      </p>
      <p>
       All the King's actions have to undergo the refrendo procedure. Through the refrendo, the responsibility of the King is transported to other persons, who will be responsible for the King's actions, if such responsibility is demanded from them. Article 64 explains the refrendo, which transports the King's responsibility unto the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_Spain" title="Prime Minister of Spain">
        Prime Minister
       </a>
       for most cases, though it also establishes the refrendo for Ministers in some cases. In general, when there is not a formed government, the responsibility is assumed by the
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Congress_of_Deputies" title="President of the Congress of Deputies">
        President of the Congress of Deputies
       </a>
       . Without the refrendo, the King's actions are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Null_and_void" title="Null and void">
        null and void
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are only two royal acts that do not require the refrendo. The first encompasses all acts related to the management of the Royal House of Spain; the King can freely hire and fire any employees of the Royal House and he receives an annual amount from the state budget to operate the Royal House, which he freely distributes across the institution. The second one refers to the King's will, which enables him to distribute his material legacy and name tutors for his children, if they are not legal adults.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="The_King.27s_functions_in_the_Spanish_state">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_King's_functions_in_the_Spanish_state">
        The King's functions in the Spanish state
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: The King's functions in the Spanish state">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Article 62 of the Spanish Constitution establishes an exhaustive list of the King's functions, all of which are symbolic and do not reflect the exercise of any political power. The King sanctions and promulagtes the laws, which are approved by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">
        Cortes Generales
       </a>
       , which the King also symbolically and formally calls and dissolves. The King also calls for periodic elections and for referendums in the cases that are included by the laws or the Constitution.
      </p>
      <p>
       The King also proposes a candidate for Prime Minister, which is probably the King's most 'political' function, as he traditionally holds meetings with the leaders of all the major political parties in order to facilitate the formation of a government. If a candidate is successfully invested by the Parliament, he formally names him Prime Minister of Spain. When a Prime Minister has been named, he also formally names all the members of his government, all of which are proposed by the Prime Minister himself. The King has both a right and a duty to be informed of all the state affairs; he is also allowed to preside over the government meetings when the Prime Minister invites him to do so, although he has the ability to reject this invitation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarding the Government, the King also formally issues the governmental decrees, as well as bestowing all the civil and military ranks and employments, and he also grants honors and distinctions according to the laws. The King is also the supreme head of the Armed Forces of Spain, although the effective lead is held by the Government of Spain. Finally, the King holds the High Patronage of all the Royal Academies and other organizations that have a royal patronage.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       The Spanish Constitution is one of the few Bill of Rights that has legal provisions for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_rights" title="Social rights">
        social rights
       </a>
       , including the definition of Spain itself as a "Social and Democratic State, subject to the rule of law" (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         Estado social y democrático de derecho
        </span>
       </i>
       ) in its preliminary title. However, those rights are not at the same level of protection as the individual rights contained in articles 14 to 28, since those social rights are considered in fact principles and directives of economic policy, but never full rights of the citizens to be claimed before a court or tribunal.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other constitutional provisions recognize the right to adequate housing,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Employment" title="Employment">
        employment
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_welfare_provision" title="Social welfare provision">
        social welfare provision
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Social_security" title="Social security">
        health protection
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pensions" title="Pensions">
        pensions
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Thanks to the political influence of
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a href="/wiki/Santiago_Carrillo" title="Santiago Carrillo">
          Santiago Carrillo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Communist_Party_of_Spain_(main)" title="Communist Party of Spain (main)">
        Communist Party of Spain
       </a>
       , and with the consensus of the other "fathers of the constitution", the right to State intervention in private companies in the public interest and the facilitation of access by workers to ownership of the means of production were also enshrined in the Constitution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reform">
        Reform
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Reform">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Constitution has been amended twice. The first time, Article 13.2, Title I was altered to extend to citizens of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       the right to active and passive suffrage (both voting rights and eligibility as candidates) in local elections. The second time, in August/September 2011, a
       <a href="/wiki/Balanced_budget_amendment" title="Balanced budget amendment">
        balanced budget amendment
       </a>
       and debt brake was added to Article 135.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The current version restricts the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_penalty" title="Death penalty">
        death penalty
       </a>
       to military courts during wartime, but the death penalty has since been removed from the Code of Military Justice and thus lost all relevance.
       <a href="/wiki/Amnesty_International" title="Amnesty International">
        Amnesty International
       </a>
       has still requested an amendment to be made to the Constitution to abolish it firmly and explicitly in all cases.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amnesty_International_Spain" title="Amnesty International Spain">
        Amnesty International Spain
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxfam_Interm%C3%B3n" title="Oxfam Intermón">
          Oxfam Intermón
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Greenpeace" title="Greenpeace">
        Greenpeace
       </a>
       launched in 2015 a campaign
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blinda_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blinda-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to amend the article 53 so that it extends the same protection to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_right" title="Economic right">
        economic
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Social_right" title="Social right">
        social
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_rights" title="Cultural rights">
        cultural rights
       </a>
       as to other rights like
       <a href="/wiki/Right_to_life" title="Right to life">
        life
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right_to_freedom" title="Right to freedom">
        freedom
       </a>
       . After that, the campaign seeks another 24 amendments protecting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_right" title="Human right">
        human rights
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
        environment
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Social_justice" title="Social justice">
        social justice
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Protected_provisions">
        Protected provisions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constitution_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Protected provisions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Title X of the Constitution establishes that the approval of a new constitution or the approval of any constitutional amendment affecting the Preliminary Title, or Section I of Chapter II of Title I (on Fundamental Rights and Public Liberties) or Title II (on the Crown), the so-called "protected provisions", are subject to a special process that requires:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        that two-thirds of each House approve the amendment,
       </li>
       <li>
        that elections are called immediately thereafter,
       </li>
       <li>
        that two-thirds of each new House approves the amendment, and
       </li>
       <li>
        that the amendment is approved by the people in a referendum.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Curiously, Title X does not include itself among the "protected provisions" and, therefore, it would be possible, at least in theory, to first amend Title X using the normal procedure to remove or reduce severity of the special requirements, and then change the formerly protected provisions. Even though such a procedure would not formally violate the law, it could be considered an attack on its spirit.
      </p>

      <p>
       The "
       <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy" title="Statute of Autonomy">
        Statutes of Autonomy
       </a>
       " of the different regions are the second most important Spanish legal normatives when it comes to the political structure of the country. Because of that, the reform attempts of some of them have been either rejected or produced considerable controversy.
      </p>
      <p>
       The plan conducted by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_president" title="Basque president">
        Basque president
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="eu">
         <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Ibarretxe" title="Juan José Ibarretxe">
          Juan José Ibarretxe
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (known as
       <a href="/wiki/Ibarretxe_Plan" title="Ibarretxe Plan">
        Ibarretxe Plan
       </a>
       ) to reform the status of the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
        Basque Country
       </a>
       in the Spanish state was rejected by the Spanish Cortes, on the grounds (among others) that it amounted to an implicit reform of the Constitution.
      </p>
      <p>
       The People's Party attempted to reject the admission into the Cortes of the 2005 reform of the
       <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy_of_Catalonia" title="Statute of Autonomy of Catalonia">
        Autonomy Statute of Catalonia
       </a>
       on the grounds that it should be dealt with as a constitutional reform rather than a mere statute reform because it allegedly contradicts the spirit of the Constitution in many points, especially the Statute's alleged breaches of the "solidarity between regions" principle enshrined by the Constitution. After failing to assemble the required majority to dismiss the text, the People's Party filed a claim of unconstitutionality against several dozen articles of the text before the Spanish Constitutional Court for them to be struck down.
      </p>
      <p>
       The amended Autonomy Statute of Catalonia has also been legally contested by the surrounding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_Communities" title="Autonomous Communities">
        Autonomous Communities
       </a>
       of Aragon, Balearic Islands and the Valencian Community
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on similar grounds as those of the PP, and others such as disputed
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_heritage" title="Cultural heritage">
        cultural heritage
       </a>
       . As of January 2008, the
       <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Court_of_Spain" title="Constitutional Court of Spain">
        Constitutional Court of Spain
       </a>
       has those alleged breaches and its actual compliance with the Constitution under
       <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review" title="Judicial review">
        judicial review
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Prominent Spanish politicians, mostly from the People's Party but also from the Socialist Party (PSOE) and other non-nationalist parties
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , have advocated for the statutory reform process to be more closely compliant with the Constitution, on the grounds that the current wave of reforms threatens the functional destruction of the constitutional system itself. The most cited arguments are the self-appointed unprecedented expansions of the powers of autonomous communities present in recently reformed statutes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The amended version of the Catalan Statute prompts the State to allot investments in Catalonia according to Catalonia's own percentage contribution to the total Spanish
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gross_Domestic_Product" title="Gross Domestic Product">
         GDP
        </a>
        . The Autonomy Statute of Andalusia, a region that contributes less to Spain's GDP than the region of Catalonia contributes, requires it in turn to allocate state investments in proportion to its population (it is the largest Spanish Autonomous Community in terms of population). These requirements are legally binding, as they are enacted as part of Autonomy Statutes, which rank only below the Constitution itself. It is self-evident that, should all autonomous communities be allowed to establish their particular financing models upon the State, the total may add up to more than 100% and that would be unviable.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Despite these changes having been proposed and approved by fellow members of the PSOE, former
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minister_of_Economy_and_Finance_(Spain)" title="Minister of Economy and Finance (Spain)">
         Finance Minister
        </a>
        <i>
         <span lang="es">
          <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Solbes" title="Pedro Solbes">
           Pedro Solbes
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        disagreed with this new trend of assigning state investment quotas to territories based on any given autonomous community custom requirement
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and has subsequently compared the task of planning the Spanish national budget to a
        <a href="/wiki/Sudoku" title="Sudoku">
         sudoku
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The Valencian statute, whose reform was one of the first to be enacted, includes the so-called Camps clause (named after the Valencian President
        <i>
         <span lang="ca">
          <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Camps" title="Francisco Camps">
           Francisco Camps
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ), which makes any powers assumed by other communities in its statutes automatically available to the Valencian Community.
       </li>
       <li>
        Autonomous communities such as Catalonia, Aragon, Andalusia or Extremadura, have included statutory clauses claiming exclusive powers over any river flowing through their territories. Nearby communities have filed complaints before the Spanish Constitutional Court on the grounds that no Community can exercise exclusive power over rivers that cross more than one Community, not even over the part flowing through its territory because its decisions affect other Communities, both downstream or upstream.
       </li>
      </ul>
