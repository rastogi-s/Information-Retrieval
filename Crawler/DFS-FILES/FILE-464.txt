      <p>
       <b>
        Case
       </b>
       is a special
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical category
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronoun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        participle
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
        numeral
       </a>
       whose value reflects the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_function" title="Grammatical function">
        grammatical function
       </a>
       performed by that word in a
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrase
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">
        clause
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)" title="Sentence (linguistics)">
        sentence
       </a>
       . In some languages, nouns, pronouns, adjectives,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiners" title="Determiners">
        determiners
       </a>
       , participles, prepositions, numerals, articles and their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modifier_(grammar)" title="Modifier (grammar)">
        modifiers
       </a>
       take different
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflected
       </a>
       forms depending on what case they are in. As a language evolves, cases can merge (for instance, in Ancient Greek, the locative case merged with the dative), a phenomenon formally called
       <a href="/wiki/Syncretism_(linguistics)" title="Syncretism (linguistics)">
        syncretism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClackson200791_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClackson200791-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/English_grammar" title="English grammar">
        English
       </a>
       has largely lost its case system, although personal pronouns still have three cases that are simplified forms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
        genitive cases
       </a>
       that are used with
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       :
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjective_case" title="Subjective case">
        subjective case
       </a>
       (I, you, he, she, it, we, they, who, whoever),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Objective_case" title="Objective case">
        objective case
       </a>
       (me, you, him, her, it, us, them, whom, whomever) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_case" title="Possessive case">
        possessive case
       </a>
       (my, mine; your, yours; his; her, hers; its; our, ours; their, theirs; whose; whosever
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ). Forms such as
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        we
       </i>
       are used for the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ("
       <b>
        I
       </b>
       kicked the ball"), whereas forms such as
       <i>
        me
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        him
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        us
       </i>
       are used for the
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       ("John kicked
       <b>
        me
       </b>
       ").
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">
        Tibetan
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_Language" title="Czech Language">
        Czech
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovak_Language" title="Slovak Language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">
        Belarusian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
        Ukrainian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">
        Lithuanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       and the majority of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caucasian_languages" title="Caucasian languages">
        Caucasian languages
       </a>
       have extensive case systems, with nouns, pronouns,
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       all inflecting (usually by means of different
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        suffixes
       </a>
       ) to indicate their case. The number of cases differs between languages:
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       has two; German and Icelandic have four; Turkish, Latin and Russian each have at least six; Armenian, Czech, Polish, Serbo-Croatian, Ukrainian, and Lithuanian have seven; Sanskrit has eight; Estonian has fourteen and Finnish has fifteen, Hungarian has eighteen and
       <a href="/wiki/Tsez_language" title="Tsez language">
        Tsez
       </a>
       has sixty-four.
      </p>
      <p>
       Commonly encountered cases include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">
        nominative
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accusative" title="Accusative">
        accusative
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative" title="Dative">
        dative
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">
        genitive
       </a>
       . A role that one of these languages marks by case will often be marked in English using a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       . For example, the English prepositional phrase
       <i>
        with (his) foot
       </i>
       (as in "John kicked the ball with his foot") might be rendered in Russian using a single noun in the
       <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">
        instrumental case
       </a>
       , or in Ancient Greek as
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        τῷ ποδί
       </span>
       (
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">
        <i>
         tôi podí
        </i>
       </span>
       , meaning "the foot") with both words (the definite article, and the noun
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        πούς
       </span>
       (
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">
        <i>
         poús
        </i>
       </span>
       ) "foot") changing to dative form.
      </p>
      <p>
       More formally, case has been defined as "a system of marking dependent nouns for the type of relationship they bear to their heads".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         p.1
        </span>
       </sup>
       Cases should be distinguished from
       <a href="/wiki/Thematic_relation" title="Thematic relation">
        thematic roles
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">
         agent
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Patient_(grammar)" title="Patient (grammar)">
         patient
        </a>
       </i>
       . They are often closely related, and in languages such as Latin several thematic roles have an associated case, but cases are a
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       notion, whereas thematic roles are a
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantic
       </a>
       one. Languages having cases often exhibit
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_word_order" title="Free word order">
        free word order
       </a>
       , because thematic roles are not required to be marked by position in the sentence.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The English word
       <i>
        case
       </i>
       used in this sense comes from the Latin
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         casus
        </span>
       </i>
       , which is derived from the verb
       <i lang="la" xml:lang="la">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cado#Latin" title="wikt:cado">
         cadere
        </a>
       </i>
       , "to fall", from the
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_root" title="Proto-Indo-European root">
        Proto-Indo-European root
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/%E1%B8%B1ad-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/ḱad-">
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          *ḱad-
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Latin word is a
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       of the Greek
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        πτῶσις
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="Ancient Greek transliteration">
        <i>
         ptôsis
        </i>
       </span>
       , lit. "falling, fall".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The sense is that all other cases are considered to have "fallen" away from the nominative. This picture is also reflected in the word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
         declension
        </a>
       </i>
       , from Latin
       <i lang="la" xml:lang="la">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/declino#Latin" title="wikt:declino">
         declinere
        </a>
       </i>
       , "to lean", from the PIE root
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/%E1%B8%B1ley-" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European/ḱley-">
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          *ḱley-
         </span>
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The equivalent to "case" in several other European languages also derives from
       <i>
        casus
       </i>
       , including
       <i lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cas#French" title="wikt:cas">
         cas
        </a>
       </i>
       in French,
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caso#Spanish" title="wikt:caso">
         caso
        </a>
       </i>
       in Spanish and
       <i lang="de" xml:lang="de">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Kasus#German" title="wikt:Kasus">
         Kasus
        </a>
       </i>
       in German. The Russian word
       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        паде́ж
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        padyézh
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       from Greek and similarly contains a root meaning "fall", and the German
       <i lang="de" xml:lang="de">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Fall#German" title="wikt:Fall">
         Fall
        </a>
       </i>
       and Czech
       <i lang="cs" xml:lang="cs">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/p%C3%A1d#Czech" title="wikt:pád">
         pád
        </a>
       </i>
       simply mean "fall", and are used for both the concept of grammatical case and to refer to physical falls. The Finnish equivalent is
       <i lang="fi" xml:lang="fi">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sija#Finnish" title="wikt:sija">
         sija
        </a>
       </i>
       , which can also mean "position" or "support".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-European_languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Indo-European languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="1536" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg/220px-E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg/330px-E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg/440px-E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:E7127-Balakhna-500-Years.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         On this sign in Russian memorializing an anniversary of the city of
         <a href="/wiki/Balakhna" title="Balakhna">
          Balakhna
         </a>
         , the word
         <i>
          Balakhna
         </i>
         on the right is in the nominative case, whereas the word
         <i>
          Balakhne
         </i>
         is in the dative case in
         <i>
          Balakhne 500 Let
         </i>
         ('Balakhna is 500 years old', or more literally 'To Balakhna 500 (of) years') on the front of the sign. Furthermore,
         <i>
          let
         </i>
         is in the genitive (plural) case.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although not very prominent in modern English, cases featured much more saliently in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">
        Old English
       </a>
       and other ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sanskrit_language" title="Sanskrit language">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       . Historically, the Indo-European languages had eight morphological cases, though modern languages typically have fewer, using prepositions and word order to convey information that had previously been conveyed using distinct noun forms. Among modern languages, cases still feature prominently in most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Balto-Slavic_languages" title="Balto-Slavic languages">
        Balto-Slavic languages
       </a>
       (except Macedonian and Bulgarian
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), with most having six to eight cases, as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       , which have four. In German, cases are mostly marked on articles and adjectives, and less so on nouns. In Icelandic, articles, adjectives, personal names and nouns are all marked for case, making it, among other things, the living
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic language
       </a>
       that could be said to most closely resemble
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">
        Proto-Germanic
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       All of the above are just rough descriptions; the precise distinctions vary from language to language, and are often quite complex. Case is based fundamentally on changes to the noun to indicate the noun's role in the sentence. This is not how English works; instead, word order and prepositions are used to achieve this.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Modern
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       has largely abandoned the inflectional case system of Indo-European in favor of
       <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">
        analytic
       </a>
       constructions. The
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       of Modern English retain morphological case more strongly than any other word class (a remnant of the more extensive
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_declension" title="Old English declension">
        case system of Old English
       </a>
       ). For other pronouns, and all nouns, adjectives, and articles, grammatical function is indicated only by
       <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">
        word order
       </a>
       , by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       , and by the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saxon_genitive" title="Saxon genitive">
        Saxon genitive
       </a>
       " (
       <i>
        -'s
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Taken as a whole,
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        English personal pronouns
       </a>
       are typically said to have three morphological cases:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
          nominative case
         </a>
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjective_pronouns" title="Subjective pronouns">
          subjective pronouns
         </a>
        </i>
        such as
        <i>
         I
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         he
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         she
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         we
        </i>
        ), used for the subject of a
        <a href="/wiki/Finite_verb" title="Finite verb">
         finite verb
        </a>
        and sometimes for the
        <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">
         complement
        </a>
        of a
        <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
         copula
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">
          oblique case
         </a>
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Object_pronoun" title="Object pronoun">
          object pronouns
         </a>
        </i>
        such as
        <i>
         me
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         him
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         her
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         us
        </i>
        ), used for the direct or indirect
        <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
         object
        </a>
        of a verb, for the object of a preposition, for an absolute disjunct, and sometimes for the complement of a copula.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
          genitive case
         </a>
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_pronouns" title="Possessive pronouns">
          possessive pronouns
         </a>
        </i>
        such as
        <i>
         my/mine
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         his
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         her(s)
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         our(s)
        </i>
        ), used for a grammatical possessor. This is not always considered to be a case; see
        <a href="/wiki/English_possessive#Status_of_the_possessive_as_a_grammatical_case" title="English possessive">
         English possessive § Status of the possessive as a grammatical case
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Most English personal pronouns have five forms: the nominative and oblique case forms, the possessive case, which has both a
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(linguistics)" title="Determiner (linguistics)">
         determiner
        </a>
       </i>
       form (such as
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        our
       </i>
       ) and a distinct
       <i>
        independent
       </i>
       form (such as
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ours
       </i>
       ) (with two exceptions: the third person singular masculine and the third person singular neuter
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       , which use the same form for both determiner and independent [
       <i>
        his car
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        it is his
       </i>
       ]), and a distinct
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
         reflexive
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        intensive
       </i>
       form (such as
       <i>
        myself
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ourselves
       </i>
       ). The interrogative personal pronoun
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       exhibits the greatest diversity of forms within the modern English pronoun system, having definite nominative, oblique, and genitive forms (
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        whom
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        whose
       </i>
       ) and equivalently coordinating indefinite forms (
       <i>
        whoever
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        whomever
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        whosever
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Though English
       <i>
        pronouns
       </i>
       can have subject and object forms (he/him, she/her),
       <i>
        nouns
       </i>
       show only a singular/plural and a possessive/non-possessive distinction (e.g.
       <i>
        chair
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chairs
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chair's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chairs'
       </i>
       ). Note that
       <i>
        chair
       </i>
       does not change form between "the chair is here" (subject) and "I saw the chair" (direct object), a distinction made by word order and context.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchy_of_cases">
        Hierarchy of cases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hierarchy of cases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Case_hierarchy" title="Case hierarchy">
        Case hierarchy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cases can be ranked in the following hierarchy, where a language that does not have a given case will tend not to have any cases to the right of the missing case:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         p.89
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
         nominative
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         accusative
        </a>
        <i>
         or
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">
         ergative
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
         genitive
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
         dative
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Locative_case" title="Locative case">
         locative
        </a>
        <i>
         or
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prepositional_case" title="Prepositional case">
         prepositional
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Ablative_case" title="Ablative case">
         ablative
        </a>
        <i>
         and/or
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">
         instrumental
        </a>
        →
        <i>
         others
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       This is, however, only a general tendency. Many forms of
       <a href="/wiki/Central_German" title="Central German">
        Central German
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colognian_language" title="Colognian language">
        Colognian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish" title="Luxembourgish">
        Luxembourgish
       </a>
       , have a dative case but lack a genitive. In
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       nouns, the nominative and accusative have fallen together, whereas the dative–locative has remained separate in some paradigms; Irish also has genitive and vocative cases. In
       <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">
        Punjabi
       </a>
       , the accusative, genitive, and dative have merged to an oblique case, but the language still retains vocative, locative, and ablative cases. Old English had an instrumental case, but not a locative or prepositional.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Case_order">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The "traditional" case order (nom-gen-dat-acc) was expressed for the first time in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Grammar" title="The Art of Grammar">

            Πτώσεις ὀνομάτων εἰσὶ πέντε· ὀρθή, γενική, δοτική, αἰτιατική, κλητική.

         </div>
        </td>
        <td style="padding-left: 2em;">

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Case_concord_systems">
        Case concord systems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Case concord systems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the most common
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       case concord system, only the head-word (the noun) in a phrase is marked for case. This system appears in many
       <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">
        Papuan languages
       </a>
       as well as in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_language" title="Mongolian language">
        Mongolian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_language" title="Quechua language">
        Quechua
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">
        Indo-Aryan
       </a>
       , and other languages. In
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       and various
       <a href="/wiki/Amazonian_languages" title="Amazonian languages">
        Amazonian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        Australian languages
       </a>
       , only the phrase-final word (not necessarily the noun) is marked for case. In many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language" title="Indo-European language">
        Indo-European
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balto-Finnic_languages" title="Balto-Finnic languages">
        Balto-Finnic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       , case is marked on the noun, the determiner, and usually the adjective. Other systems are less common. In some languages, there is
       <a href="/wiki/Suffixaufnahme" title="Suffixaufnahme">
        double-marking
       </a>
       of a word as both genitive (to indicate semantic role) and another case such as accusative (to establish concord with the head noun).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Declension_paradigms">
        Declension paradigms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Declension paradigms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        Declension
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Declension is the process or result of altering nouns to the correct grammatical cases. Languages with rich nominal inflection (use grammatical cases for many purposes) typically have a number of identifiable declension classes, or groups of nouns with a similar pattern of case inflection or declension. Sanskrit has six declension classes, whereas Latin is traditionally considered to have
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        five
       </a>
       , and Ancient Greek
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_declension" title="Greek declension">
        three declension classes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beetham_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beetham-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, Slovak has
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_declension" title="Slovak declension">
        fifteen noun declension classes
       </a>
       , five for each gender (the number may vary depending on which paradigms are counted or omitted, this mainly concerns those that modify declension of foreign words; refer to article).
      </p>
      <p>
       In Indo-European languages, declension patterns may depend on a variety of factors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       , phonological environment, and irregular historical factors. Pronouns sometimes have separate paradigms. In some languages, particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , a case may contain different groups of endings depending on whether the word is a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       or an
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       . A single case may contain many different endings, some of which may even be derived from different roots. For example, in Polish, the genitive case has
       <i>
        -a, -u, -ów, -i/-y, -e-
       </i>
       for nouns, and
       <i>
        -ego, -ej, -ich/-ych
       </i>
       for adjectives. To a lesser extent, a noun's
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender#Other_types_of_gender_classifications" title="Grammatical gender">
        animacy or humanness
       </a>
       may add another layer of complexity. For example, in Russian:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Кот
        </span>
        (NOM, animate, zero ending)
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         ло́вит мыше́й.
        </span>
        ((The) cat catches mice)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Столб
        </span>
        (NOM, inanimate, zero ending)
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         де́ржит кры́шу.
        </span>
        ((The) pillar holds a/the roof)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       vs.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Пётр гла́дит кота́
        </span>
        (ACC, animate, -a ending). (Peter strokes a/the cat) or (Peter is stroking a/the cat)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       and
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Пётр лома́ет столб
        </span>
        (ACC, inanimate, zero ending). (Peter breaks a/the pillar) or (Peter is breaking a/the pillar)
       </li>
      </ul>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       In German, grammatical case is largely preserved in the articles and adjectives, but nouns have lost many of their original endings. Below is an example of case inflection in German using the masculine

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">
        Greek
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Greek">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Modern Greek has four cases: nominative, genitive, accusative, and vocative. For neuters and most groups of feminines and plural masculines, the genitive case differs from the other three. Below is an example of the declension of
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BF%CF%85%CF%81%CE%B1%CE%BD%CF%8C%CF%82#Greek" title="wikt:ουρανός">
         ουρανός
        </a>
       </span>
       (sky), which has a different form in the singular of all four cases, together with the appropriate article in both the singular and the plural:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Nominative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         ο ουρανός / οι ουρανοί
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Genitive –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         του ουρανού / των ουρανών
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Accusative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         τον ουρανό / τους ουρανούς
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Vocative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         ουρανέ / ουρανοί
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Ancient Greek had one additional case, the dative. At some point, it was replaced with the preposition
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        εις
       </span>
       , followed by the accusative. This became necessary when pronunciation simplified, merging the two long vowels eta and omega to short. The result was that dative did not sound much different from the accusative in the singular of the first two groups. However, the dative case is still used in many expressions.
      </p>
      <p>
       With time, only the sigma of
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        εις
       </span>
       was left and got attached to the article, except when an article is not used and it becomes
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        σε
       </span>
       instead. Note that this is not a different case from the accusative.
      </p>
      <p>
       Below is an example with the dative case of the word
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        πόλη
       </span>
       (city):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Nominative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         η πόλη / οι πόλεις
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Genitive –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         της πόλης / των πόλεων
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Dative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         τη πόλη / ταις πόλεσι
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Accusative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         την πόλη / τις πόλεις
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Vocative –
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         πόλη / πόλεις
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
        Japanese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Japanese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Cases in Japanese are marked by
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_particles" title="Japanese particles">
        particles
       </a>
       placed after the nouns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tarou_takahashi_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tarou_takahashi-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A distinctive feature of Japanese is the presence of two cases, which are roughly equivalent to the nominative case in other languages: one representing the
       <a href="/wiki/Topic_and_comment" title="Topic and comment">
        sentence topic
       </a>
       , the other representing the subject. The most important case markers are the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Nominative -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         が
        </span>
        (ga) for subject,
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         は
        </span>
        (wa) for the
        <a href="/wiki/Topic_marker#Japanese:_は" title="Topic marker">
         topic
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Genitive -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         の
        </span>
        (no)
       </li>
       <li>
        Dative -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         に
        </span>
        (ni)
       </li>
       <li>
        Accusative -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         を
        </span>
        (wo)
       </li>
       <li>
        Lative -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         へ
        </span>
        (e), used for destination direction (like in "to some place")
       </li>
       <li>
        Ablative -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         から
        </span>
        (kara), used for source direction (like in "from some place")
       </li>
       <li>
        Instrumental -
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         で
        </span>
        (de)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Korean">
        Korean
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Korean">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Cases in Korean are marked by
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_postpositions" title="Korean postpositions">
        particles
       </a>
       placed after the nouns, similar to Japanese. Like Japanese, the nominative case has two distinctions, one representing the topic of a sentence and the other the subject. In informal speech, nominative (이/가 and 은/는) and accusative (을/를) particles are often omitted, while dative (에게) and ablative (에서) are shortened to simply 에, if the meaning of the sentence can easily be inferred from context. Most common case markers are the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Nominative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         이/가
        </span>
        (i/ga) for the subject,
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         은/는
        </span>
        (eun/neun) for the topic
       </li>
       <li>
        Genitive -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         의
        </span>
        (ui; although transliterated as ui, nowadays it is pronounced the same as 에)
       </li>
       <li>
        Dative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         에게
        </span>
        (ege), 한테 (hante)
       </li>
       <li>
        Accusative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         을/를
        </span>
        (eul/reul)
       </li>
       <li>
        Lative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         에
        </span>
        (e), used for destination direction (like in "to some place")
       </li>
       <li>
        Ablative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         에서
        </span>
        (eseo), used for source direction (like in "from some place")
       </li>
       <li>
        Instrumental -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         로/으로
        </span>
        (ro/uro)
       </li>
       <li>
        Vocative -
        <span lang="ko" xml:lang="ko">
         아/야
        </span>
        (a/ya)
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romanian">
        Romanian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Romanian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Romanian is the only modern major Romance language with a case system for all nouns, whereas all other Romance languages dropped the cases for nouns replacing them by prepositions. An example of Romanian case inflection is given below, using the singular form of the word "boy":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiatul
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
         nominative
        </a>
        ) "[the] boy" [as a subject] (e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiatul a stat acasă
         </span>
        </i>
        – the boy stayed home)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiat
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         accusative
        </a>
        ) "boy" [as a direct object] (e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          L-am văzut aseară pe băiat
         </span>
        </i>
        – I saw the boy last night)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiatului
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
         genitive
        </a>
        ) "[the] boy's / [of the] boy" (e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Bicicleta băiatului s-a stricat
         </span>
        </i>
        – the boy's bike broke down)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiatului
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
         dative
        </a>
        ) "[to the] boy" (e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          I-am dat un cadou băiatului
         </span>
        </i>
        – I gave the boy a gift)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Băiete!
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">
         vocative
        </a>
        ) "Boy!" (e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          Stai acasă, băiete!
         </span>
        </i>
        – stay at home boy!)
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">
        Russian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Russian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_declension" title="Russian declension">
        Russian declension
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An example of a Russian case inflection is given below (with explicit stress marks), using the singular forms of the Russian term for "sailor," which belongs to Russian's first declension class.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%D0%BC%D0%BE%D1%80%D1%8F%D0%BA#Russian" title="wikt:моряк">
          моря́к
         </a>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
         nominative
        </a>
        ) "[the] sailor" [as a subject] (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Там стоит моряк
        </span>
        : The sailor is standing there)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         моряк
         <b>
          á
         </b>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
         genitive
        </a>
        ) "[the] sailor's / [of the] sailor" (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Сын моряка — художник
        </span>
        : The sailor's son is an artist)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         моряк
         <b>
          у́
         </b>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
         dative
        </a>
        ) "[to/for the] sailor" [as an indirect object] (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Моряку подарили подарок
        </span>
        : (They/Someone) gave a present to the sailor)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         моряк
         <b>
          á
         </b>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         accusative
        </a>
        ) "[the] sailor" [as a direct object] (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Вижу моряка
        </span>
        : (I) see the sailor)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         моряк
         <b>
          о́м
         </b>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">
         instrumental
        </a>
        ) "[with/by the] sailor" [as a direct object] (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Дружу с моряком
        </span>
        : (I) have a friendship with the sailor)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         о/на/в моряк
         <b>
          е́
         </b>
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Prepositional_case" title="Prepositional case">
         prepositional
        </a>
        ) "[about/on/in the] sailor" [as a direct object] (e.g.
        <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
         Думаю о моряке
        </span>
        : (I) think about the sailor)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Up to ten additional cases are identified by linguists, although today all of them are either incomplete (do not apply to all nouns or do not form full word paradigm with all combinations of gender and number) or degenerate (appear identical to one of the main six cases). The most recognized additional cases are locative (
       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        в лесу́, на мосту́, в слеза́х
       </span>
       ), partitive (
       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        ча́ю, са́хару, песку́
       </span>
       ), and two forms of vocative — old (
       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        Го́споди, Бо́же, о́тче
       </span>
       ) and neo-vocative (
       <span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">
        Маш, пап, ребя́т
       </span>
       ). Sometimes, so called count-form (for some countable nouns after numerals) is considered to be a sub-case.
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_grammar#Nouns" title="Russian grammar">
        See details
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tamil">
        Tamil
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Tamil">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       case system is analyzed in native and missionary grammars as consisting of a finite number of cases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR600654_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JSTOR600654-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The usual treatment of Tamil case (Arden 1942)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is one in which there are seven cases: nominative (first case), accusative (second case), instrumental (third), dative (fourth), ablative (fifth), genitive (sixth), and locative (seventh). In traditional analyses, there is always a clear distinction made between post-positional morphemes and case endings. The vocative is sometimes given a place in the case system as an eighth case, but vocative forms do not participate in usual morphophonemic alternations and do not govern the use of any postpositions. Modern grammarians, however, argue that this eight-case classification is coarse and artificial
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JSTOR600654_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-JSTOR600654-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that Tamil usage is best understood if each suffix or combination of suffixes is seen as marking a separate case.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evolution">
        Evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As languages evolve, case systems change. In early
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       , for example, the genitive and ablative cases became combined, giving five cases, rather than the six retained in Latin. In modern
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       , the Sanskrit cases have been reduced to two: a direct case (for subjects and direct objects) and an
       <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">
        oblique case
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In English, apart from the pronouns discussed above, case has vanished altogether except for the possessive/non-possessive dichotomy in nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       The evolution of the treatment of case relationships can be circular.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         pp.167–174
        </span>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adposition" title="Adposition">
        Adpositions
       </a>
       can become unstressed and sound like they are an unstressed syllable of a neighboring word. A postposition can thus merge into the stem of a head noun, developing various forms depending on the phonological shape of the stem. Affixes can then be subject to various phonological processes such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       , vowel centering to the
       <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
        schwa
       </a>
       , phoneme loss, and
       <a href="/wiki/Fusion_(phonetics)" title="Fusion (phonetics)">
        fusion
       </a>
       , and these processes can reduce or even eliminate the distinctions between cases. Languages can then compensate for the resulting loss of function by creating adpositions, thus coming full circle.
      </p>
      <p>
       Recent experiments in agent-based modeling have shown how case systems can emerge and evolve in a population of language users.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The experiments demonstrate that language users may introduce new case markers to reduce the cognitive effort required for semantic interpretation, hence facilitating communication through language. Case markers then become generalized through analogical reasoning and reuse.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_typology">
        Linguistic typology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_case&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Linguistic typology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic_alignment" title="Morphosyntactic alignment">
        Morphosyntactic alignment
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Languages are categorized into several case systems, based on their
       <i>
        morphosyntactic alignment
       </i>
       —how they group verb
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agent_(linguistics)" title="Agent (linguistics)">
        agents
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patient_(linguistics)" title="Patient (linguistics)">
        patients
       </a>
       into cases:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Nominative%E2%80%93accusative_language" title="Nominative–accusative language">
          Nominative–accusative
         </a>
        </i>
        (or simply
        <i>
         accusative
        </i>
        ): The argument (subject) of an intransitive verb is in the same case as the agent (subject) of a transitive verb; this case is then called the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
          nominative case
         </a>
        </i>
        , with the patient (direct object) of a transitive verb being in the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
          accusative case
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ergative%E2%80%93absolutive_language" title="Ergative–absolutive language">
          Ergative–absolutive
         </a>
        </i>
        (or simply
        <i>
         ergative
        </i>
        ): The argument (subject) of an intransitive verb is in the same case as the patient (direct object) of a transitive verb; this case is then called the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Absolutive_case" title="Absolutive case">
          absolutive case
         </a>
        </i>
        , with the agent (subject) of a transitive verb being in the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ergative_case" title="Ergative case">
          ergative case
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergative%E2%80%93accusative_language" title="Ergative–accusative language">
          Ergative–accusative
         </a>
        </i>
        (or
        <i>
         tripartite
        </i>
        ): The argument (subject) of an intransitive verb is in its own case (the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_case" title="Intransitive case">
          intransitive case
         </a>
        </i>
        ), separate from that of the agent (subject) or patient (direct object) of a transitive verb (which is in the ergative case or accusative case, respectively).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Active%E2%80%93stative_language" title="Active–stative language">
          Active–stative
         </a>
        </i>
        (or simply
        <i>
         active
        </i>
        ): The argument (subject) of an intransitive verb can be in one of two cases; if the argument is an
        <i>
         agent
        </i>
        , as in "He ate," then it is in the same case as the agent (subject) of a transitive verb (sometimes called the
        <i>
         agentive case
        </i>
        ), and if it is a
        <i>
         patient
        </i>
        , as in "He tripped," then it is in the same case as the patient (direct object) of a transitive verb (sometimes called the
        <i>
         patientive case
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigger_language" title="Trigger language">
          Trigger
         </a>
        </i>
        : One noun in a sentence is the topic or focus. This noun is in the
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Trigger_case&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Trigger case (page does not exist)">
         trigger case
        </a>
        , and information elsewhere in the sentence (for example a
        <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
         verb
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
         affix
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
         Tagalog
        </a>
        ) specifies the role of the trigger. The trigger may be identified as the agent, patient, etc. Other nouns may be inflected for case, but the inflections are overloaded; for example, in Tagalog, the subject and object of a verb are both expressed in the
        <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
         genitive case
        </a>
        when they are not in the trigger case.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The following are systems that some languages use to mark case instead of, or in addition to, declension:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Positional
        </i>
        : Nouns are not inflected for case; the position of a noun in the sentence expresses its case.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adposition" title="Adposition">
         Adpositional
        </a>
        : Nouns are accompanied by words that mark case.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With a few exceptions, most languages in the
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic
       </a>
       family make extensive use of cases.
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       has 15 cases according to the traditional understanding (or up to 30 depending on the interpretation).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, only 12 are commonly used in speech (see
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_noun_cases" title="Finnish noun cases">
        Finnish noun cases
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian
       </a>
       has 14 and
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       has 18, both with additional archaic cases used for some words.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages have very many cases. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Tsez_language" title="Tsez language">
        Tsez
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">
        Northeast Caucasian language
       </a>
       , has 64 cases.
      </p>
      <p>
       The original version of John Quijada's
       <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">
        constructed language
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ithkuil" title="Ithkuil">
        Ithkuil
       </a>
       has 81 noun cases,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and its descendent
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilaksh" title="Ilaksh">
        Ilaksh
       </a>
       and Ithkuil after the 2011 revision both have 96 noun cases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">
        lemma
       </a>
       form of words, which is the form chosen by convention as the canonical form of a word, is usually the most
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        unmarked
       </a>
       or basic case, which is typically the nominative, trigger, or absolutive case, whichever a language may have.
      </p>
