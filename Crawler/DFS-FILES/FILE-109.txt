   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Quaternary glaciation
   </h1>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about the series of glacial periods during the last 2.58 million years. For the glacial period lasting from 110,000 to 12,000 years ago, see
       <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">
        last glacial period
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Northern_icesheet_hg.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1640" data-file-width="2236" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Northern_icesheet_hg.png/250px-Northern_icesheet_hg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Northern_icesheet_hg.png/375px-Northern_icesheet_hg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Northern_icesheet_hg.png/500px-Northern_icesheet_hg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Northern_icesheet_hg.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
          Northern Hemisphere
         </a>
         glaciation during the
         <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">
          Last Glacial Maximum
         </a>
         . The creation of 3 to 4 km (1.9 to 2.5 mi) thick ice sheets equate to a global sea level drop of about 120 m (390 ft).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Quaternary glaciation
       </b>
       , also known as the
       <b>
        Pleistocene glaciation
       </b>
       or the
       <b>
        current ice age
       </b>
       , is a series of glacial events separated by
       <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">
        interglacial
       </a>
       events during the
       <a href="/wiki/Quaternary" title="Quaternary">
        Quaternary
       </a>
       period from 2.58
       <a href="/wiki/Year#SI_prefix_multipliers" title="Year">
        Ma
       </a>
       (million years ago) to present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During this period,
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheets
       </a>
       expanded, notably from out of
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       , and fluctuating ice sheets occurred elsewhere (for example, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laurentide_ice_sheet" title="Laurentide ice sheet">
        Laurentide ice sheet
       </a>
       ). The major effects of the ice age were the
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
        erosion
       </a>
       of land and the
       <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">
        deposition
       </a>
       of material, both over large parts of the continents; the modification of
       <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(geomorphology)" title="Drainage system (geomorphology)">
        river systems
       </a>
       ; the creation of millions of
       <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">
        lakes
       </a>
       , changes in
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       , the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluvial_lake" title="Pluvial lake">
        pluvial lakes
       </a>
       far from the ice margins, the
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostatic adjustment
       </a>
       of the earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       , flooding, and abnormal winds. The ice sheets themselves, by raising the
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       (extent to which the energy of the sun is reflected back off the earth) created significant
       <a href="/wiki/Feedback" title="Feedback">
        feedback
       </a>
       to further cool the
       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
        climate
       </a>
       . These effects re-shaped entire environments on land and in the oceans, and their associated biological communities.
      </p>

      <p>
       The Quaternary glaciation was the first ice age to be demonstrated to have occurred in the field of geology, and thus proving ice ages were possible. This was done over the 1700s and 1800s CE as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" title="Scientific revolution">
        scientific revolution
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Over the last century, extensive field observations have provided evidence that continental glaciers covered large parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">
        Siberia
       </a>
       . Maps of glacial features were compiled after many years of fieldwork by hundreds of geologists who mapped the location and orientation of
       <a href="/wiki/Drumlin" title="Drumlin">
        drumlins
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Esker" title="Esker">
        eskers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moraine" title="Moraine">
        moraines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Striation_(geology)" title="Striation (geology)">
        striations
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_stream" title="Glacial stream">
        glacial stream
       </a>
       channels in order to reveal the extent of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheets
       </a>
       , the direction of their flow, and the locations of systems of meltwater channels. They also allowed scientists to decipher a history of multiple advances and retreats of the ice. Even before the theory of worldwide glaciation was generally accepted, many observers recognized that more than a single advance and retreat of the ice had occurred.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since then, other, earlier ice ages have been identified, and the Quaternary event(s) constrained.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Quaternary glaciation is the last of seven known glaciations, or ice ages, during Earth's entire history.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_Petit_data.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/300px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/450px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Vostok_Petit_data.svg/600px-Vostok_Petit_data.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vostok_Petit_data.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Graph of reconstructed temperature (blue), CO
         <sub>
          2
         </sub>
         (green), and dust (red) from the
         <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">
          Vostok Station
         </a>
         ice core for the past 420,000 years
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       On the other hand, within the Quaternary Period, or ice age, were also periodic fluctuations, of the total volume of land ice, the sea level, and the global temperature. The colder episodes are referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">
        glacial periods
       </a>
       , or simply glacials, wherein large ice sheets at least 4 km thick at their maximum existed in
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">
        Siberia
       </a>
       . The shorter and warmer intervals between glacials are referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">
        interglacials
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Initially the fluctuations were on a time scale of 41,000 years, but more recently they have been on a scale of 100,000 years, as evidenced most clearly by
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">
        ice cores
       </a>
       for the past 800,000 years and marine sediment cores for the earlier period. Over the past 740,000 years there have been eight glacial cycles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The entire Quaternary Period, starting 2.58 Ma, is referred to as an
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        ice age
       </a>
       because at least one permanent large ice sheet—the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_ice_sheet" title="Antarctic ice sheet">
        Antarctic ice sheet
       </a>
       —has existed continuously. There is uncertainty over how much of
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       was covered by ice during the previous and earlier interglacials.
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently, Earth is in an interglacial period, which marked the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">
        Holocene
       </a>
       epoch. The current interglacial began between 10,000 and 15,000 years ago, which caused the ice sheets from the
       <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">
        last glacial period
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Holocene_glacial_retreat" title="Holocene glacial retreat">
        begin to disappear
       </a>
       . Remnants of these last glaciers, now occupying about 10% of the world's land surface, still exist in Greenland, Antarctica and mountainous regions.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the glacial periods, the present (i.e. interglacial) hydrologic system was completely interrupted throughout large areas of the world and was considerably modified in others. Due to the volume of ice on land, sea level was approximately 120 meters lower than present.
      </p>
      <p>
       Extensive evidence now shows that a number of periods of growth and retreat of continental glaciers occurred during the ice age, called glacials and interglacials. The interglacial periods of warm climate are represented by buried soil profiles, peat beds, and lake and stream deposits separating the unsorted, unstratified deposits of glacial debris.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
        Causes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        Ice age
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       No completely satisfactory theory has been proposed to account for Earth's history of glaciation. The cause of glaciation may be related to several simultaneous factors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">
        astronomical cycles
       </a>
       , atmospheric composition,
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">
        ocean currents
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_cycles">
        Astronomical cycles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Astronomical cycles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">
        Milankovitch cycles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_forcing" title="Orbital forcing">
        orbital forcing
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/100,000-year_problem" title="100,000-year problem">
        100,000-year problem
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The role of Earth's orbital changes in controlling climate was first advanced by
       <a href="/wiki/James_Croll" title="James Croll">
        James Croll
       </a>
       in the late 19th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later,
       <a href="/wiki/Milutin_Milankovi%C4%87" title="Milutin Milanković">
        Milutin Milanković
       </a>
       , a Serbian
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
        geophysicist
       </a>
       , elaborated on the theory and calculated these irregularities in Earth's orbit could cause the climatic cycles known as
       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">
        Milankovitch cycles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are the result of the additive behavior of several types of cyclical changes in Earth's orbital properties.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Milankovitch_Variations.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="363" data-file-width="479" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png/300px-Milankovitch_Variations.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png/450px-Milankovitch_Variations.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7e/Milankovitch_Variations.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Milankovitch_Variations.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Relationship of Earth's orbit to periods of glaciation
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Changes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       of Earth occur on a cycle of about 100,000 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilwhy_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ilwhy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       , or tilt, of Earth's axis varies periodically between 22° and 24.5°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilwhy_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ilwhy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (The tilt of Earth's axis is responsible for the
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        seasons
       </a>
       ; the greater the tilt, the greater the contrast between summer and winter temperatures.) Changes in the tilt occur in a cycle 41,000 years long.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ilwhy_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ilwhy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_(astronomy)" title="Precession (astronomy)">
        Precession of the equinoxes
       </a>
       , or wobbles on Earth's spin axis, complete every 26,000 years. According to the Milankovitch theory, these factors cause a periodic cooling of Earth, with the coldest part in the cycle occurring about every 40,000 years. The main effect of the Milankovitch cycles is to change the contrast between the seasons, not the amount of solar heat Earth receives. These cycles within cycles predict that during maximum glacial advances, winter and summer temperatures are lower. The result is less ice melting than accumulating, and
       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
        glaciers
       </a>
       build up.
      </p>
      <p>
       Milankovitch worked out the ideas of climatic cycles in the 1920s and 1930s, but it was not until the 1970s that sufficiently long and detailed chronology of the Quaternary temperature changes were worked out to test the theory adequately.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Studies of deep-sea cores, and the fossils contained in them indicate that the fluctuation of climate during the last few hundred thousand years is remarkably close to that predicted by Milankovitch.
      </p>
      <p>
       A problem with the theory is that the astronomical cycles have been in existence for billions of years, but glaciation is a rare occurrence. Astronomical cycles correlate perfectly with glacial and interglacial periods, and their transitions,
       <i>
        inside
       </i>
       an ice age. Other factors such as the position of continents and the effects this has on Earth's oceanic currents, or long-term fluctuations inside the core of the Sun that caused Earth's temperature to drop below a critical threshold and thus initiate the ice age in the first place must also be involved. Once that occurs, Milankovitch cycles could act to force the planet in and out of
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">
        glacial periods
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_composition">
        Atmospheric composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atmospheric composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One theory holds that decreases in atmospheric
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
         CO
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
          <br/>
          2
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       , an important
       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
        greenhouse gas
       </a>
       , started the long-term cooling trend that eventually led to glaciation. Recent studies of the
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       content of gas bubbles preserved in the Greenland
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">
        ice cores
       </a>
       lend support to this idea. The
       <a href="/wiki/Geochemical_cycle" title="Geochemical cycle">
        geochemical cycle
       </a>
       of carbon indicates more than a 90% decrease in atmospheric
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       since the middle of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mesozoic" title="Mesozoic">
        Mesozoic Era
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An analysis of
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       reconstructions from
       <a href="/wiki/Alkenone" title="Alkenone">
        alkenone
       </a>
       records shows that
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       in the atmosphere declined before and during Antarctic glaciation and supports a substantial
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       decrease as the primary cause of Antarctic glaciation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       levels also play an important role in the transitions between interglacials and glacials. High
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>

        </span>
       </span>
       contents correspond to warm interglacial periods, and low

        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">

       </span>
       to glacial periods. However, studies indicate that
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">

        </span>
       </span>
       may not be the primary cause of the interglacial-glacial transitions, but instead acts as a

       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joos_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Joos-10">

        </a>

       The explanation for this observed
       <span class="chemf nowrap">

        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">

       </span>
       variation "remains a difficult attribution problem".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Joos_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Joos-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_tectonics_and_ocean_currents">
        Plate tectonics and ocean currents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Plate tectonics and ocean currents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        Plate tectonics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">
        ocean current
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An important component in the long-term temperature drop may be related to the positions of the continents, relative to the poles (but it cannot explain the rapid retreat and advances of glaciers).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This relation can control the circulation of the oceans and the atmosphere, affecting how
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_currents" title="Ocean currents">
        ocean currents
       </a>
       carry heat to high latitude. Throughout most of
       <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">
        geologic time
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       appears to have been in a broad, open ocean that allowed major ocean currents to move unabated. Equatorial waters flowed into the polar regions, warming them with water from the more temperate latitudes. This unrestricted circulation produced mild, uniform climates that persisted throughout most of geologic time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Cenozoic" title="Cenozoic">
        Cenozoic Era
       </a>
       , the large
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North American
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South American
       </a>
       continental plates moved westward from the
       <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">
        Eurasian
       </a>
       plate. This drift interlocked with the development of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       , trending north-south, with the North Pole in the small, nearly landlocked basin of the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">
        Arctic Ocean
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Isthmus_of_Panama" title="Isthmus of Panama">
        Isthmus of Panama
       </a>
       developed at a convergent plate margin about 3 million years ago, and further separated oceanic circulation, closing the last strait, outside of the polar regions, that had connected the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific
       </a>
       and Atlantic oceans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects">
        Effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The presence of so much ice upon the continents had a profound effect upon almost every aspect of Earth's hydrologic system. The most obvious effects are the spectacular mountain scenery and other continental landscapes fashioned both by glacial erosion and deposition instead of running water. Entirely new landscapes covering millions of square kilometers were formed in a relatively short period of geologic time. In addition, the vast bodies of glacial ice affected Earth well beyond the glacier margins. Directly or indirectly, the effects of glaciation were felt in every part of the world.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lakes">
        Lakes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lakes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_lake" title="Glacial lake">
        Glacial lake
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Quaternary glaciation created more lakes than all other geologic processes combined. The reason is that a continental
       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
        glacier
       </a>
       completely disrupts the preglacial
       <a href="/wiki/Drainage_system_(geomorphology)" title="Drainage system (geomorphology)">
        drainage system
       </a>
       . The surface over which the glacier moved was scoured and
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
        eroded
       </a>
       by the ice, leaving a myriad of closed, undrained depressions in the bedrock. These depressions filled with water and became
       <a href="/wiki/Lake" title="Lake">
        lakes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Very large lakes were created along the glacial margins. The ice on both
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       was about 3,000 m (9,800 ft) thick near the centers of maximum accumulation, but it tapered toward the glacier margins. Ice weight caused crustal subsidence which was greatest beneath the thickest accumulation of ice. As the ice melted, rebound of the crust lagged behind, producing a regional slope toward the ice. This slope formed basins that have lasted for thousands of years. These basins became lakes or were invaded by the ocean. The
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Lakes" title="Great Lakes">
        Great Lakes
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Baltic_Sea" title="Baltic Sea">
        Baltic Sea
       </a>
       of northern Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       were formed primarily in this way.
      </p>
      <p>
       The numerous lakes of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Shield" title="Canadian Shield">
        Canadian Shield
       </a>
       , Sweden and
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Lakeland" title="Finnish Lakeland">
        Finland
       </a>
       are thought to have originated at least partly from glacier's selective erosion of
       <a href="/wiki/Weathered" title="Weathered">
        weathered
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Bedrock" title="Bedrock">
        bedrock
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karnaetal1999_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karnaetal1999-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UppslagsFiBERG_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UppslagsFiBERG-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pluvial_lakes">
        Pluvial lakes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pluvial lakes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Pluvial_lake" title="Pluvial lake">
        Pluvial lake
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The climatic conditions that cause glaciation had an indirect effect on arid and semiarid regions far removed from the large
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheets
       </a>
       . The increased precipitation that fed the
       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
        glaciers
       </a>
       also increased the runoff of major rivers and intermittent streams, resulting in the growth and development of large pluvial lakes. Most pluvial lakes developed in relatively arid regions where there typically was insufficient rain to establish a drainage system to the sea. Instead, stream runoff in those areas flowed into closed basins and formed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Playa_lake" title="Playa lake">
        playa lakes
       </a>
       . With increased rainfall, the playa lakes enlarged and overflowed. Pluvial lakes were most extensive during glacial periods. During interglacial stages, when less precipitation fell, the pluvial lakes shrank to form small salt flats.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Isostatic_adjustment">
        Isostatic adjustment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Isostatic adjustment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">
        Post-glacial rebound
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Major isostatic adjustments of the
       <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
        lithosphere
       </a>
       during the Quaternary glaciation were caused by the weight of the ice, which depressed the continents. In
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       , a large area around
       <a href="/wiki/Hudson_Bay" title="Hudson Bay">
        Hudson Bay
       </a>
       was depressed below sea level, as was the area in Europe around the Baltic Sea. The land has been rebounding from these depressions since the ice melted. Some of these isostatic movements triggered large
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        earthquakes
       </a>
       in Scandinavia about 9,000 years ago. These earthquakes are unique in that they are not associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Studies have shown that the uplift has taken place in two distinct stages. The initial uplift following
       <a href="/wiki/Deglaciation" title="Deglaciation">
        deglaciation
       </a>
       was rapid (called "elastic"), and took place as the ice was being unloaded. After this "elastic" phase, uplift proceed by "slow viscous flow" so the rate decreased
       <a href="/wiki/Exponential_decay" title="Exponential decay">
        exponentially
       </a>
       after that. Today, typical uplift rates are of the order of 1 cm per year or less. In northern Europe, this is clearly shown by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       data obtained by the BIFROST GPS network.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Studies suggest that rebound will continue for about at least another 10,000 years. The total uplift from the end of deglaciation depends on the local ice load and could be several hundred meters near the center of rebound.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Winds">
        Winds
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Winds">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The presence of ice over so much of the continents greatly modified patterns of atmospheric circulation. Winds near the glacial margins were strong and persistent because of the abundance of dense, cold air coming off the glacier fields. These winds picked up and transported large quantities of loose, fine-grained sediment brought down by the glaciers. This dust accumulated as
       <a href="/wiki/Loess" title="Loess">
        loess
       </a>
       (wind-blown silt), forming irregular blankets over much of the
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri_River" title="Missouri River">
        Missouri River
       </a>
       valley, central Europe, and northern
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sand
       <a href="/wiki/Dune" title="Dune">
        dunes
       </a>
       were much more widespread and active in many areas during the early Quaternary period. A good example is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sand_Hills_(Nebraska)" title="Sand Hills (Nebraska)">
        Sand Hills
       </a>
       region in
       <a href="/wiki/Nebraska" title="Nebraska">
        Nebraska
       </a>
       , USA, which covers an area of about 60,000 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (23,166 sq mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This region was a large, active dune field during the
       <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
        Pleistocene
       </a>
       epoch, but today is largely stabilized by grass cover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ocean_currents">
        Ocean currents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Ocean currents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Thick glaciers were heavy enough to reach the sea bottom in several important areas, thus blocking the passage of ocean water and thereby affecting ocean currents. In addition to direct effects, this caused feedback effects as ocean currents contribute to global heat transfer.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Records_of_prior_glaciation">
        Records of prior glaciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Records of prior glaciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_glaciation" title="Timeline of glaciation">
        Timeline of glaciation
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        Ice age
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">
        paleoclimatology
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="520" data-file-width="726" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/250px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/375px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png/500px-Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Climate_Change.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         500 million years of
         <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
          climate change
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Glaciation has been a rare event in Earth's history,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but there is evidence of widespread glaciation during the late
       <a href="/wiki/Paleozoic" title="Paleozoic">
        Paleozoic
       </a>
       Era (200 to 300 Ma) and during late Precambrian (i.e. in the
       <a href="/wiki/Neoproterozoic" title="Neoproterozoic">
        Neoproterozoic
       </a>
       Era, 600 to 800 Ma).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Before the current
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        ice age
       </a>
       , which began 2 to 3 Ma, Earth's climate was typically mild and uniform for long periods of time. This climatic history is implied by the types of
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
        fossil
       </a>
       plants and animals and by the characteristics of sediments preserved in the
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum" title="Stratum">
        stratigraphic
       </a>
       record.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are, however, widespread glacial deposits, recording several major periods of ancient glaciation in various parts of the geologic record. Such evidence suggests major periods of glaciation prior to the current Quaternary glaciation.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the best documented records of pre-Quaternary glaciation, called the
       <a href="/wiki/Karoo_Ice_Age" title="Karoo Ice Age">
        Karoo Ice Age
       </a>
       , is found in the late Paleozoic rocks in
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       , Antarctica, and
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       . Exposures of ancient glacial deposits are numerous in these areas. Deposits of even older glacial sediment exist on every continent except South America. These indicate that two other periods of widespread glaciation occurred during the late Precambrian, producing the
       <a href="/wiki/Snowball_Earth" title="Snowball Earth">
        Snowball Earth
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Cryogenian" title="Cryogenian">
        Cryogenian
       </a>
       Period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Next_glacial_period">
        Next glacial period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Quaternary_glaciation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Next glacial period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="708" data-file-width="708" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/250px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/375px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg/500px-Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mauna_Loa_CO2_monthly_mean_concentration.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Atmospheric CO
         <sub>
          2
         </sub>
         concentrations measured at
         <a href="/wiki/Mauna_Loa_Observatory" title="Mauna Loa Observatory">
          Mauna Loa Observatory
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In popular culture, there is often reference to "the next ice age".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Technically, because Earth is already in an ice age at present, albeit an
       <a href="/wiki/Interglacial" title="Interglacial">
        interglacial
       </a>
       period, this usually refers to the next
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_period" title="Glacial period">
        glacial period
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1970s a few
       <a href="/wiki/Paleoclimatology" title="Paleoclimatology">
        paleoclimatologists
       </a>
       were concerned with the possibility of
       <a href="/wiki/Global_cooling" title="Global cooling">
        global cooling
       </a>
       , and suggested that the next glacial period could be rapidly approaching. The previous interglacial periods seemed to have lasted about 10,000 years each; a report in 1972 assuming that the present interglacial period would be equally long concluded, "it is likely that the present-day warm epoch will terminate relatively soon if man does not intervene".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since then, our understanding of the climate system has improved. It is known that not all interglacial periods are of the same length and that solar heating varies in a non-linear fashion forced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Milankovitch_cycles" title="Milankovitch cycles">
        Milankovitch orbital cycles
       </a>
       (see
       <i>
        Causes
       </i>
       section above). At the same time, it is also known that
       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_gas" title="Greenhouse gas">
        greenhouse gases
       </a>
       are increasing in concentration with each passing year. Based on the variations in solar heating and on the amount of
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       in the atmosphere, some calculations of future temperatures have been made. According to these estimates, the interglacial period Earth is in now may persist for another 50,000 years if
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       levels increase to 750 parts per million (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_per_million" title="Parts per million">
        ppm
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_Earth_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dynamic_Earth-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while the recent atmospheric concentration of
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       is about 407 ppm by volume.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If
       <span class="chemf nowrap">
        CO
        <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1em;font-size:80%;text-align:left">
         <br/>
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       drops instead to 210 ppm, then the next glacial period may only be 15,000 years away.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dynamic_Earth_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dynamic_Earth-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moreover, studies of seafloor sediments and
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_core" title="Ice core">
        ice cores
       </a>
       from glaciers around the world, namely
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       , indicate that climatic change is not smooth. Studies of isotopic composition of the ice cores indicate the change from warm to frigid temperatures can occur in a decade or two.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, the ice cores show that an ice age is not uniformly cold, nor are interglacial periods uniformly warm (see also
       <a href="/wiki/Stadial" title="Stadial">
        stadial
       </a>
       ). Analysis of ice cores of the entire thickness of the Greenland glacier shows that climate over the last 250,000 years has changed frequently and abruptly. The present interglacial period (the last 10,000 to 15,000 years) has been fairly stable and warm, but the previous one was interrupted by numerous frigid spells lasting hundreds of years. If the previous period was more typical than the present one, the period of stable climate in which humans flourished—inventing
       <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
        agriculture
       </a>
       and thus
       <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
        civilization
       </a>
       —may have been possible only because of a highly unusual period of stable temperature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
