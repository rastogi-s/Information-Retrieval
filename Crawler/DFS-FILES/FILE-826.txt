      <p>
       <b>
        Central American Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        español centroamericano
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        castellano centroamericano
       </i>
       ) is the general name of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       . More precisely, the term refers to the Spanish language as spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">
        El Salvador
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
        Guatemala
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       . For
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       see
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation">
        Variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While most vocabulary is common, each country has its variations, for instance, for "corner store" and "soft drink": In
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
        Guatemala
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        tienda
       </i>
       or "bodega" in some parts of the country and
       <i>
        agua,
       </i>
       respectively, except for the Jutiapa department of Guatemala where a soft drink is known as a
       <i>
        gaseosa
       </i>
       (water is
       <i>
        agua pura
       </i>
       ). In
       <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">
        El Salvador
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        tienda
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gaseosa
       </i>
       but more commonly called "soda" now. In
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        pulpería
       </i>
       (in the north called
       <i>
        trucha
       </i>
       informally) and
       <i>
        fresco
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        venta
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pulpería
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gaseosa
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       , they are
       <i>
        pulpería
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gaseosa
       </i>
       although they could also be
       <i>
        abastecedor
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        refresco
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        fresco
       </i>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       they are
       <i>
        tienda
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        soda
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics_and_phonology">
        Phonetics and phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonetics and phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some characteristics of Central American
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        /s/ at the end of a syllable or before a consonant is pronounced like [h], except in Guatemala and in Costa Rica.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         j
        </i>
        (/x/), is aspirated except in some areas of Costa Rica; it is soft as the /h/ in English (e.g.: Yahoo).
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Use of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Most phonological features of Central American Spanish are similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       , and most other
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        Latin American Spanish
       </a>
       dialects.
      </p>

       Note how the conjugation of
       <b>

       </b>
       presents fewer irregularities compared to
       <b>
        tú
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The main difference of the
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       in Argentina is the conjugation of the subjunctive.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rioplatense" title="Rioplatense">
        Rioplatense
       </a>
       Spanish prefers the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       forms of
       <b>
        tú
       </b>
       , whereas in Central America, the
       <b>
        vos
       </b>
       forms are retained.
      </p>
      <p>
       The pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is used when addressing older, unfamiliar or respected persons, as it is in most Spanish-speaking countries; however, in Costa Rica, Guatemala, and Honduras it is frequently used with younger people, and in Honduras between husband and wife, and friends. In Nicaragua, the pronoun is only used among youth during special or formal occasions or when addressing unfamiliar individuals in a formal manner. It's also used with most, if not all, profanities familiar to the region.

        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns_and_verb_conjugation">
        Pronouns and verb conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pronouns and verb conjugation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As previously mentioned, one of the features of the Central American speaking style is the
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </b>
       : the usage of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       for the second person singular, instead of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . In some Spanish-speaking regions where
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       is used, it is sometimes considered a non-standard lower-class sociolectic or regional variant, whereas in other regions
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       is standard.
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is used with forms of the verb that resemble those of the second person plural (
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       ) in Spanish from Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some people prefer to say "tú" instead of "vos" but conjugating the verbs using the vos forms; for instance: tú cantás, tú bailás, tú podés, etc. This is avoid in Southern Central America, especially in Costa Rica and Nicaragua where is associated with bad education by mixing 2 different pronouns (tú-vos).
      </p>
      <p>
       The second person plural pronoun, which is
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       in Spain, is replaced with
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       in C. American Spanish, like most other Latin American dialects. While
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is the formal second person singular pronoun, its plural
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       has a neutral connotation and can be used to address friends and acquaintances as well as in more formal occasions (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       ).
       <i>
        Ustedes
       </i>
       takes a grammatically third person plural verb. Usted is particularly used in Costa Rica between strangers, with foreign people and used by the vast majority of the population in Alajuela and rural areas of the country.
      </p>

       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       forms end in
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       , so there is no diphthong to simplify, and Central American
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       employs the same form: instead of
       <i>
        tú vives
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos vivís
       </i>
       ; instead of
       <i>
        tú vienes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos venís
       </i>
       (note the alternation).
      </p>
      <p>
       The imperative forms for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are identical to the plural imperative forms in Peninsular minus the final
       <i>
        -d
       </i>
       (stress remains the same):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hablá más alto, por favor.
        </i>
        "Speak louder, please." (
        <i>
         hablad
        </i>
        in Peninsular)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Comé un poco de torta.
        </i>
        "Eat some cake." (
        <i>
         comed
        </i>
        in Peninsular)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vení para acá.
        </i>
        "Come over here." (
        <i>
         venid
        </i>
        in Peninsular)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The plural imperative uses the
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       form (i. e. the third person plural subjunctive, as corresponding to
       <i>
        ellos
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       As for the subjunctive forms of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       verbs, most speakers use the classical
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       conjugation, employing the
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       form minus the
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       in the final diphthong. However, some prefer to use the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       subjunctive forms like in Argentina or Paraguay.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Espero que veas
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Espero que veás
        </i>
        "I hope you can see" (Peninsular
        <i>
         veáis
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo que quieras
        </i>
        or (less used)
        <i>
         Lo que querás
        </i>
        "Whatever you want" (Peninsular
        <i>
         queráis
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
        preterite
       </a>
       , an
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       is often added, for instance
       <i>
        (vos) perdistes
       </i>
       . This corresponds to the classical
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       conjugation found in literature. Compare Iberian Spanish form
       <i>
        vosotros perdisteis
       </i>
       . However, it is often deemed incorrect.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other verb forms coincide with
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       after the
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       is omitted (the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       forms are the same as
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Si salieras
        </i>
        "If you went out" (Peninsular
        <i>
         salierais
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the old times,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was used as a respectful term. In Central American Spanish, as in most other dialects which employ
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       , this pronoun has become informal, displacing
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . It is used especially for addressing friends and family members (regardless of age), but may also include most acquaintances, such as coworkers, friends of one's friends, people of similar age etc.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_tenses">
        Usage of tenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Usage of tenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although literary works use the full spectrum of verb inflections, in Central American Spanish (as well as many other Spanish dialects), the future tense has been replaced by a verbal phrase (
       <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">
        periphrasis
       </a>
       ) in the spoken language.
      </p>
      <p>
       This verb phrase is formed by the verb
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       ("go") followed by the preposition
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       and the main verb in the infinitive. This is akin to the English phrase
       <i>
        going to
       </i>
       + infinitive verb. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Creo que descansaré un poco
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Creo que voy a descansar un poco
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mañana me visitará mi madre
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Mañana me va a visitar mi madre
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Iré a visitarla mañana
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Voy a ir a visitarla mañana
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       (Spanish: Pretérito perfecto compuesto), just like
       <i>
        pretérito anterior
       </i>
       , is rarely used, so it's replaced by simple past.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Juan no ha llegado
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Juan no llegó todavía
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         El torneo ha comenzado
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         El torneo comenzó
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Central_American_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are also many words unique to Central America, for example,
       <i>
        chunche
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        chochadas
       </i>
       means
       <i>
        thing
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        stuff
       </i>
       in some places.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Also the words used to describe children (or kids) is different in various countries, for example in Nicaragua they are called
       <i>
        chavalos
       </i>
       (similar to
       <i>
        chavales
       </i>
       in Spain); or
       <i>
        sipotes;
       </i>
       while in Guatemala they are called
       <i>
        patojos
       </i>
       but in the eastern departments of Guatemala specifically the department of Jutiapa cipotes is also used to refer to children. In Honduras they're called
       <i>
        güirros
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chigüin
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cipitio" title="Cipitio">
         cipotes
        </a>
       </i>
       is used in both Honduras and El Salvador, while in Costa Rica they are called
       <i>
        güilas
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        carajillos
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In Guatemala, Honduras, and El Salvador money is called
       <i>
        pisto
       </i>
       , a term coming from the Spanish dish 'pisto'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, a common slang word used for money in all of the Central American countries (except Belize) is "plata". In Mexico "plata" refers to Mexican pesos while "oro" refers to American dollars. In addition, chucho in Guatemala, El Salvador, and Honduras means dog.
      </p>
