   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    <i>
     De jure
    </i>
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/de_jure" title="wiktionary:Special:Search/de jure">
            de jure
           </a>

      <p>
       In law and government,
       <i>
        <b>
         de jure
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʊər/: 'our' in 'tour'">
            ʊər
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       or
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʊər/: 'our' in 'tour'">
            ʊər
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         de iure
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">
         lit.
        </a>
       </small>
       'in law'
       <small>
        Latin pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Latin" title="Help:IPA/Latin">
         [deː juːre]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) describes practices that are legally recognised, whether or not the practices exist in reality.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dictionary.com_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dictionary.com-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In contrast,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
          de facto
         </a>
        </i>
       </span>
       ("in fact" or "in practice") describes situations that exist in reality, even if not legally recognised.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-OxfordDF2_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-OxfordDF2-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The terms are often used to contrast different scenarios: for a colloquial example, "I know that, de jure, this is supposed to be a parking lot, but now that the flood has left four feet of water here, it’s a de facto swimming pool".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WashULaw_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WashULaw-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=De_jure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It is possible to have multiple simultaneous conflicting (
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       ) legalities, possibly none of which is in force (
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       ). After seizing power in 1526,
       <a href="/wiki/Ahmad_ibn_Ibrahim_al-Ghazi" title="Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi">
        Ahmad ibn Ibrahim al-Ghazi
       </a>
       made his brother,
       <a href="/wiki/Umar_Din" title="Umar Din">
        Umar Din
       </a>
       , the lawful (
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/Sultan" title="Sultan">
        Sultan
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Adal_Sultanate" title="Adal Sultanate">
        Adal
       </a>
       . Ahmad, however, was in practice (
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       ) the actual Sultan, and his brother was a figurehead.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Between 1805 and 1914, the
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali_dynasty" title="Muhammad Ali dynasty">
        ruling dynasty
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Khedivate_of_Egypt" title="Khedivate of Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       ruled as
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Viceroy" title="Viceroy">
        viceroys
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       , but acted as
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       independent rulers who maintained a
       <a href="/wiki/Polite_fiction" title="Polite fiction">
        polite fiction
       </a>
       of Ottoman
       <a href="/wiki/Suzerainty" title="Suzerainty">
        suzerainty
       </a>
       . However, from about 1882, the rulers had only
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       rule over Egypt, as it had by then become a British
       <a href="/wiki/Puppet_state" title="Puppet state">
        puppet state
       </a>
       . Thus, Egypt was by Ottoman law
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       a province of the Ottoman Empire, but
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
        British Empire
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Law_of_the_United_States" title="Law of the United States">
        American law
       </a>
       , particularly after
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Brown_v._Board_of_Education" title="Brown v. Board of Education">
         Brown v. Board of Education
        </a>
       </i>
       (1954), the difference between
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       segregation (segregation that existed because of the voluntary associations and neighborhoods) and
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       segregation (segregation that existed because of local laws that mandated the segregation) became important distinctions for court-mandated remedial purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AndersonByrne2004_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AndersonByrne2004-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
