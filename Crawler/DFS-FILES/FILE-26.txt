      <p>
       <b>
        Altitude
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        height
       </b>
       (sometimes known as
       <b>
        depth
       </b>
       ) is defined based on the context in which it is used (aviation, geometry, geographical survey, sport, atmospheric pressure, and many more). As a general definition, altitude is a distance measurement, usually in the vertical or "up" direction, between a reference
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       and a point or object. The reference datum also often varies according to the context. Although the term altitude is commonly used to mean the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Height_above_sea_level" title="Height above sea level">
        height above sea level
       </a>
       of a location, in
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       the term
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       is often preferred for this usage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vertical distance measurements in the "down" direction are commonly referred to as
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/depth" title="wikt:depth">
        depth
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_aviation">
        In aviation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In aviation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <span id="Aviation">
       </span>
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level#Aviation" title="Sea level">
        Sea level § Aviation
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Vertical_distances.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="463" height="183" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vertical_distances.svg/300px-Vertical_distances.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vertical_distances.svg/450px-Vertical_distances.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Vertical_distances.svg/600px-Vertical_distances.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Vertical_distances.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Vertical distance comparison
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In aviation, the term altitude can have several meanings, and is always qualified by explicitly adding a modifier (e.g. "true altitude"), or implicitly through the context of the communication. Parties exchanging altitude information must be clear which definition is being used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFM_51-40_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AFM_51-40-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Aviation altitude is measured using either
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
        mean sea level
       </a>
       (MSL) or local ground level (above ground level, or AGL) as the reference datum.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure_altitude" title="Pressure altitude">
        Pressure altitude
       </a>
       divided by 100 feet (30 m) is the
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_level" title="Flight level">
        flight level
       </a>
       , and is used above the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transition_altitude" title="Transition altitude">
        transition altitude
       </a>
       (18,000 feet (5,500 m) in the US, but may be as low as 3,000 feet (910 m) in other jurisdictions); so when the altimeter reads 18,000 ft on the standard pressure setting the aircraft is said to be at "Flight level 180". When flying at a flight level, the altimeter is always set to standard pressure (29.92
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inches_of_mercury" title="Inches of mercury">
        inHg
       </a>
       or 1013.25
       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
        hPa
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       On the flight deck, the definitive instrument for measuring altitude is the pressure
       <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">
        altimeter
       </a>
       , which is an
       <a href="/wiki/Barometer#Aneroid_barometers" title="Barometer">
        aneroid barometer
       </a>
       with a front face indicating distance (feet or metres) instead of
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">
        atmospheric pressure
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several types of aviation altitude:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Indicated altitude
        </b>
        is the reading on the altimeter when it is set to the
        <a href="/wiki/QNH" title="QNH">
         local barometric pressure at mean sea level
        </a>
        . In UK aviation radiotelephony usage,
        <i>
         the vertical distance of a level, a point or an object considered as a point, measured from mean sea level
        </i>
        ; this is referred to over the radio as
        <b>
         altitude
        </b>
        .(see
        <a href="/wiki/QNH" title="QNH">
         QNH
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAP413_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-CAP413-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Absolute altitude
        </b>
        is the height of the aircraft above the terrain over which it is flying. It can be measured using a
        <a href="/wiki/Radar_altimeter" title="Radar altimeter">
         radar altimeter
        </a>
        (or "absolute altimeter").
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AFM_51-40_1-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-AFM_51-40-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Also referred to as "radar height" or feet/metres
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_Ground_Level" title="Above Ground Level">
         above ground level
        </a>
        (AGL).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         True altitude
        </b>
        is the actual elevation above
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
         mean sea level
        </a>
        . It is indicated altitude corrected for non-standard temperature and pressure.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Height
        </b>
        is the elevation above a ground reference point, commonly the terrain elevation. In UK aviation radiotelephony usage,
        <i>
         the vertical distance of a level, a point or an object considered as a point, measured from a specified datum
        </i>
        ; this is referred to over the radio as
        <b>
         height
        </b>
        , where the specified datum is the airfield elevation (see
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level_pressure" title="Mean sea level pressure">
         QFE
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CAP413_2-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-CAP413-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Pressure altitude
        </b>
        is the elevation above a standard datum air-pressure plane (typically, 1013.25 millibars or 29.92" Hg). Pressure altitude is used to indicate "flight level" which is the standard for altitude reporting in the U.S. in Class A airspace (above roughly 18,000 feet). Pressure altitude and indicated altitude are the same when the altimeter setting is 29.92" Hg or 1013.25 millibars.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Density_altitude" title="Density altitude">
          Density altitude
         </a>
        </b>
        is the altitude corrected for non-ISA
        <a href="/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere" title="International Standard Atmosphere">
         International Standard Atmosphere
        </a>
        atmospheric conditions. Aircraft performance depends on density altitude, which is affected by barometric pressure, humidity and temperature. On a very hot day, density altitude at an airport (especially one at a high elevation) may be so high as to preclude takeoff, particularly for helicopters or a heavily loaded aircraft.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These types of altitude can be explained more simply as various ways of measuring the altitude:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Indicated altitude
        </b>
        – the altitude shown on the altimeter.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Absolute altitude
        </b>
        – altitude in terms of the distance above the ground directly below
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         True altitude
        </b>
        – altitude in terms of elevation above sea level
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Height
        </b>
        – altitude in terms of the distance above a certain point
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Pressure altitude
        </b>
        – the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air_pressure" title="Air pressure">
         air pressure
        </a>
        in terms of altitude in the International Standard Atmosphere
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Density altitude
        </b>
        – the density of the air in terms of altitude in the International Standard Atmosphere in the air
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_atmospheric_studies">
        In atmospheric studies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In atmospheric studies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmospheric_regions">
        Atmospheric regions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Atmospheric regions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </a>
       is divided into several altitude regions. These regions start and finish at varying heights depending on season and distance from the poles. The altitudes stated below are averages:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">
         Troposphere
        </a>
        : surface to 8,000 metres (5.0 mi) at the poles, 18,000 metres (11 mi) at the
        <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
         Equator
        </a>
        , ending at the Tropopause
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">
         Stratosphere
        </a>
        : Troposphere to 50 kilometres (31 mi)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mesosphere" title="Mesosphere">
         Mesosphere
        </a>
        : Stratosphere to 85 kilometres (53 mi)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Thermosphere" title="Thermosphere">
         Thermosphere
        </a>
        : Mesosphere to 675 kilometres (419 mi)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">
         Exosphere
        </a>
        : Thermosphere to 10,000 kilometres (6,200 mi)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line" title="Kármán line">
        Kármán line
       </a>
       , at an altitude of 100 kilometres (62 mi) above
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       , by convention defines represents the demarcation between the atmosphere and
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        space
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The thermosphere and exosphere (along with the higher parts of the mesosphere) are regions of the atmosphere that are conventionally defined as space.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="High_altitude_and_low_pressure">
        High altitude and low pressure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: High altitude and low pressure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <span id="High_altitude_and_low_air_pressure">
       </span>
       Regions on the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's surface (or in its atmosphere) that are high above mean sea level are referred to as
       <b>
        high altitude
       </b>
       . High altitude is sometimes defined to begin at 2,400 meters (8,000 ft) above sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-websterMed_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-websterMed-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicalProblems_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MedicalProblems-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At high altitude, atmospheric pressure is lower than that at sea level. This is due to two competing physical effects: gravity, which causes the air to be as close as possible to the ground; and the heat content of the air, which causes the molecules to bounce off each other and expand.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nova_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nova-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature_profile">
        Temperature profile
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Temperature profile">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The temperature profile of the atmosphere is a result of an interaction between
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       . Sunlight in the
       <a href="/wiki/Visible_spectrum" title="Visible spectrum">
        visible spectrum
       </a>
       hits the ground and heats it. The ground then heats the air at the surface. If
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       were the only way to transfer heat from the ground to space, the
       <a href="/wiki/Greenhouse_effect" title="Greenhouse effect">
        greenhouse effect
       </a>
       of gases in the atmosphere would keep the ground at roughly 333 K (60 °C; 140 °F), and the temperature would decay exponentially with height.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodywilson_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-goodywilson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, when air is hot, it tends to expand, which lowers its density. Thus, hot air tends to rise and transfer heat upward. This is the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       . Convection comes to equilibrium when a parcel of air at a given altitude has the same density as its surroundings. Air is a poor conductor of heat, so a parcel of air will rise and fall without exchanging heat. This is known as an
       <a href="/wiki/Adiabatic_process" title="Adiabatic process">
        adiabatic process
       </a>
       , which has a characteristic pressure-temperature curve. As the pressure gets lower, the temperature decreases. The rate of decrease of temperature with elevation is known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Adiabatic lapse rate">
        adiabatic lapse rate
       </a>
       , which is approximately 9.8 °C per kilometer (or 5.4 °F per 1000 feet) of altitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-goodywilson_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-goodywilson-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that the presence of water in the atmosphere complicates the process of convection. Water vapor contains latent
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">
        heat of vaporization
       </a>
       . As air rises and cools, it eventually becomes
       <a href="/wiki/Dew_point" title="Dew point">
        saturated
       </a>
       and cannot hold its quantity of water vapor. The water vapor condenses (forming
       <a href="/wiki/Cloud" title="Cloud">
        clouds
       </a>
       ), and releases heat, which changes the lapse rate from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dry_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Dry adiabatic lapse rate">
        dry adiabatic lapse rate
       </a>
       to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moist_adiabatic_lapse_rate" title="Moist adiabatic lapse rate">
        moist adiabatic lapse rate
       </a>
       (5.5 °C per kilometer or 3 °F per 1000 feet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As an average, the International Civil Aviation Organization (ICAO) defines an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_standard_atmosphere" title="International standard atmosphere">
        international standard atmosphere
       </a>
       (ISA) with a temperature
       <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">
        lapse rate
       </a>
       of 6.49 °C per kilometer (3.56 °F per 1,000 feet).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ICAO_1993_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ICAO_1993-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The actual lapse rate can vary by altitude and by location.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, note that only the
       <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">
        troposphere
       </a>
       (up to approximately 11 kilometres (36,000 ft) of altitude) in the Earth's atmosphere undergoes notable convection; in the
       <a href="/wiki/Stratosphere" title="Stratosphere">
        stratosphere
       </a>
       , there is little vertical convection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_organisms">
        Effects on organisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Effects on organisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Humans">
        Humans
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Humans">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_high_altitude_on_humans" title="Effects of high altitude on humans">
        Effects of high altitude on humans
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Medicine recognizes that altitudes above 1,500 metres (4,900 ft) start to affect humans,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and there is no record of humans living at extreme altitudes above 5,500–6,000 metres (18,000–19,700 ft) for more than two years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-highestHabitation_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-highestHabitation-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the altitude increases, atmospheric pressure decreases, which affects humans by reducing the
       <a href="/wiki/Partial_pressure" title="Partial pressure">
        partial pressure
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pubmed_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pubmed-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lack of oxygen above 2,400 metres (8,000 ft) can cause serious illnesses such as
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude_sickness" title="Altitude sickness">
        altitude sickness
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_pulmonary_edema" title="High altitude pulmonary edema">
        high altitude pulmonary edema
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_altitude_cerebral_edema" title="High altitude cerebral edema">
        high altitude cerebral edema
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicalProblems_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MedicalProblems-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The higher the altitude, the more likely are serious effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MedicalProblems_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MedicalProblems-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The human body can
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altitude_acclimatization" title="Altitude acclimatization">
        adapt to high altitude
       </a>
       by breathing faster, having a higher heart rate, and adjusting its blood chemistry.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BordenHuman_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BordenHuman-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Acclimatization_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Acclimatization-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It can take days or weeks to adapt to high altitude. However, above 8,000 metres (26,000 ft), (in the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Death_zone" title="Death zone">
        death zone
       </a>
       "), altitude acclimatization becomes impossible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PBS-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a significantly lower overall mortality rate for permanent residents at higher altitudes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, there is a dose response relationship between increasing elevation and decreasing obesity prevalence in the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Voss_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Voss-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, the recent hypothesis suggests that high altitude could be protective against Alzheimer's disease via action of erythropoietin, a hormone released by kidney in response to hypoxia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, people living at higher elevations have a statistically significant higher rate of suicide.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brenner_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brenner-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cause for the increased suicide risk is unknown so far.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brenner_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brenner-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Athletes">
        Athletes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Athletes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       For athletes, high altitude produces two contradictory effects on performance. For explosive events (sprints up to 400 metres,
       <a href="/wiki/Long_jump" title="Long jump">
        long jump
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Triple_jump" title="Triple jump">
        triple jump
       </a>
       ) the reduction in atmospheric pressure signifies less atmospheric resistance, which generally results in improved athletic performance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For endurance events (races of 5,000 metres or more) the predominant effect is the reduction in oxygen which generally reduces the athlete's performance at high altitude. Sports organizations acknowledge the effects of altitude on performance: the International Association of Athletic Federations (IAAF), for example, marks record performances achieved at an altitude greater than 1,000 metres (3,300 ft) with the letter "A".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Athletes also can take advantage of altitude acclimatization to increase their performance. The same changes that help the body cope with high altitude increase performance back at sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid16497842_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid16497842-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid17805094_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid17805094-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These changes are the basis of
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude_training" title="Altitude training">
        altitude training
       </a>
       which forms an integral part of the training of athletes in a number of endurance sports including track and field, distance running, triathlon, cycling and swimming.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_organisms">
        Other organisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Altitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other organisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Organisms_at_high_altitude" title="Organisms at high altitude">
        Organisms at high altitude
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Decreased oxygen availability and decreased temperature make life at high altitude challenging. Despite these environmental conditions, many species have been successfully
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-altitude_adaptation" title="High-altitude adaptation">
        adapted at high altitudes
       </a>
       . Animals have developed physiological adaptations to enhance oxygen uptake and delivery to tissues which can be used to sustain metabolism. The strategies used by animals to adapt to high altitude depend on their
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(biology)" title="Morphology (biology)">
        morphology
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phylogeny" title="Phylogeny">
        phylogeny
       </a>
       . For example, small mammals face the challenge of maintaining body heat in cold temperatures, due to their small volume to surface area ratio. As oxygen is used as a source of metabolic heat production, the hypobaric hypoxia at high altitudes is problematic.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is also a general trend of smaller body sizes and lower
       <a href="/wiki/Species_richness" title="Species richness">
        species richness
       </a>
       at high altitudes, likely due to lower oxygen partial pressures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-macroinvertrichness_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-macroinvertrichness-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These factors may decrease
       <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(ecology)" title="Productivity (ecology)">
        productivity
       </a>
       in high altitude habitats, meaning there will be less energy available for consumption, growth, and activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trout_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-trout-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, some species, such as birds,thrive at high altitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Birds thrive because of physiological features that are advantageous for high-altitude flight.
      </p>
