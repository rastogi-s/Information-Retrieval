      <p>
       The
       <b>
        palatal lateral approximant
       </b>
       is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonantal
       </a>
       sound used in some
       <a href="/wiki/Speech" title="Speech">
        spoken
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       . The symbol in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       that represents this sound is ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʎ
       </span>
       ⟩, a rotated lowercase letter ⟨y⟩ (not to be confused with lowercase
       <a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">
        lambda
       </a>
       , ⟨λ⟩), and the equivalent
       <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">
        X-SAMPA
       </a>
       symbol is
       <code>
        L
       </code>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Many languages that were previously thought to have a palatal lateral approximant actually have a lateral approximant that is, broadly,
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">
        alveolo-palatal
       </a>
       ; that is to say, it is articulated at a place in-between the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
        alveolar ridge
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">
        hard palate
       </a>
       (excluded), and it may be variously described as alveolo-palatal, lamino-postalveolar,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or postalveolo-prepalatal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recasens1993p222_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-recasens1993p222-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       None of the 13 languages investigated by
       <a href="#CITEREFRecasens2013">
        Recasens (2013)
       </a>
       , many of them
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       , has a 'true' palatal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That is likely the case for several other languages listed here. Some languages, like Portuguese and Catalan, have a lateral approximant that varies between alveolar and alveolo-palatal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no dedicated symbol in the International Phonetic Alphabet that represents the
       <b>
        alveolo-palatal lateral approximant
       </b>
       . If precision is desired, it may be transcribed ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        l̠ʲ
       </span>
       ⟩ or ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʎ̟
       </span>
       ⟩; they are essentially equivalent because the contact includes both the blade and body (but not the tip) of the tongue. There is also a non-IPA letter ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ȴ
       </span>
       ⟩ ("l", plus the curl found in the symbols for alveolo-palatal sibilant fricatives ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolo-palatal_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolo-palatal fricative">
         ɕ
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolo-palatal_fricative" title="Voiced alveolo-palatal fricative">
         ʑ
        </a>
       </span>
       ⟩), used especially in Sinological circles.
      </p>
      <p>
       The palatal lateral approximant contrasts phonemically with its
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Voiceless palatal lateral approximant">
        voiceless counterpart
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ̥/
       </span>
       in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xumi_language" title="Xumi language">
        Xumi language
       </a>
       spoken in China.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chir1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chir1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chir2_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chir2-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatal_lateral_approximant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Features of the palatal lateral approximant:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
         manner of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
         approximant
        </a>
        , which means it is produced by narrowing the vocal tract at the place of articulation, but not enough to produce a
        <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
         turbulent airstream
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
         place of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated with the middle or back part of the
        <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
         tongue
        </a>
        raised to the
        <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">
         hard palate
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
         phonation
        </a>
        is voiced, which means the vocal cords vibrate during the articulation.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is an
        <a href="/wiki/Oral_consonant" title="Oral consonant">
         oral consonant
        </a>
        , which means air is allowed to escape through the mouth only.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is a
        <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
         lateral consonant
        </a>
        , which means it is produced by directing the airstream over the sides of the tongue, rather than down the middle.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
         airstream mechanism
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">
         pulmonic
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the
        <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">
         lungs
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">
         diaphragm
        </a>
        , as in most sounds.
       </li>
      </ul>
