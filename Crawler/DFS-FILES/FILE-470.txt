      <p>
       A
       <b>
        pro-drop language
       </b>
       (from "pronoun-dropping") is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       in which certain classes of
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       may be omitted when they are
       <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">
        pragmatically
       </a>
       or grammatically
       <a href="/wiki/Inference" title="Inference">
        inferable
       </a>
       (the precise conditions vary from language to language, and can be quite intricate). The phenomenon of "pronoun-dropping" is also commonly referred to as zero or null
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
        anaphora
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the case of pro-drop languages, null anaphora refers to the fact that that the null position does have referential properties, meaning it is not a null
       <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
        dummy pronoun
       </a>
       . Pro-drop is only licensed in languages that have a positive setting of the pro-drop parameter, which allows the null element to be identified by its governor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In pro-drop languages with a highly inflected verbal morphology, the expression of the subject pronoun is considered unnecessary because the verbal inflection indicates the person and number of the subject, thus the referent of the null subject can be inferred from the grammatical inflection on the verb.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Even though in everyday speech there are instances when who or what is being referred to can be inferred from context, non-pro-drop languages still require the pronoun. However, pro-drop languages allow those referential pronouns to be omitted, or be
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonologically
       </a>
       null. Among major languages, two of which might be called a pro-drop language are
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       (featuring pronoun deletion not only for subjects, but for practically all grammatical contexts).
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">
        American Sign Language
       </a>
       also exhibit frequent pro-drop features. In contrast, non-pro-drop is an
       <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">
        areal feature
       </a>
       of many northern European languages (see
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Average_European" title="Standard Average European">
        Standard Average European
       </a>
       ), including French, (standard) German, and English.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages might be considered only partially pro-drop in that they allow deletion of the subject pronoun. These
       <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">
        null-subject languages
       </a>
       include most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       is an exception) as well as all the Balto-Slavic languages and to a limited extent
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       . Colloquial and
       <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">
        dialectal German
       </a>
       , unlike the standard language, are also partially pro-drop; they typically allow deletion of the subject pronoun in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_clause" title="Main clause">
        main clauses
       </a>
       but not in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subordinate_clause" title="Subordinate clause">
        subordinate clauses
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_term">
        History of the term
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of the term">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term "pro-drop" stems from
       <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
        Noam Chomsky
       </a>
       's "Lectures on Government and Binding" from 1981 as a cluster of properties of which "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Null_subject" title="Null subject">
        null subject
       </a>
       " was one (for the occurrence of
       <i>
        pro
       </i>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">
        predicate
       </a>
       rather than a
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       in sentences with the
       <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
        copula
       </a>
       see Moro 1997).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus, a one-way correlation was suggested between
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        inflectional agreement
       </a>
       (AGR) and empty pronouns on the one hand and between no agreement and overt pronouns, on the other. It is worth noting that in the classical version, languages which not only lack agreement morphology but also allow extensive dropping of pronouns—such as Japanese, Chinese, Korean, and Vietnamese—are not included, as is made clear in a footnote: "The principle suggested is fairly general, but does not apply to such languages as Japanese in which pronouns can be missing much more freely." (Chomsky 1981:284, fn 47).
      </p>
      <p>
       The term pro-drop is also used in other frameworks in
       <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">
        generative grammar
       </a>
       , such as in
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_functional_grammar" title="Lexical functional grammar">
        lexical functional grammar
       </a>
       (LFG), but in a more general sense: "Pro-drop is a widespread linguistic phenomenon in which, under certain conditions, a structural NP may be unexpressed, giving rise to a pronominal interpretation." (Bresnan 1982:384).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Empty_category" title="Empty category">
        empty category
       </a>
       assumed (under
       <a href="/wiki/Government_and_binding_theory" title="Government and binding theory">
        government and binding theory
       </a>
       ) to be present in the vacant subject position left by pro-dropping is known as
       <i>
        pro
       </i>
       , or as "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Little_pro" title="Little pro">
        little pro
       </a>
       " (to distinguish it from "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Big_PRO" title="Big PRO">
        big PRO
       </a>
       ", an empty category associated with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        non-finite verb
       </a>
       phrases).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
        Japanese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Japanese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Consider the following examples from Japanese:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         このケーキは美味しい。誰が焼いたの？
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Kono kēki wa oishii. Dare ga yaita no?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        This cake TOPIC tasty-PRESENT. Who SUBJECT bake-PAST EXPLAIN.?
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "This cake is tasty. Who baked
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        ?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
         知らない。気に入った？
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Shiranai. Ki ni itta?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        know-NEGATIVE. like-PAST?
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        don't know. Did
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        like
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        ?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The words in
       <b>
        bold
       </b>
       in the English translations (
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       in the first line;
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       in the second) appear nowhere in the Japanese sentences but are understood from context. If nouns or pronouns were supplied, the resulting sentences would be grammatically correct but sound unnatural. (Learners of Japanese as a second language, especially those whose first language is non-pro-drop like English or French, often supply personal pronouns where they are pragmatically inferable, an example of
       <a href="/wiki/Language_transfer" title="Language transfer">
        language transfer
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese">
        Chinese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chinese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The above-mentioned examples from Japanese are readily rendered into Chinese:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
         这块蛋糕很美味。谁烤的?
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Zhè kuài dàngāo hĕn mĕiwèi. Shéi kăo de?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        This piece cake very beautiful taste. Who bakes?
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "This cake is very tasty. Who baked
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        ?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span lang="zh-Hans" xml:lang="zh-Hans">
         不知道。喜欢吗?
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Bù zhīdào. Xĭhuan ma?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Not know. like [QUESTION MARKER ( = not)]?
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        don't know. Do
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        like
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        ?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Though it is worthy to note that unlike Japanese, the inclusion of the dropped pronouns does not make the sentence sound unnatural.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Arabic is considered a null-subject language, as demonstrated by the following example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Arabic text: ساعد غيرك، يساعدك
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Transliteration:
        <i>
         sāʻid ghayrak, yusāʻiduk
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Literal translation: help other, helps you.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Idiomatic translation: If you help another, he helps you.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish">
        Turkish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Turkish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Sen-i gör-dü-
         <b>
          m
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        2SG-
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         ACC
        </a>
        see-
        <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
         PST
        </a>
        -
        <b>
         1SG
        </b>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        saw you.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The subject "I" above is easily inferable as the verb
       <i>
        gör-mek
       </i>
       "to see" is declined in the first person simple past tense form. The object is indicated by the pronoun
       <i>
        seni
       </i>
       in this case. Strictly speaking, pronominal objects are generally explicitly indicated, although frequently possessive suffixes indicate the equivalent of an object in English, as in the following sentence.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Gel-diğ-
         <b>
          im
         </b>
         -i gör-dü-
         <b>
          n
         </b>
         mü?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        come-
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominalisation" title="Nominalisation">
         NMLZ
        </a>
        -
        <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
         POS
        </a>
        .1SG-
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         ACC
        </a>
        see-
        <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
         PST
        </a>
        -
        <b>
         2SG
        </b>
        <a href="/wiki/Question" title="Question">
         Q
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Did
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        see
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        coming?
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In this sentence, the object of the verb is actually the action of coming performed by the speaker (
       <i>
        geldiğimi
       </i>
       "my coming"), but the object in the English sentence, "me", is indicated here by the possessive suffix
       <i>
        <b>
         -im
        </b>
       </i>
       "my" on the nominalised verb. Both pronouns can be explicitly indicated in the sentence for purposes of emphasis, as follows:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Sen ben-im
         </b>
         gel-diğ-im-i gör-dü-n mü?
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         2SG 1SG-POSS
        </b>
        come-"ing"-
        <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
         POS
        </a>
        .1SG-
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         ACC
        </a>
        see-
        <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
         PST
        </a>
        -
        <b>
         2SG
        </b>
        <a href="/wiki/Question" title="Question">
         Q
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Did
        <i>
         <b>
          you
         </b>
        </i>
        see
        <i>
         <b>
          me
         </b>
        </i>
        coming?
       </dd>
      </dl>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Swahili">
        Swahili
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Swahili">

      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Ni
         </b>
         -ta-
         <b>
          ku
         </b>
         -saidia.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          SUBJ.1SG
         </b>
         -
         <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">
          FUT
         </a>
         -
         <b>
          OBJ.2SG
         </b>
         -help
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        will help
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       English is considered a non-pro-drop language. Nonetheless, subject pronouns are almost always dropped in imperative sentences (e.g.,
       <i>
        Come here
       </i>
       ). In informal speech, pronouns may sometimes be dropped in other types of sentences, together with some other words, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
        copulas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliaries
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         [Have you] ever been there?
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         [I'm] going to the shops. [Do you] want to come?
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Seen on signs:
        <i>
         [I am/We are] out to lunch; [I/we will be] back at 1:00 [P.M].
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         What do you think [of it]?
        </i>
        –
        <i>
         I like [it]!
        </i>
        (the latter only in some dialects and registers)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In speech, when pronouns are not dropped, they are more often
       <a href="/wiki/Relaxed_pronunciation" title="Relaxed pronunciation">
        reduced
       </a>
       than other words in an utterance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Relative pronouns, provided they are not the subject, are often dropped in short restrictive clauses:
       <i>
        That's the man [whom] I saw.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that the dropping of pronouns is generally restricted to very informal speech and certain fixed expressions, and the rules for their use are complex and vary among dialects and registers. A noted instance was the "lived the dream" section of
       <a href="/wiki/George_H._W._Bush" title="George H. W. Bush">
        George H. W. Bush
       </a>
       's speech at the
       <a href="/wiki/1988_Republican_National_Convention" title="1988 Republican National Convention">
        1988 Republican National Convention
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Null-subject_languages">
        Null-subject languages

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Null-subject languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">
        Null-subject language
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Modern Greek">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Subject pronouns can be often omitted in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       . Example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Βλέπεις εκείνο το κούτσουρο; Θα ήταν καλό για τη φωτιά. Είναι τελείως ξερό.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        See that the log? Would be good for the fire. Is completely dried
        <i>
         (literal, direct translation)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        (Do)
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        see this log?
        <b>
         It
        </b>
        would be good for the fire.
        <b>
         It
        </b>
        has completely dried.
        <i>
         (idiomatic translation)
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Another phenomenon commonly encountered in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       is the omission of the preposition and article in phrases denoting place or movement to place.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        I am at home.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Είμαι σπίτι.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        I'm going home.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Πάω σπίτι.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        "I'm going [
        <b>
         there
        </b>
        ]!"
        <ul>
         <li>
          Πάω!
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       (with the notable exception of French) are often categorised as pro-drop too, most of them only in the case of subject pronouns. Unlike in Japanese, however, the missing subject pronoun is not inferred strictly from pragmatics, but partially indicated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       of the verb, which inflects for person and number of the subject.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">
        Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In Spanish, the verb is inflected for both person and number, thus expression of the pronoun is unnecessary because it is grammatically redundant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the following example, the inflection on the verb
       <i>
        ver,
       </i>
       'see', signals informal 2nd person singular, thus the pronoun is dropped. Similarly, from both the context and verbal morphology, the listener can infer that the second two utterances are referring to the log, so the speaker omits the pronoun that would appear in English as "it."
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         ¿Ves este tronco? Sería bueno para la fogata. Está completamente seco.
        </i>
        (Spanish)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        See this log? Would be good for the campfire. Is completely dry
        <i>
         (literal, direct translation)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        (Do)
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        see this log?
        <b>
         It
        </b>
        would be good for the campfire.
        <b>
         It
        </b>
        is completely dry
        <i>
         (English gloss)
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Although Spanish is a pro-drop language, not all grammatical contexts allow for a null pronoun. There are some environments that require an overt pronoun. In contrast, there are also grammatical environments that require a null pronoun. According to the
       <i>
        Real Academia Española
       </i>
       , the expression or elision of the subject pronoun is not random. Rather there are contexts in which an overt pronoun is abnormal, while in other cases the overt pronoun is possible or even required.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The third person pronouns (
       <i>
        él, ella, ellos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ellas
       </i>
       ) in most contexts can only refer to persons. Therefore, when referring to things (that are not people) an explicit pronoun is usually disallowed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Subject pronouns can be made explicit when used for a contrastive function or when the subject is the focus of the sentence. In the following example, the first person explicit pronoun is used to emphasize the subject.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Yo
        </i>
        [y no tú u otra persona]
        <i>
         creo que en eso estuvo mal.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         I [and not you or another person] think that that was wrong.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Subject pronouns can also be made explicit in order to clarify ambiguities that arise due to verb forms that are homophonous in the first person and third person. For example, in the past imperfect, conditional, and the subjunctive, the verb forms are the same for first person singular and third person singular. In these situations, using the explicit pronoun
       <i>
        yo
       </i>
       (1st person singular) or
       <i>
        él, ella
       </i>
       (3rd person singular) clarifies who the subject is, since the verbal morphology is ambiguous.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Italian">
        Italian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Italian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Vedi questo tronco? Andrebbe bene da bruciare. È completamente secco.
        </i>
        (Italian)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        See this log? Would go well for burning. Is completely dry
        <i>
         (literal, direct translation)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Do
        <b>
         you
        </b>
        see this log?
        <b>
         It
        </b>
        would be fit for burning.
        <b>

        </b>

        <i>
         (English gloss)
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      </p>

        (Do)
        <b>

        </b>

        <b>
         It
        </b>
        would be good for the campfire.
        <b>

        </b>
        has completely dried.
        <i>
         (English gloss)
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         "Tá" (está) vendo esse tronco? Seria bom pra fogueira. Secou totalmente.
        </i>
        (Brazilian Portuguese)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Are seeing this log? Would be good for-the campfire. Dried totally
        <i>

        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        (Do)
        <b>

        </b>

        <b>

        </b>

        </b>

       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Omission of object pronouns is likewise the norm when the referent is clear.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Acho que ele vai rejeitar a proposta, mas pode aceitar.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Think that he goes (to-)reject the proposal, but may accept.
        <i>
         (literally)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        I think he is going to turn down the proposal, but
        <b>
         he
        </b>
        may accept
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Ainda tem macarrão? Não, papai comeu.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Still is there pasta? No, daddy ate.
        <i>
         (literally)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Is there pasta left? No, daddy ate
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The use of the object pronoun in these examples (
       <i>
        aceitá-la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        comeu-o
       </i>
       ) would be grammatical but rather unnatural, especially in Brazil.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Ela me procurou ontem e não achou.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        She me sought yesterday and no found.
        <i>
         (literally)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        She looked for me yesterday and didn't find
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Here
       <i>
        não me achou
       </i>
       would also be possible.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        A:
        <i>
         Eu te amo; você também me ama?
        </i>
        B:
        <i>
         Amo, sim
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        A: I you love; you too me love? B: Love-1-SING, yes.
        <i>
         (literally)
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        A: I love you; do you love me too? B: I do.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Omission of the object pronoun is possible even when its referent has not been explicitly mentioned, so long as it can be inferred. The next example might be heard at a store; the referent (a dress) is clear to the interlocutor. In both Brazilian and European Portuguese the pronoun is omitted.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Viu que bonito? Não gosta? Pode comprar? (BP)
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Saw how beautiful? Don't like? Can buy? (using polite 2nd person) (BP)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Viste que bonito? Não gostas? Podes comprar? (EP)
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Saw how beautiful? Don't like? Can buy? (using informal 2nd person) (EP)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Have you seen how beautiful it is? Do you like it? Can you buy it?
        <i>
         (English gloss)
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Spanish and Portuguese are also notable amongst Romance languages because they have no specific pronouns for circumstantial
       <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">
        complements
       </a>
       (arguments denoting circumstance, consequence, place or manner, modifying the verb but not directly involved in the action) or
       <a href="/wiki/Partitive" title="Partitive">
        partitives
       </a>
       (words or phrases denoting a quantity of something).

         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">

      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>

       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Виждам го. Идва.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
         Bulgarian
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Vidim ga. Prilazi.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">
         Croatian
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Vidím ho. Jde.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
         Czech
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Го гледам. Доаѓа.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">
         Macedonian
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Widzę go. Idzie.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
         Polish
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Вижу [его]. Идёт.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
         Russian
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Видим га. Долази.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
         Serbian
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Vidím ho. Ide.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
         Slovak
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Vidim ga. Prihaja.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
         Slovene
        </a>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Бачу [його]. Йде.
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
         Ukrainian
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        see him.
        <b>
         He
        </b>
        is coming."
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Here
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       in the second sentence is inferred from context. In the East Slavic languages even the objective pronoun "
       <b>
        его
       </b>
       " can be omitted in the present and future tenses (both imperfect and perfective). As with the Romance languages mentioned above, the missing pronoun is not inferred strictly from pragmatics, but partially indicated by the morphology of the verb (Виж
       <b>
        у
       </b>
       , Виж
       <b>
        дам
       </b>
       , Widz
       <b>
        ę
       </b>
       , Vid
       <b>
        im
       </b>
       , etc...). However, the past tense of both imperfective and perfective in modern East Slavic languages inflects by gender and number rather than the person due to the fact that the present tense conjugations of the copula "to be" (Russian
       <i>
        быть
       </i>
       , Ukrainian
       <i>
        бути
       </i>
       , Belorussian
       <i>
        быць
       </i>
       ) have practically fallen out of use. As such, the pronoun is often included in these tenses, especially in writing.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Finno-Ugric_languages">
        Finno-Ugric languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Finno-Ugric languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , the verb inflection replaces first and second person pronouns in simple sentences, e.g.
       <i>
        menen
       </i>
       "I go",
       <i>
        menette
       </i>
       "all of you go". Pronouns are typically left in place only when they need to be inflected, e.g.
       <i>
        me
       </i>
       "we",
       <i>
        meiltä
       </i>
       "from us". There are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_pronoun" title="Possessive pronoun">
        possessive pronouns
       </a>
       , but
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_suffix" title="Possessive suffix">
        possessive suffixes
       </a>
       , e.g.
       <i>
        -ni
       </i>
       as in
       <i>
        kissani
       </i>
       "my cat", are also used, as in
       <i>
        Kissani söi kalan
       </i>
       ("my cat ate a fish"). A peculiarity of
       <a href="/wiki/Colloquial_Finnish" title="Colloquial Finnish">
        colloquial Finnish
       </a>
       is that the pronoun
       <i>
        me
       </i>
       ("we") can be dropped if the verb is placed in the passive voice (e.g.
       <i>
        haetaan
       </i>
       , standard "it is fetched", colloquial "we fetch"). In the
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian language
       </a>
       , a close relative of Finnish, the tendency is less clear. It generally uses explicit personal pronouns in the literary language, but these are often omitted in colloquial Estonian.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       is also pro-drop, subject pronouns are used only for emphasis, as example
       <i>
        (Én) mentem
       </i>
       "I went", and because of the definite conjugation, object pronouns can be often elided as well; for example, the question
       <i>
        (Te) láttad a macskát?
       </i>
       "Did (you) see the cat?" can be answered with just
       <i>
        láttam
       </i>
       "(I) saw (it)", because the definite conjugation renders the object pronoun superfluous.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hebrew">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Modern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       , like
       <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" title="Biblical Hebrew">
        Biblical Hebrew
       </a>
       , is a "moderately" pro-drop language. In general, subject pronouns must be included in the present tense. Since Hebrew has no verb forms expressing the present tense, the present tense is formed using the present participle (somewhat like English
       <i>
        I am
        <b>
         guarding
        </b>
       </i>
       ). The participle in Hebrew, as is the case with other adjectives, declines only in grammatical gender and number (like the
       <i>
        past
       </i>
       tense in Russian), thus:

       Since the forms used for the present tense lack the distinction between grammatical persons, explicit pronouns must be added in the majority of cases.

      <p>
       Many nouns can take suffixes to reflect the possessor, in which case the personal pronoun is dropped. In daily modern Hebrew usage, inflection of nouns is common only for simple nouns, and in most cases, inflected possessive pronouns are used. In Hebrew, possessive pronouns are treated mostly like adjectives and follow the nouns which they modify. In biblical Hebrew, inflection of more sophisticated nouns is more common than in modern usage.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations_across_languages">
        Generalizations across languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Generalizations across languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish, Italian, Catalan, Occitan and Romanian can
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        elide
       </a>
       subject pronouns only (Portuguese sometimes elides object pronouns as well), and they often do so even when the referent has not been mentioned. This is helped by person/number
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       on the verb. It has been observed that pro-drop languages are those with either rich inflection for person and number (Persian, Polish, Portuguese, etc.) or no such inflection at all (Japanese, Chinese, Korean, etc.), but languages that are intermediate (English, French, etc.) are non-pro-drop.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the mechanism by which overt pronouns are more "useful" in English than in Japanese is obscure, and there are exceptions to this observation, it still seems to have considerable descriptive validity. As Huang puts it, "Pro-drop is licensed to occur either where a language has full agreement, or where a language has no agreement, but not where a language has impoverished partial agreement."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_language_families_and_linguistic_regions">
        Other language families and linguistic regions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Other language families and linguistic regions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Among the Indo-European and
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       of India, pro-drop is the general rule though many Dravidian languages do not have overt verbal markers to indicate pronominal subjects.
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">
        Mongolic languages
       </a>
       are similar in this respect to Dravidian languages, and all
       <a href="/wiki/Paleosiberian_languages" title="Paleosiberian languages">
        Paleosiberian languages
       </a>
       are rigidly pro-drop.
      </p>
      <p>
       Outside of northern Europe, most
       <a href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Congo_languages" title="Niger–Congo languages">
        Niger–Congo languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Khoisan_languages" title="Khoisan languages">
        Khoisan languages
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">
        Southern Africa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian languages
       </a>
       of the Western Pacific, pro-drop is the usual pattern in almost all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_area" title="Linguistic area">
        linguistic regions
       </a>
       of the world. In many non-pro-drop Niger–Congo or Austronesian languages, like
       <a href="/wiki/Igbo_language" title="Igbo language">
        Igbo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
        Samoan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fijian_language" title="Fijian language">
        Fijian
       </a>
       , however, subject pronouns do not occur in the same position as a nominal subject and are obligatory, even when the latter is present. In more easterly Austronesian languages, like
       <a href="/wiki/Rapa_Nui_language" title="Rapa Nui language">
        Rapa Nui
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       , subject pronouns are often omitted even though no other subject morphemes exist.
       <a href="/wiki/Pama%E2%80%93Nyungan_languages" title="Pama–Nyungan languages">
        Pama–Nyungan languages
       </a>
       of Australia also typically omit subject pronouns even when there is no explicit expression of the subject.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Pama–Nyungan languages, however, have
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitics
       </a>
       , which often attach to nonverbal hosts to express subjects. The other languages of Northwestern Australia are all pro-drop, for all classes of pronoun. Also,
       <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">
        Papuan languages
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
        New Guinea
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">
        Nilo-Saharan languages
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/East_Africa" title="East Africa">
        East Africa
       </a>
       are pro-drop.
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       , pro-drop is almost universal, as would be expected from the generally
       <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">
        polysynthetic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Head-marking_language" title="Head-marking language">
        head-marking
       </a>
       character of the languages. That generally allows eliding of all object pronouns as well as subject ones. Indeed, most reports on Native American languages show that even the emphatic use of pronouns is exceptionally rare. Only a few Native American languages, mostly
       <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">
        language isolates
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Haida_language" title="Haida language">
        Haida
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Trumai_language" title="Trumai language">
        Trumai
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wappo_language" title="Wappo language">
        Wappo
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Oto-Manguean_languages" title="Oto-Manguean languages">
        Oto-Manguean
       </a>
       family are known for normally using subject pronouns.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pragmatic_inference">
        Pragmatic inference
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pro-drop_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Pragmatic inference">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">
        Classical Chinese
       </a>
       exhibits extensive dropping not only of pronouns but also of any terms (subjects, verbs, objects, etc.) pragmatically inferable, giving a very compact character to the language. Note, however, that Classical Chinese was a written language, and such word dropping is not necessarily representative of the spoken language or even of the same linguistic phenomenon.
      </p>
