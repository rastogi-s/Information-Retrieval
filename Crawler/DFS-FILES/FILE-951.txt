      <p>
       <b>
        Poitevin
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        Poetevin
       </i>
       ) is a language spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">
        Poitou
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       . It is one of the regional
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        languages of France
       </a>
       and is not as commonly spoken as it once was, as the standard form of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       now predominates. Poitevin is now classified as one of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_languages" title="Oïl languages">
        langues d'oïl
       </a>
       but is distinguished by certain features of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27oc" title="Langue d'oc">
        langue d'oc
       </a>
       . The language is spoken on what was the border between the two language families of
       <i>
        oïl
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oc
       </i>
       (placenames in the region clearly show historical settlement of
       <i>
        oc
       </i>
       speakers). The langue d’oïl subsequently spread south, absorbing
       <i>
        oc
       </i>
       features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Poitevin is also widely referred to as
       <i>
        parlanjhe
       </i>
       (the language).
       <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="François Rabelais">
        François Rabelais
       </a>
       wrote that he learned this dialect, along with many other languages and dialects, since he was educated in
       <a href="/wiki/Fontenay-le-Comte" title="Fontenay-le-Comte">
        Fontenay-le-Comte
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Villon" title="François Villon">
        François Villon
       </a>
       spoke some Poitevin as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest attested written use of the language is in charters and legal documents dating from the 13th century; people who spoke it were known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poitevins" title="Poitevins">
        Poitevins
       </a>
       . The earliest printed text is dated 1554 (La Gente Poitevinrie). A tradition of theatrical writing and dramatic monologues for performance typifies the literary output in the language, although from the 19th century and in the 20th century (especially with the publication of a weekly paper
       <i>
        Le Subiet
       </i>
       from 1901) regular journalistic production was also established. Geste Editions publishes a number of books in/about the Poitevin-Santongese language. Some linguists assert that the Serments de Strasbourg, the first text in French according to the official state position in France, were actually written in Poitevin.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1973, a standard
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       was proposed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The easternmost part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Poitou" title="Poitou">
        Poitou
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C3%A9gions_in_France" title="Régions in France">
         région
        </a>
       </i>
       is home to a minority of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       -speakers though. Outside
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       the language is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_California" title="Northern California">
        Northern California
       </a>
       , especially in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacramento" title="Sacramento">
        Sacramento
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plumas_County,_California" title="Plumas County, California">
        Plumas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tehama,_California" title="Tehama, California">
        Tehama
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Siskiyou_County,_California" title="Siskiyou County, California">
        Siskiyou
       </a>
       counties, the latter with both large French ancestry and speaking populations.
       <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">
        Acadian French
       </a>
       is the result of a Poitevin-Santongese language blended with French and local innovations or archaisms.
      </p>
