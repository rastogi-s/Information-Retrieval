      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish personal pronouns
       </b>
       have distinct forms according to whether they stand for a subject (
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       ), a direct object (
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative
       </a>
       ), an indirect object (
       <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
        dative
       </a>
       ), or a
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
        reflexive
       </a>
       object. Several pronouns further have special forms used after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       . Spanish is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-drop" title="Pro-drop">
        pro-drop
       </a>
       language with respect to subject pronouns. Like
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and other languages with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       , modern Spanish has a distinction in its second person pronouns that has no equivalent in modern English. Object pronouns are generally
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       , and non-emphatic
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       is most often found with dative clitics. The
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronoun
       </a>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        vos
       </a>
       " is spoken in Latin American, between Central America, Uruguay, Paraguay, Argentina, Paraguay, Chile, the Andean regions of Bolivia, Perú, Ecuador and Colombia and the Venezuelan state of Zulia.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subject_pronouns">
        Subject pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Subject pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronoun_dropping_and_grammatical_gender">
        Pronoun dropping and grammatical gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pronoun dropping and grammatical gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-drop" title="Pro-drop">
        pro-drop
       </a>
       language with respect to subject pronouns. Information contained in verb endings often renders the explicit use of subject pronouns unnecessary and even erroneous, although they may still be used for clarity or emphasis:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo hago
        </i>
        or just
        <i>
         Hago
        </i>
        = "I do"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellos vieron
        </i>
        or just
        <i>
         Vieron
        </i>
        = "They saw"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       English subject pronouns are generally not translated into Spanish when neither clarity nor emphasis is an issue. "I think" is generally translated as just
       <i>
        Pienso
       </i>
       unless the speaker is contrasting his or her views with those of someone else or placing emphasis on the fact that his views are his own and not somebody else’s.
      </p>
      <p>
       The masculine and feminine third-person pronouns (
       <i>
        él
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ella
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ellos
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        ellas
       </i>
       ) can refer to grammatically masculine and feminine objects, respectively, as well as people, although their explicit use as impersonal subjects is somewhat uncommon. The neuter third-person singular pronoun
       <i>
        ello
       </i>
       (as well as its plural
       <i>
        ellos
       </i>
       ) is likewise rarely used as an explicit subject in everyday Spanish, although such usage is found in formal and literary language. Quite unusually among European languages, the first- and second-person plural subject pronouns (
       <i>
        nosotros/nosotras
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vosotros/vosotras
       </i>
       , respectively) also inflect for gender:
       <i>
        nosotros
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       are used to refer to groups of men (as well as men and women), and
       <i>
        nosotras
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vosotras
       </i>
       are used exclusively to refer to groups of women.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="T.C3.BA.2Fvos_and_usted">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tú/vos_and_usted">
        <i>
         Tú
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tú/vos and usted">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Like
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and other languages with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       , modern Spanish has a distinction in its second person pronouns that has no equivalent in modern English. The most basic is the difference between
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       areas) and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is the "familiar" form, and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       , derived from the third-person form "your grace" (
       <i>
        vuestra merced
       </i>
       ), is the "polite" form. The appropriate usage of these forms is fundamental to interpersonal communication. Using the
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       form when addressing someone implies that the person addressed is a social superior, someone to whom respect is owed, or someone with whom one does not have a close relationship; in conservative families a child will use
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       when addressing a parent. In contrast, the use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       implies that the person addressed is an equal, a
       <a href="/wiki/Comrade" title="Comrade">
        comrade
       </a>
       , a friend, someone with whom one has a close relationship, or a child or other social inferior, including (traditionally) a maid or other household employee. One can give offense by addressing someone with
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       , similar to inappropriately calling someone by his/her first name in English. Spanish has a verb,
       <i>
        tutear
       </i>
       , meaning to use the familiar form
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       to address a person. Commonly, if a speaker feels that the relationship with the conversant has evolved - sometimes only after a few minutes of conversation - to a point where a shift from "usted" to "tú" is desirable, he or she will confirm this by asking if it is acceptable:
       <i>
        Nos tuteamos, ¿verdad?
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ¿Te puedo tutear?
       </i>
       is fairly common. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglophone" title="Anglophone">
        Anglophone
       </a>
       countries this would be parallel to asking if it is acceptable to call someone by their first name.
      </p>
      <p>
       In leftist political contexts, where everyone is to be equal, "usted" is rarely used. Conversely, in formal Spanish and hierarchical contexts (such as the Catholic church and the legal system), use of "usted" is routine.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the plural, in Spain (other than the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       and parts of western Andalusia), the usage of the familiar
       <i>
        vosotros/vosotras
       </i>
       and the polite
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is identical to the usage of
       <i>
        tú/usted
       </i>
       . In the Canary Islands as well as those parts of western Andalusia in addition to Spanish America,
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is not used except in very formal contexts such as oratory, and "ustedes" is the familiar as well as the polite plural.
      </p>
      <p>
       The distinction extends to other types of pronouns and modifiers: when using
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       one must also use the third-person object pronouns and possessive adjectives. "Tu casa" (
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       with an (acute)
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        accent
       </a>
       is the subject pronoun,
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       with no accent is a possessive adjective) means "your house" in the familiar singular: the owner of the house is one person, and it is a person with whom one has the closer relationship the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       form implies. In contrast,
       <i>
        su casa
       </i>
       can mean "his/her house", but it can also mean "your house" in the polite singular: the owner of the house is someone with whom one has the more distant or formal relationship implied by the use of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly, the use of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       requires third-person object pronouns.
       <i>
        Te lavas
       </i>
       means "you [familiar singular] wash yourself", but
       <i>
        se lava
       </i>
       can mean "you [polite singular] wash yourself", as well as "he/she/it washes himself/herself/itself"'.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impersonal_pronouns">
        Impersonal pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Impersonal pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/One_(pronoun)" title="One (pronoun)">
        impersonal pronoun
       </a>
       is
       <i>
        uno
       </i>
       ("one", as well as
       <i>
        una
       </i>
       for women), which declines as a normal third-person pronoun and is treated as such for purposes of conjugation and reflexivity. It is used in a variety of situations, although many ideas that would be expressed with an impersonal pronoun in English would more often be expressed with
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">
        passive
       </a>
       constructions in Spanish, e.g. "That would not be done" as opposed to "You (One) wouldn't do that".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reflexive_pronouns_and_intensifiers">
        Reflexive pronouns and intensifiers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Reflexive pronouns and intensifiers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The third person is the only person with a distinct reflexive pronoun:
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       . In the first and second persons, the normal object pronouns are used. Thus, the reflexive forms are:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Singular
        </th>
        <th>
        </th>
        <th>
         Plural
        </th>
        <th>
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         yo
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          me
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         nosotros/nosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          nos
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         tú/vos
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          te
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         vosotros/vosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          os
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         él/ella/ello/usted
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          se
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         ellos/ellas/ustedes
        </td>
        <td>
         <b>
          se
         </b>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The reflexive pronoun is used with pronominal verbs, also known as reflexive verbs. These verbs require the use of the reflexive pronoun, appropriate to the subject. Some transitive verbs can take on a reflexive meaning, such as
       <i>
        lavar
       </i>
       (to wash) and
       <i>
        lavarse
       </i>
       (to wash oneself). Other verbs have reflexive forms which do not take on a reflexive meaning, such as
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       (to go) and
       <i>
        irse
       </i>
       (to go away). Some verbs only have reflexive forms, such as
       <i>
        jactarse
       </i>
       (to boast).
      </p>
      <p>
       The nominal intensifier in Spanish (equivalent to English "myself", "yourself", "themselves", etc. when used after a noun) is
       <i>
        mismo
       </i>
       , which in this case is placed after the noun it modifies and behaves like a normal adjective. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo
         <b>
          mismo
         </b>
         lo hice
        </i>
        = "I [masc.] myself did it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No entiendo porque necesitas la cosa
         <b>
          misma
         </b>
        </i>
        = "I don't understand why you need the thing itself"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Dáselo a los hombres
         <b>
          mismos
         </b>
        </i>
        = "Give it to the men themselves"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         A nosotros no nos gustan las chicas
         <b>
          mismas
         </b>
        </i>
        = "We don't like the girls themselves" (lit. "The girls themselves don't please us")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Unlike English intensifiers, which are often placed several words after the noun they modify (e.g. "I did it myself"), Spanish intensifiers must come immediately after the noun they modify.
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_object_pronouns" title="Spanish object pronouns">
        Spanish object pronouns
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Object_pronoun" title="Object pronoun">
        Object pronouns
       </a>
       are personal pronouns that take the function of an
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       in a sentence. In Spanish, they may be analyzed as
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitics
       </a>
       which cannot function independently, but take the conjugated form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_verbs" title="Spanish verbs">
        Spanish verb
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Object pronouns are generally
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       , i.e. they appear before the verb of which they are the object.
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Enclitic" title="Clitic">
        Enclitic
       </a>
       pronouns (i.e. pronouns attached to the end of the verb or similar word itself) must often appear with positive
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperatives
       </a>
       and may appear with
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitives
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerunds
       </a>
       as well. In all compound infinitives that make use of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       , enclitics attach to the uninflected auxiliary verb and not the past participle(s) itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Spanish, two (and rarely three) clitic pronouns can be used with a single verb, generally one accusative and one dative. They follow a specific order based primarily on person:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proato_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-proato-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable" style="width:13em">
       <tr>
        <th align="center">
         1
        </th>
        <th>
         2
        </th>
        <th>
         3
        </th>
        <th>
         4
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         se
        </td>
        <td>
         te
         <br/>
         os
        </td>
        <td>
         me
         <br/>
         nos
        </td>
        <td>
         lo, la,
         <br/>
         los, las,
         <br/>
         le, les
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él me lo dio
        </i>
        = "He gave it to me"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellos te lo dijeron
        </i>
        = "They said it to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo te me daré
        </i>
        = "I will give myself to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vosotros os nos mostráis
        </i>
        = "You [pl.] are showing yourselves to us"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se le perdieron los libros
        </i>
        = "The books disappeared on him" (lit. "The books got lost to him")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The full and pronomial form of a reduplicated direct object must agree in gender and number:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          A las tropas las
         </b>
         dirige César.
        </i>
        = "Caesar directs the troops."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When an accusative third-person non-reflexive pronoun (
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        las
       </i>
       ) is used with a dative pronoun that is understood to also be third-person non-reflexive (
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       ), the dative pronoun is replaced by
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       . Simple non-emphatic
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       is most often found with dative clitics, although it is occasionally found with accusative clitics as well. In a wide area in central Spain, including
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , there exists the practice of
       <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
        leísmo
       </a>
       ; which is, using the indirect object pronoun
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       for the object pronoun where
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       would use
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (masculine) or
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       (feminine) for the object pronoun.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Genitive_pronouns">
        Genitive pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Genitive pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Genitive pronouns describe to whom something belongs or of whom (or sometimes what) something is a characteristic or property. They are analogous to English "mine", "yours", "his", "hers", etc., and unlike their English counterparts, they inflect for gender and number according to the thing possessed (
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       the possessor itself) and are generally used with the definite article:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi coche es más grande que
         <b>
          el tuyo
         </b>
        </i>
        = "My car is bigger than yours"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tu casa tiene más cuartos que
         <b>
          la suya
         </b>
        </i>
        = "Your house has more rooms than his/hers/yours/theirs"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estos libros son más interesantes que
         <b>
          los vuestros
         </b>
        </i>
        = "These books are more interesting than yours [pl.]"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esas camisas son más pequeñas que
         <b>
          las nuestras
         </b>
        </i>
        = "Those shirts are smaller than ours"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       After
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       , however, the definite article is usually omitted:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este coche es
         <b>
          mío
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This car is mine"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta camisa es
         <b>
          suya
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This shirt is his/hers/yours/theirs"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       To avoid ambiguity in the meaning of
       <i>
        suyo
       </i>
       , it may be replaced by
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       + the appropriate pronoun:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estos pantalones son más largos que los
         <b>
          de él
         </b>
        </i>
        = "These pants are longer than his"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta camisa es
         <b>
          de ella
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This shirt is hers"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The neuter article
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       can also be used with genitive pronouns to express the concept of "what is mine", "what is yours", "what is his", etc.:
       <i>
        lo mío
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lo tuyo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lo suyo
       </i>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       Genitive pronouns are identical in form to
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_determiners#Combining_demonstratives_and_possessives" title="Spanish determiners">
        long-form possessive adjectives
       </a>
       , which can be placed after the noun to place emphasis on the fact of possession.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Old_forms">
        Old forms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Old forms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formal_vos">
        Formal
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Formal vos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the past, the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was used as a respectful form of address, semantically equivalent to modern
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . This pronoun used the same conjugations as modern
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       (see below) and also the oblique form
       <i>
        os
       </i>
       and the possessive
       <i>
        vuestro/-a/-os/-as
       </i>
       . However, unlike
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       , which always refers to more than one person,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was usually singular in meaning. The modern
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       of several countries (see below) derives from this old form, but has become a generic form of address instead of a specifically respectful form.
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       and its related forms are still used in literature, cinema, etc. when trying to depict the language of past centuries.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variation">
        Regional variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regional variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voseo">
        <i>
         Voseo
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Voseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The pronoun "
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        vos
       </a>
       " is used in some areas of Latin America, particularly in Central America, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Chile, the state of Zulia in Venezuela, and the Andean regions of Colombia, Bolivia, Perú, and Ecuador.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       These are all distant from the large Spanish colonial cities, like Mexico City, Cartagena (Colombia), and Lima.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some areas, like the River Plate region,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       has become the only generic form of address for the second-person singular, that is, it has the same meaning that
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       has elsewhere (informal and intimate). In other areas, like Chile, it persists as a fairly stigmatized form alongside the more prestigious
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . In some other areas, it is employed among equals but not for very close people (couples or family) or to
       <i>
        inferiors
       </i>
       (children, animals etc.), where the pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       would normally be used.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       speakers use
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       as well, except that they employ it as in Old Spanish (see above), that is, as a respectful form of address, equivalent to how
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is used elsewhere. In fact, Ladino speakers do not use
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       at all because
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       implies the same respect that it once had in Old Spanish. In Ladino,
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is used towards anyone in an informal manner.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_use_of_vusted_and_vuestra_merced">
        The use of
        <i>
         vusted
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         vuestra merced
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: The use of vusted and vuestra merced">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The variant
       <i>
        vusted
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        vustedes
       </i>
       is mostly a regionalism of some South American countries. It is common to hear it in isolated areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       . Other speakers consider it archaic because it is an older form of a contraction of
       <i>
        vuestra merced
       </i>
       . In Colombia, it is not unusual to hear people use "
       <i>
        su merced
       </i>
       " interchangeably with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . It can be used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">
        vocative
       </a>
       as well, e.g. when speaking to an older person, as in
       <i>
        "Su merced, ¿por qué no vienen vusted y sus nietos a mi casa esta tarde?"
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vuestra merced
       </i>
       (literally 'your grace') is the origin of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        usarcé
       </i>
       and similar forms that govern third-person verb forms with a second-person function. They are mostly confined to period works now.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is unlikely that similar-sounding Arabic
       <i>
        ustādh أُسْتَاذْ
       </i>
       ('professor') was involved in the formation of Spanish
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       , given the weakness of the semantic link and the fact that
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is not documented before 1598 (see the online
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.corpusdelespanol.org/" rel="nofollow">
        Corpus del Español
       </a>
       ) — over a century after the fall of
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">
        Moorish Granada
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_use_of_vosotros">
        The use of
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The use of vosotros">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Today, the informal second-person plural pronoun
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is widely used by Spaniards, except in some southwestern regions and in most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       , where its use is rare. Among the former colonies of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       , the use of
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and its normal conjugations is also retained in the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Equatoguinean_Spanish" title="Equatoguinean Spanish">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Vosotros
       </i>
       is the only form used by Sephardic Jews who speak
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout Latin America, the third person plural pronoun
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is universally used orally in both formal (singular
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       ) and informal (singular
       <i>
        tú/vos
       </i>
       ) contexts. However, "that vosotros is not used in Spanish America is one of the great myths of Spanish language instruction, at least in the U.S."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The following quote from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sandino" title="Sandino">
        Sandino
       </a>
       was displayed on a billboard in Nicaragua:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Más de un batallón de los vuestros, invasor rubio, habrá mordido el polvo de mis agrestes montañas.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vosotros
       </i>
       is not unusual in
       <a href="/wiki/Orator" title="Orator">
        oratory
       </a>
       , legal documents, or other highly formal or archaic contexts.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Creoles">
        Creoles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Creoles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Forms based on
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are used in many
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">
        Spanish-based creole languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chavacano_language" title="Chavacano language">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       , spoken in the Philippines,
       <i>
        vo
       </i>
       is used alongside
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       as a singular second-person pronoun in Zamboangueño, Caviteño, and Ternateño. In Zamboangueño,
       <i>
        evos
       </i>
       is also used. For the plural, Zamboangueño has
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       while Caviteño has
       <i>
        vusos
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </a>
       , spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">
        Aruba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">
        Bonaire
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">
        Curaçao
       </a>
       , maintains
       <i>
        boso
       </i>
       (singular) and
       <i>
        bosonan
       </i>
       (plural). Since it was used with slaves, the forms that seemed disrespectful in the rest of America were common.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Menda">
        Menda
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Menda">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Menda
       </i>
       is the equivalent of
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cal%C3%B3_language" title="Caló language">
        Caló
       </a>
       , where it is concords in first person singular. In Spanish slang,
       <i>
        el menda
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        la menda
       </i>
       can be used as an emphatic
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       , concording with a third person verb, but its use is receding.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="The_use_of_le.2Fles">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_use_of_le/les">
        The use of
        <i>
         le
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         les
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: The use of le/les">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The pronouns
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       (singular) and
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       (plural)—which do not change form for gender—are used to replace the indirect object of a sentence. They also usually
       <i>
        accompany
       </i>
       an explicit indirect object, "redundantly". For example, in "Le di el libro a María" (I gave the book to María), both "Le" and "a María" refer to the same person (María) as the indirect object. In spoken language,
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       is frequently replaced by
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       , and this replacement—although not approved by normative grammar—can often be found in written, published texts as well. When the indirect object pronoun is followed in sequence by a direct object pronoun beginning with
       <i>
        l-
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        las
       </i>
       ), both
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       are replaced by
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le di el libro
        </i>
        = "I gave the book to him/her/you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo di
        </i>
        = "I gave it to him/her/you/them"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Direct-object_le.2Fles">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Direct-object_le/les">
        Direct-object
        <i>
         le/les
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Direct-object le/les">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Generally, the unstressed third-person object pronouns in Spanish are
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        las
       </i>
       . This is the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
         Real Academia Española
        </a>
        's
       </i>
       current position on the subject.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proato_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-proato-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dle_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dle-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is a reasonable generalisation given that it is true in over ninety percent of the Spanish-speaking world. However, it is helpful to take note of the various exceptions to this general rule whereby
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       rather than
       <i>
        lo, la, los, las
       </i>
       are used. Note however that this use is rather modern and often found only in part of Spain whereas the use of
       <i>
        lo, la, los, las
       </i>
       is considered more traditional.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Theoretical_basis_for_the_use_of_direct-object_le.2Fles">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_basis_for_the_use_of_direct-object_le/les">
        Theoretical basis for the use of direct-object
        <i>
         le/les
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Theoretical basis for the use of direct-object le/les">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       There are various diachronic and synchronic reasons for the use of
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       for direct objects. To understand why there is vacillation and hesitation in usage, it is helpful to understand these often-conflicting linguistic forces.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        a) Masculine
        <i>
         e
        </i>
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       There is a strong tendency in Spanish, inherited from Latin, for pronouns and determiners to have a set of three different endings for the three genders. These are:
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       for masculine pronouns,
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       for feminine pronouns and
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       for neuter pronouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus,
       <i>
        éste, ésta, esto
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        ése, ésa, eso
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        aquél, aquélla, aquello
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        el, la, lo
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        él, ella, ello
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In this context, it would make sense to say
       <i>
        le vi
       </i>
       "I saw him" for any masculine noun,
       <i>
        la vi
       </i>
       "I saw her/it" for any feminine noun, and
       <i>
        lo vi
       </i>
       "I saw it" when no noun is being referred to. This gives us a set like the above:
       <i>
        le, la, lo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        b) Indirectness for humans — general
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Spanish has a tendency, discussed at
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_prepositions#a" title="Spanish prepositions">
        Spanish prepositions
       </a>
       , to treat as indirect objects those direct objects which happen to refer to people. In this context, it would make sense to say
       <i>
        le/les vi
       </i>
       "I saw him/her/them" when referring to people and
       <i>
        lo/la/los/las vi
       </i>
       "I saw it/them" when referring to things.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        b1) Indirectness for humans — respect for the interlocutor
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The general tendency to use indirect objects for people is intensified when the speaker wishes to convey respect. The third person in Spanish can be used as the second person to mean "you". In this context, it would make sense to use
       <i>
        lo/la/los/las vi
       </i>
       "I saw him/her/it/them" when one is speaking about a third party or an object, but
       <i>
        le/les vi
       </i>
       "I saw you" when the pronoun is intended to represent
       <i>
        usted/ustedes
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        b2) Indirectness for humans — contrast with inanimate things
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The general tendency to use indirect objects for people is intensified when the subject of the sentence is not human, thus creating a contrast in the mind of the speaker between the human and the thing. In this context, it would make sense to say
       <i>
        la halagó
       </i>
       "he flattered her" when the subject is "he" referring to a person, but
       <i>
        le halagó
       </i>
       "it flattered her" when the subject is "it", a thing.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        b3) Indirectness for humans — humanity otherwise emphasised
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The general tendency to use indirect objects for people is intensified when the humanity of the person who is the object of the sentence is emphasised by the way the verb is used. In this context, it would make sense for a subtle distinction to be made between
       <i>
        lo llevamos al hospital
       </i>
       "we took/carried him to the hospital" when the patient is unconscious and
       <i>
        le llevamos al hospital
       </i>
       "we took/led him to the hospital" when the patient is able to walk.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        b4) Indirectness for humans — with impersonal
        <i>
         se
        </i>
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The general tendency to use indirect objects for people is intensified when impersonal
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       is used instead of a real subject. This is to avoid the misinterpretation of the
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       as being an indirect object pronoun. In this context, it would make sense to say
       <i>
        se le lee mucho
       </i>
       "people read him/her a lot" if "
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       " means "people" and "
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       " means "him/her", and reserve
       <i>
        se lo/la lee mucho
       </i>
       "he/she reads it a lot for him/her" for sentences in which the "
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       " is not impersonal.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Direct-object_le.2Fles_in_practice">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Direct-object_le/les_in_practice">
        Direct-object
        <i>
         le/les
        </i>
        in practice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_personal_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Direct-object le/les in practice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       All of the theoretical reasons for using
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       detailed above actually influence the way Spanish speakers use these pronouns, and this has been demonstrated by various surveys and investigations.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Extreme preference for
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       is a dialectalism known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
         leísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       ; however, not all use of direct-object
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       is dialectal. Some instances of it are universal across the educated Spanish-speaking world.
      </p>
      <p>
       Let us first look at dialectal extremes. There is
       <i>
        leísmo
       </i>
       (covered under point
       <b>
        a
       </b>
       above) motivated by the tendency towards masculine
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       in uneducated Madrid speech. This actually used to be quite standard, and the
       <i>
        Real Academia
       </i>
       only stopped endorsing it in the 1850s. We therefore find in old texts:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Unos niegan el hecho, otros
         <u>
          le
         </u>
         afirman
        </i>
        = "Some deny the fact; others assert it" (
        <a href="/wiki/Benito_Jer%C3%B3nimo_Feij%C3%B3o_y_Montenegro" title="Benito Jerónimo Feijóo y Montenegro">
         Feijóo
        </a>
        , mid-eighteenth century)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Such speakers would say
       <i>
        le afirman
       </i>
       in reference to a word like
       <i>
        el hecho
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la afirman
       </i>
       in reference to a word like
       <i>
        la verdad
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        lo afirman
       </i>
       only in reference to a general neuter "it".
      </p>
      <p>
       The second extreme
       <i>
        leísmo
       </i>
       is the one motivated by the second point mentioned: the tendency to use indirect objects for people. This is noticeable in Northwestern Spain, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
        Basque Country
       </a>
       , where regional speech uses
       <i>
        le vi
       </i>
       for "I saw him/her" and
       <i>
        lo/la vi
       </i>
       for "I saw it". The same phenomenon is sporadically heard elsewhere, e.g. in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia_(autonomous_community)" title="Valencia (autonomous community)">
        Valencia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">
        Paraguay
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Now let us look at less extremely dialectal cases. For the majority of educated speakers in Spain and parts of Latin America, neither of the two tendencies (
       <b>
        a
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        b
       </b>
       ) is enough on its own to justify the use of
       <i>
        le/les
       </i>
       ; but
       <i>
        together
       </i>
       they are. Thus, speakers who would reject sentences like
       <i>
        le vi
       </i>
       for "I saw it" and
       <i>
        le vi
       </i>
       for "I saw her" would nevertheless accept and use
       <i>
        le vi
       </i>
       for "I saw him". Indeed, this use of
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       to mean "him" is so common in an area of central Spain that some would call the use of
       <i>
        lo vi
       </i>
       to mean "I saw him" an example of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lo%C3%ADsmo" title="Loísmo">
         loísmo/laísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       , i.e. the dialectalism whereby
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       is overused. The Real Academia's current line is that
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       for "him" is officially "tolerated".
      </p>
      <p>
       A case on which the Academy is silent is the tendency described in point
       <b>
        b1
       </b>
       . It is perfectly common in educated speech in many parts of the world to distinguish between
       <i>
        no quería molestarlo
       </i>
       "I did not mean to bother him" and
       <i>
        no quería molestarle
       </i>
       "I did not mean to bother you". Those Spaniards who would not just say
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       anyway for the reasons explained in the last paragraph are likely to use
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       in this case. Butt &amp; Benjamin (1994) says that their Argentine informants made this distinction, whereas their
       <i>
        loísta
       </i>
       Colombian informants preferred
       <i>
        molestarlo
       </i>
       always.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Academy is also silent on the tendency described in
       <b>
        b2
       </b>
       ; however, it is universal across the Spanish-speaking world. In a questionnaire given to 28 Spaniards in the Madrid region, 90% preferred
       <i>
        la halagó
       </i>
       for "he flattered her" and 87% preferred
       <i>
        le halagó
       </i>
       for "it flattered her". García (1975) reports a similar but less extreme tendency in Buenos Aires: only 14% of García's sample said
       <i>
        él le convenció
       </i>
       for "he convinced him" (the rest said
       <i>
        él lo convenció
       </i>
       ). With an inanimate subject, a slight majority (54%) said
       <i>
        este color no le convence
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       García reports Buenos Aires natives differentiating between
       <i>
        lo llevaron al hospital
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        le llevaron al hospital
       </i>
       depending on how active the patient is, although anecdotal evidence suggests that Argentines are more
       <i>
        loísta
       </i>
       than this, and would prefer
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       in both cases.
      </p>
      <p>
       Point b3 is also backed up by the fact that many Latin Americans distinguish between
       <i>
        le quiero
       </i>
       "I love him" and
       <i>
        lo quiero
       </i>
       "I want him" (or indeed "I want it").
      </p>
