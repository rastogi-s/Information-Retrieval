      <p>
       <b>
        Ecclesiastical Latin
       </b>
       , also called
       <b>
        Liturgical Latin
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Church Latin
       </b>
       , is the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       that is used in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_rite" title="Roman rite">
        Roman
       </a>
       and the other
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_liturgical_rites" title="Latin liturgical rites">
        Latin rites
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
        Catholic Church
       </a>
       , as well as in the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglicanism" title="Anglicanism">
        Anglican Churches
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lutheranism" title="Lutheranism">
        Lutheran Churches
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Methodism" title="Methodism">
        Methodist Churches
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossLivingstone2005_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CrossLivingstone2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Rite_Orthodoxy" title="Western Rite Orthodoxy">
        Western Rite
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">
        Eastern Orthodox Church
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antiochian_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Antiochian-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">
        liturgical
       </a>
       purposes. It is distinguished from
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        Classical Latin
       </a>
       by some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_form" title="Lexical form">
        lexical
       </a>
       variations, a simplified syntax and a pronunciation that is based on
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Ecclesiastical Latin that is used in theological works, liturgical rites and dogmatic proclamations varies in style: syntactically simple in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vulgate_Bible" title="Vulgate Bible">
        Vulgate Bible
       </a>
       , hieratic in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Canon" title="Roman Canon">
        Roman Canon
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
        Mass
       </a>
       , terse and technical in Aquinas'
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Summa_Theologica" title="Summa Theologica">
         Summa Theologica
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciceronian" title="Ciceronian">
        Ciceronian
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_John_Paul_II" title="Pope John Paul II">
        Pope John Paul II
       </a>
       's encyclical letter
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fides_et_Ratio" title="Fides et Ratio">
         Fides et Ratio
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Ecclesiastical Latin is the
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official language
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">
        Holy See
       </a>
       and the only surviving
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolect" title="Sociolect">
        sociolect
       </a>
       of spoken Latin.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Church issued the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dogmatic_definition" title="Dogmatic definition">
        dogmatic definitions
       </a>
       of the first seven
       <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">
        General Councils
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       . Even in Rome, Greek remained at first the language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">
        liturgy
       </a>
       and the language in which the first
       <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">
        popes
       </a>
       wrote. During the Late Republic and the Early Empire, educated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_citizens" title="Roman citizens">
        Roman citizens
       </a>
       were generally fluent in Greek, but state business was conducted in Latin.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">
        Holy See
       </a>
       could change its official language from Latin. The chances of that happening are remote since the fact that Latin
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_language" title="Dead language">
        is no longer in common use
       </a>
       makes the meaning of its words less likely to change radically from century to century. Since Latin is spoken as a native language by no modern community, the language is considered a universal, internally-consistent means of communication, without regional bias.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Especially since
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">
        Vatican II
       </a>
       of 1962–1965, the Church no longer uses Latin as the exclusive language of the Roman and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ambrosian_rite" title="Ambrosian rite">
        Ambrosian
       </a>
       liturgies of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_liturgical_rites" title="Latin liturgical rites">
        Latin rites
       </a>
       of the Catholic Church. As early as 1913, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Encyclopedia" title="Catholic Encyclopedia">
         Catholic Encyclopedia
        </a>
       </i>
       commented that Latin was starting to be replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       languages. However, the Church still produces its official liturgical texts in Latin, which provide a single clear point of reference for translations into all other languages. The same holds for the official texts of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">
        canon law
       </a>
       and for all other doctrinal and pastoral communications and directives of the Holy See (and the Pope), such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_encyclical" title="Papal encyclical">
        encyclical letters
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">
         motu propriae
        </a>
       </i>
       , and declarations
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ex_cathedra" title="Ex cathedra">
         ex cathedra
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       After the use of Latin as an everyday language died out, even among scholars, the Holy See has for some centuries usually drafted papal other documents in a modern language, but the authoritative text, the one published in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Acta_Apostolicae_Sedis" title="Acta Apostolicae Sedis">
         Acta Apostolicae Sedis
        </a>
       </i>
       , generally appears in Latin, even if the translation is available only later. For example, the writers of the
       <a href="/wiki/Catechism_of_the_Catholic_Church" title="Catechism of the Catholic Church">
        Catechism of the Catholic Church
       </a>
       drafted it in French, the language in which it appeared in 1992. The Latin text appeared only five years later, in 1997, and the French text was corrected to match the Latin version. The Latin-language department of the Vatican Secretariat of State (formerly the
       <i>
        Secretaria brevium ad principes et epistolarum latinarum
       </i>
       ) is charged with the preparation in Latin of papal and curial documents.
      </p>
      <p>
       Occasionally, the official texts are published in a modern language, including such well-known texts as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Motu_proprio" title="Motu proprio">
         motu proprio
        </a>
       </i>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tra_le_sollecitudini" title="Tra le sollecitudini">
         Tra le sollecitudini
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (1903) by
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_X" title="Pope Pius X">
        Pope Pius X
       </a>
       (in Italian) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mit_brennender_Sorge" title="Mit brennender Sorge">
         Mit brennender Sorge
        </a>
       </i>
       (1937) by
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Pius_XI" title="Pope Pius XI">
        Pope Pius XI
       </a>
       (in German).
      </p>
      <p>
       The rule now in force on the use of Latin in the
       <a href="/wiki/Eucharist" title="Eucharist">
        Eucharistic
       </a>
       liturgy of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">
        Roman Rite
       </a>
       states
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
        Mass
       </a>
       is celebrated either in Latin or in another language, provided that the
       <a href="/wiki/Liturgy" title="Liturgy">
        liturgical
       </a>
       texts used have been approved according to the norm of law. Except for celebrations of the Mass that are scheduled by the ecclesiastical authorities to take place in the language of the people, priests are always and everywhere permitted to celebrate Mass in Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After the
       <a href="/wiki/Reformation" title="Reformation">
        Reformation
       </a>
       , in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lutheran_Church" title="Lutheran Church">
        Lutheran Churches
       </a>
       , Latin was retained as the language of the Mass for weekdays, although for the Sunday Sabbath, the
       <a href="/wiki/Deutsche_Messe" title="Deutsche Messe">
        Deutsche Messe
       </a>
       was to be said.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waquet2002_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Waquet2002-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
        Geneva
       </a>
       , among the
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_Reformed_church" title="Continental Reformed church">
        Reformed Churches
       </a>
       , "persons called before the
       <a href="/wiki/Consistory_(Protestantism)" title="Consistory (Protestantism)">
        consistory
       </a>
       to prove their faith answered by reciting the
       <i>
        Paternoster
       </i>
       , the
       <i>
        Ave Maria
       </i>
       , and the
       <i>
        Credo
       </i>
       in Latin."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waquet2002_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Waquet2002-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Church_of_England" title="Church of England">
        Anglican Church
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Common_Prayer" title="Book of Common Prayer">
        Book of Common Prayer
       </a>
       was published in Latin, alongside English.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossLivingstone2005_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-CrossLivingstone2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Wesley" title="John Wesley">
        John Wesley
       </a>
       , the founder of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Methodist_Church" title="Methodist Church">
        Methodist Churches
       </a>
       , "used Latin text in doctrinal writings",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossLivingstone2005_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-CrossLivingstone2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Luther" title="Martin Luther">
        Martin Luther
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/John_Calvin" title="John Calvin">
        John Calvin
       </a>
       did in their era.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossLivingstone2005_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-CrossLivingstone2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the training of Protestant clergy in
       <a href="/wiki/W%C3%BCrttemberg" title="Württemberg">
        Württemberg
       </a>
       , as well as in the
       <a href="/wiki/Rhineland" title="Rhineland">
        Rhineland
       </a>
       , universities instructed divinity students in Latin and their examinations were conducted in this language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waquet2002_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Waquet2002-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The University of Montauban under Reformed auspcices, required that seminarians complete two theses, with one being in Latin and as such, Reformed ministers were "Latinist by training", comparable to Roman Catholic seminarians.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waquet2002_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Waquet2002-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_Classical_Latin">
        Comparison with Classical Latin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Comparison with Classical Latin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Written Church Latin does not differ radically from
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        Classical Latin
       </a>
       . The study of the language of
       <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
        Cicero
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">
        Virgil
       </a>
       is adequate to understand Church Latin. However, those interested only in ecclesiastical texts may prefer to limit the time they devote to ancient authors, whose vocabulary covers matters of importance in that period but appear less frequently in Church documents.
      </p>
      <p>
       In many countries, those who speak Latin for liturgical or other ecclesiastical purposes use the pronunciation that has become traditional in Rome by giving the letters the value they have in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       but without distinguishing between
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_phonology#Vowels" title="Italian phonology">
        open and close "E" and "O"
       </a>
       . "AE" and "OE" coalesce with "E"; before them and "I", "C" and "G" are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡ʃ/
       </span>
       (English "CH") and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       (English "J"), respectively. "TI" before a vowel is generally pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tsi/
       </span>
       (unless preceded by "S", "T" or "X"). Such speakers pronounce consonantal "V" (not written as "U") as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       as in English, and double consonants are pronounced as such. The distinction in Classical Latin between long and short vowels is ignored, and instead of the 'macron', a horizontal line to mark the long vowel, an acute accent is used for stress. The first syllable of two-syllable words is stressed; in longer words, an acute accent is placed over the stressed vowel: adorémus 'let us adore'; Dómini 'of the Lord'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ecclesiastics in some countries follow slightly different traditions. For instance, in Slavic and German-speaking countries, "C" commonly receives the value of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/
       </span>
       before "E" and"I", and speakers pronounce "G" in all positions hard, never as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       (English "J") . (See also
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_regional_pronunciation" title="Latin regional pronunciation">
        Latin regional pronunciation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_spelling_and_pronunciation#Ecclesiastical_pronunciation" title="Latin spelling and pronunciation">
        Latin spelling and pronunciation: Ecclesiastical pronunciation
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_materials">
        Language materials
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language materials">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The complete text of the Bible in Latin, the revised Vulgate, appears at Nova Vulgata - Bibliorum Sacrorum Editio.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another site
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       gives the entire Bible, in the Douay version, verse by verse, accompanied by the Vulgate Latin of each verse.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1976, the
       <a href="/wiki/Latinitas_Foundation" title="Latinitas Foundation">
        Latinitas Foundation
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <i>
        Opus Fundatum Latinitas
       </i>
       in Latin) was established by
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Paul_VI" title="Pope Paul VI">
        Pope Paul VI
       </a>
       to promote the study and use of Latin. Its headquarters are in
       <a href="/wiki/Vatican_City" title="Vatican City">
        Vatican City
       </a>
       . The foundation publishes an
       <a href="/wiki/Eponym" title="Eponym">
        eponymous
       </a>
       quarterly in Latin. Other initiatives of the Latinitas Foundation include the publication, in Italian, of the 15,000-word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lexicon_Recentis_Latinitatis" title="Lexicon Recentis Latinitatis">
         Lexicon Recentis Latinitatis
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Dictionary of Recent Latin
       </i>
       ), which indicates Latin terms to use in referring to modern ideas, such as a bicycle (
       <i>
        birota
       </i>
       ), a cigarette (
       <i>
        fistula nicotiana
       </i>
       ), a computer (
       <i>
        instrumentum computatorium
       </i>
       ), a cowboy (
       <i>
        armentarius
       </i>
       ), a motel (
       <i>
        deversorium autocineticum
       </i>
       ), shampoo (
       <i>
        capitilavium
       </i>
       ), a strike (
       <i>
        operistitium
       </i>
       ), a terrorist (
       <i>
        tromocrates
       </i>
       ), a trademark (
       <i>
        ergasterii nota
       </i>
       ), an unemployed person (
       <i>
        invite otiosus
       </i>
       ), a waltz (
       <i>
        chorea Vindobonensis
       </i>
       ), and even a miniskirt (
       <i>
        tunicula minima
       </i>
       ) and hot pants (
       <i>
        brevissimae bracae femineae
       </i>
       ). Some 600 such terms extracted from the book appear on a page
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the Vatican website.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_use">
        Current use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ecclesiastical_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Current use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Latin remains the official language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">
        Holy See
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Rite" title="Roman Rite">
        Roman Rite
       </a>
       of the Catholic Church.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Until the 1960s and still later in Roman colleges like the Gregorian, Roman Catholic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Priests" title="Priests">
        priests
       </a>
       studied theology using Latin textbooks, and the language of instruction in many
       <a href="/wiki/Seminary" title="Seminary">
        seminaries
       </a>
       was also Latin, which was seen as the language of the Church Fathers. The use of Latin in
       <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">
        pedagogy
       </a>
       and in theological research, however, has since declined. Nevertheless,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">
        canon law
       </a>
       requires for seminary formation to provide for a thorough training in Latin,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though "the use of Latin in seminaries and pontifical universities has now dwindled to the point of extinction."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2005CrossLivingstone_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2005CrossLivingstone-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Latin was still spoken in recent international gatherings of Roman Catholic leaders, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">
        Second Vatican Council
       </a>
       , and it is still used at
       <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave" title="Papal conclave">
        conclaves
       </a>
       to elect a new
       <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">
        Pope
       </a>
       . The Tenth Ordinary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Assembly_of_the_Synod_of_Bishops" title="General Assembly of the Synod of Bishops">
        General Assembly of the Synod of Bishops
       </a>
       in 2004 was the most recent to have a Latin-language group for discussions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although Latin is the traditional liturgical language of the Roman (Latin) Church, the liturgical use of the vernacular has predominated since the liturgical reforms that followed the Vatican II:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_law_(Catholic_Church)" title="Canon law (Catholic Church)">
        liturgical law
       </a>
       for the Latin Church states that
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
        Mass
       </a>
       may be celebrated either in Latin or another language in which the liturgical texts, translated from Latin, have been legitimately approved.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The permission granted for continued use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tridentine_Mass" title="Tridentine Mass">
        Tridentine Mass
       </a>
       in its 1962 form authorizes use of the vernacular language in proclaiming the Scripture readings after they are first read in Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In historic Protestant Churches, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglican_Communion" title="Anglican Communion">
        Anglican Communion
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Lutheran_Federation" title="World Lutheran Federation">
        Lutheran Churches
       </a>
       , Ecclesiastical Latin is often employed in sung celebrations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
        Mass
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CrossLivingstone2005_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-CrossLivingstone2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

             The Second Vatican Council declared that the use of Latin was to be maintained the liturgy, though permission was granted for some use of the vernacular; in the outcome, the use of the vernacular has almost entirely triumphed, although the official books continue to be published in Latin. In the C of E the Latin versions of the Book of Common Prayer have never been widely used, though, for instance, John Wesley used Latin text in doctrinal writings. The option of using traditional Latin texts in sung worship has been retained by choirs in both the Anglican and Lutheran Churches.
            </q>
