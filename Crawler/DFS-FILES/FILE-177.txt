      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Antarctic Circle
       </b>
       is the most southerly of the five major
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        circles of latitude
       </a>
       that mark maps of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The region south of this circle is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       , and the zone immediately to the north is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_zone#Temperate_Zones" title="Geographical zone">
        Southern Temperate Zone
       </a>
       . South of the Antarctic Circle, the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        sun
       </a>
       is above the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       for 24 continuous
       <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">
        hours
       </a>
       at least once per year (and therefore visible at
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">
        midnight
       </a>
       ) and (at least partially) below the horizon for 24 continuous hours at least once per year (and therefore not fully visible at
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        noon
       </a>
       ); this is also true within the equivalent
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">
        polar circle
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">
        Arctic Circle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The position of the Antarctic Circle is not fixed; as of 28 January 2018, it runs 66°33′47.0″ south of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its latitude depends on the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       , which fluctuates within a margin of 2° over a 40,000-year period, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       resulting from the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        orbit of the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Consequently, the Antarctic Circle is currently
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude#Movement_of_the_Tropical_and_Polar_Circles" title="Circle of latitude">
        drifting southwards
       </a>
       at a speed of about 15 m (49 ft) per year.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Midnight_sun_and_polar_night">
        Midnight sun and polar night
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Midnight sun and polar night">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="186" data-file-width="512" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/400px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/600px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/800px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Relationship of Earth's axial tilt (ε) to the tropical and polar circles
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        Midnight sun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        Polar night
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Antarctic Circle is the northernmost latitude in the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern Hemisphere
       </a>
       at which the sun can remain continuously above or partially below the horizon for twenty-four hours; as a result, at least once each year at any location within the Antarctic Circle the sun is
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        visible at local midnight
       </a>
       , and at least once it is partially obscured at local noon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Directly on the Antarctic Circle these events occur, in principle, exactly once per year: at the
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstices
       </a>
       , respectively. However, because of
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        atmospheric refraction
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">
        mirages
       </a>
       , and because the sun appears as a disk and not a point, part of the midnight sun may be seen on the night of the southern
       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">
        summer solstice
       </a>
       up to about 50
       <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">
        minutes (′)
       </a>
       (90 km (56 mi)) north of the Antarctic Circle; similarly, on the day of the southern
       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       , part of the sun may be seen up to about 50′ south of the Antarctic Circle. That is true at sea level; those limits increase with elevation above sea level, although in mountainous regions there is often no direct view of the true horizon. Mirages on the Antarctic continent tend to be even more spectacular than in Arctic regions, creating, for example, a series of apparent sunsets and sunrises while in reality the sun remains under the horizon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_habitation">
        Human habitation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antarctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human habitation">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="576" data-file-width="1000" height="127" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG/220px-Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG/330px-Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG/440px-Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antarctic_Circle_Icberg.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An
         <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">
          iceberg
         </a>
         near the Antarctic Circle north of
         <a href="/wiki/Detaille_Island" title="Detaille Island">
          Detaille Island
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Demographics_of_Antarctica" title="Demographics of Antarctica">
        Demographics of Antarctica
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There is no permanent human population south of the Antarctic Circle, but there are several
       <a href="/wiki/Research_stations_in_Antarctica" title="Research stations in Antarctica">
        research stations in Antarctica
       </a>
       operated by various nations that are inhabited by teams of scientists who rotate on a seasonal basis. In previous centuries some semi-permanent
       <a href="/wiki/Whaling" title="Whaling">
        whaling
       </a>
       stations were established on the continent, and some whalers would live there for a year or more. At least three children have been born in Antarctica, albeit in stations north of the Antarctic Circle.
      </p>

      <p>
       The Antarctic Circle is roughly 17,662 kilometres (10,975 mi) long.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (7,700,000 sq mi) and covers roughly 4% of Earth's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continent
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       covers much of the area within the Antarctic Circle.
      </p>
