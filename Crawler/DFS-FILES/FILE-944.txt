      <p>
       <b>
        Zarphatic
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        Judeo-French
       </b>
       (Zarphatic:
       <i>
        Tzarfatit
       </i>
       ), is an
       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">
        extinct
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_language" title="Jewish language">
        Jewish language
       </a>
       that was spoken by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Jews" title="French Jews">
        French Jews
       </a>
       of northern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       and in parts of west-central
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mainz" title="Mainz">
        Mainz
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frankfurt_am_Main" title="Frankfurt am Main">
        Frankfurt am Main
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aix-la-Chapelle" title="Aix-la-Chapelle">
        Aix-la-Chapelle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zarphatic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Zarphatic
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       name for France,
       <i>
        Tzarfat
       </i>
       (צרפת), which had been used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
       name for the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Sarepta" title="Sarepta">
        Sarepta
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">
        Phoenicia
       </a>
       . Some have conjectured that the language was the original language of the Jews who eventually adopted
       <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
        Old High German
       </a>
       , which led to the development of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">
        Yiddish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was written by a modified
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_script" title="Hebrew script">
        Hebrew script
       </a>
       and first appeared in the 11th century, in
       <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">
        glosses
       </a>
       to texts of the Hebrew Bible and the
       <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">
        Talmud
       </a>
       written by the great
       <a href="/wiki/Rabbi" title="Rabbi">
        rabbis
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
        Rashi
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moshe_HaDarshan" title="Moshe HaDarshan">
        Moshe HaDarshan
       </a>
       . Constant expulsions and persecutions, resulting in great waves of Jewish migration, brought about the extinction of Zarphatic by the end of the 14th century.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">
        Writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zarphatic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One feature, unlike most other
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European
       </a>
       Jewish languages, was that to represent
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowel
       </a>
       sounds, instead of using Hebrew letters with no matching
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       in the language, it made extensive use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tiberian_vocalization" title="Tiberian vocalization">
        Tiberian
       </a>
       system of
       <a href="/wiki/Niqqud" title="Niqqud">
        vocalisation
       </a>
       to indicate the full range of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       vowels.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Zarphatic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Another interesting feature was that its vocabulary had relatively few Hebrew
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       , unlike almost all other
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_languages" title="Jewish languages">
        Jewish languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
