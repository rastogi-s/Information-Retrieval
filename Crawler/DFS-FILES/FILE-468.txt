   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Topicalization
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Topicalization
       </b>
       is a mechanism of syntax that establishes an expression as the sentence or clause
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topic_(linguistics)" title="Topic (linguistics)">
        topic
       </a>
       ; in English, by having it appear at the front of the sentence or clause (as opposed to in a canonical position further to the right). Topicalization often results in a
       <a href="/wiki/Discontinuity_(linguistics)" title="Discontinuity (linguistics)">
        discontinuity
       </a>
       and is thus one of a number of established discontinuity types (the other three being
       <a href="/wiki/Wh-movement" title="Wh-movement">
        <i>
         wh
        </i>
        -fronting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Scrambling_(linguistics)" title="Scrambling (linguistics)">
        scrambling
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Extraposition" title="Extraposition">
        extraposition
       </a>
       ). Topicalization is also used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Constituent_(linguistics)" title="Constituent (linguistics)">
        constituency test
       </a>
       ; an expression that can be topicalized is deemed a constituent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The topicalization of
       <a href="/wiki/Argument_(linguistics)" title="Argument (linguistics)">
        arguments
       </a>
       in English is rare, whereas circumstantial
       <a href="/wiki/Adjunct_(grammar)" title="Adjunct (grammar)">
        adjuncts
       </a>
       are often topicalized. Most languages allow topicalization, and in some languages, topicalization occurs much more frequently and/or in a much less
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        marked
       </a>
       manner than in English. Topicalization in English has also received attention in the pragmatics literature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Topicalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Typical cases of topicalization are illustrated with the following examples:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. The boys roll rocks
          <b>
           for entertainment
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           For entertainment
          </b>
          , the boys roll rocks.
          <small>
           -Topicalization of the adjunct
           <i>
            for entertainment
           </i>
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. Everyone refused to answer
          <b>
           because the pressure was too great
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           Because the pressure was too great
          </b>
          , everyone refused to answer.
          <small>
           - Topicalization of the adjunct
           <i>
            because the pressure was too great
           </i>
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. I won't eat
          <b>
           that pizza
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           That pizza
          </b>
          , I won't eat.
          <small>
           - Topicalization of the object argument
           <i>
            that pizza
           </i>
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. I am terrified of
          <b>
           those dogs
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           Those dogs
          </b>
          , I am terrified of.
          <small>
           - Topicalization of the object argument
           <i>
            those dogs
           </i>
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Assuming that the a-sentences represent canonical word order, the b-sentences contain instances of topicalization. The constituent in bold is fronted in order to establish it as topic. The two former examples with a topicalized adjunct are typical, whereas the two latter examples with a topicalized object argument are comparatively rare. The appearance of the demonstrative determiners
       <i>
        that
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        those
       </i>
       is important, since without them, topicalization of an argument seems less acceptable, e.g. ?
       <i>
        A pizza I won't eat
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Topicalization can occur across long distances, e.g.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. I thought you said that Tom believes the explanation needs
          <b>
           such examples
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           Such examples
          </b>
          I thought you said that Tom believes the explanation needs.
          <small>
           - Topicalization of the object argument
           <i>
            such examples
           </i>
           over a long distance
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Further_examples">
        Further examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Topicalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Further examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Topicalization is similar to
       <i>
        wh
       </i>
       -movement insofar as the constituents that can be
       <i>
        wh
       </i>
       -fronted can also be topicalized, e.g.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. Bill is living in
          <b>
           that one house on the hill
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           Which house
          </b>
          is Bill living in? -
          <small>
           <i>
            Wh
           </i>
           -fronting of NP resulting in preposition stranding
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          c.
          <b>
           That one house on the hill
          </b>
          Bill is living in. -
          <small>
           Topicalization of NP resulting in preposition stranding
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. Shelly has indeed uncovered part of
          <b>
           our plan
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b.
          <b>
           What
          </b>
          has Shelly indeed uncovered part of?
          <small>
           -
           <i>
            Wh
           </i>
           -fronting out of object NP resulting in preposition stranding
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          c.
          <b>
           Our plan
          </b>
          Shelly has indeed uncovered part of.
          <small>
           - Topicalization out of object NP resulting in preposition stranding
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       And topicalization is similar to
       <i>
        wh
       </i>
       -fronting insofar as the islands and barriers to
       <i>
        wh
       </i>
       -fronting are also islands and barriers to topicalization, e.g.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. The description of
          <b>
           his aunt
          </b>
          was really funny.
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b. *
          <b>
           Whose aunt
          </b>
          was the description of really funny? -
          <small>
           <i>
            Wh
           </i>
           -fronting impossible out of a subject in English
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          c. *
          <b>
           His aunt
          </b>
          the description of was really funny. -
          <small>
           Topicalization impossible out of a subject in English
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. He relaxes after he's played
          <b>
           Starcraft
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b. *
          <b>
           What
          </b>
          does he relax after he's played?
          <small>
           -
           <i>
            Wh
           </i>
           -fronting impossible out of adjunct clause
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          c. *
          <b>
           Starcraft
          </b>
          he relaxes after he's played.
          <small>
           - Topicalization impossible out of adjunct clause
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. She approves of the suggestion to make
          <b>
           pasta
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b. *
          <b>
           What
          </b>
          does she approve of the suggestion to make?
          <small>
           -
           <i>
            Wh
           </i>
           -fronting impossible out of complex NP
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          c. *
          <b>
           Pasta
          </b>
          she approves of the suggestion to make.
          <small>
           - Topicalization impossible out of complex NP
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       These examples illustrate the similar behavior of topicalization and
       <i>
        wh
       </i>
       -fronting. Further data, which will not be produced here, could show, however, that topicalization is unlike the other two major
       <a href="/wiki/Discontinuity_(linguistics)" title="Discontinuity (linguistics)">
        discontinuity
       </a>
       types, i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Scrambling_(linguistics)" title="Scrambling (linguistics)">
        scrambling
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Extraposition" title="Extraposition">
        extraposition
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_analyses">
        Theoretical analyses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Topicalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Theoretical analyses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The theoretical analysis of topicalization can vary greatly depending in part on the theory of sentence structure that one adopts. If one assumes the layered structures associated with many
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">
        phrase structure grammars
       </a>
       , all instances of topicalization will involve a discontinuity. If, in contrast, less layered structures are assumed as for example in
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammar
       </a>
       , then many instances of topicalization do not involve a discontinuity, but rather just
       <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(linguistics)" title="Inversion (linguistics)">
        inversion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This point is illustrated here first using flatter structures that lack a finite VP-constituent (which means the entire sentence has the status of a large VP). Both constituency- and dependency-based analyses are given. The example itself is a piece of Yoda wisdom (as he speaks to Anakin), and is certainly of questionable acceptability in this regard. It is, however, perfectly understandable:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:E-top-01.jpg" title="Topicalization in flat constituency and dependency structures">
           <img alt="Topicalization in flat constituency and dependency structures" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="727" height="384" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/97/E-top-01.jpg" width="727"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The upper two trees show the analysis using flat constituency-based structures that lack a finite VP
       <a href="/wiki/Constituent_(linguistics)" title="Constituent (linguistics)">
        constituent
       </a>
       , and the lower two trees are dependency-based,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereby dependency inherently rejects the existence of finite VP-constituents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The noteworthy aspect of these examples is that topicalization does not result in a discontinuity, since there are no crossing lines in the trees. What this means is that such cases can be analyzed purely in terms of inversion. The topicalized expression simply "inverts" to the other side of its head.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Instead of the flat trees just examined, most constituency grammars posit more layered structures that include a finite VP constituent. These more layered structures are likely to address topicalization in terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_movement" title="Syntactic movement">
        movement
       </a>
       or copying, as illustrated with the following two trees:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:E-top-02.jpg" title="Topicalization in layered constituency structures">
           <img alt="Topicalization in layered constituency structures" data-file-height="289" data-file-width="815" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6a/E-top-02.jpg" width="815"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Tree a. shows the canonical word order again, and tree b. illustrates what is known as the movement or copying analysis. The topicalized expression is first generated in its canonical position but is then copied to the front of the sentence, the original then being deleted.
      </p>
      <p>
       The movement analysis of discontinuities is one possible way to address those instances of topicalization that cannot be explained in terms of inversion. An alternative explanation is feature passing. One assumes that the topicalized expression is not moved or copied to the clause-initial position, but rather it is "base" generated there. Instead of movement, there is feature passing, however.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A link of a sort is established between the topicalized expression and its
       <a href="/wiki/Government_(linguistics)" title="Government (linguistics)">
        governor
       </a>
       . The link is the path along which information about the topicalized expression is passed to the governor of that expression. A piece of Yoda wisdom is again used for illustration, the full sentence being
       <i>
        Careful you must be when sensing the future, Anakin
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:E-top-03.jpg" title="Feature passing (constituency and dependency)">
           <img alt="Feature passing (constituency and dependency)" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="481" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/12/E-top-03.jpg" width="481"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The nodes in red mark the path of feature passing. Features (=information) about the topicalized expression are passed rightward through (and down) the tree structure to the governor of that expression. This path is present in both analyses, i.e. in the constituency-based a-analysis on the left and in the dependency-based b-analysis on the right. Since topicalization can occur over long distances, feature passing must also occur over long distances. The final example shows a dependency-based analysis of a sentence where the feature passing path is quite long:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:E-top-04.jpg" title="Long distance feature passing (dependency)">
           <img alt="Long distance feature passing (dependency)" data-file-height="312" data-file-width="732" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/88/E-top-04.jpg" width="732"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Information about the topicalized
       <i>
        such nonsense
       </i>
       is passed along the path marked in red down to the governor of the topicalized expression
       <i>
        spouting
       </i>
       . The words corresponding to the nodes in red form a
       <a href="/wiki/Catena_(linguistics)" title="Catena (linguistics)">
        catena
       </a>
       (Latin for 'chain', plural
       <i>
        catenae
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A theory of topicalization is then built up in part by examining the nature of these catenae for feature passing.
      </p>
