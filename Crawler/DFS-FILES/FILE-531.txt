      <p>
       The question about the extent of the domination of the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Internet" title="Internet">
        Internet
       </a>
       has been historically, and is still, a controversial matter and in any case the relative representation of languages in the network is a fast changing data, although it is considered that amongst the more than 6000 existing languages less than 500(only 8.33% of total) have a digital existence as of today
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-towards_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-towards-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UofCLBWApril2013_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UofCLBWApril2013-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12years_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-12years-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The two main indicators of languages on the Internet are the language of users of the Internet and the language of contents in the Internet.
      </p>
      <p>
       The data about languages can be specified either as related only to
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        mother tongue
       </a>
       (also referred to as first language and noted L1) or as related to first language plus
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       spoken (L1+L2). Data on second languages are far from being consensual and the differences are one of the main cause of discrepancy between data on languages used on the Internet.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In terms of users, there is a consensus to state that the top 3 languages are respectively
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
         Chinese
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ; beyond the consensus is lost.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In terms of contents, there is no consensus on the order of languages beyond the fact that
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        is still the first language in terms of contents, although the value of the corresponding percentage varies greatly depending on the source (from 32% to 52%).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <b>
        As for the language of users
       </b>
       , the main and most reliable source for persons connected to the Internet by country is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Telecommunications_Union" title="International Telecommunications Union">
        ITU
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ITU_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ITU-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . From this
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nation's
       </a>
       authoritative source, two sources derive the persons connected by language, with some differences:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        According to InternetWorldStats, the 10 top languages in terms of connected users are respectively:
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
         Chinese
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
         Arabic
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">
         Malay
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
         Japanese
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
         Russian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        . Besides, the source offer Internet statistics per country and region on various aspects, including some applications.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        According to the FUNREDES/MAAYA Observatory's last study
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alternative_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Alternative-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        , the 10 top languages are, respectively:
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">

        <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">

        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">

        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">

        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">

        </a>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">

        . Besides, the study offer a set of more detailed indicators for the 140 languages with more than 5 millions speakers.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The differences between the figures seems to be related to the data about second languages and to the computing of the L1+L2 populations per language.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        As for the language of contents
       </b>
       , two sources exist and they present important differences.
      </p>
      <ul>

      <p>
       FUNREDES/MAAYA observatory argues that using Alexa ranking for the 10 millions sample of websites on which W3Tech applies a language recognition algorithm provokes a huge under-estimation of many Asiatic languages, primarily Chinese and languages from India. In the referenced paper and associated presentations arguments are developed and warnings are made on the importance of
       <a href="/wiki/Bias_(statistics)" title="Bias (statistics)">
        biases
       </a>
       in the measure of languages on the Internet.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Languages_used">
        Languages used
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_used_on_the_Internet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Languages used">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is debate over the most-used languages on the Internet. A 2009 UNESCO report monitored the languages of websites for 12 years from 1996 to 2008 found a steady year-on-year decline in the percentage of webpages in English from 75 percent in 1998 to 45 percent in 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12years_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-12years-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The authors found that English remained at 45 percent of content for 2005 to the end of the study, but believe this was due to the bias of search engines indexing more English-language content rather than a true stabilization of the percentage of content in English online.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12years_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-12years-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ongoing monitoring by W3Techs showed that in March 2015, just over 55 percent of the most visited websites had English-language homepages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UofCLBWApril2013_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-UofCLBWApril2013-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other top languages that are used at least in 2 percent of the one million most visited websites according to W3Techs are
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UofCLBWApril2013_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-UofCLBWApril2013-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The figures from the W3Techs study are based on the one million most visited websites (i.e., approximately 0.27 percent of all websites according to December 2011 figures) as ranked by
       <a href="/wiki/Alexa_Internet" title="Alexa Internet">
        Alexa.com
       </a>
       , and language is identified using only the home page of the sites in most cases (i.e., all of Wikipedia is based on the language detection of
       <a class="external free" href="http://www.wikipedia.org">
        http://www.wikipedia.org
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W3TechsDetails_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-W3TechsDetails-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a consequence, the figures show a significantly higher percentage for many languages (especially for English) as compared to the figures for all websites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Alternative_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Alternative-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The figures for all websites are unknown, but some sources estimate below 50 percent for English; see for instance,
       <i>
        Towards a multilingual cyberspace
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-towards_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-towards-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the 2009 UNESCO report
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12years_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-12years-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       referenced earlier.
      </p>
      <p>
       The number of non-English pages is rapidly expanding. The use of English online increased by around 281 percent from 2001 to 2011, a lower rate of growth than that of Spanish (743 percent), Chinese (1,277 percent), Russian (1,826 percent) or Arabic (2,501 percent) over the same period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to a 2000 study, the international auxiliary language
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       ranked 40 out of all languages in search engine queries, also ranking 27 out of all languages that rely on the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
