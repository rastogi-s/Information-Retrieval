      <p>
       A
       <b>
        rotation
       </b>
       is a circular movement of an object around a center (or point) of rotation. A three-dimensional object can always be rotated around an infinite number of imaginary lines called
       <i>
        rotation axes
       </i>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          AK
         </span>
         -seez
        </i>
       </a>
       ). If the axis passes through the body's center of mass, the body is said to rotate upon itself, or spin. A rotation about an external point, e.g. the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       about the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , is called a revolution or
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbital
       </a>
       revolution, typically when it is produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       . The axis is called a
       <b>
        pole
       </b>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        Mathematically
       </a>
       , a rotation is a
       <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">
        rigid body
       </a>
       movement which, unlike a
       <a href="/wiki/Translation_(geometry)" title="Translation (geometry)">
        translation
       </a>
       , keeps a point fixed. This definition applies to rotations within both two and three dimensions (in a plane and in space, respectively.)
      </p>
      <p>
       All rigid body movements are rotations, translations, or combinations of the two.
      </p>
      <p>
       A rotation is simply a progressive radial orientation to a common point. That common point lies within the axis of that motion. The axis is 90 degrees perpendicular to the plane of the motion. If the axis of the rotation lies external of the body in question then the body is said to orbit. There is no fundamental difference between a “rotation” and an “orbit” and or "spin". The key distinction is simply where the axis of the rotation lies, either within or outside of a body in question. This distinction can be demonstrated for both “rigid” and “non rigid” bodies.
      </p>
      <p>
       If a rotation around a point or axis is followed by a second rotation around the same point/axis, a third rotation results. The reverse (
       <a href="/wiki/Inverse_element" title="Inverse element">
        inverse
       </a>
       ) of a rotation is also a rotation. Thus, the rotations around a point/axis form a
       <a href="/wiki/Group_(mathematics)" title="Group (mathematics)">
        group
       </a>
       . However, a rotation around a point or axis and a rotation around a different point/axis may result in something other than a rotation, e.g. a translation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rotations around the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       axes are called
       <i>
        principal rotations
       </i>
       . Rotation around any axis can be performed by taking a rotation around the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       axis, followed by a rotation around the
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       axis, and followed by a rotation around the
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       axis. That is to say, any spatial rotation can be decomposed into a combination of principal rotations.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">
        flight dynamics
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">
        principal rotations
       </a>
       are known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaw,_pitch,_and_roll" title="Yaw, pitch, and roll">
        <i>
         yaw
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         pitch
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         roll
        </i>
       </a>
       (known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tait%E2%80%93Bryan_angles" title="Tait–Bryan angles">
        Tait–Bryan angles
       </a>
       ). This terminology is also used in
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_graphics" title="Computer graphics">
        computer graphics
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">
        <span id="astronomy">
        </span>
        Astronomy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia,_Chile.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2667" data-file-width="4000" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg/220px-Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg/330px-Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg/440px-Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia%2C_Chile.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Star_trails_over_the_Paranal_Residencia,_Chile.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Star_trail" title="Star trail">
          Star trails
         </a>
         caused by the
         <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
          Earth's rotation
         </a>
         during the
         <a href="/wiki/Long-exposure_photography" title="Long-exposure photography">
          camera's long exposure
         </a>
         time.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , rotation is a commonly observed phenomenon.
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        Stars
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       and similar bodies all spin around on their axes. The rotation rate of planets in the solar system was first measured by tracking visual features.
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_rotation" title="Stellar rotation">
        Stellar rotation
       </a>
       is measured through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doppler_shift" title="Doppler shift">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       or by tracking active surface features.
      </p>
      <p>
       This rotation induces a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(fictitious)" title="Centrifugal force (fictitious)">
        centrifugal acceleration
       </a>
       in the reference frame of the Earth which slightly counteracts the effect of gravity the closer one is to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . One effect is that an object weighs slightly less at the equator. Another is that the Earth is slightly deformed into an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate spheroid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Another consequence of the rotation of a planet is the phenomenon of
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       . Like a
       <a href="/wiki/Gyroscope" title="Gyroscope">
        gyroscope
       </a>
       , the overall effect is a slight "wobble" in the movement of the axis of a planet. Currently the tilt of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's axis to its orbital plane (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity_of_the_ecliptic" title="Obliquity of the ecliptic">
        obliquity of the ecliptic
       </a>
       ) is 23.44 degrees, but this angle changes slowly (over thousands of years). (See also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Precession_of_the_equinoxes" title="Precession of the equinoxes">
        Precession of the equinoxes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pole_star" title="Pole star">
        Pole star
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation_and_revolution">
        Rotation and revolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Rotation and revolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_revolution" title="Orbital revolution">
        Orbital revolution
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       While revolution is often used as a synonym for rotation, in many fields, particularly astronomy and related fields, revolution, often referred to as orbital revolution for clarity, is used when one body moves around another while rotation is used to mean the movement around an axis. Moons revolve around their planet, planets revolve about their star (such as the Earth around the Sun); and stars slowly revolve about their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galaxial_center" title="Galaxial center">
        galaxial center
       </a>
       . The motion of the components of
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
        galaxies
       </a>
       is complex, but it usually includes a rotation component.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Retrograde_rotation">
        Retrograde rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Retrograde rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        Retrograde motion
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Most
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       in our
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
        solar system
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , spin in the same direction as they orbit the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . The exceptions are
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . Uranus rotates nearly on its side relative to its orbit. Current speculation is that Uranus started off with a typical prograde orientation and was knocked on its side by a large impact early in its history. Venus may be thought of as rotating slowly backwards (or being "upside down"). The
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       (formerly considered a planet) is anomalous in this and other ways.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">
        Physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        Angular momentum
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Speed#Tangential_Speed" title="Speed">
        Speed § Tangential Speed
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_energy" title="Rotational energy">
        rotational energy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">
        angular velocity
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(fictitious)" title="Centrifugal force (fictitious)">
        Centrifugal force (fictitious)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">
        centripetal force
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_motion" title="Circular motion">
        circular motion
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_orbit" title="Circular orbit">
        circular orbit
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">
        Coriolis effect
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
        spin (physics)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy" title="Rotational spectroscopy">
        rotational spectroscopy
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body_dynamics#Linear_and_angular_momentum" title="Rigid body dynamics">
        Rigid body dynamics § Linear and angular momentum
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The speed of rotation is given by the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_frequency" title="Angular frequency">
        angular frequency
       </a>
       (rad/s) or
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        frequency
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Turn_(geometry)" title="Turn (geometry)">
        turn
       </a>
       /s, turns per minute), or
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        period
       </a>
       (seconds, days, etc.). The time-rate of change of angular frequency is angular acceleration (rad/s²), This change is caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torque
       </a>
       . The ratio of the two (how heavy is it to start, stop, or otherwise change rotation) is given by the
       <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">
        moment of inertia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">
        angular velocity
       </a>
       vector (an
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axial_vector" title="Axial vector">
         axial vector
        </a>
       </i>
       ) also describes the direction of the axis of rotation. Similarly the torque is an axial vector.
      </p>
      <p>
       The physics of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis" title="Rotation around a fixed axis">
        rotation around a fixed axis
       </a>
       is mathematically described with the
       <a href="/wiki/Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation" title="Axis–angle representation">
        axis–angle representation
       </a>
       of rotations. According to the
       <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule" title="Right-hand rule">
        right-hand rule
       </a>
       , the direction away from the observer is associated with clockwise rotation and the direction towards the observer with counterclockwise rotation, like a
       <a href="/wiki/Screw" title="Screw">
        screw
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cosmological_principle">
        Cosmological principle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Cosmological principle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laws_of_physics" title="Laws of physics">
        laws of physics
       </a>
       are currently believed to be
       <a href="/wiki/Rotational_invariance#Application_to_quantum_mechanic" title="Rotational invariance">
        invariant under any fixed rotation
       </a>
       . (Although they do appear to change when viewed from a rotating viewpoint: see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotating_frame_of_reference" title="Rotating frame of reference">
        rotating frame of reference
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       In modern physical cosmology, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_principle" title="Cosmological principle">
        cosmological principle
       </a>
       is the notion that the distribution of matter in the universe is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homogeneous" title="Homogeneous">
        homogeneous
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotropic" title="Isotropic">
        isotropic
       </a>
       when viewed on a large enough scale, since the forces are expected to act uniformly throughout the universe and have no preferred direction, and should, therefore, produce no observable irregularities in the large scale structuring over the course of evolution of the matter field that was initially laid down by the Big Bang.
      </p>
      <p>
       In particular, for a system which behaves the same regardless of how it is oriented in space, its
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics" title="Lagrangian mechanics">
        Lagrangian
       </a>
       is rotationally invariant. According to
       <a href="/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem" title="Noether's theorem">
        Noether's theorem
       </a>
       , if the
       <a href="/wiki/Action_(physics)" title="Action (physics)">
        action
       </a>
       (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Integral_over_time" title="Integral over time">
        integral over time
       </a>
       of its Lagrangian) of a physical system is invariant under rotation, then
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">
        angular momentum is conserved
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Euler_rotations">
        Euler rotations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Euler rotations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Euler_angles" title="Euler angles">
        Euler angles
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Praezession.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1021" data-file-width="818" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Praezession.svg/170px-Praezession.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Praezession.svg/255px-Praezession.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Praezession.svg/340px-Praezession.svg.png 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Praezession.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Euler rotations of the Earth. Intrinsic (green), Precession (blue) and Nutation (red)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Euler rotations provide an alternative description of a rotation. It is a composition of three rotations defined as the movement obtained by changing one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Euler_angles" title="Euler angles">
        Euler angles
       </a>
       while leaving the other two constant. Euler rotations are never expressed in terms of the external frame, or in terms of the co-moving rotated body frame, but in a mixture. They constitute a mixed axes of rotation system, where the first angle moves the line of nodes around the external axis
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       , the second rotates around the line of nodes and the third one is an intrinsic rotation around an axis fixed in the body that moves.
      </p>
      <p>
       These rotations are called
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nutation" title="Nutation">
        nutation
       </a>
       , and
       <i>
        intrinsic rotation
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flight_dynamics">
        Flight dynamics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Flight dynamics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes" title="Aircraft principal axes">
        Aircraft principal axes
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Flight_dynamics_with_text.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/220px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/330px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Flight_dynamics_with_text.png/440px-Flight_dynamics_with_text.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Flight_dynamics_with_text.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The principal axes of rotation in space
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_dynamics" title="Flight dynamics">
        flight dynamics
       </a>
       , the principal rotations described with
       <a href="#Euler_rotations">
        Euler angles above
       </a>
       are known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yaw,_pitch,_and_roll" title="Yaw, pitch, and roll">
        <i>
         pitch
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         roll
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         yaw
        </i>
       </a>
       . The term
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotation_(aviation)" title="Rotation (aviation)">
        rotation
       </a>
       is also used in aviation to refer to the upward pitch (nose moves up) of an aircraft, particularly when starting the climb after takeoff.
      </p>
      <p>
       Principal rotations have the advantage of modelling a number of physical systems such as
       <a href="/wiki/Gimbal" title="Gimbal">
        gimbals
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Joystick" title="Joystick">
        joysticks
       </a>
       , so are easily visualised, and are a very compact way of storing a rotation. But they are difficult to use in calculations as even simple operations like combining rotations are expensive to do, and suffer from a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Gimbal_lock#Gimbal_lock_in_applied_mathematics" title="Gimbal lock">
        gimbal lock
       </a>
       where the angles cannot be uniquely calculated for certain rotations.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Amusement_rides">
        Amusement rides
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Amusement rides">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Amusement_ride" title="Amusement ride">
        amusement rides
       </a>
       provide rotation. A
       <a href="/wiki/Ferris_wheel" title="Ferris wheel">
        Ferris wheel
       </a>
       has a horizontal central axis, and parallel axes for each gondola, where the rotation is opposite, by gravity or mechanically. As a result, at any time the orientation of the gondola is upright (not rotated), just translated. The tip of the translation vector describes a circle. A
       <a href="/wiki/Carousel" title="Carousel">
        carousel
       </a>
       provides rotation about a vertical axis. Many rides provide a combination of rotations about several axes. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chair-O-Planes" title="Chair-O-Planes">
        Chair-O-Planes
       </a>
       the rotation about the vertical axis is provided mechanically, while the rotation about the horizontal axis is due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Centripetal_force" title="Centripetal force">
        centripetal force
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Roller_coaster_inversion" title="Roller coaster inversion">
        roller coaster inversions
       </a>
       the rotation about the horizontal axis is one or more full cycles, where inertia keeps people in their seats.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sports">
        Sports
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sports">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Spin move" redirects here. For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Spin_move_(disambiguation)" title="Spin move (disambiguation)">
        Spin move (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       Rotation of a ball or other object, usually called
       <i>
        spin
       </i>
       , plays a role in many sports, including
       <a href="/wiki/Topspin#Tennis" title="Topspin">
        topspin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Backspin" title="Backspin">
        backspin
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">
        tennis
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        English
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        follow
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        draw
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Billiards#Shooting_techniques/mechanics" title="Billiards">
        billiards and pool
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curve_ball" title="Curve ball">
        curve balls
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Baseball" title="Baseball">
        baseball
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spin_bowling" title="Spin bowling">
        spin bowling
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cricket_(sport)" title="Cricket (sport)">
        cricket
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flying_disc" title="Flying disc">
        flying disc
       </a>
       sports, etc.
       <a href="/wiki/Table_tennis" title="Table tennis">
        Table tennis
       </a>
       paddles are manufactured with different surface characteristics to allow the player to impart a greater or lesser amount of spin to the ball.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rotation of a player one or more times around a vertical axis may be called
       <i>
        spin
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_skating" title="Figure skating">
        figure skating
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        twirling
       </i>
       (of the baton or the performer) in
       <a href="/wiki/Baton_twirling" title="Baton twirling">
        baton twirling
       </a>
       , or
       <i>
        360
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        540
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        720
       </i>
       , etc. in
       <a href="/wiki/Snowboarding" title="Snowboarding">
        snowboarding
       </a>
       , etc. Rotation of a player or performer one or more times around a horizontal axis may be called a
       <a href="/wiki/Flip_(acrobatic)" title="Flip (acrobatic)">
        flip
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Roll_(gymnastics)" title="Roll (gymnastics)">
        roll
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Somersault" title="Somersault">
        somersault
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        heli
       </i>
       , etc. in
       <a href="/wiki/Gymnastics" title="Gymnastics">
        gymnastics
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterskiing" title="Waterskiing">
        waterskiing
       </a>
       , or many other sports, or a
       <i>
        one-and-a-half
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        two-and-a-half
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        gainer
       </i>
       (starting facing away from the water), etc. in
       <a href="/wiki/Diving" title="Diving">
        diving
       </a>
       , etc. A combination of vertical and horizontal rotation (back flip with 360°) is called a
       <i>
        möbius
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Waterskiing#Freestyle_jumping" title="Waterskiing">
        waterskiing freestyle jumping
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Rotation of a player around a vertical axis, generally between 180 and 360 degrees, may be called a
       <i>
        spin move
       </i>
       and is used as a deceptive or avoidance maneuver, or in an attempt to play, pass, or receive a ball or puck, etc., or to afford a player a view of the goal or other players. It is often seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Hockey" title="Hockey">
        hockey
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basketball" title="Basketball">
        basketball
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Football" title="Football">
        football
       </a>
       of various codes,
       <a href="/wiki/Tennis" title="Tennis">
        tennis
       </a>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_axis_vs._fixed_point">
        Fixed axis vs. fixed point
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rotation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Fixed axis vs. fixed point">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        end result
       </i>
       of any sequence of rotations of any object in 3D about a fixed point is always equivalent to a rotation about an axis. However, an object may
       <i>
        physically
       </i>
       rotate in 3D about a fixed point on more than one axis simultaneously, in which case there is no single fixed axis of rotation - just the fixed point. However, these two descriptions can be reconciled - such a physical motion can always be re-described in terms of a single axis of rotation, provided the orientation of that axis relative to the object is allowed to change moment by moment.
      </p>
