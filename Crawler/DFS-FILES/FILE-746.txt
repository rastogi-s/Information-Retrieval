       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_verbs" title="Spanish verbs">
        Spanish verbs
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       This article presents a set of paradigms—that is,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugation
       </a>
       tables—of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       , including examples of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_verb" title="Regular verb">
        regular verbs
       </a>
       and some of the most common
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_verb" title="Irregular verb">
        irregular verbs
       </a>
       . For other irregular verbs and their common patterns, see the article on
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
        Spanish irregular verbs
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The tables include only the "simple" tenses (that is, those formed with a single word), and not the
       <a href="/wiki/Compound_verb" title="Compound verb">
        "compound" tenses
       </a>
       (those formed with an
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verb
       </a>
       plus a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        non-finite
       </a>
       form of the main verb), such as the progressive, perfect, and passive voice. The
       <a href="/wiki/Continuous_and_progressive_aspects" title="Continuous and progressive aspects">
        progressive aspects
       </a>
       (also called "continuous tenses") are formed by using the appropriate tense of
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       + gerund, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">
        perfect
       </a>
       constructions are formed by using the appropriate tense of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       +
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       . When the past participle is used in this way, it invariably ends with
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       . In contrast, when the participle is used as an
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_modifier" title="Grammatical modifier">
        adjective
       </a>
       , it agrees in
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       with the noun modified. And similarly, the participle agrees with the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       when it is used with
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       to form the "true" (
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_verb" title="Dynamic verb">
        dynamic
       </a>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">
        passive voice
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <i>
        La carta fue escrita ayer
       </i>
       'The letter was written [got written] yesterday.'), and also when it is used with
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       to form a "passive of result", or
       <a href="/wiki/Stative_verb" title="Stative verb">
        stative
       </a>
       passive (as in
       <i>
        La carta ya está escrita
       </i>
       'The letter is already written.').
      </p>
      <p>
       The pronouns
       <i>
        yo, tú, vos,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-voseo_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-voseo-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        él, nosotros, vosotros
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vosotros_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-vosotros-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ellos
       </i>
       are used to symbolise the three
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        persons
       </a>
       and two
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        numbers
       </a>
       . Note, however, that Spanish is a
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop language
       </a>
       , and so it is the norm to omit subject pronouns when not needed for contrast or emphasis. Note also that the subject, if specified, can easily be something other than these pronouns. For example,
       <i>
        él
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ella
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       can be replaced by a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       , or the verb can appear with impersonal
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       and no subject (e.g.
       <i>
        Aquí se vive bien
       </i>
       , 'One lives well here'). The first-person plural expressions
       <i>
        nosotros
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        nosotras
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tú y yo
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        él y yo
       </i>
       can be replaced by a noun phrase that includes the speaker (e.g.
       <i>
        Los estudiantes tenemos hambre
       </i>
       , 'We students are hungry'). The same comments hold for
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ellos
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           ^
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-0">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               a
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-1">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               b
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-2">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               c
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-3">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               d
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-4">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               e
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-5">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               f
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-6">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               g
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-7">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               h
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-fut_subj_8-8">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               i
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The future subjunctive is obsolete in Spanish, except for legal language, highly literary language, or
           <a href="/wiki/Fossilization_(linguistics)" title="Fossilization (linguistics)">
            fossilized
           </a>
           expressions like
           <i>
            sea lo que
            <b>
             fuere
            </b>
           </i>
           'be what it may, whatever it may be'.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-amares-9">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           ^
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-0">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               a
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-1">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               b
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-2">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               c
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-3">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               d
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-4">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               e
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-5">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               f
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-6">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               g
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-7">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               h
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amares_9-8">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               i
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           Given the "high"
           <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
            register
           </a>
           of the future subjunctive in contrast to the informality of
           <i>
            tú
           </i>
           and
           <i>
            vos
           </i>
           , it is extremely unlikely that forms such as
           <i>
            amares
           </i>
           ,
           <i>
            tuvieres
           </i>
           , etc. would occur in Modern Spanish prose or conversation.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-amad_amar-10">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           ^
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-0">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               a
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-1">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               b
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-2">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               c
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-3">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               d
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-4">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               e
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-5">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               f
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
           <a href="#cite_ref-amad_amar_10-6">
            <sup>
             <i>
              <b>
               g
              </b>
             </i>
            </sup>
           </a>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           For every verb, the normative command form for
           <i>
            vosotros
           </i>
           ends with
           <i>
            -d
           </i>
           (
           <i>
            amad
           </i>
           ,
           <i>
            temed
           </i>
           ,
           <i>
            partid
           </i>
           , etc.), but the colloquial form ends with
           <i>
            -r
           </i>
           , making it identical to the infinitive (
           <i>
            amar
           </i>
           ,
           <i>
            temer
           </i>
           ,
           <i>
            partir
           </i>
           , etc.). When the object pronoun
           <i>
            os
           </i>
           is attached to make the corresponding
           <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_verb" title="Reflexive verb">
            reflexive
           </a>
           forms (also called a "pronominal" verb), the
           <i>
            -d
           </i>
           is dropped, but the
           <i>
            -r
           </i>
           is not. Thus
           <i>
            amad
           </i>
           &gt;
           <i>
            amaos
           </i>
           (normative) and
           <i>
            amar
           </i>
           &gt;
           <i>
            amaros
           </i>
           (non-normative), etc.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-11">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-11">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           <i>
            Estar
           </i>
           is often made pronominal in the imperative, especially in colloquial speech.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-12">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-12">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           For the imperative 'tú' form, the verb
           <i>
            estar
           </i>
           is systematically made pronominal (
           <i>
            estarse
           </i>
           )
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-13">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-13">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The usual conversational form is
           <i>
            estar
           </i>
           , which is not
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            normative
           </a>
           . For the pronominal
           <i>
            estarse
           </i>
           , the forms
           <i>
            estaros
           </i>
           and also
           <i>
            estarse
           </i>
           for some regions (instead of
           <i>
            estaos
           </i>
           ) are not
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            normative
           </a>
           , but they are used in colloquial speech. The form
           <i>
            estad
           </i>
           is only used formally.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-14">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-14">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The form is
           <i>
            ha
           </i>
           to construct the perfective tenses and in
           <i>
            haber de
           </i>
           , but
           <i>
            hay
           </i>
           in the impersonal expressions
           <i>
            hay que
           </i>
           and
           <i>
            hay
           </i>
           meaning "there is/are".
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-15">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-15">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The form is
           <i>
            habemos
           </i>
           in the rare expression
           <i>
            nos las habemos con
           </i>
           , meaning 'the matter in question is'; dialectally or archaically
           <i>
            habemos
           </i>
           may replace
           <i>
            hemos
           </i>
           , in all senses; and dialectally it may replace
           <i>
            hay
           </i>
           in the plural, meaning "there are".
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-16">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-16">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           <i>
            Hubieron
           </i>
           is commonly used by Spanish speakers as a replacement for
           <i>
            hubo
           </i>
           in the plural form, as in
           <i>
            Hubieron muchas cajas
           </i>
           , with the connotated translation of "There were many boxes", although this usage of
           <i>
            hubieron
           </i>
           is not
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            normative
           </a>
           .
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-17">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-17">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           Dialectally,
           <i>
            haiga
           </i>
           , etc.
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-18">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-18">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           Dialectally,
           <i>
            ves
           </i>
           . Not used pronominally, always
           <i>
            vete
           </i>
           .
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-19">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-19">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The verb
           <i>
            ir
           </i>
           , for its affirmative imperative with
           <i>
            vos
           </i>
           , borrows the form from
           <i>
            andar
           </i>
           ('to walk/to go').
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-20">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-20">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           In Chile,
           <i>
            anda
           </i>
           .
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-21">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-21">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           Usage in
           <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
            Central America
           </a>
           favors the regular form "
           <i>
            í
           </i>
           ".
          </span>
         </li>
         <li id="cite_note-22">
          <span class="mw-cite-backlink">
           <b>
            <a href="#cite_ref-22">
             ^
            </a>
           </b>
          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The form
           <i>
            ir
           </i>
           (instead of
           <i>
            id
           </i>
           ) is not
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            normative
           </a>
           , but it is the one used in colloquial speech. The
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            normative
           </a>
           second-person plural imperative of the pronominal verb
           <i>
            irse
           </i>
           is
           <i>
            idos
           </i>
           (i.e. it does not lose its
           <i>
            d
           </i>
           as other verbs do), but it is virtually unused, either spoken or written. The form
           <i>
            íos
           </i>
           is obsolete. The
           <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
            non-normative
           </a>
           forms used in colloquial speech are
           <i>
            iros
           </i>
           or regionally
           <i>
            irse
           </i>
           . To avoid using these forms, some writers simply use a different verb, i.e.
           <i>
            marchaos
           </i>
           .
          </span>
         </li>
        </ol>
