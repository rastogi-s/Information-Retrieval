      <p>
       <b>
        Mexican Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español mexicano
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a set of
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       as spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       and in some parts of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish was brought to Mexico in the 16th century. As in all other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish-speaking_countries" title="Spanish-speaking countries">
        Spanish-speaking countries
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       ), different accents and varieties of the language exist in different parts of the country, for both historical and sociological reasons. Among these, the varieties that are best known outside of the country are those of central Mexico—both educated and uneducated varieties—largely because the capital,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       , hosts most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_media" title="Mass media">
        mass communication media
       </a>
       with international projection. For this reason, most of the film dubbing identified abroad with the label "Mexican Spanish" or "Latin American Spanish" actually corresponds to the central Mexican variety.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mexico City was built on the site of
       <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">
        Tenochtitlan
       </a>
       , the capital of the
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Empire" title="Aztec Empire">
        Aztec Empire
       </a>
       . Besides the Aztecs or
       <a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">
        Mexica
       </a>
       , the region was home to many other
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       -speaking cultures as well; consequently many speakers of Nahuatl continued to live there and in the surrounding region, outnumbering the Spanish-speakers, and the Spanish of central Mexico incorporated a significant number of Hispanicized Nahuatl words and cultural markers. At the same time, as a result of Mexico City's central role in the colonial administration of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       , the population of the city included a relatively large number of speakers from
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       , and the city and the neighboring
       <a href="/wiki/State_of_Mexico" title="State of Mexico">
        State of Mexico
       </a>
       tended historically to exercise a standardizing effect over the language of the entire central region of the country.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       The territory of contemporary Mexico is not coextensive with what might be termed Mexican Spanish. The Spanish spoken in the southernmost state of
       <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">
        Chiapas
       </a>
       , bordering
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
        Guatemala
       </a>
       , resembles the variety of
       <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Spanish" title="Central American Spanish">
        Central American Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in that country, where
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       is used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Meanwhile, to the north, many Mexicans stayed in Texas after its independence from Mexico, and their descendants continue to speak a variety of Spanish known as "Tex-Mex".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       And after the
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Guadalupe_Hidalgo" title="Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo">
        Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo
       </a>
       many Mexicans remained in the territory ceded to the U.S., and their descendants have continued to speak Spanish within their communities in Arizona, California, Colorado, New Mexico, Nevada, Utah, and Wyoming. In addition, the waves of 19th- and 20th-century migration from Mexico to the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       (mostly to the formerly Mexican area of the
       <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">
        Southwest
       </a>
       ) have contributed greatly to making Mexican Spanish the most widely spoken variety of Spanish in the United States. The Spanish spoken in the Gulf coastal areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Veracruz" title="Veracruz">
        Veracruz
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tabasco" title="Tabasco">
        Tabasco
       </a>
       and in the states of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yucatan" title="Yucatan">
        Yucatan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quintana_Roo" title="Quintana Roo">
        Quintana Roo
       </a>
       exhibits more
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       phonetic traits than that spoken in the rest of Mexico. And the Spanish of the
       <a href="/wiki/Yucat%C3%A1n_Peninsula" title="Yucatán Peninsula">
        Yucatán Peninsula
       </a>
       is distinct from all other forms in its intonation and in the incorporation of
       <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">
        Mayan
       </a>
       words.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
        First Mexican Empire
       </a>
       comprised what is present-day
       <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">
        El Salvador
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       , Guatemala,
       <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">
        Belize
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       , aside from the mentioned present states of United States; thus
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rican_Spanish" title="Costa Rican Spanish">
        Costa Rican
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemalan_Spanish" title="Guatemalan Spanish">
        Guatemalan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honduran_Spanish" title="Honduran Spanish">
        Honduran
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexican_Spanish" title="New Mexican Spanish">
        New Mexican
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_Spanish" title="Nicaraguan Spanish">
        Nicaraguan
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Salvadoran_Spanish" title="Salvadoran Spanish">
        Salvadoran Spanish
       </a>
       were originally included in the dialects of Mexican Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarding the evolution of the Spanish spoken in Mexico, the Swedish linguist
       <a class="extiw" href="https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertil_Malmberg_(fonetiker)" title="sv:Bertil Malmberg (fonetiker)">
        Bertil Malmberg
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       points out that in Central Mexican Spanish—unlike most varieties in the other Spanish-speaking countries—the vowels lose strength, while consonants are fully pronounced. Malmberg attributes this to a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" title="Nahuatl language">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum_(linguistics)" title="Stratum (linguistics)">
        substratum
       </a>
       , as part of a broader cultural phenomenon that preserves aspects of indigenous culture through place names of Nahuatl origin, statues that commemorate Aztec rulers, etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Mexican linguist
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Miguel_Lope_Blanch" title="es:Juan Miguel Lope Blanch">
        Juan M. Lope Blanch
       </a>
       , however, finds similar weakening of vowels in regions of several other Spanish-speaking countries; he also finds no similarity between the vowel behavior of Nahuatl and that of Central Mexican Spanish; and thirdly, he finds Nahuatl syllable structure no more complex than that of Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, Nahuatl is not alone as a possible influence, as there are currently more than 90 native languages spoken in Mexico,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and they all contribute to the diversity of accents found throughout the country. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       of some varieties of Mexican Spanish is said to be influenced by that of indigenous languages, including some which are
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tone languages
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zapotec_language" title="Zapotec language">
        Zapotec
       </a>
       ). The tonal patterns and overlengthening of the vowels in some forms of Mexican Spanish were particularly strong among
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mestizos" title="Mestizos">
        mestizos
       </a>
       who spoke one of the native Mexican languages as their first language and Spanish as a second language, and it continues so today. There are some varieties in Mexico that whose main or important feature is the intonation in counterpart of a dialect,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Norte%C3%B1o_del_Noreste_Spanish_(Mexico)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Norteño del Noreste Spanish (Mexico) (page does not exist)">
        Norteño del Noreste
       </a>
       variant uses a ruralized intonation, like the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Norte%C3%B1o_del_Noroeste_Spanish_(Mexico)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Norteño del Noroeste Spanish (Mexico) (page does not exist)">
        Norteño del Noroeste
       </a>
       only to a lesser extent and with an intonation of surprise,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Western_Spanish_(Mexico)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Western Spanish (Mexico) (page does not exist)">
        Western
       </a>
       variant uses an ironic intonation when is describing a tremendous thing, while the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chiapaneco_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chiapaneco Spanish (page does not exist)">
        Chiapaneco
       </a>
       variant uses an intonation of hypocrisy that was emerged from
       <a href="/wiki/Formality" title="Formality">
        formality
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Affricates">
        Affricates
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Affricates">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Due to influence from indigenous languages, such as Nahuatl, the set of affricates in Mexican Spanish includes a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
        voiceless alveolar affricate
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡s]
       </span>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral affricate">
        voiceless alveolar
        <i>
         lateral
        </i>
        affricate
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡ɬ]
       </span>
       , represented by the respective digraphs ⟨tz⟩ and ⟨tl⟩,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lope2004_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lope2004-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as in the words
       <i>
        tlapalería
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡ɬapaleˈɾia]
       </span>
       ('hardware store') and
       <i>
        coatzacoalquense
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [koat͡sakoalˈkense]
       </span>
       ('from [the city of]
       <a href="/wiki/Coatzacoalcos" title="Coatzacoalcos">
        Coatzacoalcos
       </a>
       '). Even words of Greek and Latin origin with ⟨tl⟩, such as
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27Atl%C3%A1ntico%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''Atlántico''">
          <i>
           Atlántico
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27atleta%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''atleta''">
          <i>
           atleta
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       , are pronounced with the affricate:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈt͡ɬãn̪t̪iko̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈt͡ɬe̞t̪a]
       </span>
       (compare
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aðˈle̞t̪a]
       </span>
       in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fricatives">
        Fricatives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Fricatives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In addition to the usual voiceless fricatives of other American Spanish dialects (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       ), Mexican Spanish also has the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar sibilant">
        palatal sibilant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lope2004_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lope2004-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       mostly in words from indigenous languages—especially place names. The
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       , represented orthographically as ⟨x⟩, is commonly found in words of
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       or Mayan origin, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Metro_Xola" title="Metro Xola">
        <i>
         Xola
        </i>
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈʃola]
       </span>
       (a station in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City_Metro" title="Mexico City Metro">
        Mexico City Metro
       </a>
       ). The spelling ⟨x⟩ can additionally represent the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       (also mostly in place names), as in
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       itself (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈmexiko/
       </span>
       ); or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , as in the place name
       <a href="/wiki/Xochimilco" title="Xochimilco">
        Xochimilco
       </a>
       —as well as the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       sequence (in words of Greco-Latin origin, such as
       <i>
        anexar
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /anekˈsar/
       </span>
       ), which is common to all varieties of Spanish. In many Nahuatl words in which ⟨x⟩ originally represented
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       , the pronunciation has changed to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       (or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       )—e.g.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Xalapa" title="Xalapa">
         Jalapa/Xalapa
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [xaˈlapa]
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarding the pronunciation of the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       , the articulation in most of Mexico is velar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        caja
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈkaxa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ('box'). However, in some (but not all) dialects of southern Mexico, the normal articulation is glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (as it is in most dialects of the Caribbean, the Pacific Coast, the
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       , and most of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       in Spain).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, in these dialects,
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Jalapa
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        caja
       </i>
       are respectively pronounced [ˈmehiko], [haˈlapa], and [ˈkaha]. In dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">
        Oaxaca
       </a>
       , much of Chiapas and the southern Highland and interior regions, the pronunciation of /x/ is uvular
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [χ]
       </span>
       . This is identical to the Mayan pronunciation of the dorsal fricative which, unlike the Spanish romanization ⟨x⟩, in Mayan languages is commonly represented orthographically by ⟨j⟩. (In Spanish spelling before the 16th century, the letter ⟨x⟩ represented
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       ; historical shifts have moved this articulation to the back of the mouth in all varieties of the language except
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       In Northern Western Mexican Spanish, Peninsular Oriental, Oaxaqueño and in eastern variants influenced by Mayan languages,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       , represented by ⟨ch⟩, tends to be deaffricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       , a phonetic feature typical of both Mayan languages and southwestern Andalusian Spanish dialects.
      </p>
      <p>
       All varieties of Mexican Spanish are characterized by
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       : the letters ⟨ll⟩ and ⟨y⟩ correspond to the same phoneme,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That phoneme, in most variants of Mexican Spanish, is pronounced as either a palatal fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       or an approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       in most cases, although after a
       <a href="/wiki/Pausa" title="Pausa">
        pause
       </a>
       it is instead realized as an affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɟʝ ~ dʒ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Also present in most of the interior of Mexico is the preservation (absence of
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       ) of syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ; this, combined with frequent unstressed vowel reduction, gives the sibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       a special prominence. This situation contrasts with that in the coastal areas, on both the Pacific and the Gulf Coastal sides, where the weakening or debuccalization of syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is a sociolinguistic marker, reflecting the tension between the Mexico City norm and the historical tendency towards consonantal weakening characteristic of coastal areas in Spanish America. Dialects of both the Pacific and the Gulf Coast have received more influences from Andalusian and Canarian Spanish dialects.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Like most Spanish dialects and varieties, Mexican Spanish has five vowels: two high vowels (/
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">
         i
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">
         u
        </a>
       </span>
       /), two mid vowels (/
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid front unrounded vowel">
         e
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Mid back rounded vowel">
         o
        </a>
       </span>
       /) and one open vowel (/
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Open_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Open central unrounded vowel">
         a
        </a>
       </span>
       /).
      </p>
      <p>
       A striking feature of Mexican Spanish, particularly that of central Mexico, is the high rate of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
        reduction
       </a>
       and even
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        elision
       </a>
       of unstressed vowels, as in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈtɾasts/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        trastes
       </i>
       , 'cooking utensils'). This process is most frequent when a vowel is in contact with the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , so that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       + vowel +
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is the construction when the vowel is most frequently affected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It can be the case that the words
       <i>
        pesos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pesas
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        peces
       </i>
       are pronounced the same
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈpesəs/
       </span>
       . The vowels are slightly less frequently reduced or eliminated in the constructions
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t, p, k, d/
       </span>
       + vowel +
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , so that the words
       <i>
        pastas
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pastes
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        pastos
       </i>
       may also be pronounced the same
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈpasts/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Mexican Spanish is a
       <i>
        tuteante
       </i>
       form of the language (i.e. using
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and its traditional verb forms for the familiar second person singular). The traditional familiar second person
       <i>
        plural
       </i>
       pronoun
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       —in colloquial use only in Spain—is found in Mexico only in certain archaic texts and ceremonial language. However, since it is used in many Spanish-language Bibles throughout the country, most Mexicans are familiar with the form and understand it. An instance of it is found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Himno_Nacional_Mexicano" title="Himno Nacional Mexicano">
        national anthem
       </a>
       , which all Mexicans learn to sing:
       <i>
        Mexicanos, al grito de guerra / el acero
        <b>
         aprestad
        </b>
        y el bridón
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Central Mexico is noted for the frequent use of
       <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">
        diminutive
       </a>
       suffixes with many nouns, adverbs, and adjectives, even where no semantic diminution of size or intensity is implied. Most frequent is the
       <i>
        -ito/ita
       </i>
       suffix, which replaces the final vowel on words that have one. Words ending with
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       use the suffix
       <i>
        -cito/cita
       </i>
       . Use of the diminutive does not necessarily denote small size, but rather often implies an affectionate attitude; thus one may speak of "una casita grande" ('a nice, big house').
      </p>
      <p>
       When the diminutive suffix is applied to an adjective, often a near-equivalent idea can be expressed in English by "nice and [adjective]". So, for example, a mattress (
       <i>
        un colchón
       </i>
       ) described as
       <i>
        blandito
       </i>
       might be "nice and soft", while calling it
       <i>
        blando
       </i>
       might be heard to mean "too soft".
      </p>
      <p>
       Frequent use of the diminutive is found across all socioeconomic classes, but its "excessive" use is commonly associated with lower-class speech.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="More_suffixes">
        More suffixes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: More suffixes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some regions of Mexico, the diminutive suffix
       <i>
        -ito
       </i>
       is also used to form affectives to express politeness or submission (
       <i>
        cafecito
       </i>
       , literally "little coffee";
       <i>
        cabecita
       </i>
       , literally "little head";
       <i>
        chavito
       </i>
       "little boy"), and is attached to names (
       <i>
        Marquitos
       </i>
       , from
       <i>
        Marcos
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        Juanito
       </i>
       , from
       <i>
        Juan
       </i>
       —cf. Eng.
       <i>
        Johnny
       </i>
       ) denoting affection. In the northern parts of the country, the suffix
       <i>
        -ito
       </i>
       is often replaced in informal situations by
       <i>
        -illo
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        cafecillo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cabecilla
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        morrillo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Juanillo
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Augmentative" title="Augmentative">
        augmentative
       </a>
       suffix
       <i>
        -(z)ote
       </i>
       is typically used in Mexico to make nouns larger, more powerful, etc. For example, the word
       <i>
        camión
       </i>
       , in Mexico, means
       <i>
        bus
       </i>
       ; the suffixed form
       <i>
        camionzote
       </i>
       means "big or long bus". It can be repeated just as in the case of the suffixes
       <i>
        -ito
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ísimo
       </i>
       ; therefore
       <i>
        camionzotototote
       </i>
       means "very, very, very big bus".
      </p>
      <p>
       The suffix
       <i>
        -uco
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -ucho
       </i>
       and its feminine counterparts
       <i>
        -uca
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ucha
       </i>
       respectively, are used as a disparaging form of a noun; for example, the word
       <i>
        casa
       </i>
       , meaning "house", can be modified with that suffix (
       <i>
        casucha
       </i>
       ) to change the word's meaning to make it disparaging, and sometimes offensive; so the word
       <i>
        casucha
       </i>
       often refers to a shanty, hut or hovel. The word
       <i>
        madera
       </i>
       ("wood") can take the suffix
       <i>
        -uca
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        maderuca
       </i>
       ) to mean "rotten, ugly wood".
      </p>
      <p>
       Other suffixes include, but are not limited to:
       <i>
        -azo
       </i>
       as in
       <i>
        carrazo
       </i>
       , which refers to a very impressive car (
       <i>
        carro
       </i>
       ) such as a Ferrari or Mercedes-Benz;
       <i>
        -ón
       </i>
       , for example
       <i>
        narizón
       </i>
       , meaning "big-nosed" (
       <i>
        nariz
       </i>
       = "nose"), or
       <i>
        patona
       </i>
       , a female with large feet (
       <i>
        patas
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nicknames">
        Nicknames
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nicknames">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is common to replace
       <i>
        c-/s-
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        ch-
       </i>
       to form diminutives, e.g.
       <i>
        I
        <b>
         sabel
        </b>
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        Chabela
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Jo
        <b>
         sé Ma
        </b>
        ría
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        Chema
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         Cer
        </b>
        vez
        <b>
         a
        </b>
       </i>
       ("beer") →
       <i>
        Cheve
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         Conc
        </b>
        epción
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        Conchita
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         Sin Muela
        </b>
        s
       </i>
       ("without molars") →
       <i>
        Chimuela
       </i>
       ("toothless"). This is common in, but not exclusive to, Mexican Spanish.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">
        Syntax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Typical of Mexican Spanish is an ellipsis of the negative particle
       <i>
        no
       </i>
       in a main clause introduced by an adverbial clause with
       <i>
        hasta que
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Hasta que me tomé la pastilla se me quitó el dolor. (Until I took the pill, the pain
        <i>
         did not
        </i>
        go away.)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In this kind of construction, the main verb is implicitly understood as being negated.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mexico shares with many other areas of Spanish America the use of interrogative
       <i>
        qué
       </i>
       in conjunction with the quantifier
       <i>
        tan(to)
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        ¿Qué tan graves son los daños? (How serious are the damages?) (Compare the form typical of Spain: "¿Hay muchos daños?" (Is there a lot of damage?))
       </li>
       <li>
        ¿Qué tan buen cocinero eres? (How good a cook are you?) (Compare Spain's "¿Eres buen cocinero?" (Are you a good cook?))
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It has been suggested that there is influence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_languages" title="Native American languages">
        indigenous languages
       </a>
       on the syntax of Mexican Spanish (as well as that of other areas in the Americas), manifested, for example, in the redundant use of verbal
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitics
       </a>
       , particularly
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mexican Spanish, like that of many other parts of the Americas, prefers the preposition
       <i>
        por
       </i>
       in expressions of time spans, as in
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "Fue presidente de la compañía
        <b>
         por
        </b>
        veinte años" (He was the president of the company for twenty years)—compare the more frequent use of
        <i>
         durante
        </i>
        in Spain: "Fue presidente de la compañia
        <b>
         durante
        </b>
        veinte años."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A more or less recent phenomenon in the speech of central Mexico, having its apparent origin in the
       <a href="/wiki/State_of_Mexico" title="State of Mexico">
        State of Mexico
       </a>
       , is the use of negation in an
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        unmarked
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question" title="Yes–no question">
        yes/no question
       </a>
       . Thus, in place of "¿Quieres...?" (Would you like...?), there is a tendency to ask "¿No quieres...?" (Wouldn't you like...?).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Mexican Spanish retains a number of words that are considered archaic in Spain.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, there are a number of words widely used in Mexico which have Nahuatl, Mayan or other native origins, in particular names for flora, fauna and
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponyms
       </a>
       . Some of these words are used in most, or all, Spanish-speaking countries, like
       <i>
        chocolate
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        aguacate
       </i>
       ("avocado"), and some are only used in Mexico. The latter include
       <i>
        guajolote
       </i>
       "turkey" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        huaxōlōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [waˈʃoːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       (although
       <i>
        pavo
       </i>
       is also used, as in other Spanish-speaking countries);
       <i>
        papalote
       </i>
       "kite" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        pāpālōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [paːˈpaːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       "butterfly"; and
       <i>
        jitomate
       </i>
       "tomato" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        xītomatl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [ʃiːˈtomat͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       . For a more complete list see
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Nahuatl_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin">
        List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Other expressions that are unique to colloquial Mexican Spanish include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ahorita
        </i>
        : "soon; in a moment". Literally "right now". E.g.
        <i>
         Ahorita que acabe
        </i>
        , "As soon as I finish (this)". Considered informal.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         bronca
        </i>
        : "fight" or "problem". Literally "aggressive woman or girl, or wild female animal". Commonly used among young people.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         bronco
        </i>
        : "wild, untame". E.g.
        <i>
         leche bronca
        </i>
        : "unpasteurized milk".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chavo
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         chava
        </i>
        );
        <i>
         chamaco
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         chamaca
        </i>
        );
        <i>
         chilpayate
        </i>
        : "a child, teen, or youngster". Also
        <i>
         huerco
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         huerca
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         morro
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         morra
        </i>
        ), and
        <i>
         plebe
        </i>
        are used in northern Mexico. All these terms except
        <i>
         chilpayate
        </i>
        are also found in their diminutives:
        <i>
         chavito
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         chamaquito
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         huerquito
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         morrito
        </i>
        . Considered informal.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chichi
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        ): "breast(s)". From Nahuatl
        <i>
         chīchīhualli
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
          [tʃiːtʃiːwɑlːi]
         </a>
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        Considered informal.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chido
        </i>
        : "cool, attractive, fun, etc."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chingadera
        </i>
        : "trash; crap". Considered vulgar.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chingado
        </i>
        : "damned". Considered vulgar.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chingar
        </i>
        : "to screw/ruin/rob/steal/fuck/work/eat". Vulgar.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cholo
        </i>
        : In northern Mexico, equivalent to the English term
        <i>
         gangsta
        </i>
        ; in the rest of Mexico, equivalent to the Spanish term
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/es:pandillero" title="wikt:es:pandillero">
          pandillero
         </a>
        </i>
        ("hooligan", "gang member"), which refers to young slum-dwellers living in conditions of
        <a href="/wiki/Extreme_poverty" title="Extreme poverty">
         extreme poverty
        </a>
        , drug dependency, and malnutrition.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         En un momento
        </i>
        : "Just a minute", "Hold on a second", etc. Literally "in a moment".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         escuincle
        </i>
        : "a bratty child" or "squirt". From Nahuatl
        <i>
         itzcuīntli
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
          [it͡skʷiːnt͡ɬi]
         </a>
        </span>
        , "dog".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este...
        </i>
        : a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filler_word" title="Filler word">
         filler word
        </a>
        , similar to American English "um". Literally, "this". Also used in other countries.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/G%C3%BCero_(disambiguation)" title="Güero (disambiguation)">
          güero
         </a>
        </i>
        : "light-haired and/or light-skinned person".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         güey
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         wey
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         buey
        </i>
        : "dude", "guy" (literally, "ox"). As an adjective, "dumb", "asinine", "moronic", etc. Not to be confused with "Huey" from the Aztec title "
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huey_Tlatoani" title="Huey Tlatoani">
         Huey Tlatoani
        </a>
        ", in which "Huey" is a term of reverence.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         hablar con
        </i>
        : "to talk with (on the telephone)". Used in place of the standard
        <i>
         llamar
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         macho
        </i>
        : "manly". Applied to a woman (
        <i>
         macha
        </i>
        ): "manly" or "skillful".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Naco_(slang)" title="Naco (slang)">
          naco
         </a>
        </i>
        : "a low-class, boorish, foolish, ignorant and/or uneducated person". Pejorative.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Órale
        </i>
        : (1) similar to English "Wow!" (2) "Okay". (3) Exclamation of surprised protest. Abbreviated
        <i>
         ¡Ora!
        </i>
        by low-class people in their uneducated variety. May be considered rude.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         padre
        </i>
        : used as an adjective to denote something "cool", attractive, good, fun, etc. E.g.
        <i>
         Esta música está muy padre
        </i>
        , "This music is very cool." Literally, "father".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         pedo
        </i>
        : "problem" or "fight". Literally "fart". Also, in a greeting,
        <i>
         ¿Qué pedo, güey?
        </i>
        ("What's up, dude?"). As an adjective, "drunk", e.g.
        <i>
         estar pedo
        </i>
        , "to be drunk". Also the noun
        <i>
         peda
        </i>
        : "a drunken gathering". All forms are considered vulgar for their connection to
        <i>
         pedo
        </i>
        , "fart".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         pelo chino
        </i>
        : "curly hair".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuervas_17-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cuervas-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The word
        <i>
         chino
        </i>
        derives from the Spanish word
        <i>
         cochino
        </i>
        , "pig".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuervas_17-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cuervas-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The phrase originally referenced the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Casta" title="Casta">
          casta
         </a>
        </i>
        (racial type) known as
        <i>
         chino
        </i>
        , meaning a person of mixed indigenous and African ancestry whose hair was curly.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuervas_17-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cuervas-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Sometimes erroneously thought to be derived from Spanish
        <i>
         chino
        </i>
        , "Chinese".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuervas_17-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cuervas-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         pinche
        </i>
        : "damned", "lousy", more akin to "freaking". E.g.
        <i>
         Quita tu pinche cara de aquí.
        </i>
        ("Take your lousy face away from here"). As a noun, literally, "kitchen assistant". Considered vulgar.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         popote
        </i>
        : "drinking straw". From Nahuatl
        <i>
         popōtl
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
          [popoːt͡ɬ]
         </a>
        </span>
        , the name of a plant from which brooms and drinking straws are made, or the straws themselves.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Cómo la ves?
        </i>
        : "What do you think about it?" Literally "How do you see it?"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡Híjole!
        </i>
        : An exclamation, used variously to express surprise, frustration, etc. From
        <i>
         hijo de...
        </i>
        ("son of a..."). Also
        <i>
         ¡Híjoles!
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Mande?
        </i>
        : "Beg your pardon?". From
        <i>
         mandar
        </i>
        , "to order", formal command form.
        <i>
         ¿Cómo?
        </i>
        (literally "How?"), as in other countries, is also in use. The use of
        <i>
         ¿Qué?
        </i>
        ("What?") on its own is sometimes considered impolite, unless accompanied by a verb:
        <i>
         ¿Qué dijiste?
        </i>
        ("What did you say?").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡Puta madre!
        </i>
        : "Oh shit!". Literally, "Motherfucker!". Considered vulgar.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué onda?
        </i>
        : "What's up?". Literally, "What's the vibe?".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Most of the words above are considered informal (e.g.
       <i>
        chavo(a)
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        padre
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        güero
       </i>
       , etc.), rude (
       <i>
        güey
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        naco
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ¿cómo (la) ves?
       </i>
       , etc.) or vulgar (
       <i>
        chingadera
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pinche
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pedo
       </i>
       , etc.) and are limited to slang use among friends or in informal settings; foreigners need to exercise caution in their use. In 2009, at an audience for the signing of a Memorandum of Understanding between Mexico and the Netherlands, the then Crown Prince of the Netherlands,
       <a href="/wiki/Willem-Alexander_of_the_Netherlands" title="Willem-Alexander of the Netherlands">
        Willem-Alexander
       </a>
       , made a statement to the audience with a word which, in Mexican Spanish, is considered very vulgar. Evidently oblivious to the word's different connotations in different countries, the prince's Argentine interpreter used the word
       <i>
        chingada
       </i>
       as the ending to the familiar Mexican proverb "Cámaron que se duerme se lo lleva la corriente" (A sleeping shrimp is carried away by the tide), without realizing the vulgarity associated with the word in Mexico. The prince, also unaware of the differences, proceeded to say the word, to the bemusement and offense of some of the attendees.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_dialects">
        Similar dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Similar dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       New Mexican Spanish has many similarities with an older version of Mexican Spanish. The small amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Spanish" title="Philippine Spanish">
        Spanish spoken in the Philippines
       </a>
       has traditionally been influenced by Mexican Spanish (as the territory was initially administered for the Spanish crown by
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       and later controlled by
       <a href="/wiki/Acapulco" title="Acapulco">
        Acapulco
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_language" title="Spanish-based creole language">
        Spanish-based creole language
       </a>
       in the Philippines, is based on Mexican Spanish.
      </p>
