      <p>
       <b>
        Nicaea
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Nicea
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%9D%CE%AF%CE%BA%CE%B1%CE%B9%CE%B1" title="wikt:Νίκαια">
         Νίκαια
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="tr" xml:lang="tr">
         İznik
        </span>
       </i>
       ) was an ancient city in northwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
        Anatolia
       </a>
       , and is primarily known as the site of the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Council_of_Nicaea" title="First Council of Nicaea">
        First
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Council_of_Nicaea" title="Second Council of Nicaea">
        Second
       </a>
       Councils of Nicaea (the first and seventh
       <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_council" title="Ecumenical council">
        Ecumenical councils
       </a>
       in the early history of the
       <a href="/wiki/Christian_Church" title="Christian Church">
        Christian Church
       </a>
       ), the
       <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">
        Nicene Creed
       </a>
       (which comes from the First Council), and as the capital city of the
       <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Nicaea" title="Empire of Nicaea">
        Empire of Nicaea
       </a>
       following the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">
        Fourth Crusade
       </a>
       in 1204, until the recapture of
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       by the Byzantines in 1261.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ancient city is located within the modern
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkish
       </a>
       city of
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0znik" title="İznik">
        İznik
       </a>
       (whose modern name derives from Nicaea's), and is situated in a fertile basin at the eastern end of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lake_Ascanius" title="Lake Ascanius">
        Lake Ascanius
       </a>
       , bounded by ranges of hills to the north and south. It is situated with its west wall rising from the lake itself, providing both protection from siege from that direction, as well as a source of supplies which would be difficult to cut off. The lake is large enough that it could not be blockaded from the land easily, and the city was large enough to make any attempt to reach the harbour from shore-based siege weapons very difficult.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ancient city is surrounded on all sides by 5 kilometres (3 mi) of walls about 10 metres (33 ft) high. These are in turn surrounded by a double ditch on the land portions, and also included over 100 towers in various locations. Large gates on the three landbound sides of the walls provided the only entrance to the city.
      </p>
      <p>
       Today the walls have been pierced in many places for roads, but much of the early work survives and, as a result, it is a major tourist destination.
      </p>

        Bottiaeans
       </a>
       , and to have originally borne the name of
       <b>
        Ancore
       </b>
       (Ἀγκόρη) or
       <b>
        Helicore
       </b>
       (Ἑλικόρη), or by soldiers of
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       's army who hailed from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicaea_(Locris)" title="Nicaea (Locris)">
        Nicaea
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Locris" title="Locris">
        Locris
       </a>
       , near
       <a href="/wiki/Thermopylae" title="Thermopylae">
        Thermopylae
       </a>
       . The later version however was not widespread even in Antiquity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_2_2._Foundation,_other_names]_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_2_2._Foundation,_other_names]-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Whatever the truth, the first Greek colony on the site was probably destroyed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Mysians" title="Mysians">
        Mysians
       </a>
       , and it fell to
       <a href="/wiki/Antigonus_I_Monophthalmus" title="Antigonus I Monophthalmus">
        Antigonus I Monophthalmus
       </a>
       , one of Alexander's successors (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Diadochi" title="Diadochi">
         Diadochi
        </a>
       </i>
       ) to refound the city ca. 315 BC as
       <b>
        Antigoneia
       </b>
       (Ἀντιγονεία) after himself. Antigonus is also known to have established Bottiaean soldiers in the vicinity, lending credence to the tradition about the city's founding by Bottiaeans. Following Antigonus' defeat and death at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Ipsus" title="Battle of Ipsus">
        Battle of Ipsus
       </a>
       in 301 BC, the city was captured by
       <a href="/wiki/Lysimachus" title="Lysimachus">
        Lysimachus
       </a>
       , who renamed it
       <b>
        Nicaea
       </b>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%9D%CE%AF%CE%BA%CE%B1%CE%B9%CE%B1" title="wikt:Νίκαια">
         Νίκαια
        </a>
       </span>
       , also
       <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">
        transliterated
       </a>
       as
       <b>
        Nikaia
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Nicæa
       </b>
       ; see also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_traditional_Greek_place_names" title="List of traditional Greek place names">
        List of traditional Greek place names
       </a>
       ), in tribute to his wife
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaea_of_Macedon" title="Nicaea of Macedon">
        Nicaea
       </a>
       , who had recently died.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_2_2._Foundation,_other_names]_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_2_2._Foundation,_other_names]-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometime before 280 BC, the city came under the control of the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Bithynia" title="List of rulers of Bithynia">
        local dynasty
       </a>
       of the kings of
       <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">
        Bithynia
       </a>
       . This marks the beginning of its rise to prominence as a seat of the royal court, as well as of its rivalry with
       <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">
        Nicomedia
       </a>
       . The two cities' dispute over which one was the pre-eminent city (signified by the appellation
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Metropolis" title="Metropolis">
         metropolis
        </a>
       </i>
       ) of Bithynia continued for centuries, and the 38th oration of
       <a href="/wiki/Dio_Chrysostom" title="Dio Chrysostom">
        Dio Chrysostom
       </a>
       was expressly composed to settle the dispute.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRG_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DGRG-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">
        Roman period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Roman period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1920" data-file-width="2560" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg/250px-Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg/375px-Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg/500px-Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nicaea_theatre_990.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The theatre, restored by
         <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger" title="Pliny the Younger">
          Pliny the Younger
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Along with the rest of Bithynia, Nicaea came under the rule of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">
        Roman Republic
       </a>
       in 72 BC. The city remained one of the most important urban centres of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
        Asia Minor
       </a>
       throughout the Roman period, and continued its old competition with Nicomedia over pre-eminence and the location of the seat of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_governor" title="Roman governor">
        Roman governor
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bithynia_et_Pontus" title="Bithynia et Pontus">
        Bithynia et Pontus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The geographer
       <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
        Strabo
       </a>
       (XII.565 ff.) described the city as built in the typical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">
        Hellenistic
       </a>
       fashion with great regularity, in the form of a square, measuring 16
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadion_(unit_of_length)" title="Stadion (unit of length)">
        stadia
       </a>
       in circumference, i.e. approx. 700 m × 700 m (2,297 ft × 2,297 ft) or 0.7 km × 0.7 km (0.43 mi × 0.43 mi) covering an area of some 50 ha (124 acres) or 0.5 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (0.2 sq mi); it had four gates, and all its streets intersected one another at right angles in accordance with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hippodamian_plan" title="Hippodamian plan">
        Hippodamian plan
       </a>
       , so that from a monument in the centre all the four gates could be seen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRG_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DGRG-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_5_5._Culture_-_architecture]_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_5_5._Culture_-_architecture]-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This monument stood in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(ancient_Greece)" title="Gymnasium (ancient Greece)">
        gymnasium
       </a>
       , which was destroyed by fire but was restored with increased magnificence by
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Younger" title="Pliny the Younger">
        Pliny the Younger
       </a>
       , when he was governor there in the early 2nd century AD. In his writings Pliny makes frequent mention of Nicaea and its public buildings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRG_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-DGRG-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">
        Hadrian
       </a>
       visited the city in 123 AD after it had been severely damaged by an earthquake and began to rebuild it. The new city was enclosed by a polygonal wall of some 5 kilometres in length. Reconstruction was not completed until the 3rd century, and the new set of walls failed to save Nicaea from being sacked by the
       <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">
        Goths
       </a>
       in 258 AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_3_3._History]-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_5_5._Culture_-_architecture]_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStefanidou2003[http://asiaminor.ehw.gr/forms/fLemmaBodyExtended.aspx?lemmaid=8503&amp;boithimata_State=&amp;kefalaia_State=#chapter_5_5._Culture_-_architecture]-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The numerous coins of Nicaea which still exist attest the interest taken in the city by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_emperors" title="Roman emperors">
        Roman emperors
       </a>
       , as well as its attachment to the rulers; many of them commemorate great festivals celebrated there in honour of gods and emperors, as
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Olympic_festivals" title="Ancient Greek Olympic festivals">
        Olympia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Isthmia_(ancient_city)" title="Isthmia (ancient city)">
        Isthmia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysia" title="Dionysia">
        Dionysia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pythian_Games" title="Pythian Games">
        Pythia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Commodus" title="Commodus">
        Commodia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Severan_dynasty" title="Severan dynasty">
        Severia
       </a>
       , Philadelphia, etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRG_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-DGRG-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Byzantine_period">
        Byzantine period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Byzantine period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       By the 4th century, Nicaea was a large and prosperous city, and a major military and administrative centre. Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">
        Constantine the Great
       </a>
       convened the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Ecumenical_Council" title="First Ecumenical Council">
        First Ecumenical Council
       </a>
       there, and the city gave its name to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Creed" title="Nicene Creed">
        Nicene Creed
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGRG_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-DGRG-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The city remained important in the 4th century, seeing the proclamation of Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">
        Valens
       </a>
       (364) and the failed rebellion of
       <a href="/wiki/Procopius_(usurper)" title="Procopius (usurper)">
        Procopius
       </a>
       (365). During the same period, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Nicaea" title="See of Nicaea">
        See of Nicaea
       </a>
       became independent of Nicomedia and was raised to the status of a
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop" title="Metropolitan bishop">
        metropolitan bishopric
       </a>
       . However, the city was hit by two major earthquakes in 363 and 368, and coupled with competition from the newly established capital of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Eastern Roman Empire">
        Eastern Empire
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       , it began to decline thereafter. Many of its grand civic buildings began to fall into ruin, and had to be restored in the 6th century by Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">
        Justinian I
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The city disappears from sources thereafter and is mentioned again in the early 8th century: in 715, the deposed emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Anastasios_II" title="Anastasios II">
        Anastasios II
       </a>
       fled there, and the city successfully resisted attacks by the
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">
        Umayyad Caliphate
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_Arab_Siege_of_Constantinople" title="Second Arab Siege of Constantinople">
        716
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nicaea_(727)" title="Siege of Nicaea (727)">
        727
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The city was again damaged by an earthquake in 740, served as the base of the rebellion of
       <a href="/wiki/Artabasdos" title="Artabasdos">
        Artabasdos
       </a>
       in 741/2, and served as the meeting-place of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh_Ecumenical_Council" title="Seventh Ecumenical Council">
        Seventh Ecumenical Council
       </a>
       , which condemned
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Iconoclasm" title="Byzantine Iconoclasm">
        Byzantine Iconoclasm
       </a>
       , in 787 (the council probably met in the
       <a href="/wiki/Basilica" title="Basilica">
        basilica
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hagia_Sophia,_Iznik" title="Hagia Sophia, Iznik">
        Hagia Sophia
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463–1464_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911463–1464-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nicaea became the capital of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opsician_Theme" title="Opsician Theme">
        Opsician Theme
       </a>
       in the 8th century and remained "a center of administration and trade" (C. Foss). A
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jewish
       </a>
       community is attested in the city in the 10th century. Due to its proximity to Constantinople, the city was contested in the rebellions of the 10th and 11th centuries as a base from which to threaten the capital. It was in the wake of such a rebellion, that of
       <a href="/wiki/Nikephoros_Melissenos" title="Nikephoros Melissenos">
        Nikephoros Melissenos
       </a>
       , that it fell into the hands of Melissenos' Turkish allies in 1081.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seljuk_Turks" title="Seljuk Turks">
        Seljuk Turks
       </a>
       made Nicaea the capital of their
       <a href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Rum" title="Sultanate of Rum">
        possessions
       </a>
       in Asia Minor until 1097, when it returned to Byzantine control with the aid of the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Crusade" title="First Crusade">
        First Crusade
       </a>
       after a
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nicaea" title="Siege of Nicaea">
        long siege
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 12th century saw a period of relative stability and prosperity at Nicaea. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Komnenos_dynasty" title="Komnenos dynasty">
        Komnenian
       </a>
       emperors Alexios, John and Manuel campaigned extensively to strengthen the Byzantine presence in Asia Minor. Major fortifications were constructed across the region, especially by John and Manuel, which helped to protect the city and its fertile hinterland. There were also several military bases and colonies in the area, for example the one at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhyndakos" title="Rhyndakos">
        Rhyndakos
       </a>
       in Bithynia, where the emperor John spent a year training his troops in preparation for campaigns in southern Asia Minor.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople" title="Siege of Constantinople">
        fall
       </a>
       of Constantinople to the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Crusade" title="Fourth Crusade">
        Fourth Crusade
       </a>
       in 1204, and the establishment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_Empire" title="Latin Empire">
        Latin Empire
       </a>
       , Nicaea escaped Latin occupation and maintained an autonomous stance. From 1206 on, it became the base of
       <a href="/wiki/Theodore_I_Laskaris" title="Theodore I Laskaris">
        Theodore Laskaris
       </a>
       , who in 1208 was crowned emperor there and founded the
       <a href="/wiki/Empire_of_Nicaea" title="Empire of Nicaea">
        Empire of Nicaea
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Patriarchate of Constantinople">
        Patriarchate of Constantinople
       </a>
       , exiled from Constantinople, also took up residence in the city until the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recapture_of_Constantinople" title="Recapture of Constantinople">
        recapture of Constantinople
       </a>
       in 1261. Although Nicaea was soon abandoned as the primary residence of the Nicaean emperors, who favoured
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nymphaion_(Ionia)" title="Nymphaion (Ionia)">
        Nymphaion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnesia_on_the_Maeander" title="Magnesia on the Maeander">
        Magnesia on the Maeander
       </a>
       , the period was a lively one in the city's history, with "frequent synods, embassies, and imperial weddings and funerals", while the influx of scholars from other parts of the Greek world made it a centre of learning as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After the restoration of the Byzantine Empire in 1261, the city once again declined in importance. The neglect of the Asian frontier by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_VIII_Palaiologos" title="Michael VIII Palaiologos">
        Michael VIII Palaiologos
       </a>
       provoked a major uprising in 1262, and in 1265, panic broke out when rumours circulated of an imminent
       <a href="/wiki/Mongols" title="Mongols">
        Mongol
       </a>
       attack.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464_7-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_II_Palaiologos" title="Andronikos II Palaiologos">
        Andronikos II Palaiologos
       </a>
       visited the city in 1290 and took care to restore its defences, but Byzantium proved unable to halt the rise of the nascent
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottoman_emirate" title="Ottoman emirate">
        Ottoman emirate
       </a>
       in the region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464_7-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoss19911464-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_III_Palaiologos" title="Andronikos III Palaiologos">
        Andronikos III Palaiologos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/John_VI_Kantakouzenos" title="John VI Kantakouzenos">
        John Kantakouzenos
       </a>
       were defeated at
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Pelekanon" title="Battle of Pelekanon">
        Pelekanon
       </a>
       on 11 June 1329, the Byzantine government could no longer defend Nicaea. Nicaea finally surrendered to the Ottomans after a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nicaea_(1328%E2%80%9331)" title="Siege of Nicaea (1328–31)">
        long siege
       </a>
       2 March 1331.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ottoman_Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ottoman Empire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iznik" title="Iznik">
        Iznik
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1331, the city was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nicaea_(1328%E2%80%9331)" title="Siege of Nicaea (1328–31)">
        conquered
       </a>
       and incorporated into the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orhan_I" title="Orhan I">
        Orhan I
       </a>
       . Many of its public buildings were destroyed, and the materials were used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turks" title="Ottoman Turks">
        Ottomans
       </a>
       in erecting their mosques and other edifices. With the
       <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople" title="Fall of Constantinople">
        fall of Constantinople
       </a>
       in 1453, the town lost a great degree of its importance, but later became a major centre with the creation of a local faïence
       <a href="/wiki/Pottery" title="Pottery">
        pottery
       </a>
       -making industry in the 17th century.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ruins">
        Ruins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Ruins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ancient walls, with their towers and gates, are relatively well preserved. Their circumference is 3,100 m (10,171 ft), being at the base from 5 to 7 m (16 to 23 ft) in thickness, and from 10 to 13 m (33 to 43 ft) in height; they contain four large and two small gates. In most places they are formed of alternate courses of Roman tiles and large square stones, joined by a cement of great thickness. In some places columns and other architectural fragments from the ruins of more ancient edifices have been inserted. As with those of Constantinople, the walls seem to have been built in the 4th century. Some of the towers have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_Greek_language" title="Byzantine Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       inscriptions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp._pp._10_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Comp._pp._10-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The ruins of mosques, baths, and houses, dispersed among the gardens and apartment buildings that now occupy a great part of the space within the Roman and Byzantine fortifications, show that the Ottoman-era town center, though now less considerable, was once a place of importance; but it never was as large as the Byzantine city. It seems to have been almost entirely constructed of the remains of the Byzantine-era Nicaea, the walls of the ruined mosques and baths being full of the fragments of ancient Greek, Roman, and Byzantine temples and churches. In the northwestern parts of the town, two moles extend into the lake and form a harbour; but the lake in this part has much retreated, and left a marshy plain. Outside the walls are the remnants of an ancient aqueduct.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Comp._pp._10_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Comp._pp._10-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Church of the Dormition, the principal Greek Orthodox church in Nicaea, was one of the most architecturally important Byzantine churches in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asia_Minor" title="Asia Minor">
        Asia Minor
       </a>
       . A domed church with a cross-shaped nave and elongated apse, and dating from the perhaps as early as the end of the 6th century, its bema was decorated with very fine mosaics that had been restored in the 9th century. The Church of the Dormition was destroyed in 1922; only the lower portions of some of its walls survive today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       Excavations are underway in the Ottoman kilns where the historic Nycean tileware were made. The Hagia Sophia is also undergoing restoration.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="See_of_Nicaea">
        See of Nicaea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: See of Nicaea">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/See_of_Nicaea" title="See of Nicaea">
        See of Nicaea
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The bishopric of Nicaea remains as a
       <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">
        titular see
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church" title="Roman Catholic Church">
        Roman Catholic Church
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which has left the seat vacant since the death of its last
       <a href="/wiki/Titular_bishop" title="Titular bishop">
        titular bishop
       </a>
       in 1976.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also a titular
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_bishop" title="Metropolitan bishop">
        metropolitan
       </a>
       see of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecumenical_Patriarchate_of_Constantinople" title="Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople">
        Ecumenical Patriarchate of Constantinople
       </a>
       . The incumbent 2001–2010 was the former
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_Orthodox_Church" title="Finnish Orthodox Church">
        Archbishop of Karelia and All Finland
       </a>
       , Metropolitan
       <a href="/wiki/John_Rinne" title="John Rinne">
        Johannes (Rinne)
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
