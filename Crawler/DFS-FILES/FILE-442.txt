      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       has a long history of borrowing words, expressions and subtler features of other languages it has come in contact with.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish developed from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       , with an important lexical influence from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , and some influence from
       <a href="/wiki/Paleohispanic_languages" title="Paleohispanic languages">
        Paleohispanic languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Celtiberian_language" title="Celtiberian language">
        Celtiberian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish has also absorbed many loanwords from other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , notably from
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and in recent times also from
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Much as English in the United States and Canada, it is now spoken by a host of people in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">
        the Americas
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multicultural" title="Multicultural">
        multicultural
       </a>
       backgrounds including those of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">
        Amerindian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_people" title="African people">
        African
       </a>
       heritage. The extensive contact between Spanish and the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        native American languages
       </a>
       , has also deeply influenced not just local dialects of Spanish, but also the Spanish language as a whole.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formative_influences">
        Formative influences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formative influences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As Spanish went through its first stages of development in Spain, it probably received influences from neighbouring related languages, among them
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       , which is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">
        language isolate
       </a>
       and thus completely unrelated to Spanish in origin.
       <a href="/wiki/Umbrian_language" title="Umbrian language">
        Umbrian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oscan_language" title="Oscan language">
        Oscan
       </a>
       influences have also been postulated for the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       colonization period.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Celtiberian_influence">
        Celtiberian influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celtiberian influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Two specific types of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" title="Voiced consonant">
        voicing
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_consonant" title="Voiceless consonant">
        voiceless consonants
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        elision
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" title="Voiced consonant">
        voiced consonants
       </a>
       (both of which are discussed at greater length below), are the
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       changes of Spanish that are most often attributed to the influence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celtic_language" title="Celtic language">
        Celtic languages
       </a>
       ; they have also been attributed to the influence of the Basque language. While examples of these two types of lenition are ubiquitous and well-documented in Spanish, two assumptions need to be made if these two types of lenition are to be attributed to patterns of lenition in Celtic languages. The first assumption is that a population of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingualism" title="Bilingualism">
        bilingual
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Celtiberian_language" title="Celtiberian language">
        Celtiberian
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       speakers existed long enough to have had an influence on the development of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       . The second assumption is that
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_Celtic_languages" title="Continental Celtic languages">
        Continental Celtic
       </a>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Extinction" title="Extinction">
        extinct
       </a>
       branch of
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       , did indeed exhibit the types of lenition that are known to exist in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">
        Insular Celtic languages
       </a>
       . The latter is simply not known, and it should also be noted that such lenitions are a very common kind of change in languages all around the world, and similar phenomena are found in Corsican and Sardinian where no Celtic causation is plausible.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The Spanish development may therefore just be a natural internal process, not due to outside influence. Another possible influence of Celtic languages in Spanish and other Western Romance languages is
       <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">
        epenthetic
       </a>
       /e/ that is inserted at the beginning of any word that began with /s/ and another consonant.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_influence">
        Basque influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Basque influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many Castilians who took part in the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       and later repopulation
       <a href="/wiki/Military_campaign" title="Military campaign">
        campaigns
       </a>
       of Muslim Iberia were of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">
        Basque
       </a>
       lineage and this is evidenced by many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Place_name" title="Place name">
        place names
       </a>
       throughout Spain. The change from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Spanish#Latin_f-_to_Spanish_h-" title="History of Spanish">
        Latin 'f-' to Spanish 'h-'
       </a>
       was once commonly ascribed to the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       speakers for a few reasons. The change from [f] to [h] was first documented in the areas around
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
        Castile
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(autonomous_community)" title="La Rioja (autonomous community)">
        La Rioja
       </a>
       , areas that are close to the Basque-speaking area. The change to [h] took place to a greater degree in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
        Gascon language
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">
        Gascony
       </a>
       in France, an area that is close to the Basque Country too. The claim is that the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque language
       </a>
       lacked the [f] sound and thus substituted it with [h], the closest thing to [f] in that language.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are some difficulties with attributing this change to Basque, however. There is no hard evidence that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
        medieval
       </a>
       Basque did or did not have an [f] sound. Presumably early borrowings of forms with initial [f] into Basque were usually received as [p] or [b] (e.g. FESTA &gt; Basque
       <i>
        pesta
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        besta
       </i>
       , depending on the dialect), rather than [h]. Adding to this is the fact that the f-to-h
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       is not peculiar to Spanish. In fact, the change from [f] to [h] is one of the most common
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       changes in all kinds of world languages. According to the explanations that negate or downplay Basque influence, the change occurred in the affected dialects wholly independent of each other as the result of internal change (i.e. linguistic factors, not outside influence). It is also possible that the two forces worked in concert and reinforced each other.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another claim of Basque influence in Spanish is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_retracted_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant">
        voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant
       </a>
       [
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
         s̺
        </a>
       </span>
       ], a sound roughly intermediate between
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminodental
       </a>
       [
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
       </span>
       ] and
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       [
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
       </span>
       ]; this sound also appears in other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibero-Romance_languages" title="Ibero-Romance languages">
        Ibero-Romance languages
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       . The apico-alveolar retracted sibilant might be a result of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingualism" title="Bilingualism">
        bilingualism
       </a>
       of speakers of Basque and
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        Lenition
       </a>
       of intervocalic consonants is perhaps also influenced by Basque: lenition of intervocalic /b d g/ occurs in regular speech in most
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Basque_Country" title="Southern Basque Country">
        Southern Basque dialects
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gothic">
        Gothic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gothic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spain was controlled by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visigoth" title="Visigoth">
        Visigoths
       </a>
       between the 5th and 8th centuries. However, the influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
        Gothic language
       </a>
       (an East Germanic language) on Spanish was minimal because the invaders were already somewhat Romanized, were secluded in the upper echelons of society, and generally did not intermarry with the natives. Besides a few military words, Spanish borrowed the following from Gothic:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        A new noun
        <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
         declension
        </a>
        (nominative
        <i>
         -ā
        </i>
        , oblique
        <i>
         -āne
        </i>
        ), which originated from the Gothic n-stem declension. This was used mostly with proper names, e.g., Old Spanish
        <i>
         Fruela
        </i>
        ~
        <i>
         Froilán
        </i>
        (for the same person) and also
        <i>
         guardia
        </i>
        "guard" ~
        <i>
         guardián
        </i>
        "guardian" (from Gothic nominative
        <i>
         wardja
        </i>
        , accusative
        <i>
         wardjan
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The originally adjectivizing
        <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
         suffix
        </a>
        <i>
         -engo
        </i>
        (Germanic
        <i>
         -ing
        </i>
        ), as in
        <i>
         abolengo
        </i>
        'ancestry' (cf.
        <i>
         abuelo
        </i>
        'grandfather'),
        <i>
         abadengo
        </i>
        'abbatial',
        <i>
         realengo
        </i>
        'belonging to the Crown',
        <i>
         camarlengo
        </i>
        'chamberlain'.
       </li>
       <li>
        Perhaps the originally patronymic
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_naming_customs" title="Spanish naming customs">
         surname
        </a>
        suffixes in
        <i>
         -z
        </i>
        (as in
        <i>
         Díaz
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Pérez
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         López
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Ruiz
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Muñoz
        </i>
        , etc.) is from numerous Latinized Gothic
        <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
         genitives
        </a>
        in
        <i>
         -īcī
        </i>
        , from original -
        <i>
         iks
        </i>
        . Thus,
        <i>
         Roderic(us)
        </i>
        (→
        <i>
         Ruy
        </i>
        ) →
        <i>
         Roderīcī
        </i>
        'son of Roderick' →
        <i>
         Rodriz
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Ruiz
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        A few words of Gothic origin, e.g.,
        <i>
         ganso
        </i>
        'goose' (← *
        <i>
         gans
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         rueca
        </i>
        'distaff' (← *
        <i>
         rokko
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         tascar
        </i>
        'to brake hemp or flax' (← *
        <i>
         taskōn
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         triscar
        </i>
        'to set, tease' (←
        <i>
         þriskan
        </i>
        'to thresh'),
        <i>
         ataviar
        </i>
        'to attire, adorn' (← *
        <i>
         attaujan
        </i>
        'to mend').
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       by most accounts affected the
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       development very little,
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Germanic_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Germanic origin">
        Spanish words of Germanic origin
       </a>
       are present in all
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        varieties
       </a>
       of Modern Spanish. Many of the Spanish words of Germanic origin were already present in
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       , and so they are shared with other Romance languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other Germanic words were borrowed in more recent times; for example, the words for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" title="Cardinal directions">
        cardinal directions
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        norte
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        este
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sur
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        oeste
       </i>
       — 'north', 'east', 'south', 'west') are not documented until late in the 15th century. These direction words are thought to be from
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       , probably by way of French.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_influence_on_the_Spanish_language" title="Arabic influence on the Spanish language">
        Arabic influence on the Spanish language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 711 AD, most of the Iberian Peninsula was
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        conquered
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic-speaking
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslims" title="Muslims">
        Muslims
       </a>
       , who had recently also conquered a large part of northwest Africa. They occupied a territory that included north central Spain, where the Spanish language (i.e. Castilian) is usually assumed to have been born.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        Loanwords
       </a>
       from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       thus entered Castilian during its earliest formative period, particularly as the number of Arabic speakers in the neighboring lower reaches of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">
        Ebro
       </a>
       valley gradually increased in the 8th and 9th centuries.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="proof needed that Arabic loanwords were present before the Toledo court (though I doubt that proof exists) (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This lexical influence reached its greatest level during the Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       , when the emerging
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       conquered large territories from
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moorish
       </a>
       rulers in the 11th, 12th and 13th centuries. These territories had large numbers of speakers of Arabic, as well as many who spoke local Romance dialects (
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic language
       </a>
       ) that were heavily influenced by Arabic, both influencing Castilian. Arabic words and their derivatives had also been brought into Castilian earlier by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarab" title="Mozarab">
        Mozarab
       </a>
       Christians who emigrated northwards from
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       in times of
       <a href="/wiki/Sectarian_violence" title="Sectarian violence">
        sectarian violence
       </a>
       , particularly during the times of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">
        Almohad
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravid" title="Almoravid">
        Almoravid
       </a>
       rule in the 12th and 13th centuries.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish borrowed words from Arabic in many semantic fields:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Military and administrative terms such as
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Alc%C3%A1zar" title="Alcázar">
          alcázar
         </a>
        </i>
        "fortress" (from Latin
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">
          castrum
         </a>
        </i>
        "encampment, castle", through Arabic),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         alcalde
        </i>
        "mayor",
        <i>
         barrio
        </i>
        "ward, neighborhood",
        <i>
         aldea
        </i>
        "village";
       </li>
       <li>
        Leisure and comfort items such as
        <i>
         alfombra
        </i>
        "carpet",
        <i>
         almohada
        </i>
        "pillow",
        <i>
         guitarra
        </i>
        "guitar" (from Greek
        <i>
         kithāra
        </i>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Zither" title="Zither">
         zither
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Cithara" title="Cithara">
         cithara
        </a>
        ", through Arabic);
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Legal terms such as
        <i>
         asesino
        </i>
        "assassin, murderer",
        <i>
         rehén
        </i>
        "hostage",
        <i>
         tarifa
        </i>
        "tariff, fee",
        <i>
         arancel
        </i>
        "fee";
       </li>
       <li>
        Food and beverage names such as
        <i>
         aceite
        </i>
        "oil",
        <i>
         arroz
        </i>
        "rice",
        <i>
         espinaca
        </i>
        "spinach" and
        <i>
         naranja
        </i>
        "orange" (both from Persian, through Arabic),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         café
        </i>
        "coffee" (from Arabic through Turkish and then Italian),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         azúcar
        </i>
        "sugar";
       </li>
       <li>
        Terms of architecture and craftsmanship such as
        <i>
         alcoba
        </i>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Alcove_(architecture)" title="Alcove (architecture)">
         alcove
        </a>
        , room",
        <i>
         azotea
        </i>
        "flat roof",
        <i>
         albañil
        </i>
        "mason",
        <i>
         tabique
        </i>
        "dividing wall",
        <i>
         adoquín
        </i>
        "paving stone",
        <i>
         adobe
        </i>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Adobe" title="Adobe">
         adobe
        </a>
        ",
        <i>
         alfarero
        </i>
        "potter",
        <i>
         taza
        </i>
        "cup",
        <i>
         jarra
        </i>
        "pitcher";
       </li>
       <li>
        Chemical substances and materials such as
        <i>
         alcohol
        </i>
        "alcohol",
        <i>
         álcali
        </i>
        "alkali" (through
        <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
         Late Latin
        </a>
        , hence the initial stress),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         laca
        </i>
        "lacquer"(from Sanskrit through Persian and then Arabic);
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Mathematical and astronomical terms such as
        <i>
         cero
        </i>
        "zero" (through
        <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
         Late Latin
        </a>
        and then Italian),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         cifra
        </i>
        "cipher, figure",
        <i>
         álgebra
        </i>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">
         algebra
        </a>
        " (through
        <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
         Late Latin
        </a>
        , hence the initial stress),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         cenit
        </i>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
         zenith
        </a>
        " (Arabic
        <i>
         semt ar-ra's
        </i>
        , with an apparent misreading of
        <i>
         -m-
        </i>
        as
        <i>
         -ni-
        </i>
        in 13th-century manuscripts),
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Breve_3-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-Breve-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <i>
         guarismo
        </i>
        "number, figure";
       </li>
       <li>
        Interjections such as
        <i>
         ojalá
        </i>
        ("may it be that. ..", originally "May Allah want. .."),
        <i>
         olé
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         albricias
        </i>
        "joy!".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Many of these borrowings (especially in the scientific field) were then passed on to other languages (English acquired most of them through
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Most Spanish nouns beginning with the letters
       <i>
        al-
       </i>
       (from the Arabic definite article) have their origin in Arabic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As to how many words in Modern Spanish are of Arabic origin, the estimates vary widely, depending largely on whether the count includes derived forms and place names. One respected authority
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggests that they number more than 4,000, based on estimates of 850 of known etymology, 780 forms derived from them, 1,000 place names, 500 additional place names of "probable" Arabic origin, and "very numerous" Arabic-looking words whose affiliation has not yet been established. The largest Spanish etymological dictionary — the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Diccionario_cr%C3%ADtico_etimol%C3%B3gico_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Diccionario crítico etimológico de la lengua castellana">
         Diccionario crítico etimológico de la lengua castellana
        </a>
       </i>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/Joan_Coromines" title="Joan Coromines">
        Joan Corominas
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       — lists slightly over 1,000 words of Arabic origin, while Wikipedia's own
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Arabic_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Arabic origin">
        List of Spanish words of Arabic origin
       </a>
       , based on etymologies given by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       so far includes 1,200 confirmed Arabisms, excluding place names and derivatives.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        Morphological
       </a>
       borrowing was scarce. The suffíx
       <i>
        -í
       </i>
       (deriving adjectives from place names, as in
       <i>
        Marbellí
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Ceutí
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Iraní
       </i>
       , "from
       <a href="/wiki/Marbella" title="Marbella">
        Marbella
       </a>
       ", "from
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       ", or "from
       <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
        Iran
       </a>
       " respectively) is an example.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_borrowings">
        Modern borrowings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern borrowings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish borrowed many words from other European languages: its close neighbors such as
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       , other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       (this particularly during the Neoclassicist to Napoleonic periods, when French language and culture became the fashion at the royal court), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       like English. For example:
      </p>

        </i>

        </i>

        </a>
        (sometimes co-existing with
        <i>
         adiós
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chofer
        </i>
        "chauffeur" from French (co-existing with "conductor")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         elenco
        </i>
        "team" or "cast" from Italian (co-existing with
        <i>
         equipo
        </i>
        , when used as team, and
        <i>
         reparto
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         sandwich
        </i>
        , from English (co-existing with "emparedado" and sometimes with "bocadillo")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         briquet
        </i>
        from French (used in Colombia, co-existing with
        <i>

        </i>

       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         capot
        </i>
        from French
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>

        <i>

        <i>

        </i>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_borrowings">
        Recent borrowings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recent borrowings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In recent times, Spanish has borrowed many words and expressions from English, especially in the fields of computers and the Internet. In many cases, technical expressions that superficially employ common Spanish words are in fact
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calques
       </a>
       from English equivalents. For example,
       <i>
        disco duro
       </i>
       is a literal translation of "hard disk". Words like
       <a href="/wiki/Blog" title="Blog">
        blog
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Online_chat" title="Online chat">
        chat
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weblog" title="Weblog">
        weblog
       </a>
       are used, though
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit%C3%A1cora" title="es:Bitácora">
         bitácora
        </a>
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        cuaderno de bitácora
       </i>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Captain%27s_log" title="Captain's log">
        captain's log
       </a>
       on a boat) is also common.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Words_of_non-Latin_origin">
        Words of non-Latin origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Influences_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Words of non-Latin origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some authors estimate that seventy-five percent of Spanish words have come from Latin
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and were in use in Spain before the
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">
        Common Era
       </a>
       . The remaining 25 percent come from other languages. Of these
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       (and
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        language families
       </a>
       ), the four that have contributed the most words are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       in roughly that order.
      </p>
