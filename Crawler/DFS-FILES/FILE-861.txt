      <p>
       <b>
        Panamanian Spanish
       </b>
       is geographically defined as the form of Spanish spoken in the country of
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The variations among different speaker groups of the same language can be
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexical
       </a>
       (vocabulary),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological" title="Phonological">
        phonological
       </a>
       (pronunciation),
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       (word forms), or in the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       (grammar).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Panama" title="History of Panama">
        Historically
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       were part of the same political entity. Colombia, governed from the
       <a href="/wiki/Real_Audiencia_of_Panama" title="Real Audiencia of Panama">
        Real Audiencia of Panama
       </a>
       during the 16th century, then part of
       <a href="/wiki/Castilla_de_Oro" title="Castilla de Oro">
        Castilla de Oro
       </a>
       , with its capital in Panama, during the 17th century, and after independence from Spain, Panama voluntarily became part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Gran_Colombia" title="Republic of Gran Colombia">
        Republic of Gran Colombia
       </a>
       along with Venezuela and Ecuador, with its capital in Bogota. From the colonial times and periods and also during most of the 19th century and until 1903, and even though there are still lexical similarities shared by the two countries (e.g.,
       <i>
        pelao
       </i>
       in both
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       means "kid" or "child"), phonetically, Panamanian Spanish is very similar with the Spanish as spoken in the coastal areas around the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       , specifically
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       and the Atlantic coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Panamanian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main characteristic of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       sound at the end of a syllable or word, such as in the word
       <i>
        cascada
       </i>
       , pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kahˈkaða]
       </span>
       (like "h" in the English word "he") instead of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kasˈkaða]
       </span>
       . The aspiration is also observed in the coastal regions of Peru and Ecuador and in Chile, Argentina and Uruguay, and in Andalusia and the Canary Islands of Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another change observed in Panamanian Spanish is the deaffrication of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       (as "ch" in the English word "chips") to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (as "sh" in the English word "she") so
       <i>
        muchacho
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [muˈʃaʃo]
       </span>
       , rather than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [muˈtʃatʃo]
       </span>
       . It is found primarily among less educated speakers, but it can sometimes be observed among better educated speakers, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       is realized as glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Panamanian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lexically, Panamanian Spanish presents a variety of new terms introduced and being incorporated into the daily language all the time. The following quotation shows some common Panamanian expressions:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "Vecina, yo no soy
        <i>
         vidajena
        </i>
        , y no me gusta esa
        <i>
         vaina
        </i>
        ... pero te voy a contar un
        <i>
         bochinche
        </i>
        ...
        <br/>
        pero si me das de comer un poco de
        <i>
         chicheme
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         concolón
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         carimañola
        </i>
        , sancocho y mondongo....
        <br/>
        Ese
        <i>
         man
        </i>
        flacuchento y
        <i>
         ñato
        </i>
        vestido de
        <i>
         guayabera
        </i>
        azul y
        <i>
         sombrero montuno
        </i>
        que viene allí ... Su
        <i>
         motete
        </i>
        ya no tiene ñame, guineo ni
        <i>
         guandú
        </i>
        . Lo que tiene es un
        <i>
         pocotón
        </i>
        de
        <i>
         chécheres
        </i>
        . Según la comadre
        <i>
         fula
        </i>
        <i>
         radiobemba
        </i>
        , el cambio en ese
        <i>
         laopé
        </i>
        no se debe a una
        <i>
         macuá
        </i>
        ..." ¹
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       ¹From Carlos "Cubena" Guillermo Wilson, "El aporte cultural de la etnia negra en Panamá"
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://diadelaetnia.homestead.com/cubena.html" rel="nofollow">
        [1]
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       [Note:
       <i>
        laopé
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        pelao
       </i>
       ("boy")]
      </p>
