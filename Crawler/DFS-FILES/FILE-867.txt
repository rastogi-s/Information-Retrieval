      <p>
       <b>
        Chilean Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español chileno
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español de Chile
        </span>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         castellano de Chile
        </span>
        )
       </i>
       is any of several varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in most of
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       . Chilean Spanish dialects have distinctive pronunciation, grammar, vocabulary, and
       <a href="/wiki/Slang" title="Slang">
        slang
       </a>
       usage that differ from those of
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        standard Spanish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_and_accents">
        Variation and accents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variation and accents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Chilote_Spanish" title="Chilote Spanish">
        Chilote Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuyano_Spanish" title="Cuyano Spanish">
        Cuyano Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In Chile, there are not many differences between the Spanish spoken in the northern, central and southern areas of the country,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1981_31_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canfield_1981_31-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although there are notable differences in zones of the far south—such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ays%C3%A9n_Region" title="Aysén Region">
        Aysén
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magallanes_y_la_Ant%C3%A1rtica_Chilena_Region" title="Magallanes y la Antártica Chilena Region">
        Magallanes
       </a>
       (mainly along the border with Argentina), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chilo%C3%A9" title="Chiloé">
        Chiloé
       </a>
       —and in
       <a href="/wiki/Arica" title="Arica">
        Arica
       </a>
       in the extreme north. There is, however, much variation in the Spanish spoken by different social classes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In rural areas from Santiago to Valdivia, Chilean Spanish shows the historical influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cast%C3%BAo" title="Castúo">
        Castúo
       </a>
       dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       (Spain),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but some authors point to the Spanish province of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       and more specifically to the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       as an even greater influence on the historical development of Chilean Spanish.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics_and_phonology">
        Phonetics and phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonetics and phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are a number of phonetic features common to most Chilean accents, though none of them individually are unique to Chilean Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rather, it is the particular combination of features that sets Chilean Spanish apart from other regional Spanish dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These features include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          Yeísmo
         </a>
        </i>
        , the historical merger of the
        <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
         phoneme
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        (spelled ⟨ll⟩) with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʝ/
        </span>
        (spelled ⟨y⟩). For speakers with
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        , the verbs
        <i>
         cayó
        </i>
        's/he fell' and
        <i>
         calló
        </i>
        's/he fell silent' are
        <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
         homophones
        </a>
        , both pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʝo]
        </span>
        . (In dialects that lack
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        , maintaining the historical distinction, the two words are pronounced respectively
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʝo]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʎo]
        </span>
        .)
        <i>
         Yeísmo
        </i>
        characterizes the speech of most Spanish-speakers both in Spain and in the Americas. In Chile, there are some speakers who are not
        <i>
         yeístas
        </i>
        and maintain the distinction, mainly in a dwindling few Andean areas south of Santiago.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1981_31_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield_1981_31-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Like all American dialects of Spanish, Chilean Spanish has
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        : the traditional phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        . In much of the Andean region, the merged phoneme is pronounced as
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apicoalveolar
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
          s̺
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , a sound transitional between
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminodental
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        . This phonetic trait (unique in the Americas) is associated with a large number of northern Spanish settlers in Andean Chile.
       </li>
       <li>
        Word- and syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is often aspirated to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        or lost entirely, another feature common to many varieties of Spanish in the Americas, as well as to the Canary Islands and the southern half of Spain. Whether final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        aspirates or is elided depends on a number of social, regional, and phonological factors, but in general, aspiration is most frequent when preceding a consonant. Complete elision is most commonly found word-finally, and it carries a sociolinguistic stigma.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Thus,
        <i>
         los chilenos
        </i>
        '(the) Chileans' can be
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [lɔh t͡ʃiˈleːnɔ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velar" title="Velar">
         velar
        </a>
        consonants
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        are fronted or
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
         palatalized
        </a>
        before
        <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
         front vowels
        </a>
        . Thus,
        <i>
         queso
        </i>
        'cheese',
        <i>
         guía
        </i>
        'guide', and
        <i>
         jinete
        </i>
        'rider/horseman' are pronounced respectively
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈceːso]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈɟi.a]
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [çiˈn̪eːt̪e]
        </span>
        . For
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        , it is pronounced either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        in other phonological environments; thus
        <i>
         caja
        </i>
        'box' and
        <i>
         rojo
        </i>
        'red' are pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaxa]
        </span>
        ~
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaha]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈroxo]
        </span>
        ~
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈroho]
        </span>
        respectively.
       </li>
       <li>
        Between vowels and word-finally,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        commonly elides or
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         lenites
        </a>
        (a process common throughout the Spanish-speaking world), so that
        <i>
         contado
        </i>
        'told' and
        <i>
         ciudad
        </i>
        'city' are
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kon̪ˈt̪aːo]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [sjuˈð̞aː]
        </span>
        respectively. Elision is less common in formal or upper-class speech.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless postalveolar affricate">
         voiceless postalveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t͡ʃ/
        </span>
        is pronounced as a fricative
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        by many lower-class speakers (thus,
        <i>
         Chile
        </i>
        is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈʃiːle]
        </span>
        ). This type of pronunciation is viewed as very undesirable. Other variants are more fronted, and include an
        <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
         alveolar
        </a>
        affricate,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡s]
        </span>
        , and an even more fronted
        <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
         dental
        </a>
        affricate,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless dental sibilant affricate">
          t̪͡s̪
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , mostly used by the upper class.
       </li>
       <li>
        Unstressed word-final vowels are often devoiced.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax_and_grammar">
        Syntax and grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Syntax and grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Doubling the object clitics
        <i>
         me
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         te
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         se
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         lo(s)
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         la(s)
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         le(s)
        </i>
        before and after the verb is common in less-educated or lower-class speech. For example, 'I'm going to go' becomes
        <i>
         me voy a ir
         <b>
          me
         </b>
        </i>
        (standard Spanish accepts
        <i>
         me voy a ir
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         voy a irme
        </i>
        ). 'I'm going to give them to you' becomes
        <i>
         te las voy a dár
         <b>
          telas
         </b>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Que%C3%ADsmo" title="Queísmo">
         queísmo
        </a>
        (when
        <i>
         que
        </i>
        is used instead of
        <i>
         de que
        </i>
        ) is socially accepted and used in the media, while the
        <a href="/wiki/Deque%C3%ADsmo" title="Dequeísmo">
         dequeísmo
        </a>
        (using
        <i>
         de que
        </i>
        instead of just
        <i>
         que
        </i>
        ) is somewhat stigmatized.
       </li>
       <li>
        In common speech, the conjugations of the
        <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
         Imperative mood
        </a>
        of a small number of verbs tend to be replaced with the indicative singular third person. For example, the 2nd person singular imperative of
        <i>
         poner
        </i>
        'to put',
        <i>
         pon
        </i>
        , becomes
        <i>
         pone
        </i>
        ; the one of
        <i>
         hacer
        </i>
        'to do',
        <i>
         haz
        </i>
        , becomes
        <i>
         hace
        </i>
        ; and the one of
        <i>
         salir
        </i>
        'to exit',
        <i>
         sal
        </i>
        , becomes
        <i>
         sale
        </i>
        . Ex:
        <i>
         hace lo que te pedí
        </i>
        'do what I asked'. However, that is not allowed in formal speaking. Chilean Spanish speakers also replace the etymological 2nd person singular imperative of the verb
        <i>
         ir
        </i>
        'to go',
        <i>
         ve
        </i>
        , with the 2nd person singular imperative of
        <i>
         andar
        </i>
        'to walk',
        <i>
         anda
        </i>
        , while
        <i>
         ve
        </i>
        is reserved for the verb
        <i>
         ver
        </i>
        'to see'. Ex:
        <i>
         ve la hora
        </i>
        'look at the time'.
       </li>
       <li>
        Another feature to note is the lack of use of the possessive
        <i>
         nuestro
        </i>
        'our', which is usually replaced by
        <i>
         de nosotros
        </i>
        'of us'. Ex:
        <i>
         ándate a la casa
         <b>
          de nosotros
         </b>
        </i>
        lit. 'go to the house of us', instead of
        <i>
         ándate a nuestra casa
        </i>
        'go to our house'.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is very common in Chile, as in many other Latin American countries, to use the diminutive suffixes
        <i>
         -ito
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         -ita
        </i>
        . They do not only mean 'little', as in
        <i>
         perrito
        </i>
        'little dog' or
        <i>
         casita
        </i>
        'little house', but also have the additional functions of expressing affection, as with
        <i>
         mamita
        </i>
        'mummy, mommy'), or of diminishing the urgency, directness or importance of something, to make something annoying seem more pleasant.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        So, if someone says
        <i>
         espérese un momentito
        </i>
        lit. 'wait a little moment', it does not mean that the moment will be short, but that the speaker wants to make waiting more palatable while possibly hinting that the moment may turn out to be quite long.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns_and_verbs">
        Pronouns and verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pronouns and verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Chileans use the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuteo" title="Tuteo">
         tuteo
        </a>
       </i>
       forms for the intimate second person singular. Voseo is common in Chile, with both pronominal and verbal voseo being widely used in the spoken language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Chile there are at least four grades of formality:
      </p>
      <p>
       1. Pronominal and verbal voseo, that is, the use of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (with the corresponding voseo verbs).
       <br/>
       For example:
       <i>
        vos sabís
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos venís
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos hablái
       </i>
       , etc.
       <br/>
       This combination occurs only in very informal situations and should be approached and used with caution by foreigners. It is always considered rude and insulting but is tolerated and enjoyed as part of friendly bonding and banter. However, with even a slight change in intonation it can change from a tone of friendly banter to a form of insult in a heated argument, even among friends. Non-natives should refrain from using
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       until sufficient understanding of its use is gained.
      </p>
      <p>
       2. Verbal voseo, using the pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       .
       <br/>
       For example:
       <i>
        tú sabís
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tú venís
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tú hablái
       </i>
       , etc.
       <br/>
       This kind of voseo is the predominant form used in the spoken language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It should never be used in formal situations or with people one is not very familiar with.
      </p>
      <p>
       3. Standard tuteo.
       <br/>
       For example:
       <i>
        tú sabes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tú vienes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tú hablas
       </i>
       , etc.
       <br/>
       This is the only acceptable way of writing the intimate second person singular. Its use in spoken language is reserved for slightly more formal situations such as (some) child-to-parent, teacher-to-student or peer-to-peer relations among people who are not familiar with each other.
      </p>
      <p>
       4. The use of the pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
       <br/>
       For example:
       <i>
        usted sabe
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        usted viene
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        usted habla
       </i>
       , etc.
       <br/>
       Used for all business and other formal interactions (e.g. student-to-teacher, but not always teacher-to-student), as well as "upwards" in situations where one person is considered to be well respected, older or of an obviously higher social standing. Stricter parents will demand this kind of speech from their children as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo#Present_Indicative" title="Voseo">
        The Chilean voseo conjugation
       </a>
       has only three irregular verbs in the present indicative:
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       'to be',
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       'to go', and
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       'to have' (auxiliary).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ser">
        Ser
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ser">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Chile there are various ways to say 'you are' to one person.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vo(s) soi
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vo(s) erí(s)
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Tú soi
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Tú erí(s)
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Tú eres
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Usted es
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The first four are considered uneducated speaking and the ending
       <i>
        (s)
       </i>
       in these forms is omitted or aspirated, and the educated speaker employs only the last two cases.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Argentine_and_Rioplatense_influence">
        Argentine and Rioplatense influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Argentine and Rioplatense influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Chilean Spanish there is lexical influence from Argentine dialects, which suggests a
       <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">
        covert prestige
       </a>
       , since Chilean attitudes toward Argentine cultural traits are often negative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argentinismos2014_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Argentinismos2014-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lexical influences cut across the different social strata of Chile. Argentine summer tourism in Chile and Chilean tourism in Argentina provide a channel for influence on the speech of the upper class.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argentinismos2014_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Argentinismos2014-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The middle classes receive Argentine influence by watching
       <a href="/wiki/Association_football" title="Association football">
        football
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">
        cable television
       </a>
       and Argentine programs on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_television" title="Broadcast television">
        broadcast television
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argentinismos2014_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Argentinismos2014-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Cuarta" title="La Cuarta">
         La Cuarta
        </a>
       </i>
       , a popular Chilean newspaper, regularly employs words and expressions from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunfardo" title="Lunfardo">
        lunfardo
       </a>
       slang of the Buenos Aires region. Usually Chileans do not recognize the Argentine borrowings as such, claiming they are Chilean terms and expressions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argentinismos2014_18-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Argentinismos2014-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The relation between Argentine dialects and Chilean Spanish is one of asymmetric permeability, with Chilean Spanish adopting sayings from Argentine variants but usually not the reverse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Argentinismos2014_18-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Argentinismos2014-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: French, German and English loanwords">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are some expressions of non-Hispanic European origin such as
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        British
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        German
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        French
       </a>
       . They came with the arrival of the European immigrants in the 19th and 20th centuries. There is also a certain influence from the mass media.
      </p>
      <div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
       <ul>
        <li>
         <i>
          bifé
         </i>
         - piece of furniture, from
         <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
          French
         </a>
         <i>
          buffet
         </i>
         .
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          bistec
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          bisté
         </i>
         - meat, from
         <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
          English
         </a>
         'beefsteak'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          budín
         </i>
         - pudding, from the English 'pudding'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          chutear
         </i>
         - to shoot, from English 'shoot'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          clóset
         </i>
         - closet, from English 'closet'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          confort
         </i>
         - toilet paper, from French
         <i>
          confort
         </i>
         ; a brand name for toilet paper.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
          <a href="#cite_note-24">
           [24]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          guachimán
         </i>
         - ship watchman, from English 'watchman'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          hacer zaping
         </i>
         - to change channel whilst watching TV, from English 'to zap'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          jaibón
         </i>
         - upper class, from English 'high born'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          kuchen
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          cujen
         </i>
         - A kind of fruit cake, from
         <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
          German
         </a>
         <i>
          Kuchen
         </i>
         .
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          lobear
         </i>
         - to lobby, from English 'to lobby'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          livin
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          living
         </i>
         - living room, from English 'living room'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          lumpen
         </i>
         - lower class people, from German
         <i>
          Lumpenproletariat
         </i>
         .
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          luquear
         </i>
         - to look, from English 'to look'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          marraqueta
         </i>
         - a kind of bread, from French
         <i>
          Marraquette
         </i>
         , surname of the Frenchmen who invented it.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          panqueque
         </i>
         - pancake, from English 'pancake'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          overol
         </i>
         - overall, from English 'overall'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          short
         </i>
         - short trousers, from English 'short trousers'.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          strudel
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          estrudel
         </i>
         - dessert, from German
         <i>
          Strudel
         </i>
         , a typical German and Austrian dessert.
        </li>
        <li>
         <i>
          vestón
         </i>
         - jacket, from French
         <i>
          veston
         </i>
         .
        </li>
       </ul>
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Example">
        Example
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chilean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Example">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An example of a text in normal, carefully spoken Latin American Spanish and the same text with very relaxed pronunciation in informal Chilean Spanish of lower class people:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Text
        </th>
        <td>
         <i>
          ¡Cómo corrieron los chilenos Salas y Zamorano! Pelearon como leones. Chocaron una y otra vez contra la defensa azul. ¡Qué gentío llenaba el estadio! En verdad fue una jornada inolvidable. Ajustado cabezazo de Salas y ¡gol! Al celebrar [Salas] resbaló y se rasgó la camiseta.
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Pronunciation
         <br/>
         ("Standard" Latin American Spanish)
        </th>
        <td>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          [ˈkomo koˈrjeɾon los tʃiˈlenos ˈsalas i samoˈɾano | peleˈaɾoŋ ˈkomo ˈle‿ones | tʃoˈkaɾon ˈuna j‿ˈot̪ɾa ˈβ̞es ˈkon̪t̪ɾa la ð̞eˈfens aˈsul | ˈke xen̪ˈt̪io ʝeˈnaβ̞a‿el esˈt̪að̞jo | em beɾˈð̞að̞ ˈfwe‿una xoɾˈnað̞a‿inolβ̞iˈð̞aβ̞le | axusˈt̪að̞o kaβ̞eˈsaso ð̞e ˈsalas i ˈɣ̞ol | al seleˈβ̞ɾaɾ rezβ̞aˈlo‿i se razˈɣ̞o la kamiˈset̪a]
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Pronunciation
         <br/>
         (Chilean Spanish)
        </th>
        <td>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          [ˈkoːmo kɔˈɾjeːɾon lɔh ʃiˈleːno ˈsaːla‿i samoˈɾaːno | peˈljaːɾoŋ komo ˈljoːnɛh | ʃoˈkaːɾon ˈuːna j‿ot͡ɹ̝̝̥a ˈʋeːh kont͡ɹ̝̥a la‿eˈfeːns aˈsuːl | ˈceː çenˈt̪iːo jeˈnaː‿el ehˈt̪aːð̞jo | ʔeɱ vɛɹˈð̞aː ˈfweː‿una xonˈnaː‿inolˈʋiaːu̯le | ʔaxuhˈt̪aːo kaʋeˈsaːso‿e ˈsaːla‿i ˈɣ̞oːl | ʔal seleˈvɾaː ɹ̝ɛfaˈloː‿i se ɹ̝aˈxoː la kamiˈseːt̪a]
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Translation
        </th>
        <td>
         "How those Chileans
         <a href="/wiki/Marcelo_Salas" title="Marcelo Salas">
          Salas
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Iv%C3%A1n_Zamorano" title="Iván Zamorano">
          Zamorano
         </a>
         ran! They fought like lions. They beat again and again against
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italy_National_Football_Team" title="Italy National Football Team">
          the blues'
         </a>
         defense. What a crowd filled the stadium! In truth it was an unforgettable day. A tight header from Salas and... goal! Celebrating, Salas slid and ripped his shirt."
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
