      <p>
       The term "
       <b>
        Old World
       </b>
       " is used in the west to refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">
        Afro-Eurasia
       </a>
       , or the eastern hemisphere), regarded collectively as the part of the
       <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">
        world
       </a>
       known to its population before contact with the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">
        Oceania
       </a>
       (the western hemisphere).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is used in the context of, and contrasts with, the
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">
        Oceania
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Old World is roughly coterminous with the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">
        Eastern Hemisphere
       </a>
       ; however, the parts of Europe and Africa that are in the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">
        Western Hemisphere
       </a>
       are still considered part of the Old World, whereas Oceanic Eastern Hemisphere countries like
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       are not.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the context of
       <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
        archaeology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/World_history" title="World history">
        world history
       </a>
       , the term "Old World" includes those parts of the world which were in (indirect) cultural contact from the
       <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">
        Bronze Age
       </a>
       onwards, resulting in the parallel development of the early
       <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
        civilizations
       </a>
       , mostly in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperate" title="Temperate">
        temperate
       </a>
       zone between roughly the
       <a href="/wiki/45th_parallel_north" title="45th parallel north">
        45th
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/25th_parallel_north" title="25th parallel north">
        25th
       </a>
       parallels, in the area of the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Mediterranean_region" title="History of the Mediterranean region">
        Mediterranean
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">
        Mesopotamia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iranian_plateau" title="Iranian plateau">
        Persian plateau
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_China#Ancient_China" title="History of China">
        China
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       These regions were connected via the
       <a href="/wiki/Silk_Road" title="Silk Road">
        Silk Road
       </a>
       trade route, and they have a pronounced
       <a href="/wiki/Iron_Age" title="Iron Age">
        Iron Age
       </a>
       period following the Bronze Age. In cultural terms, the Iron Age was accompanied by the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_Age" title="Axial Age">
        Axial Age
       </a>
       , referring to cultural, philosophical and religious developments eventually leading to the emergence of the historical
       <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">
        Western
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenistic_philosophy" title="Hellenistic philosophy">
        Hellenism
       </a>
       , "
       <a href="/wiki/Classics" title="Classics">
        classical
       </a>
       "), Eastern (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zoroastrian" title="Zoroastrian">
        Zoroastrian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abrahamic" title="Abrahamic">
        Abrahamic
       </a>
       ) and Far Eastern (
       <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Buddhism" title="Buddhism">
        Buddhism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jainism" title="Jainism">
        Jainism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Confucianism" title="Confucianism">
        Confucianism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Taoism" title="Taoism">
        Taoism
       </a>
       ) cultural spheres.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The concept of the three
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continents
       </a>
       in the Old World, viz.
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       , goes back to
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">
        classical antiquity
       </a>
       . Their boundaries as defined by
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       and other geographers of antiquity were drawn along the
       <a href="/wiki/Nile" title="Nile">
        Nile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Don_River_(Russia)" title="Don River (Russia)">
        Don
       </a>
       rivers.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This definition remained influential throughout the Middle Ages (see
       <a href="/wiki/T_and_O_map" title="T and O map">
        T and O map
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern_period" title="Early Modern period">
        Early Modern period
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_names">
        Other names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_World&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">
        mainland
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Afro-Eurasia" title="Afro-Eurasia">
        Afro-Eurasia
       </a>
       (excluding
       <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">
        islands
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
        British Isles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">
        Sri Lanka
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Madagascar" title="Madagascar">
        Madagascar
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">
        Malay Archipelago
       </a>
       ) has been referred to as the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/World_Island" title="World Island">
         World Island
        </a>
       </i>
       . The term may have been coined by Sir
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Halford_John_Mackinder" title="Halford John Mackinder">
        Halford John Mackinder
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Geographical_Pivot_of_History" title="The Geographical Pivot of History">
         The Geographical Pivot of History
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The equivalent of the Old World had names in some of its ancient cultures, including
       <a href="/wiki/Midgard" title="Midgard">
        Midgard
       </a>
       in Germanic cosmology, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oikoumene" title="Oikoumene">
        Oikoumene
       </a>
       among the Greeks.
      </p>
