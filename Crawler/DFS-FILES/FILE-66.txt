      <p>
       The
       <b>
        International Meridian Conference
       </b>
       was a conference held in October 1884 in
       <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
        Washington, D.C.
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , to determine a
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       for international use.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imc_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-imc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The conference was held at the request of
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">
        U.S. President
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Chester_A._Arthur" title="Chester A. Arthur">
        Chester A. Arthur
       </a>
       . The subject to discuss was the choice of "a meridian to be employed as a common zero of longitude and standard of time reckoning throughout the world".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imc_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-imc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It resulted in selection of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwich_Meridian" title="Greenwich Meridian">
        Greenwich Meridian
       </a>
       as the international standard for zero degrees
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-imc_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-imc-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Meridian_Conference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       By the 1870s there was pressure both to establish a prime meridian for worldwide navigation purposes and to unify local times for railway timetables. The first
       <a href="/wiki/International_Geographical_Union" title="International Geographical Union">
        International Geographical Congress
       </a>
       , held in Antwerp in 1871, passed a motion in favour of the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian_(Greenwich)" title="Prime meridian (Greenwich)">
        Greenwich Meridian
       </a>
       for (smaller scale) passage
       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">
        charts
       </a>
       , suggesting that it should become mandatory within 15 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Comparative-time-table.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1891" data-file-width="1351" height="490" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Comparative-time-table.jpg/350px-Comparative-time-table.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Comparative-time-table.jpg/525px-Comparative-time-table.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Comparative-time-table.jpg/700px-Comparative-time-table.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Comparative-time-table.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Local times across the United States, major towns
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In Britain, the Great Western Railway had standardised time by 1840 and in 1847 the
       <i>
        Railway Clearing Union
       </i>
       decreed that "
       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich_Mean_Time" title="Greenwich Mean Time">
        GMT
       </a>
       be adopted at all stations as soon as the General Post Office permitted it". The Post Office was by this time transmitting time signals from Greenwich by telegraph to most parts of the country to set the clocks. By January 1848,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Bradshaw#Bradshaw's_railway_timetables" title="George Bradshaw">
        Bradshaw's railway guide
       </a>
       showed the unified times and met with general approval, although legal disputes meant that it was not until 1890 that GMT was formally established across the UK.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United States, the problems were much more severe, with one table showing over 100
       <a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">
        local
       </a>
       times varying by more than 3 hours. In 1870,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_F._Dowd" title="Charles F. Dowd">
        Charles F. Dowd
       </a>
       published a pamphlet titled
       <i>
        A System of national time and its application
       </i>
       advocating three time zones across the country based on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_meridian" title="Washington meridian">
        Washington meridian
       </a>
       , modifying this to four zones based on the Greenwich meridian in 1872.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first proposal for a consistent treatment of time worldwide was a memoir entitled "
       <i>
        Terrestrial Time
       </i>
       " by
       <a href="/wiki/Sandford_Fleming" title="Sandford Fleming">
        Sandford Fleming
       </a>
       , at the time the chief engineer of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Pacific_Railway" title="Canadian Pacific Railway">
        Canadian Pacific Railway
       </a>
       , presented to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Institute" title="Canadian Institute">
        Canadian Institute
       </a>
       in 1876. This envisaged clocks showing 24-hour
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        universal time
       </a>
       with an extra dial having a local time rounded to the nearest hour.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also pointed out that many of the corrections for
       <a href="/wiki/Local_mean_time" title="Local mean time">
        local mean time
       </a>
       were greater than those involved in abandoning
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_time" title="Solar time">
        solar time
       </a>
       . In 1878/9, he produced modified proposals using the Greenwich meridian. Fleming's two papers were considered so important that in June 1879 the British Government forwarded copies to eighteen foreign countries and to various scientific bodies in England.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the same time the
       <i>
        American Metrological Society
       </i>
       produced a "Report on Standard Time" by
       <a href="/wiki/Cleveland_Abbe" title="Cleveland Abbe">
        Cleveland Abbe
       </a>
       , chief of the United States Weather Service proposing essentially the same scheme.
      </p>
      <p>
       These proposals did not meet universal approval in the scientific community, being opposed by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Rodgers_(American_Civil_War_naval_officer)" title="John Rodgers (American Civil War naval officer)">
        John Rodgers
       </a>
       , Superintendent of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Observatory" title="United States Naval Observatory">
        Naval Observatory, Washington
       </a>
       and British
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer_Royal" title="Astronomer Royal">
        Astronomer Royal
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_Airy" title="George Airy">
        George Airy
       </a>
       who had both established wire services supporting local time in various cities. The Naval Observatory had also foiled the onward transmission of the Greenwich time signal from
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">
        Harvard
       </a>
       , where it was received via
       <a href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telegraph_cable" title="Transatlantic telegraph cable">
        transatlantic cable
       </a>
       and used to time a signal ball in
       <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
        Boston
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The International Meridian Conference had its origins in the Third International Geographical Congress held in
       <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
        Venice
       </a>
       in 1881, in which the establishment of a universal prime meridian and a uniform standard of time was high on the agenda. The Seventh
       <i>
        International Geodesic Conference
       </i>
       in Rome in October 1883 then thrashed out most of the technical details, leaving the diplomatic agreements to a later conference. The United States passed an Act of Congress on 3 August 1882 authorizing the President to call an international conference to fix on a common prime meridian for time and longitude throughout the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Before the invitations were sent out on December 1, the joint efforts of Abbe, Fleming and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Frederick_Allen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Frederick Allen (page does not exist)">
        William Frederick Allen
       </a>
       , Secretary of the US railways'
       <i>
        General Time Convention
       </i>
       and Managing Editor of the
       <i>
        Travellers' Official Guide to the Railways
       </i>
       , had brought the US railway companies to an agreement which led to standard railway time being introduced at noon on 18 November 1883 across the nation. Although this was not legally established until 1918, there was thus a strong sense of
       <i>
        fait accompli
       </i>
       that preceded the conference, although setting local times was not part of the remit of the conference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Participants">
        Participants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Meridian_Conference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Participants">

        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ):
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        That it is the opinion of this Congress that it is desirable to adopt a single prime meridian for all nations, in place of the multiplicity of initial meridians which now exist. (This resolution was unanimously adopted.)
       </li>
       <li>
        That the Conference proposes to the Governments here represented the adoption of the meridian passing through the centre of the transit instrument at the Observatory of Greenwich as the initial meridian for longitude. (Ayes, 22; noes, 1; abstaining, 2.)
       </li>
       <li>
        That from this meridian longitude shall be counted in two directions up to 180 degrees, east longitude being plus and west longitude minus. (Ayes, 14; noes, 5; abstaining, 6.)
       </li>
       <li>
        That the Conference proposes the adoption of a universal day for all purposes for which it may be found convenient, and which shall not interfere with the use of local or standard time where desirable. (Ayes, 23; abstaining, 2.)
       </li>
       <li>
        That this universal day is to be a mean solar day; is to begin for all the world at the moment of mean midnight of the initial meridian, coinciding with the beginning of the civil day and date of that meridian; and is to be counted from zero up to twenty-four hours. (Ayes, 14; noes, 3; abstaining, 7.)
       </li>
       <li>
        That the Conference expresses the hope that as soon as may be practicable the astronomical and nautical days will be arranged everywhere to begin at midnight.
       </li>
       <li>
        That the Conference expresses the hope that the technical studies designed to regulate and extend the application of the decimal system to the division of angular space and of time shall be resumed, so as to permit the extension of this application to all cases in which it presents real advantages. (Ayes, 21; abstaining, 3.)
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Resolution 2, fixing the meridian at Greenwich, was passed 22–1 (San Domingo, now the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       , voted against); France and Brazil abstained. The French did not adopt the Greenwich meridian until 1911.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Resolution 4 expressly exempts standard time from the universal day. Although two delegates, including
       <a href="/wiki/Sandford_Fleming" title="Sandford Fleming">
        Sandford Fleming
       </a>
       , proposed the adoption of standard time by all nations, other delegates objected, stating that it was outside the purview of the conference, so neither proposal was subjected to a vote. Thus the conference did
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       adopt any
       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">
        time zones
       </a>
       , contrary to popular belief.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarding resolution 6: Great Britain had already shifted the beginning of the nautical day from noon, twelve hours before midnight, to midnight in 1805, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Trafalgar" title="Battle of Trafalgar">
        Battle of Trafalgar
       </a>
       . The astronomical day was shifted from noon, twelve hours after midnight, to midnight effective
       <span class="nowrap">
        1 January 1925
       </span>
       by a resolution of the newly formed
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The following resolutions were not adopted:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        On 13 Oct : "Resolved, That the initial meridian should have a character of absolute neutrality. It should be chosen exclusively so as to secure to science and to international commerce all possible advantages, and especially should cut no great continent—neither Europe nor America." - "Twenty-one noes and three ayes." - France, Brazil, San Domingo voted in favor.
       </li>
      </ol>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Outcomes">
        Outcomes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Meridian_Conference&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Outcomes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main issue at the conference, apart from procedural issues such as the provision of an authorized French translation of the proceedings, was France's insistence that the meridian should have a strictly neutral character in the same way that they maintained that the
       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
        metre
       </a>
       was a neutral measure. This requirement conflicted with the need to base measurements on an established observatory on land and Fleming's proposal of using the
       <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">
        anti-meridian
       </a>
       of Greenwich was not supported by the British delegation. In the end, the pragmatic argument for continuity with most nautical charts won the day and the French delegation abstained in the vote.
      </p>
      <p>
       By changing the start of the astronomical day from midday to midnight, the conference avoided an issue raised by the Geodetic Conference of a change of date happening in the middle of the day in Europe, so that the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">
        International Date Line
       </a>
       could be where we now have it.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the question of universal time, Fleming's opinion to one of the lead-up committees was borne out: "In my judgment, the nearest approach to it which may be attempted with any chance of success, is to have
       <i>
        first
       </i>
       , a primary standard time, based on the prime meridian that is to be used for non-local purposes;
       <i>
        second
       </i>
       , to have twenty-four secondary standard times to govern local reckoning."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There was discussion of setting zones as small as 10 minutes (2½°), but no motion was tabled, as there was little experience to guide the choice.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most European countries
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time#Adoption_in_various_countries" title="Universal Time">
        aligned their clocks with Greenwich
       </a>
       within ten years, Sweden and North America already having done so, and the trend continued. The French maintained Paris time till 1911 and the following year convened a second conference to address the differences between different observatories which had become apparent, leading to the establishment of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Time_Bureau" title="International Time Bureau">
        Bureau International de l'Heure
       </a>
       after
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        World War I
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
