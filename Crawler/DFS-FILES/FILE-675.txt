      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       This article is about phonetic reduction, which means a change in pronunciation. For the meaning in phonology, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_merger" title="Phonemic merger">
        Phonemic merger
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        vowel reduction
       </b>
       is any of various changes in the acoustic
       <i>
        quality
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       , which are related to changes in
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sonority_hierarchy" title="Sonority hierarchy">
        sonority
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">
        duration
       </a>
       , loudness, articulation, or position in the word (e.g. for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creek_language" title="Creek language">
        Creek language
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), and which are perceived as "weakening". It most often makes the vowels shorter as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       Such a vowel may be called
       <i>
        reduced
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        weak
       </i>
       . In contrast, an unreduced vowel may be described as
       <i>
        full
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        strong
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Weakening_of_vowel_articulation">
        Weakening of vowel articulation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Weakening of vowel articulation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="745" data-file-width="1032" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg/250px-Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg/375px-Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg/500px-Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peripheral_and_central_vowel_space.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cardinal vowel chart showing peripheral (white) and central (blue) vowel space, based on the chart in
         <a href="#CITEREFCollinsMees2003">
          Collins &amp; Mees (2003
         </a>
         :227)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Phonetic reduction most often involves a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-centralized_vowel" title="Mid-centralized vowel">
        centralization
       </a>
       of the vowel, that is, a reduction in the amount of movement of the tongue in pronouncing the vowel, as with the characteristic change of many unstressed vowels at the ends of English words to something approaching
       <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
        schwa
       </a>
       . A well-researched type of reduction is that of the neutralization of acoustic distinctions in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unstressed_vowel" title="Unstressed vowel">
        unstressed vowels
       </a>
       , which occurs in many languages. The most common reduced vowel is
       <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
        schwa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Whereas full vowels are distinguished by height, backness, and roundness, according to
       <a href="#CITEREFBolinger1986">
        Bolinger (1986)
       </a>
       , reduced unstressed vowels are largely unconcerned with height or roundness. English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ə/
       </span>
       , for example, may range phonetically from mid
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə]
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɐ]
       </span>
       to open
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [a]
       </span>
       ; English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɨ/
       </span>
       ranges from close
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɪ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       , to open-mid
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       . The primary distinction is that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɨ/
       </span>
       is further front than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ə/
       </span>
       , contrasted in the numerous English words ending in unstressed
       <i>
        -ia.
       </i>
       That is, the jaw, which to a large extent controls vowel height, tends to be relaxed when pronouncing reduced vowels. Similarly, English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɵ/
       </span>
       ranges through
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʊ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o]
       </span>
       ; although it may be labialized to varying degrees, the lips are relaxed in comparison to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /uː/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /oʊ/
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɔː/
       </span>
       . The primary distinction in words like
       <i>
        folio
       </i>
       is again one of backness. However, the backness distinction is not as great as that of full vowels; reduced vowels are also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centralization_(phonetics)" title="Centralization (phonetics)">
        centralized
       </a>
       , and are sometimes referred to by that term. They may also be called
       <i>
        obscure,
       </i>
       as there is no one-to-one correspondence between full and reduced vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBolinger1986347_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBolinger1986347-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sound duration is a common factor in reduction: In fast speech, vowels are reduced due to physical limitations of the articulatory organs, e.g., the tongue cannot move to a prototypical position fast or completely enough to produce a full-quality vowel (compare with
       <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(phonetics)" title="Clipping (phonetics)">
        clipping
       </a>
       ). Different languages have different types of vowel reduction, and this is one of the difficulties in
       <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">
        language acquisition
       </a>
       ; see, e.g., "
       <a href="/wiki/Non-native_pronunciations_of_English" title="Non-native pronunciations of English">
        Non-native pronunciations of English
       </a>
       " and "
       <a href="/wiki/Anglophone_pronunciation_of_foreign_languages" title="Anglophone pronunciation of foreign languages">
        Anglophone pronunciation of foreign languages
       </a>
       ". Vowel reduction of
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       speakers is a separate study.
      </p>
      <p>
       Stress-related vowel reduction is a principal factor in the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_ablaut" title="Indo-European ablaut">
        Indo-European ablaut
       </a>
       , as well as other changes reconstructed by
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical linguistics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several ways to distinguish full and reduced vowels in transcription. Some English dictionaries mark full vowels for secondary stress, so that e.g. ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˌɪ
       </span>
       ⟩ is a full unstressed
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɪ/
       </span>
       while ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ɪ
       </span>
       ⟩ is a reduced unstressed
       <i>
        schwi
       </i>
       . The vowel quality may be considered distinct, with reduced vowels centralized, e.g. with full ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʊ
       </span>
       ⟩ vs reduced ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ɵ
       </span>
       ⟩. Some transcriptions of English and Russian use non-IPA letters for reduced vowels, such as ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ᵻ
       </span>
       ⟩ and ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ᵿ
       </span>
       ⟩ in English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Such vowel reduction is one of the sources of distinction between a
       <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">
        spoken language
       </a>
       and its
       <a href="/wiki/Written_language" title="Written language">
        written counterpart
       </a>
       . Vernacular and formal speech often have different levels of vowel reduction, and so the term "vowel reduction" is also applied to differences in a
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        language variety
       </a>
       with respect to, e.g., the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_standard" title="Language standard">
        language standard
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoken_Finnish" title="Spoken Finnish">
        Finnish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       , and classical
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , are claimed to lack vowel reduction. Such languages are often called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable-timed_language" title="Syllable-timed language">
        syllable-timed languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the other end of the spectrum,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       is characterized by the reduction or loss of the unstressed vowels, mainly when they are in contact with the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It can be the case that the words
       <i>
        pesos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pesas
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        peces
       </i>
       are pronounced the same:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpesə̥s]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_inventory_reduction">
        Vowel inventory reduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vowel inventory reduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some cases phonetic vowel reduction may contribute to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        <i>
         phonemic
        </i>
        (phonological)
       </a>
       reduction, which means merger of
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       , induced by indistinguishable pronunciation. This sense of vowel reduction may occur by means other than vowel centralisation, however. Many Germanic languages, in their early stages, reduced the number of vowels that could occur in unstressed syllables, without (or before) clearly showing centralisation.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic" title="Proto-Germanic">
        Proto-Germanic
       </a>
       and its early descendant
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
        Gothic
       </a>
       still allowed more or less the full complement of vowels and diphthongs to appear in unstressed syllables, except notably short /e/, which merged with /i/. In early
       <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
        Old High German
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Saxon" title="Old Saxon">
        Old Saxon
       </a>
       , this had been reduced to five vowels (i, e, a, o, u, some with length distinction), later reduced further to just three short vowels (i/e, a, o/u). In
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       , likewise, only three vowels were written in unstressed syllables: a, i and u (their exact phonetic quality is unknown).
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       , meanwhile, distinguished only e, a, and u (again the exact phonetic quality is unknown).
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       , also shows reduction, but in differing degrees depending on dialect. The Valencian dialect reduces the number of possible vowels from seven to five in unstressed environments, merging
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɔ]
       </span>
       into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o]
       </span>
       . The central Catalan dialect goes even further, distinguishing only
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [u]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə ~ ɐ]
       </span>
       , with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [a]
       </span>
       becoming
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə ~ ɐ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɔ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o]
       </span>
       merging into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [u]
       </span>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , the vowels noted e and o in spelling are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o]
       </span>
       in unstressed syllables.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgarian">
        Bulgarian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Bulgarian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian language
       </a>
       the vowels
       <i>
        а
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        о
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        е
       </i>
       can be reduced when unstressed to
       <i>
        ъ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        у
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        и
       </i>
       , respectively. The most prevalent is
       <i>
        а
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        ъ
       </i>
       , while
       <i>
        о
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        у
       </i>
       is considered incorrect in literary speech. The last reduction
       <i>
        е
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        и
       </i>
       is prevalent in the more Eastern dialects of the language.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">
        Russian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Russian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction_in_Russian" title="Vowel reduction in Russian">
        Vowel reduction in Russian
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There are five vowel phonemes in Standard Russian. Vowels tend to merge when they are unstressed. The vowels /a/ and /o/ have the same unstressed allophones for a number of dialects and reduce to an unclear schwa. Unstressed /e/ may become more central if it does not merge with /i/.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other types of reduction are phonetic, such as that of high vowels (/i/ and /u/), which become near-close so этап ('stage') is pronounced [ɪˈtap], and мужчина ('man') is pronounced [mʊˈɕːinə].
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Slavic_languages">
        Early Slavic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early Slavic languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Havl%C3%ADk%27s_law" title="Havlík's law">
        Havlík's law
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Slavic" title="Proto-Slavic">
        Proto-Slavic
       </a>
       language possessed two short high vowels known as
       <a href="/wiki/Yer" title="Yer">
        yers
       </a>
       : a short high front vowel denoted as ĭ or ь, and a short back vowel denoted as ŭ or ъ. These vowels underwent reduction and were eventually deleted in certain positions in a word in the early Slavic languages, beginning from the late dialects of Proto-Slavic. This process is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Havl%C3%ADk%27s_law" title="Havlík's law">
        Havlík's law
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latin">
        Latin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_reduction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Latin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Latin#Phonology" title="History of Latin">
        History of Latin § Phonology
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Latin" title="Old Latin">
        Old Latin
       </a>
       had initial stress, and short vowels in non-initial syllables were frequently reduced. Long vowels were usually not reduced.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vowels reduced in different ways depending on the phonological environment. For instance, in most cases they reduced to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       . Before
       <i>
        l pinguis
       </i>
       , an
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       not followed by
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i iː l/
       </span>
       , they became Old Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       and Classical Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       . Before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       and some consonant clusters, they became
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           fáciō
          </i>
          , *
          <i>
           ád-faciō
          </i>
          &gt; Old Latin
          <i>
           fáciō
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           áfficiō
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        "make, affect"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           fáctos
          </i>
          , *
          <i>
           ád-factos
          </i>
          &gt;
          <i>
           fáctos
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           áffectos
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        "made, affected" (
        <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
         participles
        </a>
        )
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           sáltō
          </i>
          , *
          <i>
           én-saltō
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        &gt; Old Latin
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           sáltō
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           ínsoltō
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        "I jump, I jump on"
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         parō, *pe-par-ai
        </span>
        &gt; Latin
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           párō
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           péperī
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        "I give birth, I gave birth"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        Classical Latin
       </a>
       , stress
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_spelling_and_pronunciation#Accent" title="Latin spelling and pronunciation">
        changed position
       </a>
       , so in some cases reduced vowels became stressed. Stress moved to the penult if it was
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable_weight" title="Syllable weight">
        heavy
       </a>
       or the antepenult in other cases.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Classical Latin
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           fáciō
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           affíciō
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           fáctus
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           afféctus
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           sáltō
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           īnsúltō
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
