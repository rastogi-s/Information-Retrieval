      <p>
       <b>
        Sibilance
       </b>
       is an acoustic characteristic of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricative
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricate
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       of higher
       <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">
        amplitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">
        pitch
       </a>
       , made by directing a stream of air with the tongue towards the sharp edge of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">
        teeth
       </a>
       , which are held close together; a consonant that uses sibilance may be called a
       <b>
        sibilant
       </b>
       . Examples of sibilants are the consonants at the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       words
       <i>
        sip
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        zip
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ship
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chip
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        jump
       </i>
       , and the second consonant in
       <i>
        vision
       </i>
       . The symbols in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       used to denote the sibilant sounds in these words are, respectively,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s] [z] [ʃ] [tʃ] [dʒ] [ʒ]
       </span>
       . More specifically, the sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ] [dʒ]
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        chip
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        jump
       </i>
       , are affricates, whereas the rest are fricatives. Sibilants have a characteristically intense sound, which accounts for their paralinguistic use in getting one's attention (e.g. calling someone using "psst!" or quieting someone using "shhhh!").
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       <b>
        hissing
       </b>
       sibilants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [z]
       </span>
       , the back of the tongue forms a narrow channel (is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sulcalization" title="Sulcalization">
         grooved
        </a>
       </i>
       ) to focus the stream of air more intensely, resulting in a high pitch. With the
       <b>
        hushing
       </b>
       sibilants (occasionally termed
       <i>
        shibilants
       </i>
       ), such as English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       , the tongue is flatter, and the resulting pitch lower.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="We need cite that they are not grooved. (May 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A broader category is
       <b>
        stridents
       </b>
       , which include more fricatives than sibilants. Because all sibilants are also stridents, the terms are sometimes used interchangeably. However, the terms do not mean the same thing. The English stridents are
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f, v, s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/
       </span>
       . The English sibilants are a more high pitched subset of the stridents. The English sibilants are
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s, z, ʃ, ʒ, tʃ, dʒ/
       </span>
       .
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       are stridents, but not sibilants, because they are lower in pitch.
      </p>
      <p>
       "Stridency" refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Psychoacoustics" title="Psychoacoustics">
        perceptual
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_intensity" title="Sound intensity">
        intensity
       </a>
       of the sound of a sibilant consonant, or
       <b>
        obstacle fricatives
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        affricates
       </b>
       , which refers to the critical role of the teeth in producing the sound as an obstacle to the airstream. Non-sibilant fricatives and affricates produce their characteristic sound directly with the tongue or lips etc. and the place of contact in the mouth, without secondary involvement of the teeth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The characteristic intensity of sibilants means that small variations in tongue shape and position are perceivable, with the result that there are a large number of sibilant types that contrast in various languages.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Acoustics">
        Acoustics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Acoustics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sibilants are louder than their non-sibilant counterparts, and most of their acoustic energy occurs at higher frequencies than non-sibilant fricatives.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       has the most acoustic strength at around 8,000 Hz, but can reach as high as 10,000 Hz.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       has the bulk of its acoustic energy at around 4,000 Hz, but can extend up to around 8,000 Hz.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sibilant_types">
        Sibilant types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sibilant types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All sibilants are
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal consonants
       </a>
       (made with the tip or front part of the tongue). However, there is a great deal of variety among sibilants as to tongue shape, point of contact on the tongue, and point of contact on the upper side of the mouth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The following variables affect sibilant sound quality, and, along with their possible values, are ordered from sharpest (highest-pitched) to dullest (lowest-pitched):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Tongue shape:
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grooved_consonant" title="Grooved consonant">
         grooved
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal" title="Alveolo-palatal">
         alveolo-palatal
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palato-alveolar" title="Palato-alveolar">
         palato-alveolar
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflex" title="Retroflex">
         retroflex
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
         Place of articulation
        </a>
        (point of contact on the upper side of the mouth):
        <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
         dental
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denti-alveolar" title="Denti-alveolar">
         denti-alveolar
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
         alveolar
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postalveolar" title="Postalveolar">
         postalveolar
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Point of contact on the tongue:
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminal
        </a>
        "closed" (
        <a href="#Point_of_contact_on_the_tongue">
         see below
        </a>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminal
        </a>
        non-"closed",
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apical
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Subapical_consonant" title="Subapical consonant">
         subapical
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Generally, the values of the different variables co-occur so as to produce an overall sharper or duller sound. For example, a laminal denti-alveolar grooved sibilant occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       , and a subapical palatal retroflex sibilant occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tongue_shape">
        Tongue shape
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tongue shape">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The main distinction is the shape of the tongue. Most sibilants have a
       <a href="/wiki/Sulcalization" title="Sulcalization">
        groove
       </a>
       running down the centerline of the tongue that helps focus the airstream, but it's not known how widespread this is. In addition, the following tongue shapes are described, from sharpest and highest-pitched to dullest and lowest-pitched:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Hollow (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s z]
        </span>
        ): This hollow accepts a large volume of air that is forced through a typically narrow aperture that directs a high-velocity jet of air against the teeth, which results in a high-pitched, piercing "hissing" sound. Because of the prominence of these sounds, they are the most common and most stable of sibilants cross-linguistically. They occur in
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        , where they are denoted with a letter
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         z
        </i>
        , as in
        <i>
         soon
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         zone
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
           <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (May 2011)">
            dubious
           </span>
          </a>
          <span class="metadata">
           –
           <a href="/wiki/Talk:Sibilant#Dubious" title="Talk:Sibilant">
            discuss
           </a>
          </span>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal" title="Alveolo-palatal">
         Alveolo-palatal
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɕ ʑ]
        </span>
        ): with a convex, V-shaped tongue, and highly
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         palatalized
        </a>
        (middle of the tongue strongly raised or bowed).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palato-alveolar" title="Palato-alveolar">
         Palato-alveolar
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ ʒ]
        </span>
        ): with a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Domed_consonant" title="Domed consonant">
         "domed" tongue
        </a>
        (convex and moderately palatalized). These sounds occur in
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        , where they are denoted with letter combinations such as
        <i>
         sh
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         g
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         j
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         si
        </i>
        , as in
        <i>
         shin
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         chin
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         gin
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         vision
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflex" title="Retroflex">
         Retroflex
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʂ ʐ]
        </span>
        ): with a flat or concave tongue, and no palatalization. These sounds occur in a large number of varieties, some of which also go by other names (e.g. "flat postalveolar" or "
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apico-alveolar" title="Apico-alveolar">
         apico-alveolar
        </a>
        "). The
        <a href="/wiki/Subapical_consonant" title="Subapical consonant">
         subapical
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal
        </a>
        or "true" retroflex sounds are the very dullest and lowest-pitched of all the sibilants.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The latter three post-alveolar types of sounds are often known as "hushing" sounds because of their quality, as opposed to the "hissing" alveolar sounds. The alveolar sounds in fact occur in several varieties, in addition to the normal sound of English
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         Palatalized
        </a>
        : Sibilants can occur with or without raising the tongue body to the palate (
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         palatalization
        </a>
        ). Palatalized alveolars are transcribed e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [sʲ]
        </span>
        and occur in
        <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
         Russian
        </a>
        ; they sound similar to the cluster
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [sj]
        </span>
        occurring in the middle of the English phrase
        <i>
         miss you
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lisping: Alveolar sibilants made with the tip of the tongue (
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apical
        </a>
        ) near the upper teeth have a softer sound reminiscent of (but still sharper-sounding than) the "lisping"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [θ]
        </span>
        sound of English
        <i>
         think
        </i>
        . These sounds are relatively uncommon, but occur in some of the indigenous languages of
        <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
         California
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBright1978_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBright1978-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        as well as in the
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        dialects of eastern
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
         Andalusia
        </a>
        (southeast
        <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
         Spain
        </a>
        ), in cities such as
        <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">
         Granada
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Huelva" title="Huelva">
         Huelva
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Spain" title="Córdoba, Spain">
         Cordoba
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ja%C3%A9n,_Spain" title="Jaén, Spain">
         Jaén
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almeria" title="Almeria">
         Almeria
        </a>
        . In these dialects, the lisping sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s̺]
        </span>
        (sometimes indicated in Spanish
        <a href="/wiki/Dialectology" title="Dialectology">
         dialectology
        </a>
        as ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         s̄
        </span>
        ⟩) is the normal pronunciation of the letters
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         z
        </i>
        , as well as
        <i>
         c
        </i>
        before
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        , replacing the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [θ]
        </span>
        that occurs elsewhere in Andalucia.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Speaking non-technically, the retroflex consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʂ]
       </span>
       sounds somewhat like a mixture between the regular English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       of "ship" and a strong American "r"; while the alveolo-palatal consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɕ]
       </span>
       sounds somewhat like a mixture of English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       of "ship" and the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [sj]
       </span>
       in the middle of "miss you".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Place_of_articulation">
        Place of articulation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Place of articulation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sibilants can be made at any
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal
       </a>
       articulation, i.e. the tongue can contact the upper side of the mouth anywhere from the upper teeth (
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       ) to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">
        hard palate
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       ), with the in-between articulations being
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denti-alveolar" title="Denti-alveolar">
        denti-alveolar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postalveolar" title="Postalveolar">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Point_of_contact_on_the_tongue">
        Point of contact on the tongue
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Point of contact on the tongue">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The tongue can contact the upper side of the mouth with the very tip of the tongue (an
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apical
        </a>
       </i>
       articulation, e.g.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ̺]
       </span>
       ); with the surface just behind the tip, called the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongue_blade" title="Tongue blade">
         blade
        </a>
       </i>
       of the tongue (a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminal
        </a>
       </i>
       articulation, e.g.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ̻]
       </span>
       ); or with the underside of the tip (a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Subapical_consonant" title="Subapical consonant">
         subapical
        </a>
       </i>
       articulation). Apical and subapical articulations are always
       <i>
        tongue-up
       </i>
       , with the tip of the tongue above the teeth, while laminal articulations can be either tongue-up or
       <i>
        tongue-down
       </i>
       , with the tip of the tongue behind the lower teeth. This distinction is particularly important for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflex" title="Retroflex">
        retroflex
       </a>
       sibilants, because all three varieties can occur, with noticeably different sound qualities. For more information on these variants and their relation to sibilants, see the article on
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar consonants
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For tongue-down laminal articulations, an additional distinction can be made depending on where exactly behind the lower teeth the tongue tip is placed. A little ways back from the lower teeth is a hollow area (or pit) in the lower surface of the mouth. When the tongue tip rests in this hollow area, there is an empty space below the tongue (a
       <i>
        sublingual cavity
       </i>
       ), which results in a relatively duller sound. When the tip of the tongue rests against the lower teeth, there is no sublingual cavity, resulting in a sharper sound. Usually, the position of the tip of the tongue correlates with the grooved vs. hushing tongue shape so as to maximize the differences. However, the palato-alveolar sibilants in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Caucasian_languages" title="Northwest Caucasian languages">
        Northwest Caucasian languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh
       </a>
       are an exception. These sounds have the tongue tip resting directly against the lower teeth, which gives the sounds a quality that Catford describes as "hissing-hushing". Ladefoged and Maddieson
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       term this a "
       <i>
        closed
       </i>
       laminal postalveolar" articulation, and transcribe them (following Catford) as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŝ, ẑ]
       </span>
       , although this is not an IPA notation. See the article on
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar consonants
       </a>
       for more information.
      </p>

      <p>
       Diacritics can be used for finer detail. For example, apical and laminal alveolars can be specified as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̺]
       </span>
       <i>
        vs
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̻]
       </span>
       ; a
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       (or more likely
       <i>
        denti-alveolar
       </i>
       ) sibilant as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪]
       </span>
       ; a palatalized alveolar as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [sʲ]
       </span>
       ; and a generic "retracted sibilant" as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̠]
       </span>
       , a transcription frequently used for the sharper-quality types of retroflex consonants (e.g. the laminal "flat" type and the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apico-alveolar" title="Apico-alveolar">
        apico-alveolar
       </a>
       " type). There is no diacritic to denote the laminal "closed" articulation of palato-alveolars in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Caucasian_languages" title="Northwest Caucasian languages">
        Northwest Caucasian languages
       </a>
       , but they are sometimes provisionally transcribed as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŝ ẑ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_combinations">
        Possible combinations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible combinations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The attested possibilities, with exemplar languages, are as follows. Note that the IPA diacritics are simplified; some articulations would require two diacritics to be fully specified, but only one is used in order to keep the results legible without the need for
       <a href="/wiki/OpenType" title="OpenType">
        OpenType
       </a>
       IPA fonts. Also,
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Ladefoged
       </a>
       has resurrected an obsolete IPA symbol, the under dot, to indicate
       <i>
        apical postalveolar
       </i>
       (normally included in the category of
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_consonant" title="Retroflex consonant">
        retroflex consonants
       </a>
       ), and that notation is used here. (Note that the notation
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̠, ṣ
       </span>
       is sometimes reversed; either may also be called 'retroflex' and written
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʂ
       </span>
       .)

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Whistled_sibilants">
        Whistled sibilants
        <span id="Whistled_sibilants">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Whistled sibilants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Whistled sibilants occur in speech pathology and may be caused by dental prostheses or orthodontics. However, they also occur phonemically in several southern Bantu languages, the best known being
       <a href="/wiki/Shona_language" title="Shona language">
        Shona
       </a>
       . These have been variously described—as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labialized" title="Labialized">
        labialized
       </a>
       but not velarized, as retroflex, etc., but none of these articulations are required for the sounds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Using the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extended_IPA" title="Extended IPA">
        Extended IPA
       </a>
       , Shona
       <i>
        sv
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        zv
       </i>
       may be transcribed
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         s͎
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       and
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         z͎
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       . Other transcriptions seen include purely labialized
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         s̫
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       and
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         z̫
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       (Ladefoged and Maddieson 1996) and labially co-articulated
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         sᶲ
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       and
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         zᵝ
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       or
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         s͡ɸ
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       and
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         z͜β
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       . In the otherwise IPA transcription of Shona in Doke (1967), the whistled sibilants are transcribed with non-IPA
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C8%BF" title="ȿ">
          ȿ
         </a>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C9%80" title="ɀ">
          ɀ
         </a>
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       and
       <span style="font-size: large;">
        ⟨
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         tȿ dɀ
        </span>
        ⟩
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_contrasts_among_sibilants">
        Linguistic contrasts among sibilants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Linguistic contrasts among sibilants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Not including differences in
       <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
        manner of articulation
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_articulation" title="Secondary articulation">
        secondary articulation
       </a>
       , some languages have as many as four different types of sibilants. For example,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Qiang" title="Northern Qiang">
        Northern Qiang
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Qiang" title="Southern Qiang">
        Southern Qiang
       </a>
       have a four-way distinction among sibilant affricates
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/ /tʂ/ /tʃ/ /tɕ/
       </span>
       , with one for each of the four tongue shapes.
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda
       </a>
       also has a four-way sibilant distinction, with one alveolar, one palato-alveolar, and two retroflex (apical postalveolar and subapical palatal).
      </p>
      <p>
       The now-extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh language
       </a>
       was particularly complex, with a total of 27 sibilant consonants. Not only all four tongue shapes were represented (with the palato-alveolar appearing in the laminal "closed" variation) but also both the palato-alveolars and alveolo-palatals could additionally appear
       <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">
        labialized
       </a>
       . Besides, there was a five-way manner distinction among voiceless and voiced fricatives, voiceless and voiced affricates, and
       <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">
        ejective
       </a>
       affricates. (The three labialized palato-alveolar affricates were missing, which is why the total was 27, not 30.) The Bzyp dialect of the related
       <a href="/wiki/Abkhaz_language" title="Abkhaz language">
        Abkhaz language
       </a>
       also has a similar inventory.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages have four types when
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       is considered.
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       is one example, with both palatalized and non-palatalized laminal denti-alveolars, laminal postalveolar (or "flat retroflex"), and alveolo-palatal (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪ z̪] [s̪ʲ z̪ʲ] [s̠ z̠] [ɕ ʑ]
       </span>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       has the same surface contrasts, but the alveolo-palatals are arguably not phonemic. They occur only geminate, and the retroflex consonants never occur geminate, which suggests that both are allophones of the same phoneme.
      </p>
      <p>
       Somewhat more common are languages with three sibilant types, including one hissing and two hushing. As with Polish and Russian, the two hushing types are usually postalveolar and alveolo-palatal since these are the two most distinct from each other.
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       is an example of such a language. However, other possibilities exist.
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       has alveolar, flat postalveolar and alveolo-palatal affricates whereas
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       has palato-alveolar and laminal and apical alveolar (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apico-alveolar" title="Apico-alveolar">
        apico-alveolar
       </a>
       ) fricatives and affricates (late Medieval peninsular
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       had the same distinctions among fricatives).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , have two sibilant types, one hissing and one hushing. A wide variety of languages across the world have this pattern. Perhaps most common is the pattern, as in English, with alveolar and palato-alveolar sibilants. Modern northern peninsular
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       has a single
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apico-alveolar" title="Apico-alveolar">
        apico-alveolar
       </a>
       sibilant fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̠]
       </span>
       , as well as a single palato-alveolar sibilant affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       . However, there are also languages with alveolar and apical retroflex sibilants (such as Standard
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       ) and with alveolar and alveolo-palatal postalveolars (e.g. alveolar and laminal palatalized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ ʒ tʃ dʒ]
       </span>
       i.e.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃʲ ʒʲ tʃʲ dʒʲ]
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       , the latter probably through Amerindian influence,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and alveolar and dorsal i.e.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɕ ʑ cɕ ɟʑ]
       </span>
       proper in
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Only a few languages with sibilants lack the hissing type.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Vietnamese" title="Middle Vietnamese">
        Middle Vietnamese
       </a>
       is normally reconstructed with two sibilant fricatives, both hushing (one retroflex, one alveolo-palatal). Some languages have only a single hushing sibilant and no hissing sibilant. That occurs in southern Peninsular Spanish dialects of the "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
        ceceo
       </a>
       " type, which have replaced the former hissing fricative with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       , leaving only
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages with no sibilants are fairly rare. Most have no fricatives at all or only the fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       . Examples include most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        Australian languages
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Rotokas_language" title="Rotokas language">
        Rotokas
       </a>
       , and what is generally reconstructed for
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Bantu_language" title="Proto-Bantu language">
        Proto-Bantu
       </a>
       . Languages with fricatives but no sibilants, however, do occur, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ukue_language" title="Ukue language">
        Ukue
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">
        Nigeria
       </a>
       , which has only the fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f, v, h/
       </span>
       . Also, almost all Eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">
        Polynesian languages
       </a>
       have no sibilants but do have the fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       and/or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">
        Māori
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_language" title="Tahitian language">
        Tahitian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rapa_Nui_language" title="Rapa Nui language">
        Rapa Nui
       </a>
       , most
       <a href="/wiki/Cook_Islands_M%C4%81ori" title="Cook Islands Māori">
        Cook Islands Māori
       </a>
       dialects,
       <a href="/wiki/Marquesan_language" title="Marquesan language">
        Marquesan
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Tuamotuan_language" title="Tuamotuan language">
        Tuamotuan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       only has the sibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʂ/
       </span>
       and fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       in loanwords, which are frequently replaced by native sounds. The sibilants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s, ɕ/
       </span>
       exist as allophones of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡ɕ
       </span>
       and the fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       as an allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contested_definitions">
        Contested definitions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sibilant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Contested definitions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Authors including
       <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
        Chomsky
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">
        Halle
       </a>
       group
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">
         v
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       as sibilants. However, they do not have the grooved articulation and high frequencies of other sibilants, and most phoneticians
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       continue to group them together with
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabial
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiceless bilabial fricative">
         ɸ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and (inter)dental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         θ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
         ð
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       as non-sibilant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anterior_(phonetics)" title="Anterior (phonetics)">
        anterior
       </a>
       fricatives. For a grouping of sibilants and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f, v]
       </span>
       , the term
       <i>
        strident
       </i>
       is more common. Some researchers judge
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       to be non-strident in English, based on measurements of its comparative amplitude, but to be strident in other languages (for example, in the African language
       <a href="/wiki/Ewe_language" title="Ewe language">
        Ewe
       </a>
       , where it contrasts with non-strident
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɸ]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The nature of
       <i>
        sibilants
       </i>
       as so-called 'obstacle fricatives' is complicated – there is a continuum of possibilities relating to the angle at which the jet of air may strike an obstacle. The grooving often considered necessary for classification as a
       <i>
        sibilant
       </i>
       has been observed in ultrasound studies of the tongue for the supposedly
       <i>
        non-sibilant
       </i>
       voiceless alveolar fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       of English.
