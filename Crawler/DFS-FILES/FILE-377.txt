      <p>
       The
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
         official languages
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
         United Nations
        </a>
       </b>
       are the six languages that are used in UN meetings, and in which all official UN documents are written. In alphabetical order, they are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
         Arabic
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
         Modern Standard Arabic
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         British English
        </a>
        with
        <a href="/wiki/Oxford_spelling" title="Oxford spelling">
         Oxford spelling
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">
         Mandarin
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Chinese_characters" title="Simplified Chinese characters">
         Simplified Chinese characters
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Li2006_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Li2006-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       These languages are used at meetings of various
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_System" title="United Nations System">
        UN organs
       </a>
       , particularly the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">
        General Assembly
       </a>
       (Article 51 of its Rules of Procedure), the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">
        Economic and Social Council
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council" title="United Nations Security Council">
        Security Council
       </a>
       (Article 41 of its Rules of Procedure). Each representative of a country may speak in any one of these six languages, or may speak in any language and provide interpretation into one of the six official languages. The UN provides
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_interpretation" title="Simultaneous interpretation">
        simultaneous interpretation
       </a>
       from the official language into the other five official languages, via the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Interpretation_Service" title="United Nations Interpretation Service">
        United Nations Interpretation Service
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The six official languages are also used for the dissemination of official documents. Until a document is available in all six official languages, it is not published.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Generally, the texts in each of the six languages are equally authoritative.
      </p>
      <p>
       The United Nations has drawn criticism for relying too heavily on English, and not enough on the other five official languages. Spanish-speaking member nations formally brought this to the attention of the Secretary-General in 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Secretary-General
       <a href="/wiki/Kofi_Annan" title="Kofi Annan">
        Kofi Annan
       </a>
       then responded that full parity of the six official languages was unachievable within current budgetary restraints, but he nevertheless attached great importance to improving the linguistic balance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2008 and 2009, resolutions of the General Assembly have urged the Secretariat to respect the parity of the six official languages, especially in the dissemination of public information.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 8 June 2007,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       resolutions concerning human resources management at the UN, the General Assembly had emphasized "the paramount importance of the equality of the six official languages of the United Nations" and requested that the Secretary-General "ensure that vacancy announcements specified the need for either of the working languages of the Secretariat, unless the functions of the post required a specific working language".
      </p>
      <p>
       The Secretary-General's most recent report on multilingualism was issued on 4 October 2010.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In response, on 19 July 2011, the General Assembly adopted Resolution No. A/RES/65/311 on multilingualism, calling on the Secretary-General, once again, to ensure that all six official languages are given equally favourable working conditions and resources. The resolution noted with concern that the multilingual development of the UN website had improved at a much slower rate than expected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The six official languages spoken at the UN are the
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       of 2.8 billion people on the planet, less than half of the world population. The six languages are official languages in more than half the countries in the world (about one hundred).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Charter" title="United Nations Charter">
        Charter of the United Nations
       </a>
       , its 1945 constituent document, did not expressly provide for official languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        UN
       </a>
       . The Charter was enacted in five languages (Chinese, French, Russian, English, and Spanish) and provided (in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapter_XIX_of_the_United_Nations_Charter" title="Chapter XIX of the United Nations Charter">
        Article 111
       </a>
       ) that the five texts are equally authoritative.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1946, the first session of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">
        United Nations General Assembly
       </a>
       adopted rules of procedure concerning languages that purported to apply to "all the organs of the United Nations, other than the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Court_of_Justice" title="International Court of Justice">
        International Court of Justice
       </a>
       ", setting out five official languages and two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Working_languages" title="Working languages">
        working languages
       </a>
       (English and French).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The following year, the second session of the General Assembly adopted permanent rules of procedure, Resolution 173 (II). The part of those rules relating to language closely followed the 1946 rules, except that the 1947 rules did not purport to apply to other UN organs, just the General Assembly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal.un.org_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-legal.un.org-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Meanwhile, a proposal had been in the works to add Spanish as a third working language in addition to English and French. This was adopted in Resolution 262 (III), passed on 11 December 1948.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-legal.un.org_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-legal.un.org-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1968, Russian was added as a working language of the General Assembly so that, of the GA's five official languages, four (all but Chinese) were working.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1973, the General Assembly made Chinese a working language and added Arabic as both an official language and working language of the GA. Thus all six official languages were also working languages. Arabic was made an official and working language of "the General Assembly and its Main Committees", whereas the other five languages had status in all GA committees and subcommittees (not just the main committees). The Arab members of the UN had agreed to pay the costs of implementing the resolution, for three years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1980, the General Assembly got rid of this final distinction, making Arabic an official and working language of all its committees and subcommittees, as of 1 January 1982. At the same time, the GA requested the Security Council to include Arabic among its official and working languages, and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">
        Economic and Social Council
       </a>
       to include Arabic among its official languages, by 1 January 1983.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As of 1983, the Security Council (like the General Assembly) recognized six official and working languages: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian, and Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">
        Economic and Social Council
       </a>
       , as of 1992, there are six official languages (Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian and Spanish) of which three are working languages (English, French, and Spanish).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       ,

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hindi">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       is one of the official languages of
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fiji" title="Fiji">
        Fiji
       </a>
       . It has more than 550 million speakers in India alone, of whom 422 million are native, 98.2 million are second language speakers, and 31.2 million are third language speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hindi is the
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       of the majority of North Indians with its importance as a global language increasing day by day.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hindi is the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_number_of_native_speakers" title="List of languages by number of native speakers">
        fourth most-spoken first language
       </a>
       in the world, after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Mandarin_Chinese" title="Standard Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nationalencyklopedin_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nationalencyklopedin-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/World_Hindi_Secretariat" title="World Hindi Secretariat">
        World Hindi Secretariat
       </a>
       is headquartered in
       <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">
        Mauritius
       </a>
       and the organization seeks to promote Hindi as an international language.
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi_Divas" title="Hindi Divas">
        Hindi Divas
       </a>
       is an annual day in India on the promotion of Hindi in India.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007, it was reported that the government would "make immediate diplomatic moves to seek the status of an official language for Hindi at the United Nations".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to a 2009 press release from its Ministry of External Affairs, the Government of India has been "working actively" to have
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       recognized as an official language of the UN.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2015,
       <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">
        Nepal
       </a>
       's Vice President
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parmananda_Jha" title="Parmananda Jha">
        Parmananda Jha
       </a>
       stated his firm support for the inclusion of Hindi as an official language of the UN.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Coordinator for multilingualism">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>

      <p>
       In a 1999 resolution, the General Assembly requested the Secretary-General to "appoint a senior Secretariat official as coordinator of questions relating to multilingualism throughout the Secretariat".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first such coordinator was
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Federico_Riesco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Federico Riesco (page does not exist)">
        Federico Riesco
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       , appointed on 6 September 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-un.org-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following Riesco's retirement,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Miles_Stoby&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Miles Stoby (page does not exist)">
        Miles Stoby
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">
        Guyana
       </a>
       was appointed Coordinator for Multilingualism, effective 6 September 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-un.org_40-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-un.org-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Shashi_Tharoor" title="Shashi Tharoor">
        Shashi Tharoor
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Department_of_Public_Information" title="United Nations Department of Public Information">
        Department of Public Information
       </a>
       .

         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The current coordinator for multilingualism is
       <a href="/wiki/Catherine_Pollard" title="Catherine Pollard">
        Catherine Pollard
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">
        Guyana
       </a>
       .

        </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_Days_at_the_UN">
        Language Days at the UN
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Language Days at the UN">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2010, the UN's Department of Public Information announced an initiative of six "language days" to be observed throughout the year, one for each official language, with the goal of celebrating linguistic diversity and learning about the importance of cross-cultural communication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-languagedays_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-languagedays-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The days and their historical significance are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_Arabic_Language_Day" title="UN Arabic Language Day">
         UN Arabic Language Day
        </a>
        : 18 December (the date on which the
        <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly" title="United Nations General Assembly">
         United Nations General Assembly
        </a>
        designated Arabic as the sixth official language of the United Nations in 1973)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstfrench_48-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-firstfrench-48">
          [48]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_observances_49-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-UN_observances-49">
          [49]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_Chinese_Language_Day" title="UN Chinese Language Day">
         UN Chinese Language Day
        </a>
        : first celebrated 12 November;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
         <a href="#cite_note-50">
          [50]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
         <a href="#cite_note-51">
          [51]
         </a>
        </sup>
        now set on 20 April ("to pay tribute to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cang_Jie" title="Cang Jie">
         Cang Jie
        </a>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
         <a href="#cite_note-52">
          [52]
         </a>
        </sup>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_English_Language_Day" title="UN English Language Day">
         UN English Language Day
        </a>
        : 23 April ("the date traditionally observed as the birthday of
        <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
         William Shakespeare
        </a>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
         <a href="#cite_note-53">
          [53]
         </a>
        </sup>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_French_Language_Day" title="UN French Language Day">
         UN French Language Day
        </a>
        : 20 March (corresponding to the
        <a href="/wiki/International_Francophonie_Day" title="International Francophonie Day">
         Journée internationale de la Francophonie
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
         <a href="#cite_note-54">
          [54]
         </a>
        </sup>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_Russian_Language_Day" title="UN Russian Language Day">
         UN Russian Language Day
        </a>
        : 6 June (the birthday of
        <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pushkin" title="Alexander Pushkin">
         Alexander Pushkin
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-firstfrench_48-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-firstfrench-48">
          [48]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_observances_49-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-UN_observances-49">
          [49]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UN_Spanish_Language_Day" title="UN Spanish Language Day">
         UN Spanish Language Day
        </a>
        : 12 October (celebrated in the
        <a href="/wiki/Hispanophone" title="Hispanophone">
         Spanish-speaking world
        </a>
        as "Día de la Hispanidad"; compare
        <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Day" title="Columbus Day">
         Columbus Day
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UN_observances_49-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-UN_observances-49">
          [49]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
         <a href="#cite_note-55">
          [55]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="UN_specialized_agencies">
        UN specialized agencies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: UN specialized agencies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       UN independent agencies have their own sets of official languages that sometimes are different from that of the principal UN organs. For example, the General Conference of
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       has nine official languages including Hindi, Italian, and Portuguese.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
        <a href="#cite_note-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Postal_Union" title="Universal Postal Union">
        Universal Postal Union
       </a>
       has just one official language, French.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
        <a href="#cite_note-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IFAD" title="IFAD">
        IFAD
       </a>
       has four official languages: Arabic, English, French, and Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Parallels_with_other_multilingual_institutions">
        Parallels with other multilingual institutions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Parallels with other multilingual institutions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The next largest international grouping after the UN is the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth of Nations
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       which is exclusively English speaking. All other international bodies in commerce, transport and sport have tended to the adoption of one or a few languages as the means of communication. This is usually English, closely followed by French (see:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_international_organisations_which_have_French_as_an_official_language" title="List of international organisations which have French as an official language">
        list of international organisations which have French as an official language
       </a>
       ). Regional groups have adopted what is common to other elements of their ethnic or religious background.
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
        Standard Arabic
       </a>
       is usually adopted across Muslim nation groups. Most of non-Arab Africa is either Francophone or Anglophone because of their imperial past, but there is also a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_African_countries" title="Portuguese-speaking African countries">
        Lusophone grouping of countries
       </a>
       for the same reason.
      </p>
