      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Linguasphere Observatory
       </b>
       (or "Observatoire", based upon its original French and legal title:
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatoire_Linguistique" title="fr:Observatoire Linguistique">
        Observatoire Linguistique
       </a>
       ) is a transnational linguistic research network.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It was created in
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       in 1983 and was subsequently established and registered in
       <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">
        Normandy
       </a>
       as a non-profit association under the honorary presidency of the late
       <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9opold_S%C3%A9dar_Senghor" title="Léopold Sédar Senghor">
        Léopold Sédar Senghor
       </a>
       , a French-language poet and the first president of
       <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">
        Senegal
       </a>
       . Its founding director is David Dalby, former director of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_African_Institute" title="International African Institute">
        International African Institute
       </a>
       and emeritus reader in the University of London, and its first research secretary was Philippe Blanchet, a Provençal-language poet currently serving as Professor of Sociolinguistics at the University of Rennes. Since 2010, the deputy director and webmaster of the Observatoire has been Pierrick le Feuvre,with the chairman of its research council being Roland Breton, emeritus professor at the University of Paris VIII. The Observatoire's research hub is currently based in the European Union, in
       <a href="/wiki/Carmarthenshire" title="Carmarthenshire">
        Carmarthenshire
       </a>
       , Wales (UK) and in
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       . Its title in
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       is
       <b>
        Wylfa Ieithoedd
       </b>
       , literally the "Observatory (of) languages", and its publishing associate (also in Wales) is the
       <b>
        Gwasg y Byd Iaith
       </b>
       , i.e., "Linguasphere Press" or literally "Press (of) the world (of) language".
      </p>
      <p>
       The Observatoire has developed an innovative scheme of
       <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">
        philological
       </a>
       classification, coding all living and recorded languages within a global referential framework or "linguascale". This Linguascale Framework uses a decimal structure (see below) to record both genetic and geographic categories of relationship (termed
       <i>
        phylozones
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        geozones
       </i>
       , respectively).
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1999/2000, the Observatoire published David Dalby's 2-volume
       <i>
        Linguasphere Register of the World's Languages and Speech Communities
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Reviews were published by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_J._Vajda" title="Edward J. Vajda">
        Edward J. Vajda
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        Language
       </i>
       and by Anthony P.Grant in
       <i>
        Journal of the Royal Anthropological Society
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Observatoire has now prepared a revised edition of the Linguasphere Register from 2010, the first of a projected series of regular updates at 10-year intervals. The current edition
       <a class="external text" href="http://linguasphere.info" rel="nofollow">
        (LS-2010)
       </a>
       , comprising substantial materials from the foundation edition of 2000, is published online from 2011 as a freely available public resource and an online data-base, compiled and co-ordinated by David Dalby and Pierrick le Feuvre. Provision is made for the online gathering of additional and improved data, and for the open discussion of proposals and criticisms.
      </p>
      <p>
       From 2001 until December 2005, the Linguasphere Observatory was actively involved in collaboration with the British Standards Institution
       <a href="/wiki/BSI_Group" title="BSI Group">
        BSI Group
       </a>
       and with
       <a href="/wiki/ISO/TC_37" title="ISO/TC 37">
        ISO/TC 37in
       </a>
       the design and development of a four-letter (alpha-4) code covering—potentially—every recorded language variety in the world. The Observatoire was not, however, associated with or responsible for the final
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-6" title="ISO 639-6">
        ISO 639-6
       </a>
       standard which was a partial result of this collaboration, and which was approved and published by ISO in 2009. It is the policy of the Observatoire that its on-going independent work on language coding should be complementary to and supportive of the ISO 639 international standards.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Lingua_sphere_Register_and_Linguascale_referential_framework">
        The Lingua sphere Register and Linguascale referential framework
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Lingua sphere Register and Linguascale referential framework">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Linguascale
       </i>
       framework is a referential system covering all languages, as published in the
       <i>
        Linguasphere Register
       </i>
       in 2000 and subsequently refined in 2010. It comprises a flexible coding formula or which seeks to situate each language and dialect within the totality of the world's living and recorded languages, having regard to ongoing linguistic research.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first part of this linguascale is the
       <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">
        decimal
       </a>
       classification referred to above, consisting of a
       <i>
        linguasphere key
       </i>
       of two numerals denoting the relevant phylozone or geozone: from
       <tt>
        00
       </tt>
       . to
       <tt>
        99
       </tt>
       . This provides a systematic numerical key for the initial classification of any of the world's languages, following the principles set out in the
       <i>
        Linguasphere Register
       </i>
       . The first numeral of the key represents one of the ten referential
       <i>
        sectors
       </i>
       into which the world's languages are initially divided. The sector can either be a
       <i>
        phylosector
       </i>
       , in which the constituent languages are considered to be in a diachronic relationship one with another, or a
       <i>
        geosector
       </i>
       , in which languages are grouped geographically rather than historically.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second numeral is used to represent the ten
       <i>
        zones
       </i>
       into which each geosector is divided for referential purposes. The component zones, like the sectors, are described as either
       <i>
        phylozones
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        geozones
       </i>
       , based on the nature of the relationship among their constituent languages: either historical or geographical.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second part of the linguascale consists of three capital letters (majuscules): from
       <tt>
        -AAA-
       </tt>
       to
       <tt>
        -ZZZ-
       </tt>
       . Each zone is divided into one or more
       <i>
        sets
       </i>
       , with each set being represented by the first majuscule of this three-letter (alpha-3) component. Each set is divided into one or more
       <i>
        chains
       </i>
       (represented by the second majuscule) and each chain is into one or more
       <i>
        nets
       </i>
       (represented by the third majuscule). The division of the languages of a zone into sets, chains and nets is based on relative degrees of linguistic proximity, as measured in principle by approximate proportions of shared basic vocabulary. Geozones are on average divided into more sets than phylozones because relationships among languages within the latter are by definition more obvious and much closer.
      </p>
      <p>
       The third and final part of the linguascale consists of up to three lowercase letters (minuscules), used to identify a language or dialect with precision: from
       <tt>
        aaa
       </tt>
       to
       <tt>
        zzz
       </tt>
       . The first letter of this sequence represents an
       <i>
        outer unit
       </i>
       (preferred from 2010 to the original term of "outer language", to avoid the shifting and often emotive applications of the terms "language" and "dialect"). The
       <i>
        inner units
       </i>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        language varieties
       </a>
       that may comprise any outer language are coded using a second, and wherever necessary a third minuscule letter.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The application of the linguascale may be illustrated with the concrete examples below, chosen from within the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For example,
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The code covering all forms of English is
        <tt>
         52-ABA
        </tt>
        , where
        <tt>
         5=
        </tt>
        represents the
        <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
         Indo-European phylosector
        </a>
        ,
        <tt>
         52=
        </tt>
        represents the
        <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
         Germanic phylozone
        </a>
        ,
        <tt>
         52-A
        </tt>
        represents the
        <i>
         Norsk+ Frysk set
        </i>
        (a compound-name chosen to cover the contents of the Germanic phylozone),
        <tt>
         52-AB
        </tt>
        represents the
        <i>
         English+ Anglo-Creole chain
        </i>
        , and
        <tt>
         52-ABA
        </tt>
        is the
        <i>
         English net
        </i>
        . Within this net, the outer units are:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-a
          </tt>
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
           Scots
          </a>
          + Northumbrian.
         </li>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-b
          </tt>
          – "Anglo-English" (the traditional localised varieties of southern Great Britain &amp; also Ireland).
         </li>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-c
          </tt>
          – Global English (varieties of modern English as spoken and written around the world).
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Some more specific examples of English varieties are:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-abb
          </tt>
          is the
          <a href="/wiki/Geordie" title="Geordie">
           Geordie
          </a>
          traditional variety: belonging to
          <tt>
           52-ABA-a
          </tt>
          Scots+ Northumbrian outer language, and
          <tt>
           52-ABA-ab
          </tt>
          Northumbrian.
         </li>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-bco
          </tt>
          is the
          <a href="/wiki/Norfolk" title="Norfolk">
           Norfolk
          </a>
          traditional variety: belonging to
          <tt>
           52-ABA-b
          </tt>
          "Anglo-English" outer unit, and specifically to
          <tt>
           52-ABA-bc
          </tt>
          Southern (British) traditional English.
         </li>
         <li>
          <tt>
           52-ABA-cof
          </tt>
          covers the range of (non-creolised) Nigerian English : belonging to
          <tt>
           52-ABA-c
          </tt>
          Global English outer unit, and
          <tt>
           52-ABA-co
          </tt>
          West-African English. Nigerian English is thus distinguished from the often overlapping
          <tt>
           52-ABB-bf
          </tt>
          Enpi (or "NP", from the abbreviation of so-called "Nigerian Pijin"): belonging to
          <tt>
           52-ABB
          </tt>
          Anglo-Creole net, and
          <tt>
           52-ABB-b
          </tt>
          Wes-kos (West Coast Anglo-Creole).
         </li>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Languages_of_London">
        Languages of London
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Languages of London">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A practical application of the
       <i>
        Linguasphere Register
       </i>
       and its linguascale in the study of a complex urban linguistic environment has been as the referential framework for successive surveys of over 200 languages other than English spoken by plurilingual children at state schools in London (representing just under 40% of the total number of children attending), as edited in 2000 by Baker &amp; Eversley &amp; in 2010 by Eversley et al.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id=".22Langues_de_la_Libert.C3.A9.2FLanguages_of_Liberty.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"Langues_de_la_Liberté/Languages_of_Liberty"'>
        "Langues de la Liberté/Languages of Liberty"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: "Langues de la Liberté/Languages of Liberty"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In Paris, from 1987, the Observatoire linguistique created a bilingual exhibition
       <i>
        Langues de la Liberté / Languages of Liberty
       </i>
       , tracing the transnational development of certain basic concepts of personal freedom through the interaction of English and French, rather than by the action of any one nation. At the outset of a series of 34 illustrated tryptychs, attention was drawn to the historical role of other transnational languages in the development of such concepts, including Greek and German.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The exhibition was sponsored by the government of a bilingual nation, Canada, by the international
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agence_de_coop%C3%A9ration_culturelle_et_technique" title="Agence de coopération culturelle et technique">
        francophone Agence (ACCT)
       </a>
       &amp; by the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Upper_Normandy" title="Upper Normandy">
        Haute-Normandie
       </a>
       . It was inaugurated in Paris at the
       <a href="/wiki/Centre_Georges_Pompidou" title="Centre Georges Pompidou">
        Centre Georges Pompidou
       </a>
       on 6 June 1989, &amp; presented there throughout the summer of 1989 as the official Canadian contribution to the bicentenary celebrations of the French Revolution.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the subsequent presentation of this bilingual exhibition at the Hôtel de Région in Rouen (Haute-Normandie), from 23 September to 21 October 1989, the Observatoire linguistique organised the first public display of the only surviving contemporary copy of the vernacular (&amp; arguably pre-Latin) text of England's
       <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">
        Magna Carta
       </a>
       , written in 13th century French.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thanks to continued support from Canada, the exhibition was subsequently presented by the Observatoire in Belgium &amp; England, at the Palais des Congrès in
       <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge" title="Liège">
        Liège
       </a>
       &amp; at the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Institute" title="Commonwealth Institute">
        Commonwealth Institute
       </a>
       in London in 1990, and finally in Australia, at
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Parliament_House,_Canberra" title="Old Parliament House, Canberra">
        Old Parliament House, Canberra
       </a>
       in May 1991.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the context of the need to design a plurilingual framework of ethics for a future planetary society, the Observatoire has announced its intention to return to the transnational theme of the Magna Carta in 2015, on the occasion of the 8th centenary of the signing of its formal Latin version at
       <a href="/wiki/Runnymede" title="Runnymede">
        Runnymede
       </a>
       in 1215.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id=".22In_the_galaxy_of_languages.2C_each_person.27s_voice_is_a_star.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="&quot;In_the_galaxy_of_languages,_each_person's_voice_is_a_star&quot;">
        "In the galaxy of languages, each person's voice is a star"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Linguasphere_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: &quot;In the galaxy of languages, each person's voice is a star&quot;">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The motto of the Observatoire linguistique dates from 1990—in French:
       <i>
        Dans la galaxie des langues, la voix de chaque personne est une étoile
       </i>
       (translated into English as above).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Observatoire adopted these words as its guiding philosophy on the occasion of the first series of debates organised by the Observatoire linguistique in 1990-1991, at
       <a href="/wiki/Fleury-sur-Andelle" title="Fleury-sur-Andelle">
        Fleury-sur-Andelle
       </a>
       in Haute-Normandie, at
       <a href="/wiki/Maillane" title="Maillane">
        Maillane
       </a>
       in Provence and at
       <a href="/wiki/Huy" title="Huy">
        Huy
       </a>
       in Wallonie, sponsored by each of the relevant regions, on the subject of
       <i>
        Nos langues et l'unité de l'Europe
       </i>
       ("Our languages and the unity of Europe"). The guest of honour at the first of these debates was
       <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Martinet" title="André Martinet">
        André Martinet
       </a>
       (1908–1999), doyen of trans-Atlantic linguistics.
      </p>
      <p>
       From the year 2000
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       adopted and adapted the Observatoire's motto in the form: "In the galaxy of languages, each word is a star".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
