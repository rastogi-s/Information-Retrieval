       ;
       <small>
        Spanish:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [meˈliʎa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        locally
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [meˈliʝa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">
        Berber languages
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="ber" xml:lang="ber">
        Mřič,
        <b>
         ⵎⵔⵉⵜⵙ
        </b>
       </span>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       :
       <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        مليلية
       </span>
       ‎,
       <i>
        Maliliyyah
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       autonomous city located on the north coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , sharing a border with
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       , with an area of 12.3 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (4.7 sq mi). Melilla, along with
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       , is one of two permanently inhabited Spanish cities in mainland Africa. It was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_M%C3%A1laga" title="Province of Málaga">
        Province of Málaga
       </a>
       until 14 March 1995, when the city's
       <a href="/wiki/Statute_of_Autonomy" title="Statute of Autonomy">
        Statute of Autonomy
       </a>
       was passed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Melilla, like
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       , was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_port" title="Free port">
        free port
       </a>
       before Spain joined the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In 2011 it had a population of 78,476, made up of Catholics of Iberian origin (primarily from Andalusia and Catalonia), ethnic
       <a href="/wiki/Riffian_people" title="Riffian people">
        Riffian Berbers
       </a>
       and a small number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jews" title="Sephardic Jews">
        Sephardic Jews
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sindhi_people" title="Sindhi people">
        Sindhi
       </a>
       Hindus. Spanish and
       <a href="/wiki/Riffian_language" title="Riffian language">
        Riffian-Berber
       </a>
       are the two most widely spoken languages, with Spanish as the only official language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Melilla is claimed by Morocco.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The current
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">
        Berber
       </a>
       name of Melilla is
       <i>
        Mřič
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Mlilt
       </i>
       , which means the "white one". Melilla was an ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Berbers" title="Berbers">
        Berber
       </a>
       village and a
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenicia" title="Phoenicia">
        Phoenician
       </a>
       and later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Punic" title="Punic">
        Punic
       </a>
       trade establishment under the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Rusadir" title="Rusadir">
        Rusadir
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Rusaddir
       </i>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Romans
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        Russadeiron
       </i>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Ῥυσσάδειρον
       </span>
       ) for the Greeks). Later it became a part of the Roman province of
       <a href="/wiki/Mauretania_Tingitana" title="Mauretania Tingitana">
        Mauretania Tingitana
       </a>
       . Rusaddir is mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       (IV, 1) and
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny)" title="Natural History (Pliny)">
        Pliny
       </a>
       (V, 18) who call it "oppidum et portus", also cited by
       <a href="/wiki/Mela" title="Mela">
        Mela
       </a>
       (I, 33) as Rusicada, and by the
       <i>
        Itinerarium Antonini
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CE-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rusaddir was supposed to have once been the seat of a bishop, but there is no record of any bishop of the supposed see,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CE_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CE-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is not included in the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
        Catholic Church
       </a>
       's list of
       <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">
        titular sees
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As centuries passed, it went through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vandal" title="Vandal">
        Vandal
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visigoth" title="Visigoth">
        Hispano-Visigothic
       </a>
       hands. The political history is similar to that of towns in the region of the Moroccan
       <a href="/wiki/Rif" title="Rif">
        Rif
       </a>
       and southern Spain. Local rule passed through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazigh" title="Amazigh">
        Amazigh
       </a>
       , Phoenician, Punic,
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umayyad" title="Umayyad">
        Umayyad
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idrisid" title="Idrisid">
        Idrisid
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravid" title="Almoravid">
        Almoravid
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">
        Almohad
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinid" title="Marinid">
        Marinid
       </a>
       , and then
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wattasid" title="Wattasid">
        Wattasid
       </a>
       rulers. During the Middle Ages, it was the Berber city of Mlila. It was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Fez" title="Kingdom of Fez">
        Kingdom of Fez
       </a>
       when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Catholic_Monarchs" title="The Catholic Monarchs">
        the Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       , Queen
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella I of Castile
       </a>
       and King
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand II of Aragon
       </a>
       requested
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Alfonso_P%C3%A9rez_de_Guzm%C3%A1n,_3rd_Duke_of_Medina_Sidonia" title="Juan Alfonso Pérez de Guzmán, 3rd Duke of Medina Sidonia">
        Juan Alfonso Pérez de Guzmán, 3rd Duke of Medina Sidonia
       </a>
       , to take the city.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Melilla" title="Conquest of Melilla">
        Conquest of Melilla
       </a>
       , the duke sent Pedro Estopiñán, who conquered the city virtually without a fight in 1497,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a few years after
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Castile
       </a>
       had taken control of the
       <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">
        Nasrid
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Granada" title="Kingdom of Granada">
        Kingdom of Granada
       </a>
       , the last remnant of
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       , in 1492. Melilla was immediately threatened with reconquest and was besieged during 1694–1696 and
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Melilla_(1774)" title="Siege of Melilla (1774)">
        1774–1775
       </a>
       . One Spanish officer reflected, "an hour in Melilla, from the point of view of merit, was worth more than thirty years of service to Spain."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rezette41_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rezette41-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The current limits of the Spanish territory around the fortress were fixed by treaties with Morocco in 1859,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish-Moroccan_War_(1859)" title="Spanish-Moroccan War (1859)">
        1860
       </a>
       , 1861, and 1894. In the late 19th century, as Spanish influence expanded, Melilla became the only authorized centre of trade on the Rif coast between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetuan" title="Tetuan">
        Tetuan
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">
        Algerian
       </a>
       frontier. The value of trade increased, goat skins, eggs and
       <a href="/wiki/Beeswax" title="Beeswax">
        beeswax
       </a>
       being the principal exports, and cotton goods, tea, sugar and candles being the chief imports.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1821" data-file-width="1555" height="258" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG/220px-War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG/330px-War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG/440px-War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:War_in_Morocco_Death_of_Spanish_general_Margallo.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration of the death of Spanish General
         <a href="/wiki/Juan_Garc%C3%ADa_y_Margallo" title="Juan García y Margallo">
          Juan García y Margallo
         </a>
         during the
         <a href="/wiki/First_Melillan_campaign" title="First Melillan campaign">
          First Melillan campaign
         </a>
         in 1893
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1893, the Rif Berbers launched the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Melillan_campaign" title="First Melillan campaign">
        First Melillan campaign
       </a>
       and 25,000 Spanish soldiers had to be dispatched against them. The conflict was also known as the
       <i>
        Margallo War
       </i>
       , after the Governor of Melilla and Spanish General
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Garc%C3%ADa_y_Margallo" title="Juan García y Margallo">
        Juan García y Margallo
       </a>
       , who was killed in the battle.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1908 two companies, under the protection of
       <a href="/wiki/Bou_Hmara" title="Bou Hmara">
        Bou Hmara
       </a>
       , a chieftain then ruling the Rif region, started mining lead and iron some 20 kilometers (12.4 miles) from Melilla. A railway to the mines was begun. In October of that year the Bou Hmara's vassals revolted against him and raided the mines, which remained closed until June 1909. By July the workmen were again attacked and several of them killed. Severe fighting between the Spaniards and the tribesmen followed, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Melillan_campaign" title="Second Melillan campaign">
        Second Melillan campaign
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1910, with the Rif having submitted, the Spaniards restarted the mines and undertook harbor works at Mar Chica, but hostilities broke out again in 1911. In 1921 the Berbers under the leadership of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ibn_%27Abd_al-Karim_al-Khattabi" title="Muhammad Ibn 'Abd al-Karim al-Khattabi">
        Abd el Krim
       </a>
       inflicted a grave defeat on the Spanish (see
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Annual" title="Battle of Annual">
        Battle of Annual
       </a>
       ), and were not defeated until 1926, when the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Protectorate
       </a>
       finally managed to control the area again.
      </p>
      <p>
       General
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Francisco Franco
       </a>
       used the city as one of his staging grounds for
       <a href="/wiki/July_1936_military_uprising_in_Melilla" title="July 1936 military uprising in Melilla">
        his Nationalist rebellion in 1936
       </a>
       , starting the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
        Spanish Civil War
       </a>
       . A statue of him – the last statue of Franco in Spain – is still prominently featured.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 6 November 2007, King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I" title="Juan Carlos I">
        Juan Carlos I
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Sofia" title="Queen Sofia">
        Queen Sofia
       </a>
       visited the city, which caused a massive demonstration of support. The visit also sparked protests from the Moroccan government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the first time a Spanish monarch had visited Melilla in 80 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       Melilla (and Ceuta) have declared the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muslim_holiday" title="Muslim holiday">
        Muslim holiday
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Eid_al-Adha" title="Eid al-Adha">
        Eid al-Adha
       </a>
       or Feast of the Sacrifice, as an official public holiday from 2010 onward. It is the first time a non-Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Religious_festival" title="Religious festival">
        religious festival
       </a>
       is officially celebrated in Spain since the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Melilla_en.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1038" data-file-width="978" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Melilla_en.png/220px-Melilla_en.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Melilla_en.png/330px-Melilla_en.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Melilla_en.png/440px-Melilla_en.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Melilla_en.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of Melilla
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Melilla is located in the northwest of the African continent, next to the
       <a href="/wiki/Alboran_Sea" title="Alboran Sea">
        Alboran Sea
       </a>
       and across the sea from the Spanish provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Granada" title="Province of Granada">
        Granada
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Almer%C3%ADa" title="Province of Almería">
        Almería
       </a>
       . The city layout is arranged in a wide semicircle around the beach and the Port of Melilla, on the eastern side of the peninsula of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Tres_Forcas" title="Cape Tres Forcas">
        Cape Tres Forcas
       </a>
       , at the foot of Mount Gurugú and the mouth of the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_Oro_(Melilla)" title="Río de Oro (Melilla)">
        Río de Oro
       </a>
       , 1 meter (3 ft 3 in) above sea level. The urban nucleus was originally a
       <a href="/wiki/Presidio" title="Presidio">
        fortress
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla_la_Vieja" title="Melilla la Vieja">
        Melilla la Vieja
       </a>
       , built on a peninsular mound about 30 meters (98 ft) in height.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moroccan settlement of
       <a href="/wiki/Beni_Ansar" title="Beni Ansar">
        Beni Ansar
       </a>
       lies immediately south of Melilla. The nearest Moroccan city is
       <a href="/wiki/Nador" title="Nador">
        Nador
       </a>
       , and the ports of Melilla and
       <a href="/wiki/Nador_Port" title="Nador Port">
        Nador
       </a>
       are both within the same bay; nearby is the Bou Areg Lagoon
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wps_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wps-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_administration">
        Local administration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Local administration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Melilla has held local elections for its 25-seat legislature every four years since 1979. Since its Statute of Autonomy in 1995, the legislature has been called the Assembly and its leader the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mayor-President" title="Mayor-President">
        Mayor-President
       </a>
       . In the most recent election in 2011, the
       <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People's Party (Spain)">
        People's Party
       </a>
       (PP) won 15 seats, maintaining the role of Mayor-President for
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Jos%C3%A9_Imbroda" title="Juan José Imbroda">
        Juan José Imbroda
       </a>
       , who has held office since 2000. A regional splinter of the PP, the PPL, won 2 seats and governs in coalition. Opposition consists of the regionalist and leftist
       <a href="/wiki/Coalition_for_Melilla" title="Coalition for Melilla">
        Coalition for Melilla
       </a>
       (CPM, 6 seats) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Socialist_Workers%27_Party" title="Spanish Socialist Workers' Party">
        Spanish Socialist Workers' Party
       </a>
       (PSOE, 2 seats).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dispute_with_Morocco">
        Dispute with Morocco
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dispute with Morocco">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Greater_Morocco" title="Greater Morocco">
        Greater Morocco
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The government of Morocco has requested from Spain the sovereignty of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       and Melilla,
       <a href="/wiki/Perejil_Island" title="Perejil Island">
        Perejil Island
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa" title="Plazas de soberanía">
        some other small territories
       </a>
       . The Spanish position is that both Ceuta and Melilla are integral parts of the Spanish state (it is not a colony), and have been since the 15th century, even before the foundation of Morocco as a country. Melilla has been under Spanish rule for longer than cities in northern Spain such as
       <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">
        Pamplona
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">
        Tudela
       </a>
       , and was conquered roughly in the same period as the last Muslim cities of Southern Spain such as
       <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">
        Granada
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">
        Málaga
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ronda" title="Ronda">
        Ronda
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa" title="Almería">
        Almería
       </a>
       . Morocco denies these claims and maintains that the Spanish presence on or near its coast is a remnant of the colonial past which should be ended. The
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories" title="United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories">
        United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories
       </a>
       does not include these Spanish territories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Melilla has a warm
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">
        mediterranean climate
       </a>
       influenced by its proximity to the sea, rendering much cooler summers and more precipitation than inland areas deeper into Africa. The climate in general has a lot in common with the type found in southern coastal Spain on the European mainland, with relatively small temperature differences between seasons.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
        Economy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Economy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The principal industry is fishing. Cross-border commerce (legal or smuggled) and Spanish and European grants and wages are the other income sources.
      </p>
      <p>
       Melilla is regularly connected to the Iberian peninsula by air and sea traffic and is also economically connected to Morocco: most of its fruit and vegetables are imported across the border. Moroccans in the city's hinterland are attracted to it: 36,000 Moroccans cross the border daily to work, shop or trade goods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vk1_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-vk1-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The port of Melilla offers several daily connections to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almeria" title="Almeria">
        Almeria
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">
        Málaga
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla_Airport" title="Melilla Airport">
        Melilla Airport
       </a>
       offers daily flights to Almería, Málaga and
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       . Spanish operator
       <a href="/wiki/Air_Europa" title="Air Europa">
        Air Europa
       </a>
       uses nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Nador_International_Airport" title="Nador International Airport">
        Nador International Airport
       </a>
       for their connections to mainland Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many people travelling between Europe and Morocco use the ferry links to Melilla, both for passengers and for freight. Because of this, the port and related companies form an important economic driver for the city.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vk1_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-vk1-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="City_culture_and_society">
        City culture and society
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: City culture and society">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frente_de_la_Marina,_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg/220px-Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg/330px-Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg/440px-Frente_de_la_Marina%2C_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frente_de_la_Marina,_Melilla_la_Vieja.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Old Town
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Melilla's Capilla de Santiago, or James's Chapel, by the city walls, is the only authentic
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">
        Gothic structure
       </a>
       in Africa.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the first quarter of the 20th century, Melilla became a thriving port benefitting from the recently established Protectorate of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Morocco
       </a>
       in the contiguous Rif. The new architectural style of
       <a href="/wiki/Modernisme" title="Modernisme">
        Modernisme
       </a>
       was expressed by a new bourgeois class. This style, frequently referred to as the Catalan version of
       <a href="/wiki/Art_Nouveau" title="Art Nouveau">
        Art Nouveau
       </a>
       , was extremely popular in the early part of the 20th century in Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       The workshops inspired by the Catalan architect
       <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Nieto_(architect)" title="Enrique Nieto (architect)">
        Enrique Nieto
       </a>
       continued in the modernist style, even after Modernisme went out of fashion elsewhere. Accordingly, Melilla has the second most important concentration of Modernist works in Spain after
       <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">
        Barcelona
       </a>
       . Nieto was in charge of designing the main Synagogue, the Central Mosque and various Catholic Churches.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1512" data-file-width="2268" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg/220px-Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg/330px-Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg/440px-Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Caf%C3%A9_Central_en_la_plaza_de_Espa%C3%B1a_de_Melilla.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Public Library in Plaza España
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Melilla has been praised as an example of
       <a href="/wiki/Multiculturalism" title="Multiculturalism">
        multiculturalism
       </a>
       , being a small city in which one can find four major religions represented. However, the Christian majority of the past, constituting around 65% of the population at one point, has been shrinking, while the number of Muslims has steadily increased to its present 45% of the population due to immigration from Muslim countries. The Jewish and Hindu communities have also been shrinking due to economic emigration to mainland Spain (notably Malaga and Madrid).
      </p>
      <p>
       Jews, who had lived in Melilla for centuries, have been leaving the city in recent years (from 20% of the population before World War II to less than 5% today). Most of the Jewish population has left for
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Venezuela#20th_century" title="History of the Jews in Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       . There is a small, autonomous and commercially important Hindu community present in Melilla, which numbers about 100 members today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio" title="Amateur radio">
        amateur radio
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Call_sign" title="Call sign">
        call sign
       </a>
       used for both cities is EA9.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Immigration">
        Immigration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Melilla&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Immigration">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Verjamelilla.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="675" data-file-width="900" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Verjamelilla.jpg/220px-Verjamelilla.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Verjamelilla.jpg/330px-Verjamelilla.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Verjamelilla.jpg/440px-Verjamelilla.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Verjamelilla.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Melilla_border_fence" title="Melilla border fence">
          Melilla border fence
         </a>
         aims to stop illegal immigration into Spain.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Melilla has been a popular destination for refugees and people leaving countries with poor economies in order to enter the European Union. The border is secured by the
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla_border_fence" title="Melilla border fence">
        Melilla border fence
       </a>
       , a six-metre-tall double fence with watch towers; yet refugees frequently manage to cross it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Detection wires, radar, and day/
       <a href="/wiki/Night_vision" title="Night vision">
        night vision
       </a>
       cameras are planned to increase security and prevent illegal immigration. In February 2014, over 200 migrants from sub-Saharan Africa scaled a security fence to get into the Melilla migrant reception centre. The reception centre, built for 480 migrants, was already overcrowded with 1,300 people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
