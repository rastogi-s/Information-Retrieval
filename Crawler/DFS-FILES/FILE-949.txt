      <p>
       <b>
        Anglo-Norman
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        Anglo-Norman French
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variety
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman language
       </a>
       that was used in
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_England" title="Kingdom of England">
        England
       </a>
       and, to a lesser extent, elsewhere in the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
        British Isles
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Normans" title="Anglo-Normans">
        Anglo-Norman
       </a>
       period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">
        William the Conqueror
       </a>
       led the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">
        Norman conquest of England
       </a>
       in 1066, he, his nobles, and many of his followers from
       <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">
        Normandy
       </a>
       , but also those from northern and western France, spoke a range of
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        langues d'oïl
       </a>
       (northern varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance
       </a>
       ). One of these was
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">
        Old Norman
       </a>
       , also known as "Old Northern French". Other followers spoke varieties of the
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard language
       </a>
       or western French. This amalgam developed into the unique insular dialect now known as Anglo-Norman French, which was commonly used for literary and eventually administrative purposes from the 12th until the 15th century. It is difficult to know much about what was actually spoken, as what is known about the dialect is restricted to what was written, but it is clear that Anglo-Norman was, to a large extent, the spoken language of the higher social strata in medieval England.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was spoken in the law courts, schools, and universities and, in due course, in at least some sections of the gentry and the growing bourgeoisie. Private and commercial correspondence was carried out in Anglo-Norman or Anglo-French from the 13th to the 15th century though its spelling forms were often displaced by continental spellings. Social classes other than the nobility became keen to learn French: manuscripts containing materials for instructing non-native speakers still exist, dating mostly from the late 14th century onwards.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although Anglo-Norman and Anglo-French were eventually eclipsed by modern
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , they had been used widely enough to influence English vocabulary permanently. Thus, many original
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       words, cognates of which can still be found in
       <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">
        Nordic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       , have been lost or, as more often occurs, exist alongside synonyms of Anglo-Norman French origin. Grammatically, Anglo-Norman had little lasting impact on English although it is still evident in official and legal terms where the ordinary sequence of noun and adjective
       <a href="/wiki/Postpositive_adjective" title="Postpositive adjective">
        is reversed
       </a>
       , for example
       <i>
        attorney general
       </i>
       : the spelling is English but the word order (noun then adjective) is French. Other such examples are
       <i>
        heir apparent
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        court martial
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        body politic
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_coat_of_arms_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Royal coat of arms of the United Kingdom">
        Royal coat of arms of the United Kingdom
       </a>
       still features in French the mottos of both the
       <a href="/wiki/Monarchy_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Monarchy of the United Kingdom">
        British Monarch
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dieu_et_mon_droit" title="Dieu et mon droit">
         Dieu et mon droit
        </a>
       </i>
       ("God and my right") and the
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_the_Garter" title="Order of the Garter">
        Order of the Garter
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Honi_soit_qui_mal_y_pense" title="Honi soit qui mal y pense">
         Honi soit qui mal y pense
        </a>
       </i>
       ("Shamed be he who thinks evil of it").
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Dieu et mon droit
       </i>
       was first used by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_I_of_England" title="Richard I of England">
        Richard I
       </a>
       in 1198 and adopted as the royal motto of England in the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI_of_England" title="Henry VI of England">
        Henry VI
       </a>
       . The motto appears below the shield of the Royal Coat of Arms.
      </p>

      </p>

       </ul>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_and_development">
        Use and development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Use and development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The literature of the Anglo-Norman period forms the reference point for subsequent literature in the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman language
       </a>
       , especially in the 19th century Norman literary revival and even into the 20th century in the case of André Dupont's
       <i>
        Épopée cotentine
       </i>
       . The languages and literatures of the
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">
        Channel Islands
       </a>
       are sometimes referred to as Anglo-Norman, but that usage, derived from the French
       <i>
        îles anglo-normandes
       </i>
       , is wrong: the Channel Islanders spoke and still speak a variety of Norman, not Anglo-Norman.
      </p>
      <p>
       Anglo-Norman was never the main administrative language of England: Latin was the major language of record in legal and other official documents for most of the medieval period. However, from the late 12th century to the early 15th century, Anglo-Norman French and Anglo-French were much used in law reports, charters, ordinances, official correspondence, and trade at all levels; they were the language of the King, his court and the upper class. There is evidence, too, that foreign words (
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ) often entered English via Anglo-Norman.
      </p>
      <p>
       The language of later documents adopted some of the changes ongoing in continental French and lost many of its original dialectal characteristics, so
       <i>
        Anglo-French
       </i>
       remained (in at least some respects and at least at some social levels) part of the dialect continuum of modern French, often with distinctive spellings. Over time, the use of Anglo-French expanded into the fields of law, administration, commerce, and science, in all of which a rich documentary legacy survives, indicative of the vitality and importance of the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       By the late 15th century, however, what remained of insular French had become heavily anglicised: see
       <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">
        Law French
       </a>
       . It continued to be known as "Norman French" until the end of the 19th century even though, philologically, there was nothing Norman about it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One notable survival of influence on the political system is the use of certain Anglo-French set phrases in the
       <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Parliament of the United Kingdom">
        Parliament of the United Kingdom
       </a>
       for some endorsements to bills and the granting of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Assent" title="Royal Assent">
        Royal Assent
       </a>
       to legislation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These set phrases include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Soit baille aux Communes
        </i>
        ("Let it be sent to the Commons", on a bill sent by the House of Lords to the House of Commons)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         A ceste Bille (avecque une amendement/avecque des amendemens) les Communes sont assentus
        </i>
        ("To this Bill (with an amendment/with amendments) the Commons have assented", on a bill passed by the House of Commons and returned to the House of Lords)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         A cette amendement/ces amendemens les Seigneurs sont assentus
        </i>
        ("To this amendment/these amendments the Lords have assented", on an amended bill returned by the House of Commons to the House of Lords, where the amendments were accepted)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ceste Bille est remise aux Communes avecque une Raison/des Raisons
        </i>
        ("This Bill is returned to the Commons with a reason/with reasons", when the House of Lords disagrees with amendments made by the House of Commons)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le Roy/
         <a href="/wiki/La_Reyne_le_veult" title="La Reyne le veult">
          La Reyne le veult
         </a>
        </i>
        ("The King/Queen wills it", Royal Assent for a public bill)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le Roy/La Reyne remercie ses bons sujets, accepte leur benevolence et ainsi le veult
        </i>
        ("The King/Queen thanks his/her good subjects, accepts their bounty, and wills it so", Royal Assent for a supply bill)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Soit fait comme il est désiré
        </i>
        ("Let it be done as it is desired", Royal Assent for a private bill)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le Roy/La Reyne s'avisera
        </i>
        ("The King/Queen will consider it", if Royal Assent is withheld)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The exact spelling of these phrases has varied over the years; for example,
       <i>
        s'avisera
       </i>
       has been spelled as
       <i>
        s'uvisera
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        s'advisera
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Reyne
       </i>
       as
       <i>
        Raine
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Among important writers of the Anglo-Norman cultural commonwealth is
       <a href="/wiki/Marie_de_France" title="Marie de France">
        Marie de France
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trilingualism_in_Medieval_England">
        Trilingualism in Medieval England
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Trilingualism in Medieval England">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Much of the earliest recorded
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       is in fact Anglo-Norman French. In Northern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , almost nothing was at that time being recorded in the
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       because
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       was the language of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church#Medieval_and_Renaissance_periods" title="Roman Catholic Church">
        Church
       </a>
       and consequently of
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_university" title="Medieval university">
        education
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Historiography" title="Historiography">
        historiography
       </a>
       , and was thus used for the purpose of records. Latin did not disappear in medieval England either: it was used by the Church, the royal government and much local administration, as it had been before 1066, in parallel with
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       . The early adoption of Anglo-Norman as a written and literary language probably owes something to this history of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingualism" title="Bilingualism">
        bilingualism
       </a>
       in writing.
      </p>
      <p>
       Around the same time, as a shift took place in France towards using French as a language of record]] in the mid-
       <a href="/wiki/13th_century" title="13th century">
        13th century
       </a>
       , Anglo-Norman French also became a language of record in England though Latin retained its pre-eminence for matters of permanent record (as in written
       <a href="/wiki/Chronicle" title="Chronicle">
        chronicles
       </a>
       ). From around this point onwards, considerable variation begins to be apparent in Anglo-French, which ranges from the very local (and most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicize" title="Anglicize">
        anglicized
       </a>
       ) to a level of language which approximates to and is sometimes indistinguishable from varieties of continental French. Thus, typically, local records are rather different from continental French, with diplomatic and international trade documents closest to the emerging continental norm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       English remained the vernacular of the common people throughout this period. The resulting virtual
       <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">
        trilinguism
       </a>
       in spoken and written language was one of medieval Latin, diverse French varieties and Middle English.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_the_king_and_his_court">
        Language of the king and his court
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Language of the king and his court">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       From the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">
        conquest
       </a>
       (1066) until the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/14th_century" title="14th century">
        14th century
       </a>
       , French was the language of the king and his court
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2018)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . During this period, marriages with French princesses reinforced the French status in the royal family. Nevertheless, during the 13th century, intermarriages with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_nobility" title="English nobility">
        English nobility
       </a>
       became more frequent. French became progressively a second language among the upper classes. Moreover, with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Years%27_War" title="Hundred Years' War">
        Hundred Years' War
       </a>
       and the growing spirit of English nationalism, the status of French diminished.
      </p>
      <p>
       French was the mother tongue of every
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_king" title="English king">
        English king
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/William_the_Conqueror" title="William the Conqueror">
        William the Conqueror
       </a>
       until
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_IV_of_England" title="Henry IV of England">
        Henry IV
       </a>
       (1399–1413). He was the first to take the oath in English, and his son,
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_V_of_England" title="Henry V of England">
        Henry V
       </a>
       (1413–1422), was the first to write in English. By the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/15th_century" title="15th century">
        15th century
       </a>
       , French became the second language of a cultivated elite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_the_royal_charters_and_legislation">
        Language of the royal charters and legislation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language of the royal charters and legislation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Until the end of the 13th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       was the language of all official written documents. Nevertheless, some important documents had their official Norman translation, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Magna_Carta" title="Magna Carta">
        Magna Carta
       </a>
       signed in 1215. The first official document written in Anglo-Norman was a statute promulgated by the king in 1275. Thus, from the 13th century, Anglo-Norman became used in official documents, such as those that were marked by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lord_Privy_Seal" title="Lord Privy Seal">
        private seal
       </a>
       of the king whereas the documents sealed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lord_Chancellor" title="Lord Chancellor">
        Lord Chancellor
       </a>
       were written in Latin until the end of the Middle Ages. English became the language of
       <a href="/wiki/Parliament" title="Parliament">
        Parliament
       </a>
       and of legislation in the 15th century, half a century after it had become the language of the king and of most of the English nobility.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_administration_and_justice">
        Language of administration and justice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Language of administration and justice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/12th_century" title="12th century">
        12th century
       </a>
       , development of the administrative and judicial institutions took place. Because the king and the lawyers at the time normally used French, it also became the language of these institutions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From the 12th century until the 15th century, the courts used three languages. Latin was used for writing, French was the main oral language during trials, and English was used in less formal exchanges between the judge, the lawyer, the complainant or the witnesses. The judge gave his sentence orally in Norman, which was then written in Latin. Only in the lowest level of the manorial courts were trials entirely in English.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 15th century, English became the main spoken language, but Latin and French continued to be exclusively used in official legal documents until the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/18th_century" title="18th century">
        18th century
       </a>
       . Nevertheless, the French language used in England changed from the end of the 15th century into
       <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">
        Law French
       </a>
       . This variety of French was a technical language, with a specific vocabulary, where English words were used to describe everyday experience, and French grammatical rules and morphology gradually declined, with confusion of genders and the adding of
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       to form all plurals. Law French was banished from the courts of the
       <a href="/wiki/Common_law" title="Common law">
        common law
       </a>
       in 1731, almost three centuries after the king ceased speaking primarily French.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_of_the_people">
        Language of the people
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Language of the people">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Though the great mass of ordinary people spoke Middle English, French, because of its prestigious status, spread as a second language, encouraged by its long-standing use in the school system as a medium of instruction through which Latin was taught. In the courts, the members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Jury" title="Jury">
        jury
       </a>
       , who represented the population, had to know French in order to understand the plea of the lawyer. French was used by the merchant middle class as a language of business communication, especially when it traded with the continent, and several churches used French to communicate with lay people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A small but important number of documents survive associated with the Jews of medieval England, some featuring Anglo-French written in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       script, typically in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">
        glosses
       </a>
       to the Hebrew scriptures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fuderman_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fuderman-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
        langue d'oïl
       </a>
       , Anglo-Norman developed collaterally to the central
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance" title="Gallo-Romance">
        Gallo-Romance
       </a>
       dialects which would eventually become
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Parisian
       </a>
       French in terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronunciation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       . Before the signature of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">
        Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts
       </a>
       in 1539 and long afterward in practice, French was not standardised as an official administrative language of the kingdom of France.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       was heavily influenced by Anglo-Norman and, later, Anglo-French. W. Rothwell has called Anglo-French 'the
       <a href="/wiki/Transitional_fossil#Missing_links" title="Transitional fossil">
        missing link
       </a>
       ' because many etymological
       <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">
        dictionaries
       </a>
       seem to ignore the contribution of that language in English and because Anglo-Norman and Anglo-French can explain the transmission of words from French into English and fill the void left by the absence of documentary records of English (in the main) between 1066 and c. 1380.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Anglo-Norman
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       and pronunciation can be deduced from its heritage in English. Mostly, it is done in comparison with continental Central French. English has many
       <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">
        doublets
       </a>
       as a result of this contrast:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         warranty - guarantee
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         warden - guardian
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         catch - chase
        </i>
        (see below)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Compare also:
      </p>

        </i>

        <i>
         gage
        </i>
        (French)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         wait
        </i>
        -
        <i>
         guetter
        </i>
        (French, Old French
        <i>
         guaitier
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         war
        </i>
        (from Anglo-Norman
        <i>

        </i>

        <i>
         guerre

        (French)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>
        (Anglo-Norman) -
        <i>
         guichet
        </i>
        (French, from Norman)
       </li>

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar consonants
       </a>
       before the front vowel produced different results in Norman to the central langue d'oïl dialects that developed into French. English therefore, for example, has
       <i>
        fashion
       </i>
       from Norman
       <i>
        féchoun
       </i>
       as opposed to Modern French
       <i>
        façon
       </i>
       (both developing from Latin
       <i>
        fact
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        o, fact
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        ōnem
       </i>
       ).
      </p>

         plank
        </td>
        <td>
         &lt; planque
        </td>
        <td>
         = planche, planque
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         pocket
        </td>
        <td>
         &lt; pouquette
        </td>
        <td>
         = poche
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         fork
        </td>
        <td>
         &lt; fouorque
        </td>
        <td>
         = fourche
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         garden
        </td>
        <td>
         &lt; gardin
        </td>
        <td>
         = jardin
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         cattle
        </td>
        <td>
         &lt; *cate(l)
        </td>
        <td>
         = cheptel (Old French
         <i>
          chetel
         </i>
         )
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Other words such as
       <i>
        kennel
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        chenil
       </i>
       ) exemplify how Norman retained a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       sound from Latin that was not retained in French.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, Anglo-Norman also acted as a conduit for French words to enter England; for example,
       <i>
        challenge
       </i>
       clearly displays a form of French origin, rather than the Norman
       <i>
        calenge
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There were also vowel differences: compare Anglo-Norman
       <i>
        profound
       </i>
       with Parisian French
       <i>
        profond
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        soun
       </i>
       sound with
       <i>
        son
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        round
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        rond
       </i>
       . The former words were originally pronounced something like 'profoond', 'soond', 'roond' respectively (compare the similarly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">
        denasalised
       </a>
       vowels of modern Norman), but later developed their modern pronunciation in English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since many words established in Anglo-Norman from French via the intermediary of Norman were not subject to the processes of sound change that continued in parts of the continent, English sometimes preserves earlier pronunciations. For example, 'ch' used to be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       in Medieval French; Modern French has
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       , but English has preserved the older sound (in words like chamber, chain, chase and exchequer).
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly, 'j' had an older
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       sound, which it still has in English and some dialects of modern Norman, but it has developed into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       in Modern French.
      </p>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Veil" title="Veil">
         veil
        </a>
       </i>
       retains the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ei/
       </span>
       (as does modern Norman in
       <i>
        vaile
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        laîsi
       </i>
       ) that in French has been replaced by
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /wɑː/
       </span>
       <i>
        voile
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        loisir
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mushroom" title="Mushroom">
         mushroom
        </a>
       </i>
       preserves a hush
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        mousseron
       </i>
       not recorded in French orthography, as does
       <i>
        cushion
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        coussin
       </i>
       . Conversely, the pronunciation of the word
       <i>
        sugar
       </i>
       resembles Norman
       <i>
        chucre
       </i>
       even if the spelling is closer to French
       <i>
        sucre
       </i>
       . It is possible that the original sound was an
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       sibilant, like the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       <b>
        s
       </b>
       , which is halfway between a hissing sibilant and a hushing sibilant.
      </p>
      <p>
       The doublets
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/catch" title="wikt:catch">
         catch
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Chase_(disambiguation)" title="Chase (disambiguation)">
         chase
        </a>
       </i>
       , both derived from Low Latin
       <i>
        *captiare
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Catch
       </i>
       demonstrate a Norman development while
       <i>
        chase
       </i>
       is the French equivalent imported with a different meaning.
      </p>
      <p>
       Distinctions in meaning between Anglo-Norman and French have led to many
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_friends" title="False friends">
         faux amis
        </a>
       </i>
       (words having similar form but different meanings) in Modern English and Modern French.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since although a Romance language, Norman contains a significant amount of lexical material from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">
        Norse
       </a>
       , some of the words introduced into England as part of Anglo-Norman were of Germanic origin. Indeed, sometimes one can identify cognates such as
       <i>
        flock
       </i>
       (Germanic in English existing prior to the Conquest) and
       <i>
        flloquet
       </i>
       (Germanic in Norman). The case of the word
       <i>
        mug
       </i>
       demonstrates that in instances, Anglo-Norman may have reinforced certain Scandinavian elements already present in English.
       <i>
        Mug
       </i>
       had been introduced into northern English dialects by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viking" title="Viking">
        Viking
       </a>
       settlement. The same word had been established in Normandy by the Normans (Norsemen) and was then brought over after the Conquest and established firstly in southern English dialects. It is, therefore, argued that the word
       <i>
        mug
       </i>
       in English shows some of the complicated Germanic heritage of Anglo-Norman.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many expressions used in English today have their origin in Anglo-Norman (such as the expression
       <i>
        before-hand
       </i>
       derives from Anglo-Norman
       <i>
        avaunt-main
       </i>
       ), as do many modern words with interesting etymologies.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mortgage_loan" title="Mortgage loan">
         Mortgage
        </a>
       </i>
       , for example, literally meant
       <i>
        death-wage
       </i>
       in Anglo-Norman.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Curfew" title="Curfew">
         Curfew
        </a>
       </i>
       (fr.
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Couvre-feu" title="Couvre-feu">
         couvre-feu
        </a>
       </i>
       ) meant
       <i>
        cover-fire
       </i>
       , referring to the time in the evening when all fires had to be covered to prevent the spread of fire within communities with timber buildings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The word
       <i>
        glamour
       </i>
       is derived from Anglo-Norman
       <i>
        grammeire
       </i>
       , the same word which gives us modern
       <i>
        grammar
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        glamour
       </i>
       meant first
       <i>
        book learning
       </i>
       and then the most glamorous form of book learning,
       <i>
        magic
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        magic spell
       </i>
       in Medieval times.
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of Anglo-Norman was very asymmetric: very little influence from English was carried over into the continental possessions of the Anglo-Norman kings. Some administrative terms survived in some parts of mainland Normandy:
       <i>
        forlenc
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        furrow
       </i>
       , compare
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Furlong" title="Furlong">
         furlong
        </a>
       </i>
       ) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">
        Cotentin Peninsula
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bessin" title="Bessin">
        Bessin
       </a>
       , and a general use of the word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Acre" title="Acre">
         acre
        </a>
       </i>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_measurement" title="Land measurement">
        land measurement
       </a>
       in Normandy until
       <a href="/wiki/Metrication" title="Metrication">
        metrication
       </a>
       in the 19th century, but these words are probably linguistic traces of Saxon or Anglo-Scandinavian settlements between the 4th and the 10th centuries in Normandy. Otherwise the direct influence of English in mainland Norman (such as
       <i>
        smogler
       </i>
       - to smuggle) is from direct contact in later centuries with English, rather than Anglo-Norman.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_literature" title="Anglo-Norman literature">
        Anglo-Norman literature
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       When the
       <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">
        Normans
       </a>
       invaded England,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_literature" title="Anglo-Saxon literature">
        Anglo-Saxon literature
       </a>
       had reached a very high level of development. The important
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">
        Benedictine
       </a>
       monasteries both wrote chronicles and guarded other works in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       . However, with the arrival of the Norman, Anglo-Saxon literature came to an end and literature written in Britain was in Latin or Anglo-Norman. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantagenet" title="Plantagenet">
        Plantagenet
       </a>
       kings encouraged this
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_literature" title="Anglo-Norman literature">
        Anglo-Norman literature
       </a>
       . Nevertheless, from the beginning of the 14th century, some authors chose to write in English, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Chaucer" title="Geoffrey Chaucer">
        Geoffrey Chaucer
       </a>
       . The authors of that period were influenced by the works of contemporary French writers whose language was prestigious. Chaucer is considered to be the father of the English language and the creator of English as a literary language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_English">
        Influence on English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Influence on English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg/250px-Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg/375px-Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg/500px-Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Origins_of_English_PieChart_2D.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         About 28% of English
         <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
          vocabulary
         </a>
         comes from French, including Anglo-French (green).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The major Norman-French influence on English can still be seen in today's vocabulary. An enormous number of Norman-French and other medieval French
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       came into the language, and about three-quarters of them are still used today. Very often, the Norman or French word supplanted the Anglo-Saxon term, or both words would co-exist but with slightly different nuances: for example,
       <i>
        ox
       </i>
       (describing the animal) and
       <i>
        beef
       </i>
       (describing the meat). In other cases, the Norman or French word was adopted to signify a new reality, such as
       <i>
        judge
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        castle
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        warranty
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lusignan_8-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lusignan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In general, the Norman and French borrowings concerned the fields of culture, aristocratic life, politics and religion, and war whereas the English words were used to describe everyday experience. When the Normans arrived in England, their copyists wrote English as they heard it, without realising the peculiarities of the relationship between Anglo-Saxon pronunciation and spelling and so the spelling changed. There appeared different regional Modern-English written dialects, the one that the king chose in the 15th century becoming the standard variety.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some remote areas, agricultural terms used by the rural workers may have been derived from Norman French. An example is the Cumbrian term
       <i>
        sturdy
       </i>
       for diseased sheep that walk in circles, derived from
       <i>
        etourdi
       </i>
       meaning giddy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_in_Ireland">
        Influence in Ireland
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anglo-Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influence in Ireland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_invasion_of_Ireland" title="Norman invasion of Ireland">
        Norman invasion of Ireland
       </a>
       took place in the late 12th century and led to Anglo-Norman control of much of the island. Norman-speaking administrators arrived to rule over the
       <a href="/wiki/Angevin_Empire" title="Angevin Empire">
        Angevin Empire
       </a>
       's new territory. Several Norman words became
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       words, including household terms:
       <i>
        garsún
       </i>
       (from Norman
       <i>
        garçun
       </i>
       , "boy");
       <i>
        cóta
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        cote
       </i>
       , "cloak"); hata (
       <i>
        hatte
       </i>
       , "hat");
       <i>
        gairdín
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        gardin
       </i>
       , "garden"); and terms relating to justice (Irish
       <i>
        giúistís
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        bardas
       </i>
       (corporation),
       <i>
        cúirt
       </i>
       (court)). Place-names in Norman are few, but there is
       <a href="/wiki/Buttevant" title="Buttevant">
        Buttevant
       </a>
       (from the motto of the Barry family:
       <i>
        Boutez en Avant
       </i>
       , "Push to the Fore"), the village of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brittas,_Dublin" title="Brittas, Dublin">
        Brittas
       </a>
       (from the Norman
       <i>
        bretesche
       </i>
       , "boarding, planking") and the element
       <i>
        Pallas
       </i>
       (Irish
       <i>
        pailís
       </i>
       , from Norman
       <i>
        paleis
       </i>
       , "boundary fence": cf.
       <a href="/wiki/Palisade" title="Palisade">
        palisade
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pale" title="The Pale">
        The Pale
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others exist with English or Irish roots, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Castletownroche" title="Castletownroche">
        Castletownroche
       </a>
       , which combines the English
       <i>
        Castletown
       </i>
       and the Norman
       <i>
        Roche
       </i>
       , meaning rock.
      </p>
      <p>
       Only a handful of Hiberno-Norman-French texts survive, most notably the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chanson_de_geste" title="Chanson de geste">
         chanson de geste
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Song_of_Dermot_and_the_Earl" title="The Song of Dermot and the Earl">
         The Song of Dermot and the Earl
        </a>
       </i>
       (early 13th century) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Statutes_of_Kilkenny" title="Statutes of Kilkenny">
        Statutes of Kilkenny
       </a>
       (1366).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
