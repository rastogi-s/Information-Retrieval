      <p>
       <b>
        Debuccalization
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       in which an oral
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonant
       </a>
       loses its original
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       and moves it to the
       <a href="/wiki/Glottis" title="Glottis">
        glottis
       </a>
       (usually
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_glottal_fricative" title="Voiced glottal fricative">
         ɦ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
         ʔ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The pronunciation of a consonant as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       is sometimes called aspiration but in
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">
        aspiration
       </a>
       is the burst of air accompanying a
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       . The word comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        bucca
       </i>
       , meaning "cheek".
      </p>
      <p>
       Debuccalization is usually seen as a sub-type of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       , often defined as a consonant mutation involving the weakening of a consonant by progressive shifts in pronunciation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Debuccalization processes occur in many different types of environments such as the following:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        word-initially, as in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kannada_language" title="Kannada language">
         Kannada
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        word-finally, as in
        <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">
         Burmese
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        intervocalically, as in a number of
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        varieties (e.g.
        <i>
         litter
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈlɪʔə]
        </span>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Glottal_stop">
        Glottal stop
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Glottal stop">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /q/
       </span>
       is debuccalized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʔ/
       </span>
       in several Arabic varieties, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Arabic" title="Egyptian Arabic">
        Egyptian Arabic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="British_and_American_English">
        British and American English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: British and American English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most English-speakers in England and many speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       debuccalize
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       to a glottal stop
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʔ]
       </span>
       in two environments: in word-final position before another consonant
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         get ready
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈɡɛʔˈɹɛɾi]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         not much
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnɑʔˈmʌtʃ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         not good
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnɑʔˈɡʊd]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         it says
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɪʔˈsɛz]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       and before a
       <a href="/wiki/Syllabic_consonant" title="Syllabic consonant">
        syllabic
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n̩]
       </span>
       following
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       , or a vowel. Here the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       may also be
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_release" title="Nasal release">
        nasally released
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Milton
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈmɪlʔn̩]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Martin
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈmɑɹʔn̩]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         mountain
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈmaʊnʔn̩]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cotton
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkɑʔn̩]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cockney_English">
        Cockney English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Cockney English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Cockney#Typical_features" title="Cockney">
        Cockney English
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       is often realized as a glottal stop
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʔ]
       </span>
       between vowels, liquids, and nasals (notably in the word
       <i>
        bottle
       </i>
       ), a process called
       <a href="/wiki/T-glottalization" title="T-glottalization">
        t-glottalization
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="German">
        German
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: German">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Bavarian_language" title="Bavarian language">
        Bavarian dialect
       </a>
       debuccalizes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p, t, k, b, d, ɡ/
       </span>
       that occur between two consonants (a situation often produced by vowel elision in the same dialect) and realizes them as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʔ]
       </span>
       . Thus
       <i>
        Antn
       </i>
       (ducks) and
       <i>
        Andn
       </i>
       (Andes) are both pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈɑnʔn̩]
       </span>
       , but speakers believe them to be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈɑntn̩]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈɑndn̩]
       </span>
       , respectively.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Frequency depending on the location;
       <i>
        hàn(d)
       </i>
       ("are") occurs instead of the other (and more common) Bavarian form
       <i>
        sàn(d)
       </i>
       (from the German
       <i>
        seind
       </i>
       , in contemporary German:
       <i>
        sind
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Glottal_fricative">
        Glottal fricative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Glottal fricative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slavey">
        Slavey
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Slavey">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       All coda consonants in
       <a href="/wiki/Slavey_language" title="Slavey language">
        Slavey
       </a>
       must be glottal. When a non-glottal consonant would otherwise be positioned in a syllable coda, it debuccalizes to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ts’ad/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ts’ah]
        </span>
        ('hat')
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /xaz/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [xah]
        </span>
        ('scar')
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tl’ulʒ/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tl’uh]
        </span>
        ('rope')
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_English">
        Scottish English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Scottish English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">
        Scottish English
       </a>
       , particularly on the West coast, a non word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       shifted to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       , a process called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th-debuccalization" title="Th-debuccalization">
        th-debuccalization
       </a>
       . For example,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θɪn/
       </span>
       is realised as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [hɪn]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Greek">
        Proto-Greek
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Proto-Greek">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Greek_language" title="Proto-Greek language">
        Proto-Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       shifted to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       initially and between
       <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">
        sonorants
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">
        liquids
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasals
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
         Proto-Indo-European
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European_*sept%E1%B8%BF%CC%A5" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European *septḿ̥">
         <i>
          *septḿ̥
         </i>
        </a>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
         Ancient Greek
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%91%CF%80%CF%84%CE%AC#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:ἑπτά">
         <i>
          heptá
         </i>
        </a>
        "seven" (vs.
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/septem#Latin" title="wikt:septem">
         <i>
          septem
         </i>
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       was lost by the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       , and vowels in
       <a href="/wiki/Hiatus_(linguistics)" title="Hiatus (linguistics)">
        hiatus
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Synaeresis" title="Synaeresis">
        contracted
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">
        Attic
       </a>
       dialect.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        post-PIE
        <i>
         *ǵénesos
        </i>
        → Proto-Greek
        <i>
         *génehos
        </i>
        →
        <a href="/wiki/Ionic_Greek" title="Ionic Greek">
         Ionic
        </a>
        <i>
         géneos
        </i>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">
         Attic
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%AD%CE%BD%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:γένος">
         <i>
          génous
         </i>
        </a>
        "of a race"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Before a liquid or nasal, an
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       was assimilated to the preceding vowel in Attic-Ionic and
       <a href="/wiki/Doric_Greek" title="Doric Greek">
        Doric
       </a>
       and to the following nasal in
       <a href="/wiki/Aeolic_Greek" title="Aeolic Greek">
        Aeolic
       </a>
       . The process is also described as loss of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       and subsequent lengthening of a vowel or consonant to keep the syllable the same
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable_weight" title="Syllable weight">
        length
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Compensatory_lengthening" title="Compensatory lengthening">
        compensatory lengthening
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        PIE
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Indo-European_*h%E2%82%81%C3%A9smi" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Indo-European *h₁ésmi">
         <i>
          *h₁ésmi
         </i>
        </a>
        → Proto-Greek
        <i>
         *ehmi
        </i>
        → Attic-Ionic
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B5%E1%BC%B0%CE%BC%CE%AF#Ancient_Greek" title="wikt:εἰμί">
         <i>
          ēmí
         </i>
        </a>
        : Aeolic
        <i>
         émmi
        </i>
        "I am"
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sanskrit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       (written
       <i>
        ḥ
       </i>
       in transliteration) when
       <a href="/wiki/Pausa" title="Pausa">
        utterance-final
       </a>
       , e.g.,
       <i>
        kā́mas
       </i>
       "erotic love" becomes
       <i>
        kā́maḥ
       </i>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">
        Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties#Debuccalization_of_coda_/s/" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        Spanish dialects and varieties: Debuccalization of coda /s/
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A number of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        dialects
       </a>
       debuccalize
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" title="Syllable coda">
        end
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllable
       </a>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_glottal_fricative" title="Voiced glottal fricative">
         ɦ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Galician">
        Galician
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Galician">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In many varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       as well as in
       <a href="/wiki/Castrapo" title="Castrapo">
        Galician-influenced Spanish
       </a>
       , the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /g/
       </span>
       may debuccalize (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gheada" title="Gheada">
         gheada
        </a>
       </i>
       ) to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in most or all instances, though
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         x
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_pharyngeal_fricative" title="Voiceless pharyngeal fricative">
         ħ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       are also possible realizations. There is also an inverse
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       process of older or less educated Galician speakers replacing the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         x
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       of the Spanish language by
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [g]
       </span>
       , what is called
       <i>
        gueada
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Portuguese">
        Portuguese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Portuguese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       is affected to a much lesser degree by debuccalization, but it occurs, being specially notorious in its
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian variety
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       All over Brazil, the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʁ/
       </span>
       (historically an alveolar trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       that moved to an uvular position by both French and Arab-Berber influence)
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       has a rather long inventory of allophones, or up to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
          r
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_retroflex_fricative" title="Voiceless retroflex fricative">
          ɻ̝̊
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiceless palatal fricative">
          ç
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
          x
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
          ɣ
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_fricative" title="Voiceless uvular fricative">
          χ
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_uvular_fricative" title="Voiced uvular fricative">
          ʁ
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" title="Uvular trill">
          ʀ
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_pharyngeal_fricative" title="Voiceless pharyngeal fricative">
          ħ
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_glottal_fricative" title="Voiced glottal fricative">
          ɦ
         </a>
        </span>
        ]
       </span>
       , with all but [
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
         ɣ
        </a>
       </span>
       ] being common. Few dialects, such as
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <a href="/wiki/South_Region,_Brazil" title="South Region, Brazil">
          <i>
           sulista
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_(state)" title="Rio de Janeiro (state)">
          <i>
           fluminense
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       , give preference to voiced allophones; elsewhere, they are common only as coda, before voiced consonants.
      </p>
      <p>
       In such dialects, especially among people speaking an educated variety of Portuguese, it is usual for the rhotic coda in the
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Rime" title="Syllable">
        syllable rhyme
       </a>
       to be an alveolar tap, as in European Portuguese and many registers of Spanish, or to be realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_fricative" title="Voiceless uvular fricative">
         χ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         x
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . In the rest of the country, it is generally realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , even among speakers that do not have this allophone as the dominant, or deleted entirely, as commonplace in the Vernacular.
      </p>
      <p>
       But in some
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">
           mineiro
          </a>
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mineiro
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       -influenced
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          fluminense
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       rural registers, what changes is that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       is used in the rhyme, but as an allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
         l
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       (while rhotic consonants are most often deleted in the rhyme), a
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mar-mal
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       merger, instead of the much more common and less stigmatized
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <a href="/wiki/L-vocalization#Portuguese" title="L-vocalization">
          <i>
           mau-mal
          </i>
          merger
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       characteristic of all Brazilian urban centers with the exception of those bordering
       <a href="/wiki/Mercosur" title="Mercosur">
        Mercosur
       </a>
       countries, where coda
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant#Velarized_alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
         ɫ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       was preserved, and the entire
       <a href="/wiki/North_Region,_Brazil" title="North Region, Brazil">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">
        Northeast
       </a>
       regions. It comes from the process of replacing Amerindian languages and
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%ADngua_geral" title="Língua geral">
           línguas gerais
          </a>
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       by Portuguese,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       what created
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         ɹ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_approximant" title="Retroflex approximant">
         ɻ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/R-colored_vowel" title="R-colored vowel">
        r-colored vowel
       </a>
       as allophones of both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       (now mostly
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʁ/
       </span>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       (now mostly
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labio-velar_approximant" title="Voiced labio-velar approximant">
          u̯
         </a>
        </span>
        ~
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-back_rounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-back rounded vowel">
          ʊ̯
         </a>
        </span>
        ]
       </span>
       ) phonemes in the coda since their native speakers had difficulty with reproducing them (
       <a href="/wiki/Caipira_dialect" title="Caipira dialect">
        caipira dialect
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Later Portuguese influence from other regions made those allophones get rarer in some areas, but the
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mar-mal
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       merger did not disappear in such few isolated villages and towns.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, many
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          fluminense
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       registers, specially those of the poor and of the youth, most northern and northeastern dialects, and to a much minor degree all other Brazilian dialects, debuccalize
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (that is,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɕ ~ ʑ]
       </span>
       ), but not in the strength of what is done with Spanish. Still, there is a
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mar-mas
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       merger or even a
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mar-mais
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       merger:
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mas mesmo assim
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "but even so" or
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mas mesma, sim
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "though, right, the same (f) one"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [mɐɦ ˈmeɦmə ˈsĩ]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mais
         </i>
         light
        </span>
       </i>
       "lighter, more slim", or also "less caloric/fatty"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmaɦ ˈlajtɕ]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mas de mim, não
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "but from me, no" or
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          mais de mim, não
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "not more from me"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmaɦ dʑi ˈmĩ ˈnɜ̃w]
       </span>
       . A coda rhotic in the Brazilian dialects spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Centro-Sul" title="Centro-Sul">
        Centro-Sul
       </a>
       area is generally not glottal, with few exceptions, and a debuccalized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is rarely likely to be confused with it.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romanian">
        Romanian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Romanian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Romanian, Moldavian dialect,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        să fie
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        să hie
       </i>
       etc. as happened in Old Spanish, Old Gascon and Old Japanese.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Goidelic_Languages">
        Goidelic Languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Goidelic Languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_phonology#Lenition_and_spelling" title="Scottish Gaelic phonology">
        Scottish Gaelic phonology: Lenition and spelling
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_initial_mutations" title="Irish initial mutations">
        Irish initial mutations
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">
        Scottish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish Gaelic
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       changed by
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       , spelled
       <i>
        sh
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">
        Loanwords
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Debuccalization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Loanwords">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Debuccalization can be a feature of loanword phonology. For example, while
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       allows certain coda obstruents,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       does not. Those consonants realized in Korean as unreleased voiceless stops (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p˺ t˺ k˺]
       </span>
       ) may be realized in Japanese as glottal stops:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tɕuk˺]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tɕuʔ]
        </span>
        ('porridge')
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [mok˺]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [moʔ]
        </span>
        ('neck')
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [mat˺]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [maʔ]
        </span>
        ('flavor')
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tap˺]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [taʔ]
        </span>
        ('tower')
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Similarly, debuccalization can be seen in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bahasa_Indonesia" title="Bahasa Indonesia">
        Bahasa Indonesia
       </a>
       loans into
       <a href="/wiki/Selayar_language" title="Selayar language">
        Selayar
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
