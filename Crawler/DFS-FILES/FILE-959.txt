      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Gallo-Italian
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Gallo-Italic
       </b>
       languages constitute the majority of the
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy#Northern_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        languages of northern Italy
       </a>
       . They are
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       , although there is some doubt about the position of the latter due to a number of special characteristics.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian language
       </a>
       is usually considered one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian languages
       </a>
       ; however, some publications define it as Gallo-Italian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Gallo-Italian languages have characteristics both of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance languages
       </a>
       to the northwest (including
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       ) and the Italo-Dalmatian languages to the south (including
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ). Examples of the former are the loss of all final vowels other than
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       ; the occurrence of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       ; the development of original
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kt/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jt/
       </span>
       (and often later to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       ); and the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Front_rounded_vowel" title="Front rounded vowel">
        front rounded vowels
       </a>
       (e.g. the change of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       ). Examples of the latter are the use of vowel changes to indicate plurals, in place of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ; the widespread occurrence of
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphony_(Romance_languages)" title="Metaphony (Romance languages)">
        metaphony
       </a>
       of stressed vowels, triggered by original final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       ; and the development in some areas of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       instead of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/
       </span>
       as the result of
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalisation
       </a>
       of original
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       before
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result, there is some debate over the proper grouping of the Gallo-Italian languages. They are sometimes grouped with Gallo-Romance,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethnologue1_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ethnologue1-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but other linguists group them in Italo-Dalmatian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-a-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-b_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-b-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-c-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-d_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-d-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">
        Geographic distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Traditionally spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">
        San Marino
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">
        Monaco
       </a>
       , most Gallo-Italian languages have given way in everyday use to Standard Italian. The vast majority of current speakers are bilingual with Italian. These languages are still spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_diaspora" title="Italian diaspora">
        Italian diaspora
       </a>
       in countries with Italian immigrant communities. Ligurian is formalised in Monaco as the
       <a href="/wiki/Mon%C3%A9gasque_dialect" title="Monégasque dialect">
        Monégasque dialect
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Munegascu
       </i>
       ).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Gallo-Italian languages differ somewhat in their phonology from one language to another, but the following are the most important characteristics, as contrasted with Standard
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sources_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sources-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Most Gallo-Italian languages have lost all unstressed final vowels except
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        , e.g. Lombard
        <i>
         òm
        </i>
        "man",
        <i>
         füm
        </i>
        "smoke",
        <i>
         nef
        </i>
        "snow",
        <i>
         fil
        </i>
        "wire",
        <i>
         röda
        </i>
        "wheel" (Italian
        <i>
         uomo, fumo, neve, filo, ruota
        </i>
        ). They remain, however, in Ligurian, with passage of
        <i>
         -o
        </i>
        to
        <i>
         -u
        </i>
        , except after
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        ; e.g.
        <i>
         ramu, rami, lüme, lümi
        </i>
        "branch, branches, light, lights" (Italian
        <i>
         ramo, rami, lume, lumi
        </i>
        ), but
        <i>
         can, chen
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kaŋ, keŋ/
        </span>
        "dog, dogs" (Italian
        <i>
         cane, cani
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         u
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /u/
        </span>
        tends to evolve as
        <i>
         ü
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /y/
        </span>
        , as in French and Occitan, as in Lombard
        <i>
         füm
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         fumo
        </i>
        "smoke") and Ligurian
        <i>
         lüme
        </i>
        , Piedmont
        <i>
         lüm
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         lume
        </i>
        "light"). In some parts, e.g. southern Piedmont, this has further developed into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        , e.g.
        <i>
         fis
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         fuso
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         lim
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         lume
        </i>
        "light"). In some mountainous parts of Piedmont, however (e.g. Biellese, Ossolano), this development was blocked before final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        , leading to masculine
        <i>
         crü
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         crudo
        </i>
        "raw") but feminine
        <i>
         cru(v)a
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         cruda
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Metaphony_(Romance_languages)" title="Metaphony (Romance languages)">
         Metaphony
        </a>
        is very common, affecting original open stressed
        <i>
         è
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɛ/
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         ò
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɔ/
        </span>
        when followed by
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        or sometimes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        (operating before final vowels were dropped). This leads at first to diphthongs
        <i>
         ie
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         uo
        </i>
        , but in many dialects these progress further, typically to monophthongs
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ö
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ø/
        </span>
        . Unlike standard Italian diphthongization, this typically operates both in open and closed syllables, hence in Lombardy (where typically
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        but not
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        triggers metaphony)
        <i>
         quest
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         questo
        </i>
        "this") vs.
        <i>
         quist
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         questi
        </i>
        "these").
       </li>
       <li>
        Stressed closed
        <i>
         é
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        and sometimes
        <i>
         ó
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        , when occurring in an open syllable (followed by at most one consonant) often diphthongized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ei/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ou/
        </span>
        , as in
        <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
         Old French
        </a>
        ; e.g. Piedmont
        <i>
         beive
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         bere
        </i>
        &lt; *
        <i>
         bévere
        </i>
        "to drink"),
        <i>
         teila
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         tela
        </i>
        "cloth"),
        <i>
         meis
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         mese
        </i>
        "month"). In Piedmont,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ei/
        </span>
        developed further into either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɛ/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        , e.g.
        <i>
         tèla
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tɛla/
        </span>
        &lt;
        <i>
         *teila
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         tela
        </i>
        "cloth"),
        <i>
         sira
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         sera
        </i>
        "evening"),
        <i>
         mis
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         mese
        </i>
        "month").
       </li>
       <li>
        Stressed
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        in an open syllable often fronts to
        <i>
         ä
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /æ/
        </span>
        or
        <i>
         è
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɛ/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">
        Consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         Lenition
        </a>
        affects single consonants between vowels.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /g/
        </span>
        drop;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        or drops;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        become
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /g/
        </span>
        , or drop;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p/
        </span>
        becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        , or drops.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        between vowels voices to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        .
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        between vowels sometimes becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , and this
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        sometimes drops. Double consonants are reduced to single consonants, but not otherwise lenited.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        becomes velarized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋ/
        </span>
        . These changes occur before a final vowel drops. After loss of final vowels, however, further changes sometimes affect the newly final consonants, with voiced obstruents often becoming voiceless, and final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋ/
        </span>
        sometimes dropping. Liguria, especially in former times, showed particularly severe lenition, with total loss of intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /g/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        (probably also
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p/
        </span>
        , but not
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        ) in Old Genoese, hence
        <i>
         müa
        </i>
        (Latin
        <i>
         matura
        </i>
        "early"),
        <i>
         a éia e âe?
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         aveva le ali?
        </i>
        "did it have wings?"; modern
        <i>
         a l'aveiva e ae?
        </i>
        with restoration of various consonants due to Tuscan influence). In Liguria and often elsewhere, collapse of adjacent vowels due to loss of an intervocalic consonant produced new long vowels, notated with a circumflex.
       </li>
       <li>
        Italian palatoalveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dʒ/
        </span>
        are often reduced/assibilitated to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        , respectively. This typically does not occur in Lombardy, however, and parts of Liguria have intermediate
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ts/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dz/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Italian
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kj/
        </span>
        from Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kl/
        </span>
        is further palatalized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        ; similarly
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /gj/
        </span>
        from Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /gl/
        </span>
        becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dʒ/
        </span>
        . In Liguria,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /pj/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /bj/
        </span>
        from Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /pl/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /bl/
        </span>
        are affected in the same way, e.g. Ligurian
        <i>
         cian
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         piano
        </i>
        "soft") and
        <i>
         giancu
        </i>
        (Italian
        <i>
         bianco
        </i>
        "white").
       </li>
       <li>
        Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kt/
        </span>
        develops variously into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /jt/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        (contrast Italian
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tt/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Isolated_varieties_in_Sicily_and_in_Basilicata">
        Isolated varieties in Sicily and in Basilicata
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Isolated varieties in Sicily and in Basilicata">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Sicily" title="Gallo-Italic of Sicily">
        Gallo-Italic of Sicily
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Basilicata" title="Gallo-Italic of Basilicata">
        Gallo-Italic of Basilicata
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Varieties of Gallo-Italian languages are also found in
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       , corresponding with the central-eastern parts of the island that received large numbers of immigrants from Northern Italy, called
       <a href="/wiki/Lombards_of_Sicily" title="Lombards of Sicily">
        Lombards
       </a>
       , during the decades following the
       <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">
        Norman
       </a>
       conquest of Sicily (around 1080 to 1120). Given the time that has lapsed and the influence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian language
       </a>
       itself, these dialects are best generically described as
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Sicily" title="Gallo-Italic of Sicily">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       . The major centres where these dialects can still be heard today include
       <a href="/wiki/Piazza_Armerina" title="Piazza Armerina">
        Piazza Armerina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aidone" title="Aidone">
        Aidone
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sperlinga" title="Sperlinga">
        Sperlinga
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/San_Fratello" title="San Fratello">
        San Fratello
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nicosia_(EN)" title="Nicosia (EN)">
        Nicosia
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Novara_di_Sicilia" title="Novara di Sicilia">
        Novara di Sicilia
       </a>
       . Northern Italian dialects did not survive in some towns in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Catania" title="Province of Catania">
        province of Catania
       </a>
       that developed large
       <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">
        Lombard
       </a>
       communities during this period, namely
       <a href="/wiki/Randazzo" title="Randazzo">
        Randazzo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Patern%C3%B2" title="Paternò">
        Paternò
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronte,_Catania" title="Bronte, Catania">
        Bronte
       </a>
       . However, the Northern Italian influence in the local varieties of Sicilian are marked. In the case of San Fratello, some linguists have suggested that the dialect present today has
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Provençal
       </a>
       as its basis, having been a fort manned by Provençal mercenaries in the early decades of the Norman conquest (bearing in mind that it took the Normans 30 years to conquer the whole of the island).
      </p>
      <p>
       Other dialects, attested from 13th and 14th century, are also found in
       <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">
        Basilicata
       </a>
       , more precisely in the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Potenza" title="Potenza">
        Potenza
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Tito,_Basilicata" title="Tito, Basilicata">
        Tito
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Picerno" title="Picerno">
        Picerno
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pignola" title="Pignola">
        Pignola
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vaglio_Basilicata" title="Vaglio Basilicata">
        Vaglio Basilicata
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Trecchina" title="Trecchina">
        Trecchina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rivello" title="Rivello">
        Rivello
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nemoli" title="Nemoli">
        Nemoli
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Costantino&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Costantino (page does not exist)">
        San Costantino
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparisons_between_different_Gallo-Italic_languages">
        Comparisons between different Gallo-Italic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Comparisons between different Gallo-Italic languages">
         edit
        </a>
