      <p>
       <b>
        Balearic
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
         balear
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <small>
        IPA:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Catalan" title="Help:IPA/Catalan">
         [bəɫəˈa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is the collective name for the dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">
        Balearic Islands
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
         mallorquí
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
         eivissenc
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Ibiza" title="Ibiza">
        Ibiza
       </a>
       , and
       <i>
        <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
         menorquí
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Menorca" title="Menorca">
        Menorca
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       At the last census, 746,792 people in the Balearic Islands claimed to be able to speak
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       , though some of these people may be speakers of mainland variants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balearic_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Distinctive features of Catalan in the Balearic Islands differ according to the specific variant being spoken (Mallorcan, Menorcan, or Ibizan).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetic_features">
        Phonetic features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balearic_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonetic features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Vowels
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Most variants preserve a vocalic system of eight stressed vowels;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open front unrounded vowel">
          a
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel#Mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">
          ə
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open-mid front unrounded vowel">
          ɛ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">
          e
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">
          i
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open-mid back rounded vowel">
          ɔ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">
          o
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">
          u
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          The Majorcan system has eight stressed vowels
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /a ə ɛ e i ɔ o u/
          </span>
          , reduced to four
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ə i o u]
          </span>
          in unstressed position.
         </li>
         <li>
          The Western Minorcan system has eight stressed vowels
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /a ə ɛ e i ɔ o u/
          </span>
          , reduced to three
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ə i u]
          </span>
          in unstressed position.
         </li>
         <li>
          The Eastern Minorcan and Ibizan system has seven stressed vowels
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /a ɛ e i ɔ o u/
          </span>
          reduced to three
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ə i u]
          </span>
          in unstressed position (as in
          <a href="/wiki/Central_Catalan" title="Central Catalan">
           Central Catalan
          </a>
          ). There are differences between the dialect spoken in
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibiza_Town" title="Ibiza Town">
           Ibiza Town
          </a>
          (
          <i>
           eivissenc de vila
          </i>
          ) and those of the rest of the island (
          <i>
           eivissenc pagès
          </i>
          ) and
          <a href="/wiki/Formentera" title="Formentera">
           Formentera
          </a>
          (
          <i>
           formenterer
          </i>
          ).
         </li>
         <li>
          The vowel
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /a/
          </span>
          is central
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Open_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Open central unrounded vowel">
            ä
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          in Ibizan (as most Catalan dialects), while it is front
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open front unrounded vowel">
            a
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          in Majorcan and Minorcan.
         </li>
         <li>
          The so-called "
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid-open_vowel" title="Mid-open vowel">
           open vowels
          </a>
          " (
          <i>
           vocals obertes
          </i>
          ),
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɛ/
          </span>
          and
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɔ/
          </span>
          , are generally as low as
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /a/
          </span>
          in most Balearic subvarieties. The phonetic realizations of
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɛ/
          </span>
          approaches
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Near-open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-open front unrounded vowel">
            æ
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          (as in American English l
          <b>
           a
          </b>
          d) and
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɔ/
          </span>
          is as open as
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Open_back_rounded_vowel" title="Open back rounded vowel">
            ɒ
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          (as in traditional
          <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">
           RP
          </a>
          <i>
           d
           <b>
            o
           </b>
           g
          </i>
          ) (feature shared with
          <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
           Valencian
          </a>
          ). In many Majorcan dialects
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɔ/
          </span>
          can be unrounded to
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Open_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Open back unrounded vowel">
            ɑ
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          like in American English.
         </li>
         <li>
          In the most of parts of Majorca, words with ante-penultimate stress ending in
          <i>
           -ia
          </i>
          lose the ⟨a⟩
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel#Mid_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">
            ə
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          ; e.g.
          <i>
           glòria
          </i>
          ('glory') is pronounced as
          <i>
           glòri
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ˈɡɫɔɾi]
          </span>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       Notes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In Majorcan and some Minorcan subvarieties
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        become
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal
        </a>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_stop#Palatal_or_alveolo-palatal" title="Voiceless palatal stop">
          c
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_stop#Palatal_or_alveolo-palatal" title="Voiced palatal stop">
          ɟ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , before front vowels and word-finally; e.g.
        <i>
         figuera
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [fiˈɟeɾə]
        </span>
        ('fig tree').
       </li>
       <li>
        A phonemic distinction between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        is preserved, as in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alguerese" title="Alguerese">
         Alguerese
        </a>
        and standard Valencian.
       </li>
       <li>
        As Central Catalan
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        is velarized,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant#Velarized_alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
          ɫ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , in all instances; e.g.
        <i>
         tela
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈtɛɫə]
        </span>
        ('fabric').
       </li>
       <li>
        The palatal lateral approximant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        is preserved, with absence of
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísmo
        </a>
        except for the most Castilianized speakers. Nevertheless, in most of Majorcan occurs
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://ca.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ioditzaci%C3%B3" title="ca:Iodització">
          iodització
         </a>
        </i>
        , that is, a parallel
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological_process" title="Phonological process">
         process
        </a>
        to yeísmo (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        only in Latin-derived words with intervocalic L-palatalization:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        + yod (-
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         li
        </span>
        -, -
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         le
        </span>
        -), -
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         ll
        </span>
        -, -
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         cvl
        </span>
        -, and -
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         tvl
        </span>
        -; e.g.
        <i>
         palla
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpajə]
        </span>
        'straw'). Notice, this phenomenon is more restricted than yeísmo as initial L-palatalization always remains lateral in Majorcan; e.g.
        <i>
         lluna
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈʎunə]
        </span>
        ('moon').
       </li>
       <li>
        Depalatalization of syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɲs/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ncs/
        </span>
        with compensantory diphthongization in Majorcan:
        <i>
         troncs
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈtɾojns]
        </span>
        ('logs'),
        <i>
         anys
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈajns]
        </span>
        ('years').
       </li>
       <li>
        Most Balearic variants preserve final stops in clusters; e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [mp]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [nt]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋk]
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɫt]
        </span>
        :
        <i>
         camp
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkamp]
        </span>
        'field' (feature shared with modern Valencian).
       </li>
       <li>
        Assimilation of intervocalic clusters in some Majorcan and Minorcan subvarieties:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kt/ → [tː]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ks/ → [ts]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡz/ → [dz]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /pd/, /bd/, /td/, /kd/, /ɡd/ → [dː]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /bm/, /pm/, /dm/, /tm/ → [mː]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /fɡ/ → [ɡː]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /rl/ → [ɫː]
        </span>
        , etc. (notice some of these assimilations may also occur in continental Catalan, such as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /bm/, /pm/, /dm/, /tm/ → [mː]
        </span>
        :
        <i>
         capmoix
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ˌkapˈmoʃ/ → [ˌkamˈmoʃ]~[ˌkabˈmoʃ]
        </span>
        'crestfallen').
       </li>
       <li>
        Balearic is the variant of Catalan with the strongest tendency not to pronounce historical final ⟨r⟩ in any context; e.g.
        <i>
         amor
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [əˈmo]
        </span>
        'love',
        <i>
         cor
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkɔ]
        </span>
        'heart'.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Prosody
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Except in Ibiza, in combinations of
        <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
         verb
        </a>
        and weak
        <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
         pronoun
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
         clitics
        </a>
        ), the
        <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
         accent
        </a>
        moves to the final element; e.g.
        <i>
         comprar-ne
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [komˌpɾaˈnə]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kumˌpɾaˈnə]
        </span>
        (Standard Central Catalan
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kumˈpɾar.nə]
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphosyntactic_features">
        Morphosyntactic features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balearic_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphosyntactic features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Balearic preserves the
        <i>
         salat
        </i>
        definite article (derived from Latin
        <i>
         ipse/ipsa
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ille/illa
        </i>
        ), a feature shared only with
        <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
         Sardinian
        </a>
        among extant Romance languages, but which was more common in other
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">
         Gascon
        </a>
        areas in ancient times. However, the
        <i>
         salat
        </i>
        definite article is also preserved along the
        <a href="/wiki/Costa_Brava" title="Costa Brava">
         Costa Brava
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
         Catalonia
        </a>
        ) and in the
        <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">
         Valencian
        </a>
        municipalities of Tàrbena and
        <a href="/wiki/La_Vall_de_Gallinera" title="La Vall de Gallinera">
         La Vall de Gallinera
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The personal article
        <i>
         en
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         na
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         n'
        </i>
        is used before personal names.
       </li>
       <li>
        The first person singular present indicative has a zero exponent, i.e. no visible ending. For example, what in Central Catalan would be
        <i>
         jo parlo
        </i>
        ('I speak') is realized as
        <i>
         jo parl
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In verbs of the first conjugation (in
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        ), the first and second person plural forms end in -
        <i>
         am
        </i>
        and -
        <i>
         au
        </i>
        respectively. For example,
        <i>
         cantam
        </i>
        ('we sing'),
        <i>
         cantau
        </i>
        ('you
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
         pl.
        </a>
        sing').
       </li>
       <li>
        Also in verbs of the first conjugation, the imperfect subjunctive is formed with -
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        -, e.g.
        <i>
         cantàs
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cantassis
        </i>
        . However, the Standard Catalan forms in ⟨e⟩ are nowadays also common in many places.
       </li>
       <li>
        In combinations of two unstressed pronouns preceding a verb, one direct with the form
        <i>
         el
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         la
        </i>
        , etc. and the other indirect with the form
        <i>
         me
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         te
        </i>
        , etc., the direct pronoun appears first. For example,
        <i>
         la me dóna
        </i>
        ('s/he gives it to me'), Standard Catalan
        <i>
         me la dóna
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexical_features">
        Lexical features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balearic_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lexical features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Balearic has a large quantity of characteristic vocabulary, especially archaisms preserved by the isolation of the islands and the variety of linguistic influences which surround them. The lexicon differs considerably depending on the subdialect. For example:
        <i>
         al·lot
        </i>
        for standard "
        <i>
         noi
        </i>
        " ('boy'),
        <i>
         moix
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         gat
        </i>
        " ('cat'),
        <i>
         besada
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         petó
        </i>
        " ('kiss'),
        <i>
         ca
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         gos
        </i>
        " ('dog'),
        <i>
         doblers
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         diners
        </i>
        " ('money'),
        <i>
         horabaixa
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         tarda
        </i>
        " ('evening') and
        <i>
         rata-pinyada
        </i>
        for "
        <i>
         rat-penat
        </i>
        " ('
        <a href="/wiki/Bat" title="Bat">
         bat
        </a>
        ').
       </li>
       <li>
        Minorcan has a few English loanwords dating back to the British occupation, such as
        <i>
         grevi
        </i>
        ('gravy'),
        <i>
         xumaquer
        </i>
        ('shoemaker'),
        <i>
         boínder
        </i>
        ('bow window'),
        <i>
         xoc
        </i>
        ('chalk') or
        <i>
         ull blec
        </i>
        ('black eye').
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_questions">
        Political questions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Balearic_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Political questions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some in the Balearic Islands, such as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/People%27s_Party_(Spain)" title="People's Party (Spain)">
         Partido Popular
        </a>
       </i>
       and a former regional president,
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Ram%C3%B3n_Bauz%C3%A0" title="José Ramón Bauzà">
        José Ramón Bauzà
       </a>
       , argue that the dialects of Baleric Islands are actually separate languages and not dialects of Catalan. Bauzà has campaigned against having centralized or standardized standards of Catalan in public education.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
