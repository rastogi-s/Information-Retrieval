      <p>
       An
       <b>
        obstruent
       </b>
       is a speech sound such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d͡ʒ]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       that is formed by
       <i>
        obstructing
       </i>
       airflow. Obstruents contrast with
       <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">
        sonorants
       </a>
       , which have no such obstruction and so resonate. All obstruents are
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       , but sonorants include both
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       and consonants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Obstruents are subdivided into
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        plosives
       </a>
       (oral stops), such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p, t, k, b, d, ɡ]
       </span>
       , with complete occlusion of the vocal tract, often followed by a release burst;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricatives
       </a>
       , such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f, s, ʃ, x, v, z, ʒ, ɣ]
       </span>
       , with limited closure, not stopping airflow but making it turbulent; and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricates
       </a>
       , which begin with complete occlusion but then release into a fricative-like release, such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡ʃ] and [d͡ʒ]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Obstruents are prototypically
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_consonant" title="Voiceless consonant">
        voiceless
       </a>
       , but voiced obstruents are common. This contrasts with sonorants, which are prototypically voiced and only rarely voiceless.
      </p>
