      <p>
       <b>
        Philippine Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español filipino, castellano filipino
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a variant of standard
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       mostly by
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Filipino" title="Spanish Filipino">
        Spanish Filipinos
       </a>
       . Since the independence of the Philippines from Spain (1898), the dialect has lost most of its speakers and it might be now close to disappearing.
      </p>

      <p>
       Philippine Spanish is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        dialect
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       . The variant is very similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       , because of Mexican and Latin American emigration to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">
        Spanish East Indies
       </a>
       over the years.
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1565 to 1821, the Philippines which were a part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_East_Indies" title="Spanish East Indies">
        Spanish East Indies
       </a>
       , were governed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Captaincy_General_of_the_Philippines" title="Captaincy General of the Philippines">
        Captaincy General of the Philippines
       </a>
       as a territory of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_New_Spain" title="Viceroyalty of New Spain">
        Viceroyalty of New Spain
       </a>
       centered in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       . It was only administered directly from Spain in 1821 after Mexico gained its independence that same year. Since the Philippines was a former territory of the Viceroyalty of New Spain for most of the Spanish colonial period, Spanish as was spoken in the Philippines had a greater affinity to
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        American Spanish
       </a>
       rather than to
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        Peninsular Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       An estimated 689,000 people speak
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">
        Spanish-based creole
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cervantes_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cervantes-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2010 the
       <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Cervantes" title="Instituto Cervantes">
        Instituto Cervantes
       </a>
       de Manila put the number of Spanish speakers in the Philippines in the area of three million, which included the native and the non-native Chavacano and Spanish speakers as well since there are some Filipinos who can speak Spanish and Chavacano as a second, third, or fourth language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology" title="Spanish phonology">
        Spanish phonology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Philippine Spanish has the following characteristics. Some of these have been passed on the manner of everyday speech of Filipinos; such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       being
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       and seseo.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="y.2Fll_distinction">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="y/ll_distinction">
        y/ll distinction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: y/ll distinction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As in some dialects in northern Spain and in some bilingual zones (Bolivia, Paraguay, and Peru) in Latin America, there is a distinction between ll
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ/
       </span>
       and y
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>
       . For example, "
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/silla#Spanish" title="wikt:silla">
          silla
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsiʎa/
       </span>
       and not
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsiʝa/
       </span>
       (Tagalog "
       <i>
        <span lang="gl" xml:lang="gl">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/silya#Galician" title="wikt:silya">
          silya
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "). However,
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       occurs in some words like "
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cebollas#Spanish" title="wikt:cebollas">
          cebollas
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /seˈboʝas/
       </span>
       (Tagalog "
       <i>
        <span lang="tl" xml:lang="tl">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sibuyas#Tagalog" title="wikt:sibuyas">
          sibuyas
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ") and "
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caballo#Spanish" title="wikt:caballo">
          caballo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kaˈbaʝo/
       </span>
       (Tagalog "
       <i>
        <span lang="tl" xml:lang="tl">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/kabayo#Tagalog" title="wikt:kabayo">
          kabayo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Seseo">
        Seseo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Seseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As in Latin American Spanish, the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       phonemes merge into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . For example, cerveza
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /seɾˈbesa/
       </span>
       (Tagalog "serbesa").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Retraction_of_.2Fx.2F_to_.5Bh.5D">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Retraction_of_/x/_to_[h]">
        Retraction of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Retraction of /x/ to [h]">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The velar jota sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       is retracted to glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . This also occurs in Andalusian, Caribbean, Canarian and Colombian Spanish. For example, "Jorge"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈhoɾhe ~ ˈholhe]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Merger_of_.2Fd.2F_and_.2F.C9.BE.2F.2C_.2Fp.2F_and_.2Ff.2F_and_non-open_vowels">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_of_/d/_and_/ɾ/,_/p/_and_/f/_and_non-open_vowels">
        Merger of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɾ/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        and non-open vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Merger of /d/ and /ɾ/, /p/ and /f/ and non-open vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As a Malayo-Polynesian country (such countries often interchange
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       as well as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       ) this could be observed in Philippine Spanish. For example, "pared"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [paˈdeɾ]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "pader"), Filipinas
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [piliˈpinas]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "Pilipinas"), entender
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [intinˈdiɾ]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "intindi") and ojal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [uˈhal]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "uhal").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="No_aspiration_of_.2Fs.2F">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="No_aspiration_of_/s/">
        No aspiration of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: No aspiration of /s/">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Unlike many Spanish and Latin American dialects, syllable-final "s" is pronounced as an alveolar sibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , rather than a glottal fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . For example, "amores secos"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈmoɾes ˈsekos]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Retention_of_.5Bh.5D_of_words_originating_from_Latin_f-">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Retention_of_[h]_of_words_originating_from_Latin_f-">
        Retention of [h] of words originating from Latin f-
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Retention of [h] of words originating from Latin f-">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Words like "hacer"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈseɾ]
       </span>
       , "hablar"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈblaɾ]
       </span>
       come from the Latin words facere and fabulare, respectively. However, in Philippine Spanish, the h- initial is pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , not silent nor
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         x
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . Therefore, the pronunciations would be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [haˈseɾ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [haˈblaɾ]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "habla"). Because
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       in Philippine Spanish is glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , it is best to consider those words to phonemically be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /xaˈseɾ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /xaˈblaɾ/
       </span>
       , rather than to postulate a marginal phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       . The same thing occurs sometimes in Andalusian and Caribbean Spanish.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id=".5B.C9.BE.E2.80.93l.5D_shift_at_the_end_of_syllables">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="[ɾ–l]_shift_at_the_end_of_syllables">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ–l]
        </span>
        shift at the end of syllables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Philippine_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: [ɾ–l] shift at the end of syllables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At the end of a syllable of a word or a word itself, if the syllable/word ends with an l or r,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       often. For instance, almíbar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aɾˈnibal]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "arnibal), rezar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [daˈsal]
       </span>
       (Tagalog "dasal"). This also occurs in Andalusian and Caribbean Spanish.
      </p>

        </span>

      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">
        Spanish orthography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        Diacritic marks
       </a>
       are almost always left out, save for the tilde on the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%91" title="Ñ">
         ñ
        </a>
       </i>
       , because of the use of American standard machines and because of the disallowance of using these marks by English-language media companies. Typewriters sometimes include the
       <i>
        ñ
       </i>
       , but they do not include accented vowels. Computer keyboards have always used the US standard layout, which includes neither
       <i>
        ñ
       </i>
       nor combining diacritics. Spanish words, however, are vocally stressed as they would be by Spanish speakers.
      </p>
