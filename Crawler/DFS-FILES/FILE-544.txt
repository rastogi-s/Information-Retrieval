       specifically the primary variety known as
       <b>
        Paraguayan Guarani
       </b>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endonym" title="Endonym">
        endonym
       </a>
       <i>
        avañe'ẽ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Guarani" title="Help:IPA/Guarani">
         [aʋãɲẽˈʔẽ]

       'the people's language'), is an indigenous language of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       that belongs to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tupi%E2%80%93Guarani" title="Tupi–Guarani">
        Tupi–Guarani
       </a>
       family
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tupian_languages" title="Tupian languages">
        Tupian languages
       </a>
       . It is one of the official languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">
        Paraguay
       </a>
       (along with
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ), where it is spoken by the majority of the population, and where half of the rural population is monolingual.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is spoken by communities in neighboring countries, including parts of northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       , southeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
        Bolivia
       </a>
       and southwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       , and is a second official language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Provinces_of_Argentina" title="Provinces of Argentina">
        Argentine province
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Corrientes_Province" title="Corrientes Province">
        Corrientes
       </a>
       since 2004;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it is also an official language of
       <a href="/wiki/Mercosur" title="Mercosur">
        Mercosur
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Guarani is one of the most-widely spoken
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       and the only one whose speakers include a large proportion of non-indigenous people. This is an interesting anomaly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">
        language shift
       </a>
       towards European colonial languages (in this case, the other
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official language
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ) has otherwise been a nearly universal cultural and identity marker of
       <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">
        mestizos
       </a>
       (people of mixed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">
        Amerindian
       </a>
       ancestry), and also of
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_assimilation" title="Cultural assimilation">
        culturally assimilated
       </a>
       , upwardly mobile
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">
        Amerindian
       </a>
       people.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       priest
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Ruiz_de_Montoya" title="Antonio Ruiz de Montoya">
        Antonio Ruiz de Montoya
       </a>
       , who in 1639 published the first written grammar of Guarani in a book called
       <i>
        Tesoro de la lengua guaraní
       </i>
       (Treasure of the Guarani Language), described it as a language "so copious and elegant that it can compete with the most famous [of languages]".
      </p>
      <p>
       The name "Guarani" is generally used for the official language of Paraguay. However, this is part of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialect_chain" title="Dialect chain">
        dialect chain
       </a>
       , most of whose
       <a href="/wiki/Guarani_dialects" title="Guarani dialects">
        components
       </a>
       are also often called Guarani.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The persistence of Guarani is, contrary to popular belief, not exclusively, or even primarily, due to the influence of the Jesuits in Paraguay.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       While Guarani was the only language spoken in the expansive missionary territories, Paraguayan Guarani has its roots outside of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit_Reductions" title="Jesuit Reductions">
        Jesuit reductions
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern scholarship has shown that Guarani was always the primary language of colonial Paraguay, both inside and outside the reductions. Following the
       <a href="/wiki/Suppression_of_the_Society_of_Jesus" title="Suppression of the Society of Jesus">
        expulsion of the Jesuits
       </a>
       in the 18th century, the residents of the reductions gradually migrated north and west towards
       <a href="/wiki/Asunci%C3%B3n" title="Asunción">
        Asunción
       </a>
       , a demographic shift that brought about a decidedly one-sided shift away from the Jesuit dialect that the missionaries had curated in the southern and eastern territories of the colony.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By and large, the Guarani of the Jesuits shied away from direct phonological loans from Spanish. Instead, the missionaries relied on the agglutinative nature of the language to formulate
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       terms from native morphemes. This process often led the Jesuits to employ complicated, highly synthetic terms to convey Western concepts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By contrast, the Guarani spoken outside of the missions was characterized by a free, unregulated flow of Hispanicisms; frequently, Spanish words and phrases were simply incorporated into Guarani with minimal phonological adaptation.
      </p>
      <p>
       A good example of this phenomenon is found in the word "communion". The Jesuits, using their agglutinative strategy, rendered this word "Tupârahava", a calque based on the word "Tupâ", meaning God.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In modern Paraguayan Guarani, the same word is rendered "komuño".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following the out-migration from the reductions, these two distinct dialects of Guarani came into extensive
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        contact
       </a>
       for the first time. The vast majority of speakers abandoned the less-colloquial, highly regulated Jesuit variant in favor of the variety that evolved from actual language usage by speakers in Paraguay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This contemporary form of spoken Guaraní is known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jopar%C3%A1" title="Jopará">
        Jopará
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">
        Writing system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Guarani_alphabet" title="Guarani alphabet">
        Guarani alphabet
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3648" data-file-width="2736" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg/220px-Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg/330px-Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg/440px-Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Church_of_the_Pater_Noster_gn.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Church of the Pater Noster in Guarani.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Guarani became a written language relatively recently. Its modern alphabet is basically a subset of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       (with "J", "K" and "Y" but not "W"), complemented with two diacritics and six
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraphs
       </a>
       . Its
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       is largely phonemic, with letter values mostly similar to those of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Tilde" title="Tilde">
        tilde
       </a>
       is used with many letters that are considered part of the alphabet. In the case of Ñ/ñ, it differentiates the palatal nasal from the alveolar nasal (as in Spanish), whereas it marks stressed
       <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">
        nasalisation
       </a>
       when used over a vowel (as in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ): ã, ẽ, ĩ, õ, ũ, ỹ. (Nasal vowels have been written with several other diacritics: ä, ā, â, ã.) The tilde also marks nasality in the case of G̃/g̃, used to represent the nasalized velar approximant by combining the velar approximant "G" with the nasalising tilde. The letter G̃/g̃, which is unique to this language, was introduced into the orthography relatively recently during the mid-20th century and there is disagreement over its use. It is not a
       <a href="/wiki/Precomposed_character" title="Precomposed character">
        precomposed character
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       , which can cause typographic inconveniences – such as needing to press "delete" twice – or imperfect rendering when using computers and fonts that do not properly support the complex layout feature of glyph composition.
      </p>
      <p>
       Only stressed nasal vowels are written as nasal. If an oral vowel is stressed, and it is not the final syllable, it is marked with an acute accent: á, é, í, ó, ú, ý. That is, stress falls on the vowel marked as nasalized, if any, else on the accent-marked syllable, and if neither appears, then on the final syllable.
      </p>
      <p>
       For
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blind_people" title="Blind people">
        blind people
       </a>
       there is also a
       <a href="/wiki/Guarani_Braille" title="Guarani Braille">
        Guarani Braille
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Guarani only allows syllables consisting of a consonant plus a vowel or a vowel alone; syllables ending in a consonant or two or more consonants together are not possible. This is represented as
       <i>
        (C)V(V)
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vowels" title="Vowels">
         Vowels
        </a>
        :
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/, /e/, /i/, /o/, /u/
        </span>
        correspond more or less to the Spanish and IPA equivalents, although sometimes the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allophones" title="Allophones">
         allophones
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɛ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɔ]
        </span>
        are used more frequently; the grapheme ⟨y⟩ represents the vowel
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Close central unrounded vowel">
          ɨ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        (as in
        <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
         Polish
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ], [ʒ], [j]

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɲ]
       </span>

        [h]
       </span>

        [ɣ], [ɣʷ]
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɰʷ]
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       .
      </p>

       All syllables are open, viz. CV or V, ending in a vowel.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Glottal_stop">
        Glottal stop
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Glottal stop">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
        glottal stop
       </a>
       is only written between vowels, but occurs phonetically before vowel-initial words. Because of this, Ayala (2000:19) shows that some words have several glottal stops near each other, which consequently undergo a number of different
       <a href="/wiki/Dissimilation" title="Dissimilation">
        dissimilation
       </a>
       techniques. For example, "I drink water" '
       <i>
        a'u'y
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <i>
        hau'y
       </i>
       . This suggests that even irregular verbs in Guarani are regular underlyingly. There also seems to be some degree of variation between how much the glottal stop is dropped (for example
       <i>
        aru'uka
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        aruuka
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        aruka
       </i>
       for "I bring"). It is suspected that the glottal stop was not an original phoneme but that word-internal glottal stops are only fossilized compounds where the second component was a vowel-initial (and therefore glottal stop–initial) root.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ayala_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ayala-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         19
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nasal_harmony">
        Nasal harmony
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nasal harmony">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Guarani displays an unusual degree of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_harmony" title="Nasal harmony">
        nasal harmony
       </a>
       . A nasal syllable consists of a nasal vowel, and if the consonant is voiced, it takes its nasal allophone. If a stressed syllable is nasal, the nasality spreads in
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       directions until it bumps up against a stressed syllable that is oral. This includes
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affixes
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postposition" title="Postposition">
        postpositions
       </a>
       , and compounding. Voiceless consonants do not have nasal allophones, but they do not interrupt the spread of nasality.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example,
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ⁿdo+ɾoi+ⁿduˈpã+i/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [nõɾ̃õĩnũˈpãĩ]
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ro+ᵐbo+poˈrã/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ̃õmõpõˈɾ̃ã]
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       However, a second stressed syllable, with an oral vowel, will not become nasalized:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /iᵈjaˈkãɾaˈku/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʔĩɲãˈkãɾ̃ãˈku]
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aˈkãɾaˈwe/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʔãˈkãɾ̃ãˈwe]
        </span>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       That is, for a word with a single stressed vowel, all voiced segments will be either oral or nasal, while voiceless consonants are unaffected, as in oral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ᵐbotɨ/
       </span>
       vs nasal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /mõtɨ̃/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Guaraní is a highly
       <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">
        agglutinative language
       </a>
       , often classified as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polysynthetic" title="Polysynthetic">
        polysynthetic
       </a>
       . It is a fluid-S type
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_language" title="Active language">
        active language
       </a>
       , and it has been classified as a 6th class language in
       <a href="/wiki/Milewski%27s_typology" title="Milewski's typology">
        Milewski's typology
       </a>
       . It uses
       <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subject–verb–object">
        subject–verb–object
       </a>
       word order usually, but
       <a href="/wiki/OV_language" title="OV language">
        object–verb
       </a>
       when the subject is not specified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The language lacks
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       and has no
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       , but due to influence from Spanish,
       <b>
        la
       </b>
       is used as a definite article for singular reference, and
       <b>
        lo
       </b>
       for plural reference. These are not found in Classical Guarani (
       <i>
        Guaraniete
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">
        Nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Guarani exhibits nominal tense: past, expressed with
       <i>
        -kue
       </i>
       , and future, expressed with
       <i>
        -rã
       </i>
       . For example,
       <i>
        tetã ruvichakue
       </i>
       translates to "ex-president" while
       <i>
        tetã ruvicharã
       </i>
       translates to "president-elect." The past morpheme
       <i>
        -kue
       </i>
       is often translated as "ex-", "former", "abandoned", "what was once", or "one-time". These morphemes can even be combined to express the idea of something that was going to be but didn't end up happening. So for example,
       <i>
        pa'irãgue
       </i>
       is "a person who studied to be a priest but didn't actually finish", or rather, "the ex-future priest". Note that some nouns use
       <i>
        -re
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        -kue
       </i>
       and others use
       <i>
        -guã
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        -rã
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">
        Conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Conjugation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Guarani stems can be divided into a number of conjugation classes, which are called
       <i>
        areal
       </i>
       (with the subclass
       <i>
        aireal
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        chendal
       </i>
       , respectively. The names for these classes stem from the names of the prefixes for 1st and 2nd person singular.
      </p>
      <p>
       The areal conjugation is used to convey that the participant is
       <a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">
        actively involved
       </a>
       , whereas the chendal conjugation is used to convey that the participant is the
       <a href="/wiki/Patient_(grammar)" title="Patient (grammar)">
        undergoer
       </a>
       . Note that intransitive verbs can take either conjugation, transitive verbs normally take areal, but can take chendal for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habitual" title="Habitual">
        habitual
       </a>
       readings. Nouns can also be conjugated, but only as chendal. This conveys a predicative possessive reading.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Caralho1993_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Caralho1993-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Furthermore, the conjugations vary slightly according to the stem being oral or nasal.

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">
        Negation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Negation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Negation is indicated by a
       <a href="/wiki/Circumfix" title="Circumfix">
        circumfix
       </a>
       <i>
        n(d)(V)-...-(r)i
       </i>
       in Guarani. The preverbal portion of the circumfix is
       <i>
        nd-
       </i>
       for oral bases and
       <i>
        n-
       </i>
       for nasal bases. For 2nd person singular, an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epenthetic" title="Epenthetic">
        epenthetic
       </a>
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       is inserted before the base, for 1st person plural inclusive, an epenthetic
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       is inserted.
      </p>
      <p>
       The postverbal portion is
       <i>
        -ri
       </i>
       for bases ending in
       <i>
        -i
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -i
       </i>
       for all others. However, in spoken Guarani, the "-ri" portion of the circumfix is frequently omitted for bases ending in "-i".
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>

         <p>

          </i>
          (do, make)
         </p>
        </th>

         Nasal verb
         <p>
          <i>
           kororõ
          </i>
          (roar, snore)
         </p>
        </th>
        <th>

         <p>

          </i>
          (go up, rise)
         </p>

      <p>
       The negation can be used in all tenses, but for future or irrealis reference, the normal tense marking is replaced by
       <i>
        mo'ã
       </i>
       , resulting in
       <i>
        n(d)
       </i>
       (V)
       <i>
        -base-mo'ã-i
       </i>
       as in
       <i>
        Ndajapomo'ãi
       </i>
       , "I won't do it".
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also other negatives, such as:
       <i>
        ani
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ỹhỹ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        nahániri
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        naumbre
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        na'anga
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tense_and_aspect_morphemes">
        Tense and aspect morphemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tense and aspect morphemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         ramo
        </b>
        : marks extreme proximity of the action, often translating to "just barely":
        <i>
         Oguahẽramo
        </i>
        , "He just barely arrived".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Graham,_1969_22-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Graham,_1969-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         :
         <span>
          198
         </span>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         kuri
        </b>
        : marks proximity of the action.
        <i>
         Ha'ukuri
        </i>
        , "I just ate" (
        <i>
         ha'u
        </i>
        irregular first person singular form of
        <i>
         u
        </i>
        , "to eat"). It can also be used after a pronoun,
        <i>
         ha che kuri, che po'a
        </i>
        , "and about what happened to me, I was lucky".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         va'ekue
        </b>
        : indicates a fact that occurred long ago and asserts that it's really truth.
        <i>
         Okañyva'ekue
        </i>
        , "he/she went missing a long time ago".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         ra'e
        </b>
        : tells that the speaker was doubtful before but he's sure at the moment he speaks.
        <i>
         Nde rejoguara'e peteĩ ta'angambyry pyahu
        </i>
        , "so then you bought a new television after all".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         raka'e
        </b>
        : expresses the uncertainty of a perfect-aspect fact.
        <i>
         Peẽ peikoraka'e Asunción-pe
        </i>
        , "I think you lived in Asunción for a while". Nevertheless, nowadays this morpheme has lost some of its meaning, having a correspondence with
        <i>
         ra'e
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         va'ekue
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The verb form without suffixes at all is a
       <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
        present
       </a>
       somewhat
       <a href="/wiki/Aorist" title="Aorist">
        aorist
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Upe ára resẽ reho mombyry
       </i>
       , "that day you got out and you went far".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         ta
        </b>
        : is a
        <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">
         future
        </a>
        of immediate happening, it's also used as authoritarian
        <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
         imperative
        </a>
        .
        <i>
         Oujeýta ag̃aite
        </i>
        , "he/she'll come back soon".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         ma
        </b>
        : has the meaning of "already".
        <i>
         Ajapóma
        </i>
        , "I already did it".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These two suffixes can be added together:
       <i>
        ahátama
       </i>
       , "I'm already going".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         va'erã
        </b>
        : indicates something not imminent or something that must be done for social or moral reasons, in this case corresponding to the
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Modal_verb" title="Modal verb">
         modal verb
        </a>
        <i>
         sollen
        </i>
        .
        <i>
         Péa ojejapova'erã
        </i>
        , "that must be done".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         ne
        </b>
        : indicates something that probably will happen or something the speaker imagines that is happening. It correlates in a certain way with the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
         subjunctive
        </a>
        of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Language" title="Spanish Language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        .
        <i>
         Mitãnguéra ág̃a og̃uahéne hógape
        </i>
        , "the children are probably coming home now".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         hína
        </b>
        ,
        <i>
         ína
        </i>
        after nasal words: continual action at the moment of speaking, present and pluperfect continuous or emphatic.
        <i>
         Rojatapyhína
        </i>
        , "we're making fire";
        <i>
         che ha'ehína
        </i>
        , "it's ME!".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         vo
        </b>
        : it has a subtle difference with
        <i>
         hína
        </i>
        in which
        <i>
         vo
        </i>
        indicates not necessarily what's being done at the moment of speaking.
        <i>
         amba'apóvo
        </i>
        , "I'm working (not necessarily now)".
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         pota
        </b>
        : indicates proximity immediately before the start of the process.
        <i>
         Ajukapota
        </i>
        , "I'm near the edge in which I will start to kill". (A particular sandhi rule is applied here: if the verbs ends in "po", the suffix changes to
        <i>
         mbota
        </i>
        ;
        <i>
         ajapombota
        </i>
        , "I'll do it right now").
       </li>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         pa
        </b>
        : indicates emphatically that a process has all finished.
        <i>
         Amboparapa pe ogyke
        </i>
        , "I painted the wall completely".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This suffix can be joined with
       <i>
        ma
       </i>
       , making up
       <i>
        páma
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        ñande jaikuaapáma nde remimo'ã
       </i>
       , "now we became to know all your thought".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        -
        <b>
         mi
        </b>
        : customary action in the past:
        <i>
         Oumi
        </i>
        , "He used to come a lot".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These are unstressed suffixes:
       <i>
        ta
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ma
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ne
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vo
       </i>
       , "mi"; so the stress goes upon the last syllable of the verb or the last stressed syllable.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_verbal_morphemes">

          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         :
         <span>

         </span>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Guarani_loans_in_English">
        Guarani loans in English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Guarani loans in English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       English has adopted a small number of words from Guarani (or perhaps the related
       <a href="/wiki/Tupi_language" title="Tupi language">
        Tupi
       </a>
       ) via Portuguese, mostly the names of animals. "
       <a href="/wiki/Jaguar" title="Jaguar">
        Jaguar
       </a>
       " comes from
       <i>
        jaguarete
       </i>
       and "
       <a href="/wiki/Piranha" title="Piranha">
        piranha
       </a>
       " comes from
       <i>
        pira aña
       </i>
       . Other words are: "
       <a href="/wiki/Agouti" title="Agouti">
        agouti
       </a>
       " from
       <i>
        akuti
       </i>
       , "
       <a href="/wiki/Tapir" title="Tapir">
        tapir
       </a>
       " from
       <i>
        tapira
       </i>
       , "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A%C3%A7a%C3%AD" title="Açaí">
        açaí
       </a>
       " from
       <i>
        ïwasa'i
       </i>
       , and "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warrah" title="Warrah">
        warrah
       </a>
       " from
       <i>
        aguará
       </i>
       meaning "fox".
       <a href="/wiki/Ipecacuanha" title="Ipecacuanha">
        Ipecacuanha
       </a>
       (the name of a medicinal drug) comes from a homonymous Tupi-Guaraní name that can be rendered as
       <i>
        ipe-kaa-guene
       </i>
       , meaning a creeping plant that makes one vomit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name of Paraguay is itself a Guarani word, as is the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       . However, the exact meaning of either placename is up to varied interpretations. (
       <i>
        See
       </i>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_country-name_etymologies#P" title="List of country-name etymologies">
        List of country-name etymologies
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Cougar" title="Cougar">
        Cougar
       </a>
       " is borrowed from the archaic Portuguese çuçuarana; the term was either originally derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/Tupi_language" title="Tupi language">
        Tupi language
       </a>
       <i>
        susua'rana
       </i>
       , meaning "similar to deer (in hair color)" or from the Guaraní language term
       <i>
        guaçu ara
       </i>
       while
       <i>
        puma
       </i>
       comes from the Peruvian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_language" title="Quechua language">
        Quechua language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sample_text">
        Sample text
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Sample text">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Article 1 of the
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Declaration_of_Human_Rights" title="Universal Declaration of Human Rights">
        Universal Declaration of Human Rights
       </a>
       in Guarani:
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Mayma yvypóra ou ko yvy ári iñapyty'yre ha eteĩcha tekoruvicharenda ha akatúape jeguerekópe; ha ikatu rupi oikuaa añetéva ha añete'yva, iporãva ha ivaíva, tekotevẽ pehenguéicha oiko oñondivekuéra.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       (All human beings are born free and equal in dignity and rights. They are endowed with reason and conscience and should act towards one another in a spirit of brotherhood.)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guarani_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The New Testament was translated from Greek into Guaraní by Dr John William Lindsay (1875-1946), who was a Scottish medical missionary based in Belen, Paraguay. The New Testament was printed by the British and Foreign Bible Society in 1913. It is believed to be the first New Testament translated into any South American indigenous language.
      </p>
      <p>
       A more modern translation of the whole
       <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
        Bible
       </a>
       into Guarani is known as
       <i>
        Ñandejara Ñe'ẽ
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
