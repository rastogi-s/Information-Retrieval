      <p>
       <b>
        Piedmontese
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        Piemontèis
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Lenga Piemontèisa
       </i>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Piemontese
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       spoken by over 1 million people in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmont_(Italy)" title="Piedmont (Italy)">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       , northwest
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . It is geographically and linguistically included in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       group of
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       (with
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo">
        Emiliano-Romagnolo
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       ). It is part of the wider
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
        western group of Romance languages
       </a>
       , which also includes
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Many European and North American linguists acknowledge Piedmontese as an independent language, though in Italy it is often still considered a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-consulta_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-consulta-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Today it has a certain official status recognized by the
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       regional government, but not by the national government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-consulta_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-consulta-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Piedmontese was the first language of emigrants who, in the period from 1850 to 1950, left Piedmont for countries such as
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        The United States
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Piedmontese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first documents in the Piedmontese language were written in the 12th century, the
       <i>
        sermones subalpini
       </i>
       , when it was extremely close to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       . Literary Piedmontese developed in the 17th and 18th centuries, but it did not gain literary esteem comparable to that of French or Italian, other
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       used in Piedmont. Nevertheless,
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       in Piedmontese has never ceased to be produced: it includes
       <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
        poetry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Theatre" title="Theatre">
        theatre
       </a>
       pieces,
       <a href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">
        novels
       </a>
       , and scientific work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fisica_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fisica-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">
        Current status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Piedmontese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2004, Piedmontese was recognised as Piedmont's regional language by the regional parliament,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-motion_1118_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-motion_1118-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-web1118_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-web1118-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-amble_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-amble-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although the Italian government has not yet recognised it as such. In theory it is now supposed to be taught to children in school,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arbut_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Arbut-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but this is happening only to a limited extent.
      </p>
      <p>
       The last decade has seen the publication of learning materials for schoolchildren, as well as general-public magazines. Courses for people already outside the education system have also been developed. In spite of these advances, the current state of Piedmontese is quite grave, as over the last 150 years the number of people with a written active knowledge of the language has shrunk to about 2% of native speakers, according to a recent survey.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-survey_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-survey-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the other hand, the same survey showed Piedmontese is still spoken by over half the population, alongside Italian. Authoritative sources confirm this result, putting the figure between 2 million (Assimil,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-assimil_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-assimil-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       IRES Piemonte
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IRES_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IRES-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and 3 million speakers (Ethnologue
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethno_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ethno-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) out of a population of 4.2 million people. Efforts to make it one of the official languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/2006_Winter_Olympics" title="2006 Winter Olympics">
        Turin 2006 Winter Olympics
       </a>
       were unsuccessful.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Piedmontese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1385" data-file-width="1083" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg/220px-Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg/330px-Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg/440px-Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Piemont%C3%A8is.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Piedmontese linguistic map
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some of the characteristics of the Piedmontese language are:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        The presence of
        <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
         clitic
        </a>
        so-called verbal pronouns for subjects, which give a Piedmontese verbal complex the following form: (subject) + verbal pronoun + verb, as in
        <i>
         (mi) i von
        </i>
        'I go'. Verbal pronouns are absent only in the imperative form and in the Piedmontese interrogative form.
       </li>
       <li>
        The bound form of verbal pronouns, which can be connected to dative and locative particles (
        <i>
         a-i é
        </i>
        'there is',
        <i>
         i-j diso
        </i>
        'I say to him').
       </li>
       <li>
        The interrogative form, which adds an enclitic interrogative particle at the end of the verbal form (
        <i>
         Veus-to…?
        </i>
        'Do you want to…?'])
       </li>
       <li>
        The absence of ordinal numerals higher than 'sixth', so that 'seventh' is
        <i>
         col che a fà set
        </i>
        'the one which makes seven'.
       </li>
       <li>
        The existence of three affirmative interjections (that is, three ways to say yes):
        <i>
         si, sè
        </i>
        (from Latin
        <i>
         sic est
        </i>
        , as in Italian);
        <i>
         é
        </i>
        (from Latin
        <i>
         est
        </i>
        , as in
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        );
        <i>
         òj
        </i>
        (from Latin
        <i>
         hoc est
        </i>
        , as in Occitan, or maybe
        <i>
         hoc illud
        </i>
        , as in
        <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
         Franco-Provençal
        </a>
        , French and Old
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
         Occitan
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The absence of the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         voiceless postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        (like the
        <i>
         sh
        </i>
        in English
        <i>
         sheep
        </i>
        ), for which an alveolar S sound (as in English
        <i>
         sun
        </i>
        ) is usually substituted.
       </li>
       <li>
        The existence of an S-C combination pronounced [stʃ].
       </li>
       <li>
        The existence of a
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
         velar nasal
        </a>
        [ŋ] (like the
        <i>
         ng
        </i>
        in English
        <i>
         going
        </i>
        ), which usually precedes a vowel, as in
        <i>
         lun-a
        </i>
        'moon'.
       </li>
       <li>
        The existence of the third Piedmontese vowel Ë, which is very short (close to the vowel in English
        <i>
         sir
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The absence of the phonological contrast that exists in Italian between short (single) and long (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate_consonant" title="Geminate consonant">
         double
        </a>
        ) consonants, for example, Italian
        <i>
         fata
        </i>
        'fairy' and
        <i>
         fatta
        </i>
        'done (F)'.
       </li>
       <li>
        The existence of a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosthesis_(linguistics)" title="Prosthesis (linguistics)">
         prosthetic
        </a>
        Ë sound when consonantal clusters arise that are not permitted by the phonological system. So 'seven stars' is pronounced
        <i>
         set ëstèile
        </i>
        (cf.
        <i>
         stèile
        </i>
        'stars').
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Piedmontese has a number of varieties that may vary from its basic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koine_language" title="Koine language">
        koiné
       </a>
       to quite a large extent. Variation includes not only departures from the literary grammar, but also a wide variety in dictionary entries, as different regions maintain words of
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Frankish
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">
        Lombard
       </a>
       origin, as well as differences in native Romance terminology. Words imported from various languages are also present, while more recent imports tend to come from
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       and from Italian.
      </p>
      <p>
       A variety of Piedmontese was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jud%C3%A6o-Piedmontese" title="Judæo-Piedmontese">
        Judeo-Piedmontese
       </a>
       , a dialect spoken by the Piedmontese
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       until the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_World_War" title="Second World War">
        Second World War
       </a>
       .
      </p>
