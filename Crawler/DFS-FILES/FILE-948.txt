      <p>
       <b>
        Norman
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        <span lang="roa" xml:lang="roa">
         Normaund
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         Normand
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guernésiais">
        Guernésiais
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Normand
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="Jèrriais">
        Jèrriais
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Nouormand
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_dialect" title="French dialect">
        French dialect
       </a>
       which can be classified as one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        Oïl languages
       </a>
       along with
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       . The name
       <b>
        Norman-French
       </b>
       is sometimes used to describe not only the Norman language, but also the administrative languages of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
         Anglo-Norman
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">
         Law French
        </a>
       </i>
       used in
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       . For the most part, the written forms of Norman and modern French are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_distribution">
        Geographical distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographical distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Norman is spoken in mainland
       <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">
        Normandy
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       - where it has no official status, but is classed as a
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        regional language
       </a>
       . It is taught in a few colleges near
       <a href="/wiki/Cherbourg-Octeville" title="Cherbourg-Octeville">
        Cherbourg-Octeville
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">
        Channel Islands
       </a>
       , the Norman language has developed separately, but not in isolation, to form:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="Jèrriais">
         Jèrriais
        </a>
        (in
        <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">
         Jersey
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guernésiais">
         Guernésiais
        </a>
        or Dgèrnésiais or Guernsey French (in
        <a href="/wiki/Guernsey" title="Guernsey">
         Guernsey
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sercquiais" title="Sercquiais">
         Sercquiais
        </a>
        (or Sarkese, in
        <a href="/wiki/Sark" title="Sark">
         Sark
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The British and Irish governments recognize Jèrriais and Guernésiais as
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional languages
       </a>
       within the framework of the
       <a href="/wiki/British%E2%80%93Irish_Council" title="British–Irish Council">
        British–Irish Council
       </a>
       . Sercquiais is in fact a descendant of the 16th-century Jèrriais used by the original colonists from
       <a href="/wiki/Jersey" title="Jersey">
        Jersey
       </a>
       who settled the then uninhabited island.
      </p>
      <p>
       The last
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first-language speakers
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Auregnais" title="Auregnais">
        Auregnais
       </a>
       , the dialect of Norman spoken on
       <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">
        Alderney
       </a>
       , died during the 20th century, although some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rememberer" title="Rememberer">
        rememberers
       </a>
       still exist. The dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Herm" title="Herm">
        Herm
       </a>
       also lapsed, at an unknown date.
      </p>
      <p>
       An
       <a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">
        isogloss
       </a>
       termed the "
       <a href="/wiki/Joret_line" title="Joret line">
        Joret line
       </a>
       " (
       <i>
        ligne Joret
       </i>
       ) separates the northern and southern
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of the Norman language (the line runs from
       <a href="/wiki/Granville,_Manche" title="Granville, Manche">
        Granville, Manche
       </a>
       to the French-speaking
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgian
       </a>
       border in the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Hainaut_(province)" title="Hainaut (province)">
        Hainaut
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thi%C3%A9rache" title="Thiérache">
        Thiérache
       </a>
       ). Dialectal differences also distinguish western and eastern dialects.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Three different standardized spellings are used: continental Norman, Jèrriais, and Dgèrnésiais. These represent the different developments and particular literary histories of the varieties of Norman. Norman may therefore be described as a
       <a href="/wiki/Pluricentric_language" title="Pluricentric language">
        pluricentric language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman
       </a>
       dialect of Norman served as a language of administration in
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       following the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">
        Norman conquest of England
       </a>
       in 1066. This left a legacy of
       <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">
        Law French
       </a>
       in the language of English courts (though it was also influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">
        Parisian French
       </a>
       ). In Ireland, Norman remained strongest in the area of south-east Ireland, where the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiberno-Normans" title="Hiberno-Normans">
        Hiberno-Normans
       </a>
       invaded in 1169. Norman remains in (limited) use for some very formal legal purposes in the UK, such as when the monarch gives
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_assent" title="Royal assent">
        royal assent
       </a>
       to an Act of Parliament using the phrase, "
       <a href="/wiki/La_Reyne_le_veult" title="La Reyne le veult">
        La Reyne (le Roy) le veult
       </a>
       " ("The Queen (the King) wills it").
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">
        Norman conquest of southern Italy
       </a>
       in the 11th and 12th centuries brought the language to
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       and the southern part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">
        Italian Peninsula
       </a>
       , where it has left a few traces in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian language
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        See:
        <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language#Norman_and_French_influence" title="Sicilian language">
         Norman and French influence on Sicilian
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Literature in Norman ranges from early
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_literature" title="Anglo-Norman literature">
        Anglo-Norman literature
       </a>
       through the 19th-century Norman literary renaissance to modern writers (
       <i>
        see
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Norman-language_writers" title="List of Norman-language writers">
        list of Norman-language writers
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       As of 2017
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Norman_language&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Norman language remains strongest in the less accessible areas of the former
       <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Normandy" title="Duchy of Normandy">
        Duchy of Normandy
       </a>
       : the Channel Islands and the
       <a href="/wiki/Cotentin_Peninsula" title="Cotentin Peninsula">
        Cotentin Peninsula
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Cotentinais" title="Cotentinais">
        Cotentinais
       </a>
       ) in the west, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_Caux" title="Pays de Caux">
        Pays de Caux
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Cauchois_dialect" title="Cauchois dialect">
        Cauchois dialect
       </a>
       ) in the east. Ease of access from
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       and the popularity of the coastal resorts of central Normandy, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Deauville" title="Deauville">
        Deauville
       </a>
       , in the 19th century led to a significant loss of distinctive Norman culture in the central low-lying areas of Normandy.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Norman_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">
        Old Norman
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Norsemen" title="Norsemen">
        Norse
       </a>
       invaders from modern day
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       arrived in the then-province of
       <a href="/wiki/Neustria" title="Neustria">
        Neustria
       </a>
       and settled the land that became known as Normandy, these
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       -speaking people came to live among a local
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance
       </a>
       -speaking population. In time, the communities converged, so that
       <i>
        Normandy
       </i>
       continued to form the name of the region while the original Normans became assimilated by the Gallo-Romance people, adopting their speech. Later when conquering England, the Norman rulers in England would eventually assimilate, thereby adopting the speech of the local English. However, in both cases, the
       <i>
        élites
       </i>
       contributed elements of their own language to the newly enriched languages that developed in the territories.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Normandy, the new Norman language inherited vocabulary from
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       . The influence on
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       is more disputed, although it is argued that the retention of aspirated /h/ and /k/ in Norman is due to Norse influence.
      </p>
