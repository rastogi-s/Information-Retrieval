              <td style="border:0; padding:0; vertical-align:middle;">
               <img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="215" data-file-width="327" height="13" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png/20px-Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png/30px-Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png/40px-Medici_Flag_of_Tuscany.png 2x" width="20"/>
              </td>
             </tr>
            </table>
           </td>
          </tr>
         </table>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td colspan="2">
         <b>
          Today part of
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <span class="flagicon">
          <img alt="" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1500" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Flag_of_Italy.svg/23px-Flag_of_Italy.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Flag_of_Italy.svg/35px-Flag_of_Italy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/03/Flag_of_Italy.svg/45px-Flag_of_Italy.svg.png 2x" width="23"/>
         </span>
         <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
          Italy
         </a>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Republic of Florence
       </b>
       , also known as the
       <b>
        Florentine Republic
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Repubblica Fiorentina
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <small>
        Romanian pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Romanian" title="Help:IPA/Romanian">
         [re'pubblika fjoren'tina]
        </a>
       </span>
       ), was a
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Italy#Middle_Ages" title="History of Italy">
        medieval
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Italy#Modernity" title="History of Italy">
        early modern
       </a>
       state that was centered on the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_Italy" title="List of cities in Italy">
        Italian city
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
        Florence
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brucker_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brucker-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Najemy_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Najemy-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The republic originated in 1115, when the Florentine people rebelled against the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Margraviate_of_Tuscany" title="Margraviate of Tuscany">
        Margraviate of Tuscany
       </a>
       upon the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Tuscany" title="Matilda of Tuscany">
        Matilda
       </a>
       , a woman who controlled vast territories including Florence. The Florentines formed a
       <a href="/wiki/Commune" title="Commune">
        commune
       </a>
       in her successors' place.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The republic was ruled by a council, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Signoria_of_Florence" title="Signoria of Florence">
        signoria of Florence
       </a>
       . The signoria was chosen by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gonfaloniere" title="Gonfaloniere">
         gonfaloniere
        </a>
       </i>
       (titular ruler of the city), who was elected every two months by Florentine guild members.
      </p>
      <p>
       The republic had a checkered history of coups and counter-coups against various factions. The
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">
        Medici
       </a>
       faction gained governance of the city in 1434, upon
       <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de' Medici">
        Cosimo de' Medici
       </a>
       's counter-coup against the faction that had sent him into exile the previous year. The Medici kept control of Florence until 1494. Giovanni de' Medici (later
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Leo_X" title="Pope Leo X">
        Pope Leo X
       </a>
       ) re-conquered the republic in 1512.
      </p>
      <p>
       Florence repudiated Medici authority for a second time in 1527, during the
       <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cognac" title="War of the League of Cognac">
        War of the League of Cognac
       </a>
       . The Medici re-assumed their rule in 1531, after an 11-month siege of the city. The republican government was disestablished in 1532, when
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">
        Pope Clement VII
       </a>
       appointed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alessandro_de%27_Medici" title="Alessandro de' Medici">
        Alessandro de' Medici
       </a>
       "Duke of the Florentine Republic", making the "republic" a
       <a href="/wiki/Hereditary_monarchy" title="Hereditary monarchy">
        hereditary monarchy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Florence" title="History of Florence">
        History of Florence
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="735" data-file-width="586" height="276" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg/220px-Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg/330px-Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg/440px-Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Italy_and_Illyria_1084_AD.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Italy in 1084, showing the Marquisate of Tuscany.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquisate_of_Tuscany" title="Marquisate of Tuscany">
        Marquisate of Tuscany
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The city of Florence was established in 59 B.C. by
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       . The city had been part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marquisate_of_Tuscany" title="Marquisate of Tuscany">
        Marquisate of Tuscany
       </a>
       before the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Matilda_of_Tuscany" title="Matilda of Tuscany">
        Margravine Matilda
       </a>
       in 1115. The city did not submit readily to her successor,
       <a href="/wiki/Rabodo" title="Rabodo">
        Rabodo
       </a>
       , who was killed in a dispute with the city. The first official mention of the republic was in 1138 when several cities around Tuscany formed a league against
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_X,_Duke_of_Bavaria" title="Henry X, Duke of Bavaria">
        Henry X of Bavaria
       </a>
       . The country was nominally part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
        Holy Roman Empire
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="12th_century">
        12th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 12th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Florence prospered in the 12th century, trading extensively with foreign countries. This, in turn, provided a platform for demographic growth of the city. The growth of Florence's population mirrored the rate of construction, many churches and palazzi were built. This prosperity was shattered when
       <a href="/wiki/Frederick_I,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Frederick I, Holy Roman Emperor">
        Emperor Frederick I Barbarossa
       </a>
       invaded the Italian peninsula in 1185. The Margraves of Tuscany re-acquired Florence and its townlands. The Florentines re-asserted their independence when
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Holy Roman Emperor">
        Holy Roman Emperor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_VI,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Henry VI, Holy Roman Emperor">
        Henry VI
       </a>
       died in 1197.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to a study carried out by Enrico Faini of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florence" title="University of Florence">
        University of Florence
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there were about ten old aristocratic families who moved to Florence from 1000 and 1100: Amidei; Ardinghi;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brunelleschi" title="Brunelleschi">
        Brunelleschi
       </a>
       ; Buondelmonti; Caponsacchi;
       <a href="/wiki/Donati" title="Donati">
        Donati
       </a>
       ; Fifanti;
       <a href="/wiki/Gherardini_family" title="Gherardini family">
        Gherardini of Montagliari
       </a>
       ; Guidi; Nerli; Porcelli;
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Sacchetti" title="House of Sacchetti">
        Sacchetti
       </a>
       ;Scolari; Uberti; Visdomini.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="13th_century">
        13th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 13th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Florence's population continued to grow into the 13th century, reaching 30,000 inhabitants. As has been said, the extra inhabitants supported the city's trade and vice versa. Several new bridges and churches were built, most prominently the cathedral of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_del_Fiore" title="Santa Maria del Fiore">
        Santa Maria del Fiore
       </a>
       , in 1294. The buildings from the era serve as Florence's best example of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_Architecture" title="Gothic Architecture">
        Gothic Architecture
       </a>
       . Politically, Florence was barely able to maintain peace between factions. The precarious peace that existed at the beginning of the century was destroyed in 1216 when two factions known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guelphs" title="Guelphs">
        Guelphs
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ghibellines" title="Ghibellines">
        Ghibellines
       </a>
       began to war. The Ghibellines were the noble rulers of Florence. The Guelphs were populists.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Ghibellines, who under
       <a href="/wiki/Frederick_of_Antioch" title="Frederick of Antioch">
        Frederick of Antioch
       </a>
       had ruled the city since 1244, were deposed in 1250 by the Guelphs. The Guelphs led Florence to prosper further. Their primarily mercantile orientation soon became evident in one of their earliest achievements: the introduction of a new coin, the
       <a href="/wiki/Florin_(Italian_coin)" title="Florin (Italian coin)">
        Florin
       </a>
       , in 1252. It was widely used beyond Florence's borders due to its reliable, fixed gold content and soon became one of the common currencies of Europe and the Near East. The same year saw the creation of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palazzo_Comunale_(San_Gimignano)" title="Palazzo Comunale (San Gimignano)">
        Palazzo del Popolo
       </a>
       . The Guelphs lost the reins of power after Florence suffered a catastrophic defeat at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Montaperti" title="Battle of Montaperti">
        Battle of Montaperti
       </a>
       in 1260. The Ghibellines resumed power and undid all the advances of the Guelphs. They demolished hundreds of towers, homes, and palaces. The fragility of their rule caused the Ghibellines to seek out an arbitrator in the form of Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_IV" title="Clement IV">
        Clement IV
       </a>
       . Fortunately for Florence, the Pope openly favoured the Guelphs. They were duly restored to power.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Florentine economy reached its zenith in the latter half of the 13th century. The famed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palazzo_della_Signoria" title="Palazzo della Signoria">
        Palazzo della Signoria
       </a>
       was built, designed by
       <a href="/wiki/Arnolfo_di_Cambio" title="Arnolfo di Cambio">
        Arnolfo di Cambio
       </a>
       . The Florentine townlands were divided into administrative districts in 1292. In 1293 the Ordinances of Justice were enacted which effectively became the constitution of the republic of Florence throughout the Italian Renaissance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The city's numerous luxurious
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palazzi" title="Palazzi">
        palazzi
       </a>
       were becoming surrounded by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Townhouses" title="Townhouses">
        townhouses
       </a>
       , built by the ever prospering merchant class.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1298, one of the leading banking families of Europe, the Bonsignoris, were bankrupted and so the city of Siena lost her status as the banking center of Europe to Florence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strathern,_p_18_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Strathern,_p_18-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="14th_century">
        14th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 14th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1304, the war between the Ghibellines and the Guelphs led to a great fire which destroyed much of the city. Napier gives the following account:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         Battles first began between the Cerchi and Giugni at their houses in the Via del Garbo; they fought day and night, and with the aid of the Cavalcanti and Antellesi the former subdued all that quarter: a thousand rural adherents strengthened their bands, and that day might have seen the Neri's destruction if an unforseen disaster had not turned the scale. A certain dissolute priest, called Neri Abati, prior of San Piero Scheraggio, false to his family and in concert with the Black chiefs, consented to set fire to the dwellings of his own kinsmen in Orto-san-Michele; the flames, assisted by faction, spread rapidly over the richest and most crowded part of Florence: shops, warehouses, towers, private dwellings and palaces, from the old to the new market-place, from Vacchereccia to Porta Santa Maria and the Ponte Vecchio, all was one broad sheet of fire: more than nineteen hundred houses were consumed; plunder and devastation revelled unchecked amongst the flames, whole races were reduced in one moment to beggary, and vast magazines of the richest merchandise were destroyed. The Cavalcanti, one of the most opulent families in Florence, beheld their whole property consumed, and lost all courage; they made no attempt to save it, and, after almost gaining possession of the city, were finally overcome by the opposite faction.
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td class="cquotecite" colspan="3" style="border: none; padding-right: 4%; font-size: smaller; text-align: right;">
         <cite>
          —
          <a href="/wiki/Henry_Edward_Napier" title="Henry Edward Napier">
           Henry Edward Napier
          </a>
          ,
          <i>
           Florentine History
          </i>
          , I. 394
         </cite>
        </td>
       </tr>

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Florin_(Italian_coin)" title="Florin (Italian coin)">
        golden florin
       </a>
       of the Republic of Florence was the first
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        European
       </a>
       gold coin struck in sufficient quantities to play a significant commercial role since the 7th century. As many Florentine banks were international companies with branches across Europe, the florin quickly became the dominant trade coin of Western Europe for large scale transactions, replacing silver bars in multiples of the mark (a
       <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">
        weight unit
       </a>
       equal to eight ounces).
      </p>
      <p>
       In fact, with the collapse of the Bonsignori family, several new banking families sprang up in Florence: the
       <a href="/wiki/Bardi_family" title="Bardi family">
        Bardis
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peruzzi_family" title="Peruzzi family">
        Peruzzis
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acciaioli" title="Acciaioli">
        Acciaioli
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strathern,_p_18_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Strathern,_p_18-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The friction between the Guelphs and the Ghibellines did not cease, authority still passed between the two frequently. Florence's reign as the foremost banking city of Europe did not last long; the aforesaid families were bankrupt in 1340, not because of
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">
        Edward III of England
       </a>
       's refusal to pay his debts, as is often stated (the debt was just £13,000) but because of a Europe-wide economic recession. While the banks perished, Florentine literature flourished, and Florence was home to some of the greatest writers in Italian history:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dante" title="Dante">
        Dante
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
        Petrarch
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boccaccio" title="Boccaccio">
        Boccaccio
       </a>
       . They were Europe's first vernacular writers, choosing the
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan dialect
       </a>
       of Italian (which, as a result, evolved into the standard
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       ) over
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Florence was hit hard by the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Death" title="Black Death">
        Black Death
       </a>
       . Having originated in the Orient, the plague arrived in
       <a href="/wiki/Messina" title="Messina">
        Messina
       </a>
       in 1347. The plague devastated Europe, robbing it of an estimated 1/3 of its population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This, combined with the economic downturn, took its toll on the city-state. The ensuing collapse of the
       <a href="/wiki/Feudalism" title="Feudalism">
        feudal system
       </a>
       changed the social composition of Europe forever; it was one of the first steps out of the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3038" data-file-width="2600" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png/250px-Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png/375px-Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png/500px-Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Growth_of_the_Florentine_State.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The growth of Florence from 1300 to 1500
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Eight_Saints" title="War of the Eight Saints">
        war
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avignon_papacy" title="Avignon papacy">
        Avignon papacy
       </a>
       strained the regime. In 1378 discontented wool workers revolted. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciompi_revolt" title="Ciompi revolt">
        Ciompi revolt
       </a>
       , as it is known, established a revolutionary commune. In 1382 the wealthier classes crushed the seeds of rebellion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The famous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">
        Medici
       </a>
       bank was established by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_di_Bicci_de%27_Medici" title="Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici">
        Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici
       </a>
       in October 1397.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The bank continued to exist (albeit in an extremely diminished form) until the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinando_II_de%27_Medici,_Grand_Duke_of_Tuscany" title="Ferdinando II de' Medici, Grand Duke of Tuscany">
        Ferdinando II de'Medici
       </a>
       in the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But, for now, Giovanni's bank flourished.
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in 1389,
       <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">
        Gian Galeazzo Visconti
       </a>
       of Milan expanded his dominion into the Veneto, Piedmont, Emily and Tuscany. During this period Florence, under the leadership of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maso_degli_Albizzi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maso degli Albizzi (page does not exist)">
        Maso degli Albizzi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_da_Uzzano" title="Niccolò da Uzzano">
        Niccolò da Uzzano
       </a>
       was involved in three wars with Milan (1390–92, 1397–98, 1400–02). The Florentine army, commanded by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hawkwood" title="John Hawkwood">
        John Hawkwood
       </a>
       , contained the Milanese during the first war.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruk252_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bruk252-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The second war started in March 1397. Milanese troops devastated the Florentine
       <i>
        contado
       </i>
       , but were checked in August of that year. The war expenses exceeded one million florins and necessitated tax raises and forced loans. A peace agreement in May 1398 was brokered by Venice, but left the struggle unresolved.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-najemi193_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-najemi193-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Over the next two years Florentine control of Tuscany and Umbria collapsed.
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Siena" title="Siena">
        Siena
       </a>
       as well as a number of smaller cities submitted to Gian Galeazzo, while
       <a href="/wiki/Lucca" title="Lucca">
        Lucca
       </a>
       withdrew from the anti-Visconti league, with
       <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">
        Bologna
       </a>
       remaining the only major ally.
      </p>
      <p>
       In November 1400 a conspiracy involving both exiles and internal opponents was uncovered. Two
       <a href="/wiki/Ricci" title="Ricci">
        Ricci
       </a>
       were implicated as leaders of a plot to eliminate the regime’s inner circle and open the gates to the Milanese. Confessions indicated that the plan had wide support among the elites, including a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">
        Medici
       </a>
       and several of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alberti_(family)" title="Alberti (family)">
        Alberti
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-najemi193_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-najemi193-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The republic bankrolled the emperor-elect
       <a href="/wiki/Rupert,_King_of_Germany" title="Rupert, King of Germany">
        Rupert
       </a>
       . However, he was defeated by the Milanese in the fall of 1401. Visconti then turned to Bologna. On June 26, 1402, combined Bolognese-Florentine forces were routed at
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Casalecchio" title="Battle of Casalecchio">
        Casalecchio
       </a>
       , near Bologna, which was taken on the 30th. The road to Tuscany was open. However, Florence was saved after an outbreak of
       <a href="/wiki/Plague_(disease)" title="Plague (disease)">
        plague
       </a>
       had spread from Tuscany to Emilia and Lombardy:
       <a href="/wiki/Gian_Galeazzo_Visconti" title="Gian Galeazzo Visconti">
        Gian Galeazzo
       </a>
       died from it on 3 September 1402.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruk252_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bruk252-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-najemi193_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-najemi193-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="15th_century">
        15th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 15th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Visconti domains were divided between three heirs.
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gabriele_Maria_Visconti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gabriele Maria Visconti (page does not exist)">
        Gabriele Maria Visconti
       </a>
       sold
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       to the Republic of Florence for 200,000
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Florins" title="Florins">
        florins
       </a>
       . Since the Pisans did not intend to voluntarily submit to their long-time rivals, the army under
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maso_degli_Albizzi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maso degli Albizzi (page does not exist)">
        Maso degli Albizzi
       </a>
       took Pisa on 9 October 1406 after a long siege, that was accompanied by numerous atrocities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bruk252_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-bruk252-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The state authorities had been approached by the
       <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">
        Duchy of Milan
       </a>
       in 1422, with a treaty, that prohibited Florence's interference with Milan's impending war with the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">
        Republic of Genoa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Florence obliged, but Milan disregarded its own treaty and occupied a Florentine border town. The conservative government wanted war, while the people bemoaned such a stance as they would be subject to enormous tax increases. The republic went to war with Milan, and won, upon the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Republic of Venice
       </a>
       's entry on their side. The war was concluded in 1427, and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Visconti" title="House of Visconti">
        Visconti
       </a>
       of Milan were forced to sign an unfavourable treaty.
      </p>
      <p>
       The debt incurred during the war was gargantuan, approximately 4,200,000 florins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To pay, the state had to change the tax system. The current
       <i>
        estimo
       </i>
       system was replaced with the
       <a href="/wiki/Catasto" title="Catasto">
        catasto
       </a>
       . The
       <i>
        castato
       </i>
       was based on a citizen's entire wealth, while the
       <i>
        estimo
       </i>
       was simply a form of income tax. Apart from war,
       <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Brunelleschi" title="Filippo Brunelleschi">
        Filippo Brunelleschi
       </a>
       created the renowned dome of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Maria_del_Fiore" title="Santa Maria del Fiore">
        Santa Maria del Fiore
       </a>
       , which astounded contemporaries and modern observers alike.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Medicis">
        The Medicis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: The Medicis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medici" title="Medici">
        Medici
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The son of Giovanni di Bicci de' Medici, Cosimo de' Medici succeeded his father as the head of the Medici Bank. He played a prominent role in the government of Florence until his exile in 1433, after a disastrous war with Tuscany's neighbour, the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Lucca" title="Republic of Lucca">
        Republic of Lucca
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cosimo's exile in Venice lasted for less than a year, when the people of Florence overturned Cosimo's exile in a democratic vote. Cosimo returned to the acclaim of his people and the banishment of the Albizzi family, who had exiled him.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1056" data-file-width="750" height="310" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg/220px-Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg/330px-Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg/440px-Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosimodemedicitheolder.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cosimo de' Medici, founder of the
         <a href="/wiki/House_of_Medici" title="House of Medici">
          House of Medici
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Cosimo de' Medici">
        Cosimo de' Medici
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Renaissance began during Cosimo's
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       rule of Florence, the seeds of which had arguably been laid before the Black Death tore through Europe.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Niccoli" title="Niccolò Niccoli">
        Niccolò Niccoli
       </a>
       was the leading Florence humanist scholar of the time. He appointed the first Professor of
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Chrysoloras" title="Manuel Chrysoloras">
        Manuel Chrysoloras
       </a>
       (the founder of Hellenic studies in Italy), at the University of Florence in 1397.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Niccoli was a keen collector of ancient manuscripts, which he bequeathed to Cosimo upon his death in 1437.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Poggio_Bracciolini" title="Poggio Bracciolini">
        Poggio Bracciolini
       </a>
       succeeded Niccoli as the principal humanist of Florence. Bracciolini was born
       <a href="/wiki/Arezzo" title="Arezzo">
        Arezzo
       </a>
       in 1380. He toured Europe, searching for more ancient Greco-Roman manuscripts for Niccoli. Unlike his employer, Bracciolini also authored his own works. He was made the Chancellor of Florence shortly before his death, by Cosimo, who was his best friend.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Florence hosted the Great Ecumenical Council in 1439; this council was launched in an attempt to reconcile the Byzantine
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthodox_Church" title="Orthodox Church">
        Orthodox Church
       </a>
       with Roman
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholicism" title="Catholicism">
        Catholicism
       </a>
       . Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugenius_IV" title="Eugenius IV">
        Eugenius IV
       </a>
       convened it in reply to a cry for assistance from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_of_the_Eastern_Roman_Empire" title="Emperor of the Eastern Roman Empire">
        Emperor of the Eastern Roman Empire
       </a>
       (also known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Empire
       </a>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/John_VIII_Palaiologos" title="John VIII Palaiologos">
        John VIII Palaiologos
       </a>
       . John VIII's empire was slowly being devoured by the Ottoman Turks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The council was a huge boost to Florence's international prestige. The council deliberated until July 1439. Both parties had reached a compromise, and the Pope agreed to militarily aid the Byzantine Emperor. Unfortunately, upon John VIII's homecoming to Constantinople, the Greeks rejected the compromise, leading to riots throughout what remained of the Byzantine Empire. John VIII was forced to repudiate the agreement with the Roman church to appease the rioters. As a result, no Western aid was forthcoming and the Byzantine Empire's fate was sealed. Fourteen years later in 1453, Constantinople fell to the Ottomans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cosimo's fervent patronage transformed Florence into the epitome of a Renaissance city. He employed
       <a href="/wiki/Donatello" title="Donatello">
        Donatello
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brunelleschi" title="Brunelleschi">
        Brunelleschi
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Michelozzo" title="Michelozzo">
        Michelozzo
       </a>
       . All these artistic commissions cost Cosimo over 600,000 florins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Foreign relations, both as a backdrop to Cosimo's rise to power and during first twenty years of his rule, were dominated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Wars_in_Lombardy" title="Wars in Lombardy">
        Wars in Lombardy
       </a>
       . This series of conflicts between the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Republic of Venice
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Milan" title="Duchy of Milan">
        Duchy of Milan
       </a>
       for hegemony in Northern Italy lasted from 1423 to 1454 and involved a number of Italian states, that occasionally switched sides according to their changing interests.
       <a href="/wiki/Filippo_Maria_Visconti" title="Filippo Maria Visconti">
        Filippo Maria Visconti
       </a>
       of Milan invaded Florence twice in the 1430s, and again in 1440, but his army was finally defeated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Anghiari" title="Battle of Anghiari">
        battle of Anghiari
       </a>
       . The Milanese invasions were largely instigated by the exiled
       <a href="/wiki/Albizzi" title="Albizzi">
        Albizzi
       </a>
       family.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Death of Filippo Maria in 1447 led to a major change in the alliances. In 1450 Cosimo's current ally
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Sforza" title="Francesco Sforza">
        Francesco Sforza
       </a>
       established himself as the Duke of
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       . Florentine trade interests made her support Sforza's Milan in the war against Venice, while the fall of Constantinople in 1453 dealt a blow to Venetian finances. Eventually, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peace_of_Lodi" title="Peace of Lodi">
        Peace of Lodi
       </a>
       recognized Venetian and Florentine territorial gains and the legitimacy of the Sforza rule in Milan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brucker,_p._253_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brucker,_p._253-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Milan-Florence alliance played a major role in stabilizing the peninsula for the next 40 years.
      </p>
      <p>
       The political crisis of 1458 was the first serious challenge to the Medici rule. The cost of wars had been borne by the great families of Florence, and disproportionately so by Medici's opponents. A number of them (Serragli, Baroncelli, Mancini, Vespucci, Gianni) were practically ruined and had to sell their properties, and those were acquired by Medici's partisans at bargain prices. The opposition used partial relaxation of Medici control of the republic institutions
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to demand political reforms, freedom of speech in the councils and a greater share in the decision-making. Medici's party response was to use threats of force from private armies and Milanese troops and arranging a popular assembly dominated by Cosimo's supporters. It exiled the opponents of the regime and introduced the open vote in councils, ``in order to unmask the anti-Medician rebels".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brucker,_p._253_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brucker,_p._253-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1458 Cosimo withdrew from any official public role, but his control of Florence was greater than ever. In the spring of 1459 he entertained the new pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pius_II" title="Pius II">
        Pius II
       </a>
       , who stopped in Florence on his way to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Council_of_Mantua" title="Council of Mantua">
        Council of Mantua
       </a>
       to declare a crusade against the Ottomans, and
       <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza" title="Galeazzo Maria Sforza">
        Galeazzo Maria Sforza
       </a>
       , Francesco's son, who was to escort the pope from Florence to
       <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">
        Mantua
       </a>
       . In his memoirs, Pius said that Cosimo “was considered the arbiter of war and peace, the regulator of law; less a citizen than master of his city. Political councils were held in his home; the magistrates he chose were elected; he was king in all but name and legal status. . . . Some asserted that his tyranny was intolerable."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Piero_di_Cosimo_de%27_Medici" title="Piero di Cosimo de' Medici">
        Piero di Cosimo de' Medici
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Italy_1494_AD.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1805" data-file-width="1261" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/220px-Italy_1494_AD.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/330px-Italy_1494_AD.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Italy_1494_AD.png/440px-Italy_1494_AD.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Italy_1494_AD.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of the Florentine Republic and surrounding states c. 1494.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piero_the_Gouty" title="Piero the Gouty">
        Piero the Gouty
       </a>
       was the eldest son of Cosimo. Piero, as his sobriquet
       <i>
        the gouty
       </i>
       implies, suffered from
       <a href="/wiki/Gout" title="Gout">
        gout
       </a>
       and did not enjoy good health.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_the_Magnificent" title="Lorenzo the Magnificent">
        Lorenzo the Magnificent
       </a>
       was Piero's eldest son by his wife
       <a href="/wiki/Lucrezia_Tornabuoni" title="Lucrezia Tornabuoni">
        Lucrezia Tornabuoni
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Piero's reign furthered the always fractious political divisions of Florence when he had called up huge debts owed to the Medici Bank. These debts were owed primarily by a Florentine nobleman,
       <a href="/wiki/Luca_Pitti" title="Luca Pitti">
        Luca Pitti
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lucca called for an armed insurrection against Piero, but a co-conspirator rebutted this.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Duke Francesco Sforza of Milan died in 1466, and his son
       <a href="/wiki/Galeazzo_Maria_Sforza" title="Galeazzo Maria Sforza">
        Galeazzo Maria Sforza
       </a>
       became the new Milanese duke. With the death of Francesco Sforza, Florence lost a valuable ally among the other Italian states.
      </p>
      <p>
       In August 1466, the conspirators acted. They received support from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duke_of_Ferrara" title="Duke of Ferrara">
        Duke of Ferrara
       </a>
       , who marched troops into the Florentine countryside with the intent of deposing Piero. The coup failed. The Florentines were not willing to support it, and soon after their arrival, Ferrara's troops left the city.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The conspirators were exiled for life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While the internal problems were fixed, Venice took the opportunity to invade Florentine territory in 1467. Piero appointed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Federigo_da_Montefeltro" title="Federigo da Montefeltro">
        Federigo da Montefeltro
       </a>
       , Lord of
       <a href="/wiki/Urbino" title="Urbino">
        Urbino
       </a>
       , to command his mercenaries. An inconclusive battle ensued, with the Venetians forces retreating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the winter of 1469 Piero died.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1207" data-file-width="940" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg/220px-Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg/330px-Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg/440px-Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lorenzo_de%27_Medici-ritratto.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lorenzo de' Medici
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_de%27_Medici" title="Lorenzo de' Medici">
        Lorenzo de' Medici
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lorenzo succeeded his father, Piero. Lorenzo, as heir, was accordingly groomed by his father to rule over Florence. Lorenzo was the greatest artistic patron of the Renaissance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He patronised
       <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">
        Leonardo da Vinci
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Michelangelo" title="Michelangelo">
        Michelangelo
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Botticelli" title="Botticelli">
        Botticelli
       </a>
       , among others. During Lorenzo's reign, the Renaissance truly descended on Florence. Lorenzo commissioned a multitude of amazing pieces of art and also enjoyed collecting fine gems. Lorenzo had many children with his wife
       <a href="/wiki/Clarice_Orsini" title="Clarice Orsini">
        Clarice Orsini
       </a>
       , including the future Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">
        Leo X
       </a>
       and his eventual successor in Florence,
       <a href="/wiki/Piero_the_Unfortunate" title="Piero the Unfortunate">
        Piero the Unfortunate
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lorenzo's brother
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giuliano_de%27_Medici_(1453-1478)" title="Giuliano de' Medici (1453-1478)">
        Giuliano
       </a>
       was killed before his own eyes in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pazzi_Conspiracy" title="Pazzi Conspiracy">
        Pazzi Conspiracy
       </a>
       of 1478. This plot was instigated by the Pazzi family. The coup was unsuccessful, and the conspirators were executed in a very violent manner. The scheme was supported by the Archbishop of Pisa,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francesco_Salviati_(archbishop)" title="Francesco Salviati (archbishop)">
        Francesco Salviati
       </a>
       , who was also executed in his ceremonial robes. News of this sacrilege reached Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sixtus_IV" title="Sixtus IV">
        Sixtus IV
       </a>
       (who had also supported the conspiracy against the Medicis). Sixtus IV was "outraged" and excommunicated everyone in Florence. Sixtus sent a papal delegation to Florence to arrest Lorenzo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The people of Florence were obviously enraged by the Pope's actions, and the local clergy too. The populace refused to resign Lorenzo to the papal delegation. A war followed, which lasted for two years until Lorenzo tactfully went about diplomatically securing a peace.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lorenzo died in 1492 and was succeeded by his son Piero.
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Piero_the_Unfortunate" title="Piero the Unfortunate">
        Piero the Unfortunate
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Piero ruled Florence for a mere two years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">
        Charles VIII of France
       </a>
       invaded Italy in September 1494. He demanded passage through Florence to Naples, where he intended to secure the throne for himself. Piero met Charles at the fringes of Florence to try and negotiate. Piero capitulated to all Charles' demands, and upon arriving back in the city in November, he was branded as a traitor. He was forced to flee the republic with his family.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Savonarola">
        Savonarola
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Savonarola">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_(1497).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="811" height="326" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg/220px-Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg/330px-Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg/440px-Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_%281497%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Girolamo_Savonarola_by_Fra_Bartolommeo_(1497).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Girolamo Savonarola
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">
        Girolamo Savonarola
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       After the fall of the Medici,
       <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Savonarola" title="Girolamo Savonarola">
        Girolamo Savonarola
       </a>
       ruled the state.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Savonarola was a priest from Ferrara. He came to Florence in the 1480s. By proclaiming predictions and through vigorous preaching, he won the people to his cause. Savonarola's new government ushered in democratic reforms. It allowed many exiles back into Florence, who were banished by the Medici. Savonarola's ulterior goal, however, was to transform Florence into a "city of god".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Florentines stopped wearing garish colours, and many women took oaths to become nuns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Savonarola became most famous for his "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonfire_of_the_Vanities" title="Bonfire of the Vanities">
        Bonfire of the Vanities
       </a>
       ", where he ordered all "vanities" to be gathered and burned. These included wigs, perfume, paintings, and ancient manuscripts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Savonarola's Florence collapsed a year later. He was excommunicated by Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_VI" title="Alexander VI">
        Alexander VI
       </a>
       in late 1497. In the same year, Florence embarked on a war with
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       , which had been
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
         de facto
        </a>
       </i>
       independent since
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_VIII_of_France" title="Charles VIII of France">
        Charles VIII
       </a>
       's invasion. The endeavour failed miserably, and this led to food shortages. That, in turn, led to a few isolated cases of the plague. The people blamed him for their woes, and he was tortured and executed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Piazza_della_Signoria" title="Piazza della Signoria">
        Piazza della Signoria
       </a>
       by being burned at the stake by Florentine authorities, in May 1498.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">
        16th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 16th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The city was in tatters by the time Savonarola was deposed. The state was now presided over by
       <a href="/wiki/Piero_Soderini" title="Piero Soderini">
        Piero Soderini
       </a>
       , who was elected ruler for life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This period saw a democracy in Florence, which had very little corruption. The republican government succeeded where Savonarola failed, when the Secretary of War,
       <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Machiavelli" title="Niccolò Machiavelli">
        Niccolò Machiavelli
       </a>
       , captured Pisa. It was at this time that Machiavelli introduced a standing army in Florence, replacing the traditional use of hired mercenaries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Soderini was repudiated in September 1512, when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_X" title="Leo X">
        Cardinal Giovanni de Medici
       </a>
       captured Florence with Papal troops during the
       <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cambrai" title="War of the League of Cambrai">
        War of the League of Cambrai
       </a>
       . The Medici rule of Florence was thus restored.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Emperor_charles_v.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1823" data-file-width="1794" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Emperor_charles_v.png/220px-Emperor_charles_v.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Emperor_charles_v.png/330px-Emperor_charles_v.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Emperor_charles_v.png/440px-Emperor_charles_v.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Emperor_charles_v.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Holy Roman Emperor Charles V
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Soon after arriving back in Florence, Cardinal Giovanni de Medici was called to Rome. Pope
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Julius_II" title="Julius II">
        Julius II
       </a>
       had just died, and he needed to be present for the ensuing
       <a href="/wiki/Papal_conclave" title="Papal conclave">
        Papal conclave
       </a>
       . Giovanni was elected Pope, taking the name Leo X. This effectively brought the
       <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">
        Papal States
       </a>
       and Florence into a personal union.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Leo X ruled Florence by proxy, appointing his brother
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giuliano_de%27_Medici_(1479-1516)" title="Giuliano de' Medici (1479-1516)">
        Giuliano de Medici
       </a>
       , to rule in his place.
      </p>
      <p>
       Giuliano ruled Florence until his death in 1516, and was succeeded by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorenzo_II_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Urbino" title="Lorenzo II de' Medici, Duke of Urbino">
        Lorenzo, Duke of Urbino
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He fathered
       <a href="/wiki/Catherine_de%27_Medici" title="Catherine de' Medici">
        Catherine de' Medici
       </a>
       . Lorenzo died from syphilis in 1519, just after the birth of his only child. The Medici channelled all their energy on the Papacy, which Leo X held from 1513–1521. Upon Leo's death, the Papacy passed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adrian_VI" title="Adrian VI">
        Adrian VI
       </a>
       , who ruled until 1523. Then
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clement_VII" title="Clement VII">
        Cardinal Giulio de' Medici
       </a>
       was elected Pope Clement VII.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Florence at the time was being ruled by
       <a href="/wiki/Ippolito_de%27_Medici" title="Ippolito de' Medici">
        Ippolito de' Medici
       </a>
       and Alessandro de' Medici, under the guardianship of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_Passerini" title="Cardinal Passerini">
        Cardinal Passerini
       </a>
       . Ippolito was the son of Giuliano de Medici, while Alessandro was the alleged son of Clement VII.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2552" data-file-width="1928" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/220px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/330px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg/440px-Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Raffaello_Sanzio_-_Ritratto_di_Leone_X_coi_cardinali_Giulio_de%27_Medici_e_Luigi_de%27_Rossi_-_Google_Art_Project.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Leo X and Cardinal Giulio de Medici
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In May 1527, Rome was laid siege by the Holy Roman Empire, during in the
       <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_League_of_Cognac" title="War of the League of Cognac">
        War of the League of Cognac
       </a>
       . The city was pillaged and destroyed. The Medici were once again deposed in Florence, by the anti-Medici faction, upon learning of the Papal States' defeat. A new wave of Puritanism swept over Florence.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">
        Jesus Christ
       </a>
       was appointed "King of Florence". Many new restricting fundamentalist laws were passed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Clement VII signed the
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Barcelona" title="Treaty of Barcelona">
        Treaty of Barcelona
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">
        Charles V
       </a>
       . Charles would, in exchange for the Pope's blessing, invade Florence and restore the Medici. They were restored after a protracted siege.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Destruction">
        Destruction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Destruction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Florence" title="Duchy of Florence">
        Duchy of Florence
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1533,
       <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_de%27_Medici,_Duke_of_Florence" title="Alessandro de' Medici, Duke of Florence">
        Alessandro de'Medici
       </a>
       was created
       <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Florence" title="Duchy of Florence">
        Duke of Florence
       </a>
       by his uncle
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Clement_VII" title="Pope Clement VII">
        Pope Clement VII
       </a>
       . This single act brought an end to the republic. The population were infuriated at this. There was some civil insurrection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Medici were ennobled further in 1569, when Alessandro's successor was made
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_rulers_of_Tuscany" title="List of rulers of Tuscany">
        Grand Duke of Tuscany
       </a>
       . The Medici ruled as
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Duchy_of_Tuscany" title="Grand Duchy of Tuscany">
        grand dukes
       </a>
       until their extinction in 1737.
       <span id="Government">
       </span>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Administration">
        Administration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Republic_of_Florence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Administration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Florence was governed by a council called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Signoria" title="Signoria">
         signoria
        </a>
       </i>
       , which consisted of nine men. The head of the signoria was the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gonfaloniere" title="Gonfaloniere">
         gonfaloniere
        </a>
       </i>
       , who was chosen every two months in a lottery, as was his
       <i>
        signoria
       </i>
       . To be eligible, one had to have sound finances, no arrears or bankruptcies, he had to be older than thirty, had to be a member of Florence's seven main guilds (merchant traders, bankers, two clothe guilds, and judges). The lottery was often pre-determined, and the results were usually favourable to influential families.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The roster of names in the lottery were replaced every five years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The main organs of government were known as the
       <i>
        tre maggiori
       </i>
       . They were: the twelve good men, the standard bearers of the
       <i>
        gonfaloniere
       </i>
       , and the
       <i>
        signoria
       </i>
       . The first two debated and ratified proposed legislation, but could not introduce it. The
       <i>
        gonfaloniere'
       </i>
       s initial two month-term in office was expanded upon the fall of Savonarola in 1498, to life, much like that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Venetian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Doge_of_Venice" title="Doge of Venice">
        <i>
         doge
        </i>
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        signoria
       </i>
       held meetings each day in the
       <i>
        Palazzo della Signoria.
       </i>
       Various committees controlled particular aspects of government, e.g. the Committee of War. For administrative purposes, Florence was divided into four districts, which were divided into four sub-districts. The main purpose of these counties was to ease the gathering of local militias.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       To hold an elective office, one had to be of a family that had previously held office.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_42-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Medici family effectively ruled Florence on a hereditary basis, from 1434–1494, 1512–1527, 1531, until 1533, when Alessandro de Medici was created Duke of Florence, thereby turning Florence into a hereditary monarchy.
      </p>
