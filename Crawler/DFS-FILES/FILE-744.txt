      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish object pronouns
       </b>
       are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_personal_pronoun" title="Spanish personal pronoun">
        Spanish personal pronouns
       </a>
       that take the function of an
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       in a sentence. They may be analyzed as
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitics
       </a>
       which cannot function independently, but take the conjugated form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_verbs" title="Spanish verbs">
        Spanish verb
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Object pronouns are generally
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       , i.e. they appear before the verb of which they are the object.
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Enclitic" title="Clitic">
        Enclitic
       </a>
       pronouns (i.e. pronouns attached to the end of the verb or similar word itself) may appear with positive
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperatives
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitives
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerunds
       </a>
       . In all compound infinitives that make use of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       , enclitics attach to the uninflected auxiliary verb and not the past participle(s) itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Spanish, two or three clitic pronouns can be used with a single verb, generally one accusative and one dative. They follow a specific order based on person and number. When an accusative third-person non-reflexive pronoun (
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        las
       </i>
       ) is used with a dative pronoun that is understood to also be third-person non-reflexive. Simple non-emphatic
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       is most often found with dative clitics, although it is occasionally found with accusative clitics as well. In
       <a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">
        Castilian Spanish
       </a>
       , and especially that of Spain's capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , there exists the practice of
       <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
        leísmo
       </a>
       ; which is, using the indirect object pronoun
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       for the object pronoun where
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       would use
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (masculine) or
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       (feminine) for the object pronoun.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Spanish_language" title="History of the Spanish language">
        history of the Spanish language
       </a>
       saw the shedding of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        Latin declensions
       </a>
       , only the subject and prepositional object survived as independent
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_pronouns" title="Personal pronouns">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       in Spanish: the rest became
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitics
       </a>
       . These clitics may
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proclitic" title="Proclitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enclitic" title="Enclitic">
        enclitic
       </a>
       , or doubled for emphasis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In modern Spanish, the placement of clitic pronouns is determined
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphologically
       </a>
       ; by the form of the verb. They precede a conjugated verb but follow the infinitive or positive imperative form. For example:
       <i>
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        vio
       </i>
       but
       <i>
        ver
        <b>
         me
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        viéndo
        <b>
         me
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ¡véa
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        !
       </i>
       Exceptions exist for certain
       <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">
        idiomatic
       </a>
       expressions, like "once upon a time" (
       <i>
        Érase una vez
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Old Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_in_Spanish" title="Stress in Spanish">
        Stress in Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Unstressed pronouns in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       had different rules from those for object pronouns in modern Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The old rules were more determined by
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       than by morphology:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the pronoun followed the verb, except when the verb was preceded (in the same clause) by a stressed word, such as a noun, adverb, or stressed pronoun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cantar_de_Mio_Cid" title="Cantar de Mio Cid">
         Cantar de Mio Cid
        </a>
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         e tornó
         <b>
          s
         </b>
         pora su casa, ascónden
         <b>
          se
         </b>
         de mio Cid
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         non lo desafié, aquel que gela diesse
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If the first stressed word of a clause was in the future or condtional tense, or if it was a compound verb made up of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       + a participle, then any unstressed pronoun was placed between the two elements of the compound verb.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         daregelo he
        </i>
        (modern:
        <i>
         se lo daré
        </i>
        ) = "I'll give it to him".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         daregelo ia/ie
        </i>
        (modern:
        <i>
         se lo daría
        </i>
        ) = "I would give it."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         dado gelo ha
        </i>
        (modern:
        <i>
         se lo ha dado
        </i>
        ) = "He has given it."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penny_2-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-penny-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Before the 15th century, clitics never appeared in the initial position; not even after a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinating_conjunction" title="Coordinating conjunction">
        coordinating conjunction
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Caesura" title="Caesura">
        caesura
       </a>
       . They could, however, precede a conjugated verb if there was a negative or adverbial marker. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Fuese el conde
        </i>
        = "The count left", but
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         El conde se fue
        </i>
        = "The count left"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No se fue el conde
        </i>
        = "The count did not leave"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Entonces se fue el conde
        </i>
        = "Then the count left".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The same rule applied to gerunds, infinitives, and imperatives. The forms of the future and the conditional functioned like any other verb conjugated with respect to the clitics. But a clitic following a future or conditional was usually placed between the infinitive root and the inflection. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Verme ha mañana
        </i>
        = "See me tomorrow", but
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No me verá mañana
        </i>
        = "He will not see me tomorrow"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mañana me verá
        </i>
        = "He will see me tomorrow"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pountain_1-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-pountain-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_Modern_Spanish">
        Early Modern Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Early Modern Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       By the 15th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_Spanish" title="Early Modern Spanish">
        Early Modern Spanish
       </a>
       had developed "proclisis", in which an object's agreement markers come before the verb. According to Andrés Enrique-Arias, this shift helped speed up
       <a href="/wiki/Language_processing_in_the_brain" title="Language processing in the brain">
        language processing
       </a>
       of complex morphological material in the verb's
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       (including time, manner, and aspect).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asín_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-asín-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This proclisis (
       <i>
        ascenso de clítico
       </i>
       ) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_movement" title="Syntactic movement">
        syntactic movement
       </a>
       away from the idea that an object must follow the verb. For example, in these two sentences with the same meaning:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zagona_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zagona-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <i>
         María quiere comprar
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        = "Maria wants to buy it."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         María
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         quiere comprar
        </i>
        = "Maria wants to buy it."
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       "Lo" is the object of "comprar" in the first example, but Spanish allows that clitic to appear in a preverbal positoin of a
       <a href="/wiki/Syntagma_(linguistics)" title="Syntagma (linguistics)">
        syntagma
       </a>
       that it dominates strictly, as in the second example. This movement only happens in conjugated verbs. But a special case occurs for the imperative, where we see the postverbal position of the clitic
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Llámame
        </i>
        = "Call me"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         dímelo
        </i>
        = "Tell it to me"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This is accounted for by a second syntactic movement wherein the verb "passes by" the clitic that has already "ascended".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clitic_substitution_for_the_accusative">
        Clitic substitution for the accusative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Clitic substitution for the accusative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In most cases, one can identify the direct object of a Spanish sentence by substituting it for the accusative personal pronouns "lo", "la", "los", and "las".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Aristóteles instruyó
        <b>
         a Alejandro
        </b>
        . (Aristotle instructed Alejandro)
       </li>
       <li>
        Aristóteles
        <b>
         lo
        </b>
        instruyó. (Aristotle instructed him.)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       But "lo" (only in its masculine and singular form) can also replace an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attributive_adjective" title="Attributive adjective">
        attributive adjective
       </a>
       , so it is necessary to identify first whether the sentence is
       <a href="/wiki/Predicative_expression" title="Predicative expression">
        predicative or attributive
       </a>
       . An example of the attributive:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Roma es valiente. (Rome is brave.)
       </li>
       <li>
        Roma lo es. (Rome is [brave].)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_.22.C2.BFQu.C3.A9.3F.22_method">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='The_"¿Qué?"_method'>
        The "¿Qué?" method
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: The "¿Qué?" method'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Traditional Spanish grade school
       <a href="/wiki/Language_pedagogy" title="Language pedagogy">
        language pedagogy
       </a>
       teaches that one may identify the direct object by asking the question "What?" (
       <i>
        "¿Qué?"
       </i>
       ). But this method is not always reliable, because the answer to the question "what" may be the subject:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         La nave surca
         <b>
          el mar
         </b>
        </i>
        ("The ship crosses the sea.")
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           ¿Qué surca? &gt; "La nave"
          </i>
          ("What crosses?" "The ship.")
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This method also fails to analyze question sentences:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿
         <b>
          Qué pueblos
         </b>
         sometió César?
        </i>
        ("Which peoples did Caesar conquer?")
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           ¿Qué sometió? &gt; ...
          </i>
          (Unintelligible.)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mandatory_proclitic">
        Mandatory proclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mandatory proclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Object pronouns are generally
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       , i.e. they appear before the verb of which they are the object. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo te veo
        </i>
        = "I see you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él lo dijo
        </i>
        = "He said it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tú lo has hecho
        </i>
        = "You have done it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         El libro nos fue dado
        </i>
        = "The book was given to us"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mandatory_enclitic">
        Mandatory enclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mandatory enclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In certain environments, however,
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Enclitic" title="Clitic">
        enclitic
       </a>
       pronouns (i.e. pronouns attached to the end of the verb or similar word itself) may appear. Enclitization is generally only found with:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        positive imperatives
       </li>
       <li>
        infinitives
       </li>
       <li>
        gerunds
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With positive imperatives, enclitization is always mandatory:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Haz
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        ("Do it") but never
        <i>
         Lo haz
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Dá
         <b>
          selo
         </b>
         a alguien diferente
        </i>
        ("Give it to somebody else") but never
        <i>
         Se lo da a alguien diferente
        </i>
        (as a command; that sentence can also mean "He/she/it gives it to somebody else", in which sense it is entirely correct)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With infinitives and gerunds, enclitization is often, but not always, mandatory. With all bare infinitives, enclitization is mandatory:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        = "to have it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         debér
         <b>
          telo
         </b>
        </i>
        = "to owe it to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         oír
         <b>
          nos
         </b>
        </i>
        = "to hear us"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In all compound infinitives that make use of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       (i.e. all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_aspect" title="Perfect aspect">
        perfect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">
        passive
       </a>
       infinitives), enclitics attach to the uninflected auxiliary verb and not the past participle (or participles) itself:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         haber
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         visto
        </i>
        = "to have seen it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ser
         <b>
          me
         </b>
         guardado
        </i>
        = "to be saved for me"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         habér
         <b>
          telos
         </b>
         dado
        </i>
        = "to have given them to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         haber
         <b>
          le
         </b>
         sido mostrado
        </i>
        = "to have been shown to him/her/you"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In all compound infinitives that make use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerund
       </a>
       , however, enclitics may attach to either the gerund itself or the main verb, including the rare cases when the gerund is used together with the past participle in a single infinitive:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         estar diciéndo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         estar
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         diciendo
        </i>
        = "to be saying it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         andar buscándo
         <b>
          los
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         andar
         <b>
          los
         </b>
         buscando
        </i>
        = "to go around looking for them"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         haber estado haciéndo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         haber
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         estado haciendo
        </i>
        = "to have been doing it"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With all bare gerunds, enclitization is once again mandatory. In all compound gerunds, enclitics attach to the same word as they would in the infinitive, and one has the same options with combinations of gerunds as with gerunds used in infinitives:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         haciéndo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        = "doing it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         hablándo
         <b>
          les
         </b>
        </i>
        = "talking to them"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         habiéndo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         visto
        </i>
        = "having seen it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         siéndo
         <b>
          me
         </b>
         dado
        </i>
        = "being given to me"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         habiéndo
         <b>
          le
         </b>
         sido mostrado
        </i>
        = "having been shown to him/her/you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         habiendo estado teniéndo
         <b>
          los
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         habiéndo
         <b>
          los
         </b>
         estado teniendo
        </i>
        = "having been holding them"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         andando buscándo
         <b>
          los
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         andándo
         <b>
          los
         </b>
         buscando
        </i>
        = "going around looking for them"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proclitic_or_enclitic">
        Proclitic or enclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Proclitic or enclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In constructions that make use of infinitives or gerunds as arguments of a conjugated verb, clitic pronouns may appear as proclitics before the verb (as in most verbal constructions, or, in the case of positive imperatives, as enclitics attached to it) or simply as enclitics attached to the infinitive or gerund itself. Similarly, in combinations of infinitives, enclitics may attach to any one infinitive:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Quería hacer
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         <b>
          Lo
         </b>
         quería hacer
        </i>
        = "He wanted to do it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estoy considerándo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         <b>
          Lo
         </b>
         estoy considerando
        </i>
        = "I'm considering it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Empieza a hacer
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Empiéza
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         a hacer
        </i>
        = "Start doing it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Sigue diciéndo
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Sígue
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         diciendo
        </i>
        = "Keep saying it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         querer ver
         <b>
          nos
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         querer
         <b>
          nos
         </b>
         ver
        </i>
        = "to want to see us"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener que poder hacer
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         tener que poder
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         hacer
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         tener
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         que poder hacer
        </i>
        = "to have to be able to do it"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Enclitics may be found in other environments in literary and archaic language, but such constructions are virtually absent from everyday speech.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metaplasm">
        Metaplasm
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Metaplasm">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Enclitization is subject to the following
       <a href="/wiki/Metaplasm" title="Metaplasm">
        metaplasmic
       </a>
       rules:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        in the first-person plural ending
        <i>
         -mos
        </i>
        drops before
        <i>
         nos
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         se
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         os
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         vámonos
        </i>
        ("let's go"),
        <i>
         démoselo
        </i>
        ("let's give it to him"),
        <i>
         mostrémoos
        </i>
        ("let's show you [pl.]"), etc.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         d
        </i>
        in the informal second-person plural positive imperative drops before
        <i>
         os
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         sentaos
        </i>
        ("[you all] sit down"),
        <i>
         apuraos
        </i>
        ("[you all] hurry up"), etc., except for the verb
        <i>
         ir
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         idos
        </i>
        ("[you all] leave")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Combinations_of_clitic_pronouns">
        Combinations of clitic pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Combinations of clitic pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In Spanish, two (and rarely three) clitic pronouns can be used with a single verb, generally one accusative and one dative. Whether enclitic or proclitic, they cluster in the following order:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proato_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-proato-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable" style="width:13em">
       <tr>
        <th align="center">
         1
        </th>
        <th>
         2
        </th>
        <th>
         3
        </th>
        <th>
         4
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         se
        </td>
        <td>
         te
         <br/>
         os
        </td>
        <td>
         me
         <br/>
         nos
        </td>
        <td>
         lo, la,
         <br/>
         los, las,
         <br/>
         le, les
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él me lo dio
        </i>
        = "He gave it to me"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellos te lo dijeron
        </i>
        = "They said it to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo te me daré
        </i>
        = "I will give myself to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vosotros os nos mostráis
        </i>
        = "You [pl.] are showing yourselves to us"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se le perdieron los libros
        </i>
        = "The books disappeared on him" (lit. "The books got lost to him")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The full and pronomial form of a reduplicated direct object must agree in gender and number:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          A las tropas las
         </b>
         dirige César.
        </i>
        = "Caesar directs the troops."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Like Latin, Spanish makes use of
       <a href="/wiki/Double_dative" title="Double dative">
        double dative
       </a>
       constructions, and thus up to two dative clitics can be used with a single verb. One must be the dative of benefit (i.e. someone (or something) who is indirectly affected by the action), and the other must refer to the direct recipient of the action itself. Context is generally sufficient to determine which is which:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me le arreglaron la moto
        </i>
        = roughly "They fixed the bike [motorcycle] for him on my behalf" or "They fixed the bike for me on his behalf" (literally more like "They fixed the bike for him for me" or vice-versa)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Muerte, ¿por qué te me lo llevaste tan pronto?
        </i>
        = "Death, why did you take him from me so soon?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Only one accusative clitic can be used with a single verb, however, and the same is true for any one type of dative clitic. When more than one accusative clitic or dative clitic of a specific type is used with a verb, therefore, the verb must be repeated for each clitic of the same case used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me gusta y te gusta
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Me y te gusta
        </i>
        = "You and I like him" (lit. "He pleases you and me")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo vi y te vi
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Lo y te vi
        </i>
        = "I saw him and you"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Occasionally, however, with verbs such as
       <i>
        dejar
       </i>
       ("to let"), which generally takes a direct object as well as a subsequent verb as a further grammatical argument, objects of two different verbs will appear together with only one of them and may thus appear to be objects of the same verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Me la
         </b>
         dejaron ver
        </i>
        = "They let me see her" (
        <i>
         la
        </i>
        is the object of
        <i>
         ver
        </i>
        ;
        <i>
         Me dejaron verla
        </i>
        is thus also acceptable)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Te lo
         </b>
         dejará hacer
        </i>
        = "He/she will let you do it" (
        <i>
         Te dejará hacerlo
        </i>
        is also acceptable)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Se">
        Se
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Se">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When an accusative third-person non-reflexive pronoun (
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        las
       </i>
       ) is used with a dative pronoun that is understood to also be third-person non-reflexive (
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       ), the dative pronoun is replaced by
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo di
        </i>
        = "I gave it to him"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él se lo dijo
        </i>
        = "He said it to him"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       as such is the indirect object in similar constructions, however, it is often, though not always, disambiguated with
       <i>
        a sí
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo hizo a sí
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Se lo hizo
        </i>
        = "He did it to himself"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo mantenían a sí
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Se lo mantenían
        </i>
        = "They kept it for themselves"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clitic_doubling">
        Clitic doubling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Clitic doubling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-Emphatic">
        Non-Emphatic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Non-Emphatic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Simple non-emphatic
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       is most often found with dative clitics, although it is occasionally found with accusative clitics as well. Because all personal non-clitic direct as well as indirect objects must be preceded by the preposition
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       , an appropriate dative clitic pronoun is often used to mark non-clitic indirect objects as such, even with non-personal things such as animals and inanimate objects. With all non-clitic dative personal pronouns, which take the form
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       + the prepositional case of the pronoun, and all non-pronominal indirect objects that come before the verb, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Active_voice" title="Active voice">
        active voice
       </a>
       , clitic doubling is mandatory:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-proato_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-proato-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         A mí me gusta eso
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         A mí gusta eso
        </i>
        = "I like that" (lit. "That pleases me")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Al hombre le dimos un regalo
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Al hombre dimos un regalo
        </i>
        = "We gave the man a gift"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Al perro le dijo que se siente
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Al perro dijo que se siente
        </i>
        = "He/She/You told the dog to sit"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With non-pronominal indirect objects that come after the verb, however, clitic doubling is usually optional, though generally preferred in spoken language:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Siempre (les) ofrezco café a mis huéspedes
        </i>
        = "I always offer coffee to my guests"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Le) Dijeron a José que se quedara donde estaba
        </i>
        = "They told Jose to stay where he was"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Le) Diste al gato alguna comida
        </i>
        = "You gave the cat some food"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Nevertheless, with indirect objects that do not refer to the direct recipient of the action itself as well as the dative of inalienable possession, clitic doubling is most often mandatory:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         No le gusta a la mujer la idea
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         No gusta a la mujer la idea
        </i>
        = "The woman doesn't like the idea" (lit. "The idea doesn't please the woman")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le preparé a mi jefe un informe
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Preparé a mi jefe un informe
        </i>
        = "I prepared a report for my boss"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Les cortó a las chicas el pelo
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Cortó a las chicas el pelo
        </i>
        = "He/She/You cut the girls' hair" (dative of inalienable possession, cannot be literally translated into English)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronouns" title="Indefinite pronouns">
        indefinite pronouns
       </a>
       , however, clitic doubling is optional even with such dative constructions:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta película no (le) gusta a nadie
        </i>
        = "No one likes this movie" (lit. "This move pleases no one")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Les) Preparó esta comida a todos
        </i>
        = "He/she/you made this food for everyone"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">
        passive voice
       </a>
       , where direct objects do not exist at all, simple non-emphatic dative clitic doubling is always optional, even with personal pronouns:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Le) Era guardado a mi amigo este pedazo
        </i>
        = "This piece was saved for my friend"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Te) Fue dado a ti
        </i>
        = "It was given to you"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Dative clitic personal pronouns may be used without their non-clitic counterparts, however:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él te habló
        </i>
        = "He spoke to you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo dieron
        </i>
        = "They gave it to him/her/you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nos era guardado
        </i>
        = "it was saved for us"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Simple non-emphatic clitic doubling with accusative clitics is much rarer. It is generally only found with:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the pronoun
        <i>
         todo
        </i>
        ("all, everything")
       </li>
       <li>
        numerals that refer to
        <a href="/wiki/Animate" title="Animate">
         animate
        </a>
        nouns (usually people) and are preceded by the definite article (e.g.
        <i>
         los seis
        </i>
        – "the six")
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronoun" title="Indefinite pronoun">
         indefinite pronoun
        </a>
        <i>
         uno
        </i>
        when referring to the person speaking
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         No
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         sé todo
        </i>
        = "I don't know everything"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Los
         </b>
         vi a los cinco
        </i>
        = "I saw the five (of them)"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Si no les gusta a ellos,
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         rechazarán a uno
        </i>
        = "If they don't like it, they'll reject you"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Accusative clitic doubling is also used in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-verb-subject" title="Object-verb-subject">
        object-verb-subject
       </a>
       (OVS) word order to distinguish it from simple
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" title="Subject-verb-object">
        subject-verb-object
       </a>
       (SVO). The appropriate direct object pronoun is placed between the direct object and the verb, and thus the sentence
       <i>
        La carne la come el perro
       </i>
       ("The dog eats the meat") is distinguished form the nonsensical
       <i>
        La carne come el perro
       </i>
       ("The meat eats the dog").
      </p>
      <p>
       Clitic doubling is also often necessary to modify clitic pronouns, whether dative or accusative. The non-clitic form of the accusative is usually identical to that of the dative, although non-clitic accusative pronouns cannot be used to refer to impersonal things such as animals and inanimate objects. With
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attributive_adjective" title="Attributive adjective">
        attributive adjectives
       </a>
       , nouns (
       <a href="/wiki/Apposition" title="Apposition">
        appositionally
       </a>
       , as in "us friends"), and the intensifier
       <i>
        mismo
       </i>
       , clitic doubling is mandatory, and the non-clitic form of the pronoun is used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Te vi
         <b>
          a ti muy feliz
         </b>
        </i>
        = "I saw a very happy you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Os conozco
         <b>
          a vosotros gente
         </b>
        </i>
        (or, in Latin America,
        <i>
         Los conozco
         <b>
          a ustedes gente
         </b>
        </i>
        ) = "I know you people"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Le ayudaron
         <b>
          a ella misma
         </b>
        </i>
        = "They helped her herself" (
        <i>
         ayudar
        </i>
        governs the dative)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Predicative_adjective" title="Predicative adjective">
        predicative adjectives
       </a>
       , however, clitic doubling is not necessary. Clitic pronouns may be directly modified by such adjectives, which must be placed immediately after the verb, gerund, or participle (or, if used in combination or series, the final verb, gerund, or participle):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mantente
         <b>
          informado
         </b>
        </i>
        = "Keep yourself informed"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Viéndolo
         <b>
          hecho
         </b>
         en persona, aprendí mucho
        </i>
        = "By seeing it done in person, I learned a lot"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo había oído
         <b>
          dicho
         </b>
         a veces
        </i>
        = "He/she/you had heard it said occasionally"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Emphatic">
        Emphatic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Emphatic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Clitic doubling is also the normal method of emphasizing clitic pronouns, whether accusative or dative. The clitic form is used in the normal way, and the non-clitic form is placed wherever one wishes to place emphasis:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo vi
         <b>
          a él
         </b>
        </i>
        = "I saw
        <i>
         him
        </i>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Te ama
         <b>
          a ti
         </b>
        </i>
        = "He loves
        <i>
         you
        </i>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          A ella
         </b>
         le gusta la idea
        </i>
        = "
        <i>
         She
        </i>
        likes the idea" (lit. "The idea pleases
        <i>
         her
        </i>
        ")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Because non-clitic accusative pronouns cannot have impersonal antecedents, however, impersonal accusative clitics must be used with their antecedents instead:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se las di
         <b>
          las cosas
         </b>
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Se las di ellas
        </i>
        = "I gave
        <i>
         the things
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         them
        </i>
        ) to her"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo vi
         <b>
          el libro
         </b>
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Lo vi él
        </i>
        = "I saw
        <i>
         the book
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         it
        </i>
        )"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Impersonal dative clitic pronouns, however, may be stressed as such:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se lo hiciste
         <b>
          a ellos
         </b>
        </i>
        = "You did it
        <i>
         to them
        </i>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esto le cabe
         <b>
          a ella
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This fits
        <i>
         it
        </i>
        [feminine noun]"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Emphatic non-pronominal clitic doubling is also occasionally used to provide a degree of emphasis to the sentence as a whole:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Lo
         </b>
         sé lo que dijo
        </i>
        = "I know what he/she/you said" (with a degree of emphasis)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡
         <b>
          Lo
         </b>
         hace el trabajo! ¡Déjalo solo!
        </i>
        = "He's doing his work! Leave him alone!"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prepositional_and_comitative_cases">
        Prepositional and comitative cases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Prepositional and comitative cases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The prepositional case is used with the majority of prepositions:
       <i>
        a mí
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        contra ti
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        bajo él
       </i>
       , etc., although several prepositions, such as
       <i>
        entre
       </i>
       ("between, among") and
       <i>
        según
       </i>
       ("according to"), actually govern the nominative (or
       <i>
        sí
       </i>
       in the case of
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       ):
       <i>
        entre
        <b>
         yo
        </b>
        y mi hermano
       </i>
       ("between me and my brother"),
       <i>
        según
        <b>
         tú
        </b>
       </i>
       ("according to you"),
       <i>
        entre
        <b>
         sí
        </b>
       </i>
       ("among themselves"), etc., with the exception of
       <i>
        entre
        <b>
         nos
        </b>
       </i>
       ("between us"), where the accusative may be used instead (
       <i>
        entre nosotros
       </i>
       is also acceptable). With the preposition
       <i>
        con
       </i>
       ("with"), however, the comitative is used instead.
       <i>
        Yo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Tú
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       have distinct forms in the comitative:
       <i>
        conmigo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        contigo
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        consigo
       </i>
       , respectively, in which the preposition becomes one word with its object and thus must not be repeated by itself:
       <i>
        conmigo
       </i>
       by itself means "with me", and
       <i>
        con conmigo
       </i>
       is redundant. For all other pronouns, the comitative is identical to the prepositional and is used in the same way:
       <i>
        con él
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        con nosotros
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        con ellos
       </i>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       As often with verbs used with multiple object pronouns of the same case, prepositions must be repeated for each pronoun they modify:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este vino es solamente para mí y para ti
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Este vino es solamente para mí y ti
        </i>
        = "This wine is only for me and (for) you"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ella estaba con él y con ella
        </i>
        but never
        <i>
         Ella estaba con él y ella
        </i>
        = "She was with him and (with) her"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span id="Le.C3.ADsmo">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Leísmo">
        Leísmo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_object_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Leísmo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Castilian_Spanish" title="Castilian Spanish">
        Castilian Spanish
       </a>
       , and especially that of Spain's capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , there exists the practice of
       <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
        leísmo
       </a>
       ; which is, using the indirect object pronoun
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       for the object pronoun where
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       would use
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (masculine) or
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       (feminine) for the object pronoun. In such an area,
       <i>
        Pedro le mató
       </i>
       (lit. "Pedro to him killed") and
       <i>
        Pedro lo mató
       </i>
       (lit. "Pedro killed him") would have the same meaning.
      </p>
      <p>
       To analyze a sentence to find the direct object, in a leísmo text where the dative pronouns may appear in places where one would normally expect the accusative pronoun, one may reword the sentence in the passive voice:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Rómulo asesinó
         <b>
          a Remo
         </b>
        </i>
        ("Romulus killed Remus")
        <ul>
         <li>
          "Rómulo lo asesinó" (leísmo form)
         </li>
         <li>
          "Rómulo le asesinó" (non-leísmo or standard form)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Remo fue asesinado por Rómulo.
        </i>
        ("Remus was killed by Romulus")
       </li>
      </ul>
