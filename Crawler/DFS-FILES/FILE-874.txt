      <p>
       A
       <b>
        Paisa
       </b>
       is someone from a region in the northwest of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       , including the part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andean_Region_(Colombia)" title="Andean Region (Colombia)">
        Andes in Colombia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Paisa region is formed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Colombia" title="Departments of Colombia">
        departments
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo_Department" title="Quindío Department">
        Quindío
       </a>
       . Some regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca_Department" title="Valle del Cauca Department">
        Valle del Cauca Department
       </a>
       (north) and
       <a href="/wiki/Tolima_Department" title="Tolima Department">
        Tolima Department
       </a>
       (west) culturally identify as
       <i>
        paisas
       </i>
       . The main cities of the Paisa region are
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pereira,_Colombia" title="Pereira, Colombia">
        Pereira
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manizales" title="Manizales">
        Manizales
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Armenia,_Colombia" title="Armenia, Colombia">
        Armenia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The name Paisa derives from the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">
        apocope
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Paisano
       </i>
       (countryman), but they are also known as "Antioqueños" (those from the old
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       , which included the other Paisa provinces, which was a single administrative body until the creation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas State
       </a>
       in 1905). Although many refer to Paisas as an ethnic group (
       <i>
        raza antioqueña
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        raza paisa
       </i>
       ), they are a part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_people" title="Colombian people">
        Colombians
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">
        Latin American
       </a>
       peoples.
      </p>
      <p>
       Paisas can be found in other regions of Colombia and the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       where they have migrated. They have such a particular way of speaking Spanish that some writers refer to as
       <i>
        español antioqueño
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Genetics">
        Genetics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Genetics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Paisas have been considered a
       <a href="/wiki/Peripatric_speciation" title="Peripatric speciation">
        genetically isolated population
       </a>
       according to scientific studies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As evidenced by the analysis of direct-line mitochondrial DNA or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MtDNA" title="MtDNA">
        mtDNA
       </a>
       (inherited from mother-to-child) and Y-chromosomal DNA or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Y-DNA" title="Y-DNA">
        Y-DNA
       </a>
       (inherited from father-to-son), the initial founding of the Paisa population occurred primarily through the admixture of male
       <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">
        Iberians
       </a>
       (mostly from various
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spaniard" title="Spaniard">
        Spaniard
       </a>
       ethnic groups, and a smaller
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jewish" title="Sephardic Jewish">
        Sephardic Jewish
       </a>
       element) and female
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindians" title="Amerindians">
        Amerindians
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Subsequently, within the emerging Paisa colonial society, a continued flow of additional male Spaniards immigrating into the Paisa region in the following generations encouraged marriages of these males to the early established Paisa population. This increased the overall European component and resulted in the "whitening" of the founding mixed-race population by preventing the mixed-race individuals marrying among themselves and growing in numbers, as well as preventing further unions of either Spaniards or mixed-race individuals with unmixed Amerindians.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ultimately, this led to the overall predominantly
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe" title="Ethnic groups in Europe">
        European
       </a>
       ancestry of today's Paisa population according to testing of autosomal DNA or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AtDNA" title="AtDNA">
        atDNA
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       despite the asymmetrical sex-specific genetic markers that they inherited from the founding population which indicates their Y-DNA as being predominantly from European male forebears and mtDNA as being predominantly from Amerindian female ancestors.
      </p>
      <p>
       The mountains played a large role in isolating the Paisa population until the end of the nineteenth century and the area's industrial revolution. The nineteenth and early twentieth century also brought further Spanish, European non-Spanish, and Middle Eastern immigrants to the region, notably from
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">
        Lebanon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       . Most of these immigrants ended up intermixing with the Paisa population, further diluting the overall Amerindian atDNA, though not greatly affecting the predominance of the Amerrindian mtDNA.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extremadura">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The ancestors of the Paisa are primarily Spanish immigrants from
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       , Spain (sixteenth century). The first colonizers were
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadurans" title="Extremadurans">
        Extremaduran
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Rodas" title="Gaspar de Rodas">
        Gaspar de Rodas
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trujillo,_Spain" title="Trujillo, Spain">
        Trujillo
       </a>
       , who was the first colonial governor of the region. Several towns, cities and places in the Paisa Region are also Extremaduran:
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn_(Spain)" title="Medellín (Spain)">
        Medellín of Badajoz
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1ceres,_Antioquia" title="Cáceres, Antioquia">
        Cáceres
       </a>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_municipalities_in_C%C3%A1ceres" title="List of municipalities in Cáceres">
        Province of Cáceres
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Valdivia,_Antioquia" title="Valdivia, Antioquia">
        Valdivia
       </a>
       for conqueror
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Valdivia" title="Pedro de Valdivia">
        Pedro de Valdivia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Andalusia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some conquerors from
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       like Marshall
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Robledo_(conquistador)" title="Jorge Robledo (conquistador)">
        Jorge Robledo
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Ja%C3%A9n,_Spain" title="Jaén, Spain">
        Jaén
       </a>
       , came also with the Extremadurans during the sixteenth century. However, during the seventeenth century a group of Andalusian migrants is said to have settled the region.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_people">
        Basque people
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Basque people">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The presence of Basque ancestry in the Paisa Region is exhibited by the proliferation of Basque surnames.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some scholars point out that this may be one of the regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       with the greatest concentration of ancestry from the Iberian region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Basques arrived in Antioquia during the seventeenth century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of Basque language (Euskera) terminology in the present territory of Colombia goes back to the early exploration which occurred in 1499, during the third voyage of Columbus. It is said that from that time the territory experienced a strong influx of Basques including prominent figures such as the pilot and geographer Juan de la Cosa, nicknamed "El Vizcaino" (although some reputable sources claim that he was not a native of the Basque Country, but was instead born in Santoña, Cantabria).
      </p>
      <p>
       Thereafter, the Basques began to come regularly and distributed throughout the country. Due to this presence, the Colombian department of Antioquia has been considered a major point of Basque-Navarre immigration. This occurred mainly during the colonial era, when hundreds of Basques migrated to be linked to the Spanish colonization companies.
      </p>
      <p>
       To people interested in investigating the presence of Euskal Herria in the department of Antioquia, one of the questions that troubles them relates to the use and retention of the Basque language in the department.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is estimated that for Antioquia, a region where hundreds of Spaniards arrived, of which a good portion were Basque, limited aspects of the Basque culture and traditions were brought over, without even mentioning the particular language. It has been difficult to track the use of Euskara in Antioquia and Colombia because the Basque language was always an outcast, which apparently left no written evidence in Antioquia.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This is likely because the Spanish crown, to maintain the monopoly of its overseas companies and to restrict those people not belonging to Spanish rule, did not allow languages other than Castilian to be spoken. This meant that those invited to participate in the colonization of Indian companies, and foreigners in general, had to learn the official language, i.e., Castilian, hence the prevalence of Castilian-Basque-speaking bilinguals.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite these restrictions, it is still possible to trace the history of Colombia's ties to the ancient language of the Basques. A reference that has use of Euskara in Colombian territory occurred in relation to Lope de Aguirre, a native of Gipuzkoa nicknamed "The Madman". Aguirre's rebellion defied the Spanish empire, carrying out acts against the subjects of the Spanish crown. Pedro de Ursúa, a Navarrese faithful to the Spanish king, who was also the founder of Pamplona in eastern Colombia, said that he could persuade the soldiers to be part of Aguirre's revolt, if they spoke in Euskera.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the seventeenth and eighteenth century
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">
        Basque
       </a>
       families from Northern Spain settled in the
       <a href="/wiki/Aburr%C3%A1_Valley" title="Aburrá Valley">
        Aburrá Valley
       </a>
       where
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Envigado" title="Envigado">
        Envigado
       </a>
       are located, as well as small towns in eastern Antioquia, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Marinilla" title="Marinilla">
        Marinilla
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Retiro,_Antioquia" title="El Retiro, Antioquia">
        El Retiro
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/El_Santuario" title="El Santuario">
        El Santuario
       </a>
       . This part of Antioquia reminded these families of northern Spain.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sephardi_Jews">
        Sephardi Jews
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sephardi Jews">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is debate about
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish" title="Jewish">
        Jewish
       </a>
       ancestry in the Paisa people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was known that some Spanish and Portuguese
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Christians" title="New Christians">
        New Christians
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jewish" title="Sephardic Jewish">
        Sephardic Jewish
       </a>
       ancestry (some of whom continued to
       <a href="/wiki/Crypto-Judaism" title="Crypto-Judaism">
        practice Judaism secretly
       </a>
       , and were also known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marranos" title="Marranos">
        marranos
       </a>
       , Spanish for swine) fled the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">
        Cartagena de Indias
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">
        Inquisition
       </a>
       and took refuge in the Antioquian mountains during the sixteenth and seventeenth century. Some Colombian authors like
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Isaacs" title="Jorge Isaacs">
        Jorge Isaacs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Porfirio_Barba-Jacob" title="Porfirio Barba-Jacob">
        Miguel Ángel Osorio
       </a>
       have claimed that it is indisputable that Paisas have Jewish ancestry. Several Paisa surnames are known to have been prevalent among
       <a href="/wiki/New_Christian" title="New Christian">
        New Christian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">
        conversos
       </a>
       of Sephardic Jewish origin, for example
       <a href="/wiki/Espinosa_(surname)" title="Espinosa (surname)">
        Espinosa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A9rez" title="Pérez">
        Pérez
       </a>
       , Mejía, and many others.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some scholars state that the presence of Sephardic Jews among the ancestors of Paisas is a fact, but it does not mean that all Paisas descend from them, nor that it is the only or predominant element among those that do, as is proven by the Paisas' descent from other groups like Basques, Extremadurans, Andalusians and Catalans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Canarians">
        Canarians
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Canarians">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are records also of presence of some Canarians and Canarian families, at least some of them known to be from
       <a href="/wiki/Lanzarote" title="Lanzarote">
        Lanzarote
       </a>
       , who settled in
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1ceres,_Antioquia" title="Cáceres, Antioquia">
        Cáceres, Antioquia
       </a>
       , in the second half of the 16th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DelgadoArmas2010_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DelgadoArmas2010-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others emigrated in 1678 by the terms of the
       <i>
        Tributo de Sangre
       </i>
       to Santa Marta.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guagua_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guagua-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       , in 1536,
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Lugo" title="Pedro Fernández de Lugo">
        Pedro Fernández de Lugo
       </a>
       led an expedition of 1,500 people, 400 of whom were Canarians from all the different islands that make up the archipelago
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laprocolomcanaconq_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-laprocolomcanaconq-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), for the conquest of the area around what became
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Marta,_Colombia" title="Santa Marta, Colombia">
        Santa Marta
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DelgadoArmas2010_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DelgadoArmas2010-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This contingent pacified the warring tribes on the coast and penetrated into the interior (including Paisa region). On the way, they founded several cities, two which, Las Palmas and Tenerife, still exist.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-laprocolomcanaconq_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-laprocolomcanaconq-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition,
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Heredia" title="Pedro de Heredia">
        Pedro de Heredia
       </a>
       led 100 men from the Canary Islands to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">
        Cartagena de Indias
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-González2005_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-González2005-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Catalan">
        Catalan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Catalan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although the facts of the presence of Catalan people in the origins of all Colombians have been a matter of studies, the Catalan genetic and influence in the Paisa Region has been also of particular analysis, especially in the way Paisas use their Spanish language and elements of the traditional customs as well as surnames.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the expression "Paisa" is of popular use as
       <a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">
        apocope
       </a>
       of "Paisano" (one from the same country; countryman), the origin of the expression goes back to a separatist movement that brewed through the region in the mid nineteenth Century. Those politicians that secretly supported secession would refer to the new country as "Pais A", short for Pais Antioquia. The moniker eventually was fused to create the word "paisa".
       <sup>
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
           <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (August 2011)">
            verification needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </sup>
       Consequently, "
       <i>
        Paisa Region
       </i>
       " is the region where the Paisa people live. A more ancient expression is
       <i>
        Antioqueño
       </i>
       (Antioquean; one from
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       ). This one is more official, especially during the Colony (16th - eighteenth centuries) and the nineteenth century after the Independence of Colombia. All the region made a single body as "Province of Antioquia" first and "State of Antioquia" after. In 1905 the
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas Department
       </a>
       was created from the southern part of Antioquia, rendering the word "
       <i>
        Antioqueño
       </i>
       " remain only applicable to those of Antioquia, while "Paisa" became a more cultural one for both the new Antioquia and the former Antioquia and the rest of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eje_cafetero" title="Eje cafetero">
        Eje cafetero
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although some sources argue that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">
        American Indians
       </a>
       that populated most of the Paisa Region were extinguished through European diseases and fights against the Spaniard conquerors, this has not been fully demonstrated.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_C%C3%A9sar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francisco César (page does not exist)">
        Francisco César
       </a>
       made an expedition in 1537 from
       <a href="/wiki/Urab%C3%A1_Antioquia" title="Urabá Antioquia">
        Urabá
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Cauca_River" title="Cauca River">
        Cauca River
       </a>
       to the lands of
       <a href="/wiki/Dabeiba" title="Dabeiba">
        Dabeiba
       </a>
       , but his troops were rejected by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nutibara" title="Nutibara">
        Nutibaras
       </a>
       . In 1540 Marshall
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Robledo_(conquistador)" title="Jorge Robledo (conquistador)">
        Jorge Robledo
       </a>
       founded
       <a href="/wiki/Cartago,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Cartago, Valle del Cauca">
        Cartago
       </a>
       . In 1541 he founded Arma in what is today the south of Antioquia, near today
       <a href="/wiki/Aguadas,_Caldas" title="Aguadas, Caldas">
        Aguadas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_de_Antioquia" title="Santa Fe de Antioquia">
        Santa Fe de Antioquia
       </a>
       , at the banks of the Cauca River. This last town would become the provincial capital in 1813.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first colonial governor was Don
       <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_de_Rodas" title="Gaspar de Rodas">
        Gaspar de Rodas
       </a>
       (1518–1607).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mountains of Antioquia attracted the Spaniards for its gold and lands for cattle, and the first towns were located near gold mines and rivers. Despite that, the region did not attract a population interested in creating important centers for the Spanish civilization like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">
        Cartagena de Indias
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Popay%C3%A1n" title="Popayán">
        Popayán
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">
        Bogotá
       </a>
       and it remained almost entirely isolated from the rest of the colony. This is the main reason for the cultural identity of the Paisas within the Colombian national context.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the seventeenth century and until the end of the nineteenth centuries, Paisa families moved to the southern regions of Antioquia, in what is today the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_Coffee-Growers_Axis" title="Colombian Coffee-Growers Axis">
        Colombian Coffee-Growers Axis
       </a>
       or the "
       <i>
        Viejo Caldas
       </i>
       " (
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Old Caldas
       </a>
       ), though now most Colombian nationals refer to this region as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eje_cafetero" title="Eje cafetero">
        Eje cafetero
       </a>
       . This constant internal migration is known in history as the "
       <i>
        Colonización Antioqueña
       </i>
       " (Antioquean Colonization). Most of the cities and towns founded in the Old Caldas (Caldas,
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda,_Caldas" title="Risaralda, Caldas">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo" title="Quindío">
        Quindío
       </a>
       and some towns of the north of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca" title="Valle del Cauca">
        Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       and the west of
       <a href="/wiki/Tolima_Department" title="Tolima Department">
        Tolima
       </a>
       ) are from that time.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the wars for the independence of Colombia, the most important Paisa figure was General
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_C%C3%B3rdova" title="José María Córdova">
        José María Córdova
       </a>
       . He was from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rionegro,_Antioquia" title="Rionegro, Antioquia">
        Rionegro
       </a>
       and fought important battles to free the region from the Spanish regime under the orders of
       <a href="/wiki/Sim%C3%B3n_Bol%C3%ADvar" title="Simón Bolívar">
        Simón Bolívar
       </a>
       , who never went to the region. During
       <a href="/wiki/Bol%C3%ADvar%27s_campaign_to_liberate_New_Granada" title="Bolívar's campaign to liberate New Granada">
        Bolívar's campaign to liberate New Granada
       </a>
       , Córdoba participated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Boyac%C3%A1" title="Battle of Boyacá">
        Battle of Boyacá
       </a>
       and was entitled "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lieutenant_Colonel" title="Lieutenant Colonel">
        Lieutenant Colonel
       </a>
       " by the Libertador, despite his young age (he was only 20). Then he was charged by Bolívar to defend the Province of Antioquia and in fact he defeated the Spaniards during the Campaign of
       <a href="/wiki/Nech%C3%AD" title="Nechí">
        Nechí
       </a>
       between the end of 1819 and the beginning of 1820.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1826
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       was declared the capital of the Province of Antioquia. In 1856 a Federalist Political Constitution created the State of Antioquia and it faced some civil wars among Liberals and Conservatives. In 1877 the president of the federal state was
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Justo_Berr%C3%ADo" title="Pedro Justo Berrío">
        Pedro Justo Berrío
       </a>
       , who was one of the most prominent political leaders of the region at the end of the century and developed an active politic in education, transportation (including connecting the region with the rest of the country by train in 1874) and economic development.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1886, with a centralized Political Constitution, the "Department of Antioquia" was created. Although the region was not affected directly by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thousand_Days_War" title="Thousand Days War">
        Thousand Days War
       </a>
       (1899–1902), one of the main characters of the fighting, General
       <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Uribe_Uribe" title="Rafael Uribe Uribe">
        Rafael Uribe Uribe
       </a>
       at the side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Liberal_Party" title="Colombian Liberal Party">
        Liberal Party rebels
       </a>
       , came from the area.
      </p>
      <p>
       The progressive government of General
       <a href="/wiki/Rafael_Reyes" title="Rafael Reyes">
        Rafael Reyes
       </a>
       (1904–1909) was of benefit in the development for the region. One of his projects was the creation of new departments, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas Department
       </a>
       to be taken from the southern part of
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       in 1905. During the twentieth century both Paisa departments (Antioquia and Caldas), would continue their development in industry, mining and agriculture. In 1966 the Caldas Department was divided in three parts: Caldas itself,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo" title="Quindío">
        Quindío
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       At the end of the century the region faced the crisis of growing drug traffic mafias, paramilitary groups and guerrillas, especially in Antioquia with the
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn_Cartel" title="Medellín Cartel">
        Medellín Cartel
       </a>
       and the north of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca" title="Valle del Cauca">
        Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       . However, development has proved to be a Colombian model in regions like the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Area_of_Medell%C3%ADn" title="Metropolitan Area of Medellín">
        Metropolitan Area of Medellín
       </a>
       according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Inter-American_Development_Bank" title="Inter-American Development Bank">
        Inter-American Development Bank
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Paisa_diaspora">
        Paisa diaspora
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Paisa diaspora">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although they are very attached to their country land, Paisas have been always prompt to migrate in search of better opportunities. This fact has been of great benefit for the development of some regions in Colombia, first during the "Antioquean Colonization" of the Colombian Coffee Axis between the seventeenth and nineteenth centuries, and currently to other regions of the nation like the Llanos Orientales (East of Colombia). They keep their traditions and love for commerce, agriculture and mines wherever they go. In the Llanos Orientales, for example, several haciendas of cattle are owned by Paisa families. They are one of the main Colombian groups to migrate to foreign countries, especially to United States (including
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       ), Spain and some Latin American countries like
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
        Economy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Economy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Paisas are distinguished in Colombia for their natural love and abilities for business and commerce. The Paisa Region coincided with the important economic centers of Colombia like the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Area_of_Medell%C3%ADn" title="Metropolitan Area of Medellín">
        Metropolitan Area of Medellín
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Textile" title="Textile">
        textile
       </a>
       , industries like appliances, automobiles and chemicals, services like health care and fashion); the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_Coffee-Growers_Axis" title="Colombian Coffee-Growers Axis">
        Colombian Coffee-Growers Axis
       </a>
       in agriculture and other economic activities like flower growing, cattle, gold and coal mines, tourism and others.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Culture">
        Culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Culture">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language">
        Language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Spanish" title="Colombian Spanish">
        Colombian Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The way Paisas speak Spanish, also known as
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espa%C3%B1ol_antioque%C3%B1o" title="es:Español antioqueño">
         Antioquean Spanish
        </a>
       </i>
       , is distinctive within Colombia. Paisas are said to speak Spanish fast and soft. They have many local and regional expressions that are opaque even for other Colombians. From the rural Paisa dialect, a popular urban version called
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parlache" title="es:Parlache">
        <i>
         Parlache
        </i>
       </a>
       developed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
          Voseo
         </a>
        </b>
        (using
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        ): In colloquial speech, Paisas use
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vos#Spanish" title="wikt:vos">
          vos
         </a>
        </i>
        as the second person singular informal pronoun (instead of
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/t%C3%BA#Spanish" title="wikt:tú">
          tú
         </a>
        </i>
        ) and
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/usted#Spanish" title="wikt:usted">
          usted
         </a>
        </i>
        for formal address, although it is common to use
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        even with relatives and friends. However,
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        is restricted to colloquial use and, unlike exclusively
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        regions that use it for official purposes like the press and government,
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        in the Paisa Region is rarely used in official documents. Several Paisa writers (such as
        <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Carrasquilla" title="Tomás Carrasquilla">
         Tomás Carrasquilla
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Gonz%C3%A1lez_(writer)" title="Fernando González (writer)">
         Fernando González Ochoa
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Mej%C3%ADa_Vallejo" title="Manuel Mejía Vallejo">
         Manuel Mejía Vallejo
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Vallejo" title="Fernando Vallejo">
         Fernando Vallejo
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Arango" title="Gonzalo Arango">
         Gonzalo Arango
        </a>
        ) use
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        in their works as a distinct marker of the Paisa identity. However, the use of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        is well known due to the immigration of Colombian groups like the
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_Region,_Colombia" title="Caribbean Region, Colombia">
          Costeños
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
          Seseo
         </a>
        </b>
        (merger of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
          θ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ): As with most
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
         American dialects of Spanish
        </a>
        , Paisas do not distinguish ‹s› from ‹z› or
        <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_C" title="Hard and soft C">
         soft ‹c›
        </a>
        . While
        <i>
         seseo
        </i>
        is dominant, the Paisa /s/ is articulated as an apicoalveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
          s̺
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , a sound transitional between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , as in central and northern Spain and southern Central America. The apicoalveolar 's' was influenced by Basques, Catalans, and Extremadurans, and
        <i>
         seseo
        </i>
        was influenced by Andalusians and Canarians.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          Yeísmo
         </a>
        </b>
        (merger of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
          ʎ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        into /
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
          ʝ
         </a>
        </span>
        ~
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">
          j
         </a>
        </span>
        /): Paisas pronounce ‹ll› as ‹y›, so that there is no distinction between
        <i>
         cayó
        </i>
        (it fell) and
        <i>
         calló
        </i>
        (became silent).
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
         voiced
        </a>
        consonants
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosive" title="Plosive">
         plosives
        </a>
        after and sometimes before any consonant, like other Colombian dialects (rather than the
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
         approximant
        </a>
        that is characteristic of most other dialects). Thus
        <i>
         pardo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpardo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         barba
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbarba]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         algo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈalɡo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         peligro
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈliɡɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         desde
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdezde] (dialectally [ˈdeɦde] or [ˈdedːe])
        </span>
        —rather than the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈparðo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbarβa]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈalɣo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈliɣɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdezðe] (dial. [ˈdeɦðe], [ˈdɛɦðe] or [ˈdɛðːe]
        </span>
        ) of Spain and the rest of Spanish America. A notable exception is the region of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nari%C3%B1o" title="Nariño">
         Nariño
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_24-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-auto-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and most
        <i>
         Costeño
        </i>
        speech (Atlantic coastal dialects) which feature the soft, fricative realizations common to all other Hispanic American and European dialects.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Land_and_inheritance">
        Land and inheritance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Land and inheritance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Paisas are very attached to their families and land. As their natural cultural space is on the mountains, it is also a symbol of their land. They give a great importance to surnames and ancestors. They even associate surnames to towns ("
       <i>
        los Pérez son de San Pedro de los Milagros
       </i>
       ", the Pérez [family] are from San Pedro de los Milagros). Though the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrilinear" title="Patrilinear">
        patrilinear
       </a>
       character is very important for families, Paisas keep a strong
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Matriarchal" title="Matriarchal">
        matriarchal
       </a>
       culture.
      </p>
      <p>
       Paisas are well known in Colombia for their kindness and welcoming aptitude to persons from other regions and visitors. They are known to joke and exaggerate creating enjoyable conversations, though this can confuse those who are not used to their way of speaking. They speak proudly of their land, towns, cities, history, traditions and abilities in commerce. It is common that Paisas do not use their local
       <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">
        demonym
       </a>
       (for example, "
       <i>
        medellinenses
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        manizalitas
       </i>
       , etc), but they refer to themselves as "Paisas".
      </p>

      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">

        meat
       </a>
       , tropical fruits,
       <a href="/wiki/Potato" title="Potato">

       </a>

        <i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
        Music
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Music">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tiple.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1926" data-file-width="921" height="418" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Tiple.jpg/200px-Tiple.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Tiple.jpg/300px-Tiple.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Tiple.jpg/400px-Tiple.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tiple.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Tiple" title="Tiple">
          Tiple
         </a>
         , traditional instrument in the Paisa folklore in genres like the Paisa Trova and Pasillo.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Paisa Region is center of different genres of music among traditional, modern and adopted. The most important instruments of music by tradition are the
       <a href="/wiki/Tiple" title="Tiple">
        tiple
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Guitar" title="Guitar">
        guitar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Traditional
        </b>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          <b>
           <a href="/wiki/Pasillo" title="Pasillo">
            Pasillo
           </a>
          </b>
          : In the Paisa Region has had a great diffusion as it is proven by the annual National Festival of the Colombian Pasillo in
          <a href="/wiki/Aguadas,_Caldas" title="Aguadas, Caldas">
           Aguadas
          </a>
          .
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlos_Vieco" title="Carlos Vieco">
           Carlos Vieco
          </a>
          is one of the best known Paisa composers of Pasillo for example with "
          <i>
           Hacia el Calvario
          </i>
          " ("Towards Calvary").
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           Rail Music
          </b>
          : In Spanish
          <i>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%BAsica_de_carrilera" title="es:Música de carrilera">
            Música de Carrilera
           </a>
          </i>
          is the Paisa "
          <a href="/wiki/Country_music" title="Country music">
           Country music
          </a>
          ". It was originated in Antioquia, especially along the
          <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Railway" title="Antioquia Railway">
           Antioquia Railway
          </a>
          . It is also known as "
          <i>
           Música guasca
          </i>
          ".
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           Songs of Heartbreak
          </b>
          : In Spanish
          <i>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%BAsica_de_despecho" title="es:Música de despecho">
            Música de despecho
           </a>
          </i>
          . In Colombia this genre became identity of the Paisa region. The composer and musician
          <a href="/wiki/Dar%C3%ADo_G%C3%B3mez" title="Darío Gómez">
           Darío Gómez
          </a>
          of
          <a href="/wiki/San_Jer%C3%B3nimo,_Antioquia" title="San Jerónimo, Antioquia">
           San Jerónimo
          </a>
          has been nominated "The King of the Songs of Heartbreak" (
          <i>
           El Rey del Despecho
          </i>
          ). His song "
          <i>
           Nadie es eterno
          </i>
          " ("Nobody is Eternal") became one of the most popular song in Colombia. Other artists of this genre are El Charrito Negro, Luis Alberto Posada, Jhonny Rivera, Lady Yuliana, Pipe Bueno, Giovany Ayala, Grupo Tornado, Fernando Burbano, Bera, El Andariego and many others. The Paisa - American Lucía Pulido is the main artist of this genre in United States.
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           Paisa Trova
          </b>
          : In Spanish
          <i>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trova_paisa" title="es:Trova paisa">
            Trova paisa
           </a>
          </i>
          . It shows the creativity, humor, mentality, and identity of the Paisas. The most important is to create new Trovas in every performance. Salvo Ruiz and Ñito Restrepo from
          <a href="/wiki/Concordia,_Antioquia" title="Concordia, Antioquia">
           Concordia
          </a>
          are regarded as the fathers of the Paisa Trova.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Adopted
        </b>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          <b>
           <a href="/wiki/Tango_music" title="Tango music">
            Tango
           </a>
          </b>
          : This
          <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
           Argentinian
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
           Uruguayan
          </a>
          music became popular in Antioquia during the first part of the twentieth century, maybe due to Argentinian migrations to
          <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
           Medellín
          </a>
          . In 1934 the King of Tango,
          <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Gardel" title="Carlos Gardel">
           Carlos Gardel
          </a>
          , died in a plane crash in the Paisa capital. The Paisa writer
          <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Mej%C3%ADa_Vallejo" title="Manuel Mejía Vallejo">
           Manuel Mejía Vallejo
          </a>
          wrote "
          <i>
           Aire de Tango
          </i>
          " (
          <i>
           Air of Tango
          </i>
          ), a work that shows the big influence of Tango in the modern Paisa folklore. The Tango Festival takes place in Barrio Manrique of Medellín where is the "
          <i>
           Tangovía
          </i>
          " and a monument to Gardel.
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           <a href="/wiki/Vallenato" title="Vallenato">
            Vallenato
           </a>
          </b>
          : This music from the Caribbean Region of Colombia (
          <a href="/wiki/Valledupar" title="Valledupar">
           Valledupar
          </a>
          ), has found in the Paisa Region its place. It was brought especially by young students from the north of Colombia who came to study in the Andean cities. There are several Paisa music groups of Vallenato.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
        Religion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paisa_Region&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Religion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Roman Catholicism in Colombia arrived in the region with the Spaniard colonizers at the beginning of the sixteenth century.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">
        Franciscans
       </a>
       settled along with colonizers and built churches and monasteries in the towns founded by the Spaniards. Perhaps Spanish and Portuguese
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marranos" title="Marranos">
        marranos
       </a>
       arrived in the region as well. Roman Catholicism became the predominant religion and Paisas remained devout and churchgoing. The 1991 Colombian Political Constitution that decreed freedom of religion opened the gates to other religious denominations, though Paisas are considered Catholics by culture.
