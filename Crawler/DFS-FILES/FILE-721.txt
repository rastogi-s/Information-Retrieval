   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Mutual intelligibility
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

    </div>

    </div>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        mutual intelligibility
       </b>
       is a relationship between
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       in which speakers of different but related
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       can readily understand each other without prior familiarity or special effort. It is sometimes used as an important criterion for distinguishing languages from dialects, although
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">
        sociolinguistic
       </a>
       factors are often also used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Intelligibility between languages can be asymmetric, with speakers of one understanding more of the other than speakers of the other understanding the first. When it is relatively symmetric, it is characterized as "mutual". It exists in differing degrees among many related or geographically proximate languages of the world, often in the context of a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Intelligibility">
        Intelligibility
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mutual_intelligibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Intelligibility">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>

      <p>
       For individuals to achieve moderate proficiency or understanding in a language (called L2) other than their
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first language
       </a>
       (L1) typically requires considerable time and effort through study and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Practice_(learning_method)" title="Practice (learning method)">
        practical application
       </a>
       . However, many groups of languages are partly mutually intelligible, i.e. most speakers of one language find it relatively easy to achieve some degree of understanding in the related language(s). Often the languages are
       <a href="/wiki/Genetic_relationship_(linguistics)" title="Genetic relationship (linguistics)">
        genetically related
       </a>
       , and they are likely to be similar to each other in
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronunciation
       </a>
       , or other features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Intelligibility among languages can vary between individuals or groups within a language population according to their knowledge of various registers and vocabulary in their own language, their exposure to additional related languages, their interest in or familiarity with other cultures, the domain of discussion,
       <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
        psycho
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Cognition" title="Cognition">
        cognitive
       </a>
       traits, the mode of language used (written vs. oral), and other factors.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutually_intelligible_languages_or_varieties_of_one_language">
        Mutually intelligible languages or varieties of one language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mutual_intelligibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mutually intelligible languages or varieties of one language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is no formal distinction between two distinct languages and two
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       of a single language, but linguists generally use mutual intelligibility as one of the primary factors in deciding between the two cases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KordiJin_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KordiJin-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some linguists
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       claim that mutual intelligibility is, ideally at least,
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       primary criterion separating languages from dialects. On the other hand, speakers of closely related languages can often communicate with each other to some extent; thus there are varying degrees of mutual intelligibility, and often other criteria are also used. As an example, in the case of a linear
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       that shades gradually between varieties, where speakers near the center can understand the varieties at both ends, but speakers at one end cannot understand the speakers at the other end, the entire chain is often considered a single language. If the central varieties then die out and only the varieties at both ends survive, they may then be reclassified as two languages, even though no actual language change has occurred.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition, political and social conventions often override considerations of mutual intelligibility. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">
        varieties of Chinese
       </a>
       are often considered a single language even though there is usually no mutual intelligibility between geographically separated varieties. In contrast, there is often significant intelligibility between different
       <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">
        Scandinavian languages
       </a>
       , but as each of them has its own standard form, they are classified as separate languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       To deal with the conflict in cases such as Chinese and
       <a href="/wiki/German_dialects" title="German dialects">
        German
       </a>
       , the term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Abstand_and_ausbau_languages#Roofing" title="Abstand and ausbau languages">
         Dachsprache
        </a>
       </i>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics" title="Sociolinguistics">
        sociolinguistic
       </a>
       "umbrella language") is sometimes seen: Chinese and German are languages in the sociolinguistic sense even though some speakers cannot understand each other without recourse to a standard or prestige form.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Asymmetric_intelligibility">
        Asymmetric intelligibility
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mutual_intelligibility&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Asymmetric intelligibility">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Asymmetric intelligibility refers to two languages that are considered partially mutually intelligible, but where one group of speakers has more difficulty understanding the other language than the other way around. There can be various reasons for this. If, for example, one language is related to another but has simplified its grammar, the speakers of the original language may understand the simplified language, but not vice versa. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       speakers tend to find it easier to understand
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afrikaans_language" title="Afrikaans language">
        Afrikaans
       </a>
       than vice versa as a result of Afrikaans's simplified grammar,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gooskens_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gooskens-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although the large number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/False_friends" title="False friends">
        false friends
       </a>
       between these languages can cause misunderstanding.
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps the most common reason for apparent asymmetric intelligibility is that speakers of one variety have more exposure to the other than vice versa. For example, speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">
        Scottish English
       </a>
       have frequent exposure to standard
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       through movies and TV programs, whereas speakers of American English have little exposure to Scottish English; hence, American English speakers often find it difficult to understand Scottish English or, especially,
       <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
        Scots
       </a>
       (which differs significantly from Scottish Standard English), whereas Scots tend to have few problems understanding standard American English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Northern Germanic language spoken in Scandinavia form a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       where two furthermost dialects have almost no mutual intelligibility. As such, spoken Danish and Swedish normally have low mutual intelligibility,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gooskens_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gooskens-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but Swedes in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%96resund" title="Öresund">
        Öresund
       </a>
       region (including
       <a href="/wiki/Malm%C3%B6" title="Malmö">
        Malmö
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Helsingborg" title="Helsingborg">
        Helsingborg
       </a>
       ), across a strait from the Danish capital
       <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">
        Copenhagen
       </a>
       , understand Danish somewhat better, largely due to the proximity of the region to Danish-speaking areas (see
       <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages#Mutual_intelligibility" title="North Germanic languages">
        <i>
         Mutual intelligibility in North Germanic languages
        </i>
       </a>
       ). While Norway was under
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark%E2%80%93Norway" title="Denmark–Norway">
        Danish rule
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">
        Bokmål
       </a>
       written standard of Norwegian originates from
       <a href="/wiki/Dano-Norwegian" title="Dano-Norwegian">
        Dano-Norwegian
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9" title="Koiné">
        koiné
       </a>
       that evolved among the urban elite in Norwegian cities during the later years of the union. Additionally, Norwegian assimilated a considerable amount of Danish vocabulary as well as traditional Danish expressions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gooskens_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-gooskens-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a consequence, spoken mutual intelligibility is not reciprocal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gooskens_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-gooskens-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly, in
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , standard German or Italian speakers may have great difficulty understanding the "dialects" from regions other than their own, but virtually all "dialect" speakers learn the standard languages in school and from the media.
      </p>

       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
          German
         </a>
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
         Dutch
        </a>
        . Standard Dutch and
        <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">
         Standard German
        </a>
        show a limited degree of mutual intelligibility when written. One study concluded that when concerning written language, Dutch speakers could translate 50.2% of the provided German words correctly, while the German test subjects were able to translate 41.9% of the Dutch equivalents correctly. In terms of orthography, 22% of the vocabulary of Dutch and German is identical or near identical. The
        <a href="/wiki/Levenshtein_distance" title="Levenshtein distance">
         Levenshtein distance
        </a>
        between written Dutch and German is 50.4% as opposed to 61.7% between English and Dutch.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
         <a href="#cite_note-41">
          [41]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
         <a href="#cite_note-42">
          [42]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The spoken languages are much more difficult to understand for both, with studies showing Dutch speakers having slightly less difficulty in understanding German speakers than vice versa, though it remains unclear whether this
        <a href="/wiki/Asymmetry" title="Asymmetry">
         asymmetry
        </a>
        has to do with prior knowledge of the language (German and French being obligatory foreign languages for two years in Dutch secondary education), better knowledge of another related language (
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        ) or any other non-linguistic reasons.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-let.rug.nl_43-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-let.rug.nl-43">
          [43]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DGcognateintel_44-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-DGcognateintel-44">
          [44]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
          German
         </a>
        </b>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Luxembourgish" title="Luxembourgish">
         Luxembourgish
        </a>
        . The languages are closely related. Intellegibility may require the German-speaker to read the given text aloud and slowly; in that case he/she will usually understand most texts. For the contrary direction, intellegibility cannot be tested because Luxembourgians learn German at school.
       </li>
      </ul>
