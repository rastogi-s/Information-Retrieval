      <p>
       <b>
        Manually coded languages
       </b>
       are not themselves languages but are representations of oral languages in a gestural-visual form; that is,
       <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
        signed
       </a>
       versions of oral languages (signed languages). Unlike the
       <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
        sign languages
       </a>
       that have evolved naturally in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaf_community" title="Deaf community">
        Deaf communities
       </a>
       , which have distinct spatial structures, these manual codes (MCL) are the conscious invention of
       <a href="/wiki/Hearing_(person)" title="Hearing (person)">
        deaf and hearing educators
       </a>
       , and mostly follow the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       of the oral language—or, more precisely, of the written form of the oral language. They have been mainly used in
       <a href="/wiki/Deaf_education" title="Deaf education">
        deaf education
       </a>
       in an effort to "represent English on the hands" and by sign language
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreting" title="Interpreting">
        interpreters
       </a>
       in K-12 schools, although they have had some influence on Deaf sign languages where their implementation was widespread.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manually_coded_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It is unknown when the first attempts were made to represent an oral language with gesture. Indeed, some have speculated that oral languages may have evolved from sign languages, and there may be undocumented cases in history when vocal and signed modes of a language existed side by side. It is not uncommon for people to develop gestures to replace words or phrases in contexts where speech is not possible or not permitted, such as in a
       <a href="/wiki/Television_studio" title="Television studio">
        television studio
       </a>
       , but these are usually limited in scope and rarely develop into complete representations of an oral language. One of the most elaborated examples of this kind of auxiliary manual system is
       <a href="/wiki/Warlpiri_Sign_Language" title="Warlpiri Sign Language">
        Warlpiri Sign Language
       </a>
       , a complete signed mode of
       <a href="/wiki/Warlpiri_language" title="Warlpiri language">
        spoken Warlpiri
       </a>
       which was developed by an
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">
        Indigenous
       </a>
       community in
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Australia" title="Central Australia">
        central Australia
       </a>
       due to cultural proscriptions against speech. Sign language linguists usually make a distinction between these
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auxiliary_sign_language" title="Auxiliary sign language">
        auxiliary sign languages
       </a>
       and manually coded languages; the latter are specifically designed for use in Deaf education, and usually represent the written form of the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In seventh century England, the years of (672-735CE),
       <a href="/wiki/Bede" title="Bede">
        Venerable Bede
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">
        Benedictine
       </a>
       monk, proposed a system for representing the letters of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       on the fingers called, fingerspelling.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monastic_sign_language" title="Monastic sign language">
        Monastic sign languages
       </a>
       used throughout
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_Europe" title="Medieval Europe">
        medieval Europe
       </a>
       used
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manual_alphabet" title="Manual alphabet">
        manual alphabets
       </a>
       as well as signs, and were capable of representing a written language, if one had enough patience. Aside from the commonly understood rationale of observing "
       <a href="/wiki/Vow_of_silence" title="Vow of silence">
        vows of silence
       </a>
       ", they also served as
       <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">
        mnemonics
       </a>
       (memory aids) for preachers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These manual alphabets began to be used to teach the deaf children of royalty in 17th century Spain. Such alphabets are in widespread use today by signing deaf communities for representing words or phrases of the oral language used in their part of the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest known attempt to develop a complete signed mode of a language which could be used to teach deaf children was by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abb%C3%A9_de_l%27%C3%89p%C3%A9e" title="Abbé de l'Épée">
        Abbé de l'Épée
       </a>
       , an educator from 18th century France. While the Deaf community already used a sign language (now known as
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French_Sign_Language" title="Old French Sign Language">
        Old French Sign Language
       </a>
       ), Épée thought it must be primitive, and set about designing a complete visual-gestural system to represent the concepts of religion and law that he wanted to impart to his pupils. His system of
       <i>
        signes méthodiques
       </i>
       (usually known in English as "Methodical Signs") was quite idiosyncratic, and although it wasn't a strict representation of French, its success laid the groundwork for the "
       <a href="#Signed_oral_languages">
        signed oral languages
       </a>
       " of today. The real proliferation of such systems occurred in the latter half of the 20th century, and by the 1980s, manually coded languages were the dominant form of communication used by teachers and interpreters in classrooms with deaf students in many parts of the world. Most sign language "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreting" title="Interpreting">
        interpreting
       </a>
       " seen on television in the 1970s and 1980s would have in fact been a
       <a href="/wiki/Transliteration" title="Transliteration">
        transliteration
       </a>
       of an oral language into a manually coded language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The emerging
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Recognition_of_sign_languages" title="Recognition of sign languages">
        recognition of sign languages
       </a>
       in recent times has curbed the growth of manually coded languages, and in many places, interpreting and educational services now favor the use of the natural sign languages of the Deaf community. In some parts of the world, MCLs continue to be developed and supported by state institutions; a contemporary example is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_Sign_Language" title="Arabic Sign Language">
        Arabic Sign Language
       </a>
       . Some MCL systems (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Paget_Gorman_Sign_System" title="Paget Gorman Sign System">
        Paget Gorman Sign System
       </a>
       ) have survived by shifting their focus from deaf education to people with other kinds of communication needs.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms">
        Criticisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manually_coded_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The use of MCLs is controversial, and has been opposed since Épée's time by "
       <a href="/wiki/Oralism" title="Oralism">
        oralists
       </a>
       " who believe Deaf people should speak, lipread and use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hearing_aids" title="Hearing aids">
        hearing aids
       </a>
       rather than sign—and on the other side, by members of the ASL Community (see
       <a href="/wiki/Deaf_culture" title="Deaf culture">
        Deaf culture
       </a>
       ) who resist a wide or exclusive application of MCLs for both philosophical and practical reasons. English is not fully able to express the Deaf Experience, and just as written forms of spoken languages are useful but cumbersome for daily communication, these manual codes cannot supplant a natural Signed Language. Nevertheless elements of these systems have had some influence on deaf sign languages. (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contact_Sign" title="Contact Sign">
        Contact Sign
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Research in the U.S. has shown that
       <a href="/wiki/Manually_coded_English" title="Manually coded English">
        manually coded English
       </a>
       is usually applied incompletely and inconsistently in classrooms: Hearing teachers tend to "cut corners" by not signing word endings and "
       <a href="/wiki/Function_word" title="Function word">
        function words
       </a>
       ", most likely because they slow down the pace and distort the phrasing of the teacher's natural speech. The result is a kind of "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pidgin_Sign_English" title="Pidgin Sign English">
        Pidgin Sign English
       </a>
       " which lacks the grammatical complexity of both English and
       <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">
        American Sign Language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Major_approaches">
        Major approaches
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manually_coded_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major approaches">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There have been many different approaches to manually coding oral languages. Some consist of
       <a href="/wiki/Fingerspelling" title="Fingerspelling">
        fingerspelling
       </a>
       everything, a technique sometimes known in English as the "Rochester method" after Rochester School for the Deaf in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
        New York
       </a>
       where it was used from 1878 until the 1940s. While most MCLs are slower than spoken or sign languages, this method is especially so, and in modern times is generally considered not to be accessible to children. However, some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deafblind" title="Deafblind">
        deafblind
       </a>
       people still communicate primarily using the Rochester Method. Most manually coded languages can accommodate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simultaneous_Communication" title="Simultaneous Communication">
        Simultaneous Communication
       </a>
       —that is, signing and speaking at the same time—although the natural pace of speech may need to be slowed down at times.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Paget_Gorman_Sign_System" title="Paget Gorman Sign System">
        Paget Gorman Sign System
       </a>
       (PGSS) is an MCL that began development in the 1930s by Sir Richard Paget. He studied extant sign languages and looked to create an easier way to understand signs that were pantomimic in nature. He worked with Grace Paget (his wife) and Pierre Gorman, who both took over his work after his death in 1955. Paget published a book in 1951 focusing on children's vocabulary that included 900 signs.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1964, PGSS was taught for the first time to a group of deaf adults in a experiment. It evolved from education for the deaf to teaching those with speech and language disorders. New systems were developed for deaf adults to transition into
       <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">
        British Sign Language
       </a>
       (BSL), thus causing the pivot in use.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       PGSS currently has an estimated 56,000 word combinations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Signed_oral_languages">
        Signed oral languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manually_coded_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Signed oral languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       These systems ("
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Signed_English" title="Signed English">
        Signed English
       </a>
       ", "Signed German" and so on) were the vehicle for the world-wide explosion of MCLs in deaf education in the second half of the 20th century, and are what is generally meant by the phrase "manually coded language" today. They aim to be a word-for-word representation of the written form of an oral language, and accordingly require the development of an enormous vocabulary. They usually achieve this by taking signs ("
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       ") from the local deaf
       <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
        sign language
       </a>
       as a base, then adding specially-created signs for words and word endings that don't exist in the deaf sign language, often using "initializations", and filling in any gaps with
       <a href="/wiki/Fingerspelling" title="Fingerspelling">
        fingerspelling
       </a>
       . Thus "Signed English" in America (based on
       <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">
        ASL
       </a>
       ) has a lexicon quite different from "Signed English" in
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Britain
       </a>
       (based on
       <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">
        BSL
       </a>
       ), as well as the Signed Englishes of Ireland, Australasia and South Africa. "
       <a href="/wiki/Signing_Exact_English" title="Signing Exact English">
        Signing Exact English
       </a>
       " (SEE2) was developed in the United States in 1969, has also been taught around the world, and is now used in deaf schools in
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       , and taught in classes by the Singapore Association for the Deaf.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Mouth.E2.80.93Hand_Systems">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mouth–Hand_Systems">
        Mouth–Hand Systems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manually_coded_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mouth–Hand Systems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another widespread approach is to visually represent the
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       (sounds) of an oral language, rather than using signs for the words. These systems are sometimes known as "Mouth Hand Systems" (MHS). An early example was developed in Denmark in 1903 by Georg Forchhammer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others include the Assisted Kinemes Alphabet (Belgium) and a
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       system developed in 1935 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jabar_Baghtcheban" title="Jabar Baghtcheban">
        Jabar Baghtcheban
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       —in addition to the most widespread MHS worldwide,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cued_Speech" title="Cued Speech">
        Cued Speech
       </a>
       . As the entire set of phonemes for an oral language is small (English has 35 to 45, depending on the
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       ), an MHS is relatively easy to adapt for other languages. As of 2006, 60 languages or dialects have Cued Speech systems, though many are not in use or in marginal use.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cued Speech can be seen as a manual supplement to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lipreading" title="Lipreading">
        lipreading
       </a>
       . A small number of hand shapes (representing
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       ) and locations near the mouth (representing
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       ) differentiate between sounds not distinguishable from on the lips; in
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tonal languages
       </a>
       , the inclination and movement of the hand follows the tone. When viewed together with lip patterns, the gestures render all
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       of the oral language intelligible visually.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cued Speech is not traditionally referred to as a manually coded language; although it was developed with the same aims as the signed oral languages, to improve English language literacy in Deaf children, it follows the sounds rather than the written form of the oral language. Thus, speakers with different accents will "cue" differently.
      </p>

      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Contact_sign" title="Contact sign">
         Contact sign
        </a>
        – A variety or style of signing arising from contact between a spoken or manually coded language and a deaf sign language.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manual_alphabet" title="Manual alphabet">
         Manual alphabet
        </a>
        – a means of representing the written alphabet of an oral language, but often a central part of natural sign languages.
