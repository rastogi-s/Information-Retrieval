          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'v' in 'vie'">
            v
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">
            ər
           </span>
          </span>
          ,
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="'v' in 'vie'">
            v
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">

           </span>

          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) are articulated with the tongue against or close to the superior
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
        alveolar ridge
       </a>
       , which is called that because it contains the
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_alveolus" title="Dental alveolus">
        alveoli
       </a>
       (the sockets) of the superior teeth. Alveolar consonants may be articulated with the tip of the tongue (the
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical consonants
       </a>
       ), as in English, or with the flat of the tongue just above the tip (the "blade" of the tongue; called
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminal consonants
       </a>
       ), as in French and Spanish. The laminal alveolar articulation is often mistakenly called
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       , because the tip of the tongue can be seen near to or touching the teeth. However, it is the rearmost point of contact that defines the place of articulation; this is where the oral cavity ends, and it is the resonant space of the oral cavity that gives consonants and vowels their characteristics.
       <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">
        The
        <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
         International Phonetic Alphabet
        </a>
        (IPA) does not have separate symbols for the alveolar consonants. Rather, the same symbol is used for all
        <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
         coronal
        </a>
        places of articulation that are not
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         palatalized
        </a>
        like English
        <a href="/wiki/Palato-alveolar_consonant" title="Palato-alveolar consonant">
         palato-alveolar
        </a>
        <i>
         sh
        </i>
        , or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflex" title="Retroflex">
         retroflex
        </a>
        .
       </span>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       To disambiguate, the
       <i>
        bridge
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪, t̪, n̪, l̪]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        etc.
       </i>
       ) may be used for a dental consonant, or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retracted_(phonetics)" title="Retracted (phonetics)">
        under-bar
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̠, t̠, n̠, l̠]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        etc.
       </i>
       ) may be used for the
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolars
       </a>
       . Note that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪]
       </span>
       differs from dental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       in that the former is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       and the latter is not.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̠]
       </span>
       differs from postalveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       in being unpalatalized.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The bare letters
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s, t, n, l]
       </span>
       , etc. cannot be assumed to specifically represent alveolars. The language may not make such distinctions, such that two or more coronal
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        places of articulation
       </a>
       are found
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophonically
       </a>
       , or the transcription may simply be too broad to distinguish dental from alveolar. If it is necessary to specify a consonant as alveolar, a diacritic from the
       <a href="/wiki/Extensions_to_the_International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Extensions to the International Phonetic Alphabet">
        Extended IPA
       </a>
       may be used:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s͇, t͇, n͇, l͇]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        etc.
       </i>
       , though that could also mean extra-retracted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The letters ⟨s, t, n, l⟩ are frequently called 'alveolar', and the language examples below are all alveolar sounds.
      </p>
      <p>
       (The Extended IPA diacritic was devised for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_pathology" title="Speech pathology">
        speech pathology
       </a>
       and is frequently used to mean "alveolarized", as in the labioalveolar sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p͇, b͇, m͇, f͇, v͇]
       </span>
       , where the lower lip contacts the alveolar ridge.)
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_alveolars">
        Lack of alveolars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alveolar_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lack of alveolars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The alveolar or dental consonants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n]
       </span>
       are, along with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       , the most common consonants in human languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nonetheless, there are a few languages that lack them. A few languages on
       <a href="/wiki/Bougainville_Island" title="Bougainville Island">
        Bougainville Island
       </a>
       and around
       <a href="/wiki/Puget_Sound" title="Puget Sound">
        Puget Sound
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Makah_language" title="Makah language">
        Makah
       </a>
       , lack nasals and therefore
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n]
       </span>
       , but have
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       . Colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
        Samoan
       </a>
       , however, lacks both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n]
       </span>
       , but it has a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_alveolar_approximant" title="Lateral alveolar approximant">
        lateral alveolar approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       . (Samoan words written with
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       are pronounced with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       in colloquial speech.) In Standard
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       is an allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       exist.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Labioalveolar_consonants">
        <span id="Labioalveolar">
         Labioalveolar
        </span>
        consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alveolar_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Labioalveolar consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In labioalveolars, the lower lip contacts the alveolar ridge. Such sounds are typically the result of a severe overbite. In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensions_to_the_IPA" title="Extensions to the IPA">
        Extensions to the IPA
       </a>
       for disordered speech, they are transcribed with the alveolar diacritic on labial letters: ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        m͇ p͇ b͇ f͇ v͇
       </span>
       ⟩.
      </p>
