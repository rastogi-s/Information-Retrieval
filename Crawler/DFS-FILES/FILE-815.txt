      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Llanito
        </b>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <b>
         Yanito
        </b>
       </i>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [jaˈnito]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       heavily laced with words from English and other languages such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genoese_language" title="Genoese language">
        Genoese
       </a>
       , spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" title="British overseas territory">
        British overseas territory
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-creole_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-creole-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It consists of a mix of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       , marked by a great deal of
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code switching
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglicisms" title="Anglicisms">
        anglicisms
       </a>
       and loanwords from many other Mediterranean languages and dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levey2008_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Levey2008-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       word meaning "little plain".
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltarians" title="Gibraltarians">
        Gibraltarians
       </a>
       also call themselves
       <i>
        Llanitos
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language">
        Language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Andalusian Spanish from the surrounding
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Campo_de_Gibraltar" title="Campo de Gibraltar">
         Campo de Gibraltar
        </a>
       </i>
       is the main constituent of
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       , but it is also heavily influenced by British English. However, it borrows words and expressions of many other languages, with over 500 words of
       <a href="/wiki/Genoese_dialect" title="Genoese dialect">
        Genoese
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       ) medieval dialect (with additionally some of
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       origin).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethno_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ethno-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its other main language constituents are
       <a href="/wiki/Maltese_language" title="Maltese language">
        Maltese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       . It often also involves
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code-switching
       </a>
       from Spanish to English. Some
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       words are also widely used in the neighbouring Spanish town of
       <a href="/wiki/La_L%C3%ADnea_de_la_Concepci%C3%B3n" title="La Línea de la Concepción">
        La Línea de la Concepción
       </a>
       (due to the influx of people from La Línea working in Gibraltar over many years).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-linea_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-linea-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       To some outsiders who only speak either English or Spanish,
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       may sound incomprehensible, as speakers appear to
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        switch languages in mid-sentence
       </a>
       , but to people who are
       <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">
        bilingual
       </a>
       in both languages, it can sound interesting and unique. One feature of the language is the pronunciation of English words with an Andalusian flavour. For example, bacon is pronounced
       <i>
        beki
       </i>
       ; cake,
       <i>
        keki
       </i>
       ; battery,
       <i>
        batteria
       </i>
       ; a policeman is known as
       <i>
        la parma
       </i>
       , and porridge is called
       <i>
        kuecaro
       </i>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanicization" title="Hispanicization">
        hispanicization
       </a>
       of the brand
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quaker_Oats" title="Quaker Oats">
        Quaker Oats
       </a>
       ). Most Gibraltarians, especially those with higher education, also speak standard Spanish with either
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusian_dialect" title="Andalusian dialect">
        Andalusian
       </a>
       or normative pronunciations and standard English of the British English
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variety
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the Italian scholar Giulio Vignoli, Llanito originally, in the first decades of the 19th century, was full of Genoese words, later substituted mainly by Spanish words and by some English words.
      </p>
      <p>
       Llanito has significant Jewish influence, because of a
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Gibraltar" title="History of the Jews in Gibraltar">
        long standing Jewish population in Gibraltar
       </a>
       . They introduced words and expressions from
       <a href="/wiki/Haketia" title="Haketia">
        Haketia
       </a>
       , a largely extinct Judeo-Spanish language spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardic
       </a>
       communities of Northern
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tetuan" title="Tetuan">
        Tetuan
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangiers" title="Tangiers">
        Tangiers
       </a>
       and the Spanish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exclave" title="Exclave">
        exclaves
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       in North Africa.
      </p>
      <p>
       Even though Llanito is seldom written, a Llanito dictionary,
       <i>
        Diccionario Yanito
       </i>
       , was published in 1978 by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Cavilla&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manuel Cavilla (page does not exist)">
        Manuel Cavilla
       </a>
       , and in 2001
       <a href="/wiki/Tito_Vallejo" title="Tito Vallejo">
        Tito Vallejo
       </a>
       published
       <i>
        The Yanito Dictionary. Including Place Names and Yanito Anecdotes
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Core_elements_of_Llanito">
        Core elements of Llanito
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Core elements of Llanito">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although Llanito is largely based on the colloquial Spanish spoken in the Campo de Gibraltar, there are numerous elements beyond code-switching to English which make it unique. These are as follows.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anglicisms">
        Anglicisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anglicisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Echegarai
        </i>
        : Watchman or Guard. From English
        <i>
         "Check Gate"
        </i>
        influenced by the Basque surname
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Etxegarai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Etxegarai (page does not exist)">
         Etxegarai
        </a>
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Echegaray" title="Echegaray">
         Echegaray
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Focona
        </i>
        : Gibraltar border with Spain. From English
        <i>
         "Four Corners"
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calques_from_English_to_Spanish">
        Calques from English to Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Calques from English to Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Te llamo pa'trás
        </i>
        : Literal translation into Spanish of English phrase "I'll call you back". In standard Spanish, one would normally say "I'll return your call" (Te devuelvo la llamada", "Te devolveré la llamada").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calques_from_Spanish_to_English">
        Calques from Spanish to English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Calques from Spanish to English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Don't give me the tin
        </i>
        : Literal translation of Spanish expression
        <i>
         "No me des la lata"
        </i>
        , meaning "stop annoying me".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         What a cachonfinger!
        </i>
        : This is a humorous expression based on the Spanish word
        <i>
         "cachondeo"
        </i>
        which means "piss-take" in British English. The end of the word "deo" is how the word
        <i>
         "dedo"
        </i>
        (finger) is pronounced in colloquial Spanish, thus
        <i>
         cachonfinger
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_expressions">
        Local expressions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Local expressions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tú quien te crees que eres? El hijo del Mebli?
        </i>
        Literally,
        <i>
         "Who do you think you are? The son of the Mebli?"
        </i>
        , as used when someone is acting with excessive self-importance. "Mebli" is a mispronunciation of
        <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Lord_Melville" title="Lord Melville">
         Lord Melville
        </a>
        <sup class="plainlinks noprint Inline-Template" style="vertical-align:text-top;white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a class="external text" href="http://dispenser.info.tm/~dispenser/cgi-bin/dab_solver.py?page=Llanito&amp;editintro=Template:Disambiguation_needed/editintro&amp;client=Template:Dn" rel="nofollow">
           <span title="Link needs disambiguation. (December 2016)">
            disambiguation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Borrowings_from_third_languages">
        Borrowings from third languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Borrowings from third languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Llanito_words_introduced_into_Spain">
        Llanito words introduced into Spain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Llanito words introduced into Spain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many Llanito terms have been introduced into the Andalusian Spanish dialect of the bordering city
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=L%C3%ADnea_de_la_Concepci%C3%B3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Línea de la Concepción (page does not exist)">
        Línea de la Concepción
       </a>
       , where the resulting dialect is known as Linense. However, according to Gibraltarian linguist Tito Vallejo, a few words common throughout Spain may be of Llanito origin, notably "Chachi" meaning "Cool" or "Brilliant" (from
       <a href="/wiki/Winston_Churchill" title="Winston Churchill">
        Winston Churchill
       </a>
       ) and "Napia" meaning "Big Nose" from
       <a href="/wiki/Lord_Napier" title="Lord Napier">
        Lord Napier
       </a>
       . Churchill was associated with foreign imports from the United Kingdom which were highly sought in Gibraltar and, according to Vallejo, Lord Napier had a particularly big nose.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, linguists also propose
       <i>
        chachi
       </i>
       to be a contraction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cal%C3%B3_language" title="Caló language">
        Caló
       </a>
       term
       <i>
        chachipén
       </i>
       meaning "truth", since this language is the source of a significant proportion of Spanish slang.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Broadcasting">
        Broadcasting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Broadcasting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_Broadcasting_Corporation" title="Gibraltar Broadcasting Corporation">
        Gibraltar Broadcasting Corporation
       </a>
       has also aired some programmes in Llanito including
       <i>
        Talk About Town
       </i>
       – a discussion series in which three presenters discuss local affairs, from the need to replace a street sign to important political affairs.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Pepe's Pot
       </i>
       was a cookery programme/program which also used Llanito.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">
        Film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A documentary film, "People of the Rock: The Llanitos of Gibraltar"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (2011) discusses Llanito speech characteristics, history and culture. Notable interviews include Pepe Palmero (of GBC's "Pepe's Pot"),
       <a href="/wiki/Kaiane_Aldorino" title="Kaiane Aldorino">
        Kaiane Aldorino
       </a>
       (Miss World 2009) and Tito Vallejo (author of "The Llanito Dictionary").
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demonym">
        Demonym
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Demonym">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The official
       <a href="/wiki/Demonym" title="Demonym">
        demonym
       </a>
       of Gibraltar is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gibraltarians" title="Gibraltarians">
         Gibraltarians
        </a>
       </i>
       . However, the people of Gibraltar may also be referred to as
       <i>
        Llanitos
       </i>
       (female
       <i>
        Llanitas
       </i>
       ). This term is commonly used in the neighbouring towns of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_L%C3%ADnea" title="La Línea">
        La Línea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/San_Roque,_C%C3%A1diz" title="San Roque, Cádiz">
        San Roque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Algeciras" title="Algeciras">
        Algeciras
       </a>
       and the rest of the
       <a href="/wiki/Campo_de_Gibraltar" title="Campo de Gibraltar">
        Campo de Gibraltar
       </a>
       , as well as in Gibraltar itself. When speaking in English, the people of Gibraltar tend to use the word
       <i>
        Gibraltarians
       </i>
       to refer to themselves but when speaking in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       they prefer to use the word
       <i>
        Llanitos
       </i>
       rather than the Spanish name for their official demonym,
       <i>
        Gibraltareños
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Truncation" title="Truncation">
        truncated
       </a>
       term
       <i>
        Llanis
       </i>
       is also used by the people of Gibraltar, where it can be heard all around the territory and chanted in songs during the annual
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar_National_Day" title="Gibraltar National Day">
        Gibraltar National Day
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanito&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The etymology of the term
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       is uncertain. In Spanish,
       <i>
        Llanito
       </i>
       means "little flatland" and has been interpreted as "people of the flatlands". It is thought that the inhabitants of La Línea with important social and economic ties with Gibraltar, were actually the first to be referred to as
       <i>
        Llanitos
       </i>
       since La Línea lies in the plain and marsh land surrounding The Rock.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another theory for the origin of the word is that it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">
        diminutive
       </a>
       of the name
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gianni" title="Gianni">
         Gianni
        </a>
       </i>
       : "gianito", pronounced in Genoese slang with the "g" as "j".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the late 18th century 34% of the male civilian population of Gibraltar came from
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        Gianni
       </i>
       was a common
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_name" title="Italian name">
        Italian forename
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To this day, nearly 20% of Gibraltarian surnames are Italian in origin.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">

       </sup>
      </p>
