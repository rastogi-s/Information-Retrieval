    Constituent (linguistics)
   </h1>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntactic
       </a>
       analysis, a
       <b>
        constituent
       </b>
       is a word or a group of words that function(s) as a single unit within a hierarchical structure. The analysis of constituent structure is associated mainly with
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">
        phrase structure grammars
       </a>
       , although
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammars
       </a>
       also allow sentence structure to be broken down into constituent parts. The constituent structure of sentences is identified using
       <i>
        constituency tests
       </i>
       . These tests manipulate some portion of a sentence and based on the result, clues are delivered about the
       <a href="/wiki/Immediate_constituent_analysis" title="Immediate constituent analysis">
        immediate constituent structure
       </a>
       of the sentence. Many constituents are
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrases
       </a>
       . A phrase is a sequence of one or more words (in some theories two or more) built around a
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head
       </a>
       lexical item and working as a unit within a sentence. A word sequence is shown to be a phrase/constituent if it exhibits one or more of the behaviors discussed below.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Constituency_tests">
        Constituency tests
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constituency tests">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Constituency tests are diagnostics used to identify the constituent structure of sentences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are numerous constituency tests applied to English sentences, such as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        1.
        <a href="/wiki/Topicalization" title="Topicalization">
         topicalization
        </a>
        (fronting),
       </li>
       <li>
        2.
        <a href="/wiki/Cleft_sentence" title="Cleft sentence">
         clefting
        </a>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        3. pseudoclefting,
       </li>
       <li>
        4. pro-form substitution (replacement),
       </li>
       <li>
        5.
        <a href="/wiki/Answer_ellipsis" title="Answer ellipsis">
         answer ellipsis
        </a>
        (question test),
       </li>
       <li>
        6. passivization,
       </li>
       <li>
        7. omission (deletion),
       </li>
       <li>
        8.
        <a href="/wiki/Coordination_(linguistics)" title="Coordination (linguistics)">
         coordination
        </a>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        etc.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These tests are rough-and-ready tools which grammarians employ to reveal clues about syntactic structure. A word of caution is warranted when employing these tests, since they often deliver contradictory results. Some syntacticians even arrange the tests on a scale of reliability, with less-reliable tests treated as useful to confirm constituency though not sufficient on their own.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bakovic_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bakovic-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Failing to pass a single test does not mean that the unit is not a constituent, and conversely, passing a single test does not mean necessarily that the unit is a constituent. It is best to apply as many tests as possible to a given unit in order to prove or to rule out its status as a constituent.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Topicalization_.28fronting.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Topicalization_(fronting)">
        Topicalization (fronting)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Topicalization (fronting)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Topicalization" title="Topicalization">
        Topicalization
       </a>
       involves moving the test sequence to the front of the sentence. It is a simple movement operation:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          He is going to attend another course
          <b>
           to improve his English
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           To improve his English
          </b>
          , he is going to attend another course.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clefting">
        Clefting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Clefting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clefting" title="Clefting">
        Clefting
       </a>
       involves placing a sequence of words X within the structure beginning with
       <i>
        It is/was
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        It was X that...
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          She bought
          <b>
           a pair of gloves with silk embroidery
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          It was
          <b>
           a pair of gloves with silk embroidery
          </b>
          that she bought.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudoclefting">
        Pseudoclefting
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pseudoclefting">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Pseudoclefting (also
       <b>
        preposing
       </b>
       ) is similar to clefting in that it puts emphasis on a certain phrase in a sentence. It involves inserting a sequence of words before
       <i>
        is/are what
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        is/are who
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          She bought
          <b>
           a pair of gloves with silk embroidery
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           A pair of gloves with silk embroidery
          </b>
          is what she bought.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span id="Pro-form_substitution_.28replacement.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-form_substitution_(replacement)">
        Pro-form substitution (replacement)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pro-form substitution (replacement)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-form" title="Pro-form">
        Pro-form
       </a>
       substitution, or replacement, involves replacing the test constituent with the appropriate pro-form (e.g. pronoun). Substitution normally involves using a definite pro-form like
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        there
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        here
       </i>
       , etc. in place of a phrase or a clause. If such a change yields a grammatical sentence where the general structure has not been altered, then the test sequence is a constituent:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          I don't know
          <b>
           the man who is sleeping in the car
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          *I don't know
          <b>
           him who is sleeping in the car
          </b>
          . (ungrammatical)
         </dd>
         <dd>
          I don't know
          <b>
           him
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The ungrammaticality of the first changed version and the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammaticality" title="Grammaticality">
        grammaticality
       </a>
       of the second one demonstrates that the whole sequence,
       <i>
        the man who is sleeping in the car
       </i>
       , and not just
       <i>
        the man
       </i>
       is a constituent functioning as a unit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Answer_ellipsis_.28answer_fragments.2C_question_test.2C_standalone_test.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Answer_ellipsis_(answer_fragments,_question_test,_standalone_test)">
        Answer ellipsis (answer fragments, question test, standalone test)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Answer ellipsis (answer fragments, question test, standalone test)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Answer_ellipsis" title="Answer ellipsis">
        answer ellipsis
       </a>
       test refers to the ability of a sequence of words to stand alone as a reply to a question. It is often used to test the constituency of a verbal phrase but can also be applied to other phrases:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          What did you do yesterday? -
          <b>
           Worked on my new project
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          What did you do yesterday? -
          <b>
           *Worked on
          </b>
          . (unacceptable, so
          <i>
           worked on
          </i>
          is not a constituent).
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">
        Linguists do not agree whether passing the answer ellipsis test is sufficient, though at a minimum they agree that it can help confirm the results of another constituency test.
       </span>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Passivization">
        Passivization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Passivization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Passivization involves changing an active sentence to a passive sentence, or vice versa. The
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       of the active sentence is changed to the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       of the corresponding passive sentence:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           A car driving too fast
          </b>
          nearly hit
          <b>
           the little dog
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           The little dog
          </b>
          was nearly hit by
          <b>
           a car driving too fast
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In case passivization results in a grammatical sentence, the phrases which have been moved can be regarded as constituents.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Omission_.28deletion.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Omission_(deletion)">
        Omission (deletion)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Omission (deletion)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Omission checks whether a sequence of words can be omitted without influencing the grammaticality of the sentence — in most cases, local or temporal adverbials can be safely omitted and thus qualify as constituents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Fred relaxes
          <b>
           at night on his couch
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          Fred relaxes
          <b>
           on his couch
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          Fred relaxes
          <b>
           at night
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Since they can be omitted, the prepositional phrases
       <i>
        at night
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        on his couch
       </i>
       are constituents.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coordination">
        Coordination
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Coordination">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Coordination_(linguistics)" title="Coordination (linguistics)">
        coordination
       </a>
       test assumes that only constituents can be coordinated, i.e., joined by means of a coordinator such as
       <i>
        and
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          He enjoys
          <b>
           [writing sentences]
          </b>
          and
          <b>
           [reading them]
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           [He enjoys writing]
          </b>
          and
          <b>
           [she enjoys reading]
          </b>
          sentences.
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           [He enjoys]
          </b>
          but
          <b>
           [she hates]
          </b>
          writing sentences.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Based on the fact that
       <i>
        writing sentences
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        reading them
       </i>
       are coordinated using
       <i>
        and
       </i>
       , one can conclude that they are constituents. The validity of the coordination test is challenged by additional data, however. The latter two sentences, which are instances of so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Right_node_raising" title="Right node raising">
        right node raising
       </a>
       , suggest that the sequences in bold should be understood as constituents. Most grammars do not view sequences such as
       <i>
        He enjoys
       </i>
       to the exclusion of the VP
       <i>
        writing sentences
       </i>
       as a constituent. Thus while the coordination test is widely employed as a diagnostic for constituent structure, it is faced with major difficulties and is therefore perhaps the least reliable of all the tests mentioned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Constituency_tests_and_disambiguation">
        Constituency tests and disambiguation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Constituency tests and disambiguation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Syntactic ambiguity characterizes sentences which can be interpreted in different ways depending solely on how one perceives syntactic connections between words and arranges them into phrases. Possible interpretations of the sentence
       <i>
        They killed the man with a gun
       </i>
       are:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          'The man was shot.'
         </dd>
         <dd>
          'The man who was killed had a gun with him.'
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The ambiguity of this sentence results from two possible arrangements into constituents:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          They killed [the man] [with a gun].
         </dd>
         <dd>
          They killed [the man with a gun].
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In the first sentence,
       <i>
        with a gun
       </i>
       is an independent constituent with
       <a href="/wiki/Instrumental_case" title="Instrumental case">
        instrumental
       </a>
       meaning. In the second sentence, it is embedded in the noun phrase
       <i>
        the man with a gun
       </i>
       and is modifying the noun
       <i>
        man
       </i>
       . The autonomy of the unit
       <i>
        with a gun
       </i>
       in the first interpretation can be tested by the answer ellipsis test:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          How did they kill the man? -
          <b>
           With a gun
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       However, the same test can be used to prove that
       <i>
        the man with a gun
       </i>
       in the second sentence should be treated as a unit:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Who(m) did they kill? -
          <b>
           The man with a gun
          </b>
          .
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The ability of constituency tests to disambiguate certain sentences in this manner bears witness to their utility. Most if not all syntacticians employ constituency tests in some form or another to arrive at the structures that they assign to sentences.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Competing_theories">
        Competing theories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Constituent_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Competing theories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Alternate theoretical approaches to syntax make different assumptions regarding what is considered a constituent. In mainstream
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">
        phrase structure grammar
       </a>
       (and its derivatives), individual words are constituents in and of themselves as well as being parts of other constituents, whereas in
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       certain core words in each phrase are not a constituent by themselves, but only members of a phrasal constituent. The following trees show the same sentence in two different theoretical representations, with a phrase structure representation on the left and a dependency grammar representation on the right. In both trees, a constituent is understood to be the entire tree or any labelled subtree (a node plus all the nodes dominated by that node); note that words like
       <i>
        killed
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        with
       </i>
       , for instance, form subtrees (and are considered constituents) in the phrase structure representation but not in the dependency structure representation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Theykilledthemanwithagun-1b.jpg" title="Illustrating constituency and dependency">
           <img alt="Illustrating constituency and dependency" data-file-height="270" data-file-width="578" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Theykilledthemanwithagun-1b.jpg" width="578"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
