      <p>
       <b>
        Istriot
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       spoken by about 400 people in the southwestern part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istrian
       </a>
       Peninsula in
       <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">
        Croatia
       </a>
       , particularly in
       <a href="/wiki/Rovinj" title="Rovinj">
        Rovinj
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vodnjan" title="Vodnjan">
        Vodnjan
       </a>
       . It should not be confused with the
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language#Regional_variants" title="Venetian language">
        Istrian dialect
       </a>
       of the Venetian language.
      </p>

      <p>
       Istriot is a Romance language related to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
        Ladin
       </a>
       populations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
        Alps
       </a>
       . According to the Italian linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Bartoli" title="Matteo Bartoli">
        Matteo Bartoli
       </a>
       , the Ladin area used to extend – until the year 1000 AD – from southern
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istria
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">
        Friuli
       </a>
       and eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its classification remained mostly unclear, in 2017 it was classified by the "Max Planck Institute for the Science of Human History" with the
       <a href="/wiki/Dalmatian_language" title="Dalmatian language">
        Dalmatian Language
       </a>
       in the Dalmatian Romance subgroup,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       yet due to the specificities of the language, which has always had a very limited number of speakers. Istriot was also viewed:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        as an independent Northern Italian language, belonging neither to the
        <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
         Venetian language
        </a>
        nor to the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">
         Gallo-Italic
        </a>
        group (opinion shared by linguists
        <a href="/wiki/Tullio_De_Mauro" title="Tullio De Mauro">
         Tullio De Mauro
        </a>
        and
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maurizio_Dardano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maurizio Dardano (page does not exist)">
         Maurizio Dardano
        </a>
        );
       </li>
       <li>
        as a variety of the
        <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
         Rhaeto-Romance languages
        </a>
        by the Istriot
        <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Ive" title="de:Antonio Ive">
         Antonio Ive
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTZ_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-PTZ-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        as an independent language of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian" title="Italo-Dalmatian">
         Italo-Dalmatian
        </a>
        group
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        as an autochthonous
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
         Romance language
        </a>
        heavily influenced by
        <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
         Venetian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Friulian_language" title="Friulian language">
         Friulian
        </a>
        and Slavic superstrates by
        <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirko_Deanovi%C4%87" title="de:Mirko Deanović">
         Mirko Deanović
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PTZ_5-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-PTZ-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istria
       </a>
       was a region of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy_(1861-1946)" title="Kingdom of Italy (1861-1946)">
        Kingdom of Italy
       </a>
       , Istriot was considered by the authorities as a subdialect of Venetian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, its speakers never referred to it as "Istriot"; it had six names after the six towns where it was spoken. In
       <a href="/wiki/Vodnjan" title="Vodnjan">
        Vodnjan
       </a>
       it was named "Bumbaro", in
       <a href="/wiki/Bale,_Croatia" title="Bale, Croatia">
        Bale
       </a>
       "Vallese", in Rovinj "Rovignese", in
       <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0i%C5%A1an" title="Šišan">
        Šišan
       </a>
       "Sissanese", in
       <a href="/wiki/Fa%C5%BEana" title="Fažana">
        Fažana
       </a>
       "Fasanese" and in
       <a href="/wiki/Gali%C5%BEana" title="Galižana">
        Galižana
       </a>
       "Gallesanese". The term
       <i>
        Istriot
       </i>
       was coined by the 19th-century Italian linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Graziadio_Isaia_Ascoli" title="Graziadio Isaia Ascoli">
        Graziadio Isaia Ascoli
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This language is still spoken by few people in the Istriot communities in
       <a href="/wiki/Fertilia" title="Fertilia">
        Fertilia
       </a>
       and Maristella, in
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are about 1,000 speakers left, making it an
       <a href="/wiki/Endangered_language" title="Endangered language">
        endangered language
       </a>
       .
      </p>

         Istriot
        </th>
        <th>
         Italian
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td style="background:#F1F9CA; font-weight: bold;">
         <p>
          La nostra zì oûna longa cal da griebani:
         </p>
         <p>
          i spironi da Monto inda uò salvà, e 'l brasso da Vistro uò rastà scuio pei grutoni pioûn alti del mar, ca ruzaghia sta tiera viecia-stara. Da senpro i signemo pissi sensa nom, ca da sui sa prucoûra 'l bucon par guodi la veîta leîbara del cucal, pastadi dala piova da Punente a da Livante e cume i uleîi mai incalmadi. Fra ste carme zì stà la nostra salvissa, cume i riboni a sa salva dal dulfeîn fra i scagni del sico da San Damian; el nostro pan, nato gra li gruote, zi stà inbinideî cul sudur sula iera zbruventa da Paloû... e i vemo caminà par oûna longa cal da griebani, c'ancui la riesta lissada dali nostre urme.
         </p>
        </td>
        <td>
         La nostra è una lunga strada irta di sassi:
         <p>
          gli speroni di Monto ci hanno salvato, ed il braccio di Vistro è rimasto scoglio per le grotte poste più in alto del mare, che erode questa antica terra. Da sempre siamo pesciolini che da soli si procurano il boccone per godere la libera vita del gabbiano, oppressi dalla pioggia di Ponente e di Levante come olivi senza innesti. Fra queste insenature è stata la nostra salvezza, come i pagelli si salvano dal delfino fra le tane della secca di San Damiano; il nostro pane, nato tra le grotte, è stato benedetto col sudore nell'aia ribollente di Palù... ed abbiamo camminato per una lunga strada dissestata, che oggi rimane spianate dai nostri passi.
         </p>
        </td>
