      <p>
       <b>
        Bolognese
       </b>
       (in Bolognese:
       <i>
        bulgnaiś
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [bulˈɲaiz]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a dialect of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano_language" title="Emiliano language">
        Emiliano language
       </a>
       , spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       region of Italy and along the border of
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       to the south.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
         dialect
        </a>
       </i>
       is commonly used in reference to all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minority_languages" title="Minority languages">
        minority languages
       </a>
       native to Italy, most of them are not
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and have developed independently from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Bolognese is no exception and so is a dialect of Emiliano-Romagnolo, not Italian.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Bolognese is a dialect of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo">
        Emiliano-Romagnolo
       </a>
       , one of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       family. It shares many common features with other Gallo-Italic languages such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmontese" title="Piedmontese">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       , and it is closer to them than to Italian.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div style="float:right; width:35%; padding:15px; background: #f5f8ff; border: 1px solid blue; margin-left:8px; margin-right:8px;margin-bottom:15px; text-align:center; font-size: small">
       <dl>
        <dd>
         <i>
          "… I say, then, that perhaps those are not wrong who claim that the Bolognese speak a more beautiful language than most, especially since they take many features of their own speech from that of the people who live around them, in Imola, Ferrara and Modena I believe that everybody does this with respect to his own neighbours.... So the above-mentioned citizens of Bologna take a soft, yielding quality from those of Imola, and from the people of Ferrara and Modena, on the other hand, a certain abruptness which is more typical of the Lombards.... If, then, the Bolognese take from all sides, as I have said, it seems reasonable to suggest that their language, tempered by the combination of opposites mentioned above, should achieve a praiseworthy degree of elegance; and this, in my opinion, is beyond doubt true."
         </i>
         <br/>
         (Dante Alighieri, De Vulgari Eloquentia - Liber I, xv, 2-5)
        </dd>
       </dl>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Middle Ages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Bolognese evolved a group of Gallo-Romance languages sharing features with neighbouring northern Italian languages. It developed more distinctly into the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       as a dialecy of the Emiliano-Romagnolo language. During the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       , a number of troubadours composing lyrical poetry were active in Bologna, especially during the 13th century. That served to raise cultural awareness to the possibility of composing songs, poems and other works in vernacular languages. One of the first references to Bolognese as a distinct language was made by
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante Alighieri
       </a>
       , in his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_Vulgari_Eloquentia" title="De Vulgari Eloquentia">
         De Vulgari Eloquentia
        </a>
       </i>
       , written in the beginning of the 14th century.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern">
        Modern
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Modern">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During the boom of interest in linguistic diversity during the 19th century, a number of efforts were made to create vocabularies, grammars, and collections of axioms, folk tales, and literature. The first dictionary was compiled in 1901 by Gaspare Ungarelli, who also attempted to create a writing system using the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_alphabet" title="Italian alphabet">
        Italian alphabet
       </a>
       . A period of stigmatisation followed in the 20th century, where children were punished for speaking the dialect in school,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       as it was considered to be a sign of poor education and etiquette.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1964, Alberto Menarini proposed an alphabet with many of the same letters still used. In recent times, Bolognese has enjoyed a period of rebirth with some words, such as "
       <a href="/wiki/Umarell" title="Umarell">
        Umarell
       </a>
       ", becoming popular beyond Bologna itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bolognese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Here are some prominent features of Bolognese phonology:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Central vowels
       </li>
       <li>
        A phonemic distinction between short vowels and corresponding diphthongs
       </li>
       <li>
        Nucleus vowel and coda consonant length having an inverse relationship
       </li>
       <li>
        Realisation of labio-alveolar consonants
       </li>
       <li>
        Syncopation resulting in complex consonant clusters
       </li>
       <li>
        Frequent slacking of word-final voiceless obstruents
       </li>
       <li>
        More exaggerated intonation than in Italian
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The phonemes of Bolognese are realized phonetically very differently depending on the area in or around Bologna. Much
       <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">
        free variation
       </a>
       occurs in words from complex phonological processes.
      </p>
