   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Realis mood
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
     <span class="mw-redirectedfrom">
      (Redirected from
      <a class="mw-redirect" href="/w/index.php?title=Indicative&amp;redirect=no" title="Indicative">
       Indicative
      </a>
      )
     </span>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        realis mood
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <b>
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          REAL
         </span>
        </span>
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        grammatical mood
       </a>
       which is used principally to indicate that something is a statement of fact; in other words, to express what the speaker considers to be a known state of affairs, as in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Declarative_sentence" title="Declarative sentence">
        declarative sentences
       </a>
       . Most languages have a single realis mood called the
       <b>
        indicative mood
       </b>
       , although some languages have additional realis moods, for example to express different levels of certainty. By contrast, an
       <a href="/wiki/Irrealis_mood" title="Irrealis mood">
        irrealis mood
       </a>
       is used to express something that is not known to be the case in reality.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of the contrast between realis and irrealis moods is seen in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_grammar" title="English grammar">
        English
       </a>
       sentences "He works" and "It is necessary that he work". In the first sentence
       <i>
        works
       </i>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Present_indicative" title="Present indicative">
        present indicative
       </a>
       (realis) form of the verb, and is used to make a direct assertion about the real world. In the second sentence
       <i>
        work
       </i>
       is in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_subjunctive" title="English subjunctive">
        subjunctive mood
       </a>
       , which is an irrealis mood – here
       <i>
        that he work
       </i>
       does not necessarily express a fact about the real world (he could be rejecting necessity and refusing to work), but refers to what would be a desirable state of affairs.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, since mood is a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical category
       </a>
       , referring to the form a verb takes rather than its meaning in a given instance, a given language may use realis forms for a number of purposes other than their principal one of making direct factual statements. For example, many languages use indicative verb forms to ask questions (this is sometimes called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interrogative_mood" title="Interrogative mood">
        interrogative mood
       </a>
       ) and in various other situations where the meaning is in fact of the irrealis type (as in the English "I hope it works", where the indicative
       <i>
        works
       </i>
       is used even though it refers to a desired rather than real state of affairs). The indicative might therefore be defined as the mood used in all instances where a given language does not specifically require the use of some other mood.
      </p>
      <p>
       Realis mood and indicative mood can be indicated by the respective
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        glossing abbreviations
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          REAL
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       and
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          IND
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_English_indicative">
        The English indicative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Realis_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The English indicative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Distinctions between indicative and other moods such as the
       <a href="/wiki/English_subjunctive" title="English subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">
        Modern English
       </a>
       the indicative mood is for statements of actuality or strong probability, and in addition acts as a default mood for all instances which do not require use of a specific mood:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The spine-tailed swift flies faster than any other bird in the world. (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Present_indicative" title="Present indicative">
         present indicative
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        The Missouri and Mississippi Rivers
        <a href="/wiki/Great_Flood_of_1993" title="Great Flood of 1993">
         rose to record heights
        </a>
        in 1993. (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_indicative" title="Past indicative">
         past indicative
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Mid-westerners will remember the flooding for many years to come. (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_indicative" title="Future indicative">
         future indicative
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some forms of the indicative can be used with
       <i>
        do
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        does
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        did
       </i>
       , either for emphasis, or to form questions or negatives. See
       <a href="/wiki/Do-support" title="Do-support">
        <i>
         do
        </i>
        -support
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Other moods existing in English besides the indicative are the
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperative
       </a>
       ("
       <b>
        Be
       </b>
       quiet!") and the
       <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">
        conditional
       </a>
       ("I
       <b>
        would be
       </b>
       quiet") (although this is not always analyzed as a mood) and in some dialects, the
       <a href="/wiki/English_subjunctive" title="English subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       (as in "I suggest you
       <b>
        be
       </b>
       quiet"). For some further information see
       <a href="/wiki/English_verbs" title="English verbs">
        English verbs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uses_of_English_verb_forms" title="Uses of English verb forms">
        Uses of English verb forms
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other">
        Other
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Realis_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the indicative is generally the main or only realis mood, certain other languages have additional forms which can be categorized as separate realis moods.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       and various other
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       have two kinds of
       <b>
        energetic moods,
       </b>
       which express something which is strongly believed or which the speaker wishes to emphasize. Their exact meanings are dependent upon the prefix that is attached to them. For example,
       <i>
        yaktubanna
       </i>
       يَكتُبَنَّ is in the long energetic mood and has strong obligation meanings; it means "he certainly writes" and if it is preceded by "la", "layaktubanna", it will have the meaning of "he must write." The short energetic expresses weak obligation, e.g., "yaktuban" which means "he almost writes"; if it is preceded by a prefix like "la", "layaktuban", it means "he should write".
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        declarative mood
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          DECL
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) indicates that a statement is true, without any qualifications being made. For many languages this is just an alternative name for the indicative mood, although sometimes distinctions between them are drawn.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It may contrast with
       <a href="/wiki/Inferential_mood" title="Inferential mood">
        inferential mood
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Reference is sometimes made to a "generic mood", for making general statements about a particular class of things; this may be considered to be an aspect rather than a mood. See
       <a href="/wiki/Gnomic_aspect" title="Gnomic aspect">
        gnomic aspect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For other grammatical features which may be considered to mark distinct realis moods, see
       <a href="/wiki/Evidentiality" title="Evidentiality">
        Evidentiality
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sensory_evidential_mood" title="Sensory evidential mood">
        Sensory evidential mood
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mirative" title="Mirative">
        Mirative
       </a>
       . See also
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_modality" title="Linguistic modality">
        Linguistic modality
       </a>
       .
      </p>
