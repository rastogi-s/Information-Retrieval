      <p>
       <b>
        Early Modern Spanish
       </b>
       (also called
       <i>
        classical Spanish
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">
         Golden Age Spanish
        </a>
       </i>
       , especially in literary contexts) is the variant of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       used between the end of the fifteenth century and the end of the seventeenth century, marked by a series of phonological and grammatical changes that transformed
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       into Modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Notable changes from Old Spanish to Early Modern Spanish include: (1) a readjustment of the sibilants (including their
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_voicing_and_devoicing" title="Consonant voicing and devoicing">
        devoicing
       </a>
       and changes in their
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       ), (2) the phonemic merger known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       , (3) the rise of new second-person pronouns, (4) the emergence of the "se lo" construction for the sequence of third-person indirect and direct object pronouns, and (5) new restrictions on the order of
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitic
       </a>
       pronouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       Early Modern Spanish corresponds to the period of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">
        Spanish colonization of the Americas
       </a>
       , and thus it forms the historical basis of all varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        New World Spanish
       </a>
       . Meanwhile,
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       preserves some archaisms of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       that disappeared from the rest of the variants, such as the presence of voiced sibilants and the maintenance of the phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Early_Modern_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       From the late 16th century to the mid-17th century, the voiced sibilants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̻/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̺/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       lost their voicing and merged with their respective voiceless counterparts—laminal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         s̻
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , apical
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
         s̺
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , and palatal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       —resulting in the phonemic inventory shown below:

      <ul>
       <li>
        The phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /h/
        </span>
        (from Old Spanish initial
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        ) probably was marginal by the 17th century; but even in the 20th century, it existed in some varieties of eastern Andalusia and in areas of
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        . In other dialects,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        exists today as a result of the
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         debuccalization
        </a>
        of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        (commonly termed "aspiration" in Hispanic linguistics), but it does not constitute an independent phoneme in those dialects.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
           <span title="How is it not an independent phoneme? Does it merge with another phoneme? (July 2017)">
            clarification needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the Americas, the Canary Islands, and almost all of Andalusia, the apical
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̺/
        </span>
        merged with laminal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̻/
        </span>
        (so the resulting phoneme is represented simply as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        ). In central and northern Spain, the laminal sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̻/
        </span>
        shifted to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        , while the
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apicoalveolar
        </a>
        sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̺/
        </span>
        was preserved without change; thus, it can be represented phonemically simply as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        ).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Some authors
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid_weasel_words" title="Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words">
           <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too vague attribution or weasel words. (January 2016)">
            which?
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        use the transcription
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̪/
        </span>
        for
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̻/
        </span>
        and/or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̠/
        </span>
        for
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̺/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Many dialects have lost the distinction between the phonemes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʝ/
        </span>
        in a merger, called
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        . Non-
        <i>
         yeísta
        </i>
        dialects exist both in the Iberian Peninsula and in South America, mainly in Bolivia, Paraguay, and Peru.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <ul>
       <li>
        A readjustment of the second-person pronouns differentiates Modern Spanish from
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
         Old Spanish
        </a>
        . To eliminate the ambiguity of the form
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        , which served for both second-person singular formal and second-person plural, two alternative forms were created:
        <ul>
         <li>
          The form
          <i>
           usted
          </i>
          (&lt;
          <i>
           vuesarced
          </i>
          &lt;
          <i>
           vuestra merced
          </i>
          , 'your grace') as a form of respect in the second person singular.
         </li>
         <li>
          The form
          <i>
           vosotros
          </i>
          (&lt;
          <i>
           vos otros
          </i>
          ) as a usual form of second person plural. In parts of Andalusia, in the Canary Islands, and in the Americas, however, the form did not take hold, and the form
          <i>
           ustedes
          </i>
          came to be used for both formal and informal second person plural.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
           <a href="#cite_note-2">
            [2]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Due to the loss of the phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʒ/
        </span>
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="Why couldn't they have just come to be pronounced with /S/ and eventually /x/? (January 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        , the medieval forms
        <i>
         gelo, gela, gelos, gelas
        </i>
        (consisting of an indirect object in sequence with a direct object), were reinterpreted as
        <i>
         se lo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         se la
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         se los
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         se las
        </i>
        , as in:
        <i>
         digelo
        </i>
        'I gave it to him/her' &gt; Early Modern Spanish
        <i>
         díselo
        </i>
        &gt; Modern Spanish
        <i>
         se lo di
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In Early Modern Spanish, clitic pronouns were still often suffixed to a finite verb form; but they were beginning to alternate with preverbal forms, as they do in Modern Spanish:
        <i>
         enfermóse
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         murióse
        </i>
        &gt;
        <i>
         se enfermó
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         se murió
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
