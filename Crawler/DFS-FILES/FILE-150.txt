      <p>
       The
       <b>
        metre
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">
        British and original French spelling
       </a>
       ) or
       <b>
        meter
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re,_-er" title="American and British English spelling differences">
        American spelling
       </a>
       ) (from the Greek noun μέτρον, "measure") is the
       <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">
        base unit
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Length" title="Length">
        length
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI). The SI
       <a href="/wiki/Units_of_measurement" title="Units of measurement">
        unit
       </a>
       symbol is
       <b>
        m
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The metre is defined as the length of the path travelled by light in a vacuum in
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         1
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         299 792 458
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">
        second
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Res1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Res1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The metre was originally defined in 1793 as one ten-millionth of the distance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       . In 1799, it was redefined in terms of a prototype metre bar (the actual bar used was changed in 1889). In 1960, the metre was redefined in terms of a certain number of wavelengths of a certain emission line of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Krypton-86" title="Krypton-86">
        krypton-86
       </a>
       . In 1983, the current definition was adopted.
      </p>
      <p>
       The imperial
       <a href="/wiki/Inch" title="Inch">
        inch
       </a>
       is defined as 0.0254 metres (2.54 centimetres or 25.4 millimetres). One metre is about ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        3
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         8
        </sub>
       </span>
       inches longer than a
       <a href="/wiki/Yard" title="Yard">
        yard
       </a>
       , i.e. about ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        39
        <span class="visualhide">
        </span>
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         8
        </sub>
       </span>
       inches.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">
        Spelling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spelling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Metre
       </i>
       is the standard spelling of the metric unit for length in nearly all English-speaking nations except the United States
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Philippines,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which use
       <i>
        meter.
       </i>
       Other Germanic languages, such as German, Dutch, and the Scandinavian languages
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       likewise spell the word
       <i>
        meter.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Measuring devices (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ammeter" title="Ammeter">
        ammeter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Speedometer" title="Speedometer">
        speedometer
       </a>
       ) are spelled "-meter" in all variants of English.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The suffix "-meter" has the same Greek origin as the unit of length.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The etymological roots of
       <i>
        metre
       </i>
       can be traced to the Greek verb
       <i>
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         μετρέω
        </span>
       </i>
       (metreo) (to measure, count or compare) and noun
       <i>
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         μέτρον
        </span>
       </i>
       (metron) (a measure), which were used for physical measurement, for poetic metre and by extension for moderation or avoiding extremism (as in "be measured in your response"). This range of uses is also found in Latin (
       <i>
        metior
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        mensura
       </i>
       ), French (
       <i>
        mètre
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        mesure
       </i>
       ), English and other languages. The motto
       <i>
        ΜΕΤΡΩ ΧΡΩ
       </i>
       (metro chro) in the seal of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       (BIPM), which was a saying of the Greek statesman and philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Pittacus_of_Mytilene" title="Pittacus of Mytilene">
        Pittacus of Mytilene
       </a>
       and may be translated as "Use measure!", thus calls for both measurement and moderation.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_definition">
        History of definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History of definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_metre" title="History of the metre">
        History of the metre
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1668 the English cleric and philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkins" title="John Wilkins">
        John Wilkins
       </a>
       proposed in an essay a decimal-based unit of length, the
       <i>
        universal measure
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        standard
       </i>
       based on a pendulum with a
       <a href="/wiki/Seconds_pendulum" title="Seconds pendulum">
        two-second period
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkins2007_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilkins2007-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of the seconds pendulum to define length had been suggested to the Royal Society in 1660 by
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Wren" title="Christopher Wren">
        Christopher Wren
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">
        Christiaan Huygens
       </a>
       had observed that length to be 38
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Akker" title="Akker">
        Rijnland inches
       </a>
       or 39.26
       <a href="/wiki/Inch#Modern_standardisation" title="Inch">
        English inches
       </a>
       , that is, 997
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       mm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkins2007_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilkins2007-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No official action was taken regarding these suggestions.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1670
       <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Mouton" title="Gabriel Mouton">
        Gabriel Mouton
       </a>
       , Bishop of Lyon, also suggested a universal length standard with decimal multiples and divisions, to be based on a one-minute angle of the Earth's meridian arc or (as the Earth's circumference was not easy to measure) on a pendulum with a two-second period. In 1675, the Italian scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Tito_Livio_Burattini" title="Tito Livio Burattini">
        Tito Livio Burattini
       </a>
       , in his work
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Misura Universale
        </span>
       </i>
       , used the phrase
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         metro cattolico
        </span>
       </i>
       ("universal measure"), derived from the Greek
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD" title="wikt:μέτρον">
         μέτρον
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CE%B1%CE%B8%CE%BF%CE%BB%CE%B9%CE%BA%CF%8C%CF%82" title="wikt:καθολικός">
         καθολικόν
        </a>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        métron katholikón
       </i>
       ), to denote the standard unit of length derived from a pendulum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
        French Revolution
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">
        French Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       charged a commission with determining a single scale for all measures. On 7 October 1790 that commission advised the adoption of a decimal system, and on 19 March 1791 advised the adoption of the term
       <i>
        mètre
       </i>
       ("measure"), a basic unit of length, which they defined as equal to one ten-millionth of the distance between the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1793, the French
       <a href="/wiki/National_Convention" title="National Convention">
        National Convention
       </a>
       adopted the proposal; this use of
       <i>
        metre
       </i>
       in English began at least as early as 1797.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meridional_definition">
        Meridional definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meridional definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="512" height="39" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/50px-Question_book-new.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/75px-Question_book-new.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/100px-Question_book-new.svg.png 2x" width="50"/>

         </div>
        </td>

      <p>
       In 1791, the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">
        French Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       selected the meridional definition over the pendular definition because the force of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravity
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        varies slightly
       </a>
       over the surface of the Earth, which affects the period of a pendulum.
      </p>
      <p>
       To establish a universally accepted foundation for the definition of the metre, more accurate measurements of this meridian were needed. The French Academy of Sciences commissioned an expedition led by
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Baptiste_Joseph_Delambre" title="Jean Baptiste Joseph Delambre">
        Jean Baptiste Joseph Delambre
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_M%C3%A9chain" title="Pierre Méchain">
        Pierre Méchain
       </a>
       , lasting from 1792 to 1799, which attempted to accurately measure the distance between a belfry in
       <a href="/wiki/Dunkirk" title="Dunkirk">
        Dunkerque
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montju%C3%AFc_castle" title="Montjuïc castle">
        Montjuïc castle
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">
        Barcelona
       </a>
       to estimate the length of the
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">
        meridian arc
       </a>
       through Dunkerque. This portion of the meridian, assumed to be the same length as the
       <a href="/wiki/Paris_meridian" title="Paris meridian">
        Paris meridian
       </a>
       , was to serve as the basis for the length of the half meridian connecting the North Pole with the Equator. The problem with this approach is that the exact shape of the Earth is not a simple mathematical shape, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate spheroid
       </a>
       , at the level of precision required for defining a standard of length. The irregular and particular shape of the Earth smoothed to sea level is represented by a mathematical model called a
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       , which literally means "Earth-shaped". Despite these issues, in 1793 France adopted this definition of the metre as its official unit of length based on provisional results from this expedition. However, it was later determined that the first prototype metre bar was short by about 200 micrometres because of miscalculation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">
        flattening
       </a>
       of the Earth, making the prototype about 0.02% shorter than the original proposed definition of the metre. Regardless, this length became the French standard and was progressively adopted by other countries in Europe.
      </p>
      <p>
       The expedition was fictionalised in Denis Guedj,
       <i>
        Le mètre du Monde
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ken Alder wrote factually about the expedition in
       <i>
        The Measure of All Things: the seven year odyssey and hidden error that transformed the world
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="International_prototype_metre_bar">
        International prototype metre bar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: International prototype metre bar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1867 at the second general conference of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        International Association of Geodesy
       </a>
       held in Berlin, the question of an international standard unit of length was discussed in order to combine the measurements made in different countries to determine the size and shape of the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The conference recommended the adoption of the metre and the creation of an international metre commission, according to the proposal of
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Baeyer" title="Johann Jacob Baeyer">
        Johann Jacob Baeyer
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adolphe_Hirsch&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adolphe Hirsch (page does not exist)">
        Adolphe Hirsch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_e_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_de_Ibero" title="Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero">
        Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1870s and in light of modern precision, a series of international conferences was held to devise new metric standards. The
       <a href="/wiki/Metre_Convention" title="Metre Convention">
        Metre Convention
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Convention du Mètre
       </i>
       ) of 1875 mandated the establishment of a permanent
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       (BIPM:
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         Bureau International des Poids et Mesures
        </span>
       </i>
       ) to be located in
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A8vres" title="Sèvres">
        Sèvres
       </a>
       , France. This new organisation was to construct and preserve a prototype metre bar, distribute national metric prototypes, and maintain comparisons between them and non-metric measurement standards. The organisation created such a bar in 1889 at the first
       <a href="/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures" title="General Conference on Weights and Measures">
        General Conference on Weights and Measures
       </a>
       (CGPM:
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         Conférence Générale des Poids et Mesures
        </span>
       </i>
       ), establishing the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Prototype_Metre" title="International Prototype Metre">
         International Prototype Metre
        </a>
       </i>
       as the distance between two lines on a standard bar composed of an alloy of 90%
       <a href="/wiki/Platinum" title="Platinum">
        platinum
       </a>
       and 10%
       <a href="/wiki/Iridium" title="Iridium">
        iridium
       </a>
       , measured at the melting point of ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The original
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Prototype_Metre" title="International Prototype Metre">
        international prototype of the metre
       </a>
       is still kept at the BIPM under the conditions specified in 1889.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wavelength_definition">
        Wavelength definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wavelength definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1893, the standard metre was first measured with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interferometer" title="Interferometer">
        interferometer
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_Abraham_Michelson" title="Albert Abraham Michelson">
        Albert A. Michelson
       </a>
       , the inventor of the device and an advocate of using some particular
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       as a standard of length. By 1925,
       <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">
        interferometry
       </a>
       was in regular use at the BIPM. However, the International Prototype Metre remained the standard until 1960, when the eleventh CGPM defined the metre in the new
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI) as equal to 1 650 763.73
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orange_(colour)" title="Orange (colour)">
        orange
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Red" title="Red">
        red
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">
        emission line
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Krypton" title="Krypton">
        krypton
       </a>
       -86
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atom
       </a>
       in a
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marion_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marion-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Speed_of_light_definition">
        Speed of light definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Speed of light definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       To further reduce uncertainty, the 17th CGPM in 1983 replaced the definition of the metre with its current definition, thus fixing the length of the metre in terms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Second" title="Second">
        second
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Res1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Res1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          The metre is the length of the path travelled by light in vacuum during a time interval of 1/
          <span class="nowrap">
           <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
            7008299792458000000♠
           </span>
           299
           <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
            792
           </span>
           <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
            458
           </span>
          </span>
          second.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       This definition fixed the speed of light in
       <a href="/wiki/Vacuum" title="Vacuum">
        vacuum
       </a>
       at exactly
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008299792458000000♠
        </span>
        299
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         792
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         458
        </span>
       </span>
       metres per second (≈
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7008300000000000000♠
        </span>
        300
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         000
        </span>
        km/s
       </span>
       ). An intended by-product of the 17th CGPM's definition was that it enabled scientists to compare lasers accurately using frequency, resulting in wavelengths with one-fifth the uncertainty involved in the direct comparison of wavelengths, because interferometer errors were eliminated.        <a href="/wiki/Helium%E2%80%93neon_laser" title="Helium–neon laser">
        helium–neon laser
       </a>
       "a recommended radiation" for realising the metre.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-recommendations-2_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-recommendations-2-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the purpose of delineating the metre, the BIPM currently considers the HeNe laser wavelength,

        λ
        <sub>
         HeNe
        </sub>
       </span>
       , to be

        </span>
        632.991
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         212
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">

        </span>
        nm
       </span>

       <i>

      <p>
       Practical realisation of the metre is subject to uncertainties in characterising the medium, to various uncertainties of interferometry, and to uncertainties in measuring the frequency of the source.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beers2_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beers2-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A commonly used medium is air, and the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">
        National Institute of Standards and Technology
       </a>
       (NIST) has set up an online calculator to convert wavelengths in vacuum to wavelengths in air.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NIST_calculator_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NIST_calculator-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As described by NIST, in air, the uncertainties in characterising the medium are dominated by errors in measuring temperature and pressure. Errors in the theoretical formulas used are secondary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-errors_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-errors-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By implementing a refractive index correction such as this, an approximate realisation of the metre can be implemented in air, for example, using the formulation of the metre as
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006157980076204200♠
        </span>
        1
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         579
        </span>
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         800
        </span>
        .762
        <span style="margin-left:.25em;">
         042
        </span>
        (33)
       </span>
       wavelengths of helium–neon laser light in vacuum, and converting the wavelengths in a vacuum to wavelengths in air. Air is only one possible medium to use in a realisation of the metre, and any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_vacuum" title="Partial vacuum">
        partial vacuum
       </a>
       can be used, or some inert atmosphere like helium gas, provided the appropriate corrections for refractive index are implemented.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dunning_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dunning-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The metre is
       <i>
        defined
       </i>
       as the path length travelled by light in a given time and practical laboratory length measurements in metres are determined by counting the number of wavelengths of laser light of one of the standard types that fit into the length,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and converting the selected unit of wavelength to metres. Three major factors limit the accuracy attainable with laser
       <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">
        interferometers
       </a>
       for a length measurement:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beers2_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beers2-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webster2_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Webster2-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        uncertainty in vacuum wavelength of the source,
       </li>
       <li>
        uncertainty in the refractive index of the medium,
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Least_count" title="Least count">
         least count
        </a>
        resolution of the interferometer.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Of these, the last is peculiar to the interferometer itself. The conversion of a length in wavelengths to a length in metres is based upon the relation
      </p>

              <mi>
               λ

              </mi>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mfrac>
                <mi>
                 c
                </mi>
                <mrow>
                 <mi>
                  n
                 </mi>
                 <mi>
                  f
                 </mi>
                </mrow>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
              <mo>
               ,
              </mo>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle \lambda ={\frac {c}{nf}},}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="{\displaystyle \lambda ={\frac {c}{nf}},}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c615d92ffbeb969681018817d86b01af421457ee" style="vertical-align: -2.338ex; width:8.61ex; height:5.176ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       which converts the unit of wavelength λ to metres using
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       , the speed of light in vacuum in m/s. Here
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Refractive_index" title="Refractive index">
        refractive index
       </a>
       of the medium in which the measurement is made, and
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       is the measured frequency of the source. Although conversion from wavelengths to metres introduces an additional error in the overall length due to measurement error in determining the refractive index and the frequency, the measurement of frequency is one of the most accurate measurements available.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Webster2_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Webster2-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>

       <span class="mw-editsection">

      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SI_prefix" title="SI prefix">
        SI prefixes
       </a>
       are often employed to denote decimal multiples and submultiples of the metre, as shown in the table below. As indicated in the table, some are commonly used, while others are not. Long distances are usually expressed in km,
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       (149.6 Gm),
       <a href="/wiki/Light-year" title="Light-year">
        light-years
       </a>
       (10 Pm), or
       <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">
        parsecs
       </a>
       (31 Pm), rather than in Mm, Gm, Tm, Pm, Em, Zm or Ym; "30 cm", "30 m", and "300 m" are more common than "3 dm", "3 dam", and "3 hm", respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       The terms
       <i>
        micron
       </i>
       and (occasionally)
       <i>
        millimicron
       </i>
       are often used instead of
       <i>
        micrometre
       </i>
       (μm) and
       <i>
        nanometre
       </i>
       (nm), but this practice is officially discouraged.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">

      <p>
       One metre is exactly equivalent to
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">

       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">

        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       yards.
      </p>
      <p>
       A simple
       <a href="/wiki/Mnemonic" title="Mnemonic">
        mnemonic
       </a>
       aid exists to assist with conversion, as three "3"s:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        1 metre is nearly equivalent to 3
        <span class="nowrap">
        </span>
        feet ​
        <span class="frac nowrap">
         3
         <span class="visualhide">
         </span>
         <sup>
          3
         </sup>
         ⁄
         <sub>
          8
         </sub>
        </span>
        <span class="nowrap">
        </span>
        inches.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
         <a href="#cite_note-46">
          [46]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This gives an overestimate of 0.125
        <span class="nowrap">
        </span>
        mm. However, the practice of memorising such conversion formulas has been discouraged in favour of practice and visualisation of metric units.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The ancient Egyptian
       <a href="/wiki/Cubit" title="Cubit">
        cubit
       </a>
       was about 0.5
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m (surviving rods are 523–529
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       mm). Scottish and English definitions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ell" title="Ell">
        ell
       </a>
       (two cubits) were 941
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       mm (0.941
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m) and 1143
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       mm (1.143
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m) respectively. The ancient Parisian
       <i>
        toise
       </i>
       (fathom) was slightly shorter than 2
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m and was standardised at exactly 2
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mesures_usuelles" title="Mesures usuelles">
        mesures usuelles
       </a>
       system, such that 1
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m was exactly ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       toise. The Russian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Versta" title="Versta">
        versta
       </a>
       was 1.0668
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       km. The
       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_mile" title="Scandinavian mile">
        Swedish mil
       </a>
       was 10.688
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>

       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       km when Sweden converted to metric units.

           </span>

          </a>

          <i>
           metre
          </i>
          ; an English translation, included to make the SI standard more widely accessible also uses the spelling
          <i>
           metre
          </i>
          (
          <a href="#bipm2006">
           BIPM, 2006
          </a>
          , p. 130
          <i>
           ff
          </i>
          ). However, in 2008 the U.S. English translation published by the U.S.
          <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">
           National Institute of Standards and Technology
          </a>
          (NIST) chose to use the spelling
          <i>
           meter
          </i>
          in accordance with the United States Government Printing Office Style Manual. The Metric Conversion Act of 1975 gives the Secretary of Commerce of the US the responsibility of interpreting or modifying the SI for use in the US. The Secretary of Commerce delegated this authority to the Director of the National Institute of Standards and Technology (
          <a href="#turner">
           Turner
          </a>
          ). In 2008, NIST published the US version (
          <a href="#taylor2008a">
           Taylor and Thompson, 2008a
          </a>
          ) of the English text of the eighth edition of the BIPM publication
          <i>
           <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
            Le Système international d'unités
           </span>
           (SI)
          </i>
          (BIPM, 2006). In the NIST publication, the spellings "meter", "liter" and "deka" are used rather than "metre", "litre" and "deca" as in the original BIPM English text (
          <a href="#taylor2008a">
           Taylor and Thompson (2008a), p. iii
          </a>
          ). The Director of the NIST officially recognised this publication, together with
          <a href="#taylor2008b">
           Taylor and Thompson (2008b)
          </a>
          , as the "legal interpretation" of the SI for the United States (
          <a href="#turner">
           Turner
          </a>
          ). Thus, the spelling
          <i>
           metre
          </i>
          is referred to as the "international spelling"; the spelling
          <i>
           meter
          </i>
          , as the "American spelling".
