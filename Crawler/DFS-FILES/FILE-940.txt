      <p>
       <b>
        Swiss French
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         français de Suisse
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the variety of French spoken in the French-speaking area of
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       known as
       <a href="/wiki/Romandy" title="Romandy">
        Romandy
       </a>
       . French is one of the four official languages of Switzerland, the others being
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romansch_language" title="Romansch language">
        Romansch
       </a>
       . As of 2015, around 2 million people in the country (24.4% of the population) spoke French as their primary language and around 29.1% of the population has working knowledge of French.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The French language spoken in Switzerland differs very little from that of France or Belgium, with minor and mostly lexical differences. This is in contrast to differences between
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_German" title="Standard German">
        Standard German
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Swiss_German" title="Swiss German">
        Swiss German
       </a>
       , in which differences create mutual unintelligibility between speakers of the two forms to the point that they are considered different languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Swiss variant of French is characterized by some terms adopted from
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       , a language formerly spoken largely across the alpine communities of Romandie and maintained by a minority today, as well as expressions borrowed from Swiss and Standard German. While Standard French is taught in schools and used in government, media, and business, there is no uniform vernacular form of French among the different
       <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">
        cantons of Switzerland
       </a>
       . This is exemplified by the usage of borrowed terms from German in regions bordering German-speaking communities to their complete absence by the French border area around
       <a href="/wiki/Geneva" title="Geneva">
        Geneva
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Many differences between Swiss French and French are due to the different administrative and political systems between Switzerland and France. Some of its distinctive lexical features are shared with
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        Belgian French
       </a>
       (and some also with
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
        Quebec French
       </a>
       ), such as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The use of the word
        <i>
         <span lang="fr-CH" xml:lang="fr-CH">
          <i>
           septante
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        for seventy and
        <i>
         <span lang="fr-CH" xml:lang="fr-CH">
          <i>
           nonante
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        for ninety as opposed to
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          <i>
           soixante-dix
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (literally 'sixty-ten') and
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          <i>
           quatre-vingt-dix
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (literally 'four twenties-ten') of the "
        <a href="/wiki/Vigesimal" title="Vigesimal">
         vigesimal
        </a>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/French_language#Numerals" title="French language">
         French counting system
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The use of the word
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          <i>
           déjeuner
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        for "breakfast" ("lunch" in France, which uses
        <i>
         petit déjeuner
        </i>
        for "breakfast"), and of the words
        <i>
         le dîner
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         le souper
        </i>
        for "lunch" and "dinner" respectively (in French of France,
        <i>
         déjeuner
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         dîner
        </i>
        respectively), much like the varying uses of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Dinner" title="Dinner">
          dinner
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Supper" title="Supper">
          supper
         </a>
        </i>
        throughout the English-speaking world.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other examples which are not shared with other varieties of French:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The word
        <i>
         <span lang="fr-CH" xml:lang="fr-CH">
          <i>
           huitante
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        is sometimes used for eighty instead of
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          <i>
           quatre-vingts
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (literally 'four twenties'), especially in the cantons of
        <a href="/wiki/Vaud" title="Vaud">
         Vaud
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Valais" title="Valais">
         Valais
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Canton_of_Fribourg" title="Canton of Fribourg">
         Fribourg
        </a>
        ; the term
        <i>
         <span lang="fr-CH" xml:lang="fr-CH">
          <i>
           octante
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
         Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          <i>
           octaginta
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ) is now considered defunct.
       </li>
       <li>
        The word
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_(subnational_entity)" title="Canton (subnational entity)">
         canton
        </a>
        has a different meaning in each country; in Switzerland, a canton is a
        <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Switzerland" title="Cantons of Switzerland">
         constituent state
        </a>
        of the Confederation, whereas in France, it is a
        <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_France" title="Cantons of France">
         grouping of communes
        </a>
        ; in Belgium, it is a
        <a href="/wiki/Cantons_of_Belgium" title="Cantons of Belgium">
         group of municipalities
        </a>
        , while in Quebec, it is a
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_township_municipalities_in_Quebec" title="List of township municipalities in Quebec">
         township municipality
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In France, a post office box is called a
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          <i>
           boite postale (BP)
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        whereas in Switzerland (as in French Canada), it is called a
        <i>
         <span lang="fr-CH" xml:lang="fr-CH">
          case postale (CP)
         </span>
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In colloquial Swiss French, the word
        <i>
         natel
        </i>
        is used for "mobile phone". So, "I didn't take my phone" becomes
        <i>
         Je n'ai pas pris mon natel
        </i>
        . French people use the word
        <i>
         portable
        </i>
        or simply
        <i>
         téléphone
        </i>
        .
       </li>
