      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Mediterranean Lingua Franca
       </b>
       or
       <i>
        <b>
         Sabir
        </b>
       </i>
       was a
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgin
       </a>
       language used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Basin" title="Mediterranean Basin">
        Mediterranean Basin
       </a>
       from the 11th to the 19th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mediterranean_Lingua_Franca&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lingua franca
       </i>
       means literally "language of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Franks" title="The Franks">
        the Franks
       </a>
       " in
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
        Late Latin
       </a>
       , and originally referred specifically to the language that was used around the
       <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">
        Eastern Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       as the main
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_language" title="Trade language">
        language of commerce
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the terms "Franks" and "Frankish" were actually applied to
       <a href="/wiki/Franks#Legacy" title="Franks">
        all Western Europeans
       </a>
       during the late
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Period
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HEL_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HEL-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later, the meaning of
       <i>
        lingua franca
       </i>
       expanded to mean any
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        bridge language
       </a>
       . Its other name in the Mediterranean area was
       <i>
        Sabir
       </i>
       , deriving from a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_Languages" title="Romance Languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       base meaning "to know".
      </p>
      <p>
       Based mostly on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Italian_languages" title="Northern Italian languages">
        Northern Italian languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Occitano-Romance_languages" title="Occitano-Romance languages">
        Occitano-Romance languages
       </a>
       in the eastern Mediterranean area at first, it later came to have more
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       elements, especially on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbary_coast" title="Barbary coast">
        Barbary coast
       </a>
       (today referred to as the
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       ). Sabir also borrowed from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">
        Berber
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       . This mixed language was used widely for commerce and diplomacy and was also current among slaves of the
       <a href="/wiki/Bagnio" title="Bagnio">
        bagnio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Barbary_pirates" title="Barbary pirates">
        Barbary pirates
       </a>
       and European
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/renegades" title="wikt:renegades">
        renegades
       </a>
       in pre-colonial
       <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">
        Algiers
       </a>
       . Historically the first to use it were the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_Levantine" title="Italian Levantine">
        Genoese and Venetian trading colonies
       </a>
       in the eastern Mediterranean after the year 1000.
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "As the use of Lingua Franca spread in the Mediterranean, dialectal fragmentation emerged, the main difference being more use of Italian and Provençal vocabulary in the Middle East, while Ibero-Romance lexical material dominated in the Maghreb. After France became the dominant power in the latter area in the 19th century, Algerian Lingua Franca was heavily gallicised (to the extent that locals are reported having believed that they spoke French when conversing in Lingua Franca with the Frenchmen, who in turn thought they were speaking Arabic), and this version of the language was spoken into the nineteen hundreds .... Algerian French was indeed a dialect of French, although Lingua Franca certainly had had an influence on it. Lingua Franca also seems to have affected other languages.
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eritrean_Pidgin_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eritrean Pidgin Italian (page does not exist)">
         Eritrean Pidgin Italian
        </a>
        , for instance, displayed some remarkable similarities with it, in particular the use of Italian participles as past or perfective markers. It seems reasonable to assume that these similarities have been transmitted through Italian
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Foreigner_talk&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Foreigner talk (page does not exist)">
         foreigner talk
        </a>
        stereotypes."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schuchardt" title="Hugo Schuchardt">
        Hugo Schuchardt
       </a>
       (1842–1927) was the first scholar to investigate the Lingua Franca systematically. According to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monogenetic_theory_of_the_origin_of_pidgins" title="Monogenetic theory of the origin of pidgins">
        monogenetic theory of the origin of pidgins
       </a>
       he developed, Lingua Franca was known by Mediterranean sailors including the Portuguese. When
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_discoveries" title="Portuguese discoveries">
        Portuguese started exploring
       </a>
       the seas of Africa, America, Asia and Oceania, they tried to communicate with the natives by mixing a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       -influenced version of Lingua Franca with the local languages. When English or French ships came to compete with the Portuguese, the crews tried to learn this "broken Portuguese". Through a process of
       <a href="/wiki/Relexification" title="Relexification">
        relexification
       </a>
       , the Lingua Franca and Portuguese
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       was substituted by the languages of the peoples in contact.
      </p>
      <p>
       This theory is one way of explaining the similarities between most of the European-based pidgins and
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">
        Tok Pisin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">
        Sranan Tongo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Krio_language" title="Krio language">
        Krio
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_Pidgin_English" title="Chinese Pidgin English">
        Chinese Pidgin English
       </a>
       . These languages use forms similar to
       <i>
        sabir
       </i>
       for "to know" and
       <i>
        piquenho
       </i>
       for "children".
      </p>
      <p>
       Lingua Franca left traces in present
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Algerian_slang&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Algerian slang (page does not exist)">
        Algerian slang
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polari" title="Polari">
        Polari
       </a>
       . There are traces even in geographical names, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Guardafui" title="Cape Guardafui">
        Cape Guardafui
       </a>
       (that literally means cape "look and escape" in Lingua Franca and ancient Italian).
      </p>

       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       , Italian, Spanish, Galician, Portuguese, Provençal, French, and Latin versions are not correct grammatically, as they use the
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       rather than inflected verb forms, but the Sabir form is obviously derived from the infinitive in those languages. The correct version for each language is given in italics.
