      <p>
       The language known today as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Language" title="Spanish Language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       is derived from a dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        spoken Latin
       </a>
       that evolved in the north-central part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       after the fall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">
        Western Roman Empire
       </a>
       in the fifth century. A written standard was developed in the cities of Toledo (13th to 16th centuries) and Madrid (from the 1560s).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Over the past 1,000 years, the language expanded south to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       , and was later transferred to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish colonial empire
       </a>
       , most notably to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Americas
       </a>
       . Today it is the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language" title="List of countries where Spanish is an official language">
        official language of 20 countries
       </a>
       , as well as an official language of
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language#International_organizations_where_Spanish_is_official" title="List of countries where Spanish is an official language">
        numerous international organizations
       </a>
       , including the
       <a href="/wiki/Official_languages_of_the_United_Nations" title="Official languages of the United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Main_distinguishing_features_of_the_language">
        Main distinguishing features of the language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main distinguishing features of the language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The development of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology" title="Spanish phonology">
        Spanish phonology
       </a>
       is distinguished from those of other nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       ) by several features:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Vowel_breaking" title="Vowel breaking">
         diphthongization
        </a>
        of Latin stressed short E and O in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_syllable" title="Closed syllable">
         closed syllables
        </a>
        as well as open (
        <i>
         t
         <b>
          ie
         </b>
         mpo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         p
         <b>
          ue
         </b>
         rta
        </i>
        vs. Portuguese
        <i>
         t
         <b>
          e
         </b>
         mpo, p
         <b>
          o
         </b>
         rta
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Devoicing" title="Devoicing">
         devoicing
        </a>
        and further development of the medieval Spanish
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilants" title="Sibilants">
         sibilants
        </a>
        , producing (1) the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         velar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        in words such as
        <i>
         ca
         <b>
          j
         </b>
         a
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         hi
         <b>
          j
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          g
         </b>
         ente
        </i>
        , and (2)—in many
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
         dialects
        </a>
        of Spain, including the prestige varieties of Madrid, Toledo, etc.—the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         interdental
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [θ]
        </span>
        in words such as
        <i>
         <b>
          c
         </b>
         inco
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         ha
         <b>
          c
         </b>
         er
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         la
         <b>
          z
         </b>
         o
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         debuccalization
        </a>
        and eventual loss of Latin initial
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        in most contexts, marked in modern spelling by the silent ⟨h⟩ of words such as
        <i>
         <b>
          h
         </b>
         ablar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          h
         </b>
         ilo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          h
         </b>
         oja
        </i>
        (also in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gascon_dialect" title="Gascon dialect">
         Gascon
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         hilh
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         huelha
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        early fricativization of palatal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        (from
        <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
         Vulgar Latin
        </a>
        -LY-, -CL-, -GL-), first into palatal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʒ/
        </span>
        and ultimately into velar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        , e.g.,
        <i>
         filiu
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         hi
         <b>
          j
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        , *
        <i>
         oc'lu
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         o
         <b>
          j
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        , *
        <i>
         coag'lare
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         cua
         <b>
          j
         </b>
         ar
        </i>
        ; cf. Portuguese
        <i>
         fi
         <b>
          lh
         </b>
         o, o
         <b>
          lh
         </b>
         o, coa
         <b>
          lh
         </b>
         ar
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        development of initial PL-, CL-, FL- into palatal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        in many words, e.g.,
        <i>
         plorare
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         <b>
          ll
         </b>
         orar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         clamare
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         <b>
          ll
         </b>
         amar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         flamma
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         <b>
          ll
         </b>
         ama
        </i>
        ; cf. Portuguese
        <i>
         chorar, chamar, chama
        </i>
        , Catalan
        <i>
         plorar, clamar, flama
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Vulgar Latin initial
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        (from J-, DY-, G(E)-, G(I)-) remains before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        , subsequently disappearing in an unstressed syllable (
        <i>
         yace, yeso, helar, enero, echar, hinojo
        </i>
        vs. Portuguese
        <i>
         jaz, gesso, gelar, janeiro, jeitar, joelho
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The following features are characteristic of Spanish phonology and also of some other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibero-Romance_languages" title="Ibero-Romance languages">
        Ibero-Romance languages
       </a>
       , but not the Romance languages as a whole:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
         palatalization
        </a>
        of Latin -NN- and -LL- into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɲ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        (
        <i>
         a
         <b>
          ñ
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         caba
         <b>
          ll
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        ) (also in Catalan:
        <i>
         a
         <b>
          ny
         </b>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cava
         <b>
          ll
         </b>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation#Phonemic_mergers" title="Phonemic differentiation">
         phonemic merger
        </a>
        of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        , making, for example, the noun
        <i>
         tu
         <b>
          b
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        and the verb
        <i>
         tu
         <b>
          v
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        phonetically equivalent (in all contexts except those of
        <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
         hypercorrection
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Spelling_pronunciation" title="Spelling pronunciation">
         spelling pronunciation
        </a>
        )
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        (also in Galician, Northern European Portuguese and some Catalan and Occitan varieties)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         spirantization
        </a>
        of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /g/
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [β̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ð̞]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɣ̞]
        </span>
        —not only from original Latin B, D, and G (as in Sp.
        <i>
         pro
         <b>
          b
         </b>
         ar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         su
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         ar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         le
         <b>
          g
         </b>
         umbre
        </i>
        ), but also from Latin P, T, and C (as in Sp.
        <i>
         sa
         <b>
          b
         </b>
         e
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vi
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         a
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         la
         <b>
          g
         </b>
         o
        </i>
        ) (also in Galician, European Portuguese, Catalan and parts of Occitan)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The Latin system of four
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_conjugation#The_four_conjugations" title="Latin conjugation">
        verb conjugations
       </a>
       (form classes) is reduced to three in Spanish. The Latin infinitives with the endings -ĀRE, -ĒRE, and -ĪRE become Spanish infinitives in
       <i>
        -ar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -ir
       </i>
       respectively. The Latin
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_conjugation#The_four_conjugations" title="Latin conjugation">
        third conjugation
       </a>
       —infinitives ending in -ĔRE—are redistributed between the Spanish
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ir
       </i>
       classes (e.g.
       <i>
        facere
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        hacer
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dicere
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        decir
       </i>
       ). Spanish verbal
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       continues the use of some Latin
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic
       </a>
       forms that were replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">
        analytic
       </a>
       ones in French and (partly) Italian (cf. Sp.
       <i>
        lavó
       </i>
       , Fr.
       <i>
        il a lavé
       </i>
       ), and the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Subjunctive_mood" title="Subjunctive mood">
        subjunctive mood
       </a>
       maintains separate
       <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
        present
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
        past-tense
       </a>
       forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       provides overt marking for some
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)#Types_of_object" title="Object (grammar)">
        direct objects
       </a>
       (the so-called "personal
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ", see
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_object_marking" title="Differential object marking">
        differential object marking
       </a>
       for the general phenomenon), and uses
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       with indirect objects, in which a "redundant" pronoun (
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        les
       </i>
       ) appears even in the presence of an explicit
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       . (Neither feature occurs in other
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
        Western Romance languages
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
          <span title="This text contradicts material at the article 'Clitic doubling'. (October 2012)">
           contradictory
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       but both are features of
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       , with
       <i>
        pe
       </i>
       &lt; PER corresponding to Spanish
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       .) With regard to subject pronouns, Spanish is a
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop language
       </a>
       , meaning that the
       <a href="/wiki/Verb_phrase" title="Verb phrase">
        verb phrase
       </a>
       can often stand alone without the use of a subject pronoun (or a subject
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       ). Compared to other Romance languages, Spanish has a somewhat freer
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       with relatively fewer restrictions on subject-verb-object
       <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">
        word order
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to prolonged
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        language contact
       </a>
       with other languages, the Spanish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicon_(linguistics)" title="Lexicon (linguistics)">
        lexicon
       </a>
       contains
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       , Germanic,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Accents—used in Modern Spanish to mark the vowel of the stressed syllable in words where stress is not predictable from rules—come into use sporadically in the 15th century, and massively in the 16th century. Their use begins to be standardized with the advent of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Spanish Royal Academy
       </a>
       in the 18th century. See also
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography#Older_conventions" title="Spanish orthography">
        Spanish orthography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="External_history">
        External history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: External history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="600" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/220px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/330px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/440px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         With the Reconquista in Iberia, various Vulgar Latin language groups ended up mixing (along with Basques). The largest such group was the Castilians, whose language became Spanish.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The standard Spanish language is also called
       <a href="/wiki/Names_given_to_the_Spanish_language" title="Names given to the Spanish language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       in its original variant and in order to distinguish it from other languages native to parts of Spain, such as Galician, Catalan, Basque, etc. In its earliest documented form, and up through approximately the 15th century, the language is customarily called
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       . From approximately the 16th century on, it is called Modern Spanish. Spanish of the 16th and 17th centuries is sometimes called "classical" Spanish, referring to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age#Literature" title="Spanish Golden Age">
        literary accomplishments
       </a>
       of that period. Unlike
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">
        French
       </a>
       , it is not customary to speak of a "middle" stage in the development of Spanish.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Castilian Spanish originated (after the decline of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       ) as a continuation of
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        spoken Latin
       </a>
       in several areas of northern and central Spain. Eventually, the variety spoken in the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">
        Toledo
       </a>
       around the 13th century became the basis for the written standard. With the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
         Reconquista
        </a>
       </i>
       , this northern dialect spread to the south, where it almost entirely replaced or absorbed the local Romance dialects, at the same time as it borrowed many words from
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Arabic" title="Andalusian Arabic">
        Moorish Arabic
       </a>
       and was influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       (the Romance speech of Christians living in
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Moorish territory
       </a>
       ) and medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       (Ladino). These languages had vanished in the Iberian Peninsula by the late 16th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The prestige of Castile and its language was propagated partly by the exploits of Castilian heroes in the battles of the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       —among them
       <a href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A1n_Gonz%C3%A1lez_of_Castile" title="Fernán González of Castile">
        Fernán González
       </a>
       and Rodrigo Díaz de Vivar (
       <a href="/wiki/El_Cid" title="El Cid">
        El Cid
       </a>
       )—and by the narrative poems about them that were recited in Castilian even outside the original territory of that dialect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The "first written Spanish" was traditionally considered to have appeared in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Glosas_Emilianenses" title="Glosas Emilianenses">
         Glosas Emilianenses
        </a>
       </i>
       . These are "glosses" (translations of isolated words and phrases in a form more like Hispanic Romance than Latin) added between the lines of a manuscript that was written earlier in Latin. Nowadays the language of the Glosas Emilianenses is considered to be closer to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese_language" title="Navarro-Aragonese language">
        Navarro-Aragonese language
       </a>
       than to Spanish proper. Estimates of their date vary from the late 10th to the early 11th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first steps toward
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standardization
       </a>
       of written Castilian were taken in the 13th century by King
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">
        Alfonso X of Castile
       </a>
       , known as Alfonso el Sabio (Alfonso the Wise), in his court in
       <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">
        Toledo
       </a>
       . He assembled scribes at his court and supervised their writing, in Castilian, of extensive works on history, astronomy, law, and other fields of knowledge.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">
        Antonio de Nebrija
       </a>
       wrote the first grammar of Spanish,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">
         Gramática de la lengua castellana
        </a>
       </i>
       , and presented it, in 1492, to
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Queen Isabella
       </a>
       , who is said to have had an early appreciation of the usefulness of the language as a tool of hegemony, as if anticipating the empire that was about to be founded with the voyages of
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Columbus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Old Spanish resembles the modern written language to a relatively high degree, a reader of Modern Spanish can learn to read medieval documents without much difficulty.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Spanish Royal Academy
       </a>
       was founded in 1713, largely with the purpose of standardizing the language. The Academy published its first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diccionario_de_la_lengua_espa%C3%B1ola_de_la_Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Diccionario de la lengua española de la Real Academia Española">
        dictionary
       </a>
       in six volumes over the period 1726–1739, and its first grammar in 1771,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and it continues to produce new editions of both from time to time. (The
       <a class="external text" href="http://rae.es/recursos/diccionarios/drae/" rel="nofollow">
        Academy's dictionary
       </a>
       is now accessible on the Internet.) Today, each of the Spanish-speaking countries has an analogous language academy, and an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Association_of_Spanish_Language_Academies" title="Association of Spanish Language Academies">
        Association of Spanish Language Academies
       </a>
       was created in 1951.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="America">
        America
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: America">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Beginning in the 16th century, Spanish colonization brought the language to the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       , and western and southern
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       ), where it is spoken today, as well as to several island groups in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific
       </a>
       where it is no longer spoken by any large numbers of people: the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Palau" title="Palau">
        Palau
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marianas" title="Marianas">
        Marianas
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">
        Guam
       </a>
       ), and what is today the
       <a href="/wiki/Federated_States_of_Micronesia" title="Federated States of Micronesia">
        Federated States of Micronesia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Use of the language in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       was continued by descendants of the Spaniards:
       <a href="/wiki/Criollo_people" title="Criollo people">
        Spanish
        <i>
         criollos
        </i>
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mestizos" title="Mestizos">
        Mestizos
       </a>
       . After the wars of independence fought by these colonies in the 19th century, the new ruling elites extended their Spanish to the whole population, including the Amerindian majority, to strengthen national unity, and nowadays it is the first and official language of the resulting republics, except in very isolated parts of the former Spanish colonies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the late 19th century, the still-Spanish colonies of
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       encouraged more immigrants from Spain, and similarly other Spanish-speaking countries such as
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       , and to a lesser extent
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       , attracted waves of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_ethnic_groups" title="European ethnic groups">
        European
       </a>
       immigration, Spanish and non-Spanish, in the late 19th and early 20th centuries. There, the countries' large (or sizable minority) population groups of second- and third-generation descendants adopted the Spanish language as part of their governments' official assimilation policies to include Europeans. In some countries, they had to be Catholics and agreed to take an oath of allegiance to their chosen nation's government.
      </p>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       became a possession of the United States as a consequence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">
        Spanish–American War
       </a>
       , its population—almost entirely of Spanish and mixed Afro-Caribbean/Spanish (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mulatto" title="Mulatto">
         mulatto
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mestizo" title="Mestizo">
         mestizo
        </a>
       </i>
       ) descent—retained its inherited Spanish language as a mother tongue, in co-existence with the American-imposed
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       as co-official. In the 20th century, more than a million
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" title="Puerto Rican people">
        Puerto Ricans
       </a>
       migrated to the mainland U.S. (see
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Ricans_in_the_United_States" title="Puerto Ricans in the United States">
        Puerto Ricans in the United States
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar situation occurred in the American Southwest, including
       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
        California
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
        Arizona
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       , where Spaniards, then
       <i>
        criollos
       </i>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tejanos" title="Tejanos">
        Tejanos
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Californios" title="Californios">
        Californios
       </a>
       , etc.) followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Chicano" title="Chicano">
        Chicanos
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Americans" title="Mexican Americans">
        Mexican Americans
       </a>
       ) and later Mexican immigrants, kept the Spanish language alive before, during and after the American appropriation of those territories following the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">
        Mexican–American War
       </a>
       . Spanish continues to be used by millions of citizens and immigrants to the United States from Spanish-speaking countries of the Americas (for example, many
       <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Americans" title="Cuban Americans">
        Cubans
       </a>
       arrived in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami,_Florida" title="Miami, Florida">
        Miami, Florida
       </a>
       , beginning with the
       <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Revolution" title="Cuban Revolution">
        Cuban Revolution
       </a>
       in 1959, and followed by other Latin American groups; the local majority is now Spanish-speaking). Spanish is now treated as the country's "second language," and over 5 percent of the U.S. population are Spanish-speaking, but most
       <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">
        Latino
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">
        Hispanic
       </a>
       Americans are bilingual or also regularly speak English.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">
        Africa
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Africa">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The presence of Spanish in
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       dates from the late 18th century, and it was adopted as the official language when independence was granted in 1968.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish is widely spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">
        Western Sahara
       </a>
       , which was a protectorate/colony of Spain from the 1880s to the 1970s.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Judeo-Spanish">
        Judeo-Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Judeo-Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1492 Spain expelled its Jewish population. Their
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       language, called Ladino, developed along its own lines and continues to be spoken by a dwindling number of speakers, mainly in Israel, Turkey, and Greece.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_the_Pacific">
        In the Pacific
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In the Pacific">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the Marianas, the Spanish language was retained until the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">
        Pacific War
       </a>
       , but is no longer spoken there by any significant number of people.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">
        Spain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_politics_in_Francoist_Spain" title="Language politics in Francoist Spain">
        Language politics in Francoist Spain
       </a>
       declared Spanish as the only official language in Spain, and to this day it is the most widely used language in government, business, public education, the workplace, cultural arts, and the media. But in the 1960s and 1970s,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       the Spanish parliament agreed to allow provinces to use, speak, and print official documents in three other languages:
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">
        Balearic Islands
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">
        Valencia
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
        Basque provinces
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
        Galicia
       </a>
       . Since 1975, following the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Franco
       </a>
       , Spain has become a multi-party democracy and decentralized country, constituted in
       <a href="/wiki/Autonomous_communities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous communities of Spain">
        autonomous communities
       </a>
       . Under this system, some
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Spain" title="Languages of Spain">
        languages of Spain
       </a>
       —such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aranese_language" title="Aranese language">
        Aranese
       </a>
       (an
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan language
       </a>
       of northwestern Catalonia), Basque, Catalan/Valencian, and Galician—have gained co-official status in their respective geographical areas. Others—such as
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">
        Asturian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonese_language" title="Leonese language">
        Leonese
       </a>
       —have been recognized by regional governments.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="International_projection">
        International projection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: International projection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       organization was founded in 1945, Spanish was designated one of its five
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official languages
       </a>
       (along with
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ; a sixth language,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , was added in 1973).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates_in_Literature" title="List of Nobel laureates in Literature">
        list of Nobel laureates in Literature
       </a>
       includes eleven authors who wrote in Spanish (
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Echegaray" title="José Echegaray">
        José Echegaray
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jacinto_Benavente" title="Jacinto Benavente">
        Jacinto Benavente
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gabriela_Mistral" title="Gabriela Mistral">
        Gabriela Mistral
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ram%C3%B3n_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="Juan Ramón Jiménez">
        Juan Ramón Jiménez
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_%C3%81ngel_Asturias" title="Miguel Ángel Asturias">
        Miguel Ángel Asturias
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">
        Pablo Neruda
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vicente_Aleixandre" title="Vicente Aleixandre">
        Vicente Aleixandre
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Garc%C3%ADa_M%C3%A1rquez" title="Gabriel García Márquez">
        Gabriel García Márquez
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Camilo_Jos%C3%A9_Cela" title="Camilo José Cela">
        Camilo José Cela
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">
        Octavio Paz
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mario_Vargas_Llosa" title="Mario Vargas Llosa">
        Mario Vargas Llosa
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">
        Influences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Influences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Influences_on_the_Spanish_language" title="Influences on the Spanish language">
        Influences on the Spanish language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The mention of "influences" on the Spanish language refers primarily to
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        lexical borrowing
       </a>
       . Throughout its history, Spanish has accepted
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       , first from
       <a href="/wiki/Paleohispanic_languages" title="Paleohispanic languages">
        pre-Roman languages
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_language" title="Iberian language">
        Iberian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Celtiberian_language" title="Celtiberian language">
        Celtiberian
       </a>
       ), and later from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       , from neighboring
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Native American languages
       </a>
       , and from
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The most frequently used word that entered Spanish from (or through
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) Basque is
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/izquierda#Spanish" title="wikt:izquierda">
          izquierda
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "left".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Basque is perhaps most evident in some common Spanish surnames, including
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Garc%C3%ADa_(surname)" title="García (surname)">
         García
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Etxeberria" title="Etxeberria">
         Echeverría
        </a>
       </i>
       . Basque
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Place_names" title="Place names">
        place names
       </a>
       also are prominent throughout Spain, because many Castilians who took part in the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       and repopulation of Moorish Iberia by Christians were of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_people" title="Basque people">
        Basque
       </a>
       lineage. Iberian and Celtiberian likewise are thought to have contributed place names to Spain. Words of everyday use that are attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       sources include
       <i>
        camino
       </i>
       "road",
       <i>
        carro
       </i>
       "cart", and
       <i>
        cerveza
       </i>
       "beer".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Influence of Basque phonology is credited by some researchers with softening the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodentals
       </a>
       : turning labiodental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       to bilabial
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       , and ultimately deleting labiodental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       . Others negate or downplay Basque phonological influence, claiming that these changes occurred in the affected dialects wholly as a result of factors internal to the language, not outside influence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also possible that the two forces, internal and external, worked in concert and reinforced each other.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some words of Greek origin were already present in the spoken Latin that became Spanish. Additionally, many Greek words formed part of the language of the Church. Spanish also borrowed
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       vocabulary in the areas of medical, technical, and scientific language, beginning as early as the 13th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       is, by most accounts, very little on
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       development, but rather is found mainly in the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Germanic_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Germanic origin">
        Words of Germanic origin
       </a>
       are common in all varieties of Spanish. The modern words for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardinal_directions" title="Cardinal directions">
        cardinal directions
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        norte, este, sur, oeste
       </i>
       ), for example, are all taken from Germanic words (compare
       <a href="/wiki/North" title="North">
        north
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">
        east
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/South" title="South">
        south
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">
        west
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        Modern English
       </a>
       ), after the contact with Atlantic sailors. These words did not exist in Spanish prior to the 15th century. Instead, "north" and "south" were
       <i>
        septentrion
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        meridion
       </i>
       respectively (both virtually obsolete in Modern Spanish), while "east" was
       <i>
        oriente
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        levante
       </i>
       ), and "west" was
       <i>
        occidente
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        poniente
       </i>
       ). These older words for "east" and "west" continue to have some use in Modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 711 Spain was invaded by
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       , who brought the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic language
       </a>
       to the Peninsula. From then until the fall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">
        Emirate of Granada
       </a>
       (1492), Spanish borrowed words from Arabic. It is thought that the bilingualism of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabs" title="Mozarabs">
        Mozarabs
       </a>
       facilitated the transfer of vocabulary from Arabic to Castilian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The neighboring Romance languages—
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       —contributed greatly to the Spanish lexicon throughout the Middle Ages and into the modern era.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Borrowing from Italian occurred most frequently in the 16th and 17th centuries, due largely to the influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Renaissance" title="Italian Renaissance">
        Italian Renaissance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With the development of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       in the New World came lexical borrowing from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       , especially vocabulary dealing with flora, fauna, and cultural concepts unique to the Americas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Borrowing from English has become especially strong, beginning in the 20th century, with words borrowed from many fields of activity, including sports, technology, and commerce.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The incorporation into Spanish of learned, or "bookish" words from its own ancestor language,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       , is arguably another form of lexical borrowing through the influence of written language and the liturgical language of the Church. Throughout the Middle Ages and into the early modern period, most literate Spanish-speakers were also literate in Latin; and thus they easily adopted Latin words into their writing—and eventually speech—in Spanish. The form of Latin that Spaniards spoke and the loanwords came from was
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        Classical Latin
       </a>
       , but also
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_Latin" title="Renaissance Latin">
        Renaissance Latin
       </a>
       , the form of Latin during that time.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_history">
        Internal history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Internal history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish shares with other Romance languages most of the phonological and grammatical changes that characterized
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       , such as the abandonment of distinctive
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin#Stressed_vowels" title="Vulgar Latin">
        vowel length
       </a>
       , the loss of the
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin#The_loss_of_the_noun_case_system" title="Vulgar Latin">
        case system
       </a>
       for nouns, and the loss of
       <a href="/wiki/Deponent_verb" title="Deponent verb">
        deponent verbs
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elision">
        Elision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Elision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiceless
       </a>
       intervocalic consonants often became voiced, many voiced intervocalic stops (
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       , and occasionally
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       ) were dropped from words altogether through a process called
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        elision
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       between vowels usually changed to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       in Old Spanish (e.g.
       <i>
        ha
        <b>
         b
        </b>
        ēre
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        a
        <b>
         v
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ), while Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       became
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        sa
        <b>
         p
        </b>
        ere
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        sa
        <b>
         b
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ). In modern times the two phonemes merged into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        ha
        <b>
         b
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sa
        <b>
         b
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ), realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       between vowels (see
       <a href="#Merger_of_/b/_and_/v/">
        Merger of /b/ and /v/
       </a>
       ). Latin voiced stops—
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /g/
       </span>
       , which are represented
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthographically
       </a>
       as B, D, and G respectively—and also occurred in intervocalic positions also underwent
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       :
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voicing_and_spirantization">
        Voicing and spirantization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Voicing and spirantization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In virtually all the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
        Western Romance languages
       </a>
       , the Latin
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless" title="Voiceless">
        voiceless
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stops
       </a>
       —
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , which are represented
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthographically
       </a>
       as P, T, and C (including Q) respectively—where they occurred in an "intervocalic" environment (qualified below), underwent one, two, or three successive stages of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voicing
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition#Sound_changes_associated_with_lenition" title="Lenition">
        spirantization
       </a>
       to, in some cases,
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        elision
       </a>
       (deletion). In Spanish these three consonants generally undergo both
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voicing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition#Sound_changes_associated_with_lenition" title="Lenition">
        spirantization
       </a>
       , resulting in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced" title="Voiced">
        voiced
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricatives" title="Fricatives">
        fricatives
       </a>
       :
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
         ð
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
         ɣ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although it was once speculated that this change came about as a transfer of phonological features from
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum_(linguistics)" title="Stratum (linguistics)">
        substrate
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       languages, which were in geographical proximity to Iberian Vulgar Latin (see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">
         Sprachbund
        </a>
       </i>
       ), it is now widely recognized that such change is a natural internal development
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       reappeared in Spanish through learned words from Classical Latin and also appeared in Spanish through consonant cluster simplification from Vulgar Latin (see below), and Latin voiced stops—
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /g/
       </span>
       , which are represented
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthographically
       </a>
       as B, D, and G respectively—and also occurred in intervocalic positions also underwent
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       :
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
         ð
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
         ɣ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , but appeared in Spanish also through learned words from Classical Latin and also appeared in Spanish through consonant cluster simplification from Vulgar Latin.
      </p>
      <p>
       The phonological environment of these changes is not only between vowels but also after a vowel and before a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonorant_consonant" title="Sonorant consonant">
        sonorant consonant
       </a>
       such as /r/ (Latin
       <i>
        patrem
       </i>
       &gt; Spanish
       <i>
        padre
       </i>
       )—but not the reverse (Latin
       <i>
        partem
       </i>
       &gt; Spanish
       <i>
        parte
       </i>
       , not *
       <i>
        parde
       </i>
       ).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diphthongization_in_open_and_closed_syllables">
        Diphthongization in open and closed syllables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Diphthongization in open and closed syllables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The stressed short E and O of Latin undergo
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diphthongization" title="Diphthongization">
        diphthongization
       </a>
       in many of the Western Romance languages. In Spanish this change occurs regardless of
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Structure" title="Syllable">
        syllable shape (open or closed)
       </a>
       , in contrast to French and Italian, where it takes place only in
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Structure" title="Syllable">
        open syllables
       </a>
       , and in greater contrast to Catalan and Portuguese—neighboring languages on the Iberian Peninsula—where this
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diphthongization" title="Diphthongization">
        diphthongization
       </a>
       does not occur at all. As a result, Spanish phonology exhibits a five-vowel system, not the seven-vowel system that is typical of most other Western Romance languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The stressed short
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o]
       </span>
       reappeared in Spanish through learned words from Classical Latin and appeared in Spanish that evolved from short vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       from Vulgar Latin and retention of long vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [eː]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [oː]
       </span>
       from Vulgar Latin.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Learned_words_and_consonant_cluster_simplification">
        Learned words and consonant cluster simplification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Learned words and consonant cluster simplification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Learned words—that is, "bookish" words transmitted partly through writing and thus affected by their Latin form—became increasingly frequent with the works of
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">
        Alfonso X
       </a>
       in the mid-to-late 13th century. Many of these words contained
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">
        consonant clusters
       </a>
       which, in oral transmission, had been reduced to simpler consonant clusters or single
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       in previous
       <a href="/wiki/Century" title="Century">
        centuries
       </a>
       . This same process affected many of these new, more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academics" title="Academics">
        academic
       </a>
       , words, especially when the words extended into popular usage in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       period. Some of the consonant clusters affected were -
       <i>
        ct
       </i>
       -, -
       <i>
        ct
       </i>
       [i]-, -
       <i>
        pt
       </i>
       -, -
       <i>
        gn
       </i>
       -, -
       <i>
        mn
       </i>
       -, -
       <i>
        mpt
       </i>
       -, and -
       <i>
        nct
       </i>
       -. Most of the simplified forms have since reverted to the learned forms or are now considered to be uneducated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Most of these words have modern forms which more closely resemble
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       than
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       . In Old Spanish, the simplified forms were acceptable forms which were in coexistence (and sometimes
       <a href="/wiki/Competition" title="Competition">
        competition
       </a>
       ) with the learned forms. The Spanish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Educational_system" title="Educational system">
        educational system
       </a>
       , and later the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       , with their demand that all consonants of a word be pronounced, steadily drove most simplified forms from existence. Many of the simplified forms were used in
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literary
       </a>
       works in the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       (sometimes intentionally as an
       <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">
        archaism
       </a>
       ), but have since been relegated mostly to popular and uneducated speech. Occasionally, both forms exist in Modern Spanish with different meanings or in
       <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">
        idiomatic
       </a>
       usage: for example
       <i>
        afición
       </i>
       is a 'fondness (of)' or 'taste (for)', while
       <i>
        afección
       </i>
       is 'illness'; Modern Spanish
       <i>
        respeto
       </i>
       is '(attitude of) respect', while
       <i>
        con respecto a
       </i>
       means 'with regard to'.
      </p>

       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p, t, k, b, d, g]
       </span>
       , wherein
       <i>
        -bb-, -dd-, -gg-
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [bː, dː, gː]
       </span>
       generally undergo both
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voicing
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition#Sound_changes_associated_with_lenition" title="Lenition">
        spirantization
       </a>
       , resulting in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced" title="Voiced">
        voiced
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricatives" title="Fricatives">
        fricatives
       </a>
       :
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
         ð
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
         ɣ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , respectively.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalization">
        Vocalization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Vocalization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The term "vocalization" refers to the change from a consonant to the vowel-like sound of a glide. Some syllable-final consonants, regardless of whether they were already syllable-final in Latin or brought into that position by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syncope_(phonetics)" title="Syncope (phonetics)">
        syncope
       </a>
       , became
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        glides
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">
        Labials
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       ) yielded the
       <a href="/wiki/Roundedness" title="Roundedness">
        rounded
       </a>
       glide
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       (which was in turn absorbed by a preceding
       <a href="/wiki/Roundedness" title="Roundedness">
        round vowel
       </a>
       ), while the
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       ) produced the
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">
        palatal glide
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       (which could
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalize
       </a>
       a following
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and be absorbed by the resulting
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricate
       </a>
       ). (The forms
       <i>
        debda
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cobdo
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        dubdar
       </i>
       are documented in Old Spanish; but the hypothetical forms *
       <i>
        oito
       </i>
       and *
       <i>
        noite
       </i>
       had already given way to
       <i>
        ocho
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        noche
       </i>
       by the time Castilian became a written language.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Merger_of_.2Fb.2F_and_.2Fv.2F">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Merger_of_/b/_and_/v/">
        Merger of /b/ and /v/
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Merger of /b/ and /v/">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most Romance languages have maintained the distinction between a phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       and a phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       —a
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiced
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabial
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiced
       </a>
       , usually
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodental
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       , respectively. Instances of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       phoneme could be inherited directly from Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       (when not between vowels), or they could result from the voicing of Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       between vowels. The
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       phoneme was generally derived either (1) from an allophone of Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       between vowels or (2) from the Latin phoneme corresponding to the letter ⟨v⟩ (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       in Classical Latin, but later
       <a href="/wiki/Fortition" title="Fortition">
        fortified
       </a>
       to the status of a
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative consonant
       </a>
       in Vulgar Latin). In most Romance-speaking regions,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       had
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodental
       </a>
       articulation, but in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       (which still distinguished /b/ and /v/), /v/ was probably realized as a
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabial
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . The contrast between the two phonemes was
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme#Neutralization_and_archiphonemes" title="Phoneme">
        neutralized
       </a>
       in certain environments, as the fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       also occurred as an allophone of /b/ between vowels, after a vowel, and after certain consonants in Old Spanish).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The similarity between the stop
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       and fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       resulted in their merging entirely by the end of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       period.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Modern Spanish orthography the letters ⟨b⟩ and ⟨v⟩ represent the same phoneme (usually treated as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       in phonemic transcription) which is generally realized as the fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       except when utterance-initial or after a nasal consonant, in which cases it is realized as the stop
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       . The same situation prevails in northern
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_dialects" title="Portuguese dialects">
        Portuguese dialects
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       , while the rest of Portuguese dialects maintain the distinction. The merger of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       also occurs in standard
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       in eastern Catalonia; the distinction is retained in
       <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
        standard Valencian
       </a>
       spoken in eastern Catalonia and some areas in southern Catalonia, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_dialect" title="Balearic dialect">
        Balearic dialect
       </a>
       , as well as in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alguerese" title="Alguerese">
        Alguerese
       </a>
       . In Modern Spanish, from the 16th century onward, the choice of orthographic ⟨b⟩ or ⟨v⟩ depends mainly on the etymology of the word: it attempts to mimic the Latin spelling rather than continue the pronunciation-based spelling of Old Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (Hence Old Spanish
       <i>
        bever
       </i>
       "to drink",
       <i>
        bivir/vivir
       </i>
       "to live" become
       <i>
        beber, vivir
       </i>
       , respectively, following the Latin spelling
       <i>
        bibere, vīvere
       </i>
       .) The Spanish place name
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba,_Andalusia" title="Córdoba, Andalusia">
        Córdoba
       </a>
       —more often
       <i>
        Cordova
       </i>
       in Old Spanish (the spelling that prevailed in English until the mid-20th century)—now reflects its Arabic origin in
       <i>
        Qurṭubah
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_f-_to_Spanish_h-_to_null">
        Latin
        <i>
         f-
        </i>
        to Spanish
        <i>
         h-
        </i>
        to null
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Latin f- to Spanish h- to null">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        F
       </i>
       was almost always initial in Latin words, and most of these words passed through a stage in which the consonant eventually developed to [h], then was lost phonologically. Spelling conventions are such that the
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        grapheme
       </a>
       ⟨h⟩ is used in words such as
       <i>
        humo
       </i>
       'smoke',
       <i>
        hormiga
       </i>
       'ant',
       <i>
        hígado
       </i>
       'liver' (compare Italian
       <i>
        fumo, formica, fegato
       </i>
       with /f/ intact), although in terms of both structure and pronunciation, the initial consonant has been lost: /ˈumo/, /orˈmiga/, /ˈigado/. It is thought that ⟨f⟩ represented the
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodental
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in Latin, and that through a series of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        weakening
       </a>
       changes became, successively,
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabial
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiceless bilabial fricative">
         ɸ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and then
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_consonant" title="Glottal consonant">
        glottal
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (hence the modern spelling), before being lost altogether in most varieties; ⟨h⟩ is assumed to have been "silent" in Vulgar Latin. The first written evidence of the process dates from 863, when the Latin name
       <i>
        Forticius
       </i>
       was written as
       <i>
        Ortiço
       </i>
       , which might have been pronounced with initial /h/ but certainly not /f/. (The same name appears as
       <i>
        Hortiço
       </i>
       in a document dated 927.) The replacement of ⟨f⟩ by ⟨h⟩ in spelling is not frequent before the 16th century; however, this is thought not to reflect preservation of /f/. Rather, ⟨f⟩ was consistently used to represent /h/ until the phoneme /f/ reappeared in the language (around the 16th century, as a result of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Borrowings&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Borrowings (page does not exist)">
        borrowings
       </a>
       from Classical Latin), at which point it was necessary to distinguish the two in spelling.
      </p>
      <p>
       The change from /f/ to /h/ occurred historically in the Romance speech of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Castile" title="Old Castile">
        Old Castile
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gascon_dialect" title="Gascon dialect">
        Gascon
       </a>
       , but nowhere else nearby. Since these are both areas which were historically bilingual with
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       , and Basque historically had [h] but no [f], it is often suggested that this change is due to Basque influence. However, this is contested by many linguists.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most current instances of ⟨f⟩ are either learned words (i.e. words influenced by their written Latin form, such as
       <i>
        forma
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        falso
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        fama
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        feria
       </i>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       of Arabic and Greek origin, or words whose initial ⟨f⟩ in Old Spanish is followed by a non-
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowel
       </a>
       —⟨r⟩, ⟨l⟩, or the
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        glide
       </a>
       element of a
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthong
       </a>
       —as in
       <i>
        frente
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        flor
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        fiesta
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        fuerte
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This, along with the effect of preservation of /f/ regionally (e.g. Asturian
       <i>
        fumu
       </i>
       'smoke',
       <i>
        formiga
       </i>
       'ant',
       <i>
        fégadu
       </i>
       'liver'), accounts for modern
       <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">
        doublets
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        Fernando
       </i>
       (learned) and
       <i>
        Hernando
       </i>
       (inherited) (both Spanish for "Ferdinand"),
       <i>
        fierro
       </i>
       (regional) and
       <i>
        hierro
       </i>
       (both "iron"), and
       <i>
        fondo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        hondo
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        fondo
       </i>
       means "bottom" and
       <i>
        hondo
       </i>
       means "deep");
       <i>
        hacer
       </i>
       ("to make") is the root word of
       <i>
        satisfacer
       </i>
       ("to satisfy"), and
       <i>
        hecho
       </i>
       ("made") is the root word of
       <i>
        satisfecho
       </i>
       ("satisfied").
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Silent_Latin_h-">
        Silent Latin
        <i>
         h-
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Silent Latin h-">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>

       'H' is originally pronounced in Classical Latin, but became silent in Vulgar Latin. Thus, words were spelled without any such consonant in Old Spanish; in Modern Spanish, from the 16th century onward, it attempts to mimic the Latin spelling rather than continue Old Spanish orthography.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_development_of_the_Old_Spanish_sibilants">
        Modern development of the Old Spanish sibilants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Modern development of the Old Spanish sibilants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During the 16th century, the three voiced
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       phonemes—dental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar sibilant affricate">
         d͡z
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , apico-alveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative#Voiced_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced alveolar fricative">
         z
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , and palato-alveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         ʒ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       (as in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       <i>
        fazer
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        casa
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        ojo
       </i>
       , respectively) lost their voicing and merged with their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless" title="Voiceless">
        voiceless
       </a>
       counterparts:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant affricate">
         t͡s
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        caçar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        passar
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        baxar
       </i>
       respectively. The character ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%87" title="Ç">
        ç
       </a>
       ⟩, called
       <i>
        ⟨c⟩
        <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">
         cedilla
        </a>
       </i>
       , originated in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but has been replaced by ⟨z⟩ in the modern language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Additionally, the
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricate
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s/
       </span>
       lost its
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       component, to become a laminodental
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result, the sound system then contained two sibilant fricative phonemes whose contrast depended entirely on a subtle distinction between their
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        places of articulation
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apicoalveolar
       </a>
       , in the case of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminodental
       </a>
       , in the case of the new fricative sibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̪/
       </span>
       derived from the affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s/
       </span>
       . The distinction between these sounds was widened in the dialects of northern and central Spain by
       <a href="/wiki/Dissimilation#Paradigmatic_dissimilation" title="Dissimilation">
        paradigmatic dissimilation
       </a>
       , while those of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       and the Americas
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation#Phonemic_mergers" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        merged
       </a>
       the two sounds.
      </p>
      <p>
       The dissimilation in the northern and central dialects occurred with the
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminodental
       </a>
       fricative moving forward to an
       <a href="/wiki/Interdental_consonant" title="Interdental consonant">
        interdental
       </a>
       place of articulation, losing its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilancy
       </a>
       to become
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         θ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . This sound is represented in modern spelling by ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩, and by ⟨z⟩ elsewhere. In
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        the south of Spain
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       , the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̪/
       </span>
       phonemes merged, with the new phoneme being pronounced either as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       ("
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo#seseo" title="Ceceo">
        seseo
       </a>
       "—in the Americas and parts of Andalusia) or as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       ("
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
        ceceo
       </a>
       "—in a few parts of Andalusia). In general, coastal regions of Andalusia preferred
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       , while more inland regions preferred
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       (see the map at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
        ceceo
       </a>
       ). During the colonization of the Americas, most of the settlers came from the south of Spain; as a result, language historians believe, the great majority of the Spanish-speaking
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       today speaks a language variety derived largely from the Andalusian dialect.
      </p>
      <p>
       Meanwhile, the alveopalatal fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       —the result of the merger of voiceless
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨x⟩ in Old Spanish) with voiced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       (spelled with ⟨j⟩ in some words, and in others with ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩)—was moved rearward in all dialects, becoming (depending on
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties#Pronunciation_of_'j'" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        geographical variety
       </a>
       ) velar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , uvular
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [χ]
       </span>
       (in parts of Spain), or glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       (in Andalusia, Canary Islands, and parts of the Americas, especially the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean region
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Interchange_of_the_liquids_.2Fl.2F_and_.2Fr.2F">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interchange_of_the_liquids_/l/_and_/r/">
        Interchange of the liquids /l/ and /r/
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Interchange of the liquids /l/ and /r/">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One unusual feature of Spanish etymology is the way in which the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_consonants" title="Liquid consonants">
        liquids
       </a>
       /r/ and /l/ have sometimes replaced each other in words derived from Latin, French and other sources. For example, Spanish
       <i>
        milagro
       </i>
       , "miracle", is derived from Latin
       <i>
        miraculum
       </i>
       . More rarely, this process has involved nasals such as /n/ (as in
       <i>
        alma
       </i>
       , from Latin
       <i>
        anima
       </i>
       ). Here is an incomplete list of such words:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        ancla, "
        <a href="/wiki/Anchor" title="Anchor">
         anchor
        </a>
        ",
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
         Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         ancora
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        albedrío, "
        <a href="/wiki/Volition_(psychology)" title="Volition (psychology)">
         will
        </a>
        , whim, fancy", Latin
        <i>
         arbitrium
        </i>
        , "judgment, decision, will"
       </li>

      <h3>
       <span id="Ye.C3.ADsmo">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Yeísmo">
        Yeísmo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Yeísmo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Documents from as early as the 15th century show occasional evidence of sporadic confusion between the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
         ʝ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       (generally spelled ⟨y⟩) and the palatal lateral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
         ʎ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨ll⟩). Although the distinction is maintained in spelling, in most dialects of Modern Spanish, the two have merged into the same, non-lateral palatal sound. Thus, for example, most Spanish speakers have the same pronunciation for
       <i>
        haya
       </i>
       (from the verb
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       ) as for
       <i>
        halla
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        hallar
       </i>
       ). This
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation#Phonemic_mergers" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        phonemic merger
       </a>
       is called
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       , based on one name for the letter ⟨y⟩.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For a long time, this was known as a specifically
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusian_dialect" title="Andalusian dialect">
        Andalusian trait
       </a>
       , and only seems to have reached
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       and other cities of central and northern Spain in the last 100 years or so.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
          <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2015)">
           dubious
          </span>
         </a>
         <span class="metadata">
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:History_of_the_Spanish_language#Dubious" title="Talk:History of the Spanish language">
           discuss
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Since more than half of the early settlers of Spanish America came from Andalusia,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       most Spanish-speaking regions of the Americas have
       <i>
        yeísmo
       </i>
       , although there are pockets where the sounds are still distinguished. Native-speakers of neighboring languages—such as
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_languages" title="Astur-Leonese languages">
        Astur-Leonese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       —usually do not feature
       <i>
        yeísmo
       </i>
       in their Spanish, since these languages retain the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
         ʎ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       phoneme.
      </p>
      <p>
       A related trait which has also been documented sporadically for several hundred years is
       <i>
        rehilamiento
       </i>
       (literally "whizzing"), the pronunciation of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
         ʝ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       as a sibilant fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         ʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or even an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricate
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced palato-alveolar affricate">
         dʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , which is also common among non-native Spanish speakers. (The same sound occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       , but in a different context: Judaeo-Spanish preserves the old pronunciation of "j", for example in "hijo", where modern Spanish has
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       ). The current pronunciation varies greatly depending on the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        geographical dialect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolect" title="Sociolect">
        sociolect
       </a>
       (with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced palato-alveolar affricate">
         dʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , especially, stigmatized except at the beginning of a word).
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       (of
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       ) is particularly known for the pronunciation
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         ʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       of both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
         ʝ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       and original
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
         ʎ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       . Within the last 50 years or so, however, the unvoiced pronunciation
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       has become dominant in the Argentine capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       , and is spreading outward due to the prestige associated with the capital city.
      </p>
