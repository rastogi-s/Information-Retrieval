      <p>
       <b>
        Old Occitan
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Modern Occitan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="oc" xml:lang="oc">
         occitan ancian
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
         occità antic
        </span>
       </i>
       ), also called
       <b>
        Old Provençal
       </b>
       , was the earliest form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitano-Romance_languages" title="Occitano-Romance languages">
        Occitano-Romance languages
       </a>
       , as attested in writings dating from the eighth through the fourteenth centuries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Old Occitan generally includes Early and Old Occitan. Middle Occitan is sometimes included in Old Occitan, sometimes in Modern Occitan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the term
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         occitanus
        </span>
       </i>
       appeared around the year 1300,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Old Occitan is referred to as "Romance" (Occitan:
       <i>
        <span lang="pro" xml:lang="pro">
         romans
        </span>
       </i>
       ) or "Provençal" (Occitan:
       <i>
        <span lang="pro" xml:lang="pro">
         proensals
        </span>
       </i>
       ) in medieval texts.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Occitan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Among the earliest records of Occitan are the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tomida_femina" title="Tomida femina">
         Tomida femina
        </a>
       </i>
       , the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Boecis" title="Boecis">
         Boecis
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Can%C3%A7%C3%B3_de_Santa_Fe" title="Cançó de Santa Fe">
         Cançó de Santa Fe
        </a>
       </i>
       . Old Occitan, the language used by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Troubadours" title="Troubadours">
        troubadours
       </a>
       , was the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       with a literary corpus and had an enormous influence on the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Lyric_poetry" title="Lyric poetry">
        lyric poetry
       </a>
       in other European languages. The
       <a href="/wiki/Interpunct" title="Interpunct">
        interpunct
       </a>
       was a feature of its orthography, and survives today in Catalan and
       <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
        Gascon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Catalan_language" title="Old Catalan language">
        Old Catalan language
       </a>
       and Old Occitan diverged from each other between the eleventh and fourteenth centuries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Early texts in the Catalan dialect are the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Homilies_d%27Organy%C3%A0" title="Homilies d'Organyà">
         Homilies d'Organyà
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Can%C3%A7%C3%B3_de_Santa_Fe" title="Cançó de Santa Fe">
         Cançó de Santa Fe
        </a>
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Greuges_de_Guitard_Isarn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Greuges de Guitard Isarn (page does not exist)">
         Greuges de Guitard Isarn
        </a>
       </i>
       . Catalan never underwent the shift from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       or the shift from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       (except in unstressed syllables in some dialects) so it had diverged phonologically before those changes affected Old Occitan.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Old Occitan changed and evolved somewhat during its history, but the basic sound system can be summarised as follows:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">
        Consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Occitan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Consonants">

      <p>
       Notes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Written ⟨ch⟩ is believed to have represented the affricate
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ]
        </span>
        ; but, since the spelling often alternates with ⟨c⟩, it may also have represented [k].
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final ⟨g⟩ may sometimes represent
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ]
        </span>
        , as in
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gaug#Old_Provençal" title="wikt:gaug">
          gaug
         </a>
        </i>
        "joy" (also spelled
        <i>
         gauch
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Intervocalic ⟨z⟩ could represent either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [z]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dz]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Written ⟨j⟩ could represent either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>

      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>

         [o]
        </span>
        apparently raised to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u]
        </span>
        during the twelfth and thirteenth centuries; but the spelling was unaffected, hence
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/flor#Old_Provençal" title="wikt:flor">
          flor
         </a>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /fluɾ/
        </span>
        "flower".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The open-mid vowels
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɛ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɔ]
        </span>
        appear as allophones of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /u/
        </span>
        respectively under certain circumstances in stressed syllables.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Occitan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg">
          <img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text">
         <div class="mbox-text-span">
          <b>
           This section needs expansion
          </b>
          .
          <small>
           You can help by
           <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Old_Occitan&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">
            adding to it
           </a>
           .
          </small>
          <small>
           <i>
            (December 2016)
           </i>
          </small>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Some notable characteristics of Old Occitan:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The language had a two-case system (nominative and oblique), as in
        <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
         Old French
        </a>
        , with the oblique derived from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
         Latin
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
         accusative case
        </a>
        . The declensional categories were also similar to those of Old French; for example, the Latin third-declension nouns with stress shift between the nominative and accusative were continued in Old Occitan only in nouns referring to people.
       </li>
       <li>
        There were two distinct conditional tenses, a "first conditional" that was similar to the conditional tense in other Romance languages and a "second conditional" derived from the Latin pluperfect indicative tense. The second conditional is cognate with the literary pluperfect in Portuguese, the
        <i>
         -ra
        </i>
        imperfect subjunctive in Spanish, the second preterite of very early Old French (
        <a href="/wiki/Sequence_of_Saint_Eulalia" title="Sequence of Saint Eulalia">
         Sequence of Saint Eulalia
        </a>
        ), and probably the future perfect in modern
        <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
         Gascon
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extracts">
        Extracts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <ul>
       <li>
        From
        <a href="/wiki/Bertran_de_Born" title="Bertran de Born">
         Bertran de Born
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <span lang="pro" xml:lang="pro">
          Ab joi mou lo vers e·l comens
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <abbr title="circa">
         c.
        </abbr>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
         1200
        </span>
        , translated by James H. Donalson):
       </li>

      <table role="presentation" style="margin-left:3em;">
       <tr style="vertical-align: top;">
        <td>
         <div class="poem" lang="pro" style="font-style:italic;text-align:left" xml:lang="pro">
          <p>
           Bela Domna·l vostre cors gens
           <br/>
           E·lh vostre bel olh m'an conquis,
           <br/>
           E·l doutz esgartz e lo clars vis,
           <br/>
           E·l vostre bels essenhamens,
           <br/>
           Que, can be m'en pren esmansa,
           <br/>
           De beutat no·us trob egansa:
           <br/>
           La genser etz c'om posc'e·l mon chauzir,
           <br/>
           O no·i vei clar dels olhs ab que·us remir.
          </p>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td style="padding-left: 2em;">
         <div class="poem">
          <p>
           O pretty lady, all your grace
           <br/>
           and eyes of beauty conquered me,
           <br/>
           sweet glance and brightness of your face
           <br/>
           and all your nature has to tell
           <br/>
           so if I make an appraisal
           <br/>
           I find no one like in beauty:
           <br/>
           most pleasing to be found in all the world
           <br/>
           or else the eyes I see you with have dimmed.
          </p>
         </div>
        </td>
