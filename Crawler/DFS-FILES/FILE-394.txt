   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Cartularies of Valpuesta
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="145" data-file-width="255" height="125" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/CartulariosValpuesta.jpg/220px-CartulariosValpuesta.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8c/CartulariosValpuesta.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

         </div>

         <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_script" title="Visigothic script">

         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Cartularies of Valpuesta
       </b>
       are two medieval Spanish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartularies" title="Cartularies">
        cartularies
       </a>
       which belonged to a monastery in the locality of Valpuesta in what is now the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Burgos" title="Province of Burgos">
        province of Burgos
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
        Castile and León
       </a>
       , Spain. The cartularies are called the
       <i>
        Gótico
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        Galicano
       </i>
       from the type of script used in each. They are housed in the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Archives_of_Spain" title="National Archives of Spain">
        National Archives of Spain
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Cartularies of Valpuesta are a series of 12th-century Visigothic documents which, in turn, are copies of earlier documents, some of which date back to the 9th century. These
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartularies" title="Cartularies">
        cartularies
       </a>
       contain an abundance of words of a developing Romance dialect and a copious list of place names in the Valley of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valdegov%C3%ADa" title="Valdegovía">
        Gaubea
       </a>
       and the surrounding area. Probably no other
       <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">
        codex
       </a>
       of that period offers so many tokens of an incipient Romance language with similarities with modern Spanish. The scribes did not write in pure, erudite Latin, but rather in a more evolved, Romance-like Latin, to be better understood by the common people. The transcription took place during the formative period of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       , and it might reflect the early evolution of the Castilian dialect, although a written standard had yet to be established.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the authenticity of some of the texts is disputed,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the cartularies are regarded as significant in the history of the Spanish language, and their status as manuscripts containing
       <i>
        the earliest words written in Spanish
       </i>
       has been promoted by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Spanish Royal Academy
       </a>
       and other institutions, even though the documents are meant to be written in Latin. They are written in a very late form of Latin mixed with other elements of a
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">
        Hispanic Romance
       </a>
       dialect that corresponds in some traits with modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       The preamble of the Statue of Autonomy of Castile and León mentions the cartularies, along with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nodicia_de_Kesos" title="Nodicia de Kesos">
        Nodicia de Kesos
       </a>
       , as documents that contain
       <i>
        the earliest traces of Spanish
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        las huellas más primitivas del castellano
       </i>
       ). However, there have been other documents with a claim to being the earliest in Spanish, notably, the
       <a href="/wiki/Glosas_Emilianenses" title="Glosas Emilianenses">
        Glosas Emilianenses
       </a>
       (marginalia of circa 1000 ce from La Rioja). In November of 2010, the Spanish Royal Academy endorsed the cartularies—written in "a Latin language assaulted by a living language"("una lengua latina asaltada por una lengua viva")—as the record of the earliest words written in Castilian, predating those of the Glosas Emilianenses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">
        Publication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cartularies_of_Valpuesta&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Publication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Selections from the oldest documents were published in 1900 in the French journal
       <i>
        Revue Hispanique
       </i>
       . The cartularies are available in a recent scholarly edition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
