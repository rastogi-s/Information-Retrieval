      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        trill
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonantal
       </a>
       sound produced by vibrations between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_articulator" title="Active articulator">
        active articulator
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_articulator" title="Passive articulator">
        passive articulator
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       &lt;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rr_(digraph)" title="Rr (digraph)">
        rr
       </a>
       &gt; as in
       <i>
        perro
       </i>
       , for example is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
        alveolar trill
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Trills are very different from
       <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" title="Flap consonant">
        flaps
       </a>
       . Whereas with a flap (or tap), a specific gesture is used to strike the active articulator against the passive one, in the case of a trill the articulator is held in place, where the airstream causes it to vibrate. Usually a trill vibrates for 2–3
       <i>
        periods
       </i>
       , but may be up to 5, or even more if
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate_consonant" title="Geminate consonant">
        geminate
       </a>
       . However, trills may also be produced with only a single period. Although this might seem like a flap, the articulation is different; trills will vary in the number of periods, but flaps do not.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonemic_trills">
        Phonemic trills
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trill_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonemic trills">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Trill consonants included in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        -
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
         alveolar trill
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʙ]
        </span>
        -
        <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_trill" title="Bilabial trill">
         bilabial trill
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʀ]
        </span>
        -
        <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" title="Uvular trill">
         uvular trill
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʜ]
        </span>
        -
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_epiglottal_trill" title="Voiceless epiglottal trill">
         voiceless epiglottal trill
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʢ]
        </span>
        -
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_epiglottal_trill" title="Voiced epiglottal trill">
         voiced epiglottal trill
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition,
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʩ]
        </span>
        -
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velopharyngeal_fricative" title="Velopharyngeal fricative">
         velopharyngeal fricative
        </a>
        found in disordered speech sometimes involves trilling of the velopharyngeal port, producing a 'snort'.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The bilabial trill is uncommon. The coronal trill is most frequently
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r͇]
       </span>
       , but
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       articulations
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̪]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̠]
       </span>
       also occur. An alleged
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_trill" title="Retroflex trill">
        retroflex trill
       </a>
       found in
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda
       </a>
       has been transcribed
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ]
       </span>
       (that is, the same as the
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_flap" title="Retroflex flap">
        retroflex flap
       </a>
       ), but might be less ambiguously written
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ͡r]
       </span>
       , as only the onset is retroflex, with the actual trill being alveolar. The epiglottal trills are identified by the IPA as fricatives, with the trilling assumed to be
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophonic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, analyzing the sounds as trills may be more economical.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are also so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Strident_vowel" title="Strident vowel">
        strident vowels
       </a>
       which are accompanied by epiglottal trill.
      </p>
      <p>
       The cells in the IPA chart for the
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_consonant" title="Pharyngeal consonant">
        (upper) pharyngeal
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_consonant" title="Glottal consonant">
        glottal
       </a>
       places of articulation are shaded as impossible. (The glottis quite readily vibrates, but this occurs as the
       <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
        phonation
       </a>
       of vowels and consonants, not as a consonant of its own.) According to Esling (2010),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esling2010_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-esling2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       palatal trills are also implausible. The upper pharyngeal tract cannot reliably produce a trill, but the epiglottis does, and epiglottal trills are pharyngeal in the broad sense.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esling2010_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-esling2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A partially devoiced pre-uvular (i.e. between velar and uvular)
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_trill#Voiceless_uvular_raised_non-sonorant_trill" title="Voiceless uvular trill">
        fricative trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʀ̝̊˖]
       </span>
       has been reported to occur as
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Coda" title="Syllable">
        coda
       </a>
       allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʀ/
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Limburgish_language" title="Limburgish language">
        Limburgish
       </a>
       dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Maastricht" title="Maastricht">
        Maastricht
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Weert" title="Weert">
        Weert
       </a>
       . It is in free variation with partially devoiced uvular fricative trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_raised_uvular_trill" title="Voiceless raised uvular trill">
         ʀ̝̊
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Voiceless trills occur phonemically in e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       . (See also
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_trill" title="Voiceless alveolar trill">
        voiceless alveolar trill
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_retroflex_trill" title="Voiceless retroflex trill">
        voiceless retroflex trill
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_trill" title="Voiceless uvular trill">
        voiceless uvular trill
       </a>
       .) Mangbetu and
       <a href="/wiki/Ninde_language" title="Ninde language">
        Ninde
       </a>
       have phonemically voiceless bilabial trills.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech language
       </a>
       has two contrastive alveolar trills, one a fricative trill (written
       <i>
        ř
       </i>
       in the orthography). In the fricative trill the tongue is raised, so that there is audible
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        frication
       </a>
       during the trill, sounding a little like a simultaneous
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʐ]
       </span>
       (or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̥]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʂ]
       </span>
       when devoiced). A symbol for this sound,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɼ]
       </span>
       , has been dropped from the IPA, and it is now generally transcribed as a raised
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̝]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yi_language" title="Yi language">
        Liangshan Yi
       </a>
       ("Cool Mountain" Yi) has two "buzzed" or fricative vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i̝/, /u̝/
       </span>
       (written
       <i>
        ṳ, i̤
       </i>
       ) which may also be trilled,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʙ̝], [r̝]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of languages have
       <a href="/wiki/Trilled_affricate" title="Trilled affricate">
        trilled affricates
       </a>
       such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [mbʙ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʳ]
       </span>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chapacura-Wanham_languages" title="Chapacura-Wanham languages">
        Chapakuran
       </a>
       language
       <a href="/wiki/Wari%E2%80%99_language" title="Wari’ language">
        Wari’
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muran_languages" title="Muran languages">
        Muran
       </a>
       language
       <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">
        Pirahã
       </a>
       have a very unusual trilled phoneme, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabially_post-trilled_dental_stop" title="Voiceless bilabially post-trilled dental stop">
        voiceless bilabially post-trilled dental stop
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t̪͡ʙ̥]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A nasal trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̃]
       </span>
       has been described from some dialects of Romanian, and is posited as an intermediate historical step in
       <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism_(sound_change)" title="Rhotacism (sound change)">
        rhotacism
       </a>
       . However, the phonetic variation of the sound is considerable, and it is not clear how frequently it is actually trilled.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extralinguistic_trills">
        Extralinguistic trills
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Trill_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Extralinguistic trills">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Linguolabial_consonant" title="Linguolabial consonant">
        linguolabial
       </a>
       trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r̼]
       </span>
       is not known to be used phonemically, but occurs when
       <a href="/wiki/Blowing_a_raspberry" title="Blowing a raspberry">
        blowing a raspberry
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Snoring" title="Snoring">
        Snoring
       </a>
       typically consists of vibration of the uvula and the
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">
        soft palate
       </a>
       (velum). Although the former part is simply a uvular trill, there is no standard linguistic term for the latter. It does not constitute a velar trill, because the velum is here the
       <i>
        active
       </i>
       articulator, not the passive; the tongue is not involved at all. (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Speculative_Grammarian" title="Speculative Grammarian">
         Speculative Grammarian
        </a>
       </i>
       has famously proposed a jocular symbol for this sound (also the sound used to imitate a pig's snort), a double-wide
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʘ
       </span>
       with double dot, suggesting a pig's snout.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensions_to_the_IPA" title="Extensions to the IPA">
         Extensions to the IPA
        </a>
       </i>
       identifies a fricative pronounced with this same configuration as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velopharyngeal" title="Velopharyngeal">
         velopharyngeal
        </a>
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        Lateral
       </a>
       trills are also possible. They may be pronounced by initiating
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɬ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɮ]
       </span>
       with an especially forceful airflow. There is no symbol for them in the IPA. Lateral coronal trills are sometimes used to imitate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_call" title="Bird call">
        bird calls
       </a>
       , and are a component of
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Duck_talk" title="Donald Duck talk">
        Donald Duck talk
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_trill" title="Labiodental trill">
        labiodental trill
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʙ̪]
       </span>
       , is most likely to be lateral, but laterality is not distinctive among labial sounds.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">
        Ejective
       </a>
       trills are not known from any language, despite being easy to produce. They may occur as mimesis of a cat's purr.
      </p>
