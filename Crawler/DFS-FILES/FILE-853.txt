      <p>
       <b>
        Dominican Spanish
       </b>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       as spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       ; and also among the Dominican diaspora, most of whom live in the United States, chiefly in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Jersey" title="New Jersey">
        New Jersey
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Boston" title="Boston">
        Boston
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">
        Miami
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Dominican Spanish, a subset of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       , is based on the Andalusian and Canarian Spanish dialects of southern Spain, and has borrowed vocabulary from
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_language" title="Taíno language">
        Taíno
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Arawakan_languages" title="Arawakan languages">
        Arawakan languages
       </a>
       , from West African languages,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and from English. Speakers of Dominican Spanish may also use
       <a href="/wiki/Conservative_(language)" title="Conservative (language)">
        conservative words
       </a>
       that in the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        upper-class speech of Spain
       </a>
       would be considered
       <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">
        archaisms
       </a>
       . The variety spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cibao" title="Cibao">
        Cibao
       </a>
       region is a mixture of two dialects: that of the 16th- and 17th-century Portuguese colonists in the Cibao valley, and that of the 18th-century Canarian settlers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HenriquezUrenna_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HenriquezUrenna-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In spite of the African presence in the lexicon, and contrary to some earlier theories, claims of phonological influence from African languages on Caribbean Spanish are not borne out by concrete data, according to the linguist
       <a href="/wiki/John_M._Lipski" title="John M. Lipski">
        John Lipski
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski1_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lipski1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most of the Spanish-speaking settlers came from
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       (southern
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . When they first arrived in what is now the Dominican Republic, the first native people they had contact with were the
       <a href="/wiki/Arawak_language" title="Arawak language">
        Arawak
       </a>
       -speaking
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taino_people" title="Taino people">
        Taino people
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish, just as in other Latin American countries, completely replaced the indigenous languages (
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_language" title="Taíno language">
        Taíno
       </a>
       and the language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ciguayos" title="Ciguayos">
        Ciguayos
       </a>
       ) of the Dominican Republic to the point where they became entirely extinct, mainly due to the fact that the majority of the indigenous population quickly died out only a few years after European contact.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, when the Spanish arrived, they found the flora and fauna of the island, as well as various cultural artifacts, very different from those of Spain, so many of the words used by the natives to name these things were conserved and assimilated, thereby enriching Spanish lexicon. Some of these words include:
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chili_pepper" title="Chili pepper">
         ají
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Annona_squamosa" title="Annona squamosa">
         anón
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Sweet_potato" title="Sweet potato">
         batata
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Barbecue" title="Barbecue">
         barbacoa
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Guaco" title="Guaco">
         bejuco
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Bixa_orellana" title="Bixa orellana">
         bija
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Caiman_(genus)" title="Caiman (genus)">
         caiman
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Canoe" title="Canoe">
         canoa
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Mahogany" title="Mahogany">
         caoba
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Subsistence_agriculture" title="Subsistence agriculture">
         conuco
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Soursop#Annona_muricata" title="Soursop">
         guanábana
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Guava" title="Guava">
         guayaba
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Hammock" title="Hammock">
         hamaca
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Spondias_mombin" title="Spondias mombin">
         hobo (jobo)
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Genipa_americana" title="Genipa americana">
         jagua
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Peanut" title="Peanut">
         maní
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Papaya" title="Papaya">
         papaya (lechosa)
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Savanna" title="Savanna">
         sabana
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manihot_esculenta" title="Manihot esculenta">
         yuca
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Dominican Spanish lexicon also includes words borrowed from African languages spoken by the Africans brought to the island after the
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">
        Taíno
       </a>
       extinction, such as
       <i>
        cachimbo
       </i>
       , which was borrowed from the Portuguese word "cacimba", having the latter being borrowed from the Bantu "cazimba".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dominican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Like many other Spanish dialects, Dominican Spanish features
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          yeísmo
         </a>
        </i>
        : the sounds represented by
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        (the palatal lateral
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        ) and
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        (historically the palatal approximant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        ) have fused into one. This merged phoneme is generally pronounced as a
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ]
        </span>
        (these are the sounds in
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        <i>
         <u>
          Y
         </u>
         ork
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <u>
          J
         </u>
         ohn
        </i>
        ). That is, in the Dominican Republic (as in most of
        <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
         Latin America
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
         Spain
        </a>
        ),
        <i>
         se cayó
        </i>
        "he fell down" is homophonous with
        <i>
         se calló
        </i>
        "he became silent / he shut up".
       </li>
       <li>
        Dominican Spanish has
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        (traditional
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        ). That is,
        <i>
         casa
        </i>
        ("house") is homophonous with
        <i>
         caza
        </i>
        ("hunt").
        <i>
         Seseo
        </i>
        is common to nearly all of Hispanic America, the Canary Islands, and southern Spain.
       </li>
       <li>
        Strong contraction in everyday speech is common, as in
        <i>
         "voy a"
        </i>
        into
        <i>
         "vuá"
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         "voá"
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         "¿para adónde vas?"
        </i>
        into
        <i>
         "¿p'ónde va'?"
        </i>
        . Another example:
        <i>
         "David 'tá 'co'ta'o"
        </i>
        , from
        <i>
         "David está acostado"
        </i>
        ("David is lying down / David is sleeping").
       </li>
       <li>
        The fricative
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        has a tendency to become an indistinct aspiration or disappear or to become a
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         voiceless glottal fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        at the end of syllables. The change may be realized only at the word level or it may also cross word boundaries. That is,
        <i>
         las mesas son blancas
        </i>
        "the tables are white" is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [lahˈmesah sɔn ˈblaŋkah]
        </span>
        , but in
        <i>
         las águilas azules
        </i>
        "the blue eagles", syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        in
        <i>
         las
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         águilas
        </i>
        might be resyllabified into the initial syllable of the following vowel-initial words and remain
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [laˈsaɣilasaˈsulɛh]
        </span>
        ), or become
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        (it varies by speaker). Aspiration or disappearance of syllable-final /s/ is common to all of Hispanic America, the Canary Islands, and southern Spain.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Example 1: To say
          <i>
           lo niño
          </i>
          or
          <i>
           los niño
          </i>
          , instead of
          <i>
           los niños
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          Example 2: To say
          <i>
           lluvia ailada
          </i>
          or
          <i>
           lluvias ailada
          </i>
          , instead of
          <i>
           lluvias aisladas
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        In some areas, speakers tend to drop the final
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        sound in verb infinitives. The elision is considered a feature of uneducated speakers in some places, but it is widespread in others, at least in rapid speech.
       </li>
       <li>
        Syllable-final
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        tends to be changed in many words by an
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        sound in the Northerly
        <a href="/wiki/Cibao" title="Cibao">
         Cibao
        </a>
        region and by an
        <i>
         l
        </i>
        (L) in the Eastern and in the capital city (
        <a href="/wiki/Santo_Domingo" title="Santo Domingo">
         Santo Domingo
        </a>
        ): the verb
        <i>
         correr
        </i>
        (to run) is pronounced
        <i>
         correi
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         correl
        </i>
        respectively, and
        <i>
         perdón
        </i>
        (forgiveness) becomes
        <i>
         peidón
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         peldón
        </i>
        . This substitution of
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        is delicately (almost mutely) present in
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian Spanish
        </a>
        , and also the
        <i>
         l
        </i>
        use is prototypical, and more marked, in
        <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
         Puerto Rican Spanish
        </a>
        . It is believed to be of Andalusian origin.
       </li>
       <li>
        Dominican Spanish uses the common Caribbean inverted placement of the second person singular pronoun
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        in front of the verb in questions:
        <i>
         "¿Cómo tú estás?"
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         "¿Cómo estás tú?"
        </i>
        . When speaking formally or with those not spoken to with familiarity, they use the more formal
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        , instead of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        , the conventional word order is used.
       </li>
       <li>
        The "d" is silent in the common word-ending
        <i>
         -ado
        </i>
        . For example, the words
        <i>
         casado
        </i>
        (married) and
        <i>
         lado
        </i>
        (side, way) are pronounced as
        <i>
         casao
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         lao
        </i>
        in Dominican Spanish.
       </li>
       <li>
        In a few parts of the country, an "el" at the end of a word is pronounced as "err." For example,
        <i>
         Miguel
        </i>
        may be pronounced as
        <i>
         Miguer
        </i>
        in Dominican Spanish, a feature shared with
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian Spanish
        </a>
        and in contrast to
        <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
         Puerto Rican Spanish
        </a>
        , where the reverse occurs, e.g. pronouncing the name
        <i>
         Arturo
        </i>
        (Arthur) as
        <i>
         Alturo
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other differences with
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       include subtleties like
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       , in particular, adding the
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       erroneously, thus overcompensating the habit of omitting it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Example 1:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        standard:
        <i>
         administraciones públicas
        </i>
        [public administrations]
       </li>
       <li>
        vernacular:
        <i>
         aminitracione pública
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        hypercorrected:
        <i>
         asministracione púsblica
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Example 2:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        standard:
        <i>
         jaguar
        </i>
        [jaguar]
       </li>
       <li>
        vernacular:
        <i>
         jagual
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         jaguai
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        hypercorrected:
        <i>
         jasguar
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The hypercorrected form is more of a blatantly sarcastic mode of speech, commonly used for joking rather than everyday speech.
      </p>

      <p>
       As in every dialect, Dominican Spanish has numerous vocabulary differences from other forms of the language. The Dominican Academy of Letters (Academia Dominicana de la Lengua) published in November 2013 a dictionary of Dominican terms (
       <i>
        Diccionario del español dominicano
       </i>
       ) containing close to 11,000 words and phrases peculiar to the Dominican dialect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">

      <p>
       A slightly pejorative slang expression also common around most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       basin is
       <i>
        vaina
       </i>
       . The Castilian meanings are "sheath", "pod", "shell", "shell casing", and "hull" (of a plant). It is descended from the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       word "vāgīna", which meant "sheath".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the Dominican Republic "vaina" is mainly a thing, a matter, or simply "stuff". For example,
       <i>
        ¿Qué vaina es esa?
       </i>
       means
       <i>
        ¿Qué cosa es esa?
       </i>
       , "What is that thing/stuff?".
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Anglicism" title="Anglicism">
        Anglicisms
       </a>
       , due to cultural and commercial influence from the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Dominican_Republic" title="History of the Dominican Republic">
        American occupations of the Dominican Republic
       </a>
       during 1916–1924 and 1965–1966, are extremely common in Dominican Spanish, more so than in any other Spanish variant, save for Puerto Rican and perhaps Northern
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       . A prime example of this is "
       <i>
        vaguada
       </i>
       ", which is a corruption of the English "
       <i>
        bad weather
       </i>
       ", though in Dominican Spanish the term has come to mean storm or torrential downpour, rather than a spot of unpleasant climate. Hence, a common Dominican expression: "
       <i>
        Viene una vaguada
       </i>
       ", "here comes a
       <i>
        vaguada
       </i>
       ", or "here comes a storm". Another excellent example of this is "
       <i>
        boche
       </i>
       ", a corruption of the English "bull shit", though in Dominican Spanish the term has come to mean a reprimanding, fulmination, or harangue in general terms. Hence, a common Dominican expression: "
       <i>
        Me echaron un boche
       </i>
       ", "they threw me a
       <i>
        boche
       </i>
       ", or "they reprimanded me". The pronoun "they" in Dominican Spanish often refers to the third person singular, so that
       <i>
        me dijeron
       </i>
       , "they told me", is sometimes used instead of "a man told me", or "a woman told me", or "the young boy by the lemon tree told me". Furthermore, is the Dominican Spanish word for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SUV" title="SUV">
        SUV
       </a>
       , "yipeta", "jeepeta", or rarely "gipeta". This term is a corruption of the American "Jeep", which was the primary mode of transport for the GI's throughout the country during the occupation in the 1960s. Dominican license plates for SUVs are marked with a "G" for "
       <i>
        gipeta
       </i>
       ", a variant of, and pronounced like, "yipeta", before their serial number. The word "tichel", from "T-shirt", also refers to a rugby shirt, association football jersey, or undershirt, and similarly, "
       <a href="/wiki/Corn_flakes" title="Corn flakes">
        corn flakes
       </a>
       " and its variant "
       <i>
        con fléi
       </i>
       " can refer to any
       <a href="/wiki/Breakfast_cereal" title="Breakfast cereal">
        breakfast cereal
       </a>
       , in Dominican Spanish, be it
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Puffed_corn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Puffed corn (page does not exist)">
        puffed corn
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bran_flakes" title="Bran flakes">
        bran flakes
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puffed_wheat" title="Puffed wheat">
        puffed wheat
       </a>
       . The borrowing "
       <a href="/wiki/Polo_shirt" title="Polo shirt">
        polo shirt
       </a>
       " is frequently pronounced
       <i>
        polo ché
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Another phenomenon related to Anglicisms is the usage of brand names as common names for certain objects. For example, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gillette_(brand)" title="Gillette (brand)">
        Gillette
       </a>
       " and its derivative
       <i>
        yilé
       </i>
       refer to any razor, and while the machete is known as
       <i>
        machete
       </i>
       , this being originally a Spanish word, it is sometimes referred to as a "colín", derived from "Collins &amp; Co.", name of a former
       <a href="/wiki/Connecticut" title="Connecticut">
        Connecticut
       </a>
       toolmaker.
      </p>
