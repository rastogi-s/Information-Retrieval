   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    <i>
     n
    </i>
    -vector
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        -vector
       </b>
       representation is a three-parameter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_singularity" title="Mathematical singularity">
        non-singular
       </a>
       representation well-suited for replacing
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       as
       <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_position_representation" title="Horizontal position representation">
        horizontal position representation
       </a>
       in mathematical calculations and computer algorithms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geometrically, the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector for a given position on an
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       is the outward-pointing
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_vector" title="Unit vector">
        unit vector
       </a>
       that is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">
        normal
       </a>
       in that position to the ellipsoid. For representing horizontal positions on Earth, the ellipsoid is a
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       and the vector is
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_decomposition" title="Vector decomposition">
        decomposed
       </a>
       in an
       <a href="/wiki/ECEF" title="ECEF">
        Earth-centered Earth-fixed
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinate_system" title="Coordinate system">
        coordinate system
       </a>
       . It behaves smoothly at all Earth positions, and it holds the mathematical
       <a href="/wiki/Injective_function" title="Injective function">
        one-to-one
       </a>
       property.
      </p>
      <p>
       More in general, the concept can be applied to representing positions on the boundary of a strictly
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">
        convex
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bounded_subset" title="Bounded subset">
        bounded subset
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       -dimensional
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">
        Euclidean space
       </a>
       , provided that that boundary is a
       <a href="/wiki/Differentiable_manifold" title="Differentiable manifold">
        differentiable manifold
       </a>
       . In this general case, the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector consists of
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       parameters.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="General_properties">
        General properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=N-vector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: General properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_vector" title="Normal vector">
        normal vector
       </a>
       to a strictly
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">
        convex
       </a>
       surface can be used to uniquely define a surface position.
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector is an outward-pointing normal vector with
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_vector" title="Unit vector">
        unit length
       </a>
       used as a position representation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gade_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gade-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For most applications the surface is the
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       of the Earth, and thus
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector is used to represent a horizontal position. Hence, the angle between
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector and the equatorial plane corresponds to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_latitude" title="Geodetic latitude">
        geodetic latitude
       </a>
       , as shown in the figure.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:372px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:N_vector_and_geodetic_latitude.png">
         <img alt="Alt" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="551" height="365" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/N_vector_and_geodetic_latitude.png/370px-N_vector_and_geodetic_latitude.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/48/N_vector_and_geodetic_latitude.png 1.5x" width="370"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:N_vector_and_geodetic_latitude.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The direction of
         <i>
          n
         </i>
         -vector corresponds to geodetic latitude
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A surface position has two
       <a href="/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics)" title="Degrees of freedom (mechanics)">
        degrees of freedom
       </a>
       , and thus two parameters are sufficient to represent any position on the surface. On the reference ellipsoid,
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       are common parameters for this purpose, but as all
       <i>
        two-parameter representations
       </i>
       , they have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_singularity" title="Mathematical singularity">
        singularities
       </a>
       . This is similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_formalisms_in_three_dimensions" title="Rotation formalisms in three dimensions">
        orientation
       </a>
       , which has three degrees of freedom, but all
       <i>
        three-parameter representations
       </i>
       have singularities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In both cases the singularities are avoided by adding an extra parameter, i.e. to use
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector (three parameters) to
       <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_position_representation" title="Horizontal position representation">
        represent horizontal position
       </a>
       and a unit
       <a href="/wiki/Quaternion" title="Quaternion">
        quaternion
       </a>
       (four parameters) to
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_formalisms_in_three_dimensions" title="Rotation formalisms in three dimensions">
        represent orientation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector is a
       <a href="/wiki/Injective_function" title="Injective function">
        one-to-one
       </a>
       representation, meaning that any surface position corresponds to one unique
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector, and any
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector corresponds to one unique surface position.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_vector" title="Euclidean vector">
        Euclidean 3D vector
       </a>
       , standard 3D
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_calculus" title="Vector calculus">
        vector algebra
       </a>
       can be used for the position calculations, and this makes
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vector well-suited for most horizontal position calculations.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Example:_Great_circle_distance">
        Example: Great circle distance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=N-vector&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Example: Great circle distance">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Finding the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_circle_distance" title="Great circle distance">
        great circle distance
       </a>
       between two horizontal positions (assuming spherical Earth) is usually done by means of latitude and longitude.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_circle_distance#Formulas" title="Great circle distance">
        Three different expressions
       </a>
       for this distance are common; the first is based on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_function" title="Inverse trigonometric function">
        arccos
       </a>
       , the second is based on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_function" title="Inverse trigonometric function">
        arcsin
       </a>
       , and the final is based on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_function" title="Inverse trigonometric function">
        arctan
       </a>
       . The expressions, which are successively more complex to avoid
       <a href="/wiki/Numerical_stability" title="Numerical stability">
        numerical instabilities
       </a>
       , are not easy to find, and since they are based on latitude and longitude, the Pole singularities may become a problem. They also contain
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_(letter)" title="Delta (letter)">
        deltas
       </a>
       of latitude and longitude, which in general should be used with care near the ±
       <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">
        180° meridian
       </a>
       and the Poles.
      </p>

        dot product
       </a>

      <p>
       where
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbf {n} _{a}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

       </span>
       and
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbf {n} _{b}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>

           </annotation>

         </math>
        </span>

       </span>
       are the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -vectors representing the two positions
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       .
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

       </span>
       is the angular difference, and thus the great-circle distance is achieved by multiplying with the Earth radius. This expression also works at the poles and at the ±180° meridian. Note that

       must be replaced by
       <i>
