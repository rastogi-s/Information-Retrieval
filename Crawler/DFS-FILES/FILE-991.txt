          </a>
          phonetic symbols.
         </b>
         Without proper
         <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Fonts" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
          rendering support
         </a>
         , you may see
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Replacement_character" title="Replacement character">
          question marks, boxes, or other symbols
         </a>
         instead of
         <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
          Unicode
         </a>
         characters. For an introductory guide on IPA symbols, see
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
          Help:IPA
         </a>
         .
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <b>
        Corsican
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        corsu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lingua corsa
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       within the
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian subfamily
       </a>
       . It is closely related to the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       and especially to its
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan
       </a>
       branch. It is spoken and written on the islands of
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ) and northern
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ). Corsican used to play the role of a
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       , while
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       was the official language in Corsica until 1859; afterwards Italian was replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , owing to the acquisition of the island by France from the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">
        Republic of Genoa
       </a>
       in 1768. Over the next two centuries, the use of French grew to the extent that, by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liberation_of_France" title="Liberation of France">
        Liberation
       </a>
       in 1945, all islanders had a working knowledge of French. The 20th century saw a wholesale language shift, with islanders changing their language practices to the extent that there were no monolingual Corsican speakers left by the 1960s. By 1995, an estimated 65 percent of islanders had some degree of proficiency in Corsican,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a small minority, perhaps 10 percent, used Corsican as a first language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cif_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cif-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_by_subjective_analysis">
        Classification by subjective analysis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification by subjective analysis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As for Corsican, a bone of contention is whether it should be considered an Italian
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       or its own
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       . Usually, it is not possible to ascertain what an author means by these terms. For example, one might read from some scholars that Corsican either belongs to the "centrosouthern
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        Italian dialects
       </a>
       " along with Tuscan/Standard Italian,
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan
       </a>
       and others
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or that it is "closely related to the Tuscan dialect of Italian".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the characteristics of standard Italian is the retention of the -
       <i>
        re
       </i>
       infinitive ending, as in Latin
       <i>
        mittere
       </i>
       "send". Such infinitival ending is lost in both Corsican
       <a href='/wiki/Tuscan_dialect#Loss_of_infinitival_"-re"' title="Tuscan dialect">
        and Tuscan
       </a>
       , which has
       <i>
        mette
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        metta
       </i>
       , "to put". The Latin relative pronouns
       <i>
        qui
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        quae
       </i>
       "who", and
       <i>
        quod
       </i>
       "what", are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflected" title="Inflected">
        inflected
       </a>
       in Latin; whereas the relative pronoun in Italian for "who" is
       <i>
        chi
       </i>
       and "what" is
       <i>
        che
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        (che) cosa
       </i>
       , it is an uninflected
       <i>
        chì
       </i>
       in Corsican.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Corsican language has been influenced by the languages of the major powers taking an interest in Corsican affairs; earlier by those of the medieval Italian powers:
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       (828–1077),
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       (1077–1282) and
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       (1282–1768), more recently by France (1768–present), which, since 1789, has promulgated the official Parisian French. The term
       <i>
        gallicised Corsican
       </i>
       refers to Corsican up to about the year 1950. The term
       <i>
        distanciated Corsican
       </i>
       refers to an idealized Corsican from which various agents have removed French or other elements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 40 AD, the natives of Corsica did not speak
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       . The Roman exile,
       <a href="/wiki/Seneca_the_Younger" title="Seneca the Younger">
        Seneca the Younger
       </a>
       , reports that both coast and interior were occupied by natives whose language he did not understand (see
       <a href="/wiki/Prehistory_of_Corsica#Ligurian_hypothesis" title="Prehistory of Corsica">
        the Ligurian hypothesis
       </a>
       ). Whatever language was spoken is still visible in the toponymy or in some words, for instance in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gallurese_dialect" title="Gallurese dialect">
        Gallurese dialect
       </a>
       spoken in Sardinia
       <i>
        zerru
       </i>
       'pig'. A similar situation is valid for
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       and Sicilian. The occupation of the island by the
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       around the year 469 marked the end of authoritative influence by Latin speakers (see
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Corsica" title="Medieval Corsica">
        Medieval Corsica
       </a>
       ). If the natives of that time spoke Latin, they must have acquired it during the late empire.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">
        Dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Corsica">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The two most widely spoken forms of the Corsican language are
       <i>
        Supranacciu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Cismuntincu
       </i>
       , spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Bastia" title="Bastia">
        Bastia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Corte,_Haute-Corse" title="Corte, Haute-Corse">
        Corte
       </a>
       area (generally throughout the northern half of the island, known as
       <a href="/wiki/Haute-Corse" title="Haute-Corse">
        Haute-Corse
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Cismonte
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Corsica suprana
       </i>
       ), and
       <i>
        Suttanacciu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Pumuntincu
       </i>
       , spoken around
       <a href="/wiki/Sart%C3%A8ne" title="Sartène">
        Sartène
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Porto-Vecchio" title="Porto-Vecchio">
        Porto-Vecchio
       </a>
       (generally throughout the southern half of the island, known as
       <a href="/wiki/Corse-du-Sud" title="Corse-du-Sud">
        Corse-du-Sud
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Pumonti
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Corsica suttana
       </i>
       ). The dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Ajaccio" title="Ajaccio">
        Ajaccio
       </a>
       has been described as in transition. The dialects spoken at
       <a href="/wiki/Calvi,_Haute-Corse" title="Calvi, Haute-Corse">
        Calvi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bonifacio,_Corse-du-Sud" title="Bonifacio, Corse-du-Sud">
        Bonifacio
       </a>
       are closer to the
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoese
       </a>
       dialect, also known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This division along the Girolata-Porto Vecchio line was due to the massive immigration from Tuscany which took place in Corsica during the lower Middle Ages: as a result, the northern Corsican dialects became very close to a central Italian dialect like Tuscan, while the southern Corsican varieties could keep the original characteristics of the language which make it much more similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian
       </a>
       and, only to some extent,
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gallurese.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="721" data-file-width="1105" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gallurese.png/250px-Gallurese.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gallurese.png/375px-Gallurese.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/Gallurese.png/500px-Gallurese.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gallurese.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Languages in northern
         <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
          Sardinia
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sardinia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Gallurese_dialect" title="Gallurese dialect">
        Gallurese dialect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sassarese_language" title="Sassarese language">
        Sassarese language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Gallurese variety is spoken in the extreme north of
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       , including the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Gallura" title="Gallura">
        Gallura
       </a>
       and the archipelago of
       <a href="/wiki/La_Maddalena" title="La Maddalena">
        La Maddalena
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sassarese_language" title="Sassarese language">
        Sassarese
       </a>
       is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Sassari" title="Sassari">
        Sassari
       </a>
       and in its neighbourhood, in the northwest of
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       . Whether the two should be included either in Corsican or in Sardinian as dialects or considered independent languages is still subject of debate.
      </p>
      <p>
       On
       <a href="/wiki/Maddalena_archipelago" title="Maddalena archipelago">
        Maddalena archipelago
       </a>
       the local dialect (called
       <i>
        Isulanu, Maddaleninu, Maddalenino
       </i>
       ) was brought by fishermen and shepherds from Bonifacio during immigration in the 17th and 18th centuries. Though influenced by Gallurese, it has maintained the original characteristics of Corsican. There are also numerous words of
       <a href="/wiki/Genoese_dialect" title="Genoese dialect">
        Genoese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ponza" title="Ponza">
        Ponzese
       </a>
       origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e18_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-e18-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On October 14, 1997, Article 2 Item 4 of Law Number 26 of the Autonomous Region of Sardinia granted "
       <i>
        al dialetto sassarese e a quello gallurese
       </i>
       " – equal legal status with the other languages on
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       . They are thus legally defined as different languages from
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       by the Sardinian government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Number_of_speakers">
        Number of speakers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Number of speakers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The January 2007 estimated population of Corsica was 281,000, whereas the figure for the March 1999 census, when most of the studies—though not the linguistic survey work referenced in this article—were performed, was about 261,000 (see under
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       ). Only a fraction of the population at either time spoke Corsican with any fluency.
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of Corsican language over French language has been declining. In 1980 about 70 percent of the population of the island "had some command of the Corsican language."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1990 out of a total population of about 254,000 the percentage had declined to 50 percent, with only 10 percent using it as a first language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cif_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cif-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (These figures do not count varieties of Corsican spoken in Sardinia.) The language appeared to be in serious decline when the French government reversed its unsupportive stand and initiated some strong measures to save it.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to an official survey run on behalf of the
       <i>
        Collectivité territoriale de Corse
       </i>
       which took place in April 2013, in Corsica the Corsican language has a number of speakers situated between 86,800 and 130,200, out of a total population amounting to 309,693 inhabitants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The percentage of those who have a solid knowledge of the language varies between a minimum of 25 percent in the 25-34 age group and the maximum of 65 percent in the over-65 age group: almost a quarter of the former age group does not understand Corsican, while only a small minority of the older people do not understand it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While 32 percent of the population of northern Corsica speaks Corsican quite well, this percentage drops to 22 percent for South Corsica.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moreover, 10 percent of the population of Corsica speaks only French, while 62 percent speak both French and Corsican.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, only 8 percent of the Corsicans know how to write correctly in Corsican, while about 60 percent of the population does not know how to write in Corsican.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While 90 percent of the population is in favor of a Corsican-French bilingualism, 3 percent would like to have Corsican as the only official language in the island, and 7 percent would prefer French in this role.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inch_13-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-inch-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       classifies Corsican as a "definitely endangered language."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Corsican language is a key vehicle for Corsican culture, which is notably rich in
       <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">
        proverbs
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Polyphony" title="Polyphony">
        polyphonic
       </a>
       song.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Governmental_support">
        Governmental support
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Governmental support">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:289px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Corsican_nationalism.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Corsican_nationalism.jpg/287px-Corsican_nationalism.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Corsican_nationalism.jpg/431px-Corsican_nationalism.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Corsican_nationalism.jpg/574px-Corsican_nationalism.jpg 2x" width="287"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Corsican_nationalism.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Bilingual road-signs, with official (
         <a href="/wiki/Institut_g%C3%A9ographique_national" title="Institut géographique national">
          IGN
         </a>
         ) French names (often in Italian) being crossed out by some local
         <a href="/wiki/Corsican_nationalism" title="Corsican nationalism">
          nationalists
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The 1991 "Joxe Statute", in setting up the Collectivité Territoriale de Corse, also provided for the
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_Assembly" title="Corsican Assembly">
        Corsican Assembly
       </a>
       , and charged it with developing a plan for the optional teaching of Corsican. The
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Corsica_Pasquale_Paoli" title="University of Corsica Pasquale Paoli">
        University of Corsica Pasquale Paoli
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Corte,_Haute-Corse" title="Corte, Haute-Corse">
        Corte, Haute-Corse
       </a>
       took a central role in the planning.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the primary school level Corsican is taught up to a fixed number of hours per week (three in the year 2000) and is a voluntary subject at the secondary school level,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but is required at the University of Corsica. It is available through adult education. It can be spoken in court or in the conduct of other government business if the officials concerned speak it. The Cultural Council of the Corsican Assembly advocates for its use; for example, on public signs.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the anthropologist Dumenica Verdoni, writing new literature in modern Corsican, known as the
       <i>
        Riacquistu
       </i>
       , is an integral part of affirming Corsican identity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some individuals have returned from careers in continental France to write in Corsican, including Dumenicu Togniotti, director of the
       <i>
        Teatru Paisanu
       </i>
       , which produced polyphonic musicals, 1973–1982, followed in 1980 by Michel Raffaelli's
       <i>
        Teatru di a Testa Mora
       </i>
       , and Saveriu Valentini's
       <i>
        Teatru Cupabbia
       </i>
       in 1984.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Modern prose writers include Alanu di Meglio, Ghjacumu Fusina, Lucia Santucci, and Marcu Biancarelli.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There were writers working in Corsican in the 1700s and 1800s
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Gregorovius" title="Ferdinand Gregorovius">
        Ferdinand Gregorovius
       </a>
       , 19th century traveller and enthusiast of Corsican culture, reported that the preferred form of the literary tradition of his time was the
       <i>
        vocero
       </i>
       , a type of polyphonic ballad originating from funeral obsequies. These laments were similar in form to the chorales of Greek drama except that the leader could improvise. Some performers were noted at this, such as the 1700s Mariola della Piazzole and Clorinda Franseschi.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the trail of written popular literature of known date in Corsican currently goes no further back than the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An undated corpus of proverbs from communes may well precede it (see under
       <i>
        External links
       </i>
       below). Corsican has also left a trail of legal documents ending in the late 12th century. At that time the monasteries held considerable land on Corsica and many of the churchmen were
       <a href="/wiki/Notary" title="Notary">
        notaries
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Between 1200 and 1425 the monastery of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorgona,_Italy" title="Gorgona, Italy">
        Gorgona
       </a>
       , which belonged to the
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">
        Order of Saint Benedict
       </a>
       for much of that time and was in the territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       , acquired about 40 legal papers of various sorts related to Corsica. As the church was replacing Pisan prelates with Corsican ones there, the legal language shows a transition from entirely Latin through partially Latin and partially Corsican to entirely Corsican. The first known surviving document containing some Corsican is a bill of sale from
       <a href="/wiki/Patrimonio" title="Patrimonio">
        Patrimonio
       </a>
       dated to 1220.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These documents were moved to Pisa before the monastery closed its doors and were published there. Research into earlier evidence of Corsican is ongoing.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet_and_spelling">
        Alphabet and spelling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alphabet and spelling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3456" data-file-width="4608" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg/220px-FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg/330px-FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg/440px-FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:FuneraryInscriptionInErbaggio.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Funerary Inscription In Corsican language at the cemetery of Erbaggio (
         <a href="/wiki/Nocario" title="Nocario">
          Nocario
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_alphabet" title="Corsican alphabet">
        Corsican alphabet
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Corsican is written in the standard
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       , using 21 of the letters for native words. The letters j, k, w, x, and y are found only in foreign names and French vocabulary. The digraphs and trigraphs
       <i>
        chj
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ghj
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sc
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        sg
       </i>
       are also defined as "letters" of the alphabet in its modern scholarly form (compare the presence of
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ll
       </i>
       in the Spanish alphabet) and appear respectively after
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The primary diacritic used is the
       <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">
        grave accent
       </a>
       , indicating word stress when it is not
       <a href="/wiki/Penult" title="Penult">
        penultimate
       </a>
       . In scholarly contexts,
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        disyllables
       </a>
       may be distinguished from
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongs
       </a>
       by use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">
        diaeresis
       </a>
       on the former vowel (as in Italian and distinct from French and English). In older writing, the
       <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">
        acute accent
       </a>
       is sometimes found on stressed ⟨e⟩, the
       <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">
        circumflex
       </a>
       on stressed ⟨o⟩, indicating respectively (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       ) and (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       ) phonemes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Corsican has been regarded as a dialect of Italian historically, similar to the Romance lects developed on the Italian peninsula, and in writing, it also resembles Italian (with the generalised substitution of -
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       for final -
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       and the articles
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        il/lo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       respectively; however, both the dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Cap_Corse" title="Cap Corse">
        Cap Corse
       </a>
       and Gallurese retain the original articles
       <i>
        lu
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ). On the other hand, the phonemes of the modern Corsican dialects have undergone complex and sometimes irregular phenomena depending on phonological context, so the pronunciation of the language for foreigners familiar with other Romance languages is not straightforward.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Corsican_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As in Italian, the grapheme ⟨i⟩ appears in some
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraphs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Trigraph_(orthography)" title="Trigraph (orthography)">
        trigraphs
       </a>
       in which it does not represent the phonemic vowel. All vowels are pronounced except in a few well-defined instances. ⟨i⟩ is not pronounced between ⟨sc/sg/c/g⟩ and ⟨a/o/u⟩:
       <i>
        sciarpa
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈʃarpa]
       </span>
       ; or initially in some words:
       <i>
        istu
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈstu]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Vowels may be nasalized before ⟨n⟩ (which is assimilated to ⟨m⟩ before ⟨p⟩ or ⟨b⟩) and the palatal nasal consonant represented by ⟨gn⟩. The nasal vowels are represented by the vowel plus ⟨n⟩, ⟨m⟩ or ⟨gn⟩. The combination is a digraph or trigraph indicating the nasalized vowel. The consonant is pronounced in weakened form. The same combination of letters might not be the digraph or trigraph but might be just the non-nasal vowel followed by the consonant at full weight. The speaker must know the difference. Example of nasal: ⟨pane⟩ is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpãnɛ]
       </span>
       and not
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpanɛ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Northern and central dialects in the vicinity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Taravo" title="Taravo">
        Taravo
       </a>
       river adopt the Italian seven-vowel system, whereas all the Southern ones around the so-called "archaic zone" with its centre being the town of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sartene" title="Sartene">
        Sartene
       </a>
       (including the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallurese" title="Gallurese">
        Gallurese
       </a>
       dialect spoken in Northern Sardinia) resort to a five-vowel system without length differentiation, like
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The vowel inventory, or collection of phonemic vowels (and the major allophones), transcribed in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IPA" title="IPA">
        IPA
       </a>
       symbols, is:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-omni_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-omni-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
