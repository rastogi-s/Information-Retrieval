       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       that either results in a
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
        palatalized consonant
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowel
       </a>
       , or is triggered by one of them. Palatalization involves change in the
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
        manner of articulation
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       , or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fronting_(phonology)" title="Fronting (phonology)">
        fronting
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raising_(phonology)" title="Raising (phonology)">
        raising
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       . In some cases, palatalization involves
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of palatalization in English is the colloquial pronunciation of
       <i>
        did you?
       </i>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dɪdʒuː]
       </span>
       rather than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dɪdjuː]
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Palatalization is sometimes an example of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       . In some cases, it is triggered by a palatal or palatalized consonant or front vowel, but in other cases, it is not conditioned in any way.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consonant">
        Consonant
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consonant">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Palatalization changes
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
        manner of articulation
       </a>
       of consonants. It may add palatal secondary articulation or change primary articulation from
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       to palatal or
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       , alveolar to
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It may also cause a consonant to change its manner of articulation from stop to affricate or fricative. The change in the manner of articulation is a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       . However, the lenition is frequently accompanied by a change in place of articulation.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_stop" title="Voiceless velar stop">
          k
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kʲ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_stop#Palatal_or_alveolo-palatal" title="Voiceless palatal stop">
          c
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">
          tʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant affricate">
          ts
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Palatalization of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velar_consonants" title="Velar consonants">
        velar
       </a>
       consonants commonly causes them to front, and
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal
       </a>
       consonants are usually raised. In the process, stop consonants are often spirantised except for palatalized labials.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Palatalization, as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       , is usually triggered only by
       <a href="/wiki/Mid_vowel" title="Mid vowel">
        mid
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Close_vowel" title="Close vowel">
        close
       </a>
       (high) front vowels and the
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        semivowel
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       . The sound that results from palatalization may vary from language to language. For example, palatalization of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       may produce
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʲ], [tʃ], [tɕ], [tsʲ], [ts]
       </span>
       , etc. A change from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       may pass through
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʲ]
       </span>
       as an intermediate state, but there is no requirement for that to happen.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some
       <a href="/wiki/Zoque_languages" title="Zoque languages">
        Zoque languages
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       does not palatalize velar consonants but it turns alveolars into palato-alveolars. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Nupe_language" title="Nupe language">
        Nupe language
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       are palatalized both before front vowels and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , while velars are only palatalized before front vowels. In
       <a href="/wiki/Luba-Kasai_language" title="Luba-Kasai language">
        Ciluba
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       palatalizes only a preceding
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       . In some variants of
       <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe_language" title="Ojibwe language">
        Ojibwe
       </a>
       , velars are palatalized before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , but apicals are not. In
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">
        Indo-Aryan languages
       </a>
       , dentals and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       are palatalized when occurring in clusters before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , but velars are not.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel">
        Vowel
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vowel">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Palatalization sometimes refers to
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_shift" title="Vowel shift">
        vowel shifts
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fronting_(phonology)" title="Fronting (phonology)">
        fronting
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">
        back vowel
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raising_(phonology)" title="Raising (phonology)">
        raising
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowel
       </a>
       . The shifts are sometimes triggered by a nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       or palatalized consonant or by a high front vowel. The
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_umlaut" title="Germanic umlaut">
        Germanic umlaut
       </a>
       is a famous example.
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar change is reconstructed in the history of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       in which
       <a href="/wiki/Bartsch%27s_law" title="Bartsch's law">
        Bartsch's law
       </a>
       turned
       <a href="/wiki/Open_vowel" title="Open vowel">
        open vowels
       </a>
       into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       after a palatalized velar consonant. If it was true for all open vowels in Old French, it would explain the palatalization of velar plosives before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Erzya_language" title="Erzya language">
        Erzya
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic language
       </a>
       , the open vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Open front unrounded vowel">
         a
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       is raised to near-open
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Near-open_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Near-open front unrounded vowel">
         æ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       after a palatalized consonant, as in the name of the language,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [erzʲæ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       , the back vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u o/
       </span>
       are fronted to central
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʉ ɵ]
       </span>
       , and the open vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       is raised to near-open
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [æ]
       </span>
       , near palatalized consonants. The palatalized consonants also factor in how
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction_in_Russian" title="Vowel reduction in Russian">
        unstressed vowels are reduced
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Unconditioned">
        Unconditioned
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Unconditioned">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Palatalization is sometimes unconditioned or spontaneous, not triggered by a palatal or palatalized consonant or front vowel.
      </p>
      <p>
       In southwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       , clusters of a voiceless
       <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">
        obstruent
       </a>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       were palatalized once or twice. This first palatalization was unconditioned. It resulted in a cluster with a palatal lateral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʎ]
       </span>
       , a palatal lateral on its own, or a cluster with a palatal approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       . In a second palatalization, the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       was affricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       or spirantized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
         Vulgar Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         clāmāre
        </i>
        "to call" &gt;
        <a href="/wiki/Aromanian_language" title="Aromanian language">
         Aromanian
        </a>
        <i>
         cl'imari
        </i>
        /kʎimari/,
        <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
         Aragonese
        </a>
        <i>
         clamar
        </i>
        /kʎamar/, Spanish
        <i>
         llamar
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎamar/
        </span>
        , Italian
        <i>
         chiamare
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kjaˈmare/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        &gt;
        <a href="/wiki/Istriot_language" title="Istriot language">
         Istriot
        </a>
        <i>
         ciamà
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃaˈma/
        </span>
        , Portuguese
        <i>
         chamar
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃɐˈmaɾ/
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
        Western Romance languages
       </a>
       , Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kt]
       </span>
       was palatalized once or twice. The first palatalization was unconditioned: the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       was vocalized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i̯t]
       </span>
       or spirantized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [çt]
       </span>
       . In a second palatalization, the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       was affricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃ]
       </span>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Vulgar Latin
        <i>
         noctem
        </i>
        "night" &gt;
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        <i>
         nuit
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [nɥi]
        </span>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        <i>
         noite
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnoj.tɨ]
        </span>
        , eastern
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         Occitan
        </a>
        <i>
         nuèit
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        <i>
         nit
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
         Mozarabic
        </a>
        <i>
         noxte
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /noçte/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        &gt; Spanish
        <i>
         noche
        </i>
        , western Occitan
        <i>
         nuèch
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">
         Romansh
        </a>
        <i>
         notg
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In many dialects of English, the back vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /uː/
       </span>
       is fronted to near-back
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [u̟ː]
       </span>
       , central
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʉː]
       </span>
       , or front
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [yː]
       </span>
       . This vowel shift is unconditioned, happening in all cases, and not triggered by another sound.
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar change is reconstructed for
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/Attic_Greek" title="Attic Greek">
        Attic dialect
       </a>
       before the
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Greece" title="Classical Greece">
        Classical period
       </a>
       , the back vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u uː/
       </span>
       were fronted to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [y yː]
       </span>
       . During the
       <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">
        Koine
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Greek" title="Medieval Greek">
        Medieval Greek
       </a>
       period, they were unrounded to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i iː]
       </span>
       , and they finally merged as short
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       , the pronunciation that they have in
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipatory_and_progressive">
        Anticipatory and progressive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Allophony_and_phonemic_split">
        Allophony and phonemic split
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Allophony and phonemic split">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Palatalization may result in a
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_change#Phonemic_splits" title="Phonological change">
        phonemic split
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical change
       </a>
       by which a
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       becomes two new phonemes over time through palatalization.
      </p>
      <p>
       Old historical splits have frequently drifted since the time they occurred and may be independent of current phonetic palatalization. The
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       tendency of palatalized consonants (by
       <a href="/wiki/Assibilation" title="Assibilation">
        assibilation
       </a>
       and deaffrication) is important. According to some analyses,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the lenition of the palatalized consonant is still a part of the palatalization process itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       , allophonic palatalization affected the
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       plosives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       , turning them into
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">
        alveolo-palatal
       </a>
       affricates
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tɕ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʑ]
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/R%C5%8Dmaji" title="Rōmaji">
        romanized
       </a>
       as ⟨ch⟩ and ⟨j⟩ respectively. Japanese has, however, recently regained phonetic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ti]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [di]
       </span>
       from loanwords, and the originally-allophonic palatalization has thus become lexical. A similar change has also happened in
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Belarusian_language" title="Belarusian language">
        Belarusian
       </a>
       . That would also be true about most dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       but for the strong
       <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">
        phonotactical
       </a>
       resistancy of its native speakers that turn dental plosives into post-alveolar affricates even in loanwords: McDonald's
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">
         [mɛ̞kiˈdõnɐwdʑ(is)]
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Votic_language" title="Votic language">
        Votic
       </a>
       has undergone such a change historically,
       <i>
        *keeli
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        tšeeli
       </i>
       'language', but there is currently an additional distinction between palatalized laminal and non-palatalized apical consonants. An extreme example occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , whose palatalized (
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_G" title="Hard and soft G">
        'soft'
       </a>
       )
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       has ended up as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       from a long process where Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       became palatalized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡʲ]
       </span>
       (Late Latin) and then affricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       (Proto-Romance), deaffricated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       (Old Spanish), devoiced to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       (16th century), and finally retracted to a
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       , giving
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       (c. 1650). (See
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Spanish_language" title="History of the Spanish language">
        History of the Spanish language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives" title="Phonological history of Spanish coronal fricatives">
        Phonological history of Spanish coronal fricatives
       </a>
       for more information).
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anglo-Frisian">
        Anglo-Frisian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Anglo-Frisian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_Old_English#Palatalization" title="Phonological history of Old English">
        Phonological history of Old English § Palatalization
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Frisian_languages" title="Anglo-Frisian languages">
        Anglo-Frisian
       </a>
       , the language that gave rise to English and the
       <a href="/wiki/Frisian_languages" title="Frisian languages">
        Frisian languages
       </a>
       , the velar stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k g/
       </span>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_cluster" title="Consonant cluster">
        consonant cluster
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sk/
       </span>
       were palatalized in certain cases and became the sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       . Many words with Anglo-Frisian palatalization survive in Modern English, and the palatalized sounds are typically spelled ⟨ch, (d)ge, y, sh⟩ in Modern English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Palatalization only occurred in certain environments, and so it did not apply to all words from the same
       <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
        root
       </a>
       . This is the origin of some
       <a href="/wiki/Alternation_(linguistics)" title="Alternation (linguistics)">
        alternations
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
        cognate
       </a>
       words, such as
       <i>
        speak
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        speech
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈspiːk ˈspiːtʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        cold
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        chill
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkoʊld ˈtʃɪl/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        burrow
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bury
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈbʌroʊ ˈbɛri/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        dawn
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        day
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈdɔːn ˈdeɪ/
       </span>
       . Here ⟨k⟩ originates from unpalatalized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       and ⟨w⟩ from unpalatalized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /g/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some English words with palatalization have unpalatalized cognates from the
       <a href="/wiki/Northumbrian_dialect" title="Northumbrian dialect">
        Northumbrian dialect
       </a>
       and from
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       , such as
       <i>
        shirt
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        skirt
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈʃərt ˈskərt/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        church
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        kirk
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈtʃərtʃ ˈkərk/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        ditch
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        dike
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈdɪtʃ ˈdaɪk/
       </span>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       only underwent
       <a href="/wiki/High_German_consonant_shift#/s/_&gt;_/ʃ/" title="High German consonant shift">
        palatalization of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /sk/
        </span>
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        cheese
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃiːz/
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        Käse
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kɛːzə/
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        lie
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        lay
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈlaɪ ˈleɪ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        liegen
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        legen
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈliːgən ˈleːgən/
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        fish
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Fisch
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /fɪʃ/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The pronunciation of
       <i>
        wicca
       </i>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈwɪkə]
       </span>
       with a hard
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spelling_pronunciation" title="Spelling pronunciation">
        spelling pronunciation
       </a>
       , since the actual Old English pronunciation gave rise to
       <i>
        witch
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Yod-coalescence">
        Yod-coalescence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Yod-coalescence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English_consonant_clusters#Yod-coalescence" title="Phonological history of English consonant clusters">
        Phonological history of English consonant clusters § Yod-coalescence
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       Later in English, another palatalization called yod-coalescence occurred. Alveolar stops and affricates were palatalized before the palatal approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , changing into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ tʃ ʃ ʒ]
       </span>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">
        General American
       </a>
       , yod-coalescence applies only in
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        unstressed

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages#Palatalization" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages § Palatalization
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       developed from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       , the colloquial form of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       . Various palatalizations occurred during the historical development of the Romance languages. Some groups of the Romance languages underwent more palatalizations than others. One palatalization affected all groups, some palatalizations affected most groups, and one affected only a few groups.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gallo-Romance">
        Gallo-Romance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Gallo-Romance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance
       </a>
       , Vulgar Latin *
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ka]
       </span>
       became *
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʃa]
       </span>
       very early, with the subsequent deaffrication and some further developments of the vowel. For instance:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cattus
        </i>
        "cat" &gt;
        <i>
         chat
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃa/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         calva
        </i>
        "bald" (fem.) &gt;
        <i>
         chauve
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃov/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        *
        <i>
         blanca
        </i>
        "white" (fem.) &gt;
        <i>
         blanche
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /blɑ̃ʃ/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         catēna
        </i>
        "chain" &gt;
        <i>
         chaîne
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃɛn/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         carus
        </i>
        "dear" &gt;
        <i>
         cher
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃɛʁ/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Early English borrowings from French show the original affricate, as
       <i>
        chamber
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈtʃeɪmbəɾ/
       </span>
       "(private) room" &lt; Old French
       <i>
        chambre
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃɑ̃mbrə/
       </span>
       &lt; Vulgar Latin
       <i>
        camera
       </i>
       ; compare French
       <i>
        chambre
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃɑ̃bʁ/
       </span>
       "room".
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Mouill.C3.A9">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mouillé">
        Mouillé
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <b>
        Mouillé
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        French pronunciation: ​
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [muje]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        moistened
       </i>
       ) is a term for palatal consonants in the Romance languages. Palatal consonants in the Romance languages developed from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">

         n
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       by palatalization.

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɲ/
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>

       </span>

           <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
            /x/
           </span>
           , represented by ⟨j⟩.
          </span>
         </li>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satem_languages">
        Satem languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Satem languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centum%E2%80%93satem_isogloss" title="Centum–satem isogloss">
        Centum–satem isogloss
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In certain Indo-European language groups, the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_reconstruction" title="Linguistic reconstruction">
        reconstructed
       </a>
       "palato-velars" of
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       (
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *ḱ, *ǵ, *ǵʰ
       </span>
       ) were palatalized into
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilants
       </a>
       . The language groups with and without palatalization are called satem and centum languages, after the characteristic developments of the PIE word for "hundred":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        PIE
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *(d)ḱm̥tóm
        </span>
        &gt;
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Avestan_language" title="Avestan language">
         Avestan
        </a>
        <i>
         satəm
        </i>
        (palatalization)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
           Latin
          </a>
          <i>
           <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
            centum
           </span>
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /kentum/
          </span>
          (no palatalization)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slavic_languages">
        Slavic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Slavic languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_first_palatalization" title="Slavic first palatalization">
        Slavic first palatalization
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_second_palatalization" title="Slavic second palatalization">
        Slavic second palatalization
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the Slavic languages, two palatalizations took place. Both affected the Proto-Slavic velars
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *k *g *x
       </span>
       . In the first palatalization, the velars before the front vowels
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *e *ē *i *ī
       </span>
       and the palatal approximant
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *j
       </span>
       changed to
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *č *ž *š
       </span>
       . In the second palatalization, the velars changed to
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        š
       </i>
       before the Proto-Slavic diphthongs
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *aj *āj
       </span>
       , which must have been
       <a href="/wiki/Monophthongization" title="Monophthongization">
        monophthongized
       </a>
       to
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *ē
       </span>
       by this time.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mandarin_Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Palatalization_(sound_change)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Mandarin Chinese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese#Initials" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese § Initials
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese_phonology#Palatal_series" title="Standard Chinese phonology">
        Standard Chinese phonology § Palatal series
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In many dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title='Which dialects have palatalization? Varieties of Chinese says "many northern and central varieties" have palatalization of velars or alveolars or both, but is not specific. (February 2015)'>
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       the alveolar sibilants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts tsʰ s/
       </span>
       and the velars
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k kʰ x/
       </span>
       were palatalized before the
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Chinese_model" title="Syllable">
        medials
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j ɥ/
       </span>
       and merged in pronunciation, yielding the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">
        alveolo-palatal sibilants
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tɕ tɕʰ ɕ/
       </span>
       . Alveolo-palatal consonants occur in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">
        Standard Chinese
       </a>
       and are written as ⟨
       <i lang="zh-Latn" title="ISO 7098 Chinese (Chinese language) transliteration" xml:lang="zh-Latn">
        j q x
       </i>
       ⟩ in
       <a href="/wiki/Pinyin" title="Pinyin">
        Pinyin
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postal_romanization" title="Postal romanization">
        Postal romanization
       </a>
       does not show palatalized consonants, reflecting the
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_(late_imperial_lingua_franca)" title="Mandarin (late imperial lingua franca)">
        dialect of the imperial court
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Qing_dynasty" title="Qing dynasty">
        Qing dynasty
       </a>
       . For instance, the name of the capital of
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       was formerly spelled
       <i>
        Peking
       </i>
       , but is now spelled
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
         Beǐjīng
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="nounderlines nowrap">
        <span class="unicode haudio">
         <span class="fn">
          <span style="white-space:nowrap">
           <a href="/wiki/File:Zh-Beijing.ogg" title="About this sound">
            <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
           </a>
          </span>
          <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Zh-Beijing.ogg" title="Zh-Beijing.ogg">
           <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
            [peɪ̀.tɕíŋ]
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
       , and
       <i>
        Tientsin
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Sian
       </i>
       were the former spellings of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tianjin" title="Tianjin">
         Tiānjīn
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="nounderlines nowrap">
        <span class="unicode haudio">
         <span class="fn">
          <span style="white-space:nowrap">
           <a href="/wiki/File:Zh-Tianjin.ogg" title="About this sound">
            <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
           </a>
          </span>
          <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Zh-Tianjin.ogg" title="Zh-Tianjin.ogg">
           <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
            [tʰjɛ́n.tɕín]
           </span>
          </a>
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Xi%27an" title="Xi'an">
         Xī'ān
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɕí.án]
       </span>
       .
