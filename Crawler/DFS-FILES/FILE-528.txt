      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        minimal pairs
       </b>
       are pairs of
       <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
        words
       </a>
       or phrases in a particular
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       that differ in only one phonological element, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toneme" title="Toneme">
        toneme
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Chroneme" title="Chroneme">
        chroneme
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and have distinct meanings. They are used to demonstrate that two
       <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
        phones
       </a>
       are two separate phonemes in the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many phonologists in the middle part of the 20th century had a strong interest in developing techniques for discovering the phonemes of unknown languages, and in some cases, they set up writing systems for the languages. The major work of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Pike" title="Kenneth Pike">
        Kenneth Pike
       </a>
       on the subject is
       <i>
        Phonemics: a technique for reducing languages to writing
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The minimal pair was an essential tool in the discovery process and was found by
       <b>
        substitution
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        commutation
       </b>
       tests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern phonology is much less interested in such issues, and the minimal pair is consequently considered
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2017)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       to be of little theoretical importance.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As an example for
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       , the pair "l
       <b>
        e
       </b>
       t" + "l
       <b>
        i
       </b>
       t" can be used to demonstrate that the phones
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       (in l
       <b>
        e
       </b>
       t) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɪ]
       </span>
       (in l
       <b>
        i
       </b>
       t) actually represent distinct phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɛ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɪ/
       </span>
       . An example for English
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consonants" title="Consonants">
        consonants
       </a>
       is the minimal pair of "
       <b>
        p
       </b>
       at" + "
       <b>
        b
       </b>
       at". The following table shows other pairs demonstrating the existence of various distinct phonemes in English. All of the possible minimal pairs for any language may be set out in the same way.
      </p>

      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        Phonemic differentiation
       </a>
       may vary between different
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of a language so a particular minimal pair in one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accent_(dialect)" title="Accent (dialect)">
        accent
       </a>
       may be a pair of
       <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
        homophones
       </a>
       in another. That means not that one of the phonemes is absent in the homonym accent but only that it is not contrastive in the same range of contexts.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minimal_pair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In addition to the minimal pairs of vowels and consonants provided above, others may be found:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantity">
        Quantity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minimal_pair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Quantity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many languages show contrasts between long and short vowels and consonants. A distinctive difference in length is attributed by some phonologists to a unit called a
       <a href="/wiki/Chroneme" title="Chroneme">
        chroneme
       </a>
       . Thus,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       has the following minimal pair that is based on long and short
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       :
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         spelling
        </th>
        <th>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntactic_gemination">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

       In some languages like Italian, word-initial consonants are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate" title="Geminate">

       <a href="/wiki/Prosodic_unit" title="Prosodic unit">

       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Juncture">
        Juncture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minimal_pair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Juncture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       English-speakers are able to hear the difference between, for example, "great ape" and "grey tape", but phonemically, the two phrases are identical:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡreɪteɪp/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The difference between the two phrases, which constitute a minimal pair, is said to be one of
       <a href="/wiki/Juncture" title="Juncture">
        juncture
       </a>
       . At the word boundary, a "plus juncture" /+/ is posited and said to be the factor conditioning allophones to allow distinctivity: the result is that "great ape" has an
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eɪ/
       </span>
       diphthong shortened by
       <a href="/wiki/Clipping_(phonetics)" title="Clipping (phonetics)">
        pre-fortis clipping
       </a>
       and, since it is not syllable-initial, a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       with little
       <a href="/wiki/Aspirated_consonant" title="Aspirated consonant">
        aspiration
       </a>
       (variously
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t˭]
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/T-glottalization" title="T-glottalization">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʔt]
        </span>
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
         ʔ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , etc., depending on dialect); meanwhile in "grey tape", the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eɪ/
       </span>
       has its full length and the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       is aspirated
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tʰ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Only languages with allophonic differences associated with grammatical boundaries have juncture as a phonological element. It is claimed that
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       does not have juncture as a phonological element
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so, for example, "
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         des petits trous
        </span>
       </i>
       " (little holes) and "
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         des petites roues
        </span>
       </i>
       " (little wheels), phonemically both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /depətitʁu/
       </span>
       , are phonetically identical.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Minimal_sets">
        Minimal sets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minimal_pair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Minimal sets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The principle of a simple binary opposition between the two members of a minimal pair may be extended to cover a
       <b>
        minimal set
       </b>
       in which a number of words differ from one another in terms of one phone in a particular position in the word.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">
        Swahili
       </a>
       are shown to be distinct by the following set of words:
       <i>
        pata
       </i>
       'hinge',
       <i>
        peta
       </i>
       'bend',
       <i>
        pita
       </i>
       'pass',
       <i>
        pota
       </i>
       'twist',
       <i>
        puta
       </i>
       'thrash'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, establishing such sets is not always straightforward
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and may require very complex study of multiple oppositions as expounded by, for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Trubetzkoy" title="Nikolai Trubetzkoy">
        Nikolai Trubetzkoy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The subject is beyond the scope of this article.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Teaching">
        Teaching
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Minimal_pair&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Teaching">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Minimal pairs were an important part of the theory of pronunciation teaching during its development in the period of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structuralist_linguistics" title="Structuralist linguistics">
        structuralist linguistics
       </a>
       , particularly in the 1940s and 1950s, and minimal pair drills were widely used to train students to discriminate among the phonemes of the target language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, later writers have criticized the approach as being artificial and lacking in relevance to language learners' needs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some writers have claimed that learners are likely not to hear differences between phones if the difference is not a phonemic one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of the objectives of
       <a href="/wiki/Contrastive_analysis" title="Contrastive analysis">
        contrastive analysis
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of languages' sound systems was to identify points of likely difficulty for language learners that would arise from differences in phoneme inventories between the native language and the target language. However, experimental evidence for this claim is hard to find, and the claim should be treated with caution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
