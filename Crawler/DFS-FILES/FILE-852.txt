      <p>
       <b>
        Cuban Spanish
       </b>
       —also referred to colloquially as simply
       <i>
        cubano
       </i>
       , or even
       <i>
        cubañol
       </i>
       —
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="Quotation needed from source to verify. (January 2018)">
           need quotation to verify
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       is the variety of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       as it is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       . As a
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean language variety
       </a>
       , Cuban Spanish shares a number of features with nearby varieties, including
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Coda" title="Syllable">
        coda
       </a>
       deletion,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
        seseo
       </a>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       ("aspiration").
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cuban_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Characteristic of Cuban Spanish is the weak pronunciation of consonants, especially at the end of a syllable. Syllable-final /s/ weakens to [h] or disappears entirely; word-final /n/ becomes [ŋ]; syllable-final /r/ may become [l] or [j], or even become entirely silent. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricative
       </a>
       variants of /d/, /b/, /g/ (i.e.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð], [β], [ɣ]
       </span>
       ) are also significantly weakened when occurring after a vowel:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð]
       </span>
       tends to disappear entirely, while
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       become weak
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approximant" title="Approximant">
        approximants
       </a>
       , with no friction at all and often barely audible as consonants. All of these characteristics occur to one degree or another in other Caribbean varieties, as well as in many dialects in
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       (in southern Spain) – the place of historical origin of these characteristics.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the most prominent features of Cuban Spanish is the
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable#Coda" title="Syllable">
        syllable coda
       </a>
       . This trait is shared with most American varieties of Spanish spoken in coastal and low areas (
       <i>
        Lowland Spanish
       </i>
       ), as well as with
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian Spanish
       </a>
       and the Spanish spoken in the southern half of the Iberian Peninsula.
      </p>
      <p>
       Take for example, the following sentence:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Esos perros no tienen dueños
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈesoh ˈperoh no ˈtjeneŋ ˈdweɲoh]
        </span>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        ('Those dogs do not have owners')
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Also, because
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       may also be deleted in the syllable coda and because this feature has variable realizations,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guitart1997_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guitart1997-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       any or all instances of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       in the above example may be dropped, potentially rendering
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈeso ˈpero no ˈtjeneŋ ˈdweɲo]
       </span>
       . Other examples:
       <i>
        disfrutar
       </i>
       ("to enjoy") is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dihfruˈtar]
       </span>
       , and
       <i>
        fresco
       </i>
       ("fresh") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈfrehko]
       </span>
       . In Havana,
       <i>
        después
       </i>
       ("after[ward]") is typically pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dehˈpwe]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Another instance of consonant weakening ("
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       ") in Cuban Spanish (as in many other dialects) is the deletion of intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       in the participle ending
       <i>
        ‑ado
       </i>
       , as in
       <i>
        cansado
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kanˈsa.o]
       </span>
       "tired"). More typical of Cuba and the Caribbean is the
       <a href="/wiki/Dissimilation" title="Dissimilation">
        dissimilation
       </a>
       of final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       in some verb infinitives; e.g.
       <i>
        parar
       </i>
       , to stop, can be realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [paˈral]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish of the eastern provinces (the five provinces comprising what was formerly
       <a href="/wiki/Oriente_Province" title="Oriente Province">
        Oriente Province
       </a>
       ) is closer to
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Spanish" title="Dominican Spanish">
        that of the Dominican Republic
       </a>
       than to the Spanish spoken in the western part of the island.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_syntax">
        Morphology and syntax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cuban_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphology and syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Cuban Spanish typically uses the diminutive endings
       <i>
        -ico
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ica
       </i>
       (instead of the standard
       <i>
        -ito
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ita
       </i>
       ) with stems that end in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       . For example,
       <i>
        plato
       </i>
       ("plate") &gt;
       <i>
        platico
       </i>
       (instead of
       <i>
        platito
       </i>
       ), and
       <i>
        momentico
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        momentito
       </i>
       ; but
       <i>
        cara
       </i>
       ("face") becomes
       <i>
        carita
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This form is common to the Venezuelan, Cuban, Costa Rican, Dominican, and Colombian dialects.
      </p>
      <p>
       The suffix
       <i>
        -ero
       </i>
       is often used with a place name to refer to a person from that place; thus
       <i>
        habanero
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        guantanamera
       </i>
       , etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A person from
       <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cuba" title="Santiago de Cuba">
        Santiago de Cuba
       </a>
       is
       <i>
        santiaguero
       </i>
       (compare
       <i>
        santiagués
       </i>
       "from
       <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">
        Santiago de Compostela
       </a>
       (Spain)",
       <i>
        santiaguino
       </i>
       "from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Chile" title="Santiago de Chile">
        Santiago de Chile
       </a>
       ").
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Question#By_grammatical_form" title="Question">
        <i>
         Wh
        </i>
        -questions
       </a>
       , when the subject is a pronoun, are usually not inverted. Where speakers of most other varieties of Spanish would ask "¿Qué quieres?" or "¿Qué quieres tú?", Cuban speakers would more often ask "¿Qué tú quieres?"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (This form is also characteristic of
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Spanish" title="Dominican Spanish">
        Dominican
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
        Puerto Rican Spanish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </p>
      <p>
       In keeping with the socialist polity of the country, the term
       <i>
        compañero/compañera
       </i>
       ("comrade" or "friend") is often used instead of the traditional
       <i>
        señor/señora
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (For a contrary view, see Corbett (2007: 137).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) Similarly, Cuban Spanish uses the familiar second-person pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       in many contexts where other varieties of Spanish would use the formal
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
       is practically non-existent in Cuba.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cuban_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Of all the regional variations of the Spanish language, traditional Cuban Spanish is most similar to and originates largely from the Spanish that is spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       of Spain. Cuba owes much of its speech patterns to the heavy Canarian migrations of the 19th and early 20th centuries. Migrations of other
       <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">
        Spaniards
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Galicians" title="Galicians">
        Galicians
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catalan_people" title="Catalan people">
        Catalans
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">
        Basques
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Asturians" title="Asturians">
        Asturians
       </a>
       also occurred but with less influence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Much of the typical Cuban replacements for standard Spanish vocabulary stems from Canarian
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       . For example,
       <i>
        guagua
       </i>
       ('bus') differs from standard Spanish
       <i>
        autobús
       </i>
       . The word
       <i>
        guagua
       </i>
       originated in the Canaries and is an
       <a href="/wiki/Onomatopoeia" title="Onomatopoeia">
        onomatopoeia
       </a>
       stemming from the sound of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Klaxon" title="Klaxon">
        Klaxon
       </a>
       horn. An example of Canarian usage for a Spanish word is the verb
       <i>
        fajarse
       </i>
       ('to fight').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Spain, the verb would be
       <i>
        pelearse
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        fajar
       </i>
       exists as a non-reflexive verb related to the hemming of a
       <a href="/wiki/Skirt" title="Skirt">
        skirt
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Much of the vocabulary that is peculiar to Cuban Spanish comes from the different historic influences on the island. Many words come from the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       , but some words are of West African, French, or indigenous
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_language" title="Taíno language">
        Taino
       </a>
       origin, as well as peninsular Spanish influence from outside the Canary Islands, such as Andalusian or Galician. American English has lent several words, including some for clothing, such as
       <i>
        pulóver
       </i>
       [sic] (which is used to mean "T-shirt") and
       <i>
        chor
       </i>
       ("shorts", with the typical Spanish change from English
       <i>
        sh
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Manners">
        Manners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cuban_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Manners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       To speak to the elderly or to strangers, Cubans sometimes speak more formally as a sign of respect. They shake hands both on greeting and on leaving someone. Men often exchange friendly hugs (
       <i>
        abrazos
       </i>
       ), and both men and women often greet friends and family with a hug and a kiss on the cheek.
      </p>
      <p>
       However Cubans tend to speak informally, such as by addressing a stranger with
       <i>
        mi corazón
       </i>
       ("my heart"),
       <i>
        mi vida
       </i>
       ("my life"), or
       <i>
        cariño
       </i>
       ("dear") are common.
       <i>
        Mi amor
       </i>
       ("my love") is used, even between strangers, when at least one of them is a woman (for example, in being served in a shop). Cubans are less likely to use the formal second-person singular pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       to speak to a stranger, elder or superior.
       <i>
        Tú
       </i>
       is considered acceptable in all but very formal situations; regular use of the
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       form can be seen by some Cubans as an affectation or a mark of coldness.
      </p>
