      <p>
       <b>
        Guatemalan Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Español guatemalteco
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the national variant of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central American
       </a>
       country of
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
        Guatemala
       </a>
       . About 13.7 million of the 16 million population speak Spanish. It includes the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        second-person
       </a>
       singular
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronoun
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         vos
        </a>
       </i>
       alongside the standard Spanish second-person singular pronouns
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       to form a three-level system of second-person singular address.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics_and_phonology">
        Phonetics and phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guatemalan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonetics and phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The presence of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo#Seseo" title="Seseo">
         Seseo
        </a>
        wherein
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        . Seseo is common to
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
         Canarian Spanish
        </a>
        varieties.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The pronunciation of intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        (orthographic
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        ) is "weak", without friction.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_2-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no confusion between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , as in the Caribbean.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        is pronounced velar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_2-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_3-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As Guatemala was part of
        <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
         First Mexican Empire
        </a>
        , Guatemalan dialect adopted the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
         voiceless alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡s]
        </span>
        and the cluster
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tl]
        </span>
        (originally
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tɬ/
        </span>
        ) represented by the respective digraphs &lt;tz&gt; and &lt;tl&gt; in loanwords of Nahuatl origin,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Quetzal" title="Quetzal">
          quetzal
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tlapalería
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡ɬapaleˈɾia]
        </span>
        ('hardware store'). Even words of Greek and Latin origin with &lt;tl&gt;, such as
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27Atl%C3%A1ntico%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''Atlántico''">
           <i>
            Atlántico
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27atleta%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''atleta''">
           <i>
            atleta
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        , are pronounced with the affricate:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        (compare
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Guatemalan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A number of words widely used in Guatemala which have
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">
        Mayan
       </a>
       or other native origins, in particular names for flora, fauna and
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponyms
       </a>
       . Some of these words are used in most, or all, Spanish-speaking countries, like
       <i>
        chocolate
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        aguacate
       </i>
       ("avocado"), and some are only used in Mexico and most Central American countries, like Guatemala and El Salvador. The latter include
       <i>
        guajolote
       </i>
       "turkey" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        huaxōlōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [waˈʃoːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       (although
       <i>
        chompipe
       </i>
       is also used;
       <i>
        pavo
       </i>
       is also used, as in other Spanish-speaking countries);
       <i>
        papalote
       </i>
       "kite" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        pāpālōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [paːˈpaːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       "butterfly"; and
       <i>
        jitomate
       </i>
       "tomato" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        xītomatl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [ʃiːˈtomat͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       . For a more complete list see
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Nahuatl_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin">
        List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin
       </a>
       . Local words include
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        chapín – Guatemalan
       </li>
       <li>
        chish - interjection signifying disgust
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        cincho - belt
       </li>
       <li>
        chucho - dog
       </li>
       <li>
        chumpa - jacket
       </li>
       <li>
        canche – blonde(not used in all of Guatemala)
       </li>
       <li>
        cabal – right on
       </li>
       <li>
        colocho - curly (usually when speaking of hair)
       </li>
       <li>
        ishto - kid (brat) (not used in all of Guatemala, varies from regions)
       </li>
       <li>
        mosh - oat porridge (not used in all of Guatemala, more formally used and said is "avena")
       </li>
       <li>
        patojo – child (young people)
       </li>
       <li>
        pisto - money
       </li>
       <li>
        pajilla - straw
       </li>
       <li>
        poporopo - popcorn
       </li>
       <li>
        shuco - dirty (not to be confused with a type of hot-dog of the same name native only to Guatemala City)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There are also many words unique to Central America, for example,
       <i>
        chunche
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        chochadas
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        babosadas
       </i>
       means "thing" or
       <i>
        stuff
       </i>
       in some places.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The words used to describe children (or kids) vary among the countries in Central America; in Guatemala they are often called
       <i>
        patojos
       </i>
       . Cipotes is also used in Guatemala in the eastern departments. In the western and northern departments of Guatemala "chamacos" is used to say children or kids. In Guatemala and Honduras the word
       <i>
        güiros
       </i>
       is also used.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In Guatemala (also in Honduras and El Salvador) money is called
       <i>
        pisto
       </i>
       , a term originally used by
       <a href="/wiki/Maya_peoples" title="Maya peoples">
        Maya peoples
       </a>
       in Guatemala.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
