   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Branching (linguistics)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        branching
       </b>
       refers to the shape of the
       <a href="/wiki/Parse_tree" title="Parse tree">
        parse trees
       </a>
       that represent the structure of sentences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Assuming that the language is being written or transcribed from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left_to_right" title="Left to right">
        left to right
       </a>
       , parse trees that grow down and to the right are
       <i>
        right-branching
       </i>
       , and parse trees that grow down and to the left are
       <i>
        left-branching
       </i>
       . The direction of branching reflects the position of
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        heads
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrases
       </a>
       , and in this regard, right-branching structures are
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head-initial" title="Head-initial">
         head-initial
        </a>
       </i>
       , whereas left-branching structures are
       <i>
        head-final
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       English has both right-branching (head-initial) and left-branching (head-final) structures, although it is more right-branching than left-branching.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       are almost fully left-branching (head-final). Some languages are mostly right-branching (head-initial), for example, Arabic.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Languages typically construct phrases with a
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head word
       </a>
       (or nucleus) and zero or more
       <i>
        dependents
       </i>
       (modifiers). The following phrases show the phrase heads in bold.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of left-branching phrases (= head-final phrases):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          the
          <b>
           house
          </b>
          <small>
           - Noun phrase (NP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          very
          <b>
           happy
          </b>
          <small>
           - Adjective phrase (AP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          too
          <b>
           slowly
          </b>
          <small>
           - Adverb phrase (AdvP)
           <br/>
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Examples of right-branching phrases (= head-initial phrases):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           laugh
          </b>
          loudly
          <small>
           - Verb phrase (VP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           with
          </b>
          luck
          <small>
           - Prepositional phrase (PP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <b>
           that
          </b>
          it happened
          <small>
           - Subordinator phrase (SP = subordinate clause)
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Examples of phrases that contain both left- and right-branching (= head-medial phrases):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          the
          <b>
           house
          </b>
          there
          <small>
           - Noun phrase (NP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          very
          <b>
           happy
          </b>
          with it
          <small>
           - Adjective phrase (AP)
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          only
          <b>
           laugh
          </b>
          loudly
          <small>
           - Verb phrase (VP)
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Concerning phrases such as
       <i>
        the house
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        the house there
       </i>
       , this article assumes the traditional NP analysis, meaning that the noun is deemed to be head over the determiner. On a DP-analsyis (
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner_phrase" title="Determiner phrase">
        determiner phrase
       </a>
       ), the phrase
       <i>
        the house
       </i>
       would be right-branching instead of left-branching.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tree_structures">
        Tree structures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tree structures">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Left- and right-branching structures are illustrated with the trees that follow. Each example appears twice, once according to a constituency-based analysis associated with a
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">
        phrase structure grammar
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and once according to a dependency-based analysis associated with a
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammar
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first group of trees illustrate left-branching:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching1.jpg" title="Branching picture 1">
             <img alt="Branching picture 1" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="401" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Branching1.jpg" width="401"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The upper row shows the constituency-based structures, and the lower row the dependency-based structures. In the constituency-based structures, left-branching is present (but not really visible) in so far as the non-head daughter is to the left of the head. In the corresponding dependency-based structures in the lower row, the left-branching is clear; the dependent appears to the left of its head, the branch extending down to the left. The following structures demonstrate right-branching:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching2.jpg" title="Branching picture 2">
             <img alt="Branching picture 2" data-file-height="291" data-file-width="432" height="291" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/82/Branching2.jpg" width="432"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The upper row again shows the constituency-based structures, and the lower row the dependency-based structures. The constituency-based structures are right-branching insofar as the non-head daughter is to the right of the head. This right-branching is completely visible in the lower row of dependency-based structures, where the branch extends down to the right. The (c)-examples contain one instance of right-branching (the upper branch) and one instance of left-branching (the lower branch). The following trees illustrate phrases that combine both types of branching:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching3.jpg" title="Branching picture 3">
             <img alt="Branching picture 3" data-file-height="282" data-file-width="553" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/23/Branching3.jpg" width="553"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The combination of left- and right-branching is now completely visible in both the constituency- and dependency-based trees. The head appears in a medial position, which means that the phrase combines both types of branching. Note that the (b)-trees also contain a PP phrase that is an instance of pure right-branching.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Full_trees">
        Full trees
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Full trees">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The nature of branching is most visible with full trees. The following trees have been carefully chosen to best illustrate the extent to which a structure can be entirely left- or entirely right-branching. The following sentence is completely left-branching. The constituency-based trees are on the left, and the dependency-based trees are on the right:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching4.jpg" title="Branching picture 4">
             <img alt="Branching picture 4" data-file-height="207" data-file-width="497" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4a/Branching4.jpg" width="497"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The category Po (= possessive) is used to label possessive '
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       . The following sentence is completely right-branching:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching5.jpg" title="Branching picture 5">
             <img alt="Branching picture 5" data-file-height="207" data-file-width="543" height="207" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/Branching5.jpg" width="543"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Most structures in English are, however, not completely left- or completely right-branching, but rather they combine both. The following trees illustrate what can be seen as a stereotypical combination of left- and right-branching in English:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching6.jpg" title="Branching picture 6">
             <img alt="Branching picture 6" data-file-height="243" data-file-width="660" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/66/Branching6.jpg" width="660"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Determiners (e.g.
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       ) always and subjects (e.g.
       <i>
        the child
       </i>
       ) usually appear on left branches in English, but infinitival verbs (e.g.
       <i>
        try
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eat
       </i>
       ) and the verb particle
       <i>
        to
       </i>
       usually appear on right branches. In the big picture, right-branching structures tend to outnumber the left-branching structures in English, which means that trees usually grow down to the right.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="X-bar_schema">
        X-bar schema
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: X-bar schema">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/X-bar_theory" title="X-bar theory">
        X-bar schema
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       combines left- and right-branching. The standard X-bar schema has the following structure:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching7.jpg" title="Branching picture 7">
             <img alt="Branching picture 7" data-file-height="98" data-file-width="126" height="98" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2c/Branching7.jpg" width="126"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       This structure is both left- and right branching. It is left-branching insofar as the bar-level projection of the head (X') follows the specifier, but it is right-branching insofar as the actual head (X
       <sup>
        0
       </sup>
       ) precedes the complement. Despite these conflicting traits, most standard X-bar structures (in English) are more right-branching than left-branching because specifiers tend to be less complex (i.e. fewer words) than complements.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Binary_vs._n-ary_branching">
        Binary vs. n-ary branching
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Binary vs. n-ary branching">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Much work in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_and_Binding_Theory" title="Government and Binding Theory">
        Government and Binding Theory
       </a>
       (GB), the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minimalist_Program" title="Minimalist Program">
        Minimalist Program
       </a>
       (MP), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_Functional_Grammar" title="Lexical Functional Grammar">
        Lexical Functional Grammar
       </a>
       (LFG) assumes all branching to be binary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other theories (both constituency- and dependency-based ones), e.g. early
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transformational_Grammar" title="Transformational Grammar">
        Transformational Grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Head-Driven_Phrase_Structure_Grammar" title="Head-Driven Phrase Structure Grammar">
        Head-Driven Phrase Structure Grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meaning-Text_Theory" title="Meaning-Text Theory">
        Meaning-Text Theory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Word_grammar" title="Word grammar">
        Word Grammar
       </a>
       , etc. allow for n-ary branching. This distinction can have a profound impact on the overall nature of the theory of syntax. The two main possibilities in a phrase structure grammar are illustrated with the following trees:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Branching8.jpg" title="Branching picture 8">
             <img alt="Branching picture 8" data-file-height="133" data-file-width="369" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/eb/Branching8.jpg" width="369"/>
            </a>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The binary branching on the left is closely associated with the structures of GB, MP, and LFG, and it is similar to what the X-bar schema assumes. The n-ary branching structure on the right is a more traditional approach to branching. One can muster arguments for both approaches. For instance, the critics of the strictly binary branching structures charge that the strict binarity is motivated more by a desire for theoretical purity than by empirical observation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Strictly binary branching structures increase the amount of syntactic structure (number of nodes) to the upper limit of what is possible, whereas flatter n-ary branching tends to restrict the amount of structure that the theory can assume. Worth noting in this area is that the more layered the syntactic structures are, the more
       <a href="/wiki/Discontinuity_(linguistics)" title="Discontinuity (linguistics)">
        discontinuities
       </a>
       can occur, which means the component of the theory that addresses discontinuities must play a greater role. Given the flatter structures associated with n-ary branching, certain phenomena (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Inversion_(linguistics)" title="Inversion (linguistics)">
        inversion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Shifting_(syntax)" title="Shifting (syntax)">
        shifting
       </a>
       ) do not result in discontinuities, a fact that reduces the role that the component for discontinuities must play in the theory.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tendencies">
        Tendencies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Branching_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Tendencies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As stated above, the main branching trait for a language is just a tendency and it often shows exceptions.
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , for example, while overwhelmingly right-branching, puts numeral modifiers before nouns and, in certain cases, objects before verbs. Languages like English and German, though regarded as being right-branching because the main verbs precede direct objects, place adjectives and numerals before their nouns.
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and most other languages of northeastern Asia and the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       , on the other hand, are practically a model for rigidly left-branching languages. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mon%E2%80%93Khmer_languages" title="Mon–Khmer languages">
        Mon–Khmer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian languages
       </a>
       of southeast Asia and many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_languages" title="African languages">
        African languages
       </a>
       come close to rigidly right-branching, with numerals as well as adjectives following their nouns and with degree words like
       <i>
        very
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        too
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        extremely
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        quite
       </i>
       following the adjectives they modify.
      </p>
