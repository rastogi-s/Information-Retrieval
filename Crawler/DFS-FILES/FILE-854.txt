      <p>
       <b>
        Puerto Rican Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        español puertorriqueño
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [espaˈɲol pweɾtoriˈkeɲo]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is the Spanish language as characteristically spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       and by millions of people of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_people" title="Puerto Rican people">
        Puerto Rican descent
       </a>
       living in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and elsewhere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It belongs to the group of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       variants and, as such, is largely derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">
        Influences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Influences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Andalusia_and_the_Canary_Islands">
        Andalusia and the Canary Islands
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Andalusia and the Canary Islands">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Since most of the original farmers and commoners of
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       between the 15th and 18th centuries came from
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       , the basis for most of Puerto Rican Spanish is
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       (particularly that of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       ). For example, the endings -ado, -ido, -edo often drop intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       in both Seville and
       <a href="/wiki/San_Juan,_Puerto_Rico" title="San Juan, Puerto Rico">
        San Juan
       </a>
       : hablado &gt; hablao, vendido &gt; vendío, dedo &gt; deo (intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       dropping is quite widespread in coastal American dialects).
      </p>
      <p>
       Seville Spanish is also the source of the merger of phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (e.g.,
       <i>
        co
        <b>
         s
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        co
        <b>
         c
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       ) that are both pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       in much of Andalusia and generally in all
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       dialects. This merger is called
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       and makes such pairs as cocer/coser, abrazar/abrasar, has/haz, and vez/ves homophonous. Another Andalusian trait is the tendency to weaken postvocalic consonants, particularly /-s/: 'los dos &gt; lo(h) do(h), 'buscar' &gt; buhcá(l) (aspiration or elimination of syllable-final /s/ is quite widespread in coastal American dialects).
      </p>
      <p>
       Pronouncing "l" instead of "r" in syllable-final position is also a trait of Puerto Rican Spanish that has similarities in Spain: - Andalusians sometimes do the opposite, replacing the letter "l" at the end of a syllable with "r" (e.g. saying "Huerva" instead of "
       <a href="/wiki/Huelva" title="Huelva">
        Huelva
       </a>
       ". People from working class areas of Seville can sometimes sound almost indistinguishable from Puerto Ricans (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Zatu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Zatu (page does not exist)">
        Zatu
       </a>
       , the singer of the band
       <a href="/wiki/SFDK_(band)" title="SFDK (band)">
        SFDK
       </a>
       from the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pino_Montano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pino Montano (page does not exist)">
        Pino Montano
       </a>
       district of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       being an example). This distinction is the main way of distinguishing between the two accents when examples in the "transition zone" exist.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nevertheless,
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian Spanish
       </a>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       ) made the major contribution to Puerto Rican Spanish, and can be considered the basis of the dialect and accent. Many Canarians came in hopes of establishing a better life in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Americas" title="The Americas">
        the Americas
       </a>
       . Most Puerto Rican immigration in the early 19th century included people from the Canary Islands, who, like Puerto Ricans, had inherited most of their linguistic traits from Andalusia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Canarian influence is most present in the language of those Puerto Ricans who live in the central mountain region, who blended it with the remnant vocabulary of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno" title="Taíno">
        Taíno
       </a>
       . Canarian and Caribbean dialects share a similar intonation which, in general terms, means that stressed vowels are usually quite long. Puerto Rican and Canarian Spanish accents are strikingly similar. When visiting
       <a href="/wiki/Tenerife" title="Tenerife">
        Tenerife
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Las_Palmas" title="Las Palmas">
        Las Palmas
       </a>
       , Puerto Ricans are usually taken at first hearing for fellow-Canarians from a distant part of the Canary archipelago.
      </p>
      <p>
       Later in the 19th century other Spanish immigrants from
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">
        Balearic Islands
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">
        Asturias
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
        Galicia
       </a>
       plus other European settlers—mostly from
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       (including
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
        Scotland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       , and even some
       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">
        overseas Chinese
       </a>
       —settled in Puerto Rico. Words from these regions and countries joined the linguistic stew.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Ta.C3.ADno_influence">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Taíno_influence">
        Taíno influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Taíno influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       settlers colonized Puerto Rico in the early 16th century, thousands of Taíno people lived on the island, but almost immediately fell victim to European diseases (chicken pox, measles, smallpox, influenza and the common cold) to which they had no natural immunity. This caused the rapid decline and almost complete destruction of the indigenous Taínos within the first fifty years of exposure to the European explorers and colonists.
      </p>
      <p>
       A great number of
       <a href="/wiki/Ta%C3%ADno_language" title="Taíno language">
        Taíno
       </a>
       words like hamaca (hammock), hurakán (hurricane), and tabaco (tobacco) came into general Spanish usage, similar to the employment of indigenous words on the North American mainland by the English-speaking colonists, i.e., maize (corn), moccasin (moccasin), moose (moose). Taíno names and/or Hispanicized Taíno names for geographic locations such as Arecibo, Bayamón, Caguas, Canóvanas, Guaynabo, Gurabo, Jayuya, Luquillo, Mayagüez, Moca, Naguabo and Vieques are to be found throughout Puerto Rico, again similar to the use of Native American words in respect to geographic locations on the North American mainland (Alabama, Connecticut, Dakota, Idaho, Illinois, Kentucky, Michigan, Mississippi, Ohio, Oklahoma, Tennessee, and Utah).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="African_influence">
        African influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: African influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        African
       </a>
       slaves were brought to the island in the 16th century. Although several African tribes have been recorded in Puerto Rico, it is the
       <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">
        Kongo
       </a>
       from Central Africa that is considered to have had the most influence on Puerto Rican Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the early colonial period many African slaves in Puerto Rico spoke
       <a href="/wiki/Bozal_Spanish" title="Bozal Spanish">
        Bozal Spanish
       </a>
       . Words like
       <i>
        gandul
       </i>
       (pigeon pea),
       <i>
        fufú
       </i>
       (a spell), and
       <i>
        malanga
       </i>
       (a root vegetable), are commonly used and are of African origin. There also is the Afro-Caribbean/West Indian influence, for example many words and expressions come from patois and creolized languages from the neighboring islands.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States_influence_and_Puerto_Rican_Anglicisms">
        United States influence and Puerto Rican Anglicisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States influence and Puerto Rican Anglicisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1898, during the armed conflicts of the
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Campaign" title="Puerto Rican Campaign">
        Puerto Rican Campaign
       </a>
       , Spain ceded Puerto Rico to the United States as part of a peace treaty that brought the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">
        Spanish–American War
       </a>
       to a sudden conclusion. The
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
        United States Army
       </a>
       and the early colonial administration tried to impose the
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        English
       </a>
       language on island residents. Between 1902 and 1948, the main language of instruction in public schools (used for all subjects except Spanish language courses) was English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Consequently, many American English words are now found in the Puerto Rican vocabulary.
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        English
       </a>
       has had a fluctuating status as a second official language of the Island, depending on the political party in power at the moment. The majority of Puerto Ricans today do not speak English at home, and Spanish remains the mother tongue of Puerto Ricans, regardless of their political views.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many third and fourth generation Puerto Ricans who live in the United States borrow English words or phrases in mid-sentence in a phenomenon called
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code-switching
       </a>
       , sometimes characterized as
       <a href="/wiki/Spanglish" title="Spanglish">
        Spanglish
       </a>
       . Puerto Rican writer
       <a href="/wiki/Giannina_Braschi" title="Giannina Braschi">
        Giannina Braschi
       </a>
       published the first Spanglish novel,
       <i>
        Yo-Yo Boing!
       </i>
       , in 1998, a book that represents the code-switching linguistic style of some Latino immigrants in the United States. However, this mixture of Spanish and English is simply an informal blending of languages, not a separate language or dialect, and is not a fundamental characteristic of Spanish or Puerto Rican culture. It is merely an occasional convenience used by speakers who are very fluent in the two languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       Puerto Rico has representation in the Royal Spanish Academy (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       ) and has its own national academy along with the other Spanish-speaking countries of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Accent">
        Accent
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Accent">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Puerto Rican accents, both in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , could be described as a reflection of
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       's historical Euro-Ethnic cultures.
      </p>
      <p>
       To understand the concept of the Puerto Rican accent in Spanish, one must remember that every country in Hispanic America has different accents in this language, many of which are very similar. The
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguayan
       </a>
       accents, for example, were heavily influenced by the presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italians
       </a>
       in those countries.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Puerto Rico's case,
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spaniards
       </a>
       arrived from many regions within Spain and brought with them their own regional dialects/accents. However, a large number of European immigrants to Puerto Rico throughout its history came from the south of Spain known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalucia" title="Andalucia">
        Andalucía
       </a>
       . An additional large number arrived from Spain's islands off the coast of North Africa, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . When visiting Tenerife or Las Palmas (Islas Canarias, Spain), Puerto Ricans are usually taken at first hearing for fellow Canarians from a distant part of the Canary archipelago. It is the accents of these regions which served as the basis of the style of Spanish spoken in Puerto Rico.
      </p>
      <p>
       The indigenous population of Taínos left a great many words, e.g., in the names of geographical areas of the Island (Jayuya, Mayagüez, etc.), while others are used to name everyday items such as "hamaca" (hammock) or to describe natural phenomena such as "huracán" (hurricane).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black_history_in_Puerto_Rico" title="Black history in Puerto Rico">
        Africans in Puerto Rico
       </a>
       were brought in as slave labor, the majority for work on coastal or lowland sugar plantations. They contributed a large number of words to colloquialisms and island cuisine as well as contributing to the intonation of words and influencing rhythms of speech. Such can be attested to by visiting those parts of the island where historically they have been present (almost exclusively along the coasts). Additionally, the Afro-Caribbean/West Indian patois/Creole linguistic presence is very strong and has influenced Puerto Rican culture, reflected in music (such as reggae) and culinary dishes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_Puerto_Rican" title="Chinese Puerto Rican">
        Chinese Puerto Ricans
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asians
       </a>
       that have established themselves in Puerto Rico also adopt the accent, but keep a tinge of an East Asian one.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Puerto Rican accent is somewhat similar to the accents used by those from the Spanish-speaking Caribbean basin, including
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       and those from the Caribbean/coastal regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       (particularly to a non-Puerto Rican). However, any similarity will depend on the level of education of the Puerto Rican speaker and their immediate Island geographic location. It also continues to be extremely similar to the accent of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canarian_people" title="Canarian people">
        Canary Islanders
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusian_people" title="Andalusian people">
        Andalusians
       </a>
       in southern Spain. Overall, most Puerto Ricans make an emphatic distinction between their accent and other Caribbean Spanish accents.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="U.S.-Puerto_Rican_accent_in_Spanish">
        U.S.-Puerto Rican accent in Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: U.S.-Puerto Rican accent in Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       U.S.-resident Puerto Ricans, descended from the large number of migrants who left the island throughout the 20th Century, can be found today en masse along the Eastern coast of the United States. In addition to major metropolitan areas such as New York, many Puerto Ricans also migrated to areas such as Connecticut, Chicago, New Jersey, Florida, Massachusetts, and even California. Because of their high-rates of military enlistment, large Puerto Rican populations are also found in isolated spots across the U.S. near military installations.
      </p>
      <p>
       The accent of post-first generation Puerto Rican migrants to the U.S. is heavily influenced by their predominantly English-speaking surroundings. While still sounding Puerto Rican, their accents are tinged by the area of the United States where they grew up. Their grammatical composition is sometimes influenced by American English rather than Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       First-generation Americans who grew up in Puerto Rico, whether born there or not, speak Spanish with an accent of the origin of their parents.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Puerto_Rican_accent_in_English">
        Puerto Rican accent in English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Puerto_Rican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Puerto Rican accent in English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During the time of the Spanish–American War, the United States Army and the early colonial administration imposed the English language on island residents. Consequently, many American words are now found in Puerto Rican vocabularies.
      </p>
      <p>
       As with any other case of a non-native learning a language, many Puerto Ricans learn a particular accent of English. If learned in the US, they may speak English as it is spoken in their region. Some Puerto Ricans still residing in the island acquire a distinctly American accent. Others will develop different variations of the accent depending on who or what the main influence was during the learning process. Not only English is taught from the first grade in most schools but also most English teachers (particularly private school teachers) are very fluent in the language.
      </p>
