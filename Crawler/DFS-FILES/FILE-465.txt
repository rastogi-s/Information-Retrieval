      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , an
       <b>
        adjective
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        <b>
         adj
        </b>
       </span>
       ) is a describing word, the main
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntactic
       </a>
       role of which is to
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_modifier" title="Grammatical modifier">
        qualify
       </a>
       a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       , giving more information about the object signified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Adjectives are one of the English
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
        parts of speech
       </a>
       , although historically they were classed together with the nouns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trask-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain words that were traditionally considered to be adjectives, including
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        this
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       , etc., are today usually classed separately, as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(class)" title="Determiner (class)">
        determiners
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech#History" title="Part of speech">
        Part of speech § History
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Noun#History" title="Noun">
        Noun § History
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Adjective
       </i>
       comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="la">
         (nōmen) adjectīvum
        </span>
       </i>
       "additional (noun)",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc">
        ἐπίθετον (ὄνομα)
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">
         translit.
        </a>
       </small>
       <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration">
        epítheton (ónoma)
       </i>
       ,
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">
         lit.
        </a>
       </small>
       'additional (noun)'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the grammatical tradition of Latin and Greek, because adjectives were
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflected
       </a>
       for gender, number, and case like nouns (a process called
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       ), they were considered a subtype of noun. The words that are today typically called nouns were then called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substantive" title="Substantive">
         substantive
        </a>
        nouns
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        nōmen substantīvum
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McMenomy_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McMenomy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The terms
       <i>
        noun substantive
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        noun adjective
       </i>
       were formerly used in English, but the terms are now obsolete.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trask-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_use">
        <span id="Attributive_adjective">
        </span>
        <span id="Predicative_adjective">
        </span>
        <span id="Substantive_adjective">
        </span>
        <span id="Form">
        </span>
        Types of use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A given occurrence of an adjective can generally be classified into one of three kinds of use:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         Attributive adjectives
        </b>
        are part of the
        <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
         noun phrase
        </a>
        headed by the noun they modify; for example,
        <i>
         happy
        </i>
        is an attributive adjective in "happy people". In some languages, attributive adjectives precede their nouns; in others, they follow their nouns; and in yet others, it depends on the adjective, or on the exact relationship of the adjective to the noun. In English, attributive adjectives usually precede their nouns in simple phrases, but often follow their nouns when the adjective is modified or qualified by a phrase acting as an adverb. For example: "I saw three happy kids", and "I saw three kids happy enough to jump up and down with glee." See also
        <a href="/wiki/Postpositive_adjective" title="Postpositive adjective">
         Postpositive adjective
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Predicative adjectives
        </b>
        are linked via a
        <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
         copula
        </a>
        or other linking mechanism to the noun or pronoun they modify; for example,
        <i>
         happy
        </i>
        is a predicate adjective in "they are happy" and in "that made me happy." (See also:
        <a href="/wiki/Predicative_expression" title="Predicative expression">
         Predicative expression
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Subject_complement" title="Subject complement">
         Subject complement
        </a>
        .)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nominalized_adjective" title="Nominalized adjective">
         Nominal adjectives
        </a>
        act almost as nouns. One way this can happen is if a noun is
        <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
         elided
        </a>
        and an attributive adjective is left behind. In the sentence, "I read two books to them; he preferred the sad book, but she preferred the happy",
        <i>
         happy
        </i>
        is a nominal adjective, short for "happy one" or "happy book". Another way this can happen is in phrases like "out with the old, in with the new", where "the old" means, "that which is old" or "all that is old", and similarly with "the new". In such cases, the adjective functions either as a
        <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">
         mass noun
        </a>
        (as in the preceding example) or as a plural
        <a href="/wiki/Count_noun" title="Count noun">
         count noun
        </a>
        , as in "The meek shall inherit the Earth", where "the meek" means "those who are meek" or "all who are meek".
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
        Distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Adjectives feature as a
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
        part of speech
       </a>
       (word class) in most
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       . In some languages, the words that serve the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">
        semantic
       </a>
       function of adjectives are categorized together with some other class, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        nouns
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       . In the phrase "a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford" title="Ford">
        Ford
       </a>
       car", "Ford" is unquestionably a noun, but its function is adjectival: to modify "car". In some languages adjectives can function as nouns: "un rojo", "a red (object)" (Span.). As for "confusion" with verbs, rather than an adjective meaning "big", a language might have a verb that means "to be big", and could then use an
       <a href="/wiki/Attributive_verb" title="Attributive verb">
        attributive verb
       </a>
       construction analogous to "big-being house" to express what English expresses as "big house". Such an analysis is possible for the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grammar" title="Chinese grammar">
        grammar of Standard Chinese
       </a>
       , for example.
      </p>
      <p>
       Different languages do not always use adjectives in exactly the same situations. For example, where English uses
       <i>
        to be hungry
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        hungry
       </i>
       being an adjective),
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_grammar" title="Dutch grammar">
        Dutch
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_grammar" title="French grammar">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">
        Spanish
       </a>
       use
       <i>
        honger hebben
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        avoir faim
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        tener hambre
       </i>
       respectively (literally "to have hunger", the words for "hunger" being nouns). Similarly, where Hebrew uses the adjective זקוק
       <i>
        zaqūq
       </i>
       (roughly "in need of"), English uses the verb "to need".
      </p>
      <p>
       In languages which have adjectives as a word class, they are usually an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_class_(linguistics)" title="Open class (linguistics)">
        open class
       </a>
       ; that is, it is relatively common for new adjectives to be formed via such processes as
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">
        derivation
       </a>
       . However,
       <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">
        Bantu languages
       </a>
       are well known for having only a small closed class of adjectives, and new adjectives are not easily derived. Similarly, native
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_adjectives" title="Japanese adjectives">
        Japanese adjectives
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       -adjectives) are considered a closed class (as are native verbs), although nouns (an open class) may be used in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">
        genitive
       </a>
       to convey some adjectival meanings, and there is also the separate open class of
       <a href="/wiki/Adjectival_noun_(Japanese)" title="Adjectival noun (Japanese)">
        adjectival nouns
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        na
       </i>
       -adjectives).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adverbs">
        Adverbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adverbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many languages, including English, distinguish between adjectives, which qualify nouns and pronouns, and
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverbs
       </a>
       , which mainly modify
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       , adjectives, and other adverbs. Not all languages have exactly this distinction and many languages, including English, have words that can function as both. For example, in English,
       <i>
        fast
       </i>
       is an adjective in "a
       <b>
        fast
       </b>
       car" (where it qualifies the noun
       <i>
        car
       </i>
       ), but an adverb in "he drove fast" (where it modifies the verb
       <i>
        drove
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_grammar" title="Dutch grammar">
        Dutch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/German_grammar" title="German grammar">
        German
       </a>
       , adjectives and adverbs are usually identical in form and many grammarians do not make the distinction, but patterns of inflection can suggest a difference:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Eine
         <b>
          kluge
         </b>
         neue Idee.
        </i>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          A
          <b>
           clever
          </b>
          new idea.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Eine
         <b>
          klug
         </b>
         ausgereifte Idee.
        </i>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          A
          <b>
           cleverly
          </b>
          developed idea.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       A German word like
       <i>
        klug
       </i>
       ("clever(ly)") takes endings when used as an attributive adjective, but not when used adverbially. (It also takes no endings when used as a predicative adjective:
       <i>
        er ist klug
       </i>
       , "he is clever".) Whether these are distinct parts of speech or distinct usages of the same part of speech is a question of analysis. It can be noted that while German linguistic terminology distinguishes
       <i>
        adverbiale
       </i>
       from
       <i>
        adjektivische Formen
       </i>
       , German refers to both as
       <i>
        Eigenschaftswörter
       </i>
       ("property words").
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Determiners">
        Determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        Determiner
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Linguists today distinguish determiners from adjectives, considering them to be two separate parts of speech (or
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_of_speech" title="Parts of speech">
         lexical categories
        </a>
       </i>
       ), but formerly determiners were considered to be adjectives in some of their uses. In English dictionaries, which typically still do not treat determiners as their own part of speech, determiners are often recognizable by being listed both as adjectives and as pronouns. Determiners are words that are neither nouns nor pronouns, yet reference a thing already in context. Determiners generally do this by indicating
       <a href="/wiki/Definiteness" title="Definiteness">
        definiteness
       </a>
       (as in
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">
        quantity
       </a>
       (as in
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        some
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        many
       </i>
       ), or another such property.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjective_phrases">
        Adjective phrases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Adjective phrases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective_phrase" title="Adjective phrase">
        Adjective phrase
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An adjective acts as the head of an
       <i>
        adjective phrase
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        adjectival phrase
       </i>
       (AP). In the simplest case, an adjective phrase consists solely of the adjective; more complex adjective phrases may contain one or more
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverbs
       </a>
       modifying the adjective ("
       <i>
        very
       </i>
       strong"), or one or more
       <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">
        complements
       </a>
       (such as "worth
       <i>
        several dollars
       </i>
       ", "full
       <i>
        of toys
       </i>
       ", or "eager
       <i>
        to please
       </i>
       "). In English, attributive adjective phrases that include complements typically follow the noun that they qualify ("an evildoer
       <i>
        devoid of redeeming qualities
       </i>
       ").
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_modifiers_of_nouns">
        Other modifiers of nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other modifiers of nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In many languages, including English, it is possible for nouns to modify other nouns. Unlike adjectives, nouns acting as modifiers (called
       <i>
        attributive nouns
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">
         noun adjuncts
        </a>
       </i>
       ) usually are not predicative; a beautiful park is beautiful, but a car park is not "car". The modifier often indicates origin ("
       <i>
        Virginia
       </i>
       reel"), purpose ("
       <i>
        work
       </i>
       clothes"), semantic
       <a href="/wiki/Patient_(grammar)" title="Patient (grammar)">
        patient
       </a>
       ("
       <i>
        man
       </i>
       eater") or semantic
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ("
       <i>
        child
       </i>
       actor"); however, it may generally indicate almost any semantic relationship. It is also common for adjectives to be
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">
        derived
       </a>
       from nouns, as in
       <i>
        boyish
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        birdlike
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        behavioral (behavioural)
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        famous
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        manly
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        angelic
       </i>
       , and so on.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many languages have special verbal forms called
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        participles
       </a>
       that can act as noun modifiers (alone or as the head of a phrase). Sometimes participles develop into pure adjectives. Examples of this in English include
       <i>
        relieved
       </i>
       (the past participle of the verb
       <i>
        relieve
       </i>
       , used as an adjective in sentences such as "I am so relieved to see you"),
       <i>
        spoken
       </i>
       (as in "the spoken word"), and
       <i>
        going
       </i>
       (the present participle of the verb
       <i>
        go
       </i>
       , used as an adjective in such phrases as "the going rate").
      </p>
      <p>
       Other constructs that often modify nouns include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositional
       </a>
       phrases (as in "a rebel
       <i>
        without a cause
       </i>
       "),
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">
        relative clauses
       </a>
       (as in "the man
       <i>
        who wasn't there
       </i>
       "), and
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       phrases (as in "a cake
       <i>
        to die for
       </i>
       "). Some nouns can also take complements such as
       <a href="/wiki/Content_clause" title="Content clause">
        content clauses
       </a>
       (as in "the idea
       <i>
        that I would do that
       </i>
       "), but these are not commonly considered
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_modifier" title="Grammatical modifier">
        modifiers
       </a>
       . For more information about possible modifiers and dependents of nouns, see
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase#Components_of_noun_phrases" title="Noun phrase">
        Components of noun phrases
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Order">
        Order
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Order">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In many languages, attributive adjectives usually occur in a specific order. In general, the adjective order in English can be summarised as: opinion, size, age, shape, colour, origin, material, purpose. This sequence is sometimes referred to by the mnemonic OSASCOMP.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Determiners – articles, adverbs, and other limiters (e.g.
        <i>
         three
        </i>
        blind mice)
       </li>
       <li>
        Observation/opinion – postdeterminers and limiter adjectives (e.g. a
        <i>
         real
        </i>
        hero, a
        <i>
         perfect
        </i>
        idiot) and adjectives subject to subjective measure (e.g. beautiful, interesting), or objects with a value (e.g.
        <i>
         good
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         bad
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         costly
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Size – adjectives denoting physical size (e.g.
        <i>
         tiny
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         big
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         extensive
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Age – adjectives denoting age (e.g., young, old, new, ancient, six-year-old)
       </li>
       <li>
        Shape – adjectives describing more detailed physical attributes than overall size (e.g.
        <i>
         round
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         sharp
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         swollen
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Colour – adjectives denoting colour (e.g.
        <i>
         white
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         black
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         pale
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Origin – denominal adjectives denoting source (e.g. French, volcanic, extraterrestrial).
       </li>
       <li>
        Material – denominal adjectives denoting what something is made of (e.g., woollen, metallic, wooden)
       </li>
       <li>
        Qualifier/purpose – final limiter, sometimes regarded as part of the noun (e.g., rocking chair, hunting cabin, passenger car, book cover)
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       This means that in English, adjectives pertaining to size precede adjectives pertaining to age ("little old", not "old little"), which in turn generally precede adjectives pertaining to color ("old white", not "white old"). So, we would say "One (quantity) nice (opinion) little (size) old (age) round (shape) white (color) brick (material) house."
      </p>
      <p>
       This order may be more rigid in some languages than others; in some, like Spanish, it may only be a default (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
         unmarked
        </a>
       </i>
       ) word order, with other orders being permissible. Other languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
        Tagalog
       </a>
       , follow their adjectival orders
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_grammar#Sequence_of_modifiers_in_a_noun_phrase" title="Tagalog grammar">
        as rigidly as
       </a>
       English.
      </p>
      <p>
       The normal adjectival order of English may be overridden in certain circumstances. For instance, the standard order of adjectives in English would mandate the phrase, "the bad big wolf," (opinion before size) but instead the correct phrase is, "the big bad wolf," as the ablaut reduplication rule that high vowels precede low vowels overrides the normal order of adjectives.
      </p>
      <p>
       Owing partially to borrowings from French, English has some adjectives that follow the noun as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodifier" title="Postmodifier">
        postmodifiers
       </a>
       , called
       <a href="/wiki/Postpositive_adjective" title="Postpositive adjective">
        postpositive adjectives
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        time immemorial
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Attorney_general" title="Attorney general">
         attorney general
        </a>
       </i>
       . Adjectives may even change meaning depending on whether they precede or follow, as in
       <i>
        proper
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        They live in a proper town
       </i>
       (a real town, not a village) vs.
       <i>
        They live in the town proper
       </i>
       (in the town itself, not in the suburbs). All adjectives can follow nouns in certain constructions, such as
       <i>
        tell me something new
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison">
        Comparison
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Comparison">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Comparison_(grammar)" title="Comparison (grammar)">
        Comparison (grammar)
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">
        Comparative
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In many languages, some adjectives are
       <i>
        comparable
       </i>
       . For example, a person may be "polite", but another person may be "more polite", and a third person may be the "most polite" of the three. The word "more" here modifies the adjective "polite" to indicate a comparison is being made, and "most" modifies the adjective to indicate an absolute comparison (a
       <i>
        superlative
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Among languages that allow adjectives to be compared, different means are used to indicate comparison. Some languages do not distinguish between
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">
        comparative
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superlative" title="Superlative">
        superlative
       </a>
       forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, many adjectives can take the suffixes "-er" and "-est" (sometimes requiring additional letters before the suffix; see forms for
       <i>
        far
       </i>
       below) to indicate the
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">
        comparative
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superlative" title="Superlative">
        superlative
       </a>
       forms, respectively:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "great", "greater", "greatest"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "deep, "deeper", "deepest"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Some adjectives are
       <i>
        irregular
       </i>
       in this sense:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "good", "better", "best"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "bad", "worse", "worst"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "many", "more", "most" (sometimes regarded as an
        <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
         adverb
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
         determiner
        </a>
        )
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "little", "less", "least"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Some adjectives can have both
       <i>
        regular
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        irregular
       </i>
       variations:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "old", "older", "oldest"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "far", "farther", "farthest"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       also
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "old", "elder", "eldest"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "far", "further", "furthest"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Another way to convey comparison is by incorporating the words "more" and "most". There is no simple rule to decide which means is correct for any given adjective, however. The general tendency is for simpler adjectives, and those from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_language" title="Anglo-Saxon language">
        Anglo-Saxon
       </a>
       to take the suffixes, while longer adjectives and those from
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       do not—but sometimes
       <i>
        sound
       </i>
       of the word is the deciding factor.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many adjectives do not naturally lend themselves to comparison. For example, some English speakers would argue that it does not make sense to say that one thing is "more ultimate" than another, or that something is "most ultimate", since the word "ultimate" is already absolute in its semantics. Such adjectives are called
       <i>
        non-comparable
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        absolute
       </i>
       . Nevertheless, native speakers will frequently play with the raised forms of adjectives of this sort. Although "pregnant" is logically non-comparable (either one is pregnant or not), one may hear a sentence like "She looks more and more pregnant each day". Likewise "extinct" and "equal" appear to be non-comparable, but one might say that a language about which nothing is known is "more extinct" than a well-documented language with surviving literature but no speakers, while
       <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">
        George Orwell
       </a>
       wrote "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others". These cases may be viewed as evidence that the base forms of these adjectives are not as absolute in their semantics as is usually thought.
      </p>
      <p>
       Comparative and superlative forms are also occasionally used for other purposes than comparison. In English comparatives can be used to suggest that a statement is only tentative or tendential: one might say "John is more the shy-and-retiring type," where the comparative "more" is not really comparing him with other people or with other impressions of him, but rather, could be substituting for "on the whole". In Italian, superlatives are frequently used to put strong emphasis on an adjective:
       <i>
        Bellissimo
       </i>
       means "most beautiful", but is in fact more commonly heard in the sense "extremely beautiful".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Restrictiveness">
        Restrictiveness
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Restrictiveness">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Restrictiveness" title="Restrictiveness">
        Restrictiveness
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Attributive adjectives, and other noun
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_modifier" title="Grammatical modifier">
        modifiers
       </a>
       , may be used either
       <i>
        restrictively
       </i>
       (helping to identify the noun's referent, hence "restricting" its reference) or
       <i>
        non-restrictively
       </i>
       (helping to describe an already-identified noun). For example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "He was a lazy sort, who would avoid a
        <b>
         difficult task
        </b>
        and fill his working hours with easy ones."
        <dl>
         <dd>
          "difficult" is restrictive – it tells us which tasks he avoids, distinguishing these from the easy ones: "Only those tasks that are difficult".
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "She had the job of sorting out the mess left by her predecessor, and she performed this
        <b>
         difficult task
        </b>
        with great acumen."
        <dl>
         <dd>
          "difficult" is non-restrictive – we already know which task it was, but the adjective describes it more fully: "The aforementioned task, which (by the way) is difficult"
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In some languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , restrictiveness is consistently marked; for example, in Spanish
       <i>
        la tarea difícil
       </i>
       means "the difficult task" in the sense of "the task that is difficult" (restrictive), whereas
       <i>
        la difícil tarea
       </i>
       means "the difficult task" in the sense of "the task, which is difficult" (non-restrictive). In English, restrictiveness is not marked on adjectives, but is marked on
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">
        relative clauses
       </a>
       (the difference between "the man
       <i>
        who recognized me
       </i>
       was there" and "the man,
       <i>
        who recognized me
       </i>
       , was there" being one of restrictiveness).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Agreement">
        Agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Adjective&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Agreement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some languages, adjectives alter their form to reflect the gender, case and number of the noun that they describe. This is called
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       or concord. Usually it takes the form of inflections at the end of the word, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <table border="0">
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            puella bon
            <b>
             a
            </b>
           </i>
          </td>
          <td rowspan="4" style="width:30px">
          </td>
          <td>
           (good girl, feminine singular nominative)
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            puellam bon
            <b>
             am
            </b>
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           (good girl, feminine singular accusative/object case)
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            puer bon
            <b>
             us
            </b>
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           (good boy, masculine singular nominative)
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            pueri bon
            <b>
             i
            </b>
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           (good boys, masculine plural nominative)
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic languages
       </a>
       , however, initial consonant
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       marks the adjective with a feminine singular noun, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <table border="0">
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            buachaill
            <b>
             m
            </b>
            aith
           </i>
          </td>
          <td rowspan="4" style="width:30px">
          </td>
          <td>
           (good boy, masculine)
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            girseach
            <b>
             mh
            </b>
            aith
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           (good girl, feminine)
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Often a distinction is made here between attributive and predicative usage. Whereas English is an example of a language in which adjectives never agree and French of a language in which they always agree, in German they agree only when used attributively, and in Hungarian only when used predicatively.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <table border="0">
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            The good
           </i>
           (Ø)
           <i>
            boys
           </i>
           .
          </td>
          <td rowspan="4" style="width:30px">
          </td>
          <td>
           <i>
            The boys are good
           </i>
           (Ø).
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            Les bon
            <b>
             s
            </b>
            garçons
           </i>
           .
          </td>
          <td>
           <i>
            Les garçons sont bon
            <b>
             s
            </b>
           </i>
           .
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            Die brav
            <b>
             en
            </b>
            Jungen
           </i>
           .
          </td>
          <td>
           <i>
            Die Jungen sind brav
           </i>
           (Ø).
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            A jó
           </i>
           (Ø)
           <i>
            fiúk.
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           <i>
            A fiúk jó
            <b>
             k
            </b>
            .
           </i>
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </dd>
      </dl>
