      <p>
       The
       <b>
        South Pole
       </b>
       , also known as the
       <b>
        Geographic South Pole
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Terrestrial South Pole
       </b>
       , is one of the two points where the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's axis of rotation
       </a>
       intersects its surface. It is the southernmost point on the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and lies on the opposite side of the Earth from the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Situated on the continent of
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       , it is the site of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Amundsen%E2%80%93Scott_South_Pole_Station" title="Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station">
        Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station
       </a>
       , which was established in 1956 and has been permanently staffed since that year. The Geographic South Pole is distinct from the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Magnetic_Pole" title="South Magnetic Pole">
        South Magnetic Pole
       </a>
       , the position of which is defined based on the Earth's magnetic field. The South Pole is at the center of the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern Hemisphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>
       For most purposes, the Geographic South Pole is defined as the southern point of the two points where the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">
        axis of rotation
       </a>
       intersects its surface (the other being the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        Geographic North Pole
       </a>
       ). However, the Earth's axis of rotation is actually subject to very small "wobbles" (
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">
        polar motion
       </a>
       ), so this definition is not adequate for very precise work.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate" title="Geographic coordinate">
        geographic coordinates
       </a>
       of the South Pole are usually given simply as 90°S, since its longitude is geometrically undefined and irrelevant. When a longitude is desired, it may be given as
       <span class="nowrap">
        0°.
       </span>
       At the South Pole, all directions face north. For this reason, directions at the Pole are given relative to "grid north", which points northwards along the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Along tight latitude circles, clockwise is east, and counterclockwise is west, opposite to the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Geographic South Pole is located on the continent of
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       (although this has not been the case for all of
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Earth" title="History of Earth">
        Earth's history
       </a>
       because of
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">
        continental drift
       </a>
       ). It sits atop a featureless, barren, windswept and icy plateau at an altitude of 2,835 metres (9,301 ft) above sea level, and is located about 1,300 km (800 mi) from the nearest open sea at
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Whales" title="Bay of Whales">
        Bay of Whales
       </a>
       . The ice is estimated to be about 2,700 metres (9,000 ft) thick at the Pole, so the land surface under the
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheet
       </a>
       is actually near sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The polar ice sheet is moving at a rate of roughly 10 metres per year in a direction between 37° and 40° west of grid north,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       down towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Weddell_Sea" title="Weddell Sea">
        Weddell Sea
       </a>
       . Therefore, the position of the station and other artificial features relative to the geographic pole gradually shift over time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Geographic South Pole is marked by a stake in the ice alongside a small sign; these are repositioned each year in a ceremony on
       <a href="/wiki/New_Year%27s_Day" title="New Year's Day">
        New Year's Day
       </a>
       to compensate for the movement of the ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antarcticsun2006_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-antarcticsun2006-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The sign records the respective dates that
       <a href="/wiki/Roald_Amundsen" title="Roald Amundsen">
        Roald Amundsen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">
        Robert F. Scott
       </a>
       reached the Pole, followed by a short quotation from each man, and gives the elevation as "9,301
       <i>
        FT.
       </i>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A new marker stake is designed and fabricated each year by staff at the site.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-antarcticsun2006_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-antarcticsun2006-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </a>
       . It is located some meters from the Geographic South Pole, and consists of a metallic sphere on a short bamboo pole, surrounded by the flags of the original
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty" title="Antarctic Treaty">
        Antarctic Treaty
       </a>
       signatory
       <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_state" title="Sovereign state">
        states
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <b>
        Amundsen's Tent
       </b>
       : The tent was erected by the Norwegian expedition led by Roald Amundsen on its arrival on 14 December 1911. It is currently buried beneath the snow and ice in the vicinity of the Pole. It has been designated a
       <a href="/wiki/Historic_Sites_and_Monuments_in_Antarctica" title="Historic Sites and Monuments in Antarctica">
        Historic Site or Monument
       </a>
       (HSM 80), following a proposal by Norway to the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System" title="Antarctic Treaty System">

       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atcm_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-atcm-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The precise location of the tent is unknown, but based on calculations of the rate of movement of the ice and the accumulation of snow, it is believed, as of 2010, to lie between 1.8 and 2.5 km (1.1 and 1.6 miles) from the Pole at a depth of 17 m (56 ft) below the present surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]

       <a href="/wiki/Operaci%C3%B3n_90" title="Operación 90">
        First Argentine Overland Polar Expedition
       </a>
       has been designated a
       <a href="/wiki/Historic_Sites_and_Monuments_in_Antarctica" title="Historic Sites and Monuments in Antarctica">
        Historic Site or Monument

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-1900">
        Pre-1900
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pre-1900">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1820, several expeditions claimed to have been the first to have sighted Antarctica, with the very first
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="Very first what? Claim or sighting? (September 2016)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       being the Russian expedition led by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faddey_Bellingshausen" title="Faddey Bellingshausen">
        Faddey Bellingshausen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mikhail_Lazarev" title="Mikhail Lazarev">
        Mikhail Lazarev
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first landing was probably just over a year later when American Captain John Davis, a sealer, set foot on the ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The basic geography of the Antarctic coastline was not understood until the mid-to-late 19th century. American naval officer
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Wilkes" title="Charles Wilkes">
        Charles Wilkes
       </a>
       claimed (correctly) that Antarctica was a new continent, basing the claim on his exploration in 1839–40,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while
       <a href="/wiki/James_Clark_Ross" title="James Clark Ross">
        James Clark Ross
       </a>
       , in his expedition of 1839–43, hoped that he might be able to sail all the way to the South Pole. (He was unsuccessful.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="1900.E2.80.931950">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1900–1950">
        1900–1950
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 1900–1950">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="321" data-file-width="486" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg/220px-Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg/330px-Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg/440px-Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aan_de_Zuidpool_-_p1913-160.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Amundsen's party at the South Pole, December 1911. From left to right:
         <a href="/wiki/Roald_Amundsen" title="Roald Amundsen">
          Amundsen
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Helmer_Hanssen" title="Helmer Hanssen">
          Hanssen
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Sverre_Hassel" title="Sverre Hassel">
          Hassel
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Wisting" title="Oscar Wisting">
          Wisting
         </a>
         (photo by fifth member
         <a href="/wiki/Olav_Bjaaland" title="Olav Bjaaland">
          Bjaaland
         </a>
         ).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       British explorer
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">
        Robert Falcon Scott
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_Expedition" title="Discovery Expedition">
        Discovery Expedition
       </a>
       of 1901–04 was the first to attempt to find a route from the Antarctic coastline to the South Pole. Scott, accompanied by
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Shackleton" title="Ernest Shackleton">
        Ernest Shackleton
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Adrian_Wilson" title="Edward Adrian Wilson">
        Edward Wilson
       </a>
       , set out with the aim of travelling as far south as possible, and on 31 December 1902, reached 82°16′ S.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Shackleton later returned to Antarctica as leader of the British Antarctic Expedition (
       <a href="/wiki/Nimrod_Expedition" title="Nimrod Expedition">
        Nimrod Expedition
       </a>
       ) in a bid to reach the Pole. On 9 January 1909, with three companions, he reached 88°23' S – 112 miles (180 km) from the Pole – before being forced to turn back.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first men to reach the Geographic South Pole were the
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Amundsen%27s_South_Pole_expedition" title="Amundsen's South Pole expedition">
        Roald Amundsen and his party
       </a>
       on December 14, 1911. Amundsen named his camp
       <a href="/wiki/Polheim" title="Polheim">
        Polheim
       </a>
       and the entire plateau surrounding the Pole
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_Haakon_VII_Vidde" title="King Haakon VII Vidde">
         King Haakon VII Vidde
        </a>
       </i>
       in honour of King
       <a href="/wiki/Haakon_VII_of_Norway" title="Haakon VII of Norway">
        Haakon VII of Norway
       </a>
       . Robert Falcon Scott returned to Antarctica with his second expedition, the
       <a href="/wiki/Terra_Nova_Expedition" title="Terra Nova Expedition">
        Terra Nova Expedition
       </a>
       , initially unaware of Amundsen's secretive expedition. Scott and four other men reached the South Pole on January 17, 1912, thirty-four days after Amundsen. On the return trip, Scott and his four companions all died of starvation and extreme cold.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1914 Ernest Shackleton's
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Trans-Antarctic_Expedition" title="Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition">
        Imperial Trans-Antarctic Expedition
       </a>
       set out with the goal of crossing Antarctica via the South Pole, but his ship, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Endurance_(1912_ship)" title="Endurance (1912 ship)">
         Endurance
        </a>
       </i>
       , was frozen in
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_ice" title="Sea ice">
        pack ice
       </a>
       and sank 11 months later. The overland journey was never made.
      </p>
      <p>
       US Admiral
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_Evelyn_Byrd" title="Richard Evelyn Byrd">
        Richard Evelyn Byrd
       </a>
       , with the assistance of his first pilot
       <a href="/wiki/Bernt_Balchen" title="Bernt Balchen">
        Bernt Balchen
       </a>
       , became the first person to fly over the South Pole on November 29, 1929.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="1950.E2.80.93present">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1950–present">
        1950–present
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1950–present">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SPSM.05.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="2756" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/SPSM.05.jpg/220px-SPSM.05.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/SPSM.05.jpg/330px-SPSM.05.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/SPSM.05.jpg/440px-SPSM.05.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SPSM.05.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Amundsen%E2%80%93Scott_South_Pole_Station" title="Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station">
          Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station
         </a>
         . The ceremonial pole and flags can be seen in the background, slightly to the left of centre, below the tracks behind the buildings. The actual geographic pole is a few more metres to the left. The buildings are raised on stilts to prevent snow build up.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       It was not until 31 October 1956 that humans once again set foot at the South Pole, when a party led by Admiral
       <a href="/wiki/George_J._Dufek" title="George J. Dufek">
        George J. Dufek
       </a>
       of the US Navy landed there in an R4D-5L Skytrain (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-47_Skytrain" title="C-47 Skytrain">
        C-47 Skytrain
       </a>
       ) aircraft. The US
       <a href="/wiki/Amundsen%E2%80%93Scott_South_Pole_Station" title="Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station">
        Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station
       </a>
       was established by air over 1956–1957 for the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">
        International Geophysical Year
       </a>
       and has been continuously staffed since then by research and support personnel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After Amundsen and Scott, the next people to reach the South Pole
       <i>
        overland
       </i>
       (albeit with some air support) were
       <a href="/wiki/Edmund_Hillary" title="Edmund Hillary">
        Edmund Hillary
       </a>
       (January 4, 1958) and
       <a href="/wiki/Vivian_Fuchs" title="Vivian Fuchs">
        Vivian Fuchs
       </a>
       (January 19, 1958) and their respective parties, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_Trans-Antarctic_Expedition" title="Commonwealth Trans-Antarctic Expedition">
        Commonwealth Trans-Antarctic Expedition
       </a>
       . There have been many subsequent expeditions to arrive at the South Pole by surface transportation, including those by Havola,
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_P._Crary" title="Albert P. Crary">
        Crary
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ranulph_Twistleton-Wykeham_Fiennes" title="Ranulph Twistleton-Wykeham Fiennes">
        Fiennes
       </a>
       . First group of women to reach the pole were Pam Young, Jean Pearson,
       <a href="/wiki/Lois_Jones_(scientist)" title="Lois Jones (scientist)">
        Lois Jones
       </a>
       , Eileen McSaveney, Kay Lindsay and Terry Tickhill in 1969.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1978 Michele Eileen Raney becomes the first woman to winter at the South Pole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Subsequent to the establishment, in 1987, of the logistic support base at
       <a href="/wiki/Patriot_Hills_Base_Camp" title="Patriot Hills Base Camp">
        Patriot Hills Base Camp
       </a>
       , the South Pole became more accessible to non-government expeditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       On December 30, 1989,
       <a href="/wiki/Arved_Fuchs" title="Arved Fuchs">
        Arved Fuchs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Reinhold_Messner" title="Reinhold Messner">
        Reinhold Messner
       </a>
       were the first to traverse Antarctica via the South Pole without animal or motorized help, using only skis and the help of wind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two women,
       <a href="/wiki/Tori_Murden" title="Tori Murden">
        Victoria E. Murden
       </a>
       and Shirley Metz reached the pole by land on January 17, 1989.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The fastest unsupported journey to the Geographic South Pole from the ocean is 24 days and one hour from
       <a href="/wiki/Hercules_Inlet" title="Hercules Inlet">
        Hercules Inlet
       </a>
       and was set in 2011 by Norwegian adventurer Christian Eide,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who beat the previous solo record set in 2009 by American
       <a href="/wiki/Todd_Carmichael" title="Todd Carmichael">
        Todd Carmichael
       </a>
       of 39 days and seven hours, and the previous group record also set in 2009 of 33 days and 23 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The fastest solo (female), unsupported and unassisted trek to the south pole was performed by
       <a href="/wiki/Hannah_McKeand" title="Hannah McKeand">
        Hannah McKeand
       </a>
       from the UK in 2006. She made the journey in 39 days 9hrs 33mins. She started on the 19th November 2006 and finished on the 28 December 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 2011/12 summer, separate expeditions by Norwegian
       <a href="/wiki/Aleksander_Gamme" title="Aleksander Gamme">
        Aleksander Gamme
       </a>
       and Australians
       <a href="/wiki/Cas_and_Jonesy" title="Cas and Jonesy">
        James Castrission and Justin Jones
       </a>
       jointly claimed the first unsupported trek without dogs or kites from the Antarctic coast to the South Pole and back. The two expeditions started from
       <a href="/wiki/Hercules_Inlet" title="Hercules Inlet">
        Hercules Inlet
       </a>
       a day apart, with Gamme starting first, but completing according to plan the last few kilometres together. As Gamme traveled alone he thus simultaneously became the first to complete the task solo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate_and_day_and_night">
        Climate and day and night
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Climate and day and night">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_Antarctica" title="Climate of Antarctica">
        Climate of Antarctica
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        Midnight sun
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        Polar night
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       During the southern winter (March–September), the South Pole receives no sunlight at all, and from May 11 to August 1, between extended periods of twilight, it is completely dark (apart from moonlight). In the summer (September–March), the sun is continuously above the horizon and appears to move in a counter-clockwise circle. However, it is always low in the sky, reaching a maximum of 23.5° in December. Much of the sunlight that does reach the surface is reflected by the white snow. This lack of warmth from the sun, combined with the high altitude (about 2,800 metres (9,200 ft)), means that the South Pole has one of the coldest climates on Earth (though it is not quite the coldest; that record goes to the region in the vicinity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Vostok_Station" title="Vostok Station">
        Vostok Station
       </a>
       , also in Antarctica, which lies at a higher elevation).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Temperatures at the South Pole are much lower than at the North Pole, primarily because the South Pole is located at altitude in the middle of a continental land mass, while the North Pole is at sea level in the middle of an ocean, which acts as a reservoir of heat.
      </p>
      <p>
       The South Pole is at an altitude of 9,300 feet (2,800 m) but feels like 11,000 feet (3,400 m).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Centrifugal force from the spin of the planet pulls the atmosphere toward the equator. The South Pole is colder than the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       because of the elevation difference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The North Pole is a few feet from sea level.
      </p>
      <p>
       In midsummer, as the sun reaches its maximum elevation of about 23.5 degrees, high temperatures at the South Pole in January average at −25.9 °C (−15 °F). As the six-month "day" wears on and the sun gets lower, temperatures drop as well: they reach −45 °C (−49 °F) around sunset (late March) and sunrise (late September). In midwinter, the average temperature remains steady at around −58 °C (−72 °F). The highest temperature ever recorded at the Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station was −12.3 °C (9.9 °F) on Christmas Day, 2011,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the lowest was −82.8 °C (−117.0 °F) on June 23, 1982
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (for comparison, the lowest temperature directly recorded anywhere on earth was −89.2 °C (−128.6 °F) at Vostok Station on July 21, 1983, though −93.2 °C (−135.8 °F) was measured indirectly by satellite in
       <a href="/wiki/East_Antarctica" title="East Antarctica">
        East Antarctica
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Dome_A" title="Dome A">
        Dome A
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dome_F" title="Dome F">
        Dome F
       </a>
       in August 2010
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The South Pole has a
       <a href="/wiki/Desert" title="Desert">
        desert
       </a>
       climate, receiving very little precipitation. Air humidity is near zero. However, high winds can cause the blowing of snowfall, and the accumulation of snow amounts to about 20 cm (8 in) per year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The former dome seen in pictures of the Amundsen–Scott station is partially buried due to snow storms, and the entrance to the dome had to be regularly bulldozed to uncover it. More recent buildings are raised on stilts so that the snow does not build up against the sides of them.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Time">
        Time
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Time">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In most places on Earth, local time is determined by
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       , such that the time of day is more-or-less synchronised to the position of the sun in the sky (for example, at midday the sun is roughly at its highest). This line of reasoning fails at the South Pole, where the sun rises and sets only once per year, and all lines of longitude, and hence all time zones, converge. There is no
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_priori_and_a_posteriori" title="A priori and a posteriori">
         a priori
        </a>
       </i>
       reason for placing the South Pole in any particular time zone, but as a matter of practical convenience the Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station keeps
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Zealand_Time" title="New Zealand Time">
        New Zealand Time
       </a>
       (UTC+12). This is because the US flies its resupply missions ("
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Deep_Freeze" title="Operation Deep Freeze">
        Operation Deep Freeze
       </a>
       ") out of
       <a href="/wiki/McMurdo_Station" title="McMurdo Station">
        McMurdo Station
       </a>
       , which is supplied from
       <a href="/wiki/Christchurch" title="Christchurch">
        Christchurch
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flora_and_fauna">
        Flora and fauna
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=South_Pole&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Flora and fauna">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Due to its exceptionally harsh climate, there are no native resident plants or animals at the South Pole. Remarkably, though, off-course
       <a href="/wiki/South_polar_skua" title="South polar skua">
        south polar skuas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Snow_petrel" title="Snow petrel">
        snow petrels
       </a>
       are occasionally seen there.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2000 it was reported that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microbes" title="Microbes">
        microbes
       </a>
       had been detected living in the South Pole ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
