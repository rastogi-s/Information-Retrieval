      <p>
       <b>
        Lard
       </b>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Domestic_pig" title="Domestic pig">
        pig
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fat" title="Fat">
        fat
       </a>
       in both its
       <a href="/wiki/Rendering_(animal_products)" title="Rendering (animal products)">
        rendered
       </a>
       and unrendered forms. It is obtained from any part of the pig where there is a high proportion of
       <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">
        adipose tissue
       </a>
       . It can be rendered by steaming it or boiling it in water and then separating the insoluble fat from the water, or by the use of dry heat. It is a semi-soft white fat with a high saturated fatty acid content and no transfats. Refined lard is usually sold as paper-wrapped blocks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Lard is commonly used in many cuisines around the world as a
       <a href="/wiki/Cooking" title="Cooking">
        cooking
       </a>
       fat or
       <a href="/wiki/Shortening" title="Shortening">
        shortening
       </a>
       , or as a
       <a href="/wiki/Spread_(food)" title="Spread (food)">
        spread
       </a>
       similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">
        butter
       </a>
       . It is an ingredient in various savoury dishes such as sausages, pâtés and fillings, and it is particularly favored for the preparation of pastry because of the "flakiness" it brings to the product. Its use in western contemporary cuisine has diminished with the increased popularity of vegetable oils, but many contemporary cooks and bakers still favor it over other fats for certain select uses. The culinary qualities of lard vary somewhat depending on the part of the pig from which the fat has been taken, and how the lard is processed.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lard_production">
        Lard production
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lard can be obtained from any part of the pig where there is a high concentration of
       <a href="/wiki/Adipose_tissue" title="Adipose tissue">
        fatty tissue
       </a>
       . The highest grade of lard, known as leaf lard, is obtained from the "flare"
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visceral_fat" title="Visceral fat">
        visceral fat
       </a>
       deposit surrounding the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kidneys" title="Kidneys">
        kidneys
       </a>
       and inside the
       <a href="/wiki/Loin" title="Loin">
        loin
       </a>
       . Leaf lard has little pork flavor, making it ideal for use in baked goods, where it is valued for its ability to produce flaky, moist pie crusts. The next-highest grade is obtained from
       <a href="/wiki/Fatback" title="Fatback">
        fatback
       </a>
       , the hard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subcutaneous_fat" title="Subcutaneous fat">
        subcutaneous fat
       </a>
       between the back skin and muscle of the pig. The lowest grade (for purposes of rendering into lard) is obtained from the soft
       <a href="/wiki/Caul_fat" title="Caul fat">
        caul fat
       </a>
       surrounding digestive organs, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Small_intestines" title="Small intestines">
        small intestines
       </a>
       , though caul fat is often used directly as a wrapping for roasting lean meats or in the manufacture of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P%C3%A2t%C3%A9s" title="Pâtés">
        pâtés
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-edibleren_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-edibleren-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lard may be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rendering_(animals)" title="Rendering (animals)">
        rendered
       </a>
       by either of two processes: wet or dry. In wet rendering, pig fat is boiled in water or steamed at a high temperature and the lard, which is insoluble in water, is skimmed off the surface of the mixture or separated in an industrial
       <a href="/wiki/Centrifuge" title="Centrifuge">
        centrifuge
       </a>
       . In dry rendering, the fat is exposed to high heat in a pan or oven without the presence of water (a process similar to frying bacon). The two processes yield somewhat differing products. Wet-rendered lard has a more neutral flavor, a lighter color, and a high
       <a href="/wiki/Smoke_point" title="Smoke point">
        smoke point
       </a>
       . Dry-rendered lard is somewhat more browned in color and flavor and has a lower smoke point.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-joyofcooking_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-joyofcooking-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rendered lard produces an unpleasant smell when mixed with oxygen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Industrially-produced lard, including much of the lard sold in supermarkets, is rendered from a mixture of high and low quality fat sourced from throughout the pig.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooks_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cooks-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lard is often
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogenated_oil" title="Hydrogenated oil">
        hydrogenated
       </a>
       to improve its stability at room temperature. Hydrogenated lard sold to consumers typically contains fewer than 0.5 g of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transfat" title="Transfat">
        transfats
       </a>
       per 13 g serving.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lard is also often treated with bleaching and deodorizing agents,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emulsifiers" title="Emulsifiers">
        emulsifiers
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antioxidants" title="Antioxidants">
        antioxidants
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Butylated_hydroxytoluene" title="Butylated hydroxytoluene">
        BHT
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These treatments make it more consistent and prevent spoilage. (Untreated lard must be refrigerated or frozen to prevent
       <a href="/wiki/Rancidification" title="Rancidification">
        rancidity
       </a>
       .)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siprelle_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-siprelle-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Consumers wanting a higher-quality source of lard typically seek out
       <a href="/wiki/Artisan" title="Artisan">
        artisanal
       </a>
       producers of the product, or render it themselves from leaf lard or fatback.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cooks_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cooks-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-siprelle_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-siprelle-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seatimes_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-seatimes-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfchron_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sfchron-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times1_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-times1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A by-product of dry-rendering lard is
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_frying" title="Deep frying">
        deep-fried
       </a>
       meat, skin and membrane tissue known as
       <a href="/wiki/Pork_rind" title="Pork rind">
        cracklings
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="893" data-file-width="1100" height="179" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png/220px-Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png/330px-Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png/440px-Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trimyristin-3D-vdW.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">
          triglyceride
         </a>
         molecule, the main constituent of lard.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lard consists mainly of fats, which in the language of chemistry are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Triglyceride" title="Triglyceride">
        triglycerides
       </a>
       . These triglycerides are composed of three
       <a href="/wiki/Fatty_acid" title="Fatty acid">
        fatty acids
       </a>
       and the distribution of fatty acids varies from oil to oil. In general lard is similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">
        tallow
       </a>
       in its composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pigs that have been fed different diets will have lard with a significantly different fatty acid content and
       <a href="/wiki/Iodine_value" title="Iodine value">
        iodine value
       </a>
       . Peanut-fed hogs or the acorn-fed pigs raised for
       <a href="/wiki/Jam%C3%B3n_ib%C3%A9rico" title="Jamón ibérico">
        Jamón ibérico
       </a>
       therefore produce a somewhat different kind of lard compared to pigs raised in North American farms that are fed corn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourcebook_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-sourcebook-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_cultural_use">
        History and cultural use
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tourtiere_Lard.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Tourtiere_Lard.jpg/220px-Tourtiere_Lard.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Tourtiere_Lard.jpg/330px-Tourtiere_Lard.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Tourtiere_Lard.jpg/440px-Tourtiere_Lard.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tourtiere_Lard.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Raw fatback being diced to prepare
         <a href="/wiki/Tourti%C3%A8re" title="Tourtière">
          tourtière
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lard has always been an important cooking and baking staple in cultures where
       <a href="/wiki/Pork" title="Pork">
        pork
       </a>
       is an important dietary item, the fat of pigs often being as valuable a product as their meat.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 19th century lard was used similarly to
       <a href="/wiki/Butter" title="Butter">
        butter
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       and many European nations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ullmann_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ullmann-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lard remained about as popular as butter in the early 20th century, and was widely used as a substitute for butter during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       . As a readily available
       <a href="/wiki/By-product" title="By-product">
        by-product
       </a>
       of modern pork production, lard had been cheaper than most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetable_oils" title="Vegetable oils">
        vegetable oils
       </a>
       , and it was common in many people's diet until the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Industrial_revolution" title="Industrial revolution">
        industrial revolution
       </a>
       made vegetable oils more common and more affordable.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vegetable_shortenings" title="Vegetable shortenings">
        Vegetable shortenings
       </a>
       were developed in the early 1900s, which made it possible to use vegetable-based fats in baking and in other uses where solid fats were called for. Negative publicity was generated by
       <a href="/wiki/Upton_Sinclair" title="Upton Sinclair">
        Upton Sinclair
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Jungle" title="The Jungle">
         The Jungle
        </a>
       </i>
       which, though fictional, portrayed men falling into rendering vats and being sold as lard.
      </p>
      <p>
       By the late 20th century lard began to be considered less healthy than vegetable oils (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Olive_oil" title="Olive oil">
        olive
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunflower" title="Sunflower">
        sunflower
       </a>
       oil) because of its high content of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturated_fatty_acids" title="Saturated fatty acids">
        saturated fatty acids
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cholesterol" title="Cholesterol">
        cholesterol
       </a>
       . However, despite its reputation, lard has less saturated fat, more
       <a href="/wiki/Unsaturated_fat" title="Unsaturated fat">
        unsaturated fat
       </a>
       and less cholesterol than an equal amount of butter by weight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sourcebook_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-sourcebook-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unlike many
       <a href="/wiki/Margarine" title="Margarine">
        margarines
       </a>
       and vegetable shortenings, unhydrogenated lard contains no transfats. It has also been regarded as a "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poverty_food" title="Poverty food">
        poverty food
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many restaurants in the western nations have eliminated the use of lard in their kitchens because of the health-related dietary restrictions of many of their customers, and religious pork-based dietary restrictions such as
       <a href="/wiki/Kashrut" title="Kashrut">
        Kashrut
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Halal" title="Halal">
        Halal
       </a>
       mean that some bakers will substitute beef
       <a href="/wiki/Tallow" title="Tallow">
        tallow
       </a>
       for lard.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, in the 1990s and early 2000s the unique culinary properties of lard were rediscovered by chefs and bakers, leading to a partial rehabilitation of this fat among "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foodies" title="Foodies">
        foodies
       </a>
       ". This trend has been partially driven by negative publicity about the transfat content of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partially_hydrogenated_vegetable_oils" title="Partially hydrogenated vegetable oils">
        partially hydrogenated vegetable oils
       </a>
       in vegetable shortening. Chef and food writer
       <a href="/wiki/Rick_Bayless" title="Rick Bayless">
        Rick Bayless
       </a>
       is a prominent proponent of the virtues of lard for certain types of cooking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-seatimes_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-seatimes-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sfchron_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-sfchron-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-times1_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-times1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wmail_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wmail-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is also again becoming popular in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       among aficionados of traditional British cuisine. This led to a "lard crisis" in late 2004.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-independent-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Culinary_use">
        Culinary use
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lard is one of the few edible oils with a relatively high smoke point, attributable to its high saturated fatty acids content. Pure lard is especially useful for cooking since it produces little smoke when heated and has a distinct flavor when combined with other foods. Many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chefs" title="Chefs">
        chefs
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bakers" title="Bakers">
        bakers
       </a>
       prize lard over other types of shortening because of its flavor and range of applications.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10Reasons_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-10Reasons-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Because of the relatively large fat crystals found in lard, it is extremely effective as a
       <a href="/wiki/Shortening" title="Shortening">
        shortening
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Baking" title="Baking">
        baking
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Pie" title="Pie">
        Pie
       </a>
       crusts made with lard tend to be flakier than those made with butter. Many cooks employ both types of fat in their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pastries" title="Pastries">
        pastries
       </a>
       to combine the shortening properties of lard with the flavor of butter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes2_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nytimes2-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kingarthur_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kingarthur-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lard was once widely used in the cuisines of Europe, China and the New World and still plays a significant role in
       <a href="/wiki/British_cuisine" title="British cuisine">
        British
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/European_cuisine" title="European cuisine">
        Central European
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_cuisine" title="Mexican cuisine">
        Mexican
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_cuisine" title="Chinese cuisine">
        Chinese
       </a>
       cuisines. In British cuisine, lard is a traditional ingredient in mince pies and Christmas puddings,
       <a href="/wiki/Lardy_cake" title="Lardy cake">
        lardy cake
       </a>
       and for frying
       <a href="/wiki/Fish_and_chips" title="Fish and chips">
        fish and chips
       </a>
       as well as many other uses. Indeed, there are some people in England that eat lard neat, especially at the Lard Championships, held each year in Dorset, with 5000 people attending in the summer of 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guardian_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-guardian-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-independent_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-independent-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lard is traditionally one of the main ingredients in the
       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
        Scandinavian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/P%C3%A2t%C3%A9" title="Pâté">
        pâté
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leverpostej" title="Leverpostej">
        leverpostej
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>

      <p>
       In Spain, one of the most popular versions of the Andalusian breakfast includes several kinds of
       <i>
        mantecas
       </i>
       differently seasoned, consumed spread over toasted bread. Among other variants,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Manteca_color%C3%A1" title="Manteca colorá">
         manteca colorá
        </a>
       </i>
       (lard with paprika)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        zurrapa de lomo
       </i>
       (lard with pork flakes)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are the preferred ones. In
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_cuisine" title="Catalan cuisine">
        Catalan cuisine
       </a>
       lard is used to make the dough for the pastry known as
       <a href="/wiki/Coca_(pastry)" title="Coca (pastry)">
        coca
       </a>
       . In the Balearics particularly,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ensaimada" title="Ensaimada">
        ensaimades
       </a>
       dough also contains lard.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="383" data-file-width="620" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg/220px-Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg/330px-Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg/440px-Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Chleb_ze_smalcem.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A slice of bread spread with lard was a typical staple in traditional rural cuisine of many countries.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lard consumed as a
       <a href="/wiki/Spread_(food)" title="Spread (food)">
        spread
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Bread" title="Bread">
        bread
       </a>
       was once very common in Europe and North America, especially those areas where dairy fats and vegetable oils were rare.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-penguin1_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-penguin1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As the demand for lard grows in the high-end restaurant industry, small farmers have begun to specialize in heritage hog breeds with higher body-fat contents than the leaner, modern hog. Breeds such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Mangalica" title="Mangalica">
        Mangalitsa hog
       </a>
       of Hungary or
       <a href="/wiki/Large_Black_pig" title="Large Black pig">
        Large Black pig
       </a>
       of Great Britain are experiencing an enormous resurgence, to the point that breeders are unable to keep up with demand.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HeritageBreeds_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HeritageBreeds-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When used without qualification the word 'lard' in English generally refers to wet-rendered lard, which has a very mild, neutral flavor as opposed to the more noticeably pork-flavored dry-rendered lard, or
       <a href="/wiki/Dripping" title="Dripping">
        dripping
       </a>
       . Dripping sandwiches are still popular in several European countries—
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_cuisine" title="Hungarian cuisine">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       <i>
        zsíroskenyér
       </i>
       ("lardy bread") or
       <i>
        zsírosdeszka
       </i>
       ("lardy plank"), and
       <a href="/wiki/German_cuisine" title="German cuisine">
        German
       </a>
       "Fettbemme", seasoned pork fat. Similar snacks are sometimes served with beer in Poland, Czech Republic and Slovakia. They are generally topped with
       <a href="/wiki/Onion" title="Onion">
        onions
       </a>
       , served with
       <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
        salt
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paprika" title="Paprika">
        paprika
       </a>
       , and eaten as a side-dish with
       <a href="/wiki/Beer" title="Beer">
        beer
       </a>
       . All of these are commonly translated on menus as "lard" sandwiches, perhaps due to the lack of familiarity of most contemporary English native speakers with dripping. Attempts to use Hungarian
       <i>
        zsír
       </i>
       or Polish
       <i>
        smalec
       </i>
       (both meaning "fat/lard") when British recipes calling for lard will reveal the difference between the wet-rendered lard and dripping.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">
        Taiwan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
        Hong Kong
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macao" title="Macao">
        Macao
       </a>
       , as well as in many parts of
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       , lard was often consumed mixed into cooked
       <a href="/wiki/Rice" title="Rice">
        rice
       </a>
       along with
       <a href="/wiki/Soy_sauce" title="Soy sauce">
        soy sauce
       </a>
       to make "lard rice" (豬油拌飯 or 豬油撈飯). And in Japan, back loin (fatback) lard is frequently used for
       <a href="/wiki/Ramen" title="Ramen">
        ramen
       </a>
       , creating a thick, nutty, slightly sweet and very hearty dish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Traditionally, along with peanut oil, lard is extensively used in Asian cooking as a general-purpose cooking oil, esp. in stir-fries and deep-frying.
      </p>

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       lard is called
       <i>
        Schweineschmalz
       </i>
       (literally, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rendered_fat" title="Rendered fat">
        rendered fat
       </a>
       from swine") and has been a longtime favorite as a
       <a href="/wiki/Spread_(food)" title="Spread (food)">
        spread
       </a>
       . It can be served plain, or it can be mixed with seasonings: pork fat can be enhanced with small pieces of pork skin, called
       <i>
        Grieben
       </i>
       (cf. Yiddish
       <a href="/wiki/Gribenes" title="Gribenes">
        gribenes
       </a>
       ) to create
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Griebenschmalz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Griebenschmalz (page does not exist)">
         Griebenschmalz
        </a>
       </i>
       . Other recipes call for small pieces of apple or onion. In English, however,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Schmaltz" title="Schmaltz">
         schmaltz
        </a>
       </i>
       usually refers to kosher fat rendered from chicken, duck or goose.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Schmalzbrot
       </i>
       ("bread with
       <i>
        Schmalz
       </i>
       ") can be found on the menu in grounded restaurants or brewery pubs.
       <i>
        Schmalzbrot
       </i>
       is often served as
       <i>
        Griebenschmalz
       </i>
       on rye bread accompanied with pickled gherkin.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vegetarian
       <i>
        Grieben
       </i>
       from onions or apples, which began as a makeshift means of diluting
       <i>
        Schmalz
       </i>
       in time of need, became rather popular on their own account because they allow for a specific taste and a lower fat content. Completely vegetarian
       <i>
        Schmalz
       </i>
       -like spreads based on vegetable fats use those ingredients as well. In Germany it is forbidden to use the term
       <i>
        Schmalz
       </i>
       for non-lard products.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
        Poland
       </a>
       lard is often served as a starter. It is mixed with fruit, usually chopped apple, and spread on thick slices of bread.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_uses">
        Other uses
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Rendered lard can be used to produce
       <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
        biofuel
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Soap" title="Soap">
        soap
       </a>
       . Lard is also useful as a
       <a href="/wiki/Cutting_fluid" title="Cutting fluid">
        cutting fluid
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Machining" title="Machining">
        machining
       </a>
       . Its use in machining has declined since the mid-20th century as other specially engineered cutting fluids became prominent. However it is still a viable option. Lard and other animal fats were formerly used as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anti-foaming_agent" title="Anti-foaming agent">
        anti-foaming agent
       </a>
       in industrial
       <a href="/wiki/Fermentation" title="Fermentation">
        fermentation
       </a>
       processes such as
       <a href="/wiki/Brewing" title="Brewing">
        brewing
       </a>
       ; there, animal fats have been superseded by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyether" title="Polyether">
        polyethers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
