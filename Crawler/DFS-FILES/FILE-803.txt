      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish proverbs
       </b>
       are a subset of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proverbs" title="Proverbs">
        proverbs
       </a>
       that are used in Western cultures in general; there are many that have essentially the same form and content as their counterparts in other Western languages. Proverbs that have their origin in Spanish have migrated to and from
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flemish_language" title="Flemish language">
        Flemish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       and other languages.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_proverbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many Spanish proverbs have a long history of cultural diffusion; there are proverbs, for example, that have their origin traced to
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylon
       </a>
       and that have come down to us through
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greece
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       ; equivalents of the Spanish proverb
       <i>
        “En boca cerrada no entran moscas”
       </i>
       (Silence is golden) belong to the cultural tradition of many
       <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
        north-African countries
       </a>
       as far as
       <a href="/wiki/Ethiopia" title="Ethiopia">
        Ethiopia
       </a>
       ; having gone through multiple languages and millennia, this proverb can be traced back to an ancient Babylonian proverb.
      </p>
      <p>
       The written evidence of the use of Spanish proverbs goes far back in Spanish literature.
       <i>
        El Cantar de Mio Cid
       </i>
       , written at the end of the 11th or the beginning of the 12th century, is the first instance. Examples of other early works that use Spanish proverbs are the
       <i>
        Libro de Buen Amor
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ruiz" title="Juan Ruiz">
        Juan Ruiz
       </a>
       (14th century) and
       <i>
        El Corbacho
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_Toledo" title="Alfonso Martínez de Toledo">
        Alfonso Martínez de Toledo
       </a>
       (15th century). The first anthology of Spanish proverbs,
       <i>
        Proverbios que dicen las viejas tras el fuego
       </i>
       , was written by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C3%8D%C3%B1igo_L%C3%B3pez_de_Mendoza,_marqu%C3%A9s_de_Santillana" title="Íñigo López de Mendoza, marqués de Santillana">
        Íñigo López de Mendoza, Marques of Santillana
       </a>
       (15th century). Also in the 15th century was written the
       <i>
        Seniloquium
       </i>
       , an erudite and anonymous work containing a compendium of Spanish sayings and proverbs with commentaries. The language of the characters in
       <a href="/wiki/Fernando_de_Rojas" title="Fernando de Rojas">
        Fernando de Rojas
       </a>
       ’
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Celestina" title="La Celestina">
         La Celestina
        </a>
       </i>
       (15th – 16th century) is enlivened with the use of proverbs. And then, of course, in the 17th century there is the incomparable
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Don_Quijote_de_la_Mancha" title="Don Quijote de la Mancha">
         Don Quijote de la Mancha
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sancho_Panza" title="Sancho Panza">
        Sancho Panza
       </a>
       , Cervantes’ wonderful, earthy, character, is the essential common man. His thinking habitually relies on the authority he vests in the wealth of popular cultural wisdom expressed in proverbs, which he continually quotes. In many of his utterances there is reference to one and sometimes to more proverbs.
       <i>
        Don Quijote
       </i>
       is a veritable treasure trove of Spanish proverbs.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are Spanish proverbs that contradict others; the “wisdom” that they encapsulate is not, of course, absolute. People will use those proverbs that best conform to their own particular way of approaching life. Taken together, however, they reveal the deep wellsprings of Spanish culture and of human nature in general.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_proverbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Al buen callar llaman Sancho
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            They call the person that shuts up, Sancho.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Recommends prudence and moderation in talk.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Comments:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            According to some authors, for instance José Mª Sbarbi, this proverb has its origin in a historical episode involving
            <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sancho_II_of_Castile" title="Sancho II of Castile">
             Sancho II of Castile
            </a>
            . When his father
            <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Leon_and_Castile" title="Ferdinand I of Leon and Castile">
             Ferdinand I of Leon and Castile
            </a>
            at his death in 1065 divided up his kingdom among his three sons, including himself, Sancho II remained silent. Soon after his father's death, however, he turned on his brothers and succeeded in dispossessing them, reuniting thus his father's possessions under his control in 1072. The author Correas, however, sustains that
            <i>
             Sancho
            </i>
            is used as a variation of the adjective
            <i>
             santo
            </i>
            (saintly) and should therefore be written in lower-case.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Cada buhonero alaba sus agujas
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            A peddler praises his needles (wares).
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meanings/uses:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Each seller tries to convince potential buyers that his merchandise is the best.
           </dd>
           <dd>
            In a broader sense, people tend to praise what is theirs, often overstating qualities.
           </dd>
           <dd>
            Used ironically to criticize a person who boasts about his merits.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Cada gallo canta en su muladar
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Each rooster sings on its dung-heap.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meanings/uses:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Each person rules in his own house or territory.
           </dd>
           <dd>
            A person manifests his true nature when surrounded by family or close friends, when in his own ambience and in his place of origin.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Cada martes tiene su domingo
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Each Tuesday has its Sunday.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/Use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Exhorts to optimism, reminding that bad comes in alternation with good.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Comments:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            In this Spanish proverb “good” is represented by Sunday, a festive day in Christian culture, whereas Tuesday, a week-day of less joyous character, stands for “bad”.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Cada uno habla de la feria como le va en ella
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            One talks about the fair according to how one fares.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Our way of talking about things reflects our relevant experience, good or bad.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Dime con quien andas y te diré quién eres.
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Tell me who you walk with, and I will tell you who you are.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            According to your friends, mates, etc. you will be either a good person or a not so good person.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Donde comen dos, comen tres.
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Wherever two people eat, three people eat.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            You can add one person more in any situation you are managing.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         El amor es ciego
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Love is blind
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            We are blind to the defects and failings of our beloved (person or thing).
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         El amor todo lo iguala
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Love smoothes life out.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Love makes difficulties endurable.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         El mejor escribano echa un borrón
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            The best scribe makes a blot.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Excuses a first-time fault, especially of a very able person.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         El tiempo todo lo cura
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal translation:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Time cures all.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            There are problems, ills and circumstances that are only healed with the passing of time, either by them being actually solved or by us learning to cope with them.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         La avaricia rompe el saco
        </b>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Literal meaning:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Greed bursts the sack.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Meaning/use:
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            Greed and excessive ambition can stand in the way of obtaining benefit or success.
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Comments:
          </i>
