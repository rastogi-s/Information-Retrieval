     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        clitic
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       κλιτικός
       <i>
        klitikos
       </i>
       , "inflexional") is a
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morpheme
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       that has syntactic characteristics of a word, but depends
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonologically
       </a>
       on another word or phrase. In this sense, it is syntactically independent but phonologically dependent, always attached to a host.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil-clitic_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sil-clitic-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term is derived from the Greek for "leaning".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crystal_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-crystal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A clitic is pronounced like an
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affix
       </a>
       , but plays a syntactic role at the
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrase
       </a>
       level. In other words, clitics have the
       <i>
        form
       </i>
       of affixes, but the distribution of
       <a href="/wiki/Function_word" title="Function word">
        function words
       </a>
       . For example, the contracted forms of the auxiliary verbs in
       <i>
        I'm
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        we've
       </i>
       are clitics.
      </p>
      <p>
       Clitics can belong to any grammatical category, although they are commonly
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adposition" title="Adposition">
        adpositions
       </a>
       . Note that
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       is not always a good guide for distinguishing clitics from affixes: clitics may be written as separate words, but sometimes they are joined to the word on which they depend (like the Latin clitic
       <i>
        -que
       </i>
       , meaning "and"), or separated by special characters such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">
        hyphens
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">
        apostrophes
       </a>
       (like the English clitic
       <i>
        ’s
       </i>
       in "it's" for "it has" or "it is").
      </p>

      </p>

      <p>
       Clitics fall into various categories depending on their position in relation to the word to which they are connected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crystal_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-crystal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proclitic">
        Proclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A proclitic appears before its host.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crystal_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-crystal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is common in Romance languages. For example, in French, there is "il
       <b>
        s'
       </b>
       est réveillé" ("he woke up"), or "je
       <b>
        t'
       </b>
       aime" ("I love you").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Enclitic">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>

       </sup>
      </p>

         </i>
         <b>
          que
         </b>
         Romanus

       </li>
      </ul>

            <b>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mesoclitic">
        Mesoclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mesoclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A mesoclitic appears between the
       <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">
        stem
       </a>
       of the host and other affixes. For example, in Portuguese,
       <i>
        conquistar-
        <b>
         se
        </b>
        -á
       </i>
       ("it will be conquered"),
       <i>
        dá-
        <b>
         lo
        </b>
        -ei
       </i>
       ("I will give it"),
       <i>
        matá-
        <b>
         la
        </b>
        -ia
       </i>
       ("he/she/it would kill her"). These are found much more often in writing than in speech. It is even possible to use two pronouns inside the verb, as in
       <i>
        dar-
        <b>
         no
        </b>
        -
        <b>
         lo
        </b>
        -á
       </i>
       ("he/she/it will give it to us"), or
       <i>
        dar-
        <b>
         ta
        </b>
        -ei
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        ta
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        te
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       , "I will give it/her to you"). As in other Romance languages, the Portuguese synthetic future tense comes from the merging of the infinitive and the corresponding finite forms of the verb
       <i>
        haver
       </i>
       (from Latin
       <i>
        habēre
       </i>
       ), which explains the possibility of separating it from the infinitive.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Endoclitic">
        Endoclitic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Endoclitic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The endoclitic splits apart the root and is inserted between the two pieces. Endoclitics defy the
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_Integrity_Hypothesis" title="Lexical Integrity Hypothesis">
        Lexical Integrity Hypothesis
       </a>
       (or Lexicalist Hypothesis) and so were long claimed to be impossible. However, evidence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Udi_language" title="Udi language">
        Udi language
       </a>
       suggests that they exist.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Endoclitics are also found in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
        Pashto
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and are reported to exist in
       <a href="/wiki/Degema_language" title="Degema language">
        Degema
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction">
        Distinction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distinction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One important distinction divides the broad term 'clitics' into two categories, simple clitics and special clitics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-miller-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This distinction is, however, disputed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_clitics">
        Simple clitics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Simple clitics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Simple clitics are free morphemes, meaning they can stand alone in a phrase or sentence. They are unaccented and thus phonologically dependent upon a nearby word. They only derive meaning from this "host".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-miller-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Special_clitics">
        Special clitics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Special clitics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Special clitics are morphemes that are bound to the word they are dependent upon, meaning they exist as a part of their host. This form, which is unaccented, represents a variant of a free form that does carry stress. While the two variants carry similar meaning and phonological makeup, the special clitic is bound to a host word and unaccented.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-miller_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-miller-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">
        Properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some clitics can be understood as elements undergoing a historical process of
       <a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">
        grammaticalization
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        lexical item → clitic → affix
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klavans_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-klavans-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       According to this model from
       <a href="/wiki/Judith_Klavans" title="Judith Klavans">
        Judith Klavans
       </a>
       , an autonomous lexical item in a particular context loses the properties of a fully independent word over time and acquires the properties of a morphological affix (prefix, suffix, infix, etc.). At any intermediate stage of this evolutionary process, the element in question can be described as a "clitic". As a result, this term ends up being applied to a highly heterogeneous class of elements, presenting different combinations of word-like and affix-like properties.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klavans_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-klavans-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prosody">
        Prosody
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Prosody">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One characteristic shared by many clitics is a lack of
       <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">
        prosodic
       </a>
       independence. A clitic attaches to an adjacent word, known as its
       <i>
        host
       </i>
       . Orthographic conventions treat clitics in different ways: Some are written as separate words, some are written as one word with their hosts, and some are attached to their hosts, but set off by punctuation (a hyphen or an apostrophe, for example).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_affixes">
        Comparison with affixes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Comparison with affixes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although the term "clitic" can be used descriptively to refer to any element whose grammatical status is somewhere in between a typical word and a typical affix, linguists have proposed various definitions of "clitic" as a technical term. One common approach is to treat clitics as words that are prosodically deficient: they cannot appear without a host, and they can only form an accentual unit in combination with their host. The term "
       <b>
        postlexical clitic
       </b>
       " is used for this narrower sense of the term.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Given this basic definition, further criteria are needed to establish a dividing line between postlexical clitics and morphological affixes, since both are characterized by a lack of prosodic autonomy. There is no natural, clear-cut boundary between the two categories (since from a historical point of view, a given form can move gradually from one to the other by morphologization). However, by identifying clusters of observable properties that are associated with core examples of clitics on the one hand, and core examples of affixes on the other, one can pick out a battery of tests that provide an empirical foundation for a clitic/affix distinction.
      </p>
      <p>
       An affix syntactically and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonologically
       </a>
       attaches to a base
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morpheme
       </a>
       of a limited
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
        part of speech
       </a>
       , such as a verb, to form a new word. A clitic syntactically functions above the word level, on the
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrase
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">
        clause
       </a>
       level, and attaches only phonetically to the first, last, or only word in the phrase or clause, whichever part of speech the word belongs to.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The results of applying these criteria sometimes reveal that elements that have traditionally been called "clitics" actually have the status of affixes (e.g., the Romance pronominal clitics discussed
       <a href="#Romance_languages">
        below
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Spencer-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Zwicky and Pullum postulated five characteristics that distinguish clitics from affixes:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Spencer-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Clitics do not select their hosts. That is, they are "promiscuous", attaching to whichever word happens to be in the right place. Affixes do select their host: They only attach to the word they are connected to semantically, and generally attach to a particular part of speech.
       </li>
       <li>
        Clitics do not exhibit arbitrary gaps. Affixes, on the other hand, are often lexicalized and may simply not occur with certain words. (English plural -s, for example, does not occur with "child".)
       </li>
       <li>
        Clitics do not exhibit morphophonological idiosyncrasies. That is, they follow the morphophonological rules of the rest of the language. Affixes may be irregular in this regard.
       </li>
       <li>
        Clitics do not exhibit semantic idiosyncrasies. That is, the meaning of the phrase-plus-clitic is predictable from the meanings of the phrase and the clitic. Affixes may have irregular meanings.
       </li>
       <li>
        Clitics can attach to material already containing clitics (and affixes). Affixes can attach to other affixes, but not to material containing clitics.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       An example of differing analyses by different linguists is the discussion of the possessive ('s) in English, some linguists treating it as an affix, while others treat it as a special clitic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_words">
        Comparison with words
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Comparison with words">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Similar to the discussion above, clitics must be able to be distinguished from words. There have been a number of linguistic tests proposed to differentiate between the two categories. Some tests, specifically, are based upon the understanding that when comparing the two, clitics resemble affixes, while words resemble syntactic phrases. Clitics and words resemble different categories in the sense that they share certain properties with them. Six such tests are described below. These, of course, are not the only ways to differentiate between words and clitics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zwicky_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-zwicky-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        If a morpheme is bound to a word and can never occur in complete isolation, then it is likely a clitic. In contrast, a word is not bound and can appear on its own.
       </li>
       <li>
        If the addition of a morpheme to a word prevents further affixation, then it is likely a clitic.
       </li>
       <li>
        If a morpheme combines with single words to convey a further degree of meaning, then it is likely a clitic. A word will combine with a group of words or phrases to denote further meaning.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Category:Articles_contradicting_other_articles" title="Category:Articles contradicting other articles">
           <span title="Further above it is said that clitics work on the phrase level. The English possessive, for example, attaches to a complete phrase. (October 2014)">
            contradictory
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        If a morpheme is required to be in a certain order with respect to other morphemes within the construction, then it is likely a clitic. Independent words enjoy free ordering with respect to other words, within the confines of the word order of the language.
       </li>
       <li>
        If a morpheme’s allowable behavior is determined by one principle, it is likely a clitic. For example, "a" proceeds indefinite nouns in English. Words can rarely be described with one such description.
       </li>
       <li>
        In general, words are more morphologically complex than clitics. Clitics are rarely composed of more than one morpheme.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-zwicky_14-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-zwicky-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Word_order">
        Word order
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Word order">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Clitics do not always appear next to the word or phrase that they are associated with grammatically. They may be subject to global word order constraints that act on the entire sentence. Many
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , for example, obey
       <b>
        Wackernagel's law
       </b>
       (named after
       <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Wackernagel" title="Jacob Wackernagel">
        Jacob Wackernagel
       </a>
       ), which requires clitics to appear in "second position", after the first syntactic phrase or the first stressed word in a clause:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klavans_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-klavans-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
         Latin
        </a>
        had three enclitics that appeared in second or third position of a clause:
        <i>
         enim
        </i>
        'indeed, for',
        <i>
         autem
        </i>
        'but, moreover',
        <i>
         vero
        </i>
        'however'. For example,
        <i>
         quis enim potest negare?
        </i>
        (from
        <a href="/wiki/Martial" title="Martial">
         Martial
        </a>
        's epigram LXIV, literally "who indeed can deny [her riches]?"). Spevak (2010) reports that in her corpus of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caesar" title="Caesar">
         Caesar
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
         Cicero
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Sallust" title="Sallust">
         Sallust
        </a>
        , these three words appear in such position in 100% of the cases.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       enclitics include the contracted versions of auxiliary verbs, as in
       <i>
        I'm
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        we've
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some also regard the
       <a href="/wiki/English_possessive" title="English possessive">
        possessive marker
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        The Queen of England's crown
       </i>
       as an enclitic, rather than a (phrasal) genitival inflection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/English_articles" title="English articles">
        English articles
       </a>
       <i>
        a, an, the
       </i>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Uses_of_English_verb_forms#To-infinitive" title="Uses of English verb forms">
        infinitive marker
       </a>
       <i>
        to
       </i>
       are considered by some to be proclitics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The negative marker
       <i>
        n’t
       </i>
       as in
       <i>
        couldn’t
       </i>
       etc. is often thought to be a clitic developed from the lexical item
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       ; linguists
       <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Zwicky" title="Arnold Zwicky">
        Arnold Zwicky
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Pullum" title="Geoffrey Pullum">
        Geoffrey Pullum
       </a>
       argue, however, that the form has the properties of an affix rather than a syntactically independent clitic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Clitic&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronoun
       </a>
       forms are sometimes claimed to be clitics; others claim that they are actually affixes, as they only attach to the verb they are the object of.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Spencer-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is no general agreement on the issue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_object_pronouns" title="Spanish object pronouns">
        Spanish object pronouns
       </a>
       , for example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         atamos
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         aˈtamos]
        </span>
        ("
        <b>
         it
        </b>
        tied-
        <small>
         1PL
        </small>
        " = "we tied it"; can only occur with the verb it is the object of)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         dá
         <b>
          melo
         </b>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈda
         <b>
          melo
         </b>
         ]
        </span>
        ("give
        <b>
         me it
        </b>
        ")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
       allows object suffixes before the conditional and future suffixes of the verbs:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ela levá-
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         -ia
        </i>
        ("
        <i>
         She take-
         <b>
          it
         </b>
         -would
        </i>
        " – "She would take it").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Eles dar-
         <b>
          no
         </b>
         -
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         -ão
        </i>
        ("
        <i>
         They give-
         <b>
          us
         </b>
         -
         <b>
          it
         </b>
         -will
        </i>
        " – "They will give it to us").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       of Brazil and Portugal and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       of the former
       <a href="/wiki/Gran_Colombia" title="Gran Colombia">
        Gran Colombia
       </a>
       allow ser to be conjugated as a verbal clitic adverbial adjunct to emphasize the importance of the phrase compared to its context or with the meaning of "really" or "in truth":
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ele estava
         <b>
          era
         </b>
         gordo
        </i>
        ("
        <i>
         He was
         <b>
          was
         </b>
         fat
        </i>
        " – "He was very fat").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ele ligou
         <b>
          é
         </b>
         para Paula
        </i>
        ("
        <i>
         He phoned
         <b>
          is
         </b>
         Paula
        </i>
        " – "He phoned Paula (
        <i>
         with emphasis
        </i>
        )").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that this clitic form is only for the verb
       <b>
        ser
       </b>
       and is restricted to only third-person singular conjugations. It is not used as a verb in the grammar of the sentence but introduces prepositional phrases and adds emphasis. It does not need to concord with the tense of the main verb, as in the second example, and can be usually removed from the sentence without affecting the simple meaning.
      </p>

        ;
        <i>
         -wo
        </i>
        'in' (also attached to a verb)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Somali_language" title="Somali language">
         Somali
        </a>
        : pronominal clitics, either subject or object clitics, are required in Somali. These exist as simple clitics postponed to the noun they apply to. Lexical arguments can be omitted from sentences, but pronominal clitics cannot be.

         <a href="#cite_note-25">
