      <p>
       <b>
        Picard
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_dialect" title="French dialect">
        French dialect
       </a>
       , spoken in the far north of France, and as such is one of the larger group of
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       . It is spoken in the French
       <a href="/wiki/Hauts-de-France" title="Hauts-de-France">
        Hauts-de-France
       </a>
       administrative region and in parts of the
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgian
       </a>
       region of
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       along the French border - in the districts of
       <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">
        Tournai
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">
        Mons
       </a>
       , commonly referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wallonie_Picarde" title="Wallonie Picarde">
        Walloon Picardy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Picard language group is known by several different names. Residents of
       <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">
        Picardy
       </a>
       simply call it
       <i>
        <b>
         picard
        </b>
       </i>
       , but it is more commonly known as
       <i>
        <b>
         chti
        </b>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <b>
         chtimi
        </b>
       </i>
       in the more populated
       <a href="/wiki/Nord-Pas-de-Calais" title="Nord-Pas-de-Calais">
        Nord-Pas-de-Calais
       </a>
       (around the metropolis of
       <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">
        Lille
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Douai" title="Douai">
        Douai
       </a>
       ) and in North-East
       <a href="/wiki/Artois" title="Artois">
        Artois
       </a>
       (around
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9thune" title="Béthune">
        Béthune
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lens,_Pas-de-Calais" title="Lens, Pas-de-Calais">
        Lens
       </a>
       ). It is also named
       <i>
        <b>
         rouchi
        </b>
       </i>
       around
       <a href="/wiki/Valenciennes" title="Valenciennes">
        Valenciennes
       </a>
       ; or simply as
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">
         patois
        </a>
       </b>
       by Northerners in general.
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        Linguists
       </a>
       group all of them under the name
       <i>
        Picard
       </i>
       . In general, the variety spoken in Picardy is understood by speakers in Nord-Pas-de-Calais and vice versa.
      </p>

       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champagne,_France" title="Champagne, France">
        Champagne
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Lorraine_Franconian" title="Lorraine Franconian">
        Lorraine German
       </a>
       in its 1990 decree. The French government has not followed suit and has not recognized Picard as an official regional language (in line with its policy of linguistic unity, which allows for only one official language in France), but some reports have recognized Picard as a language distinct from French.
      </p>
      <p>
       A 1999 report by Bernard Cerquiglini, the director of the
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         Institut national de la langue française
        </span>
       </i>
       (National Institute of the French Language) stated:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The gap has continued to widen between French and the varieties of
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_languages" title="Oïl languages">
          langues d'oïl
         </a>
        </i>
        , which today we would call "French dialects";
        <a href="/wiki/Frainc-Comtou_dialect" title="Frainc-Comtou dialect">
         Franc-comtois
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
         Walloon
        </a>
        , Picard,
        <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
         Norman
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">
         Gallo
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poitevin_(language)" title="Poitevin (language)">
         Poitevin
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saintongeais" title="Saintongeais">
         Saintongeais
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_language_(O%C3%AFl)" title="Burgundian language (Oïl)">
         Bourguignon-morvandiau
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
         Lorrain
        </a>
        must be accepted among the regional
        <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
         languages of France
        </a>
        ; by placing them on the list [of French regional languages], they will be known from then on as
        <i>
         langues d'oïl
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Even if it has no official status as a language in France, Picard, along with all the other languages spoken in France, benefits from actions led by the Culture Minister's General Commission on the French Language and the Languages of France (
       <i>
        la Délégation générale à la langue française et aux langues de France
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_dialectic_variations">
        Origins and dialectic variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;">

         spread of
         <b>
          Picard
         </b>
         (Picard, Chti, Rouchi, etc.)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Picard, like French, is one of the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_languages" title="Oïl languages">
         langues d'oïl
        </a>
       </i>
       and belongs to the
       <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">
        Gallo
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Roman
       </a>
       family of languages. It consists of all the varieties used for writing (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         scriptae
        </span>
       </i>
       ) in the north of France from before 1000 (in the south of France at that time the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan language
       </a>
       was used). Often, the
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       are referred to simply as
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Picard is phonetically quite different from the North-central
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       , which evolved into modern French. Among the most notable traits, the evolution in Picard towards
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       is less marked than in the central
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       in which it is particularly striking;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , tonic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       , as well as in front of tonic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɔ/
       </span>
       (the open
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       of the French
       <i>
        p
        <b>
         o
        </b>
        rte
       </i>
       ) in central Old French but not in Picard:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Picard
        <i>
         keval
        </i>
        ~ Old French
        <i>
         cheval
        </i>
        (horse; pronounced

         [
         <b>
          tʃ
         </b>
         eval]
        </span>
        rather than the modern
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <b>
          ʃ
         </b>
         əval]
        </span>
        ), from
        <i>

        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
         vulgar Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         caballus
        </i>
        ): retaining the original
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        in Picard before tonic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɔ/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Picard
        <i>
         gambe
        </i>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <i>

        <b>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>
        : Picard
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <i>

       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        + tonic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɔ/
        </span>
        : Picard
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        ~ Old French
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dʒ/
        </span>

       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There are striking differences, such as Picard
       <i>
        cachier
       </i>
       ('to hunt') ~ Old French
       <i>
        chacier
       </i>
       , which later took the modern French form of
       <i>
        chasser
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of the proximity of
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       to the northernmost regions of France, French (that is, the languages that were spoken in and around Paris) greatly influenced Picard and vice versa. The closeness between Picard and French made the former not always be recognised as a language in its own right, but rather a "distortion of French" as it is often viewed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Picard includes a variety of very closely related dialects. It is difficult to list them all accurately in the absence of specific studies on the dialectical variations, but these varieties can probably
       <i>
        provisionally
       </i>
       be distinguished
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       : Amiénois, Vimeu-Ponthieu, Vermandois, Thiérache, Beauvaisis, "chtimi" (Bassin Minier,
       <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">
        Lille
       </a>
       ), dialects in other regions near Lille (Roubaix, Tourcoing, Mouscron, Comines), "rouchi" (
       <a href="/wiki/Valenciennes" title="Valenciennes">
        Valenciennois
       </a>
       ) and Tournaisis, Borain, Artésien rural, Boulonnais.
      </p>
      <p>
       The varieties are defined by specific phonetic, morphological and lexical traits and sometimes by a distinctive literary tradition.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Some_phrases">
        Some phrases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Picard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Some phrases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many words are very similar to French, but a large number are totally specific to Picard - principally terms relating to
       <a href="/wiki/Mining" title="Mining">
        mining
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Agriculture" title="Agriculture">
        farming
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Here are several typical phrases in Picard, accompanied by French and English translations:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <b>
         J'ai prins min louchet por mi aler fouir min gardin.
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           J'ai pris ma bêche pour aller bêcher mon jardin.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "I took my spade to go dig my garden."
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Mi, à quate heures, j'archine eune bonne tartine.
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Moi, à quatre heures, je mange une bonne tartine.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "At four o'clock, I eat a good snack."
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Quind un Ch'ti mi i'est à l'agonie, savez vous bin che qui li rind la vie ? I bot un d'mi.
        </b>
        (
        <i>
         Les Capenoules
        </i>
        (a music group))
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Quand un gars du Nord est à l'agonie, savez-vous bien ce qui lui rend la vie ? Il boit un demi.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "When a northerner is dying, do you know what revives him? He drinks a pint."
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Pindant l'briquet un galibot composot, assis sur un bos,
        </b>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         L'air d'eune musique qu'i sifflotot
        </b>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Ch'étot tellemint bin fabriqué, qu'les mineurs lâchant leurs briquets
        </b>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Comminssotent à's'mette à'l'danser (Edmond Tanière -
         <i>
          La polka du mineur
         </i>
         )
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Pendant le casse-croûte un jeune mineur composa, assis sur un bout de bois
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           L'air d'une musique qu'il sifflota
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           C'était tellement bien fait que les mineurs, lâchant leurs casse-croûte
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Commencèrent à danser.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "During lunch a young miner composed, seated on a piece of wood
           </dd>
           <dd>
            "The melody of a tune that he whistled
           </dd>
           <dd>
            "It was so well done that the miners, leaving their sandwiches,
           </dd>
           <dd>
            "Started to dance to it" (Edmond Tanière -
            <i>
             La polka du mineur
            </i>
            , "The Miner's Polka")
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         I n'faut pas qu'ches glaines is cantent pus fort que ch'co.
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Il ne faut pas que les poules chantent plus fort que le coq.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "Hens must not sing louder than the rooster" (n. b. this saying really refers to men and women rather than poultry)
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         J' m'in vo à chlofe, lo qu'i n'passe poin d'caroche.
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Je vais au lit, là où il ne passe pas de carrosse.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "I go to bed where no car is running."
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Moqueu d'gins
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           railleur
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           persifleur
          </i>
          (lit.
          <i>
           moqueur des gens
          </i>
          )
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "someone who mocks or jeers at people" (compare
            <i>
             gens
            </i>
            , which is French for "people")
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Ramaseu d'sous
        </b>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           personne âpre au gain
          </i>
          (lit.
          <i>
           ramasseur de sous
          </i>
          )
          <dl>
           <dd>
            "a greedy person"
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use">
        Use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Picard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG/250px-Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG/375px-Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/69/Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG/500px-Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Enseigne_caf%C3%A9_picard_Cayeux_sur_mer.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Picard language signage in
         <a href="/wiki/Cayeux-sur-Mer" title="Cayeux-sur-Mer">
          Cayeux-sur-Mer
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Picard is not taught in
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        French
       </a>
       schools (apart from a few one-off and isolated courses) and is generally only spoken among friends or family members. It has nevertheless been the object of scholarly research at universities in
       <a href="/wiki/Lille" title="Lille">
        Lille
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">
        Amiens
       </a>
       , as well as at
       <a href="/wiki/Indiana_University" title="Indiana University">
        Indiana University
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since people are now able to move around France more easily than in past centuries, the different varieties of Picard are converging and becoming more similar. In its daily use, Picard is tending to lose its distinctive features and may be confused with regional French. At the same time, even though most Northerners can understand Picard today, fewer and fewer are able to speak it, and people who speak Picard as their
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first language
       </a>
       are increasingly rare, particularly under 50.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 2008 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bienvenue_chez_les_Ch%27tis" title="Bienvenue chez les Ch'tis">
         Bienvenue chez les Ch'tis
        </a>
       </i>
       , starring comedian
       <a href="/wiki/Dany_Boon" title="Dany Boon">
        Dany Boon
       </a>
       , deals with Ch'ti language and culture and the perceptions of the region by outsiders.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Written_Picard">
        Written Picard
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Picard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Written Picard">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Today Picard is primarily a spoken language, but in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
        medieval
       </a>
       period, there is a wealth of literary texts in Picard. However, Picard was not able to compete with French and was slowly reduced to the status of a regional language.
      </p>
      <p>
       A more recent body of Picard literature, written during the last two centuries, also exists. Modern written Picard is generally a transcription of the spoken language. For that reason, words are often spelled in a variety of different ways (in the same way that English and French were before they were standardised).
      </p>
      <p>
       One system of spelling for Picard words is very similar to that of French. It is undoubtedly the easiest for French speakers to understand but can also contribute the stereotype that Picard is only a corruption of French rather than a language in its own right.
      </p>
      <p>
       Various spelling methods have been proposed since the 1960s to offset the disadvantage and to give Picard a visual identity that is distinct from French. There is now a consensus, at least between universities, in favor of the written form known as
       <b>
        Feller-Carton
       </b>
       (based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       spelling system, which was developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Feller" title="Jules Feller">
        Jules Feller
       </a>
       , and adapted for Picard by Professor
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fernand_Carton&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fernand Carton (page does not exist)">
        Fernand Carton
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Learning_Picard">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>

       <a href="/wiki/P%27tit_Quinquin_(song)" title="P'tit Quinquin (song)">

       " for example), comic books, etc.
      </p>

       A number of dictionaries and
       <i>
        patois
       </i>
       guides also exist (for French speakers):
      </p>
