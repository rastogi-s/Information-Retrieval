     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        stative verb
       </b>
       is one that describes a state of being, in contrast to a
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_verb" title="Dynamic verb">
        dynamic verb
       </a>
       , which describes an action. The difference can be categorized by saying that stative verbs are static or unchanging throughout their entire duration, whereas dynamic verbs describe a process that changes over time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Binnick_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Binnick-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many languages distinguish between these two types in terms of how they can be used grammatically.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="From_dynamic">
        From dynamic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: From dynamic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some languages use the same verbs for dynamic and stative situations, while others use different (but often related) verbs with some kind of qualifiers to distinguish between the usages. Some verbs may act as either stative or dynamic. A phrase like "he plays the piano" may be either stative or dynamic, according to context. When in a given context the verb "play" relates to a state (an interest or a profession) he could be an amateur who enjoys music or a professional pianist. The dynamic interpretation emerges from a specific context in the case "play" describes an action, "what does he do on Friday evening? He plays the piano".
      </p>
      <p>
       The distinction between stative and dynamic verbs can be correlated with:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the distinction between intransitive and transitive
       </li>
       <li>
        the grammatical case used with a prepositional phrase associated with the verb
       </li>
       <li>
        the possibility of using the progressive aspect with the verb
       </li>
       <li>
        morphological markers
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction_between_intransitive_and_transitive">
        Distinction between intransitive and transitive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_case">
        Grammatical case
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grammatical case">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some languages make distinctions between stative and dynamic verbs in sentences. In German, for instance, several prepositions (
       <i>
        Wechselpräpositionen
       </i>
       – "changing prepositions") take different
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_cases" title="Noun cases">
        noun cases
       </a>
       when they accompany stative and dynamic verbs. For stative verbs, the corresponding preposition takes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative" title="Dative">
        dative
       </a>
       case, whereas for dynamic verbs, the preposition takes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accusative" title="Accusative">
        accusative
       </a>
       case. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ich
        </i>
        lege
        <i>
         den Stift auf
         <b>
          den
         </b>
         Tisch.
        </i>
        (I lay the pen on(to) the table.) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Den
         </b>
        </i>
        here is the masculine definite article in accusative case. [
        <i>
         lege
        </i>
        – infinitive:
        <i>
         legen
        </i>
        ]
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        BUT
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Der Stift
        </i>
        liegt
        <i>
         auf
         <b>
          dem
         </b>
         Tisch.
        </i>
        (The pen lies on the table.) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Dem
         </b>
        </i>
        here is masculine definite article in dative case. [
        <i>
         liegt
        </i>
        – infinitive:
        <i>
         liegen
        </i>
        ]
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The same scheme also applies with stative and dynamic verbs in general, i.e. when the verb is stative (albeit the dynamic counterpart is non-existent), the preposition will always take dative, and vice versa.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ich
        </i>
        bin
        <i>
         in
         <b>
          der
         </b>
         Schule.
        </i>
        (I am at school. – literally
        <i>
         I am in the school.
        </i>
        ) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Der
         </b>
        </i>
        here is the feminine definite article in dative case, since the verb
        <i>
         bin
        </i>
        (infinitive:
        <i>
         sein
        </i>
        – to be) is a stative verb.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ich
        </i>
        gehe
        <i>
         in
         <b>
          die
         </b>
         Schule.
        </i>
        (I go to school. – literally
        <i>
         I go in the school.
        </i>
        ) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Die
         </b>
        </i>
        here is the feminine definite article in accusative case, since the verb
        <i>
         gehe
        </i>
        (infinitive:
        <i>
         gehen
        </i>
        – to go) is a dynamic verb.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <b>
         BUT
        </b>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Ich
        </i>
        gehe
        <i>
         zu
         <b>
          r
         </b>

        </i>

        <i>
         zur
        </i>

        <i>
         zu
        </i>
        +
        <b>
         der
        </b>
        ] (I go to school. – literally
        <i>
         I go to the school.
        </i>
        ) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Der
         </b>
        </i>
        here is the feminine definite article in dative case. Note that
        <i>
         <b>
          zu
         </b>
        </i>
        is not a
        <i>
         Wechselpräposition
        </i>
        and always takes dative case.
       </dd>
      </dl>

       <li>
        <i>
         <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
          Wo
         </span>
        </i>
        bist
        <i>
         du?
        </i>
        –
        <i>
         I
         <b>

         </b>

        </i>

        <i>
         im
        </i>
        =
        <i>
         in
        </i>

        <b>

        </b>
        ] (Where are you? – In the cinema.) –
        <i>
         <b>
          Dem
         </b>
        </i>
        here is the neuter definite article in dative case. [
        <i>
         bist
        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Progressive_aspect">
        Progressive aspect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Progressive aspect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In English and many other languages, stative and dynamic verbs differ in whether or not they can use the
       <a href="/wiki/Continuous_and_progressive_aspects" title="Continuous and progressive aspects">
        progressive aspect
       </a>
       . Dynamic verbs such as "go" can be used in the progressive (
       <i>
        I am going to school
       </i>
       ) whereas stative verbs such as "know" cannot (*
       <i>
        I am knowing the answer
       </i>
       ). A verb that has both dynamic and stative uses cannot normally be used in the progressive when a stative meaning is intended: e.g. one cannot normally say, idiomatically, "Every morning, I am going to school". In other languages, statives can be used in the progressive as well: in Korean, for example, the sentence 미나가 인호를 사랑하고있다 (
       <i>
        Mina is loving Inho
       </i>
       ) is perfectly valid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphological_markers">
        Morphological markers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Morphological markers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some languages, stative and dynamic verbs will use entirely different morphological markers on the verbs themselves. For example, in the Mantauran dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Rukai_language" title="Rukai language">
        Rukai
       </a>
       , an indigenous language of Taiwan, the two types of verbs take different prefixes in their finite forms, with dynamic verbs taking
       <i>
        o-
       </i>
       and stative verbs taking
       <i>
        ma-
       </i>
       . Thus, the dynamic verb "jump" is
       <i>
        o-coroko
       </i>
       in the active voice, while the stative verb "love" is
       <i>
        ma-ðalamə
       </i>
       . This sort of marking is characteristic of other
       <a href="/wiki/Formosan_languages" title="Formosan languages">
        Formosan languages
       </a>
       as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_from_inchoative">
        Difference from inchoative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Difference from inchoative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In English, a verb that expresses a state can also express the entrance into a state. This is called
       <a href="/wiki/Inchoative_aspect" title="Inchoative aspect">
        inchoative aspect
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_past_(English)" title="Simple past (English)">
        simple past
       </a>
       is sometimes inchoative. For example, the present-tense verb in the sentence "He understands his friend" is stative, while the past-tense verb in the sentence "Suddenly he understood what she said" is inchoative, because it means "He understood henceforth". On the other hand, the past-tense verb in "At one time, he understood her" is stative.
      </p>
      <p>
       The only way the difference between stative and inchoative can be expressed in English is through the use of modifiers, as in the above examples ("suddenly" and "at one time").
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise, in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        ancient Greek
       </a>
       , a verb that expresses a state (e.g.,
       <i>
        ebasíleuon
       </i>
       "I was king") may use the
       <a href="/wiki/Aorist_(Ancient_Greek)" title="Aorist (Ancient Greek)">
        aorist
       </a>
       to express entrance into the state (e.g.,
       <i>
        ebasíleusa
       </i>
       "I became king"). But the aorist can also simply express the state as a whole, with no focus on the beginning of the state (
       <i>
        eíkosi étē ebasíleusa
       </i>
       "I ruled for twenty years").
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Dowty.27s_analysis">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dowty's_analysis">
        Dowty's analysis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Dowty's analysis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Dowty gives several tests to decide whether an English verb is stative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are as follows:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Statives do not occur in the progressive:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           John is running.
          </i>
          (non-stative)
         </li>
         <li>
          *
          <i>
           John is knowing the answer.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        They cannot be complements of "force":
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           I forced John to run.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          *
          <i>
           I forced John to know the answer.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        They do not occur as imperatives, except when used in an inchoative manner.
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Run!
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          *
          <i>
           Know the answer!
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           Know thyself!
          </i>
          (inchoative, not stative; archaic)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        They cannot appear in the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Cleft_sentence" title="Cleft sentence">
          pseudo-cleft construction
         </a>
        </i>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           What John did was run.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          *
          <i>
           What John did was know the answer.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Categories">
        Categories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stative_verb&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Categories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Stative verbs are often divided into sub-categories, based on their semantics or syntax.
      </p>
      <p>
       Semantic divisions mainly involve verbs that express someone's state of mind, or something's properties (of course, things can also be expressed via other language mechanisms as well, particularly adjectives). The precise categories vary by linguist. Huddleston and Pullum, for example, divide stative verbs into the following semantic categories: verbs of perception and sensation (
       <i>
        see, hear
       </i>
       ), verbs of hurting (
       <i>
        ache, itch
       </i>
       ), stance verbs (
       <i>
        stand, sit
       </i>
       ), and verbs of cognition, emotion, and sensation (
       <i>
        believe, regret
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Novakov, meanwhile, uses the slightly different categories: verbs denoting sensations (
       <i>
        feel, hear
       </i>
       ), verbs denoting reasoning and mental attitude (
       <i>
        believe, understand
       </i>
       ), verbs denoting positions/stance (
       <i>
        lie, surround
       </i>
       ), and verbs denoting relations (
       <i>
        resemble, contain
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Syntactic divisions involve the types of
       <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">
        clause
       </a>
       structures in which a verb may be used. In the following examples, an asterisk (*) indicates that the sentence is ungrammatical:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        John believes that Fido is a dog.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        *John believes on Fido barking.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        John believes Fido to bark.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        *Joan depends that Fido is a dog.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Joan depends on Fido barking.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        *Joan depends Fido to bark.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Jim loathes that Fido is a dog.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        *Jim loathes on Fido barking.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        *Jim loathes Fido to bark.
       </dd>
      </dl>
