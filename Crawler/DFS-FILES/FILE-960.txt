      <p>
       <b>
        Ligurian
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        ligure
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lengua ligure
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       language spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">
        Liguria
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       , parts of the Mediterranean coastal zone of France,
       <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">
        Monaco
       </a>
       and in the villages of
       <a href="/wiki/Carloforte" title="Carloforte">
        Carloforte
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Calasetta" title="Calasetta">
        Calasetta
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       . It is part of the Gallo-Italic and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Romance" title="Western Romance">
        Western Romance
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Genoese_dialect" title="Genoese dialect">
        Genoese
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Zeneize
       </i>
       ), spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       , the capital of Liguria, is the language's prestige dialect on which the standard is based.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a long literary tradition of Ligurian poets and writers that goes from the 13th century to the present, such as Luchetto (the Genoese Anonym), Martin Piaggio and Gian Giacomo Cavalli.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Ligurian has almost 500,000 native speakers, and is still widely spoken throughout Liguria, especially in the rural regions and smaller towns. Unlike other regional languages spoken throughout Italy, Ligurian is not experiencing a decline in a number of native speakers due to the influence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       , nor is it perceived as a regional
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">
         patois
        </a>
       </i>
       spoken by the less-educated by its speakers. As such, the language itself is actively preserved by various groups, most notable of which is the
       <i>
        Associazione Culturale O Castello
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Chiavari" title="Chiavari">
        Chiavari
       </a>
       , which offers free Ligurian (Genovese dialect) language courses. Notable native speakers of Ligurian include
       <a href="/wiki/Niccol%C3%B2_Paganini" title="Niccolò Paganini">
        Niccolò Paganini
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Garibaldi" title="Giuseppe Garibaldi">
        Giuseppe Garibaldi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eugenio_Montale" title="Eugenio Montale">
        Eugenio Montale
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Giulio_Natta" title="Giulio Natta">
        Giulio Natta
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italo_Calvino" title="Italo Calvino">
        Italo Calvino
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Fabrizio_De_Andr%C3%A9" title="Fabrizio De André">
        Fabrizio De André
       </a>
       . There is also a popular musical group,
       <a href="/wiki/Buio_Pesto" title="Buio Pesto">
        Buio Pesto
       </a>
       , who compose songs entirely in the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Besides
       <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">
        Liguria
       </a>
       (Ligurian
       <i>
        Liguria
       </i>
       ), the language is traditionally spoken in coastal, northern
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       (part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Alessandria" title="Province of Alessandria">
        province of Alessandria
       </a>
       ), western extremes of
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       (some areas in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Piacenza" title="Province of Piacenza">
        province of Piacenza
       </a>
       ), in a small area of southern
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       (Italy), the
       <a href="/wiki/Alpes-Maritimes" title="Alpes-Maritimes">
        Alpes-Maritimes
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       (mostly the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur" title="Côte d'Azur">
        Côte d'Azur
       </a>
       from the Italian border to and including
       <a href="/wiki/Monaco" title="Monaco">
        Monaco
       </a>
       ), and in a township at the south of
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       (France). It has been adopted formally in Monaco as the
       <a href="/wiki/Mon%C3%A9gasque_dialect" title="Monégasque dialect">
        Monégasque dialect
       </a>
       ; or locally,
       <i>
        Munegascu
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Mentonasc dialect, spoken in the East of the
       <a href="/wiki/County_of_Nice" title="County of Nice">
        County of Nice
       </a>
       , is considered to be a transitional
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       dialect to Ligurian; conversely, the Roiasc and Pignasc spoken further North in the Eastern margin of the County are Ligurian dialects with Occitan influences.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ligurian was also once spoken outside the borders of the modern province, but has since given way to
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       in Northwestern Italy and to
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       in Southern France.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ligurian_(Romance_language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As a Gallo-Italic language, Ligurian is most closely related to
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol language
       </a>
       , all of which are spoken in neighboring provinces. Unlike the aforementioned languages, however, it exhibits distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       features. No link between Romance Ligurian and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(ancient)" title="Ligurian language (ancient)">
        Ligurian language of the ancient Ligurian populations
       </a>
       , in the form of a substrate or otherwise, can be demonstrated by linguistic evidence. Only the toponyms are known to have survived from ancient Ligurian, the name
       <i>
        Liguria
       </i>
       being the most obvious example.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet">
        Alphabet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ligurian_(Romance_language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alphabet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Ligurian alphabet has:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        7 vowels:
        <b>
         a
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         ò
        </b>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
         IPA
        </a>
        :
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɔ]
        </span>
        ),
        <b>
         o
        </b>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u]
        </span>
        ,
        <b>
         u
        </b>
        [
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         y
        </span>
        ],
        <b>
         æ
        </b>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɛ]
        </span>
        , plus the group
        <b>
         eu
        </b>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ø]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        19 consonants:
        <b>
         b
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         c
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         ç
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         d
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         f
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         g
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         h
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         l
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         m
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         n
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         ñ (or nn- like in
         <i>
          singing
         </i>
         ),
        </b>
        <b>
         p
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         q
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         s
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         t
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         v
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         x
        </b>
        ,
        <b>
         z
        </b>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        It uses the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">
         umlaut
        </a>
        (¨),
        <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">
         circumflex
        </a>
        (ˆ),
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acute_(diacritic)" title="Acute (diacritic)">
         acute
        </a>
        (´), and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grave_(accent)" title="Grave (accent)">
         grave
        </a>
        (`) accents on most vowels when the full pronunciation key is given in the official spelling. It also uses the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C-cedilla" title="C-cedilla">
         c-cedilla
        </a>
        (ç).
       </li>
      </ul>
