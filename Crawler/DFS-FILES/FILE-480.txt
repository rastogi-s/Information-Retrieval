      <p>
       <b>
        Morphophonology
       </b>
       (also
       <b>
        morphophonemics
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        morphonology
       </b>
       ) is the branch of
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       that studies the interaction between
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
        phonetic
       </a>
       processes. Its chief focus is the sound changes that take place in
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morphemes
       </a>
       (minimal meaningful units) when they combine to form words.
      </p>
      <p>
       Morphophonological analysis often involves an attempt to give a series of formal rules that successfully predict the regular sound changes occurring in the morphemes of a given language. Such a series of rules converts a theoretical
       <a href="/wiki/Underlying_representation" title="Underlying representation">
        underlying representation
       </a>
       into a surface form that is actually heard. The units of which the underlying representations of morphemes are composed are sometimes called
       <b>
        morphophonemes
       </b>
       . The surface form produced by the morphophonological rules may consist of
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       (which are then subject to ordinary phonological rules to produce speech sounds or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
         phones
        </a>
       </i>
       ), or else the morphophonological analysis may bypass the phoneme stage and produce the phones itself.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphophonemes_and_morphophonological_rules">
        Morphophonemes and morphophonological rules
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morphophonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Morphophonemes and morphophonological rules">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morphemes
       </a>
       combine, they influence each other's sound structure (whether analyzed at a phonetic or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic" title="Phonemic">
        phonemic
       </a>
       level), resulting in different variant pronunciations for the same morpheme. Morphophonology attempts to analyze these processes. A language's morphophonological structure is generally described with a series of rules which, ideally, can predict every morphophonological
       <a href="/wiki/Alternation_(linguistics)" title="Alternation (linguistics)">
        alternation
       </a>
       that takes place in the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of a morphophonological alternation in English is provided by the
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        plural
       </a>
       morpheme, written as "-s" or "-es". Its pronunciation alternates between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [z]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɪz]
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        cats
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dogs
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        horses
       </i>
       respectively. A purely phonological analysis would most likely assign to these three endings the phonemic representations
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɪz/
       </span>
       . On a morphophonological level, however, they may all be considered to be forms of the underlying object
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //z//
       </span>
       , which is a
       <b>
        morphophoneme
       </b>
       . The different forms it takes are dependent on the segment at the end of the morpheme to which it attaches: the dependencies are described by morphophonological rules. (The behaviour of the English past tense ending "-ed" is similar: it can be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɪd/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        hoped
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        bobbed
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        added
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       The plural suffix "-s" can also influence the form taken by the preceding morpheme, as in the case of the words
       <i>
        leaf
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        knife
       </i>
       , which end with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       in the singular/but have
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       in the plural (
       <i>
        leaves
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        knives
       </i>
       ). On a morphophonological level, the morphemes may be analyzed as ending in a morphophoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //F//
       </span>
       , which becomes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" title="Voiced consonant">
        voiced
       </a>
       when a voiced consonant (in this case the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //z//
       </span>
       of the plural ending) is attached to it. The rule may be written symbolically as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /F/
       </span>
       -&gt; [αvoice] /
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        __
       </span>
       [αvoice]. This expression is called Alpha Notation in which α can be +(positive value) or -(negative value).
      </p>
      <p>
       Common conventions to indicate a morphophonemic rather than phonemic representation are double slashes (//  //) (as above, implying that the transcription is 'more phonemic than simply phonemic'), pipes (|  |), double pipes (‖  ‖)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and curly brackets ({  }).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For instance, the English word
       <i>
        cats
       </i>
       may be transcribed phonetically as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkʰæts]
       </span>
       , phonemically as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkæts/
       </span>
       and morphophonemically as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //ˈkætz//
       </span>
       , if the plural is argued to be underlyingly
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //z//
       </span>
       , assimilating to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       after a voiceless nonsibilant. The tilde ~ may indicate morphological alternation, as in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //ˈniː~ɛl+t//
       </span>
       for
       <i>
        kneel~knelt
       </i>
       (the plus sign '+' indicates a morpheme boundary).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_changes">
        Types of changes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morphophonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of changes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Inflected and agglutinating languages may have extremely complicated systems of morphophonemics. Examples of complex morphophonological systems include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sandhi" title="Sandhi">
         Sandhi
        </a>
        , the phenomenon behind the English examples of plural and past tense above, is found in virtually all languages to some degree. Even
        <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
         Mandarin
        </a>
        , which is sometimes said to display no
        <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
         morphology
        </a>
        , nonetheless displays
        <a href="/wiki/Tone_sandhi" title="Tone sandhi">
         tone sandhi
        </a>
        , a morphophonemic alternation.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Consonant_gradation" title="Consonant gradation">
         Consonant gradation
        </a>
        , found in some
        <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
         Uralic languages
        </a>
        such as
        <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
         Finnish
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
         Estonian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Northern_Sami" title="Northern Sami">
         Northern Sámi
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Nganasan_language" title="Nganasan language">
         Nganasan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
         Vowel harmony
        </a>
        , which occurs in varying degrees in languages all around the world, notably
        <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
         Turkic languages
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablaut" title="Ablaut">
         Ablaut
        </a>
        , found in English and other
        <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
         Germanic languages
        </a>
        . Ablaut is the phenomenon wherein stem vowels change form depending on context, as in English
        <i>
         sing
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         sang
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         sung
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relation_with_phonology">
        Relation with phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morphophonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relation with phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Until the 1950s, many phonologists assumed that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutralization_(linguistics)" title="Neutralization (linguistics)">
        neutralizing
       </a>
       rules generally applied before
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophonic
       </a>
       rules. Thus phonological analysis was split into two parts: a morphophonological part, where neutralizing rules were developed to derive phonemes from morphophonemes; and a purely phonological part, where phones were derived from the phonemes. Since the 1960s (in particular with the work of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" title="Generative linguistics">
        generative
       </a>
       school, such as Chomsky and Halle's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sound_Pattern_of_English" title="The Sound Pattern of English">
         The Sound Pattern of English
        </a>
       </i>
       ) many linguists have moved away from making such a split, instead regarding the surface phones as being derived from the underlying morphophonemes (which may be referred to using various terminology) through a single system of
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_rule" title="Phonological rule">
        (morpho)phonological rules
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The purpose of both phonemic and morphophonemic analysis is to produce simpler underlying descriptions for what appear on the surface to be complicated patterns. In purely phonemic analysis the data is just a set of words in a language, while for the purposes of morphophonemic analysis the words must be considered in grammatical
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        paradigms
       </a>
       to take account of the underlying
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphemes" title="Morphemes">
        morphemes
       </a>
       . It is postulated that morphemes are recorded in the speaker's "
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       " in an invariant (morphophonemic) form, which, in a given environment, is converted by rules into a surface form. The analyst attempts to present as completely as possible a system of underlying units (morphophonemes) and a series of rules that act on them, so as to produce surface forms consistent with the linguistic data.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Isolation_forms">
        Isolation forms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morphophonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Isolation forms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        isolation form
       </b>
       of a morpheme is the form in which that morpheme appears in isolation (when not subject to the effects of any other morpheme). In the case of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bound_morpheme" title="Bound morpheme">
        bound morpheme
       </a>
       , such as the English past tense ending "-ed", it will generally not be possible to identify an isolation form, since such a morpheme does not occur in isolation.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is often reasonable to assume that the isolation form of a morpheme provides its underlying representation. For example, in some varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        plant
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [plænt]
       </span>
       , while
       <i>
        planting
       </i>
       is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈplænɪŋ]
       </span>
       , where the morpheme "plant-" appears in the form
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [plæn]
       </span>
       . Here the underlying form can be assumed to be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //plænt//
       </span>
       , corresponding to the isolation form, since rules can be set up to derive the reduced form
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [plæn]
       </span>
       from this (while it would be difficult or impossible to set up rules that would derive the isolation form
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [plænt]
       </span>
       from an underlying
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        //plæn//
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       That is not always the case, however; sometimes the isolation form itself is subject to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neutralization_(linguistics)" title="Neutralization (linguistics)">
        neutralization
       </a>
       that does not apply to some other instances of the morpheme. For example, the French word
       <i>
        petit
       </i>
       ("small") is pronounced in isolation without the final [t] sound, but in certain derived forms (such as the feminine
       <i>
        petite
       </i>
       ), the [t] is heard. If the isolation form were adopted as the underlying form, the information that there is a final "t" would be lost, and it would be hard then to explain the appearance of the "t" in the inflected forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assume that the grammar of a language has two rules,rule A and rule B, with A ordered before B. If, in a given derivation, the application of rule A creates the environment for rule B to apply, which was not present before the application of rule A, rule A and B are in a
       <i>
        feeding relationship
       </i>
       so rule A feeds rule B.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assuming again rule A and B, with A ordered before B in the derivation in which rule A destroys the environment to which rule B shall apply, one says A and B are in a
       <i>
        bleeding order
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       If it is assumed again that a pair of rules A and B, with A ordered before B and B creating an environment to which A could have applied, B is said to counterfeed A, and the relationship is
       <i>
        counterfeeding
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       If one assumes a pair of rules A and B and A ordered before B is in a
       <i>
        counterbleeding
       </i>
       relationship if B destroys the environment that A applies to and has already applied, therefore, B ordered after A has missed its chance to bleed A.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Conjunctive ordering
       </i>
       is the ordering that ensures that all rules are applied in a derivation before the surface representation is arrived at. One says that rules applied in a feeding relationship are
       <i>
        conjunctively ordered
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Disjunctive ordering
       </i>
       is a rule that applies and prevents the other rule to apply up to the surface representation. Such rules have a bleeding relationship and are
       <i>
        disjunctively ordered
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
        Orthography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Morphophonology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orthography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The principle behind
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabetic
       </a>
       writing systems is that the letters (
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        graphemes
       </a>
       ) represent
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       . However, in many
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthographies
       </a>
       based on such systems the correspondences between graphemes and phonemes are not exact, and it is sometimes the case that certain spellings better represent a word's morphophonological structure rather than the purely phonological. An example of this is that the English plural morpheme is written
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       regardless of whether it is pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       ; we write
       <i>
        cat
        <b>
         s
        </b>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        dog
        <b>
         s
        </b>
       </i>
       , not
       <i>
        dog
        <b>
         z
        </b>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The above example involves active morphology (
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       ), and morphophonemic spellings are common in this context in many languages. Another type of spelling that can be described as morphophonemic is the kind that reflects the
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       of words. Such spellings are particularly common in English; examples include
       <i>
        <b>
         sci
        </b>
        ence
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /saɪ/
       </span>
       vs.
       <i>
        uncon
        <b>
         sci
        </b>
        ous
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         pre
        </b>
        judice
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /prɛ/
       </span>
       vs.
       <i>
        <b>
         pre
        </b>
        quel
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /priː/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         sign
        </b>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /saɪn/
       </span>
       <i>
        <b>
         sign
        </b>
        ature
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sɪɡn/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         na
        </b>
        tion
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /neɪ/
       </span>
       vs.
       <i>
        <b>
         na
        </b>
        tionalism
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /næ/
       </span>
       , and
       <i>
        <b>
         spec
        </b>
        ial
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /spɛ/
       </span>
       vs.
       <i>
        <b>
         spec
        </b>
        ies
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /spiː/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For more detail on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography" title="Phonemic orthography">
        Phonemic orthography
       </a>
       , in particular the section on
