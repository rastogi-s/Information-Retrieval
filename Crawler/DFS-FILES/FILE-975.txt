      <p>
       <b>
        Gallo-Italic of Sicily
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Gallo-italico di Sicilia
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a group of
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       found in about 15 isolated communities of central eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       . Forming a
       <a href="/wiki/Language_island" title="Language island">
        language island
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian language
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TosoTreccani_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TosoTreccani-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it dates back to migrations from
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        northern Italy
       </a>
       during the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">
        Norman
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_I_of_Sicily" title="Roger I of Sicily">
        Roger I of Sicily
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and his successors.
      </p>
      <p>
       Towns inhabited by the new immigrants became known as the "Lombard communities" (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         oppida Lombardorum
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="scn" xml:lang="scn">
         cumuna lummardi
        </span>
       </i>
       ). The settlers, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Lombards_of_Sicily" title="Lombards of Sicily">
        Lombards of Sicily
       </a>
       , actually came principally from the
       <a href="/wiki/Aleramici" title="Aleramici">
        Aleramici
       </a>
       fiefdoms of
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">
        Liguria
       </a>
       , "Lombardy" being the name for the whole northern Italy during the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       . In addition to a common place of origin, the colonizers brought their
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       . These languages added to the Gallic influence of the developing Sicilian language (influences which included
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Occitan" title="Old Occitan">
        Old Occitan
       </a>
       ) to become the Gallo-Italic of Sicily
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        language family
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo-Italic_of_Sicily&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:160px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Renouncing.png">
         <img alt="Illustration from a 14th-century French poem" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="259" data-file-width="158" height="259" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/dd/Renouncing.png" width="158"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Renouncing.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Adelaide del Vasto, third wife of Roger I of Sicily
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_southern_Italy" title="Norman conquest of southern Italy">
        Norman conquest of southern Italy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lombards_of_Sicily" title="Lombards of Sicily">
        Lombards of Sicily
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although Roger I took 30 years to take complete control of Sicily (from 1061 to 1091), by 1080 he had effective control of much of the island. During this conquest, some areas of central Sicily became depopulated as some, but far from all, of its
       <a href="/wiki/Muslim" title="Muslim">
        Muslim
       </a>
       population was expelled. Roger and his Norman successors encouraged migration to the region, especially by those closely allied with the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_Church" title="Latin Church">
        Latin Church
       </a>
       . Most of the migration was from northern Italy, particularly from
       <a href="/wiki/Adelaide_del_Vasto" title="Adelaide del Vasto">
        his wife
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Aleramici" title="Aleramici">
        family
       </a>
       holdings in
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">
        Liguria
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       In some of these towns, the northern Italian influence on the local varieties of Sicilian is marked; in others, the Lombard communities did not influence the local dialect. Similar communities have survived outside Sicily in
       <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">
        Basilicata
       </a>
       in southern Italy, which was subject to similar influences; the dialects spoken there are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_of_Basilicata" title="Gallo-Italic of Basilicata">
        Gallo-Italic of Basilicata
       </a>
       .
      </p>
