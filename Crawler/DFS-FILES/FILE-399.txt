      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Iberian language
       </b>
       was the language of an indigenous pre-
       <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
        Migration Period
       </a>
       people identified by
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greek
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       sources who lived in the eastern and southeastern regions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       . The ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Iberians" title="Iberians">
        Iberians
       </a>
       can be identified as a rather nebulous local culture between the 7th and 1st century BC. The Iberian language, like all the other
       <a href="/wiki/Paleohispanic_languages" title="Paleohispanic languages">
        Paleohispanic languages
       </a>
       except
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       , became
       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">
        extinct
       </a>
       by the 1st to 2nd centuries AD, after being gradually replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Iberian is
       <a href="/wiki/Unclassified_language" title="Unclassified language">
        unclassified
       </a>
       : while the scripts used to write it have been deciphered to various extents, the language itself remains largely unknown. Links with other languages have been suggested, especially the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque language
       </a>
       , based largely on the proximity between the two, but they have not been clearly corroborated and remain speculative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trask-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>

      </div>
      <p>
       The Iberian language was widely spoken along the Mediterranean coast of the Iberian Peninsula.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the north, the Iberian language inscriptions reached the south of France up to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H%C3%A9rault_river" title="Hérault river">
        Hérault river
       </a>
       . Important written remains have been found in
       <a href="/wiki/Oppidum_d%27Ens%C3%A9rune" title="Oppidum d'Ensérune">
        Ensérune
       </a>
       , between
       <a href="/wiki/Narbonne" title="Narbonne">
        Narbonne
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9ziers" title="Béziers">
        Béziers
       </a>
       in France, in an
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oppidum" title="Oppidum">
         oppidum
        </a>
       </i>
       with mixed Iberian and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celt" title="Celt">
        Celtic
       </a>
       elements. The southern limit would be
       <a href="/wiki/Porcuna" title="Porcuna">
        Porcuna
       </a>
       , in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ja%C3%A9n_(Spanish_province)" title="Jaén (Spanish province)">
        Jaén
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       ), where splendid sculptures of Iberian riders have been found. Towards inland the exact distribution of the Iberian language inscriptions is uncertain. It seems that the culture reached the interior through the
       <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">
        Ebro
       </a>
       river (
       <i>
        Iberus
       </i>
       in Latin) as far as
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Salduie&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Salduie (page does not exist)">
        Salduie
       </a>
       (Zaragoza) but not farther.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the pre-Roman peoples of the Iberian Peninsula the following might have spoken the Iberian language:
       <a href="/wiki/Ausetani" title="Ausetani">
        Ausetani
       </a>
       (northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Ilergetes" title="Ilergetes">
        Ilergetes
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lleida_(province)" title="Lleida (province)">
        Lleida
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huesca_(province)" title="Huesca (province)">
        Huesca
       </a>
       up to the Pyrenees),
       <a href="/wiki/Indigetes" title="Indigetes">
        Indigetes
       </a>
       (coast of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Girona_(province)" title="Girona (province)">
        Girona
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Laietani" title="Laietani">
        Laietani
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barcelona_(province)" title="Barcelona (province)">
        Barcelona
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassetani" title="Cassetani">
        Cassetani
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tarragona_(province)" title="Tarragona (province)">
        Tarragona
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Ilercavones" title="Ilercavones">
        Ilercavones
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Levante,_Spain" title="Levante, Spain">
        Levante
       </a>
       up to Tarragona),
       <a href="/wiki/Edetani" title="Edetani">
        Edetani
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia_(province)" title="Valencia (province)">
        Valencia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castell%C3%B3n_(province)" title="Castellón (province)">
        Castellón
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teruel_(province)" title="Teruel (province)">
        Teruel
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Contestani" title="Contestani">
        Contestani
       </a>
       (Valencia,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alicante_(province)" title="Alicante (province)">
        Alicante
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_(Spain)" title="Cartagena (Spain)">
        Cartagena
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albacete_(province)" title="Albacete (province)">
        Albacete
       </a>
       ),

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_(province)" title="Almería (province)">
        Almería
       </a>
       and Murcia) and
       <a href="/wiki/Oretani" title="Oretani">
        Oretani
       </a>
       (Jaén,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ciudad_Real_(province)" title="Ciudad Real (province)">

       </a>
       , Albacete and
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cuenca" title="Province of Cuenca">
        Cuenca

       <a href="/wiki/Turduli" title="Turduli">
        Turduli
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turdetani" title="Turdetani">

       <a href="/wiki/Tartessian_language" title="Tartessian language">
        Tartessian language
       </a>

      </p>
      <p>
       For some scholars, such as
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Velaza&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Velaza (page does not exist)">
        Velaza
       </a>
       (2006), Iberian could have been the language spoken by the autochthonous population of these territories, while for others, such as
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=De_Hoz&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="De Hoz (page does not exist)">
        De Hoz
       </a>
       (1993), Iberian could have been more of a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
         lingua franca
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

        </a>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mapa_escriptures_paleohisp%C3%A0niques-ang.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

      <p>
       The origin of the language is unknown. Although Iberian ceased to be written in the 1st century AD, it may have survived in some areas until the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visigoth" title="Visigoth">
        Visigothic
       </a>
       period, according to Menéndez Pidal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">
        Writing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bronce_ibero.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="615" data-file-width="1000" height="185" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bronce_ibero.jpg/300px-Bronce_ibero.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bronce_ibero.jpg/450px-Bronce_ibero.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Bronce_ibero.jpg/600px-Bronce_ibero.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bronce_ibero.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lead plaque from
         <a href="/wiki/Ullastret" title="Ullastret">
          Ullastret
         </a>
         using the dual variant of the
         <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Iberian_script" title="Northeastern Iberian script">
          northeastern Iberian script
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_scripts" title="Iberian scripts">
        Iberian scripts
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The oldest Iberian inscriptions date to the 4th century BC or maybe the 5th century BC and the latest ones date from the end of the 1st century BC or maybe the beginning of the 1st century AD. More than two thousand Iberian inscriptions are currently known. Most are short texts on
       <a href="/wiki/Ceramic" title="Ceramic">
        ceramic
       </a>
       with personal names, which are usually interpreted as ownership marks. Many
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Iberian_coinage" title="Ancient Iberian coinage">
        coins
       </a>
       minted by Iberian communities during the Roman Republic have legends in Iberian. The longest Iberian texts were made on
       <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">
        lead
       </a>
       plaques; the most extensive is from
       <a href="/wiki/Y%C3%A1tova" title="Yátova">
        Yátova
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia_(province)" title="Valencia (province)">
        Valencia
       </a>
       ) with more than six hundred signs.
      </p>
      <p>
       Three different scripts have remained for the Iberian language:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Iberian_script" title="Northeastern Iberian script">
         Northeastern Iberian script
        </a>
        <ul>
         <li>
          Dual variant (4th century BC and 3rd century BC)
         </li>
         <li>
          Non-dual variant (2nd century BC and 1st century BC)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Iberian_script" title="Southeastern Iberian script">
         Southeastern Iberian script
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Greco-Iberian_alphabet" title="Greco-Iberian alphabet">
         Greco-Iberian alphabet
        </a>
        (most of the aforementioned
        <i>
         Leads of La Serreta
        </i>
        are written in this version).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Northeastern_.28or_Levantine.29_Iberian_script">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Northeastern_(or_Levantine)_Iberian_script">
        Northeastern (or Levantine) Iberian script
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northeastern (or Levantine) Iberian script">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plom_I_de_La_Bastida_(Cara_A).jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plom_I_de_La_Bastida_(Cara_A).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lead plaque from La Bastida de les Alcuses (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mogente" title="Mogente">
          Mogente
         </a>
         ) using the
         <a href="/wiki/Southeastern_Iberian_script" title="Southeastern Iberian script">
          southeastern Iberian script
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Iberian_script" title="Northeastern Iberian script">
        northeastern Iberian script
       </a>
       is also known as the Iberian script, because it is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_script" title="Iberian script">
        Iberian script
       </a>
       most frequently used (95% of the extant texts (Untermann 1990)). The northeastern Iberian inscriptions have been found mainly in the northeastern quadrant of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       : chiefly on the coast from
       <a href="/wiki/Languedoc-Roussillon" title="Languedoc-Roussillon">
        Languedoc-Roussillon
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">
        Alicante
       </a>
       , but with a deep penetration into the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ebro_valley" title="Ebro valley">
        Ebro valley
       </a>
       . This script is almost completely deciphered.
      </p>
      <p>
       All the
       <a href="/wiki/Paleohispanic_scripts" title="Paleohispanic scripts">
        paleohispanic scripts
       </a>
       , with the exception of the
       <a href="/wiki/Greco-Iberian_alphabet" title="Greco-Iberian alphabet">
        Greco-Iberian alphabet
       </a>
       , share a common distinctive typological characteristic: they use signs with syllabic value for the
       <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">
        occlusives
       </a>
       and signs with monophonematic value for the remaining
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consonants" title="Consonants">
        consonants
       </a>
       and for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vowels" title="Vowels">
        vowels
       </a>
       . From a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Writing_systems" title="Writing systems">
        writing systems
       </a>
       point of view they are neither
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alphabets" title="Alphabets">
        alphabets
       </a>
       nor
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllabaries" title="Syllabaries">
        syllabaries
       </a>
       ; rather, they are mixed scripts that are normally identified as
       <a href="/wiki/Semi-syllabary" title="Semi-syllabary">
        semi-syllabaries
       </a>
       . Regarding their origin there is no agreement among researchers; for some they are linked only to the
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">
        Phoenician alphabet
       </a>
       , while for others the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       played a part.
      </p>

          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PLOMO.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lead plaque from Castellet de Bernabè, Valencia
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Greco-Iberian
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabet
       </a>
       is a direct adaptation of an
       <a href="/wiki/Ionic_Greek" title="Ionic Greek">
        Ionic
       </a>
       variant of a
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       to the specificities of the Iberian language. The inscriptions that use the Greco-Iberian alphabet have been found mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">
        Alicante
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_extent_of_linguistic_knowledge">
        Current extent of linguistic knowledge
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Current extent of linguistic knowledge">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Very little is known for certain about Iberian. The investigation of the language is past its initial phase of transcription and compiling of material, and is currently in the phase of identifying grammatical elements in the texts.
      </p>
      <p>
       The hypotheses currently proposed are unconfirmed, and are likely to remain so unless the discovery of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilingual" title="Bilingual">
        bilingual
       </a>
       text allows linguists to confirm their deductions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Iberian appears to have five vowels commonly transcribed as
       <i>
        a e i o u
       </i>
       . Other modern languages on the peninsula such as
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       also have such systems. Although five-vowel systems are extremely common all over the world, it has been suggested that this may point to a
       <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">
        Sprachbund
       </a>
       amongst the ancient languages of the Iberian peninsula.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trask_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trask-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The front vowels (in frequency order:
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       ) appear more frequently than the back vowels (
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       ). Although there are indications of a nasal vowel (
       <i>
        ḿ
       </i>
       ), this is thought to be an
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophone
       </a>
       . Judging by Greek transcriptions, it seems that there were no vowel length distinctions; if this is correct then Iberian uses the long
       <i>
        ē
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ῆτα
       <i>
        ēta
       </i>
       ) as opposed to the short epsilon (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ἔψιλόν
       <i>
        épsilón
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diphthongs">
        Diphthongs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Diphthongs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       It seems that the second element of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diphthongs" title="Diphthongs">
        diphthongs
       </a>
       was always a closed vowel, as in
       <i>
        ai
       </i>
       (śaitabi),
       <i>
        ei
       </i>
       (neitin), and
       <i>
        au
       </i>
       (lauŕ). Untermann observed that the diphthong
       <i>
        ui
       </i>
       could only be found in the first cluster.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Semivowels">
        Semivowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Semivowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       It is possible that Iberian had the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semivowels" title="Semivowels">
        semivowels
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       (in words such as
       <i>
        aiun
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        iunstir
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       (only in loanwords such as
       <i>
        diuiś
       </i>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       ). The fact that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       is lacking in native words casts doubt on whether semivowels really existed in Iberian outside of foreign borrowings and diphthongs.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consonants">
        Consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
         Vibrants
        </a>
        : There are two vibrants
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        . Iberian specialists do not agree about the phonetic values assigned to either vibrant. Correa (1994) hypothesized that
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        was an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
         alveolar flap
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        was a "compound vibrant", that is, a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
         trill
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        . Later, Rodríguez Ramos (2004) suggested that
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        was an alveolar flap
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        is a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retroflex" title="Retroflex">
         retroflex
        </a>
        flap
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɽ]
        </span>
        in line with Ballester (2001) who thought that
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        represents a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uvular" title="Uvular">
         uvular
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
         fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʁ]
        </span>
        . However, Ballester (2005) later changed his hypothesis and took
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        for an alveolar flap
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        for the alveolar trill
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        . Neither
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        nor
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        occurs word-initially, which is also the case in
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
         Basque
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
         Sibilants
        </a>
        : There are two sibilants
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ś
        </i>
        . The distinction is unclear, and there are multiple proposals. Ballester (2001) theorizes that
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        was an alveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         ś
        </i>
        was an
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolo-palatal_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolo-palatal fricative">
         alveolo-palatal
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɕ]
        </span>
        . Rodríguez Ramos (2004) proposes that
        <i>
         ś
        </i>
        was alveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        was an affricate, either dental
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ts]
        </span>
        or palatal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ]
        </span>
        (like English "ch"). This proposal coincides with the observation by Correa on adaptations of Gallic names in Iberian texts.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
         Laterals
        </a>
        : The lateral
        <i>
         l
        </i>
        is normally interpreted as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [l]
        </span>
        . It is extremely rare in final position and it could be that the distribution is on occasion complementary with
        <i>
         ŕ
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         aŕika
         <b>
          l
         </b>
         -er
        </i>
        ~
        <i>
         aŕika
         <b>
          ŕ
         </b>
         -bi
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">
         Nasals
        </a>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          The
          <i>
           n
          </i>
          was probably alveolar
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [n]
          </span>
          .
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           m
          </i>
          : Researchers studying Iberian do not agree on the kind of nasal represented by this letter. The letter
          <i>
           m
          </i>
          rarely occurs word-initially. Velaza (1996) hypothesizes it could be an allophone of medial
          <i>
           n
          </i>
          , as shown in the example of
          <i>
           iumstir/iunstir
          </i>
          . José A. Correa (1999) suggests it may be a
          <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">
           geminate
          </a>
          or strong nasal. Ballester (2001) considers it to be a
          <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">
           labialized
          </a>
          nasal in Iberian and in Celtiberian. Rodríguez Ramos (2004) mentions that it could be an allophone of
          <i>
           n
          </i>
          where it nasalizes the preceding vowel.
         </li>
         <li>
          There is some controversy over the sound
          <i>
           ḿ
          </i>
          . While it's thought to be some type of nasal, there is no certainty as to its value. Several linguists agree on the value
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [na]
          </span>
          , based on similarities with texts written in the Greek alphabet, as there are similarities between the suffixes
          <i>
           -ḿi
          </i>
          /
          <i>
           -nai
          </i>
          , and in the
          <a href="/wiki/Onomastics" title="Onomastics">
           onomastic
          </a>
          elements
          <i>
           -ḿbar-
          </i>
          /
          <i>
           -nabar-
          </i>
          . Another part of this theory seems to contradict itself with the transcription of
          <i>
           ḿbar-beleś
          </i>
          into Latin as VMARBELES. Correa (1999) proposes that it was a
          <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">
           labialized
          </a>
          nasal. It is not even clear that the sign is always pronounced in the same form. Rodríguez Ramos (2004) considers it a nasalized vowel, produced by progressive nasalization.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosives" title="Plosives">
         Plosives
        </a>
        : There are five plosives.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <table align="center" border="0" cellpadding="2" cellspacing="2">
       <tr>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
         unvoiced
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
         voiced
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
         velar
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="whitesmoke">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /k/
         </span>
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="whitesmoke">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /ɡ/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
         dental
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="whitesmoke">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /t/
         </span>
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="whitesmoke">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /d/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td bgcolor="aliceblue">
         labial
        </td>
        <td>
        </td>
        <td bgcolor="whitesmoke">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /b/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        The evidence indicates the non-existence of a phoneme
        <i>
         p
        </i>
        as it is not documented either in the Greek alphabet or in the dual Iberian systems. It is only found in Latin inscriptions naming native Iberians and is thought to be an allophone of
        <i>
         b
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        It has been suggested that the phoneme
        <i>
         b
        </i>
        would on occasions have been pronounced similar to
        <i>
         w
        </i>
        (this would be explained by the frequency of the sign
        <i>
         bu
        </i>
        ), and as such it could have had a nasalized pronunciation.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are a number of known
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affixes
       </a>
       , especially applied to last names. For the Iberian language these seem to be
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        postpositional
       </a>
       , and apparently more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agglutinative" title="Agglutinative">
        agglutinative
       </a>
       than
       <a href="/wiki/Fusional_language" title="Fusional language">
        fusional
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The best-known are the following.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        : applied to proper names to mark possession.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -en
        </i>
        : of a similar or identical use to
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -ka
        </i>
        : seems to indicate the person who receives something
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -te
        </i>
        : seems to indicate the ergative
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -ku
        </i>
        : seems to indicate the ablative
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Possibly related to the Basque local genitive
        <i>
         -ko
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -ken
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         -sken
        </i>
        : usually understood as genitive plural because of its use on coins in ethnical names (with parallels on Latin and Greek coins).
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         -k
        </i>
        : has been proposed on occasions to mark the plural.
        <i>
         -k
        </i>
        is a plural marker in Basque.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are some words for which there has been surmised a more or less probable meaning:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         aŕe take
        </i>
        as akin to the Latin formula
        <i>
         hic est situs
        </i>
        ("here he is") (Untermann 1990, 194) because of a bilingual inscription from
        <a href="/wiki/Tarragona" title="Tarragona">
         Tarragona
        </a>
        C.18.6
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         eban
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ebanen
        </i>
        as equivalent to the Latin
        <i>
         coeravit
        </i>
        ("he cared [to be done]") in tombstones (Untermann 1990, 194), because of a bilingual inscription from
        <a href="/wiki/Sagunto" title="Sagunto">
         Sagunto
        </a>
        F.11.8
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         iltiŕ
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         iltun
        </i>
        as typical Iberian toponyms for city names, meaning something like "city" / "town"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ekiar
        </i>
        : verb or verbal noun with a meaning like "to do" / "to make" compared with the Basque verb
        <i>
         egin
        </i>
        (Beltrán 1942;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Correa 1994, 284).
        <i>
         likine-te ekiar usekerte-ku
        </i>
        with a meaning akin to "made by Likinos of Osicerda" (Correa 1994, 282)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         seltar
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         siltar
        </i>
        as meaning something like "tomb" on tombstones (Untermann 1990, 194).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         śalir
        </i>
        as meaning something like "money" / "coin", because of its use in coins (as
        <i>
         iltiŕta-śalir-ban
        </i>
        ) and its use in lead plaque inscriptions besides numbers and quantities (Untermann 1990, 191).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_names">
        Personal names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Personal names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Thanks to the Latin Inscription of the plaque of Ascoli, which includes a list of Iberian cavalry soldiers in the Roman army (the
       <a href="/wiki/Turma" title="Turma">
        Turma
       </a>
       Salluitana), the forms of Iberian proper names have been unraveled. Iberian names are formed mainly by two interchangeable elements, each usually formed of two syllables, which are written together (Untermann 1998). For example, the element "iltiŕ" can be found in the following names:
       <i>
        iltiŕaŕker
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        iltiŕbaś
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        iltiŕtikeŕ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tursiltiŕ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        baiseiltiŕ
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        bekoniltiŕ
       </i>
       . This discovery was a giant step: from this moment it was possible to identify with some kind of confidence the names of persons in the texts. Nevertheless, the list of components of Iberian names varies between researchers. The basic list comes from Untermann (1990) and was recently updated by Rodríguez Ramos (2002b); complementary data and criteria can be found in the Faria papers (the last two: 2007a and 2007b).

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Iberian_and_Basque">
        Iberian and Basque
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Iberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Iberian and Basque">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Whether Iberian and Basque are two languages of the same language family is still a much-debated question. Many experts on Iberian suspect that there is a relationship of some sort between Iberian and
       <a href="/wiki/Aquitanian_language" title="Aquitanian language">
        Aquitanian
       </a>
       , a precursor of the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque language
       </a>
       . But there is not enough evidence to date to ascertain whether the two languages belong to the same language family or whether the relationship is due to
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        linguistic borrowing
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lexical and onomastic coincidences could be due to borrowing, while the similarities in the phonological structures of the two languages could be due to linguistic areal phenomena (cf. the similarities between Basque and Old Spanish in spite of their being languages of two different families). More scientific studies on Iberian language are needed to shed light on this question.
      </p>
      <p>
       From a historical perspective, the first features where a relationship between Basque and Iberian was claimed were:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the suffixes
        <i>
         -sken
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         -ken
        </i>
        on Iberian coins (which were compared to the genitive plural on similar ancient coins) with the Basque plural (-k) and genitive (-en) endings
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Iberian town names containing
        <i>
         ili
        </i>
        (particularly
        <i>
         iliberri
        </i>
        ), where parallels were drawn with Basque
        <i>
         hiri
        </i>
        ("town") and
        <i>
         berri
        </i>
        ("new").
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although other pairs have been proposed (such as
       <i>
        eban
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ars
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ka
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -te
       </i>
       ), the meanings of these Iberian morphs are still controversial. The main arguments today which relate to coinciding surface forms between Basque and Iberian are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Phonetics:
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Basque" title="Proto-Basque">
         Proto-Basque
        </a>
        phonology, first proposed by
        <a href="/wiki/Koldo_Mitxelena" title="Koldo Mitxelena">
         Michelena
        </a>
        , appears to be very similar to what is known about the Iberian phonological system. It has been claimed that the lack of /m/, common to both Proto-Basque and Iberian, is especially significant
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Onomastics: Aquitanian-Latin inscriptions contain personal and deity names which can clearly be related to modern Basque words, but also show structural and lexical resemblances with Iberian personal names.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        But Iberian influence on the Aquitanian name system, rather than a genetic link, cannot be dismissed either.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Iberian
        <i>
         iltiŕ
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         iltur
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         ili
        </i>
        is read "city".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Modern Basque
        <i>
         hiri
        </i>
        , "city", is derived from the very similar Proto-Basque root *
        <i>
         ili
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Iberian genitive ending
        <i>
         -en
        </i>
        and maybe the genitive plural
        <i>
         -(s)ken
        </i>
        , compared to the Basque genitive
        <i>
         -en
        </i>
        and the Basque genitive plural *
        <i>
         ag-en
        </i>
        as reconstructed by Michelena.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
         <a href="#cite_note-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
        But Michelena himself was sceptical about this comparison.
       </li>
       <li>
        An Iberian formula which frequently appears on tombstones,
        <i>
         aŕe take
        </i>
        , with variants such as
        <i>
         aŕe teike
        </i>
        , which on a bilingual inscription from Tarragona may be equivalent to the Latin
        <i>
         hic situs est
        </i>
        ("here is"), as proposed by
        <a href="/wiki/Emil_H%C3%BCbner" title="Emil Hübner">
         Hübner
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
         <a href="#cite_note-21">
          [21]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This was compared by
        <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schuchardt" title="Hugo Schuchardt">
         Schuchardt
        </a>
        (1907)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
         <a href="#cite_note-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        with Basque "(h)ara dago" “there is/stays”.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Iberian word
        <i>
         ekiar
        </i>
        , explained as something akin to “he made”,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
         <a href="#cite_note-23">
          [23]
         </a>
        </sup>
        proposed to be linked with the Basque verb ‘egin’ "make"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Iberian word
        <i>
         śalir
        </i>
        explained as “money”, “coin” or “value”, proposed to be linked to Basque word ‘sari’ (probably Proto-Basque *sali) meaning “value”, “payment”, “reward”.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
         <a href="#cite_note-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       Even so, Orduña does not claim this comparison to be a proof of a family relation between Iberian and Basque, but rather owing to Iberian loanwords in the Basque language. In contrast, Ferrer believes that the similarities could be caused due to both the genetic relationship or the loan, but indicates that the loan of the entire system of numerals is rare (but has known to occur such as the case of
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">
        Middle Chinese
       </a>
       numeral being borrowed wholesale into
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_numerals" title="Vietnamese numerals">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_numerals" title="Japanese numerals">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_numerals" title="Korean numerals">
        Korean
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thai_numerals" title="Thai numerals">
        Thai
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Joseba Lakarra (2010) has rejected both hypotheses: loan or genetic relationship. Lakarra’s arguments focus almost exclusively on the field of Basque historical grammar, but also arguments, following de Hoz (1993) hypothesis, that the hypothesis of the borrowing have already turned out implausible due to the limited and remote extension of the territory where Iberian was spoken as first language in South-East Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Javier_de_Hoz" title="Javier de Hoz">
        Javier de Hoz
       </a>
       (2011, pp. 196–198) considers plausible the internal contextual and combinatorial arguments that would support the hypothesis that these Iberian elements could be interpreted as numerals. In fact, concerning the specific values, he considers valid the proposed equivalences between Iberian ban with 'one' and between Iberian erder with 'half', according to the marks of value found in coins, while he considers that the rest of the proposed equivalences are a working hypothesis. Regarding the equivalence between the possible Iberian numerals and the Basque numerals, he agrees with Lakarra (2010) that the shape of the documented Iberian forms does not fit the expected protobasque forms. Finally, he considers that the greatest difficulty in accepting this hypothesis is, paradoxically, its extent and systematic nature, because if it was correct, it would result in a close relationship between Iberian and Basque, which should allow the identification of other relationships between Iberian and Basque subsystems, as clearly as this one, relationships that no investigator using reasonable linguistic arguments has been able to identify.
      </p>
      <p>
       Eduardo Orduña (2011) insists that the Iberian elements proposed as numerals are not only similar to the Basque numerals, but also they combine as numerals and appear in contexts where numerals are expected. And remarks that Lakarra (2010) does not dispute these arguments [neither does de Hoz (2010)]. As regards the de Hoz hypothesis about considering the Iberian language as a
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       , Orduña remarks its hypothetical character, although Lakarra presents that hypothesis as an established fact. The problems of this hypothesis have been collected by Ferrer (2013) in a later work. Regarding the phonetic difficulties indicated by Lakarra, Orduña argues that its proposals are compatible with the Proto-Basque reconstructed of Michelena, which is for chronology and security the reconstruction that an iberist has to consider, while the hypothesis of internal Basque reconstruction of Lakarra has a vague chronology and a much lower degree of security. Finally, contrary to his first opinion in favor of the loan, concludes that the most economical hypothesis to explain the similarities between the Iberian numeral system and the Basque numeral system is the genetic relationship.
      </p>
      <p>
       Francisco Villar (2014, 259) notes that the similarities between Iberian numerals and Basque numerals are of the same order as those documented among Indo-European languages and consequently argues that the only sustainable hypothesis at this point is the genetic relationship between Iberian and Basque. Villar also believes that if the reconstruction of Proto-Basque proposed by Lakarra (2010) is incompatible with the evidence derived from the numerals, the reconstruction must be corrected, as like all reconstructions, is hypothetical and perfectible.
      </p>
