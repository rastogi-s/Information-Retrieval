       <tr>

         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_direction" title="Vertical direction">
          vertical
         </a>

       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td style="padding:0 0.1em 0.4em;text-align:left;">
         Lines of latitude appear
         <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_plane" title="Horizontal plane">
          horizontal
         </a>
         with varying curvature in this projection; but are actually circular with different radii. All locations with a given latitude are collectively referred to as a
         <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
          circle of latitude
         </a>
         .
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td style="padding:0 0.1em 0.4em;text-align:left;;padding-bottom:0;">

          </a>

         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
           equator
          </a>

         <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
          Northern Hemisphere
         </a>
         and a
         <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
          Southern Hemisphere
         </a>
         , and has a latitude of 0°.

       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       and denoted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek letter
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lambda" title="Lambda">
        lambda
       </a>
       (λ). Meridians (lines running from the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       ) connect points with the same longitude. By convention, one of these, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       , which passes through the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
        Royal Observatory, Greenwich
       </a>
       , England, was allocated the position of zero degrees longitude. The longitude of other places is measured as the angle east or west from the Prime Meridian, ranging from 0° at the Prime Meridian to +180° eastward and −180° westward. Specifically, it is the angle between a plane containing the Prime Meridian and a plane containing the North Pole, South Pole and the location in question. (This forms a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-handed_coordinate_system" title="Right-handed coordinate system">
        right-handed coordinate system
       </a>
       with the
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        z
       </span>
       axis (right hand thumb) pointing from the Earth's center toward the North Pole and the
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        x
       </span>
       axis (right hand index finger) extending from Earth's center through the equator at the Prime Meridian.)
      </p>
      <p>
       A location's north–south position along a meridian is given by its
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       , which is approximately the angle between the local vertical and the plane of the Equator.
      </p>
      <p>
       If the Earth were perfectly spherical and homogeneous, then the longitude at a point would be equal to the angle between a vertical north–south plane through that point and the plane of the Greenwich meridian. Everywhere on Earth the vertical north–south plane would contain the Earth's axis. But the Earth is not homogeneous, and has mountains—which have gravity and so can shift the vertical plane away from the Earth's axis. The vertical north–south plane still intersects the plane of the Greenwich meridian at some angle; that angle is the astronomical longitude, calculated from star observations. The longitude shown on maps and GPS devices is the angle between the Greenwich plane and a not-quite-vertical plane through the point; the not-quite-vertical plane is perpendicular to the surface of the spheroid chosen to approximate the Earth's sea-level surface, rather than perpendicular to the sea-level surface itself.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Longitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_longitude" title="History of longitude">
        History of longitude
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Longitude_Vespucci.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="377" data-file-width="360" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Longitude_Vespucci.png/220px-Longitude_Vespucci.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Longitude_Vespucci.png/330px-Longitude_Vespucci.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Longitude_Vespucci.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Longitude_Vespucci.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Amerigo Vespucci's means of determining longitude
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The measurement of longitude is important both to
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       and for ocean
       <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">
        navigation
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mariner" title="Mariner">
        Mariners
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Explorer" title="Explorer">
        explorers
       </a>
       for most of history struggled to determine longitude. Finding a method of determining longitude took centuries, resulting in the history of longitude recording the effort of some of the greatest scientific minds.
      </p>
      <p>
       Latitude was calculated by observing with
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrant_(instrument)" title="Quadrant (instrument)">
        quadrant
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">
        astrolabe
       </a>
       the altitude of the sun or of charted stars above the horizon, but longitude is harder.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Amerigo_Vespucci" title="Amerigo Vespucci">
        Amerigo Vespucci
       </a>
       was perhaps the first European to proffer a solution, after devoting a great deal of time and energy studying the problem during his sojourns in the
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        As to longitude, I declare that I found so much difficulty in determining it that I was put to great pains to ascertain the east-west distance I had covered. The final result of my labours was that I found nothing better to do than to watch for and take observations at night of the conjunction of one planet with another, and especially of the conjunction of the moon with the other planets, because the moon is swifter in her course than any other planet. I compared my observations with an almanac. After I had made experiments many nights, one night, the twenty-third of August 1499, there was a conjunction of the moon with Mars, which according to the almanac was to occur at midnight or a half hour before. I found that...at midnight Mars's position was three and a half degrees to the east.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="443" data-file-width="355" height="212" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg/170px-John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg/255px-John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg/340px-John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:John_Harrison_Uhrmacher.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         John Harrison solved the greatest problem of his day.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       By comparing the positions of the moon and Mars with their anticipated positions, Vespucci was able to crudely deduce his longitude. But this method had several limitations: First, it required the occurrence of a specific astronomical event (in this case, Mars passing through the same
       <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">
        right ascension
       </a>
       as the moon), and the observer needed to anticipate this event via an astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">
        almanac
       </a>
       . One needed also to know the precise time, which was difficult to ascertain in foreign lands. Finally, it required a stable viewing platform, rendering the technique useless on the rolling deck of a ship at sea. See
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(navigation)" title="Lunar distance (navigation)">
        Lunar distance (navigation)
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1612
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo Galilei
       </a>
       demonstrated that with sufficiently accurate knowledge of the orbits of the moons of Jupiter one could use their positions as a universal clock and this would make possible the determination of longitude, but the method he devised was impracticable for navigators on ships because of their instability.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1714 the British government passed the
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude_Act" title="Longitude Act">
        Longitude Act
       </a>
       which offered large financial rewards to the first person to demonstrate a practical method for determining the longitude of a ship at sea. These rewards motivated many to search for a solution.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Longitude_(PSF).png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2532" data-file-width="2437" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Longitude_%28PSF%29.png/220px-Longitude_%28PSF%29.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Longitude_%28PSF%29.png/330px-Longitude_%28PSF%29.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Longitude_%28PSF%29.png/440px-Longitude_%28PSF%29.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Longitude_(PSF).png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Drawing of Earth with longitudes but without latitudes.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Harrison" title="John Harrison">
        John Harrison
       </a>
       , a self-educated
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        English
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Clockmaker" title="Clockmaker">
        clockmaker
       </a>
       , invented the
       <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">
        marine chronometer
       </a>
       , the key piece in solving the problem of accurately establishing longitude at sea, thus revolutionising and extending the possibility of safe long distance sea travel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrison_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harrison-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the Board of Longitude rewarded John Harrison for his marine chronometer in 1773, chronometers remained very expensive and the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(navigation)" title="Lunar distance (navigation)">
        lunar distance method
       </a>
       continued to be used for decades. Finally, the combination of the availability of marine chronometers and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_telegraph" title="Wireless telegraph">
        wireless telegraph
       </a>
       time signals put an end to the use of lunars in the 20th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike latitude, which has the equator as a natural starting position, there is no natural starting position for longitude. Therefore, a reference meridian had to be chosen. It was a popular practice to use a nation's capital as the starting point, but other locations were also used. While
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        British
       </a>
       cartographers had long used the Greenwich meridian in London, other references were used elsewhere, including
       <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">
        El Hierro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Copenhagen" title="Copenhagen">
        Copenhagen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
        Jerusalem
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">
        Saint Petersburg
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pisa" title="Pisa">
        Pisa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Philadelphia" title="Philadelphia">
        Philadelphia
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_D.C." title="Washington D.C.">
        Washington D.C.
       </a>
       In 1884 the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference" title="International Meridian Conference">
        International Meridian Conference
       </a>
       adopted the Greenwich meridian as the
       <i>
        universal Prime Meridian
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        zero point of longitude
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noting_and_calculating_longitude">
        Noting and calculating longitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Longitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Noting and calculating longitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Longitude is given as an
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angular measurement
       </a>
       ranging from 0° at the Prime Meridian to +180° eastward and −180° westward. The Greek letter λ (lambda),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is used to denote the location of a place on Earth east or west of the Prime Meridian.
      </p>
      <p>
       Each degree of longitude is sub-divided into 60
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        minutes
       </a>
       , each of which is divided into 60
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        seconds
       </a>
       . A longitude is thus specified in
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       notation as 23° 27′ 30″ E. For higher precision, the seconds are specified with a
       <a href="/wiki/Decimal#Decimal_fractions" title="Decimal">
        decimal fraction
       </a>
       . An alternative representation uses degrees and minutes, where parts of a minute are expressed in decimal notation with a fraction, thus: 23° 27.5′ E. Degrees may also be expressed as a decimal fraction: 23.45833° E. For calculations, the angular measure may be converted to
       <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">
        radians
       </a>
       , so longitude may also be expressed in this manner as a signed fraction of
       <span class="texhtml">
        π
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Pi" title="Pi">
        pi
       </a>
       ), or an unsigned fraction of 2
       <span class="texhtml">
        π
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For calculations, the West/East suffix is replaced by a negative sign in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_hemisphere" title="Western hemisphere">
        western hemisphere
       </a>
       . Confusingly, the convention of negative for East is also sometimes seen. The preferred convention—that East is positive—is consistent with a right-handed
       <a href="/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system" title="Cartesian coordinate system">
        Cartesian coordinate system
       </a>
       , with the North Pole up. A specific longitude may then be combined with a specific latitude (usually positive in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_hemisphere" title="Northern hemisphere">
        northern hemisphere
       </a>
       ) to give a precise position on the Earth's surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no other physical principle determining longitude directly but with time. Longitude at a point may be determined by calculating the time difference between that at its location and
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        Coordinated Universal Time
       </a>
       (UTC). Since there are 24 hours in a day and 360 degrees in a circle, the sun moves across the sky at a rate of 15 degrees per hour (360° ÷ 24 hours = 15° per hour). So if the
       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">
        time zone
       </a>
       a person is in is three hours ahead of UTC then that person is near 45° longitude (3 hours × 15° per hour = 45°). The word
       <i>
        near
       </i>
       is used because the point might not be at the center of the time zone; also the time zones are defined politically, so their centers and boundaries often do not lie on meridians at multiples of 15°. In order to perform this calculation, however, a person needs to have a
       <a href="/wiki/Marine_chronometer" title="Marine chronometer">
        chronometer
       </a>
       (watch) set to UTC and needs to determine local time by solar or astronomical observation. The details are more complex than described here: see the articles on
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        Universal Time
       </a>
       and on the
       <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_time" title="Equation of time">
        equation of time
       </a>
       for more details.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Singularity_and_discontinuity_of_longitude">
        Singularity and discontinuity of longitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Longitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Singularity and discontinuity of longitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Note that the longitude is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_singularity" title="Mathematical singularity">
        singular
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">
        Poles
       </a>
       and calculations that are sufficiently accurate for other positions, may be inaccurate at or near the Poles. Also the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Discontinuity_(mathematics)" title="Discontinuity (mathematics)">
        discontinuity
       </a>
       at the ±
       <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">
        180° meridian
       </a>
       must be handled with care in calculations. An example is a calculation of east displacement by subtracting two longitudes, which gives the wrong answer if the two positions are on either side of this meridian. To avoid these complexities, consider replacing latitude and longitude with another
       <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_position_representation" title="Horizontal position representation">
        horizontal position representation
       </a>
       in calculation.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plate_movement_and_longitude">
        Plate movement and longitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Longitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Plate movement and longitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        tectonic plates
       </a>
       move relative to one another in different directions at speeds on the order of 50 to 100mm per year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       So points on the Earth's surface on different plates are always in motion relative to one another. For example, the longitudinal difference between a point on the Equator in Uganda, on the
       <a href="/wiki/African_Plate" title="African Plate">
        African Plate
       </a>
       , and a point on the Equator in Ecuador, on the
       <a href="/wiki/South_American_Plate" title="South American Plate">
        South American Plate
       </a>
       , is increasing by about 0.0014
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc#Symbols_and_abbreviations" title="Minute of arc">
        arcseconds
       </a>
       per year. These tectonic movements likewise affect latitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       If a
       <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_datum" title="Geodetic datum">
        global reference frame
       </a>
       (such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       , for example) is used, the longitude of a place on the surface will change from year to year. To minimize this change, when dealing just with points on a single plate, a different reference frame can be used, whose coordinates are fixed to a particular plate, such as "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAD83" title="NAD83">
        NAD83
       </a>
       " for North America or "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ETRS89" title="ETRS89">
        ETRS89
       </a>
       " for Europe.
      </p>

      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_meridian_(planets)" title="Prime meridian (planets)">
        Prime meridian (planets)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        Planetary
       </a>
       co-ordinate systems are defined relative to their mean
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">
        axis of rotation
       </a>
       and various definitions of longitude depending on the body. The longitude systems of most of those bodies with observable rigid surfaces have been defined by references to a surface feature such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        crater
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_pole" title="North pole">
        north pole
       </a>
       is that pole of rotation that lies on the north side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Invariable_plane" title="Invariable plane">
        invariable plane
       </a>
       of the solar system (near the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ). The location of the Prime Meridian as well as the position of body's north pole on the celestial sphere may vary with time due to precession of the axis of rotation of the planet (or satellite). If the position angle of the body's Prime Meridian increases with time, the body has a direct (or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Direct_motion" title="Direct motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       ) rotation; otherwise the rotation is said to be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the absence of other information, the axis of rotation is assumed to be normal to the mean
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        orbital plane
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       and most of the satellites are in this category. For many of the satellites, it is assumed that the rotation rate is equal to the mean
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       . In the case of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        giant planets
       </a>
       , since their surface features are constantly changing and moving at various rates, the rotation of their
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic fields
       </a>
       is used as a reference instead. In the case of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , even this criterion fails (because its magnetosphere is very complex and does not really rotate in a steady fashion), and an agreed-upon value for the rotation of its equator is used instead.
      </p>
      <p>
       For
       <i>
        planetographic longitude
       </i>
       , west longitudes (i.e., longitudes measured positively to the west) are used when the rotation is prograde, and east longitudes (i.e., longitudes measured positively to the east) when the rotation is retrograde. In simpler terms, imagine a distant, non-orbiting observer viewing a planet as it rotates. Also suppose that this observer is within the plane of the planet's equator. A point on the Equator that passes directly in front of this observer later in time has a higher planetographic longitude than a point that did so earlier in time.
      </p>
      <p>
       However,
       <i>
        planetocentric longitude
       </i>
       is always measured positively to the east, regardless of which way the planet rotates.
       <i>
        East
       </i>
       is defined as the counter-clockwise direction around the planet, as seen from above its north pole, and the north pole is whichever pole more closely aligns with the Earth's north pole. Longitudes traditionally have been written using "E" or "W" instead of "+" or "−" to indicate this polarity. For example, the following all mean the same thing:
      </p>
      <div class="div-col columns column-count column-count-2" style="-moz-column-count: 2; -webkit-column-count: 2; column-count: 2;">
       <ul>
        <li>
         −91°
        </li>
        <li>
         91°W
        </li>
        <li>
         +269°
        </li>
        <li>
         269°E.
        </li>
       </ul>
      </div>
      <p>
       The reference surfaces for some planets (such as Earth and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       ) are
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoids
       </a>
       of revolution for which the equatorial radius is larger than the polar radius; in other words, they are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate spheroids
       </a>
       . Smaller bodies (
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       , etc.) tend to be better approximated by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Triaxial_ellipsoids&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Triaxial ellipsoids (page does not exist)">
        triaxial ellipsoids
       </a>
       ; however, triaxial ellipsoids would render many computations more complicated, especially those related to
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        map projections
       </a>
       . Many projections would lose their elegant and popular properties. For this reason spherical reference surfaces are frequently used in mapping programs.
      </p>
      <p>
       The modern standard for maps of Mars (since about 2002) is to use planetocentric coordinates. The meridian of Mars is located at
       <a href="/wiki/Airy-0" title="Airy-0">
        Airy-0
       </a>
       crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_lock" title="Tidal lock">
        Tidally-locked
       </a>
       bodies have a natural reference longitude passing through the point nearest to their parent body: 0° the center of the primary-facing hemisphere, 90° the center of the leading hemisphere, 180° the center of the anti-primary hemisphere, and 270° the center of the trailing hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However,
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       due to non-circular orbits or axial tilts causes this point to move around any fixed point on the celestial body like an
       <a href="/wiki/Analemma" title="Analemma">
        analemma
       </a>
       .
      </p>
