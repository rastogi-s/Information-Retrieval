      <p>
       <b>
        Mean sea level (MSL)
       </b>
       (often shortened to
       <b>
        sea level
       </b>
       ) is
       <a href="/wiki/Mean" title="Mean">
        an average
       </a>
       level of the surface of one or more of
       <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">
        Earth's oceans
       </a>
       from which heights such as elevations may be measured. MSL is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_datum#Vertical_datum" title="Geodetic datum">
        vertical datum
       </a>
       – a standardised
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodetic
       </a>
       reference point – that is used, for example, as a
       <a href="/wiki/Chart_datum" title="Chart datum">
        chart datum
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Navigation" title="Navigation">
        marine navigation
       </a>
       , or, in aviation, as the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_sea_level" title="Standard sea level">
        standard sea level
       </a>
       at which
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">
        atmospheric pressure
       </a>
       is measured to
       <a href="/wiki/Calibration" title="Calibration">
        calibrate
       </a>
       altitude and, consequently, aircraft
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_level" title="Flight level">
        flight levels
       </a>
       . A common and relatively straightforward mean sea-level standard is the midpoint between a
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        mean low and mean high tide
       </a>
       at a particular location.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sea levels can be affected by many factors and are known to have varied greatly over
       <a href="/wiki/Geologic_time_scale" title="Geologic time scale">
        geological time scales
       </a>
       . The careful measurement of variations in MSL can offer insights into ongoing
       <a href="/wiki/Climate_change" title="Climate change">
        climate change
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">
        sea level rise
       </a>
       has been widely quoted as evidence of ongoing
       <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">
        global warming
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        above sea level
       </i>
       generally refers to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Above_mean_sea_level" title="Above mean sea level">
        above mean sea level
       </a>
       (AMSL).
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement">
        Measurement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Measurement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="373" data-file-width="537" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/220px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/330px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png/440px-Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Recent_Sea_Level_Rise.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sea level measurements from 23 long
         <a href="/wiki/Tide_gauge" title="Tide gauge">
          tide gauge
         </a>
         records in geologically stable environments show a rise of around 200 millimetres (7.9 in) during the 20th century (2 mm/year).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Precise determination of a "mean sea level" is difficult to achieve because of the many factors that affect sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNRC32_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USNRC32-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sea level varies quite a lot on several scales of time and space. This is because the sea is in constant motion, affected by the tides, wind, atmospheric pressure, local gravitational differences, temperature,
       <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">
        salinity
       </a>
       and so forth. The easiest way this may be calculated is by selecting a location and calculating the mean sea level at that point and use it as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       . For example, a period of 19 years of hourly level observations may be averaged and used to determine the mean sea level at some measurement point.
      </p>
      <p>
       To an operator of a
       <a href="/wiki/Tide_gauge" title="Tide gauge">
        tide gauge
       </a>
       , MSL means the "still water level"—the level of the sea with motions such as
       <a href="/wiki/Wind_wave" title="Wind wave">
        wind waves
       </a>
       averaged out—averaged over a period of time such that changes in sea level, e.g., due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       , also get averaged out. One measures the values of MSL in respect to the land. Hence a change in MSL can result from a real change in sea level, or from a change in the height of the land on which the tide gauge operates.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the UK, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ordnance_Datum" title="Ordnance Datum">
        Ordnance Datum
       </a>
       (the 0 metres height on UK maps) is the mean sea level measured at
       <a href="/wiki/Newlyn" title="Newlyn">
        Newlyn
       </a>
       in Cornwall between 1915 and 1921. Prior to 1921, the datum was MSL at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Victoria_Dock_(Liverpool)" title="Victoria Dock (Liverpool)">
        Victoria Dock, Liverpool
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In Hong Kong, "mPD" is a surveying term meaning "metres above Principal Datum" and refers to height of 1.230m below the average sea level.
      </p>
      <p>
       In France, the Marégraphe in Marseilles measures continuously the sea level since 1883 and offers the longest collapsed data about the sea level. It is used for a part of continental Europe and main part of Africa as official sea level. Elsewhere in Europe vertical elevation references (European Vertical Reference System) are made to the
       <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Ordnance_Datum" title="Amsterdam Ordnance Datum">
        Amsterdam Peil
       </a>
       elevation, which dates back to the 1690s.
      </p>
      <p>
       Satellite altimeters have been making precise measurements of sea level
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       since the launch of
       <a href="/wiki/TOPEX/Poseidon" title="TOPEX/Poseidon">
        TOPEX/Poseidon
       </a>
       in 1992. A joint mission of
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">
        CNES
       </a>
       , TOPEX/Poseidon was followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Jason-1" title="Jason-1">
        Jason-1
       </a>
       in 2001 and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_Surface_Topography_Mission" title="Ocean Surface Topography Mission">
        Ocean Surface Topography Mission
       </a>
       on the Jason-2 satellite in 2008.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Height_above_mean_sea_level">
        <span id="Above_mean_sea_level">
        </span>
        <span id="AMSL">
        </span>
        Height above mean sea level
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Height above mean sea level">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        Elevation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Height above mean sea level
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        AMSL
       </i>
       ) is the elevation (on the ground) or altitude (in the air) of an object, relative to the average sea level datum. It is also used in aviation, where some heights are recorded and reported with respect to mean sea level (MSL) (contrast with
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_level" title="Flight level">
        flight level
       </a>
       ), and in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_sciences" title="Atmospheric sciences">
        atmospheric sciences
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_surveying" title="Land surveying">
        land surveying
       </a>
       . An alternative is to base height measurements on an
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       of the entire Earth, which is what systems such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       do. In aviation, the ellipsoid known as
       <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">
        World Geodetic System
       </a>
       84 is increasingly used to define heights; however, differences up to 100 metres (328 feet) exist between this ellipsoid height and mean tidal height. The alternative is to use a
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       -based vertical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_Vertical_Datum_of_1988" title="North American Vertical Datum of 1988">
        NAVD88
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geographic
       </a>
       features such as mountains on a
       <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">
        topographic map
       </a>
       , variations in elevation are shown by
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
        contour lines
       </a>
       . The elevation of a mountain denotes the highest point or summit and is typically illustrated as a small circle on a topographic map with the AMSL height shown in metres, feet or both.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the rare case that a location is below sea level, the elevation AMSL is negative. For one such case, see
       <a href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Airport_Schiphol" title="Amsterdam Airport Schiphol">
        Amsterdam Airport Schiphol
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulties_in_use">
        Difficulties in use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       To extend this definition far from the sea means comparing the local height of the mean sea surface with a "level" reference surface, or geodetic datum, called the geoid. In a state of rest or absence of external forces, the mean sea level would coincide with this geoid surface, being an equipotential surface of the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational
       </a>
       field. In reality, due to currents, air pressure variations, temperature and salinity variations, etc., this does not occur, not even as a long-term average. The location-dependent, but persistent in time, separation between mean sea level and the geoid is referred to as (stationary)
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_topography" title="Ocean surface topography">
        ocean surface topography
       </a>
       . It varies globally in a range of ±
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       2
       <span class="nowrap">
       </span>
       m.
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, adjustments were made to sea-level measurements to take into account the effects of the 235 lunar month
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        Metonic cycle
       </a>
       and the 223-month
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse_cycle" title="Eclipse cycle">
        eclipse cycle
       </a>
       on the tides.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Several terms are used to describe the changing relationships between sea level and dry land. When the term "relative" is used, it means change relative to a fixed point in the sediment pile. The term "eustatic" refers to global changes in sea level relative to a fixed point, such as the centre of the earth, for example as a result of melting ice-caps. The term "steric" refers to global changes in sea level due to
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">
        thermal expansion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Salinity" title="Salinity">
        salinity
       </a>
       variations. The term "isostatic" refers to changes in the level of the land relative to a fixed point in the earth, possibly due to thermal buoyancy or
       <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">
        tectonic
       </a>
       effects; it implies no change in the volume of water in the oceans. The melting of
       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
        glaciers
       </a>
       at the end of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_age" title="Ice age">
        ice ages
       </a>
       is one example of eustatic
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level_rise" title="Sea level rise">
        sea level rise
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Subsidence" title="Subsidence">
        subsidence
       </a>
       of land due to the withdrawal of
       <a href="/wiki/Groundwater" title="Groundwater">
        groundwater
       </a>
       is an isostatic cause of relative sea level rise.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleoclimatologist" title="Paleoclimatologist">
        Paleoclimatologists
       </a>
       can track sea level by examining the rocks deposited along coasts that are very tectonically stable, like the east coast of North America. Areas like volcanic islands are experiencing relative sea level rise as a result of isostatic cooling of the rock which causes the land to sink.
      </p>
      <p>
       On other planets that lack a liquid ocean,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetologist" title="Planetologist">
        planetologists
       </a>
       can calculate a "mean altitude" by averaging the heights of all points on the surface. This altitude, sometimes referred to as a "sea level", serves equivalently as a reference for the height of planetary features.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_and_eustatic">
        Local and eustatic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Local and eustatic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="423" data-file-width="700" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png/220px-Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png/330px-Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png/440px-Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mass_balance_atmospheric_circulation.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Water_cycle" title="Water cycle">
          Water cycles
         </a>
         between ocean,
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
          atmosphere
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
          glaciers
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Local mean sea level (LMSL) is defined as the height of the sea with respect to a land benchmark, averaged over a period of time (such as a month or a year) long enough that fluctuations caused by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_wave" title="Ocean surface wave">
        waves
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       are smoothed out. One must adjust perceived changes in LMSL to account for vertical movements of the land, which can be of the same order (mm/yr) as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_level_change" title="Sea level change">
        sea level changes
       </a>
       . Some land movements occur because of
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostatic
       </a>
       adjustment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       to the melting of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheets
       </a>
       at the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/Last_glacial_period" title="Last glacial period">
        last ice age
       </a>
       . The weight of the ice sheet depresses the underlying land, and when the ice melts away the
       <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">
        land slowly rebounds
       </a>
       . Changes in ground-based ice volume also affect local and regional sea levels by the readjustment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/True_polar_wander" title="True polar wander">
        true polar wander
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">
        Atmospheric pressure
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_current" title="Ocean current">
        ocean currents
       </a>
       and local ocean temperature changes can affect LMSL as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Eustatic
       </i>
       change (as opposed to local change) results in an alteration to the global sea levels due to changes in either the volume of water in the world's oceans or net changes in the volume of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_basin" title="Ocean basin">
        ocean basins
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oilfield_Glossary_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Oilfield_Glossary-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_changes">
        Recent changes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Recent changes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sea_level_rise" title="Current sea level rise">
        Current sea level rise
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Global_warming" title="Global warming">
        Global warming
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_heat_content" title="Ocean heat content">
        Ocean heat content
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Effects_of_global_warming_on_oceans" title="Effects of global warming on oceans">
        Effects of global warming on oceans
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       For at least the last 100 years, sea level has been rising at an average rate of about 1.8 mm (0.1 in) per year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most of this rise can be attributed to the increase in temperature of the sea and the resulting slight
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">
        thermal expansion
       </a>
       of the upper 500 metres (1,640 feet) of sea water. Additional contributions, as much as one-quarter of the total, come from water sources on land, such as melting snow and
       <a href="/wiki/Glacier" title="Glacier">
        glaciers
       </a>
       and extraction of groundwater for irrigation and other agricultural and human uses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IPCC4_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IPCC4-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">
        Aviation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sea_level&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Aviation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude#Aviation" title="Altitude">
        Altitude in aviation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Pilots can estimate height above sea level with an
       <a href="/wiki/Altimeter" title="Altimeter">
        altimeter
       </a>
       set to a defined
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barometric_pressure" title="Barometric pressure">
        barometric pressure
       </a>
       . Generally, the pressure used to set the altimeter is the barometric pressure that would exist at MSL in the region being flown over. This pressure is referred to as either
       <a href="/wiki/QNH" title="QNH">
        QNH
       </a>
       or "altimeter" and is transmitted to the pilot by radio from
       <a href="/wiki/Air_traffic_control" title="Air traffic control">
        air traffic control
       </a>
       (ATC) or an
       <a href="/wiki/Automatic_terminal_information_service" title="Automatic terminal information service">
        automatic terminal information service
       </a>
       (ATIS). Since the terrain elevation is also referenced to MSL, the pilot can estimate height above ground by subtracting the terrain altitude from the altimeter reading.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aviation_chart" title="Aviation chart">
        Aviation charts
       </a>
       are divided into boxes and the maximum terrain altitude from MSL in each box is clearly indicated. Once above the transition altitude, the altimeter is set to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_standard_atmosphere" title="International standard atmosphere">
        international standard atmosphere
       </a>
       (ISA) pressure at MSL which is 1013.25 hPa or 29.92 inHg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
