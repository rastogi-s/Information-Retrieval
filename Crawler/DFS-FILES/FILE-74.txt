      <p>
       A
       <b>
        circle of latitude
       </b>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       is an abstract
       <a href="/wiki/East" title="East">
        east
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/West" title="West">
        west
       </a>
       circle connecting all locations around Earth (ignoring
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       ) at a given
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Circles of latitude are often called
       <b>
        parallels
       </b>
       because they are parallel to each other; that is, any two circles are always the same distance apart. A location's position along a circle of latitude is given by its
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . Circles of latitude are unlike circles of longitude, which are all
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circles
       </a>
       with the centre of Earth in the middle, as the circles of latitude get smaller as the distance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       increases. Their length can be calculated by a common
       <a href="/wiki/Sine" title="Sine">
        sine
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosine" title="Cosine">
        cosine
       </a>
       function. The
       <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_north" title="60th parallel north">
        60th parallel north
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/60th_parallel_south" title="60th parallel south">
        south
       </a>
       is half as long as the Equator (disregarding Earth's minor
       <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">
        flattening
       </a>
       by 0.3%). A circle of latitude is perpendicular to all
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
        meridians
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The latitude of the circle is approximately the
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angle
       </a>
       between the Equator and the circle, with the angle's
       <a href="/wiki/Vertex_(geometry)" title="Vertex (geometry)">
        vertex
       </a>
       at Earth's centre. The equator is at 0°, and the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       are at 90° north and 90° south, respectively. The Equator is the longest circle of latitude and is the only circle of latitude which also is a great circle.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are 89
       <a href="/wiki/Integer" title="Integer">
        integral
       </a>
       (whole
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degree
       </a>
       ) circles of latitude between the equator and the Poles in each
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemispheres_of_the_Earth" title="Hemispheres of the Earth">
        hemisphere
       </a>
       , but these can be divided into more precise measurements of latitude, and are often represented as a decimal degree (e.g. 34.637°N) or with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        minutes and seconds
       </a>
       (e.g. 22°14'26"S). There is no limit to how precisely latitude can be measured, and so there are an infinite number of circles of latitude on Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       On a map, the circles of latitude may or may not be parallel, and their spacing may vary, depending on which
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        projection
       </a>
       is used to map the surface of the Earth onto a plane. On an
       <a href="/wiki/Equirectangular_projection" title="Equirectangular projection">
        equirectangular projection
       </a>
       , centered on the equator, the circles of latitude are horizontal, parallel, and equally spaced. On other cylindrical and pseudocylindrical projections, the circles of latitude are horizontal and parallel, but may be spaced unevenly to give the map useful characteristics. For instance, on a
       <a href="/wiki/Mercator_projection" title="Mercator projection">
        Mercator projection
       </a>
       the circles of latitude are more widely spaced near the poles to preserve local scales and shapes, while on a
       <a href="/wiki/Gall%E2%80%93Peters_projection" title="Gall–Peters projection">
        Gall–Peters projection
       </a>
       the circles of latitude are spaced more closely near the poles so that comparisons of area will be accurate. On most non-cylindrical and non-pseudocylindrical projections, the circles of latitude are neither straight nor parallel.
      </p>
      <p>
       Arcs of circles of latitude are sometimes used as boundaries between countries or regions where distinctive natural borders are lacking (such as in deserts), or when an artificial border is drawn as a "line on a map", which was made in massive scale during the 1884
       <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Conference" title="Berlin Conference">
        Berlin Conference
       </a>
       , regarding huge parts of the African continent. North American nations and states have also mostly been created by straight lines, which are often parts of circles of latitudes. For instance, the northern border of
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       is at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/41%C2%B0_N" title="41° N">
        41°N
       </a>
       while the southern border is at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/37%C2%B0_N" title="37° N">
        37°N
       </a>
       . Roughly half the length of border between the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       follows
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/49%C2%B0_N" title="49° N">
        49°N
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Major_circles_of_latitude">
        Major circles of latitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Major circles of latitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="651" data-file-width="1300" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg/300px-World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg/450px-World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg/600px-World_map_with_major_latitude_circles.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

      <p>
       There are five major circles of latitude, listed below from north to south. The position of the Equator is fixed (90 degrees from Earth's axis of rotation) but the latitudes of the other circles depend on the tilt of this axis relative to the plane of Earth's orbit, and so are not perfectly fixed. The values below are for 26 January 2018:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">
         Arctic Circle
        </a>
        (66°33′47.0″ N)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
         Tropic of Cancer
        </a>
        (23°26′13.0″ N)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
         Equator
        </a>
        (0° latitude)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
         Tropic of Capricorn
        </a>
        (23°26′13.0″ S)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">
         Antarctic Circle
        </a>
        (66°33′47.0″ S)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These circles of latitude, excluding the Equator, mark the divisions between the five principal
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_zone" title="Geographical zone">
        geographical zones
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Equator">
        Equator
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Equator">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The equator is the circle that is equidistant from the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       . It divides the Earth into the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern Hemisphere
       </a>
       . Of the parallels or circles of latitude, it is the longest, and the only '
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       ' (a circle on the surface of the Earth, centered on Earth's center). All the other parallels are smaller and centered only on Earth's axis.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_Circles">
        Polar Circles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Polar Circles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Arctic Circle is the southernmost latitude in the Northern Hemisphere at which the sun can remain continuously above or below the horizon for 24 hours (at the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstices
       </a>
       respectively). Similarly, the Antarctic Circle marks the northernmost latitude in the Southern Hemisphere at which the sun can remain continuously above or below the horizon for 24 hours (at the
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/June_Solstice" title="June Solstice">
        June Solstices
       </a>
       respectively).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tropical_Circles">
        Tropical Circles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tropical Circles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Tropic of Cancer and Tropic of Capricorn mark the northernmost and southernmost latitudes at which the sun may be seen directly overhead (at the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstice
       </a>
       respectively).
      </p>
      <p>
       The latitude of the tropical circles is equal to the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       , about 23°.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Movement_of_the_Tropical_and_Polar_Circles">
        Movement of the Tropical and Polar Circles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Movement of the Tropical and Polar Circles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        Axial tilt
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       By definition, the positions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
        Tropic of Cancer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">
        Arctic Circle
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">
        Antarctic Circle
       </a>
       all depend on the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        tilt of the Earth's axis
       </a>
       relative to the plane of its orbit around the sun (the "obliquity of the ecliptic"). If the Earth were "upright" (its axis at right angles to the orbital plane) there would be no Arctic, Antarctic, or Tropical circles: at the poles the sun would always circle along the horizon, and at the equator the sun would always rise due east, pass directly overhead, and set due west.
      </p>
      <p>
       The positions of the Tropical and Polar Circles are not fixed because the axial tilt changes slowly – a complex motion determined by the superimposition of many different cycles (some of which are described below) with short to very long periods. In 2000 the mean value of the tilt was about 23° 26′ 21″.
      </p>
      <p>
       The main long-term cycle causes the axial tilt to fluctuate between about 22.1° and 24.5° with a period of 41,000 years. Currently, the
       <i>
        average
       </i>
       value of the tilt is decreasing by about 0.47″ per year. As a result, (approximately, and on average) the Tropical Circles are drifting towards the equator (and the Polar Circles towards the poles) by 15 metres per year, and the area of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        Tropics
       </a>
       is decreasing by 1,100 square kilometres (420 sq mi) per year.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Earth's axial tilt has additional shorter-term variations due to
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_nutation" title="Astronomical nutation">
        nutation
       </a>
       , of which the main term, with a period of 18.6 years, has an
       <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">
        amplitude
       </a>
       of 9.2" (corresponding to almost 300 metres north and south).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are many smaller terms, resulting in varying daily shifts of some metres in any direction.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, the Earth's rotational axis is not exactly fixed in the Earth, but undergoes small fluctuations (on the order of 15 meters) called
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">
        polar motion
       </a>
       , which have a small effect on the Tropics and Polar Circles and also on the Equator.
      </p>
      <p>
       Short-term fluctuations over a matter of days do not directly affect the location of the extreme latitudes at which the sun may appear directly overhead, or at which 24-hour day or night is possible, except when they actually occur at the time of the solstices. Rather, they cause a theoretical shifting of the parallels, that would occur if the given axis tilt were maintained throughout the year.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Altitude">
        Altitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Circle_of_latitude&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Altitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:258px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg/256px-Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg/384px-Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg/512px-Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg.png 2x" width="256"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Circle_of_latitude_elevation.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Note that the features of the
         <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
          spheroid
         </a>
         cross-section (orange) in this image are exaggerated with respect to the Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Altitude has an effect on a location's position relative to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">
        plane
       </a>
       formed by a circle of latitude. Since altitude is determined by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">
        normal
       </a>
       to the Earth's surface, locations sharing the same latitude—but having different elevations (e.g., lying along this normal)—no longer lie within this plane. Rather, all points sharing the same latitude and of varying elevation occupy a
       <a href="/wiki/Conical_surface" title="Conical surface">
        cone
       </a>
       formed by the rotation of this normal around the Earth's axis.
      </p>
