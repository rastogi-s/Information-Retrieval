      <p>
       The
       <b>
        langues d'oïl
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'w' in 'wind'">
            w
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [lɑ̃ɡᵊdɔjl]
        </a>
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        oïl languages
       </b>
       (also in French:
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         langues d'oui
        </span>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [lɑ̃ɡᵊdwi]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) are a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       that includes standard
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and its closest
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_language" title="Indigenous language">
        autochthonous
       </a>
       relatives historically spoken in the northern half of
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">
        Channel Islands
       </a>
       . These belong to the larger
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance languages
       </a>
       , which also include the historical languages of east-central France and western Switzerland (
       <a href="/wiki/Arpitania" title="Arpitania">
        Arpitania
       </a>
       ), southern France (
       <a href="/wiki/Occitania" title="Occitania">
        Occitania
       </a>
       ), portions of northern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Aran" title="Val d'Aran">
        Val d'Aran
       </a>
       in Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Linguists divide the Romance languages of France, and especially of Medieval France, into three geographical subgroups: the first two are
       <i>
        Langues d'oïl
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         occitan
        </a>
       </i>
       , both named after their words for 'yes' (
       <i>
        oïl
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        òc
       </i>
       , respectively), and the third is
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       (Arpitan), which is considered transitional.

           citation needed

        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       <i>
        Langue d'oïl
       </i>
       (in the singular),
       <i>
        Oïl dialects
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Oïl languages
       </i>
       (in the plural) designate the ancient northern Gallo-Romance languages as well as their modern-day descendants. They share many linguistic features, a prominent one being the word
       <i>
        oïl
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       . (
       <i>
        Oc
       </i>
       was and still is the southern word for
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       , hence the
       <i>
        langue d'oc
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         Occitan languages
        </a>
       </i>
       ). The most widely spoken modern Oïl language is
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        oïl
       </i>
       was pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o.il]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o.i]
       </span>
       , which has become
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [wi]
        </a>
       </span>
       , in modern French
       <i>
        oui
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

       <i>
        oïl
       </i>

      <h3>
       <span id="Langue_d.27o.C3.AFl">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Langue_d'oïl">
        Langue d'oïl
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Langue d'oïl">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the singular, Langue d'oïl refers to the mutually intelligible linguistic variants of
       <i>
        lingua romana
       </i>
       spoken since the 9th century in northern France and southern Belgium (
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       ), since the 10th century in the Channel Islands, and between the 11th and 14th centuries in
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman language
       </a>
       ).
       <i>
        Langue d'oïl
       </i>
       , the term itself, has been used in the singular since the 12th century to denote this ancient linguistic grouping as a whole. With these qualifiers,
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       sometimes is used to mean the same as
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       (see
       <i>
        History
       </i>
       below).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="O.C3.AFl_dialects">

       </i>
       refer to the

        varieties
       </a>
       of the ancient
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="O.C3.AFl_languages">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Oïl_languages">
        Oïl languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Oïl languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the plural,
       <i>
        Oïl languages
       </i>
       refer to those modern-day descendants that evolved separately from the varieties of the ancient
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       . Consequently,
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       today may apply either: to all the modern-day languages of this family
       <i>
        except
       </i>
       the French language; or to this family
       <i>
        including
       </i>
       French. "
       <i>
        Oïl
       </i>
       dialects" or "French dialects" are also used to refer to the
       <i>
        Oïl languages except French
       </i>
       —as some extant Oïl languages are very close to modern French. Because the term
       <i>
        dialect
       </i>
       is sometimes considered pejorative, the trend today among French linguists is to refer to these languages as
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       rather than
       <i>
        dialects
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Development">
        Development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        For the history of phonology, orthography, syntax and morphology, see
       </i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_French_language" title="History of the French language">
        History of the French language
       </a>
       <i>
        and the relevant individual Oïl language articles.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Each of the Oïl languages has developed in its own way from the common ancestor, and division of the development into periods varies according to the individual histories. Modern linguistics uses the following terms:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        9th–13th centuries
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
           Old French
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Old_Norman" title="Old Norman">
           Old Norman
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          etc
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        French
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">
           Middle French
          </a>
          for the period 14th–15th centuries
         </li>
         <li>
          16th century:
          <i>
           français
           <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
            renaissance
           </a>
          </i>
          (Renaissance French)
         </li>
         <li>
          17th to 18th century:
          <i>
           français classique
          </i>
          (
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_French" title="Classical French">
           Classical French
          </a>
          )
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romana_lingua">
        Romana lingua
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Romana lingua">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the 9th century,
       <i>
        romana lingua
       </i>
       (the term used in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oaths_of_Strasbourg" title="Oaths of Strasbourg">
         Oaths of Strasbourg
        </a>
       </i>
       of 842) was the first of the Romance languages to be recognized by its speakers as a distinct language, probably because it was the most different from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       compared with the other Romance languages (see
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_the_French_language" title="History of the French language">
         History of the French language
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many of the developments that we now consider typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       appeared between the 8th and 12th centuries. Walloon "had a clearly defined identity from the beginning of the thirteenth century". In any case, linguistic texts from the time do not mention the language, even though they mention others in the Oïl family, such as Picard and Lorrain. During the 15th century, scribes in the region called the language "Roman" when they needed to distinguish it. It is not until the beginning of the 16th century that we find the first occurrence of the word "Walloon" in the same linguistic sense that we use it today.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Langue_d.27o.C3.AFl_2">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Langue_d'oïl_2">
        Langue d'oïl
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Langue d'oïl">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       By late- or post-Roman times
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       within France had developed two distinctive terms for signifying assent (
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       ):
       <i>
        hoc ille
       </i>
       ("this (is) it") and
       <i>
        hoc
       </i>
       ("this"), which became
       <i>
        oïl
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oc
       </i>
       , respectively. Subsequent development changed "oïl" into "oui", as in modern French. The term
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       itself was first used in the 12th century, referring to the Old French linguistic grouping noted above. In the 14th century, the Italian poet
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante
       </a>
       mentioned the
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       distinctions in his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_vulgari_eloquentia" title="De vulgari eloquentia">
         De vulgari eloquentia
        </a>
       </i>
       . He wrote in
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">
        Medieval Latin
       </a>
       : "
       <i>
        nam alii oc, alii si, alii vero dicunt oil
       </i>
       " ("some say 'oc', others say 'si', others say 'oïl'")—thereby distinguishing at least three classes of Romance languages:
       <i>
        oc languages
       </i>
       (in southern France);
       <i>
        si languages
       </i>
       (in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberia
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        oïl languages
       </i>
       (in northern France).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other Romance languages derive their word for "yes" from the classical Latin
       <i>
        sic,
       </i>
       "thus", such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       <i>
        sì
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       <i>
        sí
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       <i>
        sim
       </i>
       , and even
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       <i>
        si
       </i>
       (used when contradicting another's negative assertion).
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       is an exception in that its word for "yes",
       <i>
        eja
       </i>
       , is from neither origin.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Similarly
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       uses
       <i>
        da
       </i>
       for "yes", which is of Slavic origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, neither
       <i>
        lingua romana
       </i>
       nor
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       referred, at their respective time, to a single homogeneous language but to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible_languages" title="Mutually intelligible languages">
        mutually intelligible linguistic varieties
       </a>
       . In those times, spoken languages in Western Europe were not codified (except Latin and Medieval Latin), the region's population was considerably lower than today, and population centers were more isolated from each other. As a result, mutually intelligible linguistic varieties were referred to as one language.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="French_.28Old_French.2FStandardized_O.C3.AFl.29_or_lingua_Gallicana">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="French_(Old_French/Standardized_Oïl)_or_lingua_Gallicana">
        French (Old French/Standardized Oïl) or lingua Gallicana
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: French (Old French/Standardized Oïl) or lingua Gallicana">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the 13th century these varieties were recognized and referred to as
       <i>
        dialects
       </i>
       ("idioms") of a single language, the
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       . However, since the previous centuries a common literary and juridical "interdialectary" langue d'oïl had emerged, a kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">
        koiné
       </a>
       . In the late 13th century this common langue d'oïl was named
       <i>
        French
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        françois
       </i>
       in French,
       <i>
        lingua gallica
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        gallicana
       </i>
       in Medieval Latin). Both aspects of
       <i>
        "dialects of a same language"
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        "French as the common langue d'oïl"
       </i>
       appear in a text of
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Bacon" title="Roger Bacon">
        Roger Bacon
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Opus_Majus" title="Opus Majus">
         Opus maius
        </a>
       </i>
       , who wrote in Medieval Latin but translated thus: "
       <i>
        Indeed, idioms of a same language vary amongst people, as it occurs in the French language which varies in an idiomatic manner amongst the French,
        <a href="/wiki/Picardy" title="Picardy">
         Picards
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Normans" title="Normans">
         Normans
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">
         Burgundians
        </a>
        . And terms right to the Picards horrify the Burgundians as much as their closer neighbours the French"
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is from this period though that definitions of individual Oïl languages are first found. The Picard language is first referred to by name as
       <i>
        "langage pikart"
       </i>
       in 1283 in the
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Livre_Roisin&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Livre Roisin (page does not exist)">
         Livre Roisin
        </a>
       </i>
       . The author of the
       <i>
        Vie du bienheureux Thomas Hélye de Biville
       </i>
       refers to the Norman character of his writing. The
       <i>
        Sermons poitevins
       </i>
       of around 1250 show the Poitevin language developing as it straddled the line between oïl and oc.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result, in modern times the term
       <i>
        langue d'oïl
       </i>
       also refers to that
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       which was not as yet named
       <i>
        French
       </i>
       but was already—before the late 13th century—used as a literary and juridical
       <i>
        interdialectary language
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francien" title="Francien">
         Francien
        </a>
       </i>
       is a linguistic
       <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">
        neologism
       </a>
       coined in the 19th century to name the hypothetical variant of Old French allegedly spoken by the
       <i>
        late 14th century
       </i>
       in the ancient province of
       <i>
        Pays de France
       </i>
       —the then
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       region later called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">
         Île-de-France
        </a>
       </i>
       . This
       <i>
        Francien
       </i>
       , it is claimed, became the Medieval French language. Current linguistic thinking mostly discounts the
       <i>
        Francien
       </i>
       theory, although it is still often quoted in popular textbooks. The term
       <i>
        francien
       </i>
       was never used by those people supposed to have spoken the variant; but today the term could be used to designate that specific
       <i>
        10th-and-11th centuries
       </i>
       variant of langue d'oïl spoken in the Paris region; both variants contributed to the koine, as both were called
       <i>
        French
       </i>
       at that time.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Rise_of_French_.28Standardized_O.C3.AFl.29_versus_other_O.C3.AFl_languages">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rise_of_French_(Standardized_Oïl)_versus_other_Oïl_languages">
        Rise of French (Standardized Oïl) versus other Oïl languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Rise of French (Standardized Oïl) versus other Oïl languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       For political reasons it was in Paris and Île-de-France that this koiné developed from a written language into a spoken language. Already in the 12th century
       <a href="/wiki/Conon_de_B%C3%A9thune" title="Conon de Béthune">
        Conon de Béthune
       </a>
       reported about the French court who blamed him for using words of
       <a href="/wiki/Artois" title="Artois">
        Artois
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       By the late 13th century the written koiné had begun to turn into a
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        spoken and written standard language
       </a>
       , and was named
       <i>
        French
       </i>
       . Since then French started to be imposed on the other Oïl dialects as well as on the territories of
       <i>
        langue d'oc
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       However, the Oïl dialects and
       <i>
        langue d'oc
       </i>
       continued contributing to the lexis of French.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 16th century the French language was imposed even more by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">
        Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts
       </a>
       to replace Latin in judgements and official acts and deeds (although the local Oïl languages had always been the language respectively spoken in justice courts). It is argued that the Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts was not intended to make French a national language, merely a chancery language for law and administration. Although there were competing literary standards among the Oïl languages in the mediaeval period, the centralisation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">
        French kingdom
       </a>
       and its influence even outside its formal borders sent most of the Oïl languages into comparative obscurity for several centuries. The development of
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       in this new language encouraged writers to use French rather than their own
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional languages
       </a>
       . This led to the decline of
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_literature" title="Vernacular literature">
        vernacular literature
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
        French Revolution
       </a>
       which imposed French on the people as the official language in all the territory. As the influence of French (and in the Channel Islands, English) spread among sectors of provincial populations, cultural movements arose to study and standardise the vernacular languages. From the 18th century and into the 20th century, societies were founded (such as the "Société liégoise de Littérature wallonne" in 1856), dictionaries (such as
       <a href="/wiki/George_M%C3%A9tivier" title="George Métivier">
        George Métivier
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Dictionnaire franco-normand
       </i>
       of 1870) were published, groups were formed and literary movements developed to support and promote the Oïl languages faced with competition. Until the
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        First World War
       </a>
       , the regional
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        languages of France
       </a>
       were still the languages most used in the home and in the fields.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This was also generally the case in areas where Oïl languages were spoken. French is now the best-known of the Oïl languages.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1608" data-file-width="999" height="354" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg/220px-Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg/330px-Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg/440px-Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bram_Bilo_1890.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Oïl languages have literary traditions, as for example seen in this 19th-century collection of Jèrriais short stories
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Besides the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/French_literature" title="French literature">
        French literature
       </a>
       , small-scale literature has survived in the other Oïl languages. Theatrical writing is most notable in
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       (which maintains a genre of vernacular
       <a href="/wiki/Marionette" title="Marionette">
        marionette
       </a>
       theatre),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poitevin_(language)" title="Poitevin (language)">
        Poitevin
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saintongeais" title="Saintongeais">
        Saintongeais
       </a>
       . Oral performance (story-telling) is a feature of
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">
        Gallo
       </a>
       , for example, while
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       literature, especially from the early 19th century tend to focus on written texts and poetry (see, for example,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Wace" title="Wace">
         Wace
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais_literature" title="Jèrriais literature">
         Jèrriais literature
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       As the vernacular Oïl languages were displaced from towns, they have generally survived to a greater extent in rural areas - hence a preponderance of literature relating to rural and peasant themes. The particular circumstances of the self-governing Channel Islands developed a lively strain of political comment, and the early industrialisation in Picardy led to survival of Picard in the mines and workshops of the regions. The mining poets of Picardy may be compared with the tradition of rhyming
       <a href="/wiki/Weaver_Poets" title="Weaver Poets">
        Weaver Poets
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Ulster_Scots_dialects" title="Ulster Scots dialects">
        Ulster Scots
       </a>
       in a comparable industrial milieu.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are some regional magazines, such as
       <i>
        Ch'lanchron
       </i>
       (Picard),
       <i>
        Le Viquet
       </i>
       (Norman),
       <i>
        Les Nouvelles Chroniques du Don Balleine
       </i>
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.societe-jersiaise.org/langsec/chroniques.html" rel="nofollow">
        [1]
       </a>
       (Jèrriais), and
       <i>
        El Bourdon
       </i>
       (Walloon), which are published either wholly in the respective Oïl language or bilingually with French. These provide a platform for literary writing.
      </p>

      <p>
       Apart from French, an official language in many countries (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_countries_where_French_is_an_official_language" title="List of countries where French is an official language">
        list
       </a>
       ), the Oïl languages have enjoyed little status in recent times.
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently Walloon,
       <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
        Lorrain
       </a>
       (under the local name of
       <i>
        Gaumais
       </i>
       ), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champenois" title="Champenois">
        Champenois
       </a>
       have the status of regional languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Norman languages of the Channel Islands enjoy a certain status under the governments of their
       <a href="/wiki/Bailiwick" title="Bailiwick">
        Bailiwicks
       </a>
       and within the regional and lesser-used language framework of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British-Irish_Council" title="British-Irish Council">
        British-Irish Council
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman language
       </a>
       , a variant of Norman once the official language of England, today holds mostly a place of ceremonial honor in the United Kingdom (now referred to as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Law_French" title="Law French">
         Law French
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The French government recognises the Oïl languages as
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        languages of France
       </a>
       , but the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutional_Council_of_France" title="Constitutional Council of France">
        Constitutional Council of France
       </a>
       barred ratification of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">
        European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
        Influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       were more or less influenced by the native languages of the conquering
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_tribes" title="Germanic tribes">
        Germanic tribes
       </a>
       , notably the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       . This was apparent not so much in the vocabulary (which remained overwhelmingly of Latin origin) as in the phonology and syntax; the invading Franks, Burgundians and Normans became the rulers and their accents were imposed as standard on the rest of the population. This accounts in large part for the relative distinctiveness of French compared to other Romance languages.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Signal%C3%A9tique_gallo,_m%C3%A9tro_de_Rennes.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="549" data-file-width="279" height="433" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Signal%C3%A9tique_gallo%2C_m%C3%A9tro_de_Rennes.jpg/220px-Signal%C3%A9tique_gallo%2C_m%C3%A9tro_de_Rennes.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/62/Signal%C3%A9tique_gallo%2C_m%C3%A9tro_de_Rennes.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Signal%C3%A9tique_gallo,_m%C3%A9tro_de_Rennes.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Signage in
         <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">
          Gallo
         </a>
         in the metro of
         <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">
          Rennes
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       was heavily influenced by contact with Norman following the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norman_Conquest" title="Norman Conquest">
        Norman Conquest
       </a>
       and much of the adopted vocabulary shows typically Norman features.
      </p>
      <p>
       Portuguese was heavily influenced by more than a millennium of perennial contact with several dialects of both Oïl and
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       language groups, in lexicon (up to 15–20% in some estimates, at least 5000 word roots), phonology and orthography.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The influence of Occitan was, nevertheless, the most marked, through the status Provençal in particular achieved in southwestern Europe around the
       <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">
        troubadour
       </a>
       apex in the Middle Ages, when
       <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese_lyric" title="Galician-Portuguese lyric">
        Galician-Portuguese lyric
       </a>
       was developed. Aside the direct influence of Provençal literature, the presence of languages from modern-day France in the Galician-Portuguese area was also strong due to the rule of the
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_House_of_Burgundy" title="Portuguese House of Burgundy">
        House of Burgundy
       </a>
       , the establishment of the Orders of
       <a href="/wiki/Cluniac_Reforms" title="Cluniac Reforms">
        Cluny
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cistercians" title="Cistercians">
        Cister
       </a>
       , the many
       <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago_(route_descriptions)" title="Camino de Santiago (route descriptions)">
        sections
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago" title="Camino de Santiago">
        Way of St. James
       </a>
       pilgrimage route that come from elsewhere in Europe out of the Iberian Peninsula, and the settlement in Iberia of people from the other side of the Pyrenees, arriving during and after the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lusophobia" title="Lusophobia">
        anti-Portuguese factor
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_nationalism" title="Brazilian nationalism">
        Brazilian nationalism
       </a>
       in the 19th century led to an increased use of the French language in detriment of Portuguese, as France was seen at the time as a model of civilization and progress.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The learning of French has historically been important and strong among the Lusophone elites, and for a great span of time it was also the foreign language of choice among the middle class of both Portugal and Brazil, only surpassed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
        globalised
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Postmodernity" title="Postmodernity">
        postmodernity
       </a>
       by English, in both, and more recently by
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , in the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        French spoken in Belgium
       </a>
       shows some influence from
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The development of French in
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       was influenced by the speech of settlers originating from northwestern France, many of whom introduced features of their Oïl varieties into the French they spoke. (See also
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_the_United_States" title="French language in the United States">
         French language in the United States
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_Canada" title="French language in Canada">
         French language in Canada
        </a>
       </i>
       )
      </p>
