      <p>
       <b>
        Earth radius
       </b>
       is the approximate
       <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">
        distance
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's center to its surface, about 6,371 km (3,959 mi). This distance is used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_of_length" title="Unit of length">
        unit of length
       </a>
       , especially in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
        geophysics
       </a>
       , where it is usually denoted by
       <i>
        R
       </i>
       <sub>
        ⊕
       </sub>
       . Strictly speaking, the term "radius" is a property of a true sphere. Since
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       is only approximately
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        spherical
       </a>
       , no single value serves as its definitive
       <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">
        radius
       </a>
       . Meaningful values range from 6,353 to 6,384 kilometres (3,948 to 3,967 mi).
      </p>
      <p>
       A distance from the center of Earth to some point on its surface might be referred to as Earth’s radius at that point. More commonly, Earth radius means a computed
       <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">
        average
       </a>
       of distances to the surface or to some idealized surface. The idealized surface is usually mean
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea levels
       </a>
       globally or else an
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       that approximates sea level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If elevations were to be included, average radius would increase by about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7002230000000000000♠
        </span>
        230 m
       </span>
       over sea level, or less than one part in 25,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chambat_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-chambat-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first published reference to the Earth's size appeared around 350 BC, when
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       reported in his book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/On_the_Heavens" title="On the Heavens">
         On the Heavens
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that mathematicians had guessed the circumference of the Earth to be 400,000
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadia_(length)" title="Stadia (length)">
        stadia
       </a>
       . Scholars have interpreted Aristotle's figure to be anywhere from highly accurate
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to almost double the true value.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first known scientific measurement and calculation of the radius of the Earth was performed by
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       in about 240 BC. Estimates of the accuracy of Eratosthenes’s measurement range from 0.5% to 17%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For both Aristotle and Eratosthenes, uncertainty in the accuracy of their estimates is due to modern uncertainty over which stadion length they meant.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">
        Introduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg/300px-Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg/450px-Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f9/Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg/600px-Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth_oblateness_to_scale.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Scale drawing of the oblateness of the 2003 IERS reference ellipsoid. The outer edge of the dark blue line is an ellipse with the same eccentricity as that of the Earth, with north at the top. For comparison, the outer edge of light blue area is a circle of diameter equal to the minor axis. The red line denotes the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karman_line" title="Karman line">
          Karman line
         </a>
         and the yellow area, the range of the
         <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
          International Space Station
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Figure_of_Earth" title="Figure of Earth">
        Figure of Earth
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's rotation
       </a>
       , internal density variations, and external
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       cause its shape to deviate systematically from a perfect sphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Local
       <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
        topography
       </a>
       increases the variance, resulting in a surface of profound complexity. Our descriptions of the Earth's surface must be simpler than reality in order to be tractable. Hence, we create models to approximate characteristics of the Earth's surface, generally relying on the simplest model that suits the need.
      </p>
      <p>
       Each of the models in common use involve some notion of the geometric
       <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">
        radius
       </a>
       . Strictly speaking, spheres are the only solids to have radii, but broader uses of the term "radius" are common in many fields, including those dealing with models of the Earth. Here is a partial list of models of Earth's surface, ordered from exact to more approximate:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The actual surface of Earth;
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
         geoid
        </a>
        , defined by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
         mean sea level
        </a>
        at each point on the real surface;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [c]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        An ellipsoid,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_system#Geodetic_versus_geocentric_latitude" title="Geodetic system">
         geocentric
        </a>
        to model the entire Earth, or else
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_system#Geodetic_versus_geocentric_latitude" title="Geodetic system">
         geodetic
        </a>
        for regional work;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [d]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        A sphere.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the case of the geoid and ellipsoids, the fixed distance from any point on the model to the specified center is called
       <i>
        "a radius of the Earth"
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        "the radius of the Earth at that point"
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [e]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also common to refer to any
       <i>
        <a href="#Mean_radii">
         mean radius
        </a>
       </i>
       of a spherical model as
       <i>
        "the radius of the earth"
       </i>
       . When considering the Earth's real surface, on the other hand, it is uncommon to refer to a "radius", since there is generally no practical need. Rather, elevation above or below sea level is useful.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regardless of the model, any radius falls between the polar minimum of about 6,357 km and the equatorial maximum of about 6,378 km (3,950 to 3,963 mi). Hence, the Earth deviates from a perfect sphere by only a third of a percent, which supports the spherical model in many contexts and justifies the term "radius of the Earth". While specific values differ, the concepts in this article generalize to any major
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Physics_of_Earth.27s_deformation">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics_of_Earth's_deformation">
        Physics of Earth's deformation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physics of Earth's deformation">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Rotation of a planet causes it to approximate an
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
         oblate ellipsoid
        </a>
        /spheroid
       </i>
       with a bulge at the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       and flattening at the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Poles
       </a>
       , so that the
       <i>
        equatorial radius
       </i>
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        a
       </span>
       is larger than the
       <i>
        polar radius
       </i>
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        b
       </span>
       by approximately
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        aq
       </span>
       . The
       <i>
        oblateness constant
       </i>
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        q
       </span>
       is given by
      </p>

       <dd>

         [f]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the Earth

         [8]
        </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crustal
       </a>
       thickness causes gravity to vary across the surface and in time, so that the mean sea level differs from the ellipsoid. This difference is the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
         geoid
        </a>
        height
       </i>
       , positive above or outside the ellipsoid, negative below or inside. The geoid height variation is under 110 m (360 ft) on Earth. The geoid height can change abruptly due to earthquakes (such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake">
        Sumatra-Andaman earthquake
       </a>
       ) or reduction in ice masses (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Not all deformations originate within the Earth. The gravity of the Moon and Sun cause the Earth's surface at a given point to undulate by tenths of meters over a nearly 12-hour period (see
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_tide" title="Earth tide">
        Earth tide
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radius_and_local_conditions">
        Radius and local conditions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Radius and local conditions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1000" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg/220px-Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg/330px-Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg/440px-Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Al-Biruni#Geodesy_and_geography" title="Al-Biruni">
          Al-Biruni
         </a>
         's (973–1048) method for calculation of the Earth's radius improved accuracy.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Given local and transient influences on surface height, the values defined below are based on a "general purpose" model, refined as globally precisely as possible within 5 m (16 ft) of reference ellipsoid height, and to within 100 m (330 ft) of mean sea level (neglecting geoid height).
      </p>
      <p>
       Additionally, the radius can be estimated from the curvature of the Earth at a point. Like a
       <a href="/wiki/Torus" title="Torus">
        torus
       </a>
       , the curvature at a point will be greatest (tightest) in one direction (north–south on Earth) and smallest (flattest) perpendicularly (east–west). The corresponding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radius_of_curvature_(applications)" title="Radius of curvature (applications)">
        radius of curvature
       </a>
       depends on the location and direction of measurement from that point. A consequence is that a distance to the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        true horizon
       </a>
       at the equator is slightly shorter in the north/south direction than in the east-west direction.
      </p>
      <p>
       In summary, local variations in terrain prevent defining a single "precise" radius. One can only adopt an idealized model. Since the estimate by
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       , many models have been created. Historically, these models were based on regional topography, giving the best
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth#Historical_Earth_ellipsoids" title="Figure of the Earth">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       for the area under survey. As satellite remote sensing and especially the
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        Global Positioning System
       </a>
       gained importance, true global models were developed which, while not as accurate for regional work, best approximate the Earth as a whole.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fixed_radius">
        Fixed radius
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fixed radius">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The following radii are fixed and do not include a variable location dependence. They are derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">
        World Geodetic System
       </a>
       1984 (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS-84" title="WGS-84">
        WGS-84
       </a>
       ) standard ellipsoid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tr8350_2_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tr8350_2-16">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The value for the equatorial radius is defined to the nearest 0.1 m in WGS-84. The value for the polar radius in this section has been rounded to the nearest 0.1 m, which is expected to be adequate for most uses. Refer to the WGS-84 ellipsoid if a more precise value for its polar radius is needed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The radii in this section are for an idealized surface. Even the idealized radii have an uncertainty of ±2 m.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discrepancy between the ellipsoid radius and the radius to a physical location may be significant. When identifying the position of an observable location, the use of more precise values for WGS-84 radii may not yield a corresponding improvement in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accuracy" title="Accuracy">
        accuracy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The symbol given for the named radius is used in the formulae found in this article.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Equatorial_radius">
        Equatorial radius
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Equatorial radius">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Earth's equatorial radius
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        a
       </span>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       , is the distance from its center to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       and equals 6,378.1370 km (3,963.1906 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The equatorial radius is often used to compare Earth with other
       <a href="/wiki/Planet#Attributes" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Polar_radius">
        Polar radius
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Polar radius">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Earth's polar radius
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        b
       </span>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-minor_axis" title="Semi-minor axis">
        semi-minor axis
       </a>
       , is the distance from its center to the North and South Poles, and equals 6,356.7523 km (3,949.9028 mi).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location-dependent_radii">
        Location-dependent radii
        <span id="Radii_with_location_dependence">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Location-dependent radii">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Average_distance_from_center_to_surface">
        Average distance from center to surface
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Average distance from center to surface">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most global mean radii are based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       , which approximates the geoid. The geoid has no direct relationship with surface topography, however. An alternative calculation averages elevations everywhere, resulting in a mean radius
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7002230000000000000♠
        </span>
        230 m
       </span>
       larger than the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius#Mean_radius" title="Earth radius">
        IUGG mean radius
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius#Authalic_radius" title="Earth radius">
        authalic radius
       </a>
       , or the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius#Volumetric_radius" title="Earth radius">
        volumetric radius
       </a>
       . This average is 6,371.230 km (3,958.899 mi) with uncertainty of 10 m (33 ft).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chambat_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-chambat-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Osculating_sphere">
        Osculating sphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_radius&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Osculating sphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The best spherical approximation to the ellipsoid in the vicinity of a given point is given by the
       <a href="/wiki/Osculating_circle" title="Osculating circle">
        osculating
       </a>
       sphere. Its radius equals the Gaussian radius of curvature as above, the center of the sphere is located at the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_curvature" title="Center of curvature">
        center of curvature
       </a>
       of the ellipsoid, and its radial direction coincides with the ellipsoid
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_direction" title="Normal direction">
        normal direction
       </a>
       . This concept aids the interpretation of terrestrial and planetary
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_occultation" title="Radio occultation">
        radio occultation
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refraction
       </a>
       measurements.
      </p>
