      <p>
       <b>
        ED50
       </b>
       ("
       <b>
        European Datum 1950
       </b>
       ") is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        geodetic datum
       </a>
       which was defined after
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       for the international connection of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_network" title="Geodetic network">
        geodetic networks
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some of the important battles of World War II were fought on the borders of
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Netherlands
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , and the mapping of these countries had incompatible
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       positioning. This led to the setting up of ED50 as a consistent mapping datum for much of
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">
        Western Europe
       </a>
       . It was, and still is, used in much of Western Europe apart from
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ireland" title="Ireland">
        Ireland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
        Sweden
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       , which have their own datums.
      </p>
      <p>
       It used the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Ellipsoid" title="International Ellipsoid">
        International Ellipsoid
       </a>
       of 1924 ("
       <a href="/wiki/John_Fillmore_Hayford" title="John Fillmore Hayford">
        Hayford
       </a>
       -Ellipsoid" of 1909) (
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        radius
       </a>
       of the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       6378.388 km,
       <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">
        flattening
       </a>
       1/297, both exact). That spheroid was an early attempt to model the whole Earth and was widely used around the world until the 1980s when
       <a href="/wiki/GRS_80" title="GRS 80">
        GRS 80
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS_84" title="WGS 84">
        WGS 84
       </a>
       were established.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many national coordinate systems of
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Kr%C3%BCger_coordinate_system" title="Gauss–Krüger coordinate system">
        Gauss–Krüger
       </a>
       are defined by ED50 and oriented by means of
       <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_astronomy" title="Geodetic astronomy">
        geodetic astronomy
       </a>
       . Up to now it has been used in
       <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">
        databases
       </a>
       of gravity fields,
       <a href="/wiki/Cadastre" title="Cadastre">
        cadastre
       </a>
       , small
       <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
        surveying
       </a>
       networks in Europe and America, and by some developing countries with no modern baselines. ED50 was also part of the fundamentals of the
       <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">
        NATO
       </a>
       coordinates (
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Kr%C3%BCger_coordinate_system" title="Gauss–Krüger coordinate system">
        Gauss–Krüger
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator_coordinate_system" title="Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system">
        UTM
       </a>
       ) up to the 1980s.
      </p>
      <p>
       The geodetic datum of
       <b>
        ED50
       </b>
       was centred at the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Helmertturm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Helmertturm (page does not exist)">
        Helmertturm
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Telegrafenberg" title="Telegrafenberg">
        Telegrafenberg
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Potsdam" title="Potsdam">
        Potsdam
       </a>
       , (then East)
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       ; the intent was to encourage cooperation with the socialist states during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cold_war" title="Cold war">
        cold war
       </a>
       , which failed. The adjustments for later versions of the datum (
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ED77&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ED77 (page does not exist)">
        ED77
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=ED79&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="ED79 (page does not exist)">
        ED79
       </a>
       ) used the
       <a href="/wiki/Munich_Frauenkirche" title="Munich Frauenkirche">
        Munich Frauenkirche
       </a>
       as starting point.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Datum_shift_between_ED50_and_WGS_84">
        Datum shift between ED50 and WGS 84
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ED50&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Datum shift between ED50 and WGS 84">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The longitude and latitude lines on the two datums are the same in the Archangel region of north-west
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       . As one moves westwards across Europe, the longitude lines on ED50 gradually become further west than their WGS 84 equivalents, and are around 100 metres west in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       . Moving southwards, the latitude lines on ED50 gradually become further south than the WGS 84 lines, and are around 100 m south in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This means that if the lines are further west, the longitude value of any given point becomes more easterly. Similarly, if the lines are south, the values become northerly. This is due to the fact that the International Ellipsoid is larger than the GRS 80/WGS 84 ellipsoid, so a given north-south distance will cover more latitude on the new ellipsoid, and a given east-west distance will cover more longitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       The datum shift for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator" title="Universal Transverse Mercator">
        Universal Transverse Mercator
       </a>
       grid is different. The eastings vary between 0 m in Eastern Europe and 100 m in the far west of the continent, roughly similar to the longitude shift, but the northings are about 200 m different across Europe, with the ED50 UTM northing lines south of the WGS 84 UTM northing lines. UTM northings are measured from the Equator; distance from the equator to latitude 50 degrees is 112 m more on the International than on the WGS 84 spheroid.
      </p>
