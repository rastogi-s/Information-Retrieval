      <p>
       <b>
        Venezuelan Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        castellano venezolano
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        español venezolano
       </i>
       ) refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       as spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish was introduced in Venezuela by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonizer" title="Colonizer">
        colonizers
       </a>
       . Most of them were from
       <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
        Galicia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(historical_territory)" title="Basque Country (historical territory)">
        Basque Country
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Perhaps the last of these has been the most fundamental influence on modern Venezuelan Spanish, to the point that
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian
       </a>
       and Venezuelan accents may seem indistinguishable to other Spanish speakers.
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       immigrants from the late 19th and early 20th century have also had an influence. German settlers also left an influence when Venezuela was contracted as a concession by the King of Spain to the German Welser banking family (
       <a href="/wiki/Klein-Venedig" title="Klein-Venedig">
        Klein-Venedig
       </a>
       , 1528–1546).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spaniards additionally brought
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        African
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Slavery" title="Slavery">
        slaves
       </a>
       . This is the origin of expressions such as
       <i>
        chévere
       </i>
       ("excellent"), which comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">
        Yoruba
       </a>
       <i>
        ché egberi
       </i>
       . Other non-Romance words came from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous people of the Americas">
        Native
       </a>
       languages, such as
       <i>
        guayoyo
       </i>
       (a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Coffee" title="Coffee">
        coffee
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        caraota
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Black_turtle_bean" title="Black turtle bean">
        black bean
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Venezuelan "snob" (or "sifrino" in colloquial Venezuelan Spanish) accent is often thought of as the "pretty-boy/pretty-girl" or "boy band" accent of Spanish. However the majority of Venezuelan Spanish widely ranges from its occasional formal form, to the more common, everyday form full of slang, to the heavily "thug" or "thuggish" ("malandro" in Venezuelan Spanish) inflected manner, often found in the slums or "barrios" of the country.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Venezuelan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Venezuelan Spanish often shortens words, such as
        <i>
         para
        </i>
        ("for") to
        <i>
         pa
        </i>
        . In addition,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        between vowels is often dropped (
        <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
         elision
        </a>
        ):
        <i>
         helado
        </i>
        ("ice cream") becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [eˈlao]
        </span>
        . Originally from southern Spain and the Canary Islands, those traits are common to many other Spanish variations and in the Caribbean.
       </li>
       <li>
        Another common feature is the debuccalization of syllable-final /s/, whereby
        <i>
         adiós
        </i>
        ("goodbye") becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈðjoʰ]
        </span>
        . It is common to most coastal areas in America, the Canary Islands, and the southern half of Spain.
       </li>
       <li>
        Syllable-final /n/-velarisation, or /n/-assimilation:
        <i>
         ambientación
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aNbi̯eNtaˈsi̯oN/
        </span>
        ("atmosphere") becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ãmbjẽn̪t̪aˈsjõŋ]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ãⁿbjẽⁿt̪aˈsjõⁿ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Also like most other American versions of Spanish, Venezuelan Spanish has
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísmo
        </a>
        (a merger of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
          ʎ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
          ʝ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ), and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        (traditional
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        ). That is,
        <i>
         calló
        </i>
        ("s/he became silent") and
        <i>
         cayó
        </i>
        ("s/he fell") are homophones, and
        <i>
         casa
        </i>
        ("house") is homophonous with
        <i>
         caza
        </i>
        ("hunt").
        <i>
         Seseo
        </i>
        is common to all of America, the Canary Islands, and southern Spain, and
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        is prevalent in most Spanish variations.
       </li>
       <li>
        The phoneme /x/ is realized as glottal [h] in the
        <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Caribbean" title="Venezuelan Caribbean">
         Caribbean coast of Venezuela
        </a>
        like the pronunciation of El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Colombia, Spanish Caribbean islands, Canary Islands, and southern Spain, sometimes in Ecuador, Peru, Chile, and Argentina.
       </li>
       <li>
        A characteristic common to Spanish in Venezuela, Colombia, the Dominican Republic, Cuba and Costa Rica is the use of the
        <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">
         diminutive
        </a>
        <i>
         -ico
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         -ica
        </i>
        instead of the standard
        <i>
         -ito
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         -ita
        </i>
        , restricted to words with
        <i>
         -t
        </i>
        in the last syllable:
        <i>
         rata
        </i>
        ("rat") becomes
        <i>
         ratica
        </i>
        ("little rat"). Other diminutive of notice is "manito" instead of the more common "manita".
       </li>
       <li>
        The second-person singular informal pronoun is usually
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        , as in most of Latin America and also in Spain. This practice is referred to as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuteo" title="Tuteo">
         tuteo
        </a>
        . However, in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zulia_(state)" title="Zulia (state)">
         Zulia
        </a>
        and some parts of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falc%C3%B3n_(state)" title="Falcón (state)">
         Falcón
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Trujillo_(state)" title="Trujillo (state)">
         Trujillo
        </a>
        , it is common to find
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
        , the use of
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        . This phenomenon is present in many other Latin American variations (notably
        <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Spanish" title="Central American Spanish">
         Central American Spanish
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
         Rioplatense
        </a>
        ), but Zulian voseo is
        <i>
         diptongado
        </i>
        : conjugation preserves the
        <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
         diphthongs
        </a>
        of the historical
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        conjugation that have been
        <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">
         monophthongized
        </a>
        in Rioplatense (so the Zulian forms are the same as those used in Spanish from Spain for the second person plural
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        ): instead of
        <i>
         tú eres, tú estás
        </i>
        , Zulian says
        <i>
         vos sois, vos estáis
        </i>
        (compare with plural forms in Spanish from Spain
        <i>
         vosotros sois
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vosotros estáis
        </i>
        ; and with Rioplatense forms
        <i>
         vos sos
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vos estás
        </i>
        ). Another exception to the tuteo of Venezuelan Spanish is the use of the second-person singular formal pronoun
        <i>
         Usted
        </i>
        interchangeably with
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        in informal speech, a practice unique to the states of
        <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida_(state)" title="Mérida (state)">
         Mérida
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%A1chira_(state)" title="Táchira (state)">
         Táchira
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Trujillo_(state)" title="Trujillo (state)">
         Trujillo
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        As in all of the Spanish-speaking Americas, the only plural form of the second person is
        <i>
         ustedes
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The word
        <i>
         vaina
        </i>
        is used with a variety of meanings (such as "shame", "thing or topic", "pity", and many others) and often as an interjection or a nonsensical filler.
       </li>
       <li>
        Venezuelan Spanish has a lot of
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_borrowed_from_Italian" title="List of Spanish words borrowed from Italian">
         Italianisms
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Gallicism" title="Gallicism">
         Gallicisms
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Germanism_(linguistics)#Spanish" title="Germanism (linguistics)">
         Germanisms
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Anglicism" title="Anglicism">
         Anglicisms
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variations">
        Regional variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Venezuelan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regional variations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are several subdialects within Venezuelan Spanish.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Caracas" title="Caracas">
         Caracas
        </a>
        dialect, spoken in the capital, Caracas, is used by the media and is considered to be the standard Spanish of Venezuela, with its variants generally related to the social classes there. It is used mostly in the capital and in other areas of the country such as Maracay and Valencia.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Zulia" title="Zulia">
         Zulian
        </a>
        dialect in the northwest of the country, also called
        <i>
         maracucho
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         marabino
        </i>
        , which uses
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lara,_Venezuela" title="Lara, Venezuela">
         Lara
        </a>
        dialect, with the older Spanish verbal inflections
        <i>
         -ades
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -edes
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -odes
        </i>
        gave
        <i>
         -ás
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -és
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         -ós"
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
          <a href="#cite_note-2">
           [2]
          </a>
         </sup>
         ("vos cantáis", "vos coméis", "vos sois").
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
         Andean
        </a>
        dialect, in particular the state of Táchira near the Colombian border. It is characterized by a nonaspirated pronunciation of 's' and use of
        <i>
         Usted
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        , even within informal contexts. Another variant, in the states of Mérida and Trujillo, still uses
        <i>
         Usted
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        but uses the aspirated pronunciation of the s. /s/ is pronounced as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_retracted_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant">
         voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s̺]
        </span>
        , also called apico-alveolar or grave, which is transitional between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ; the phonetic trait (unique in the Americas) is from a large number of northern Spanish settlers in Andean Venezuela.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Margaritan dialect (oriental), spoken in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Margarita" title="Isla Margarita">
         Isla Margarita
        </a>
        and the northeast of mainland Venezuela. The Margaritan dialect sometimes has an interdental for pre-vowel 's' and uses a strong 'r' instead of 'l' for most words.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <li>
        <b>
         Vacilar
        </b>
        = v. To enjoy something/have a good time. Example: "Estoy vacilando" = I am having fun. Also used as a noun: "Vacile," as in "qué malvacile" = What a bad time. Lit. Vacillate
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vaina
        </b>
        = adj. or n. Thing, annoyance, problem, predicament, situation, endeavor, liaison.
        <i>
         Vaina
        </i>
        is one of the most versatile Venezuelan words, not necessarily having a negative connotation (mildly profane). Lit. Pod, sheath.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Verga
        </b>
        = n. Dick (profane). In the Western part of the country, especially in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zulia_State" title="Zulia State">
         Zulia state
        </a>
        , it is a nonsensical filler as an alternative to
        <i>
         vaina
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Verga
        </b>
        = exp. Used to convey a feeling of shock, disgust or alert (profane).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         ¡Vergación!
        </b>
        = exp. superlative form of Verga (profane).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vergatario(a)
        </b>
        = adj. Something excellent, or someone who has done something very well.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vete al Carajo
        </b>
        = exp. (or
        <b>
         Vete al coño de tu madre
        </b>
        ) "Fuck You" or "Go fuck Yourself". Lit. "Go to the
        <a href="/wiki/Crow%27s_nest" title="Crow's nest">
         crow's nest
        </a>
        " / "Go to your mother's cunt" (profane).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         ¡Vete al coñísimo de tu madre!
        </b>
        = exp. Superlative form of the term above (very profane).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Violín
        </b>
        = n. Bad odor in armpits. See
        <i>
         Tufo
        </i>
        . Lit.
        <a href="/wiki/Violin" title="Violin">
         Violin
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Yesquero
        </b>
        = n. A
        <a href="/wiki/Lighter" title="Lighter">
         lighter
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Yeyo
        </b>
        = n. Low blood pressure, dizziness or faint generally caused by a strong impression or stress.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zampar
        </b>
        = v. To French kiss somebody.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zanahoria
        </b>
        = n. Someone who zealously takes care of his/her own health. A vegetarian. A person that behaves well, nerd. Straight, clean. adj. A boring, dull person. Lit. Carrot.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zancudo
        </b>
        = n.
        <a href="/wiki/Mosquito" title="Mosquito">
         Mosquito
        </a>
        . Lit. "The one that walks on stilts" as a metaphor for the insect's long legs.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zapatero
        </b>
        = exp. To lose in a game with zero points. Lit. Shoemaker.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zapatos de goma
        </b>
        = n. Sneakers. Lit. Rubber soled shoes.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zapatos de patente
        </b>
        = n. Patent-leather shoes.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zumbado(a)
        </b>
        = adj. (or
        <b>
         Zumba'o/Zumbá
        </b>
        ) Forward, crazy, nutty, careless person.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Zamuro
        </b>
        = n. Stalker person
       </li>
      </ul>
