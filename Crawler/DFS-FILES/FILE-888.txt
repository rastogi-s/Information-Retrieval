      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_among_languages">
        Variation among languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variation among languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In spite of their common origin, the descendants of Vulgar Latin have many differences. These occur at all levels, including the sound systems, the orthography, the nominal, verbal, and adjectival inflections, the auxiliary verbs and the semantics of verbal tenses, the function words, the rules for subordinate clauses, and, especially, in their vocabularies. While most of those differences are clearly due to independent development after the breakup of the Roman Empire (including invasions and cultural exchanges), one must also consider the influence of prior languages in territories of Latin Europe that fell under Roman rule, and possible
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heterogeneity" title="Heterogeneity">
        heterogeneity
       </a>
       in Vulgar Latin itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Romanian, together with other related languages, like
       <a href="/wiki/Aromanian_language" title="Aromanian language">
        Aromanian
       </a>
       , has a number of grammatical features which are unique within Romance, but are shared with other non-Romance languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">
        Balkans
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">
        Macedonian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       . These include, for example, the structure of the vestigial case system, the placement of articles as suffixes of the nouns (
       <i>
        <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cer#Romanian" title="wikt:cer">
          cer
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       = "sky",
       <i>
        <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cerul#Romanian" title="wikt:cerul">
          cerul
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       = "the sky"), and several more. This phenomenon, called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_language_area" title="Balkan language area">
        Balkan language area
       </a>
       , may be due to contacts between those languages in post-Roman times.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_plurals">
        Formation of plurals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Formation of plurals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_plurals" title="Romance plurals">
        Romance plurals
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/La_Spezia%E2%80%93Rimini_Line" title="La Spezia–Rimini Line">
        La Spezia–Rimini Line
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some Romance languages form plurals by adding
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (derived from the plural of the Latin accusative case), while others form the plural by changing the final vowel (by influence of Latin nominative plural endings, such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       ) from some masculine nouns.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Plural in
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        : Portuguese, Galician, Spanish, Catalan, Occitan, Sardinian, Friulian, Romansh.
       </li>
       <li>
        Vowel change: Italian, Romanian.
       </li>
       <li>
        Special case of French: Falls into the first group historically (and orthographically), but the final
        <i>
         -s
        </i>
        is no longer pronounced (except in

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_number_16">
        The number 16
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The number 16">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Romanian constructs the names of the numbers 11–19 by a regular Slavic-influenced pattern that could be translated as "one-over-ten", "two-over-ten", etc. All the other Romance languages use a pattern like "one-ten", "two-ten", etc. for 11–15, and the pattern "ten-and-seven, "ten-and-eight", "ten-and-nine" for 17–19. For 16, however, they split into two groups: some use "six-ten", some use "ten-and-six":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "Sixteen": Italian
        <i>
         sedici
        </i>
        , Catalan and Occitan
        <i>
         setze
        </i>
        , French
        <i>
         seize
        </i>
        , Venetian
        <i>
         sédexe
        </i>
        , Romansh
        <i>
         sedesch
        </i>
        , Friulian
        <i>
         sedis
        </i>
        , Lombard
        <i>
         sedas / sedes
        </i>
        , Franco-Provençal
        <i>
         sèze
        </i>
        , Sardinian
        <i>
         sèighi
        </i>
        , Piedmontese
        <i>
         sëddes
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         sëddes
        </i>
        is borrowed from Lombard and replaced the original
        <i>
         sëzze
        </i>
        since the 18th century).
       </li>
       <li>
        "Ten and six": Portuguese
        <i>
         dezasseis
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         dezesseis
        </i>
        , Galician
        <i>
         dezaseis
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         decem ac sex
        </i>
        ), Spanish
        <i>
         dieciséis
        </i>
        (romance construction:
        <i>
         diez y seis
        </i>
        ), the Marchigiano dialect
        <i>
         digissei
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        "Six over ten": Romanian
        <i>
         șaisprezece
        </i>
        (where
        <i>
         spre
        </i>
        derives from
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         super
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Classical Latin uses the "one-ten" pattern for 11–17 (
       <i>
        ūndecim
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        duodecim
       </i>
       , ...,
       <i>
        septendecim
       </i>
       ), but then switches to "two-off-twenty" (
       <i>
        duodēvigintī
       </i>
       ) and "one-off-twenty" (
       <i>
        ūndēvigintī
       </i>
       ). For the sake of comparison, note that English and German use two special words derived from "one left over" and "two left over" for 11 and 12, then the pattern "three-ten", "four-ten", ..., "nine-ten" for 13–19.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="To_have_and_to_hold">
        To have and to hold
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: To have and to hold">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The verbs derived from Latin
       <i>
        habēre
       </i>
       "to have",
       <i>
        tenēre
       </i>
       "to hold", and
       <i>
        esse
       </i>
       "to be" are used differently in the various Romance languages, to express possession, to construct perfect tenses, and to make
       <a href="/wiki/Existential_clause" title="Existential clause">
        existential statements
       </a>
       ("there is"). If we use
       <b>
        T
       </b>
       for
       <i>
        tenēre
       </i>
       ,
       <b>
        H
       </b>
       for
       <i>
        habēre
       </i>
       , and
       <b>
        E
       </b>
       for
       <i>
        esse
       </i>
       , we have the following distribution:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         HHE
        </b>
        : Romanian, Italian, Gallo-Italic languages.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         HHH
        </b>
        : Occitan, French, Romansh, Sardinian.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         THH
        </b>
        : Spanish, Catalan, Aragonese.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         TTH
        </b>
        : European Portuguese.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         T-T
        </b>
        : Brazilian Portuguese. (colloquial)
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="To_have_or_to_be">
        To have or to be
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: To have or to be">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some languages use their equivalent of 'have' as an
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verb
       </a>
       to form the compound forms (e. g. French
       <i>
        passé composé
       </i>
       ) of all verbs; others use 'be' for some verbs and 'have' for others.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        'have' only: Standard Catalan, Spanish, Romanian, Sicilian.
       </li>
       <li>
        'have' and 'be': Occitan, French, Sardinian, Italian, Northern-Italian languages (Piedmontese, Lombard, Ligurian, Venetian, Friulan), Romansh, Central Italian languages (Tuscan, Umbrian, Corsican) some Catalan dialects (although such usage is recessing in those).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the latter type, the verbs which use 'be' as an auxiliary are
       <a href="/wiki/Unaccusative_verb" title="Unaccusative verb">
        unaccusative verbs
       </a>
       , that is, intransitive verbs that often show motion not directly initiated by the subject or changes of state, such as 'fall', 'come', 'become'. All other verbs (intransitive
       <a href="/wiki/Unergative_verb" title="Unergative verb">
        unergative verbs
       </a>
       and all transitive verbs) use 'have'. For example, in French,
       <i>
        J'ai vu
       </i>
       or Italian
       <i>
        ho visto
       </i>
       'I have seen' vs.
       <i>
        Je suis tombé
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sono caduto
       </i>
       'I have (lit. am) fallen'. Note, however, the difference between French and Italian in the choice of auxiliary for the verb 'be' itself: Fr.
       <i>
        J'ai été
       </i>
       'I have been' with 'have', but Italian
       <i>
        sono stato
       </i>
       with 'be'. In Southern Italian languages the principles governing auxiliaries can be quite complex, including even differences in persons of the subject. A similar distinction exists in the Germanic languages, which share a
       <a href="/wiki/Sprachbund" title="Sprachbund">
        language area
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       ; German and the Scandinavian languages use 'have' and 'be', while modern English now uses 'have' only (although 'be' remains in certain relic phrases:
       <i>
        Christ is risen
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Joy to the world: the Lord is come
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       "Be" is also used for reflexive forms of the verbs, as in French
       <i>
        j'ai lavé
       </i>
       'I washed [something]', but
       <i>
        je me suis lavé
       </i>
       'I washed myself', Italian
       <i>
        ho lavato
       </i>
       'I washed [something]' vs.
       <i>
        mi sono lavato
       </i>
       'I washed myself'.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tuscan uses
       <i>
        si
       </i>
       forms identical to the 3rd person reflexive in a usage interpreted as 'we' subject, triggering 'be' as auxiliary in compound constructions, with the subject pronoun
       <i>
        noi
       </i>
       'we' optional. If the verb employed is one that otherwise selects 'have' as auxiliary, the past participle is unmarked:
       <i>
        si è lavorato
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        abbiamo lavorato
       </i>
       'we (have) worked'. If the verb is one that otherwise selects 'be', the past participle is marked plural:
       <i>
        si è arrivati
       </i>
       =
       <i>
        siamo arrivati
       </i>
       'we (have) arrived'.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Difficulties_of_classification">
        Difficulties of classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Difficulties of classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comparative_method" title="Comparative method">
        comparative method
       </a>
       used by linguists to build family language trees is based on the assumption that the member languages evolved from a single
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-language" title="Proto-language">
        proto-language
       </a>
       by a sequence of binary splits, separated by many centuries. With that hypothesis, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Glottochronology" title="Glottochronology">
        glottochronological
       </a>
       assumption that the degree of linguistic change is roughly proportional to elapsed time, the sequence of splits can be deduced by measuring the differences between the members.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, the history of Romance languages, as we know it, makes the first assumption rather problematic. While the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       lasted, its educational policies and the natural mobility of its soldiers and administrative officials probably ensured some degree of linguistic homogeneity throughout its territory. Even if there were differences between the
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       spoken in different regions, it is doubtful whether there were any sharp boundaries between the various dialects. On the other hand, after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">
        Empire's collapse
       </a>
       , the population of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       speakers was separated—almost instantaneously, by the standards of
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical linguistics
       </a>
       —into a large number of politically independent states and feudal domains whose populations were largely bound to the land. These units then interacted, merged and split in various ways over the next fifteen centuries, possibly influenced by languages external to the family (as in the so-called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balkan_language_area" title="Balkan language area">
        Balkan language area
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       To sum it up, the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Latin" title="History of Latin">
        history of Latin
       </a>
       and Romance-speaking peoples can hardly be described by a binary branching pattern; therefore, one may argue that any attempt to fit the Romance languages into a tree structure is inherently flawed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this regard, the genealogical structure of languages forms a typical
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Linkage_(linguistics)" title="Linkage (linguistics)">
         linkage
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, the tree structure may be meaningfully applied to any subfamilies of Romance whose members did diverge from a common ancestor by binary splits. That may be the case, for example, of the dialects of Spanish and Portuguese spoken in different countries, or the regional variants of spoken
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        standard Italian
       </a>
       (but not the so-called "
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        Italian dialects
       </a>
       ", which are distinct languages evolved directly from Vulgar Latin).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_standard_proposal">
        The standard proposal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The standard proposal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Nevertheless, by applying the comparative method, some linguists have concluded that the earliest split in the Romance family tree was between
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       and the remaining group, called Continental Romance. Among the many peculiar Sardinian distinguishing features are its articles (derived from Latin
       <i>
        IPSE
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        ILLE
       </i>
       ) and retention of the "hard" sounds of "c" and "g" before "e" and "i". This view is challenged in part by the existence of definite articles continuing
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        ipse
       </span>
       forms (e.g.
       <i>
        sa mar
       </i>
       'the sea') in some varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       , best known as typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_dialect" title="Balearic dialect">
        Balearic dialects
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       According to this view, the next split was between
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       in the east, and the other languages (the
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Western_languages" title="Italo-Western languages">
        Italo-Western languages
       </a>
       ) in the west. One of the characteristic features of Romanian is its retention of three of Latin's seven noun cases. The third major split was more evenly divided, between the Italian branch, which comprises many languages spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">
        Italian Peninsula
       </a>
       , and the Gallo-Iberian branch.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Another_proposal">
        Another proposal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Another proposal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       However, this is not the only view. Another common classification begins by splitting the Romance languages into two main branches, East and West. The East group includes Romanian, the languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       , and all
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        languages of Italy
       </a>
       south of a line through the cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Rimini" title="Rimini">
        Rimini
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/La_Spezia" title="La Spezia">
        La Spezia
       </a>
       (see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Spezia%E2%80%93Rimini_Line" title="La Spezia–Rimini Line">
         La Spezia–Rimini Line
        </a>
       </i>
       ). Languages in this group are said to be more conservative, i.e. they retained more features of the original Latin.
      </p>
      <p>
       The latter then split into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance" title="Gallo-Romance">
        Gallo-Romance
       </a>
       group, which became the
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        Oïl languages
       </a>
       (including French),
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italian
       </a>
       , Occitan,
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">
        Romansh
       </a>
       , and an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_Languages" title="Iberian Romance Languages">
        Iberian Romance
       </a>
       group which became Spanish and Portuguese.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_wave_hypothesis">
        The wave hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The wave hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Linguists like Jean-Pierre Chambon claim that the various regional languages did not evolve in isolation from their neighbours; on the contrary, they see many changes propagating from the more central regions (Italy and France) towards the periphery (Iberian Peninsula and Romania).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These authors see the Romance family as a
       <a href="/wiki/Linkage_(linguistics)" title="Linkage (linguistics)">
        linkage
       </a>
       rather than a tree-like family, and insist that the
       <a href="/wiki/Wave_model" title="Wave model">
        Wave model
       </a>
       is better suited than the
       <a href="/wiki/Tree_model" title="Tree model">
        Tree model
       </a>
       for representing the history of Romance.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Degree_of_separation_from_Latin">
        Degree of separation from Latin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Classification_of_Romance_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Degree of separation from Latin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a study by linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Mario_Pei" title="Mario Pei">
        Mario Pei
       </a>
       (1949), the degrees of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_modernisation" title="Language modernisation">
        evolution
       </a>
       of the Romance languages with respect to the ancestral Latin were found to be as follows
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">

By assigning 3½ change points per vowel (with 2 points for diphthongization, 1 point for modification in vowel quantity, ½ point for changes due to nasalization, palatalization or umlaut, and −½ point for failure to effect a normal change), there is a maximum of 77 change points for free and checked stressed vowel sounds (11×2×3½=77). According to this system (illustrated by seven charts at the end of the article), the percentage of change is greatest in French (44%) and least in Italian (12%) and Sardinian (8%). A statistical method attempting to evaluate the evidence quantitatively was developed in order to provide not only a classification, but at the same time a measure of the divergence among the languages. The earliest attempt was made in 1949 by Mario Pei (1901–1978), who measured the divergence of seven modern Romance languages from Classical Latin, taking as his criterion the evolution of stressed vowels. Pei's results do not show the degree of contemporary divergence among the languages from each other but only the divergence of each one from Classical Latin. The closest language turned out to be Sardinian with 8% of change. Then followed Italian — 12%; Spanish — 20%; Romanian — 23,5%; Provençal — 25%; Portuguese — 31%; French — 44%.”
