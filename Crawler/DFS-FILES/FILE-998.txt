       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Illyrian_languages" title="Illyrian languages">

       <a href="/wiki/Dalmatae" title="Dalmatae">

        official language

       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">
        Republic of Ragusa
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Herzegovinian_dialect" title="Eastern Herzegovinian dialect">
        Eastern Herzegovinian dialect
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       (for the Slavophone population), came to supersede it.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">
        Dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Almost every city developed its own dialect. Most of these became extinct before they were recorded, so the only trace of these ancient dialects is some words borrowed into local dialects of today's Croatia.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ragusan_dialect">
        Ragusan dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ragusan dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dubrovacka_republika.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="428" data-file-width="768" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dubrovacka_republika.png/250px-Dubrovacka_republika.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dubrovacka_republika.png/375px-Dubrovacka_republika.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/Dubrovacka_republika.png/500px-Dubrovacka_republika.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dubrovacka_republika.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Republic of Ragusa before 1808
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Ragusan
       </b>
       is the Southern dialect, whose name is derived from the Romance name of Dubrovnik,
       <i>
        Ragusa
       </i>
       . It came to the attention of modern scholars in two letters, from 1325 and 1397, and other mediaeval texts, which show a language influenced heavily by
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       . The available sources include some 260 Ragusan words including
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         pen
        </span>
       </i>
       'bread',
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         teta
        </span>
       </i>
       'father',
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         chesa
        </span>
       </i>
       'house', and
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         fachir
        </span>
       </i>
       'to do', which were quoted by the Dalmatian Filippo Diversi, the rector of Ragusa in the 1430s.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Maritime_republics" title="Maritime republics">
        Maritime Republic
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ragusa" title="Republic of Ragusa">
        Ragusa
       </a>
       had, at one time, an important fleet, but its influence decreased over time, to the point that, by the 15th century, it had been reduced to only about 300 ships.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The language was threatened by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">
        Slav
       </a>
       expansion, as the Ragusan Senate decided that all debates had to be held in
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         lingua veteri ragusea
        </span>
       </i>
       (ancient Ragusan language) and the use of the
       <i>
        Slav
       </i>
       was forbidden. Nevertheless, during the 16th century, Ragusan fell out of use and came to the brink of extinction.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vegliot_dialect">
        Vegliot dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vegliot dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        Vegliot
       </b>
       (the native name being
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         Viklasun
        </span>
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is the Northern dialect. The language's name is derived from the Italian name of
       <a href="/wiki/Krk" title="Krk">
        Krk
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Veglia
        </span>
       </i>
       , an island in
       <a href="/wiki/Kvarner_Gulf" title="Kvarner Gulf">
        Kvarner
       </a>
       , called
       <i>
        Vikla
       </i>
       in Vegliot. On the inscription dating from the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/4th_century" title="4th century">
        4th century
       </a>
       CE, Krk is named as
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Splendissima civitas Curictarum
        </span>
       </i>
       . The Serbo-Croatian name derives from the Roman name (
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Curicum, Curicta
        </span>
       </i>
       ), whereas the younger name
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         Vecla, Vegla, Veglia
        </span>
       </i>
       (meaning
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "Old Town"
       </span>
       ) was created in the mediaeval Romanesque period.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="857" data-file-width="1094" height="274" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg/350px-Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg/525px-Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg/700px-Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Dalmatian_language_map_bgiu.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Areas of Dalmatian dialects.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">
        Roman Republic
       </a>
       gradually came to occupy the territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Illyria" title="Illyria">
        Illyria
       </a>
       between 229 and 155 BC. Merchants and authorities settling from Rome brought with them the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       language, and eventually the indigenous inhabitants mostly abandoned their languages (prevalently varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Illyrian_languages" title="Illyrian languages">
        Illyrian
       </a>
       ) for
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . After the Roman capital moved to Constantinople, Greek began to replace Latin as the
       <i>
        Lingua Franca
       </i>
       in the empire (eventually becoming official in 620), but Illyrian towns continued to speak Latin (see
       <a href="/wiki/Illyro-Roman" title="Illyro-Roman">
        Illyro-Roman
       </a>
       ), which evolved over time into regional dialects and eventually into distinct Romance languages.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dalmatian was spoken on the Dalmatian coast from Fiume (now
       <a href="/wiki/Rijeka" title="Rijeka">
        Rijeka
       </a>
       ) as far south as Cottora (
       <a href="/wiki/Kotor" title="Kotor">
        Kotor
       </a>
       ) in Montenegro. Speakers lived mainly in the coastal towns of Jadera (
       <a href="/wiki/Zadar" title="Zadar">
        Zadar
       </a>
       ), Tragurium (
       <a href="/wiki/Trogir" title="Trogir">
        Trogir
       </a>
       ), Spalatum
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Split,_Croatia" title="Split, Croatia">
        Split
       </a>
       ), Ragusa (
       <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">
        Dubrovnik
       </a>
       ) and Acruvium (
       <a href="/wiki/Kotor" title="Kotor">
        Kotor
       </a>
       ), and also on the islands of Curicta (
       <a href="/wiki/Krk" title="Krk">
        Krk
       </a>
       ), Crepsa (
       <a href="/wiki/Cres" title="Cres">
        Cres
       </a>
       ) and Arba (
       <a href="/wiki/Rab" title="Rab">
        Rab
       </a>
       ). Almost every city developed its own dialect, but the most important dialects we know of were
       <i>
        Vegliot
       </i>
       , a northern dialect spoken on the island of Curicta, and
       <i>
        Ragusan
       </i>
       , a southern dialect spoken in and around Ragusa (
       <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">
        Dubrovnik
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Dalmatian dialect of Ragusa is known from two letters, dated 1325 and 1397, as well as from other mediaeval texts. The oldest preserved documents written in Dalmatian are 13th century inventories in Ragusan. The available sources include roughly 260 Ragusan words. Surviving words include
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         pen
        </span>
       </i>
       'bread',
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         teta
        </span>
       </i>
       'father',
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         chesa
        </span>
       </i>
       'house', and
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         fachir
        </span>
       </i>
       'to do', which were quoted by the Dalmatian,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Filippo_Diversi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Filippo Diversi (page does not exist)">
        Filippo Diversi
       </a>
       , Rector of the republic of Ragusa in the 1430s. The earliest reference to the Dalmatian language dates from the tenth century and it has been estimated that about 50,000 people spoke it at that time, though the main source of this information, the Italian linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Bartoli" title="Matteo Bartoli">
        Matteo Bartoli
       </a>
       , may have exaggerated his figures.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dalmatian was influenced particularly heavily by
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       (despite the latter, the Latin roots of Dalmatian remained prominent). A 14th-century letter from Zadar (origin of the Iadera dialect) shows strong influence from Venetian, the language that after years under Venetian rule superseded Iadera and other dialects of Dalmatian. Other dialects met their demise with the settlement of populations of Slavic speakers.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction">
        Extinction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extinction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TuoneUdaina.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="575" data-file-width="579" height="218" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TuoneUdaina.jpg/220px-TuoneUdaina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TuoneUdaina.jpg/330px-TuoneUdaina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/TuoneUdaina.jpg/440px-TuoneUdaina.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TuoneUdaina.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Tuone_Udaina" title="Tuone Udaina">
          Tuone Udaina
         </a>
         , the last speaker of Dalmatian
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1897, the scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Matteo_Bartoli" title="Matteo Bartoli">
        Matteo Bartoli
       </a>
       , himself a native of nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istria
       </a>
       , visited
       <i>
        Burbur
       </i>
       ('barber' in Dalmatian
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Tuone Udaina article says it means grumpy (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/Tuone_Udaina" title="Tuone Udaina">
        Tuone Udaina
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Antonio Udina
        </span>
       </i>
       ), the last speaker of any Dalmatian dialect, to study his language, writing down approximately 2,800 words, stories, and accounts of his life, which were published in a book that has provided much information on the vocabulary, phonology, and grammar of the language. Bartoli wrote in Italian and published a translation in German (
       <i>
        <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
         Das Dalmatische
        </span>
       </i>
       ) in 1906. The Italian language manuscripts were reportedly lost, and the work was not re-translated into Italian until 2001.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Just one year later, on 10 June 1898,
       <a href="/wiki/Tuone_Udaina" title="Tuone Udaina">
        Tuone Udaina
       </a>
       was accidentally killed at 77 in a roadwork explosion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roegiest_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roegiest-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brahms_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brahms-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most recent classification from in 2017 it was classified by the "Max Planck Institute for the Science of Human History" with the
       <a href="/wiki/Istriot_language" title="Istriot language">
        Istriot Language
       </a>
       in the Dalmatian Romance subgroup.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Once it was thought to be a language that bridged the gap between the
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , it was only distantly related to the nearby Romanian dialects, such as the nearly extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Istro-Romanian_language" title="Istro-Romanian language">
        Istro-Romanian
       </a>
       , spoken in nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">
        Croatia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of its features are quite archaic. Dalmatian is the only Romance language that has palatalised /k/ and /g/ before /i/, but not before /e/ (others have palatalised them in both situations, except
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       , which has not palatalised them at all):
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         civitate
        </span>
       </i>
       &gt; Vegliot:
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         cituot
        </span>
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "city"
       </span>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         cenare
        </span>
       </i>
       &gt; Vegliot:
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         kenur
        </span>
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "to dine"
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some Dalmatian words have been preserved as borrowings in South Slavic languages, mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/Chakavian" title="Chakavian">
        Chakavian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dalmatian_grammar" title="Dalmatian grammar">
        Dalmatian grammar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       An
       <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">
        analytic
       </a>
       trend can be observed in Dalmatian: nouns and adjectives began to lose their gender and number inflexions, the noun declension disappeared completely, and the verb conjugations began to follow the same path, but the verb maintained a person and number distinction except in the third person (in common with Romanian and several dialects of Italy).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       precedes the noun, unlike in the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Romance_languages" title="Eastern Romance languages">
        Eastern Romance languages
       </a>
       like Romanian, which have it postposed to the noun.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Dalmatian kept Latin words related to urban life, lost (or if preserved, not with the original sense) in Romanian, such as
       <i>
        <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
         cituot
        </span>
       </i>
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "city"
       </span>
       (in old Romanian
       <i>
        <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
         cetate
        </span>
       </i>
       means
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "city"
       </span>
       ; in modern Romanian
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "fort"
       </span>
       ; compare also Albanian
       <i>
        <span lang="sq" xml:lang="sq">
         qytet
        </span>
       </i>
       , borrowed from Latin, which, too, means
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        "city"
       </span>
       ). The Dalmatians retained an active urban society in their city-states, whereas most Romanians were driven into small mountain settlements during the
       <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
        Great Migrations
       </a>
       of 400 to 800 AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       became a major influence on the language as Venetian commercial influence grew. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chakavian_dialect" title="Chakavian dialect">
        Chakavian dialect
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dubrovnik" title="Dubrovnik">
        Dubrovnik
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shtokavian_dialect" title="Shtokavian dialect">
        Shtokavian dialect
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbo-croatian" title="Serbo-croatian">
        Serbo-croatian
       </a>
       , which were spoken outside the cities since the immigration of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">
        Slavs
       </a>
       , gained importance in the cities by the 16th century, and it eventually replaced Dalmatian as the day-to-day language. Nevertheless, some words were loaned into coastal Serbo-Croatian varieties:

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Parable_of_the_Prodigal_Son">
        Parable of the Prodigal Son
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dalmatian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Parable of the Prodigal Son">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a class="mw-selflink selflink">
         Dalmatian
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         <span lang="dlm" xml:lang="dlm">
          E el daic: Jon ciairt jomno ci avaja doi feil, e el plé pedlo de louro daic a soa tuota: Tuota duoteme la puarte de moi luc, che me toca, e jul spartait tra louro la sostuanza e dapù pauch dai, mais toich indajoi el feil ple pedlo andait a la luorga, e luoc el dissipuat toich el soo, viviand malamiant. Muà el ju venait in se stiass, daic: quinci jomni de journata Cn cuassa da me tuota i ju bonduanza de puan e cua ju muor de fum.
         </span>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        :
        <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
         And He said: There was a man who had two sons, and the younger of them said to his father: "Father give me the share of his property that will belong to me." So he divided the property between them. A few days later the younger son gathered all he had and travelled to a distant country, and there he squandered his property in dissolute living. But when he came to himself he said: "How many of my father's hired hands have bread enough and to spare, but here I am dying of hunger."
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
