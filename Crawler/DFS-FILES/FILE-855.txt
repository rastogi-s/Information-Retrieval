      <p>
       <b>
        Belizean Spanish
       </b>
       (Spanish:
       <i>
        español beliceño
       </i>
       ) is the dialect of Spanish spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Belize" title="Belize">
        Belize
       </a>
       . It is similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian Spanish
       </a>
       . While English is the only official language of Belize, Spanish is the common language of majority (62.8%), wherein 174,000 (43% of Belizeans) speak some variety of Spanish as a native language. Belizean Spanish is spoken by Belizean-born mestizos and Belizean-born citizens of pure Spanish blood. Belizeans of Guatemalan, Honduran, Mexican (including Mexican Mennonites), Nicaraguan, and Salvadoran (including Salvadoran Mennonites) descent may speak different dialects of Spanish, but since they all grow up in Belize, they all adopt the local accent.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Belizean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        As in all of the Americas and parts of Spain, there is no distinction of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        , they are pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        As in most American lowland varieties of Spanish and in southern Spain,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        at the end of a syllable or before a consonant is realized typically as a glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , as in several American lowland varieties and in parts of Spain.
       </li>
       <li>
        Intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b, d/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /g/
        </span>
        show no sign of
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         lenition
        </a>
        to approximants. This is unusual among Spanish dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no confusion between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , unlike in Caribbean Spanish.
       </li>
       <li>
        As Belize is bordered by Mexico and was inhabited by Mayan and Nahuatl peoples, Belizean Spanish adopted the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
         voiceless alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡s]
        </span>
        and the cluster
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tl]
        </span>
        (originally
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tɬ/
        </span>
        ) represented by the respective digraphs &lt;tz&gt; and &lt;tl&gt; in loanwords of Nahuatl origin,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Quetzal" title="Quetzal">
          quetzal
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tlapalería
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡ɬapaleˈɾia]
        </span>
        ('hardware store'). Even words of Greek and Latin origin with &lt;tl&gt;, such as
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27Atl%C3%A1ntico%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''Atlántico''">
           <i>
            Atlántico
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27atleta%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''atleta''">
           <i>
            atleta
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        , are pronounced with the affricate:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        (compare
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        , and [l], syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        can be realized as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
          ɹ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , an influence of
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         British English
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         "verso"'
        </i>
        (verse) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbeɹso]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbeɾso]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈberso]
        </span>
        , or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbelso]
        </span>
        , "
        <i>
         invierno
        </i>
        " (winter) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjeɹno]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjeɾno]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjerno]
        </span>
        , or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjelno]
        </span>
        , and "
        <i>
         parlamento
        </i>
        " (parliament) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [paɹlaˈmento]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [paɾlaˈmento]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [parlaˈmento]
        </span>
        , or [
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         palaˈmento
        </span>
        ]. In word-final position,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        will usually be;
        <ul>
         <li>
          either a trill, a tap, approximant,
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [l]
          </span>
          , or elided when followed by a consonant or a pause, as in
          <i>
           amo
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [r ~ ɾ ~ ɹ ~ l]
          </span>
          <i>
           paterno
          </i>
          'paternal love',
          <i>
           amor
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [aˈmo]
          </span>
          ) (elided word-final /r/ is almost similar to British English),
         </li>
         <li>
          a tap, approximant, or
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [l]
          </span>
          when the followed by a vowel-initial word, as in
          <i>
           amo
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ɾ~ ɹ ~ l]
          </span>
          <i>
           eterno
          </i>
          'eternal love').
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
