         Subapical retroflex plosive
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        retroflex consonant
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal consonant
       </a>
       where the tongue has a flat, concave, or even curled shape, and is articulated between the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
        alveolar ridge
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">
        hard palate
       </a>
       . They are sometimes referred to as
       <b>
        cerebral
       </b>
       consonants, especially in
       <a href="/wiki/Indology" title="Indology">
        Indology
       </a>
       . Other terms occasionally encountered are
       <b>
        apico-domal
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        cacuminal
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       -derived word
       <i>
        retroflex
       </i>
       means "bent back"; some retroflex consonants are pronounced with the tongue fully curled back so that articulation involves the underside of the tongue tip (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subapical" title="Subapical">
        subapical
       </a>
       ). These sounds are sometimes described as "true" retroflex consonants. However, retroflexes are commonly taken to include other consonants having a similar
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       without such extreme curling of the tongue; these may be articulated with the tongue tip (
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       ) or the tongue blade (
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminal
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retroflex_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Retroflex consonants, like other
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal consonants
       </a>
       , come in several varieties, depending on the shape of the tongue. The tongue may be either flat or concave, or even with the tip curled back. The point of contact on the tongue may be with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongue_tip" title="Tongue tip">
        tip
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       ), with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tongue_blade" title="Tongue blade">
        blade
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminal
       </a>
       ), or with the underside of the tongue (
       <a href="/wiki/Subapical_consonant" title="Subapical consonant">
        subapical
       </a>
       ). The point of contact on the roof of the mouth may be with the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
        alveolar ridge
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       ), the area behind the alveolar ridge (
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       ), or the
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_palate" title="Hard palate">
        hard palate
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       ). Finally, both
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricate
       </a>
       ) and nonsibilant (
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_consonant" title="Rhotic consonant">
        rhotic
       </a>
       ) consonants can have a retroflex articulation.
      </p>
      <p>
       The greatest variety of combinations occurs with sibilants, because for these, small changes in tongue shape and position cause significant changes in the resulting sound. Retroflex sounds in general have a duller, lower-pitched sound than other alveolar or postalveolar consonants, and especially the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grooved_consonant" title="Grooved consonant">
        grooved
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       sibilants. The farther back the point of contact with the roof of the mouth, the more concave is the shape of the tongue, and the duller (lower pitched) is the sound, with subapical consonants being the most extreme.
      </p>
      <p>
       The main combinations normally observed are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Laminal post-alveolar, with a flat tongue. These occur, for example, in
        <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
         Polish
        </a>
        <i>
         cz, sz, ż (rz), dż
        </i>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
         Mandarin
        </a>
        <i>
         zh, ch, sh, r
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Apical post-alveolar, with a somewhat concave tongue. These occur, for example, in
        <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
         Hindi
        </a>
        and other
        <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">
         Indo-Aryan languages
        </a>
        . (Hindi has no retroflex sibilants, although some of the other Indo-Aryan languages do.)
       </li>
       <li>
        Subapical palatal, with a highly concave tongue. These occur particularly in the
        <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
         Dravidian languages
        </a>
        . These are the dullest and lowest-pitched type, and when following a vowel often add strong
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_vowel" title="Rhotic vowel">
         <i>
          r
         </i>
         -coloring
        </a>
        to the vowel, sounding as if an
        <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
         American English
        </a>
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        occurs between the vowel and consonant. These are not a place of articulation, as the IPA chart implies, but a shape of the tongue analogous to laminal and apical.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The subapical sounds are sometimes called "true retroflex" because of the curled-back shape of the tongue, while the other sounds sometimes go by other names. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Ladefoged
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">
        Maddieson
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       prefer to call the laminal post-alveolar sounds "flat post-alveolar".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_sounds">
        Other sounds
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retroflex_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Other sounds">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Retroflex sounds need to be distinguished from other consonants made in the same parts of the mouth:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Palato-alveolar_consonant" title="Palato-alveolar consonant">
         palato-alveolar consonants
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ ʒ]
        </span>
        ), such as the
        <i>
         sh
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         zh
        </i>
        occurring in
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        words like
        <i>
         ship
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         chip
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         vision
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">
         alveolo-palatal consonants
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɕ ʑ]
        </span>
        ), such as the
        <i>
         q, j
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        occurring in
        <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
         Mandarin Chinese
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_consonant" title="Dorsal consonant">
         dorsal
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal consonants
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ç ʝ ɲ]
        </span>
        ), such as the
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ç]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        <i>
         ich
        </i>
        or the
        <i>
         ñ
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɲ]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        <i>
         año
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Sulcalization" title="Sulcalization">
         grooved
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
         alveolar consonants
        </a>
        (e.g.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s z]
        </span>
        ), such as the
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         z
        </i>
        occurring in
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        words like
        <i>
         sip
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         zip
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The first three types of sounds above have a convex tongue shape, which gives them an additional
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_articulation" title="Secondary articulation">
        secondary articulation
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       . The last type has a groove running down the center line of the tongue, which gives it a strongly hissing quality. The retroflex sounds, however, have a flat or concave shape, with no associated palatalization, and no groove running down the tongue. The term "retroflex", in fact, literally means "bent back" (concave), although consonants with a flat tongue shape are commonly considered retroflex as well.
      </p>

        <td>
         summer
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          ǃ˞ (‼)
         </span>
        </th>
        <td>
         <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_clicks" title="Retroflex clicks">
          retroflex click release
         </a>
         (many distinct consonants)
        </td>
        <td>
         <a href="/wiki/Ekoka_!Kung" title="Ekoka !Kung">
          Ekoka !Kung
         </a>
        </td>
        <td>
         —
        </td>
        <td>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          [
          <b>
           ɡǃ˞
          </b>
          ú]
         </span>
        </td>
        <td>
         water
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Some linguists restrict these symbols for consonants with subapical palatal articulation, in which the tongue is curled back and contacts the hard palate, and use the alveolar symbols with the
       <a href="/wiki/Obsolete_and_nonstandard_symbols_in_the_International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Obsolete and nonstandard symbols in the International Phonetic Alphabet">
        obsolete IPA underdot symbol
       </a>
       for an apical post-alveolar articulation: ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ṭ, ḍ, ṇ, ṣ, ẓ, ḷ, ɾ̣, ɹ̣
       </span>
       ⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       An option more consistent with the IPA
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="Why? The r-colored diacritic is typically used for vowels. (February 2015)">
           further explanation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       is to use the
       <a href="/wiki/R-colored_vowel" title="R-colored vowel">
        r-colored vowel
       </a>
       the apical retroflexes: ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        t˞, d˞, n˞, s˞, z˞, l˞, ɾ˞, ɹ˞
       </span>
       ⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       Laminal retroflexes, as in Polish and Russian, are often transcribed with a retraction diacritic, as ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̠
       </span>
       ⟩. Otherwise they are typically but inaccurately transcribed as if they were palato-alveolar, as ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *ʃ
       </span>
       ⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       Consonants with more forward articulation, in which the tongue touches the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        Alveolar
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       region rather than the hard palate, can be indicated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_articulation#Advanced_and_retracted" title="Relative articulation">
        retracted diacritic
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Plus_and_minus_signs#Other_uses" title="Plus and minus signs">
        minus sign below
       </a>
       ). This occurs especially for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̱ ẕ]
       </span>
       ; other sounds indicated this way, such as ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ṉ ḻ ḏ
       </span>
       ⟩, tend to refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolo-palatal_consonant" title="Alveolo-palatal consonant">
        alveolo-palatal
       </a>
       rather than retroflex consonants.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
        Occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retroflex_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although data is not precise, about 20 percent of the world's languages contain retroflex consonants of one sort or another.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       About half of these possess only retroflex
       <a href="/wiki/Continuant" title="Continuant">
        continuants
       </a>
       , with most of the rest having both stops and continuants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Retroflex consonants are concentrated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       , particularly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Aryan_languages" title="Indo-Aryan languages">
        Indo-Aryan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       , but are found in other languages of the region as well, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Munda_languages" title="Munda languages">
        Munda languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Burushaski" title="Burushaski">
        Burushaski
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Nuristani_languages" title="Nuristani languages">
        Nuristani languages
       </a>
       of eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Afghanistan" title="Afghanistan">
        Afghanistan
       </a>
       also have retroflex consonants. Among
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Iranian_languages" title="Eastern Iranian languages">
        Eastern Iranian languages
       </a>
       , they are common in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
        Pashto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wakhi_language" title="Wakhi language">
        Wakhi
       </a>
       , Sanglechi-
       <a href="/wiki/Ishkashimi_language" title="Ishkashimi language">
        Ishkashimi
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Munji_language" title="Munji language">
        Munji
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Yidgha_language" title="Yidgha language">
        Yidgha
       </a>
       . They also occur in some other Asian languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">
        Javanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The other major concentration is in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        indigenous languages of Australia
       </a>
       and the Western Pacific (notably
       <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">
        New Caledonia
       </a>
       ). Here, most languages have retroflex plosives,
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximants
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Retroflex consonants are relatively rare among
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Europe" title="Languages of Europe">
        European languages
       </a>
       but are used in such languages as
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
        Northern Europe
       </a>
       , some
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Europe" title="Southern Europe">
        Southern Europe
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian
       </a>
       , some Italian dialects such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calabrian_languages" title="Calabrian languages">
        Calabrian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salentino" title="Salentino">
        Salentino
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunigiana" title="Lunigiana">
        Lunigianese
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , and some
       <a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">
        Asturian
       </a>
       dialects), and (sibilants only)
       <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
        Faroese
       </a>
       and several
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sorbian_languages" title="Sorbian languages">
        Sorbian
       </a>
       ). In Swedish and Norwegian, a sequence of
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       plus a coronal consonant may be replaced by the coronal's retroflex equivalent, e.g. the name
       <i>
        Martin
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Swedish" title="Help:IPA/Swedish">
         [ˈmaʈːɪn]
        </a>
       </span>
       (Swedish) or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Norwegian" title="Help:IPA/Norwegian">
         [ˈmɑʈːin]
        </a>
       </span>
       (Norwegian), and
       <i>
        nord
       </i>
       ("north") is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [nuːɖ]
       </span>
       . This is sometimes done for several consonants in a row after an
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       —
       <a href="/wiki/Hornstull" title="Hornstull">
        Hornstull
       </a>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [huːɳʂˈʈɵlː]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_approximant" title="Retroflex approximant">
        retroflex approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɻ/
       </span>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophone
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
        alveolar approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɹ/
       </span>
       in many dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       , particularly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">
        Midwestern
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . Polish and Russian possess retroflex
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilants
       </a>
       , but no stops or liquids at this place of articulation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Retroflex consonants are largely absent from indigenous languages of the Americas with the exception of the extreme south of South America, an area in
       <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">
        Southwestern US
       </a>
       as in
       <a href="/wiki/Hopi_language" title="Hopi language">
        Hopi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/O%27odham_language" title="O'odham language">
        O'odham
       </a>
       , and in
       <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">
        Alaska
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yukon_Territory" title="Yukon Territory">
        Yukon Territory
       </a>
       as in the
       <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">
        Athabaskan languages
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gwich%E2%80%99in_language" title="Gwich’in language">
        Gwich’in
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4n_language" title="Hän language">
        Hän
       </a>
       . In African languages retroflex consonants are also very rare, reportedly occurring in a few
       <a href="/wiki/Nilo-Saharan_languages" title="Nilo-Saharan languages">
        Nilo-Saharan languages
       </a>
       . In southwest Ethiopia, phonemically distinctive retroflex consonants are found in
       <a href="/wiki/Bench_language" title="Bench language">
        Bench
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sheko_language" title="Sheko language">
        Sheko
       </a>
       , two contiguous, but not closely related,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omotic" title="Omotic">
        Omotic
       </a>
       languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several retroflex consonants not yet recognized by the IPA. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Iwaidja_language" title="Iwaidja language">
        Iwaidja language
       </a>
       of northern Australia has a
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_lateral_flap" title="Retroflex lateral flap">
        retroflex lateral flap
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɺ̢]
       </span>
       as well as a retroflex tap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ]
       </span>
       and retroflex lateral approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɭ]
       </span>
       ; and the
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian language
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda
       </a>
       has a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_retroflex_lateral_fricative" title="Voiceless retroflex lateral fricative">
        subapical retroflex lateral fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɬ̢]
       </span>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_trill" title="Retroflex trill">
        retroflexed trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ͡r]
       </span>
       . Because of the regularity of deriving retroflex symbols from their alveolar counterparts, people will occasionally use a
       <a href="/wiki/Font_editor" title="Font editor">
        font editor
       </a>
       to create the appropriate symbols for such sounds. (Here they were written with diacritics.) The
       <a href="/wiki/Ngadha_language" title="Ngadha language">
        Ngad'a language
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">
        Flores
       </a>
       has been reported to have a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_retroflex_implosive" title="Voiced retroflex implosive">
        retroflex implosive
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ᶑ]
       </span>
       , but in this case the expected symbol is coincidentally supported by Unicode. Subapical retroflex clicks occur in Central
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juu_languages" title="Juu languages">
        Juu
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Damin" title="Damin">
        Damin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Most languages with retroflex sounds typically have only one retroflex sound with a given
       <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
        manner of articulation
       </a>
       . An exception, however, is the
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda language
       </a>
       , with a two-way distinction among retroflex sibilants between apical (post)alveolar and subapical palatal.
      </p>
