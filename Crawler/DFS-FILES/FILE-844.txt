      <p>
       <b>
        Peter Nielsen Ladefoged
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         English:
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'f' in 'find'">
            f
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">
            ɡ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <small>
        Danish:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Danish" title="Help:IPA/Danish">
         [pedɐ nelsn̩ ˈlæːðfowð]
        </a>
       </span>
       ; 17 September 1925 – 24 January 2006) was a British
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguist
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetician
       </a>
       who travelled the world to document the distinct sounds of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endangered_languages" title="Endangered languages">
        endangered languages
       </a>
       and pioneered ways to collect and study data.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was active at the universities of
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">
        Edinburgh
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
        Scotland
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Ibadan" title="University of Ibadan">
        Ibadan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">
        Nigeria
       </a>
       1953–61.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At Edinburgh he studied
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Abercrombie_(phonetician)" title="David Abercrombie (phonetician)">
        David Abercrombie
       </a>
       , who himself had studied with
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Jones_(phonetician)" title="Daniel Jones (phonetician)">
        Daniel Jones
       </a>
       and was thus connected to
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Sweet" title="Henry Sweet">
        Henry Sweet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the time of his death, he was Professor of Phonetics Emeritus at
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">
        University of California, Los Angeles
       </a>
       (UCLA), where he taught from 1962 to 1991. His book
       <i>
        A Course in Phonetics
       </i>
       is a common introductory text in phonetics, and
       <i>
        The Sounds of the World's Languages
       </i>
       (co-authored with
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">
        Ian Maddieson
       </a>
       ) is widely regarded as a standard phonetics reference. Ladefoged also wrote several books on the phonetics of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/African_languages" title="African languages">
        African languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_and_education">
        Early life and education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Ladefoged&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life and education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Peter Ladefoged was born on 17 September 1925, in
       <a href="/wiki/Sutton,_London" title="Sutton, London">
        Sutton
       </a>
       (then in
       <a href="/wiki/Surrey" title="Surrey">
        Surrey
       </a>
       , now in
       <a href="/wiki/Greater_London" title="Greater London">
        Greater London
       </a>
       ), England to Niels, an importer of Danish bacon and cheese, and his wife, Marie Frances. He attended
       <a href="/wiki/Haileybury_and_Imperial_Service_College" title="Haileybury and Imperial Service College">
        Haileybury College
       </a>
       from 1938–43, and
       <a href="/wiki/Gonville_and_Caius_College,_Cambridge" title="Gonville and Caius College, Cambridge">
        Caius College
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        Cambridge University
       </a>
       from 1943–44. His university education was then interrupted by service in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Royal_Sussex_Regiment" title="The Royal Sussex Regiment">
        Royal Sussex Regiment
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       from 1944 to 1947. He resumed his education at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Edinburgh" title="University of Edinburgh">
        University of Edinburgh
       </a>
       , intending to study English literature but soon became fascinated by the sounds of speech. He received an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Master%27s_Degree" title="Master's Degree">
        MA
       </a>
       (1951) and a PhD (1959) in Phonetics.
      </p>
      <p>
       He was able to receive a first degree after only two years as an undergraduate at Edinburgh because returning World War II servicemen were allowed a year off from the usual three-year requirement for an Ordinary degree. After receiving an M.A. (ordinary) (war emergency) in 1951, he went on to do a year's postgraduate work in phonetics. At the end of that year he got his first job, as a lab assistant cutting vinyl recordings. On 1 January 1953, he was promoted to Assistant Lecturer in Phonetics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the late 1950s, Ladefoged decided to work in the United States, but this required a PhD degree. The university
       <a href="/wiki/Registrar_(education)" title="Registrar (education)">
        registrar
       </a>
       allowed him to count the three years that he was a member of the faculty. All he needed was to complete a thesis. Ladefoged's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dissertation" title="Dissertation">
        dissertation
       </a>
       was on the "nature of vowel quality," specifically on the cardinal vowels and their articulatory vs. auditory basis. After consultation with and advice from David Abercrombie, the head of the Phonetics Department, Ladefoged took three papers that he had already published on aspects of vowel quality, and added an introductory survey. He also appended some work that he had been doing on
       <a href="/wiki/Cardinal_vowels" title="Cardinal vowels">
        cardinal vowels
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Jones_(phonetician)" title="Daniel Jones (phonetician)">
        Daniel Jones
       </a>
       , who had recently retired from the chair of phonetics at
       <a href="/wiki/University_College_London" title="University College London">
        University College London
       </a>
       . Abercrombie arranged a grant enabling Jones to be a consultant on Ladefoged's project to study the acoustic quality of cardinal vowels, which enabled him an opportunity to work with the leading phonetician of the time. The sets of vowels recorded under Jones's supervision were made by his former pupils. Although not very noteworthy, this part of his project provided an early example of the problems of analysing vowels spectrographically.
      </p>
      <p>
       After completing his thesis, Ladefoged received his PhD upon completion of an oral exam that included Walter Lawrence – the inventor of PAT, the first
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Parametric_speech_synthesizer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Parametric speech synthesizer (page does not exist)">
        parametric speech synthesizer
       </a>
       – as an outside examiner. It was through Lawrence that Ladefoged met
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Broadbent" title="Donald Broadbent">
        Donald Broadbent
       </a>
       , who was a psychologist working in Cambridge at the time. They teamed together to conduct experiments using synthetic speech, about the relative nature of vowel quality. This led to their working together on other aspects of speech perception, and through Broadbent he learned how to do work in perceptual psychology.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another person whom Ladefoged was able to work with through David Abercrombie was
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Whitteridge&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Whitteridge (page does not exist)">
        David Whitteridge
       </a>
       , the Professor of Physiology, who was interested in the control of the respiratory system in speech. Ladefoged started working in Whitteridge's lab, at first every Saturday morning, then for days at a time, and then longer and longer as they realised that the control of the respiratory muscles was no simple matter. Said Ladefoged, "It was really Whitteridge who taught me to be a scientist."
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Academic_career">
        Academic career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Ladefoged&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Academic career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       At the same time, he began important research projects with Donald Broadbent, Walter Lawrence, Morris H. Draper, and David Whitteridge, with his first publications appearing in 1956. His 1957 paper with Donald Broadbent, "Information conveyed by vowels", was particularly influential.
      </p>
      <p>
       Soon after moving to Los Angeles from Scotland to become an assistant professor at UCLA in 1962, Ladefoged had a brief career in
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       as the chief linguistic consultant on the 1964 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/My_Fair_Lady_(film)" title="My Fair Lady (film)">
         My Fair Lady
        </a>
        .
       </i>
       Director
       <a href="/wiki/George_Cukor" title="George Cukor">
        George Cukor
       </a>
       wanted him to teach the film's star,
       <a href="/wiki/Rex_Harrison" title="Rex Harrison">
        Rex Harrison
       </a>
       – who would win an
       <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">
        Oscar
       </a>
       for the role of Professor Henry Higgins – to behave like a phonetician. It is Ladefoged's voice that is heard producing the vowel sounds in the film.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged was involved with the phonetics laboratory at UCLA, which he established in 1962. He also was interested in listening to and describing every sound used in spoken human language, which he estimated at 900
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       and 200
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       . This research formed the basis of much of
       <i>
        The Sounds of the World's Languages
       </i>
       . In 1966 Ladefoged moved from the UCLA English Department to join the newly established Linguistics Department.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged's opinion on studying endangered languages was that linguists should record languages but not necessarily try to save them, even though he predicted that all but a handful of the world's 6,500 languages would
       <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">
        disappear
       </a>
       over the next thousand years. He argued that preserving languages could weaken
       <a href="/wiki/Nationalism" title="Nationalism">
        national unity
       </a>
       , encourage
       <a href="/wiki/Tribalism" title="Tribalism">
        tribalism
       </a>
       , and absorb scarce resources that might otherwise be used for
       <a href="/wiki/Economic_development" title="Economic development">
        development
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged was also a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Association" title="International Phonetic Association">
        International Phonetic Association
       </a>
       for a long time, and was President of the Association from 1986 to 1991. His was deeply involved in maintaining its
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       , and was the principal mover of the 1989
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Association_Kiel_Convention" title="International Phonetic Association Kiel Convention">
        International Phonetic Association Kiel Convention
       </a>
       . He was also editor of the
       <i>
        Journal of the International Phonetic Association
       </i>
       . Ladefoged served on the board of directors of the
       <a href="/wiki/Endangered_Language_Fund" title="Endangered Language Fund">
        Endangered Language Fund
       </a>
       since its inception.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged is a founding member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Association_for_Laboratory_Phonology" title="Association for Laboratory Phonology">
        Association for Laboratory Phonology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Family_life">
        Family life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Ladefoged&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Family life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ladefoged married Jenny MacDonald in 1953, a marriage which lasted over 50 years. They had three children: Lise Friedman, a bookseller; Thegn Ladefoged,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Archaeologist" title="Archaeologist">
        archaeologist
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Auckland" title="University of Auckland">
        University of Auckland
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Katie Weiss, attorney and
       <a href="/wiki/Public_defender" title="Public defender">
        public defender
       </a>
       , residing in
       <a href="/wiki/Nashville,_Tennessee" title="Nashville, Tennessee">
        Nashville
       </a>
       , Tennessee. He also had five grandchildren Zelda Ladefoged, Ethan Friedman, Amy Friedman, Joseph Weiss, and Catherine Weiss.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged died on 24 January 2006 at the age of 80 in hospital. After a research trip to
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       , he had a safe flight from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bombay" title="Bombay">
        Bombay
       </a>
       to London, England, but suffered a small stroke at
       <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">
        Heathrow Airport
       </a>
       . He was then taken to a hospital, where he suffered a second, massive, stroke and died soon after.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
        Legacy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peter_Ladefoged&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Legacy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During his academic career, Ladefoged was a worldwide field linguist, as he visited Nigeria,
       <a href="/wiki/Botswana" title="Botswana">
        Botswana
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">
        Ghana
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">
        Uganda
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">
        Tanzania
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Leone" title="Sierra Leone">
        Sierra Leone
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">
        Senegal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yemen" title="Yemen">
        Yemen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Papua_New_Guinea" title="Papua New Guinea">
        Papua New Guinea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">
        Nepal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">
        Thailand
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       , Australia,
       <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">
        Korea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Scotland" title="Scotland">
        Scotland
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aleutians" title="Aleutians">
        Aleutians
       </a>
       , and China. Much of his fieldwork remains unique to this day and he originated or refined many data collection and analytic techniques in the field. His classic 1996
       <i>
        Sounds of the World's Languages
       </i>
       (with Ian Maddieson) summarised his knowledge of all the sounds he had studied and remains the definitive reference work. His 20 PhD students include such influential figures as
       <a href="/wiki/Victoria_Fromkin" title="Victoria Fromkin">
        Vicki Fromkin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Ohala" title="John Ohala">
        John Ohala
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">
        Ian Maddieson
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_M._Goldstein" title="Louis M. Goldstein">
        Louis Goldstein
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cathe_Browman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cathe Browman (page does not exist)">
        Cathe Browman
       </a>
       . His textbook
       <i>
        A Course in Phonetics
       </i>
       , whose seventh edition appeared in 2014, is the standard in phonetics.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged also pioneered the use of state-of-the-art equipment in the field. His first portable phonetics lab that included a tape recorder and various scientific instruments weighed 100 pounds (45 kg) and required a porter but enabled him to do more than listen: He could take quantitative measurements, such as gauging how much air escaped from the nose or throat when a sound was made.
      </p>
      <p>
       In an earlier trip to India, he recorded the
       <a href="/wiki/Toda_language" title="Toda language">
        Toda language
       </a>
       , which is spoken by fewer than 1,000 people, as he documented its six trills produced by the tip of the tongue. In the Kalahari Desert, he studied the click sounds native to that part of Africa. According to Ladefoged, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/!Kung_people" title="!Kung people">
        !Kung
       </a>
       , whose members knew their language was vanishing, chose not to co-operate because they did not want to reveal their culture to outsiders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
