       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ladin:
       <i>
        Ladin
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it">
         Ladino
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="de">
         Ladinisch
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       consisting of a group of dialects that some consider part of a unitary
       <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
        Rhaeto-Romance language
       </a>
       , mainly spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Dolomites" title="Dolomites">
        Dolomite Mountains
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       in the provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/South_Tyrol" title="South Tyrol">
        South Tyrol
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Trentino" title="Trentino">
        Trentino
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">
        Belluno
       </a>
       , by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ladin_people" title="Ladin people">
        Ladin people
       </a>
       . It exhibits similarities to Swiss
       <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">
        Romansh
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Friulian_language" title="Friulian language">
        Friulian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The precise extension of the Ladin language area is the subject of scholarly debates. A more narrow perspective includes only the dialects of the valleys around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sella_group" title="Sella group">
        Sella group
       </a>
       , while wider definitions comprise the dialects of adjacent valleys in the Province of Belluno and even dialects spoken in the northwestern Trentino.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard
       </a>
       written variety of Ladin (
       <i>
        Ladin Dolomitan
       </i>
       ) has been developed by the Office for Ladin Language Planning as a common communication tool across the whole Ladin-speaking region,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but it is not popular among Ladin speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladin should not be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       (also called Judeo-Spanish), which, although also Romance, is derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">

         Contraction of the area of the Rhaeto-Romance languages
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ladin is recognized as a minority language in 54
       <a href="/wiki/Comune" title="Comune">
        Italian municipalities
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       belonging to the provinces of South Tyrol, Trentino and Belluno. It is not possible to assess the exact number of Ladin speakers, because only in the provinces of South Tyrol and Trentino are the inhabitants asked to identify their native language in the general census of the population, which takes place every 10 years.
      </p>

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Non_Valley" title="Non Valley">
        Non Valley
       </a>

       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Solandro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Solandro language (page does not exist)">
        Solandro language
       </a>
       found in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sole_Valley" title="Sole Valley">
        Sole Valley
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance languages
       </a>
       and often grouped together into a single linguistic unit due to their similarity. They are spoken in 38 municipalities but have no official status. Their more precise classification is uncertain. Both dialects show a strong resemblance to Trentinian dialect and
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard_dialect" title="Eastern Lombard dialect">
        Eastern Lombard
       </a>
       , and scholars debate whether they are Ladin dialects or not.
      </p>
      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Fassa_Valley" title="Fassa Valley">
        Fassa Valley
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minority_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Minority-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In order to stress the difference between the dialects in Non and Fassa valleys, it has been proposed to distinguish between
       <i>
        ladins dolomitiches
       </i>
       (Dolomitic Ladinians) and
       <i>
        ladins nonejes
       </i>
       (Non Valley Ladinians) at the next census.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Province_of_Belluno">
        Province of Belluno
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Province of Belluno">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is no linguistic census in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">
        province of Belluno
       </a>
       , but the number of Ladin speakers has been estimated using a 2006 survey. In this area, there are about 1,166 people who speak the standard Ladin and 865 who speak the dialect of Ladin, so out of 8,495 inhabitants they are the 23.9%. They live in the part of the province that was part of the County of Tyrol until 1918, comprising the communes of
       <a href="/wiki/Cortina_d%27Ampezzo" title="Cortina d'Ampezzo">
        Cortina d'Ampezzo

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="1520" height="155" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg/230px-Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg/345px-Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg/460px-Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg 2x" width="230"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pfl%C3%BCgerWengen60.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Ladin farmers in 1960s
         <a href="/wiki/La_Val" title="La Val">
          La Val
         </a>
         , South Tyrol
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The name derives from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       , because Ladin is originally a
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       language left over from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Romanized
       </a>
       Alps. Ladin is often attributed to be a relic of Vulgar Latin dialects associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
        Rhaeto-Romance languages
       </a>
       . Whether a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Romance_language" title="Proto-Romance language">
        proto-Romance language
       </a>
       ever existed is controversially discussed amongst linguists and historians, a debate known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Questione_Ladina" title="Questione Ladina">
         Questione Ladina
        </a>
       </i>
       . Starting in the 6th century, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bavarii" title="Bavarii">
        Bavarii
       </a>
       started moving in from the north, while from the south
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       started pushing in, which further shrank the original extent of the Ladin area. Only in the more remote mountain valleys did Ladin survive among the isolated populations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Starting in the very early Middle Ages, the area was mostly ruled by the
       <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">
        County of Tyrol
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Brixen" title="Bishopric of Brixen">
        Bishopric of Brixen
       </a>
       , both belonging to the realms of the Austrian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Habsburg" title="Habsburg">
        Habsburg
       </a>
       rulers. The area of Cadore was under the rule of the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Republic of Venice
       </a>
       . During the period of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire_of_the_German_Nation" title="Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation">
        Holy Roman Empire of the German Nation
       </a>
       and, after 1804, the Austrian Empire, the Ladins underwent a process of
       <a href="/wiki/Germanisation" title="Germanisation">
        Germanization
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4055" data-file-width="2608" height="342" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg/220px-Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg/330px-Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg/440px-Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Josef_Anton_Vian.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Kurat Josef Anton Vian - anonymous author of the first Ladin-Gherdëina grammar AD 1864
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
          <a href="#cite_note-19">
           [19]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       After the end of World War I in 1918, Italy annexed the southern part of Tyrol, including the Ladin areas. The Italian nationalist movement of the 19th and 20th centuries regarded Ladin as an "
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">
        Italian dialect
       </a>
       ", a notion rejected by various Ladin exponents and associations,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       despite their having been counted as Italians by the Austrian authorities as well. The programme of
       <a href="/wiki/Italianization" title="Italianization">
        Italianization
       </a>
       , professed by fascists such as
       <a href="/wiki/Ettore_Tolomei" title="Ettore Tolomei">
        Ettore Tolomei
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Benito_Mussolini" title="Benito Mussolini">
        Benito Mussolini
       </a>
       , added further pressure on the Ladin communities to subordinate their identities to Italian. This included changing Ladin place names into the Italian pronunciation according to Tolomei's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prontuario_dei_nomi_locali_dell%27Alto_Adige" title="Prontuario dei nomi locali dell'Alto Adige">
         Prontuario dei nomi locali dell'Alto Adige
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Following the end of World War II, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gruber-De_Gasperi_Agreement" title="Gruber-De Gasperi Agreement">
        Gruber-De Gasperi Agreement
       </a>
       of 1946 between Austria and Italy introduced a level of autonomy for Trentino and South Tyrol but did not include any provisions for the Ladin language. Only in the second autonomy statute for South Tyrol in 1972 was Ladin recognized as a partially official language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">
        Status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Scuola_ladina.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="509" data-file-width="767" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Scuola_ladina.JPG/220px-Scuola_ladina.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Scuola_ladina.JPG/330px-Scuola_ladina.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Scuola_ladina.JPG/440px-Scuola_ladina.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Scuola_ladina.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Plaque of a Ladin school in Santa Cristina.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ladin is officially recognised in Trentino and South Tyrol by provincial and national law. Italy signed the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">
        European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages
       </a>
       of 1991, but it has not ratified it so far. The charter calls for
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_rights" title="Minority rights">
        minority rights
       </a>
       to be respected and
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">
        minority languages
       </a>
       , to which Ladin belongs, to be appropriately protected and promoted. Starting in the 1990s, the Italian parliament and provincial assembly have passed laws and regulations protecting the Ladin language and culture. A cultural institute was founded to safeguard and educate in the language and culture. School curricula were adapted in order to teach in Ladin, and street signs are being changed to bilingual.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladin is also recognized as a protected language in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Belluno" title="Province of Belluno">
        Province of Belluno
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">
        Veneto
       </a>
       region pursuant to the Standards for Protection of Historic Language Minorities Act No. 482 (1999). In comparison with South Tyrol and Trentino, the wishes of the Ladins have barely been addressed by the regional government. In a popular referendum in October 2007, the inhabitants of
       <a href="/wiki/Cortina_d%27Ampezzo" title="Cortina d'Ampezzo">
        Cortina d'Ampezzo
       </a>
       overwhelmingly voted to leave Veneto and return to South Tyrol.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The redrawing of the provincial borders would return Cortina d'Ampezzo,
       <a href="/wiki/Livinallongo_del_Col_di_Lana" title="Livinallongo del Col di Lana">
        Livinallongo del Col di Lana
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Colle_Santa_Lucia" title="Colle Santa Lucia">
        Colle Santa Lucia
       </a>
       to South Tyrol, to which they traditionally belonged when part of the
       <a href="/wiki/County_of_Tyrol" title="County of Tyrol">
        County of Tyrol
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Bishopric_of_Brixen" title="Bishopric of Brixen">
        Bishopric of Brixen
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1488" data-file-width="1260" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg/220px-Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg/330px-Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg/440px-Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Trilingual_road_sign_in_Val_Gardena.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Trilingual traffic sign.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although the Ladin communities are spread out over three neighbouring regions, the
       <i>
        Union Generala di Ladins dles Dolomites
       </i>
       is asking that they be reunited.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Ladin_Autonomist_Union" title="Ladin Autonomist Union">
        Ladin Autonomist Union
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Fassa_Association" title="Fassa Association">
        Fassa Association
       </a>
       run on a Ladin list and have sought more rights and autonomy for Ladin speakers. Ladins are also guaranteed political representations in the assemblies of Trentino and South Tyrol due to a reserved seats system.
      </p>
      <p>
       In South Tyrol, in order to reach a fair allocation of jobs in public service, a system called "ethnic proportion" was established in the 1970s. Every 10 years, when the general census of population takes place, each citizen has to identify with a linguistic group. The results determine how many potential positions in public service are allocated for each linguistic group. This has theoretically enabled Ladins to receive guaranteed representation in the South Tyrolean civil service according to their numbers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The recognition of minority languages in Italy has been criticised since the implementation of Act No. 482 (1999), especially due to alleged financial benefits. This applies also to Ladin language, especially in the province of Belluno.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdivisions">
        Subdivisions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Subdivisions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A possible subdivision of Ladin language
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       identifies six major groups.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Athesian_Group_of_the_Sella">
        Athesian Group of the Sella
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Athesian Group of the Sella">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The dialects of the Athesian group (from the river
       <a href="/wiki/Adige" title="Adige">
        Adige
       </a>
       Basin) of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sella_group" title="Sella group">
        Sella
       </a>
       are spoken in South Tyrol:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Gherdëina, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Val_Gardena" title="Val Gardena">
         Val Gardena
        </a>
        by 8,148 inhabitants (80–90% of the population);
       </li>
       <li>
        Badiot and Maró, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Val_Badia" title="Val Badia">
         Val Badia
        </a>
        and in
        <a href="/wiki/Mareo" title="Mareo">
         Mareo
        </a>
        by 9,229 people, i.e. 95%, as native language.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The South Tyrolean dialects are most similar to the original Ladin.
      </p>
      <table class="mbox-small" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000;">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trentinian_Group_of_the_Sella">
        Trentinian Group of the Sella
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Trentinian Group of the Sella">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The names of the Ladin dialects spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Fassa_Valley" title="Fassa Valley">
        Fassa Valley
       </a>
       in Trentino are Moenat, Brach, and Cazet. 82.8% of the inhabitants of Fassa Valley are native Ladin speakers;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Ladin language in Fassa is influenced by Trentinian dialects.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Agordino_Group_of_the_Sella">
        Agordino Group of the Sella
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Agordino Group of the Sella">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the Province of Belluno the following dialects are considered as part of the Agordino group:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fodom" title="Fodom">
         Fodom
        </a>
        , also called
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Livinallese&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Livinallese (page does not exist)">
         Livinallese
        </a>
        , spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Livinallongo_del_Col_di_Lana" title="Livinallongo del Col di Lana">
         Livinallongo del Col di Lana
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Colle_Santa_Lucia" title="Colle Santa Lucia">
         Colle Santa Lucia
        </a>
        , native language of 80–90% of the people;
       </li>
       <li>
        Rocchesano in the area of
        <a href="/wiki/Rocca_Pietore" title="Rocca Pietore">
         Rocca Pietore
        </a>
        . While
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laste" title="Laste">
         Laste di Sopra
        </a>
        (Ladin
        <i>
         Laste de Sora
        </i>
        ) and Sottoguda (Ladin
        <i>
         Stagùda
        </i>
        ) are predominantly Ladin, in
        <a href="/wiki/Alleghe" title="Alleghe">
         Alleghe
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Tomaso&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Tomaso (page does not exist)">
         San Tomaso
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Falcade" title="Falcade">
         Falcade
        </a>
        so-called Ladin-Venetian dialects are spoken, with strong Venetian influence;
       </li>
       <li>
        Ladin in the area of Agordo and
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Biois&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Valle del Biois (page does not exist)">
         Valle del Biois
        </a>
        , even if some regard it rather as Venetian-Ladin.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ampezzan_Group">
        Ampezzan Group
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Ampezzan Group">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Cortina_d%27Ampezzo" title="Cortina d'Ampezzo">
        Cortina d'Ampezzo
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Anpezo
       </i>
       ), similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Cadorino_dialect" title="Cadorino dialect">
        Cadorino dialect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Even in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_di_Zoldo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Valle di Zoldo (page does not exist)">
        Valle di Zoldo
       </a>
       (from Forno-Fôr upwards) there are elements of the Ampezzan Group.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cadorino_Group">
        Cadorino Group
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Cadorino Group">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Cadore" title="Cadore">
        Cadore
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Comelico" title="Comelico">
        Comelico
       </a>
       and best known as
       <a href="/wiki/Cadorino_dialect" title="Cadorino dialect">
        Cadorino dialect
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nones_and_Solandro_Group">
        Nones and Solandro Group
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ladin_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nones and Solandro Group">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Western Trentino, in
       <a href="/wiki/Non_Valley" title="Non Valley">
        Non Valley
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Val_di_Sole" title="Val di Sole">
        Val di Sole
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Val_di_Peio&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Val di Peio (page does not exist)">
        Val di Peio
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Val_di_Rabbi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Val di Rabbi (page does not exist)">
        Val di Rabbi
       </a>
       , and part of
       <a href="/wiki/Val_Rendena" title="Val Rendena">
        Val Rendena
       </a>
       , detached from the dolomitic area, dialects are spoken that are often considered to be part of the Ladin language (Anaunic Ladin), but enjoy strong influences from Trentinian and Eastern Lombard dialects.
      </p>
