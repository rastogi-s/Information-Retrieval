      <p>
       <b>
        Renaissance Latin
       </b>
       is a name given to the distinctive form of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       style developed during the European
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       of the fourteenth to fifteenth centuries, particularly by the
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance_humanism" title="Renaissance humanism">
        Renaissance humanism
       </a>
       movement.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ad_fontes">
        Ad fontes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Renaissance_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ad fontes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ad_fontes" title="Ad fontes">
         Ad fontes
        </a>
       </i>
       ("to the sources") was the general cry of the humanists, and as such their Latin style sought to purge Latin of the
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">
        medieval Latin
       </a>
       vocabulary and stylistic accretions that it had acquired in the centuries after the fall of the Roman Empire. They looked to golden age Latin literature, and especially to
       <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
        Cicero
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">
        prose
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">
        Virgil
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
        poetry
       </a>
       , as the arbiters of Latin style. They abandoned the use of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sequence_(poetry)" title="Sequence (poetry)">
        sequence
       </a>
       and other accentual forms of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metre_in_poetry" title="Metre in poetry">
        metre
       </a>
       , and sought instead to revive the Greek formats that were used in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_poetry" title="Latin poetry">
        Latin poetry
       </a>
       during the Roman period. The humanists condemned the large body of medieval Latin literature as "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Medieval" title="High Medieval">
        Gothic
       </a>
       "—for them, a term of abuse—and believed instead that only ancient Latin from the Roman period was "real Latin".
      </p>
      <p>
       Some 16th-century Ciceronian humanists also sought to purge written Latin of medieval developments in its
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       . They insisted, for example, that
       <i>
        ae
       </i>
       be written out in full wherever it occurred in classical Latin; medieval scribes often wrote
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        ae
       </i>
       . They were much more zealous than medieval Latin writers that
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       be distinguished; because the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       made them
       <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
        homophones
       </a>
       , medieval scribes often wrote, for example,
       <i>
        eciam
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        etiam
       </i>
       . Their reforms even affected
       <a href="/wiki/Handwriting" title="Handwriting">
        handwriting
       </a>
       ; Humanists usually wrote Latin in a
       <a href="/wiki/Humanist_minuscule" title="Humanist minuscule">
        humanist minuscule
       </a>
       script derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_minuscule" title="Carolingian minuscule">
        Carolingian minuscule
       </a>
       , the ultimate ancestor of most contemporary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower-case" title="Lower-case">
        lower-case
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">
        typefaces
       </a>
       , avoiding the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Black-letter" title="Black-letter">
        black-letter
       </a>
       scripts used in the Middle Ages. This sort of writing was particularly vigilant in edited works, so that international colleagues could read them more easily, while in their own handwritten documents the Latin is usually written as it is pronounced in the vernacular. Therefore, the first generations of humanists did not dedicate much care to the orthography till the late sixteenth and seventeenth century.
       <a href="/wiki/Erasmus" title="Erasmus">
        Erasmus
       </a>
       proposed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_regional_pronunciation" title="Latin regional pronunciation">
        then-traditional pronunciations
       </a>
       of Latin be abolished in favour of his
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_spelling_and_pronunciation" title="Latin spelling and pronunciation">
        reconstructed version
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        classical Latin
       </a>
       pronunciation, even though one can deduce from his works that he himself used the ecclesiastical pronunciation.
      </p>
      <p>
       The humanist plan to remake Latin was largely successful, at least in
       <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">
        education
       </a>
       . Schools taught the humanistic spellings, and encouraged the study of the texts selected by the humanists, to the large exclusion of later Latin literature. On the other hand, while humanist Latin was an elegant
       <a href="/wiki/Literary_language" title="Literary language">
        literary language
       </a>
       , it became much harder to write books about
       <a href="/wiki/Law" title="Law">
        law
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">
        medicine
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       or contemporary
       <a href="/wiki/Politics" title="Politics">
        politics
       </a>
       in Latin while observing all of the Humanists' norms about vocabulary purging and classical usage.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Renaissance Latin gradually developed into the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Latin" title="New Latin">
        New Latin
       </a>
       of the 16th–19th centuries, used as the language of choice for authors discussing subjects considered sufficiently important to merit an international (i.e., pan-European) audience.
      </p>
