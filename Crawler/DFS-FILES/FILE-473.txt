    Verb framing
   </h1>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        verb-framing
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        satellite-framing
       </b>
       are typological descriptions of a way that verb phrases in a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       can describe the
       <i>
        path
       </i>
       of motion or the
       <i>
        manner
       </i>
       of motion, respectively. Some languages use it and others do not.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Manner_and_path">
        Manner and path
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Verb_framing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Manner and path">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The manner of motion refers to a type of distinct motion described by a particular verb: running, tumbling, sliding, walking, crawling, etc. The path of motion refers to the direction of the movement: movement into, out of, across, etc. The two concepts can be encoded in the verb as part of its root meaning or in a separate particle associated to the verb (a
       <i>
        satellite
       </i>
       ). Manner may also not be expressed at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages are considered
       <i>
        verb-framed
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        satellite-framed
       </i>
       based on how the motion path is encoded.
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       verbs use
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">
        particles
       </a>
       to show the path of motion ("run into", "go out", "fall down"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), and its verbs usually show manner of motion; thus, English is a satellite-framed language. English verbs that are exceptions are mostly derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       , such as "exit", "ascend", or "enter".
      </p>
      <p>
       All
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       are satellite-framed languages. Accordingly, "to go out" is
       <i>
        hinausgehen
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        uitgaan
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        gå ut
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       , wherein
       <i>
        gehen
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        gaan
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        gå
       </i>
       are equivalents of "to go", and
       <i>
        hinaus
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        uit
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        ut
       </i>
       are equivalents of "out". In this manner, Germanic languages can form all kinds of compounds, even less manifest ones like (German)
       <i>
        hinaustanzen
       </i>
       "to dance out" and so on.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       are verb-framed.
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , for example, makes heavy use of verbs of motion like
       <i>
        entrar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        salir
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        subir
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        bajar
       </i>
       ("go in", "go out", "go up", "go down"), which directly encode motion path, and may leave out the manner of motion or express it in a complement of manner (typically a
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        participle
       </a>
       ):
       <i>
        entró corriendo
       </i>
       "he ran in", literally "he entered running";
       <i>
        salió flotando
       </i>
       "it floated out", literally "it exited floating".
      </p>
      <p>
       The terms verb framing and satellite framing are not restricted to Romance and Germanic languages, respectively. Many languages can be assigned to one of the two systems. For example, verb framing is used in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       . (In the latter,
       <i>
        dakhala rākiḍan
       </i>
       means "he entered running", with the perfect form
       <i>
        dakhala
       </i>
       meaning "he entered" and the participle
       <i>
        rākiḍan
       </i>
       meaning "running".) Satellite framing is common in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       . Some languages use both strategies.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_from_English_and_French">
        Examples from English and French
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Verb_framing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples from English and French">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Romance languages, such as French, are normally verb-framed, and Germanic languages, such as English, are satellite-framed. This means that when expressing motion events, English speakers typically express manner in the verb, and French speakers (like Italian &amp; Spanish speakers) typically express path in the verb and either leave out the manner of motion completely or express it in a complement of manner. Thus, to take one example, "He ran into the room" is routinely translated as
       <i>
        Il est entré dans la pièce
       </i>
       ; only sometimes will it be
       <i>
        Il est entré dans la pièce en courant
       </i>
       ("he entered the room running").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This means, first, that the verb itself normally does not express manner in French, as opposed to what is generally the case in English; and if manner is expressed, it is expressed in a complement (or, more precisely, an adjunct) of manner:
       <i>
        en courant
       </i>
       ("running").
      </p>
      <p>
       The question, then, remains of whether to express manner or not. It is not always easy to know, but manner is generally left unexpressed when it can be considered to be self-evident and can be inferred from the context; expressing the manner then tends to sound unnatural.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, "He ran into the room" can be translated as
       <i>
        Il est entré dans la pièce en courant
       </i>
       because it is slightly unusual to run into a room and so manner should be mentioned, but translating "He walked into the room" as
       <i>
        Il est entré dans la pièce à pied
       </i>
       ("on foot") or
       <i>
        en marchant
       </i>
       ("walking") is distinctly odd because it is the usual way in which one enters a room. Only in a case where someone was riding a bicycle before or moving in another unusual way can the fact that he "walked" into the room be considered to be relevant. The same can be said for this example: saying "I'm flying to London" when on a plane is normal in English, but saying "Je vole" ("I'm flying") in French for the same situation is odd: first, because the verb is not where one should normally express manner in the first place and also because it can be self-evident in the situation.
      </p>
      <p>
       This means that the choice of complement and in particular the choice of the preposition can also be affected: in English, the particle or the prepositional phrase (the "satellite") is where the path is expressed, with the use of a dynamic preposition: "(walk) into (the room)", "(fly) to (London)", whereas in French, it is the verb that normally expresses the path. A preposition like "à" ("to, at, in") is ambiguous between a static reading (
       <i>
        Je suis à Paris
       </i>
       /"I'm in Paris") and a dynamic reading (
       <i>
        Je vais à Paris
       </i>
       /"I'm going to Paris"). If the verb is dynamic and expresses directed motion (motion with an intrinsic direction),
       <i>
        à
       </i>
       can express movement (
       <i>
        Je vais à Paris
       </i>
       ). If not, as is the case for instance with
       <i>
        voler
       </i>
       ("to fly"), which expresses manner of motion but not directed motion,
       <i>
        à
       </i>
       tends to receive a static and not a dynamic, interpretation:
       <i>
        je vole à Paris
       </i>
       would mean something like "I'm flying IN Paris", and not "I'm flying TO Paris". Using the same structure as in English can be doubly misleading, as the verb and the preposition are both unusual;
       <i>
        Je vais à
       </i>
       ("I'm going to")/
       <i>
        Je suis en route
       </i>
       ("I am on my way")
       <i>
        vers
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        pour Paris
       </i>
       ("towards/for Paris") are much better.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_and_its_limitations">
        Opposition and its limitations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Verb_framing&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Opposition and its limitations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although languages can generally be classified as "verb-framed"/"satellite-framed", not everything works exactly in that way. First, languages can use both strategies, as is the case with the Latinate verbs mentioned supra for English: "enter", "ascend", "exit". The existence of equipollently-framed languages in which both manner and path are expressed in verbs has been pointed out (Slobin 2004). It could be true of
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for instance.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Native American languages, such as the extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Atsugewi_language" title="Atsugewi language">
        Atsugewi
       </a>
       , do not select verbs of motion based on path or manner. Instead, verbs of motion are specific to the kind of object that is moving or being moved.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
