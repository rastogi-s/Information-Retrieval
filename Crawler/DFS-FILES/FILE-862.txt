      <p>
       <b>
        Salvadoran Spanish
       </b>
       is geographically defined as the form of Spanish spoken in the country of
       <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">
        El Salvador
       </a>
       . The Spanish dialect in El Salvador shares many similarities to that of its neighbors in the region, but it has its stark differences in pronunciation and usage. El Salvador, like most of Central America, uses
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       Spanish as its written and spoken form, similar to that of Argentina. Vos is used, but many Salvadorans understand tuteo. Vos can be heard in television programs and can be seen in written form in publications.
       <i>
        Usted
       </i>
       is used as a show of respect, when someone is speaking to an elderly person.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics_and_phonology">
        Phonetics and phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonetics and phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Notable characteristics of Salvadoran phonology include the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The presence of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo#Seseo" title="Seseo">
         Seseo
        </a>
        wherein
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        . Seseo is common to
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
         Canarian Spanish
        </a>
        varieties.
       </li>
       <li>
        Syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        (mainly on the Eastern departments as Usulután and San Miguel).
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_2-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The pronunciation of intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        (orthographic
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        ) is "weak", without friction.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_2-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        often disappears; the ending
        <i>
         -ado
        </i>
        is often
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ao]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_2-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no confusion between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , as in the Caribbean.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        is pronounced velar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_1-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_2-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As El Salvador was part of the
        <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
         First Mexican Empire
        </a>
        , the Salvadoran dialect adopted the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
         voiceless alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡s]
        </span>
        and the cluster
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tl]
        </span>
        (originally
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tɬ/
        </span>
        ) represented by the respective digraphs &lt;tz&gt; and &lt;tl&gt; in loanwords of Nahuatl origin,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Quetzal" title="Quetzal">
          quetzal
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tlapalería
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡ɬapaleˈɾia]
        </span>
        ('hardware store'). Even words of Greek and Latin origin with &lt;tl&gt;, such as
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27Atl%C3%A1ntico%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''Atlántico''">
           <i>
            Atlántico
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27atleta%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''atleta''">
           <i>
            atleta
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        , are pronounced with the affricate:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        (compare
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns_and_verb_conjugation">
        Pronouns and verb conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pronouns and verb conjugation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       "Vos" is the dominant second person singular pronoun used by many speakers in familiar or informal contexts. Salvadoran
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       comes from Gothic Spain and was brought to El Salvador by militaristic Spaniards who owned the land. Its counterparts are French
       <i>
        vous
       </i>
       , Portuguese
       <i>
        vós
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
       is most commonly used among people in the same age group in addressing one another. It is common to hear young children address each other with "vos." The phenomenon also occurs among adults who address one another in familiar or informal contexts. "Vos" is also used by adults in addressing children or juveniles. However, the relationship does not reoccur when children address adults. Children address adults with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       regardless of age, status or context. The preservation of voseo in Salvadoran Spanish was thanks to El Salvador's ties to the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       . When El Salvador became independent, it discontinued to have trade links with Spain, unlike other
       <i>
        tuteo
       </i>
       countries. El Salvador's main trading partners were the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       , thus Spain did not influence El Salvador's language anymore, and Spain changed to tuteo. In turn, English words influenced Salvadoran Spanish and voseo was conserved.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ustedeo">
        Ustedeo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ustedeo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Usted" title="Usted">
        Usted
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       "Usted" is the formal second person singular pronoun in Salvadoran Castilian. "Usted" is used in addressing foreigners formally, for acquaintances, and in business settings. Unlike nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       , "usted" is not the dominant second person pronoun for addressing a person.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tuteo">
        Tuteo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tuteo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuteo" title="Tuteo">
        Tuteo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       "Tú" is hardly used; the use of tú is limited strictly to foreigners. It is used in addressing foreigners familiarly and when writing correspondence to foreigners (again in familiar contexts).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Salvadoran_Caliche.2FCaliche_Salvadore.C3.B1o">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Salvadoran_Caliche/Caliche_Salvadoreño">
        Salvadoran Caliche/Caliche Salvadoreño
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Salvadoran_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Salvadoran Caliche/Caliche Salvadoreño">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The definition for Caliche is an informal term for Salvadoran Spanish due to colloquialisms and unique indigenous lexical words that are different from Salvadoran Spanish. Caliche refers to the Nahuatl influenced dialect of Spanish spoken in El Salvador. Many words have gone through the process of deletion, vowel
       <a href="/wiki/Assimilation_(phonology)" title="Assimilation (phonology)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">
        epenthesis
       </a>
       to make it easier for the speaker to understand. Salvadorian Caliche is used across social classes, although professional individuals tend to avoid it because it is not considered "proper" Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, this table shows the difference between Standard Salvadoran Spanish and Caliche:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Español Salvadoreño
        </th>
        <th>
         Caliche
        </th>
        <th>
         English
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Acá, así es la situación
        </td>
        <td>
         Así es la onda
        </td>
        <td>
         This is the situation
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Está difícil
        </td>
        <td>
         Está yuca
        </td>
        <td>
         It is difficult
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Sabemos progresar
        </td>
        <td>
         Sabemos socarla
        </td>
        <td>
         We know how to progress
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Nos gusta salir a pasear
        </td>
        <td>
         Nos gusta chotiar
        </td>
        <td>
         We like to walk
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Está fácil
        </td>
        <td>
         Está chiche
        </td>
        <td>
         It is easy
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Words like this are not unique to El Salvador, and when heard by someone that is Salvadoran or from neighbouring countries they are understood. Nahuatl's influence appears in the word "chiche", which means "parte del seno de la mujer" (DRAE
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (breast area) from Nahuatl Chichi "mama, teta"). But chiche in El Salvador means "facil" ("que no requiere gran esfuerzo"/"easy—it does not require much effort"). Another word influenced by Nahuatl is the word
       <i>
        guishte
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that means a piece of broken glass. This word does not appear in any dictionary so its origin cannot be traced, but the only hypothesis behind this word was proposed by Pedro Geoffroy Rivas—an anthropologist, poet, and linguist—who believed that it came from the
       <a href="/wiki/Pipil_language" title="Pipil language">
        Pipil language
       </a>
       , since El Salvador's Spanish has been heavily influenced by it.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unfortunately, Caliche is not described in studies on Salvadoran Spanish. The philologist John M. Lipski points out that Centro American Spanish (including the Spanish spoken in El Salvador) lacks adequate sources for linguistic and literary research. Lipski further elaborates that such linguistic shortage indicates a possible generalization that in recent decades Salvadoran dialectology has failed to advance as rapidly as the comparative work in other Latin American nations.
      </p>
