    Plazas de soberanía
   </h1>

         The
         <i>
          plazas de soberanía
         </i>
         , plus
         <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
          Ceuta
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
          Melilla
         </a>
         , and
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albor%C3%A1n_Island" title="Alborán Island">
          Alborán Island
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <b>
         plazas de soberanía
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <small>
        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈplaθaz ðe soβeɾaˈni.a]
        </a>
       </span>
       , literally "places of sovereignty") are the
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       sovereign territories in
       <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
        North Africa
       </a>
       . These are separate pieces of land scattered along the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">
        Mediterranean
       </a>
       coast bordering
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       . The name refers to the fact that these territories have been a part of Spain since the formation of the modern Spanish State (1492–1556), and are distinguished from African territories obtained by Spain during the 19th and 20th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, a distinction was made between the so-called "major sovereign territories", comprising the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autonomous_cities_of_Spain" title="Autonomous cities of Spain">
        cities
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       , and the "minor sovereign territories", referring to a number of smaller enclaves and islands along the coast. In the present, the term refers mainly to the latter.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="629" data-file-width="981" height="141" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg/220px-Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg/330px-Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6c/Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg/440px-Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alhucemas_penon_1925.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Aerial view of the Peñón de Alhucemas c. 1925
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       and mainly following the conquest of
       <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">
        Granada
       </a>
       in 1492, forces of the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Castilian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       kingdoms conquered and maintained numerous posts in North Africa for trade and as a defence against
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbary_corsairs" title="Barbary corsairs">
        Barbary piracy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1415 the Portuguese conquered
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       . In 1481 the
       <a href="/wiki/Papal_bull" title="Papal bull">
        Papal bull
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aeterni_regis" title="Aeterni regis">
         Æterni regis
        </a>
       </i>
       had granted all land south of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       to Portugal. Only this archipelago and the possessions of
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Cruz_de_la_Mar_Peque%C3%B1a" title="Santa Cruz de la Mar Pequeña">
        Santa Cruz de la Mar Pequeña
       </a>
       (1476–1524),
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Melilla" title="Conquest of Melilla">
        conquered
       </a>
       by Pedro de Estopiñán in 1497),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_Cisneros" title="Villa Cisneros">
        Villa Cisneros
       </a>
       (founded in 1502 in current
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">
        Western Sahara
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mazalquivir" title="Mazalquivir">
        Mazalquivir
       </a>
       (1505),
       <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera" title="Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera">
        Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera
       </a>
       (1508),
       <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">
        Oran
       </a>
       (1509–1790),
       <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">
        Algiers
       </a>
       (1510–1529),
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9ja%C3%AFa" title="Béjaïa">
        Bugia
       </a>
       (1510–1554),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tripoli,_Libya" title="Tripoli, Libya">
        Tripoli
       </a>
       (1511–1551), and
       <a href="/wiki/Tunis" title="Tunis">
        Tunis
       </a>
       (1535–1569) remained as Spanish territory in Africa. Finally, following the independence of Portugal from Spain,
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       was ceded by Portugal to Spain in 1668.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1848, Spanish troops conquered the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islas_Chafarinas" title="Islas Chafarinas">
        Islas Chafarinas
       </a>
       . In the late 19th century, after the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Scramble_for_Africa" title="Scramble for Africa">
        Scramble for Africa
       </a>
       , European nations had taken over colonial control of most of the African continent. The
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Fez" title="Treaty of Fez">
        Treaty of Fez
       </a>
       (signed on 30 March, 1912) made most of Morocco a
       <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">
        protectorate
       </a>
       of France, while Spain assumed the role of
       <a href="/wiki/Protecting_power" title="Protecting power">
        protecting power
       </a>
       over the northern part,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Morocco
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When Spain relinquished its protectorate and recognized Morocco's independence in 1956, it did not give up these minor territories. Spain had held them well before the establishment of its protectorate.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 11 July, 2002, Morocco stationed six gendarmes on
       <a href="/wiki/Perejil_Island" title="Perejil Island">
        Perejil Island
       </a>
       , which was at the time a source of complaint by Spain. The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Armed_Forces" title="Spanish Armed Forces">
        Spanish Armed Forces
       </a>
       responded by launching a military operation code-named
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Operation_Romeo-Sierra" title="Operation Romeo-Sierra">
        Operation Romeo-Sierra
       </a>
       . The attack was carried out by Spanish commandos of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grupo_de_Operaciones_Especiales_(Spain)" title="Grupo de Operaciones Especiales (Spain)">
         Grupo de Operaciones Especiales
        </a>
       </i>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Navy" title="Spanish Navy">
        Spanish Navy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Air_Force" title="Spanish Air Force">
        Spanish Air Force
       </a>
       provided support; the six Moroccan navy cadets did not offer any resistance and were captured and evicted from the island. It has since been evacuated by both countries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ElPais_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ElPais-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>

      </p>

      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="848" data-file-width="1136" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg/220px-Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg/330px-Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg/440px-Velez_de_la_Gomera.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

         , seen from the Moroccan coast
        </div>

      <p>
       There are historically three
       <i>
        <b>
         plazas de soberanía
        </b>
       </i>
       :
      </p>

         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
          <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa&amp;params=35_12_54_N_3_53_47_W_">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_islands">
        Other islands
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other islands">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Apart from the three distinct groups, there are two other islands usually considered within the
       <i>
        plazas de soberanía
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The disputed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_Perejil" title="Isla Perejil">
        Isla Perejil
       </a>
       , a small uninhabited islet close to Ceuta, considered by Spain to be a part of Ceuta and not a territory in its own right.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tremlett_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tremlett-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isla_de_Albor%C3%A1n" title="Isla de Alborán">
        Isla de Alborán
       </a>
       , another small island in the western Mediterranean, about 50 kilometres (31.05 miles) from the African coast and 90 kilometres (55.92 miles) from Europe, is attached to the municipality of
       <a href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa" title="Almería">
        Almería
       </a>
       on the Iberian Peninsula.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_geography">
        Political geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Political geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        plazas de soberanía
       </i>
       are small islands and peninsulas off the coast of Morocco (the only peninsula,
       <a href="/wiki/Pe%C3%B1%C3%B3n_de_V%C3%A9lez_de_la_Gomera" title="Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera">
        Peñón de Vélez de la Gomera
       </a>
       , was an island until a 1934 storm formed a
       <a href="/wiki/Tombolo" title="Tombolo">
        sand bridge
       </a>
       with the mainland). They are guarded by military garrisons and administered directly by the
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Spain" title="Government of Spain">
        Spanish central government
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Like Ceuta and Melilla, they are a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       , therefore also part of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       , and their currency is the
       <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">
        euro
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Claimants">
        Claimants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Plazas_de_soberan%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Claimants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       claims sovereignty over the Spanish North African territories, plus the autonomous cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       .
      </p>
