       <b>
        Papiamento
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         English:
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>

            ɑː
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
          </span>
          ,
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
          </span>
          -/
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lpd_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lpd-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        Papiamentu
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         English:
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /-
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">
            uː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_languages" title="Portuguese-based creole languages">
        Portuguese-based creole language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_West_Indies" title="Dutch West Indies">
        Dutch West Indies
       </a>
       . It is the most-widely spoken language on the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/ABC_islands_(Lesser_Antilles)" title="ABC islands (Lesser Antilles)">
        ABC islands
       </a>
       , having official status in
       <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">
        Aruba
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">
        Curaçao
       </a>
       . The language is also recognized in
       <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">
        Bonaire
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Dutch
       </a>
       government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-languages_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-languages-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Papiamento is derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Africa" title="Languages of Africa">
        African
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese languages
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with some influences from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Indigenous American languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa,_Bottelier.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1067" data-file-width="1600" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/220px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/330px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg/440px-Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa%2C_Bottelier.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Monument_and_crypt_in_Morada_Santa,_Bottelier.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Burial site and monument to Doctor
         <a href="/wiki/Moises_Frumencio_da_Costa_Gomez" title="Moises Frumencio da Costa Gomez">
          Moises Frumencio da Costa Gomez
         </a>
         , first prime minister of the
         <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">
          Netherlands Antilles
         </a>
         , with a message enscribed in Papiamento: "No hasi ku otro loke bo no ke pa otro hasi ku bo", roughly meaning: "Do unto others as you would have others do unto you.”
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The precise historical origins of Papiamento have not been established. Historical constraints, core vocabulary and grammatical features that Papiamento shares with
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
        Cape Verdean Creole
       </a>
       suggest that the basic ingredients are Portuguese,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that other influences occurred at a later time (17th and 18th centuries, respectively).
      </p>
      <p>
       Its parent language is
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">
        Iberian
       </a>
       for sure, but scholars dispute whether Papiamento
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_languages" title="Portuguese-based creole languages">
        is derived from Portuguese
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">
        from Spanish
       </a>
       . A summary of the century-long debate on Papiamento's origins is provided in Jacobs (2009a).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name of the language itself comes from
       <i>
        papia
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pap(e)o
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pap(e)ar
       </i>
       ("to chat", "to talk"), a word present in Portuguese (
       <i>
        um papo
       </i>
       , "a chat") and colloquial Spanish; compare with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kristang_language" title="Kristang language">
         Papiá Kristang
        </a>
       </i>
       ("Christian talk"), a Portuguese-based creole of
       <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
        Malaysia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
        Cape Verdean Creole
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        papiâ
       </i>
       ("to talk"), or elsewhere in the Caribbean (Puerto Rico, Dominican Republic, Cuba)
       <i>
        papear
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       —"to talk excessively" (and without sense) or "to stutter" (but also, "to eat" or "food". Castilian Spanish
       <i>
        papeo
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Portuguese
       <i>
        papar
       </i>
       is a children's term for "to eat").
      </p>
      <p>
       Spain claimed dominion over the islands in the 15th century, but made little use of them. Portuguese merchants had been trading extensively in the West Indies, and with the Union with Castille, this trade extended to the Castillian West Indies, as the Spanish kings favoured the free movement of people. In 1634, the
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_West_India_Company" title="Dutch West India Company">
        Dutch West India Company
       </a>
       (WIC) took possession of the islands, deporting most of the small remaining
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arawak_peoples" title="Arawak peoples">
        Arawak
       </a>
       and Spanish population to the continent, and turned them into the hub of the Dutch slave trade between Africa and the Caribbean.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first evidence of widespread use of Papiamento in Aruba can be seen through the Curaçao official documents in the early 18th century. In the 19th century, most materials in the islands were written in Papiamento including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic" title="Roman Catholic">
        Roman Catholic
       </a>
       schoolbooks and hymnals. The first Papiamento newspaper was published in 1871 and titled
       <i>
        Civilisado
       </i>
       (The Civilized).
       <i>
        Civilizado
       </i>
       (stress on /za/) is Spanish and Portuguese for "civilized" but can also be understood as having a suppressed final "r" from the word
       <i>
        civilizador
       </i>
       (stress on /do/) ("civilizer").
      </p>
      <p>
       An outline of the competing theories is provided below.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Local_development_theory">
        Local development theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Local development theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Unreferenced" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">

         </div>

            sources
           </a>
          </b>

      <p>
       There are various local development theories. One such theory proposes that Papiamento developed in the Caribbean from an original Portuguese-African
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgin
       </a>
       used for communication between African slaves and Portuguese slavetraders, with later Dutch and Spanish (and even some Arawak) influences. Another theory is that Papiamento first evolved from the use in this region since 1499 of 'lenguas' and the first Repopulation of the ABC islands by the Spanish by the Cédula real decreed in November 1525, in which Juan Martinez de Ampués, factor of Española, had been granted the right to repopulate the depopulated Islas inútiles of Oroba, Islas de los Gigantes and Buon Aire. Columbus took ten natives back to Europe precisely so that they could acquire knowledge of the Spanish language and culture, a policy he maintained throughout future voyages. On his return to America, Columbus was accompanied by two interpreters ('lenguas', literally, 'tongues'), Alonso de Cáceres and a young boy from Guanahani (the Bahamas) who was given the name Diego Colón. Subsequent expeditions followed the same pattern. In 1499 Alonso de Ojeda, Juan de la Cosa and Amerigo Vespuccio took captives to serve as
       <i>
        lenguas
       </i>
       . Ojeda actually married his native interpreter and guide, Isabel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The evolution of Papiamento continued under the Dutch Colonization under the influence of the 16th century 'Dutch'/European/Native American (ABC islands) and 'Portuguese'/Native American (Brazilian) languages with the second Repopulation of these ABC islands under
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Stuyvesant" title="Peter Stuyvesant">
        Peter Stuyvesant
       </a>
       , who arrived here from the ex-Dutch Brazilian colonies.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Judaeo-Portuguese population of the ABC islands increased substantially after 1654, when the Portuguese recovered the
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Brazil" title="Dutch Brazil">
        Dutch-held territories in Northeast
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Colonial_Brazil" title="Colonial Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       – causing most of the Portuguese-speaking Jews in those lands to flee from religious persecution. The precise role of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jew" title="Sephardic Jew">
        Sephardic Jews
       </a>
       in the early development is unclear, but it is certain
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
        Jews
       </a>
       played a prominent role in the later development of Papiamento. Many early residents of Curaçao were Sephardic Jews either from Portugal, Spain, or Portuguese Brazil. Therefore, it can be assumed
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       that
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       was brought to the island of Curaçao, where it gradually spread to other parts of the community. As the Jewish community became the prime merchants and traders in the area, business and everyday trading was conducted in Papiamento with some
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       influences. While various nations owned the island and official languages changed with ownership, Papiamento became the constant language of the residents.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="European_and_African_origin_theory">
        European and African origin theory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European and African origin theory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Peter Stuyvesant's appointment to the ABC islands followed his service in Brazil. He brought Indians, soldiers, etc. from Brazil to Curaçao as well as to
       <a href="/wiki/New_Netherland" title="New Netherland">
        New Netherland
       </a>
       . In Stuyvesant's Resolution Book, document #4b in the Curaçao Papers presents the multi-ethnic makeup of the garrison and the use of local Indians as cowboys: "... whereas the number of Indians, together with those of Aruba and Bonnairo, have increased here by half, and we have learned that they frequently ride ..." They communicated with each other in 'Papiamento' a language originating when the first Europeans began to arrive on these islands under Ojeda, Juan de Ampues, Bejarano and mixing with the natives. Stuyvesant also took some
       <a href="/wiki/Esopus_Wars" title="Esopus Wars">
        Esopus Indians
       </a>
       captives in New Netherland and brought them as slaves to Curaçao. There was little Dutch government activity in the management of DWI because during the period 1568–1648, they were actively fighting for their independence and were not in a position to manage their colonies.
      </p>
      <p>
       A more recent theory holds that the origins of Papiamento lie in the Afro-Portuguese creoles that arose almost a century earlier, in the west coast of Africa and in the
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Cape_Verde" title="Portuguese Cape Verde">
        Portuguese Cape Verde
       </a>
       islands. From the 16th to the late 17th century, most of the slaves taken to the Caribbean came from Portuguese trading posts ("factories") in those regions. Around those ports there developed several Portuguese-African pidgins and creoles, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Creole" title="Guinea-Bissau Creole">
        Guinea-Bissau Creole
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mina_language_(India)" title="Mina language (India)">
        Mina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
        Cape Verdean Creole
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angolar_language" title="Angolar language">
        Angolar
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guene_language&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guene language (page does not exist)">
        Guene
       </a>
       . The latter bears strong resemblances to Papiamento. According to this theory, Papiamento was derived from those pre-existing pidgins/creoles, especially Guene, which were brought to the ABC islands by slaves and/or traders from Cape Verde and West Africa.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some specifically claim that the Afro-Portuguese mother language of Papiamento arose from a mixture of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mina_language_(India)" title="Mina language (India)">
        Mina
       </a>
       pidgin/creole (a mixture of Cape Verdean pidgin/creole with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Twi_language" title="Twi language">
        Twi
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angolar_language" title="Angolar language">
        Angolar
       </a>
       creole (derived from languages of Angola and
       <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">
        Congo
       </a>
       ). Proponents of this theory of Papiamento contend that it can easily be compared and linked with other Portuguese creoles, especially the African ones (namely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forro_language" title="Forro language">
        Forro
       </a>
       , Guinea-Bissau Creole, and the Cape Verdean Creole). For instance, compare
       <i>
        mi
       </i>
       ("I" in Cape Verdean Creole and Papiamento) or
       <i>
        bo
       </i>
       (meaning
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       in both creoles).
       <i>
        Mi
       </i>
       is from the Portuguese
       <i>
        mim
       </i>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">
         [mĩ]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) "me", and
       <i>
        bo
       </i>
       is from Portuguese
       <i>
        vós
       </i>
       "you".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of "b" instead of "v" is very common in the African Portuguese Creoles (probably deriving from the pronunciation of Portuguese settlers in Africa, numerous from
       <a href="/wiki/Norte_Region,_Portugal" title="Norte Region, Portugal">
        Northern Portugal
       </a>
       ). However, because of the similarities between Portuguese and Spanish, it can also be argued that these two words derive from Spanish "mi" and "vos" (usually pronounced
       <i>
        bos
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Papiamento is, in some degree, intelligible with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_creole" title="Cape Verdean creole">
        Cape Verdean creoles
       </a>
       and could be explained by the immigration of Portuguese Sephardic Jews from Cape Verde to these Caribbean islands, although this same fact could also be used by dissenters to explain a later Portuguese influence on an already existing Spanish-based creole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another comparison is the use of the verb
       <i>
        ta
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        taba ta
       </i>
       from vernacular Portuguese
       <i>
        tá
       </i>
       (an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphesis" title="Aphesis">
        aphesis
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       , "to be" or
       <i>
        está
       </i>
       , "it is") with verbs where Portuguese does and with others where it does not use it: "
       <i>
        Mi ta
       </i>
       + verb" or "
       <i>
        Mi taba ta
       </i>
       + verb", also the rule in the
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Vicente_Creole" title="São Vicente Creole">
        São Vicente Creole
       </a>
       and other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barlavento_Creoles" title="Barlavento Creoles">
        Barlavento
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
        Cape Verdean Creoles
       </a>
       . These issues can also be seen in other Portuguese Creoles (Martinus 1996; see also Fouse 2002 and McWhorter 2000), but some are also found in colloquial Spanish.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_and_historical_ties_with_Upper_Guinea_Portuguese_Creole">
        Linguistic and historical ties with Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Linguistic and historical ties with Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Current research on the origins of Papiamento focuses specifically on the linguistic and historical relationships between Papiamento and Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole as spoken on the Santiago island of Cape Verde and in Guinea-Bissau and Casamance. Elaborating on comparisons done by Martinus (1996) and Quint (2000),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jacobs (2008,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       2009a, 2009b
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) defends the hypothesis that Papiamento is a relexified offshoot of an early Upper Guinea Portuguese Creole variety, transferred from Senegambia to Curaçao in the second half of the 17th century, a period in which the Dutch controlled the harbour of Gorée, just below the tip of the Cape Verde Peninsula. On Curaçao, this variety underwent internal changes as well as contact-induced changes at all levels of the grammar (though particularly in the lexicon) due to contact with Spanish and, to a lesser extent, Dutch as well as with a variety of Kwa and Bantu languages. These changes notwithstanding, the morpho-syntactic framework of Papiamento is still remarkably close to that of the Upper Guinea Creoles of Cape Verde and Guinea-Bissau/Casamance.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_status">
        Present status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Present status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many Papiamento speakers are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multilingual" title="Multilingual">
        multilingual
       </a>
       and are also able to speak Dutch,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       and Spanish. Papiamento has been an official language of
       <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">
        Aruba
       </a>
       since May, 2003.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the former
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands_Antilles" title="Netherlands Antilles">
        Netherlands Antilles
       </a>
       (which at the time comprised
       <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">
        Bonaire
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">
        Curaçao
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Saba" title="Saba">
        Saba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sint_Eustatius" title="Sint Eustatius">
        Sint Eustatius
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sint_Maarten" title="Sint Maarten">
        Sint Maarten
       </a>
       ) Papiamento was made an official language on March 7, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/Dissolution_of_the_Netherlands_Antilles" title="Dissolution of the Netherlands Antilles">
        its dissolution
       </a>
       , the language's official status was confirmed in the newly formed
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Netherlands" title="Caribbean Netherlands">
        Caribbean Netherlands
       </a>
       (part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Netherlands
       </a>
       proper, and compromising Bonaire, Saba and Sint Eustatius),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until January 1, 2011; since then, Bonaire is the only portion of the Caribbean Netherlands in which it is recognized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-languages_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-languages-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Papiamento is also spoken elsewhere in the
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Netherlands
       </a>
       , particularly on Saba and Sint Eustatius, and on St. Maarten, by immigrants from Aruba, Bonaire and Curaçao.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
        Venezuelan Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       are constant influences today.
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        Code-switching
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexical_borrowing" title="Lexical borrowing">
        lexical borrowing
       </a>
       between Papiamento, Spanish, Dutch and English among native speakers is common. This is perceived as a threat to the further development of Papiamento due to a
       <a href="/wiki/Language_ideology" title="Language ideology">
        language ideology
       </a>
       that is committed to preserving the authentic African or Creole "feel" of Papiamento.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_dialects">
        Distribution and dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Distribution and dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Papiamento has two main dialects, one in
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Aruba" title="Languages of Aruba">
        Aruba
       </a>
       and one in
       <a href="/wiki/Bonaire#Languages" title="Bonaire">
        Bonaire
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao#Languages" title="Curaçao">
        Curaçao
       </a>
       , with lexical and intonational differences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are also minor differences between Curaçao and Bonaire.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spoken (Aruban) Papiamento sounds much more like Spanish. The most apparent difference between the two dialects is given away in the name difference. Whereas Bonaire and Curaçao opted for a phonology-based spelling, Aruba uses an etymology-based spelling. Many words in Aruba end with "o" while that same word ends with "u" in Bonaire and Curaçao. And even in Curaçao, the use of the u-ending is still more pronounced among the Sephardic Jewish population. Similarly, there is also a difference between the usage of "k" in Bonaire and Curaçao and "c" in Aruba.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stress_and_tone">
        Stress and tone
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stress and tone">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Polysyllabic words that end in vowels are stressed on the next-to-last syllable; most words ending in consonants are stressed on the final syllable. There are exceptions. When a word deviates from these rules, the stressed vowel should be indicated by an acute accent mark. The accent marks are often omitted in casual writing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Papiamento words have distinct tone patterns. According to recent linguistic research, there are two classes of words: those that typically have rising pitch on the stressed syllable, and those that typically have falling pitch on the stressed syllable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter category includes most of the two-syllable verbs in the language. Any given word's tone contours may change depending on discursive factors such as whether the sentence is affirmative, interrogative, or imperative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Altering tone in Papiamento can distinguish meaning and grammatical function: compare noun 'para' (PA-ra:
       <i>
        bird
       </i>
       ) with verb 'para' (pa-RA:
       <i>
        stand
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        stop
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2011)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Independently from tone, stress can also be altered: compare '
       <b>
        pa
       </b>
       -ra' (
       <i>
        stand
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        stop
       </i>
       ) with 'pa-
       <b>
        ra'
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        stopped
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        standing
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Papiamento/u uses prosodic
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        accent
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        Tone
       </a>
       (with stress) is largely dependent on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_function" title="Grammatical function">
        grammatical function
       </a>
       of the word in sentence. Compare:
      </p>

      <p>
       The following are the grammatical rules of Papiamento intonation:
      </p>
      <p>
       -Verbs usually have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rising_tone" title="Rising tone">
        rising tone
       </a>
       ; a following adverb receives high intonation (ex. 'bira RONT:'
       <i>
        turn around
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       -Nouns (substantives) and adjectives usually have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falling_tone" title="Falling tone">
        falling tone
       </a>
       , a following adjective receives low intonation (ex. 'PA-lu haltu:'
       <i>
        tall tree
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       -In words of more than three syllables, grammatical tone or accent will fall on the last stressed syllable. The first stressed syllable receives the opposite tone for contrast: compare noun 'kini-kini' (kini-KI-ni):
       <i>
        falcon
       </i>
       with adverb 'poko-poko' (PO-ko-poko):
       <i>
        slowly
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       -An adverb has rising tone, so a following adjective receives high tone (ex. 'masha BON'
       <i>
        very good
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       !!! – The adverbs 'bon' (
       <i>
        good
       </i>
       ) and 'mal' (
       <i>
        bad
       </i>
       ), even though they are adjectives, in grammar will always have adverbial, rising tone character (ex. 'bon ha-SI:'
       <i>
        well-done
       </i>
       ). They will always behave like adverbs, even when they qualify nouns (ex. 'bon DI-a:'
       <i>
        good day
       </i>
       ). They behave like adverbs even when doubled for emphasis ('bon-BON:'
       <i>
        very good
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       (Note: in all above examples,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primary_stress" title="Primary stress">
        primary stress
       </a>
       remains on the second word, while
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_stress" title="Secondary stress">
        secondary stress
       </a>
       remains on the first word, independently of tone changes. It is thus more accurate to transcribe 'PA-lu
       <b>
        hal
       </b>
       -tu' and 'bira
       <b>
        RONT'
       </b>
       , with
       <b>
        bold typing
       </b>
       indicating
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       and CAPITAL LETTERS indicating
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_tone" title="High tone">
        high tone
       </a>
       syllables. Unstressed syllables' tone is dependent on contact syllables.)
      </p>
      <p>
       -The
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Particle_of_negation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Particle of negation (page does not exist)">
        particle of negation
       </a>
       'no' always receives rising tone: the following verb is inevitably raised in pitch: compare 'mi ta
       <b>
        PA
       </b>
       -pia' (
       <i>
        I speak
       </i>
       ) and 'mi no TA
       <b>
        PA
       </b>
       -pia' (
       <i>
        I do not speak
       </i>
       ). This negating pitch-raise is crucial and is retained even after
       <a href="/wiki/Synalepha" title="Synalepha">
        contraction
       </a>
       of the particle in informal speech: 'mi'n TA papia' ("I don't speak")
      </p>
      <p>
       It is theorized that the unusual presence of both stress and tone in Papiamento is an inheritance of African languages (which use tone) and Portuguese (which has stress)
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Papiamento&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most of the vocabulary is derived from Spanish and Portuguese and most of the time the real origin is unknown due to the great similarity between the two Iberian languages and the adaptations required by Papiamento. A 100-Swadesh List of Papiamento can be found online.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Linguistic studies have shown that roughly two thirds of the words in Papiamento's present vocabulary are of
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian
       </a>
       origin, a quarter are of Dutch origin, some of Native American origin, and the rest come from other tongues. A recent study by Buurt &amp; Joubert inventoried several hundred words of indigenous Arawak origins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of words of Iberian and Roman, Latin origin, which are impossible to label as either Portuguese or Spanish:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        por fabor/sea asina di = please – Spanish/Portuguese,
        <i>
         por favor
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        señora = mrs, madam – Spanish,
        <i>
         señora
        </i>
        ; Portuguese,
        <i>
         senhora
        </i>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        kuá? = which? Spanish,
        <i>
         ¿Cuál?
        </i>
        ; Portuguese,
        <i>
         Qual?
        </i>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        Kuantu? = how much? – Spanish,
        <i>
         ¿Cuánto?
        </i>
        ; Portuguese,
        <i>
         Quanto?
        </i>
        ;
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       While the presence of word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       can easily be traced to Portuguese, the
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongization
       </a>
       of some vowels is characteristic of Spanish. The use of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       (rather than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       ) is difficult to interpret; although the two are separate
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       in standard Portuguese, they merge in the dialects of northern Portugal, just like they do
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology" title="Spanish phonology">
        in Spanish
       </a>
       . Also, a sound-shift could have occurred in the direction of Spanish, whose influence on Papiamento came later than that of Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other words can have dual origin, and certainly dual influence. For instance:
       <i>
        subrino
       </i>
       (nephew):
       <i>
        sobrinho
       </i>
       in Portuguese,
       <i>
        sobrino
       </i>
       in Spanish. The pronunciation of "o" as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       is traceable to Portuguese, while the use of "n" instead of "nh" (IPA
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɲ/
       </span>
       ) in the ending "-no", relates to Spanish.
      </p>
