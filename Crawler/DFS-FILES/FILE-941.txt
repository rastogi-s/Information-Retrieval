   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    French language in Vietnam
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
       </b>
       was the
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official language
       </a>
       of
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
         Vietnam
        </a>
       </b>
       from the beginning of French colonial rule in the mid-19th century until independence under the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geneva_Conference_(1954)" title="Geneva Conference (1954)">
        Geneva Accords of 1954
       </a>
       , and maintained
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       official status in
       <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">
        South Vietnam
       </a>
       until its collapse in 1975. Vietnam contains the largest of the three Francophone communities in Southeast Asia, the others being found in
       <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">
        Laos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">
        Cambodia
       </a>
       . French is spoken by over 5% of the population and is sometimes used in international relations and education.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=French_language_in_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The French language's presence in Vietnam began in the 18th century when French explorers and merchants began sailing near the
       <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">
        Indochina
       </a>
       coast. When the French replaced the Portuguese as the primary European power in Southeast Asia in the 1790s by
       <a href="/wiki/French_assistance_to_Nguy%E1%BB%85n_%C3%81nh" title="French assistance to Nguyễn Ánh">
        helping to unify Vietnam under the Nguyen Dynasty
       </a>
       and later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cochinchina_campaign" title="Cochinchina campaign">
        colonizing Southern Vietnam
       </a>
       , they introduced the French language to locals. French became the governing language of
       <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">
        French Indochina
       </a>
       , which included present-day Vietnam,
       <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">
        Laos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">
        Cambodia
       </a>
       . Many Vietnamese began learning French, which replaced the native
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       and royal court
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       languages and eventually the Vietnamese language's official script was in the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
        Latin alphabet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The building of missionary and government schools spread the French language among educated Vietnamese and it soon became the language of the elite classes by the end of the nineteenth century. By the early twentieth century, the French language began spreading to the urban masses and became the primary language of education. A French
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pidgin_language" title="Pidgin language">
        pidgin
       </a>
       called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%A2y_B%E1%BB%93i" title="Tây Bồi">
        Tây Bồi
       </a>
       was developed among Vietnamese servants in French households and those who spoke partial French. Nevertheless, at the French language's height in Vietnam between the 1900s and 1940s, a large number of Vietnamese did not speak French well or learn the language and some revolutionaries refused to learn the colonial language, though ironically speeches and papers written to promote independence were written in French. During
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Empire" title="Japanese Empire">
        Japan
       </a>
       briefly occupied Vietnam and established Vietnamese as the sole official and educational language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c309_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-c309-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2015)">
           verification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of the French language in Vietnam slowly began to decline after World War II as revolutionary movements increased and their works began to be written more in Vietnamese. Poorer and generally, more rural populations began to resist French rule and guerrilla forces, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viet_Minh" title="Viet Minh">
        Viet Minh
       </a>
       attacked the French and sparked the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Indochina_War" title="First Indochina War">
        First Indochina War
       </a>
       . The French language however, continued its presence in government, education and media in areas not held by the Viet Minh. At the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Dien_Bien_Phu" title="Battle of Dien Bien Phu">
        Battle of Dien Bien Phu
       </a>
       in 1954, the Viet Minh defeated the French and Vietnam gained its independence, though the nation was soon divided into a communist, Soviet-oriented
       <a href="/wiki/North_Vietnam" title="North Vietnam">
        north
       </a>
       , and pro-French, pro-U.S. government in the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Vietnam" title="South Vietnam">
        south
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-guerreindochinenewsreel_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-guerreindochinenewsreel-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fearing persecution by the communist government, hundreds of thousands fled to the south, including French-educated and speaking elite. Despite the
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
        Vietnam War
       </a>
       erupting shortly afterwards, French continued a healthy presence in South Vietnam, where it was an administrative and educational language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-k_early_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-k_early-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (May 2015)">
           verification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The sharpest decline of the French language in Vietnam was after the
       <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_Saigon" title="Fall of Saigon">
        Fall of Saigon
       </a>
       in 1975 as the communist government imposed Vietnamese as the sole official and educational language on the entire nation, including the south, which was in a transitional phase until 1976.
      </p>
      <p>
       The number of students receiving their education in French in Vietnam declined to about 40% by the 1980s and continued to decline well into the 1990s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, a large number of French-speakers who were anti-communist fled Vietnam and immigrated to nations such as the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       (most particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
        Ontario
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       . As of 2000, only about 5% of students received their education in French. Meanwhile, the rise of the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       caused a further decline in the status of French in Vietnam as English became seen as the language of international trade, commerce and diplomacy. While English is now the most studied foreign language, in recent years French has been revived in Vietnamese education and the nation is a member of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Francophonie" title="La Francophonie">
        La Francophonie
       </a>
       . Furthermore, French has somewhat of a diplomatic language position in Vietnam.
      </p>
      <p>
       In higher education and professions, French remains the principal language and many educated Vietnamese continue to speak French, especially among the elderly population, a legacy of the colonial era. Recently, the number of secondary schools using French as main language of instruction has increased in many localities and schools in major cities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vietnam has also recently become a destination for students from other parts of Asia to come and study the French language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vietnam_French_language_training_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vietnam_French_language_training-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Communities of returned refugees from France and Quebec as well as students who have studied in Francophone nations have also added to the French-speaking population of Vietnam.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialect_characteristics">
        Dialect characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=French_language_in_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialect characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Vietnamese French is based on standard French, but contains words that have been influenced not only by Vietnamese but also by Chinese and English, the latter due to U.S. presence in the south during the Vietnam War. Additionally, the pronoun
       <i>
        vous
       </i>
       used as the formal and plural form of you is used not only to address elders but also to adults of the same age unlike Standard French, where
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       is used among adult friends of the same age.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite these minor differences, the form of French taught at Vietnamese schools and colleges is that of standard French.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Vietnamese">
        Influence on Vietnamese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=French_language_in_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence on Vietnamese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ga-saigon2.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="800" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ga-saigon2.jpg/150px-Ga-saigon2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ga-saigon2.jpg/225px-Ga-saigon2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Ga-saigon2.jpg/300px-Ga-saigon2.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ga-saigon2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saigon_Railway_Station" title="Saigon Railway Station">
          Saigon Railway Station
         </a>
         sign features a loanword from French.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Vietnamese language contains a significant number of French loanwords and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Placenames" title="Placenames">
        placenames
       </a>
       . The majority of words having French origin are those relating to objects, food and technology introduced to the Vietnamese during the colonial era. Additionally, the
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_alphabet" title="Vietnamese alphabet">
        Vietnamese alphabet
       </a>
       came to be written in a Latin-based script instead of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chu_Nom" title="Chu Nom">
        Chinese script
       </a>
       traditionally used by the former royal court. It became heavily promoted by the French colonial government, which got rid of Chinese influence on the Vietnamese education system by imposing a
       <a href="/wiki/Education_in_France" title="Education in France">
        French-based system
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Media">
        Media
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=French_language_in_Vietnam&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Media">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Despite the decline of French in the late 1970s to 2000s, Vietnam continues to have a French-media market and presence. A small number of French-language newspapers used to circulate in the country, most dominately the now extinct
       <i>
        Saigon Eco
       </i>
       and the only remaining state-owned paper -
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le_Courrier_du_Vietnam" title="Le Courrier du Vietnam">
         Le Courrier du Vietnam
        </a>
       </i>
       . News broadcasts as well as television programs in French are shown on Vietnamese television channels daily. Radio broadcasts in French are also present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
