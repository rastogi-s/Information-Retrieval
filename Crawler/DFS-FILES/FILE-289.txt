      <p>
       <b>
        Argant
       </b>
       is an ancient variety of red
       <a href="/wiki/Wine" title="Wine">
        wine
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Grape" title="Grape">
        grape
       </a>
       . It originated in Spain and may have been taken to eastern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       by the Romans. It was briefly popular, but little of it remains today. It used to be common in southwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
        Austria
       </a>
       under the name Gänsfüßer ("goose-feet").
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Argant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As the alternative name Espagnol suggests, tradition says that Argant came from Spain. It appears on a list of 14 varieties recommended for winemaking in the Jura in 1774 and plantings seem to have expanded over the next century, but were never very extensive and the story seems to have been one of decline since then.
      </p>
      <p>
       One claim to fame is as a parent, with
       <a href="/wiki/Pinot_noir" title="Pinot noir">
        Pinot noir
       </a>
       , of the
       <a href="/wiki/Burgundy_wine" title="Burgundy wine">
        Burgundian
       </a>
       variety
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_(grape)" title="César (grape)">
        César
       </a>
       , which inherits color and tannins from Argant. Unfortunately it also inherited Argant's early bud break, which makes it susceptible to frost. César (also known as 'Romain') takes its name from the tradition that it was brought to Burgundy by the Romans - since Pinot is native to eastern France it is more likely that if any grape was carried by the legions, it would have been Argant rather than its offspring.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_and_Wines">
        Distribution and Wines
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Argant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Distribution and Wines">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The variety of synonyms point to Argant having been planted across a wide area of Western Europe, but little survives today. The Jura was always something of a stronghold, and there's almost none left there now.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vine_and_Viticulture">
        Vine and Viticulture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Argant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vine and Viticulture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One reason for Argant's popularity in the 19th century was its good resistance to mildew.
      </p>
