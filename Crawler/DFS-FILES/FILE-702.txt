      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Department of Antioquia
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [anˈtjokja]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is one of the 32
       <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Colombia" title="Departments of Colombia">
        departments of Colombia
       </a>
       , located in the central northwestern part of Colombia with a narrow section that borders the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">
        Caribbean Sea
       </a>
       . Most of its territory is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mountainous" title="Mountainous">
        mountainous
       </a>
       with some
       <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">
        valleys
       </a>
       , much of which is part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andes
       </a>
       mountain range. Antioquia has been part of many territorial divisions of former countries created over the present day territory of Colombia, and prior to the
       <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Constitution_of_1886" title="Colombian Constitution of 1886">
        Colombian Constitution of 1886
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_State" title="Antioquia State">
        Antioquia State
       </a>
       had its own sovereign government.
      </p>
      <p>
       The department covers an area of 63,612 km² (24,427 sq mi), and has a population of 5,819,358 (2006 estimate); 6.6 million (2010 estimate). Antioquia borders with the
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_Department" title="Córdoba Department">
        Córdoba Department
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">
        Caribbean Sea
       </a>
       to the north,
       <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Chocó Department">
        Chocó
       </a>
       to the west, to the east it borders the departments of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolivar_Department" title="Bolivar Department">
        Bolivar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Santander_Department" title="Santander Department">
        Santander
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Boyac%C3%A1_Department" title="Boyacá Department">
        Boyaca
       </a>
       , and to the south the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       is Antioquia's capital city, and the second-largest city in the country. Other important towns are
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_de_Antioquia" title="Santa Fe de Antioquia">
        Santa Fe de Antioquia
       </a>
       , the old capital located on the Cauca, and
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Berr%C3%ADo" title="Puerto Berrío">
        Puerto Berrío
       </a>
       on the Magdalena.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Antioquia is the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Colombian_Departments_by_area" title="List of Colombian Departments by area">
        sixth-largest Department of Colombia
       </a>
       . It is predominantly mountainous, crossed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Central (Colombia)">
        Cordillera Central
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Occidental_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Occidental (Colombia)">
        Cordillera Occidental
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andes
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Central (Colombia)">
        Cordillera Central
       </a>
       , further divides to form the Aburrá valley, in which the capital
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       is located. The
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Central (Colombia)">
        Cordillera Central
       </a>
       forms the plateaus of
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Rosa_de_Osos" title="Santa Rosa de Osos">
        Santa Rosa de Osos
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rionegro,_Antioquia" title="Rionegro, Antioquia">
        Rionegro
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite 80% of the department's territory being mountainous, Antioquia also has lowlands in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bajo_Cauca" title="Bajo Cauca">
        Bajo Cauca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Magdalena_Medio_Antioquia" title="Magdalena Medio Antioquia">
        Magdalena Medio
       </a>
       , and eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Sons%C3%B3n" title="Sonsón">
        Sonsón
       </a>
       as well as a coast on the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">
        Caribbean Sea
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Urab%C3%A1_Antioquia" title="Urabá Antioquia">
        Urabá
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This area has a tropical climate and is of high strategical importance due to its location.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Native_people_of_Antioquia">
        Native people of Antioquia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Native people of Antioquia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Before Spanish colonization, a large tribe, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muisca_people" title="Muisca people">
        Muisca
       </a>
       , inhabited this part of modern Colombia. There is still much uncertainty about the origin of the tribes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Antioquia was primarily populated by Caribs, although some scattered groups of Muisca were present in the
       <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_Province" title="Darién Province">
        Darién
       </a>
       region (in modern-day
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       ), a coastal region in the far north of Antioquia. However, there are no historical records for these groups of Muisca in Antioquia.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Caribs present in Antioquia were further classified into smaller groups, called
       <i>
        families
       </i>
       . Some of the most prominent native families in the region include the Catías, Nutabes and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taham%C3%ADes_Indians" title="Tahamíes Indians">
        Tahamíes
       </a>
       , which all inhabited the central region of Antioquia.
      </p>
      <p>
       An important group that inhabited southern Antioquia was the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quimbaya" title="Quimbaya">
        Quimbaya
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There were other groups, but the Quimbaya, Carib and Muisca tribes were the most prominent groupings that were found by the
       <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">
        conquistadors
       </a>
       upon their arrival in Antioquia. The Quimbayas had little to do with the evolution of the department, because
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Robledo_(conquistador)" title="Jorge Robledo (conquistador)">
        Jorge Robledo
       </a>
       , the main conquistador of Antioquia, quickly subjected the few Quimbaya that he found and the rest disappeared.
      </p>
      <p>
       The history was centered then in the turbulent relationships of the Spaniards with the Caribs. Despite the number of Caribs and their well-known warring culture, they would end up dominated or exterminated by the Spaniards in the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_(military)" title="Conquest (military)">
        conquest
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Colonization" title="Colonization">
        colonization
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       During these processes bloody confrontations were presented that caused the surviving natives to disperse, and even commit suicide, before they were subjected. Many of the survivors fled to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Choc%C3%B3" title="Department of Chocó">
        department of Chocó
       </a>
       . Thus, in Antioquia, the natives disappeared almost completely. At present, the autochthon population of the department of Antioquia scarcely reaches 0.5% of the total population.
      </p>

      <p>
       A debate, centered around the apparently significant Jewish origin of Antioquians, took place from mid-nineteenth century to the twentieth century. Others, later pointed to Basque origins as a way to understand the population's idiosyncrasies. Prominent among these, were two American historians: Everett Hagen and Leonard Kasdan. Hagen looked at the telephone directory in Medellin in 1957 and found that 15% of the surnames were of Basque origin, finding then that employers in the percentage of surnames was up to 25%, which led him to conclude that Basque settlers were very important in explaining the increased industrial development of Antioquia in the Colombian context. These ideas were supported by representatives of developmental theories, who sought to justify business growth based on "the character of social groups."
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Euskera_.28Basque_language.29_in_Antioquia">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Euskera_(Basque_language)_in_Antioquia">
        Euskera (Basque language) in Antioquia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Euskera (Basque language) in Antioquia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The use of
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque language
       </a>
       (Euskera) terminology in the present territory of Colombia goes back to the early exploration in 1499, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">
        third voyage of Columbus
       </a>
       , it is said that from that time the territory experienced a strong presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">
        Basques
       </a>
       including prominent figures such as the pilot and geographer
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_la_Cosa" title="Juan de la Cosa">
        Juan de la Cosa
       </a>
       , nicknamed "El Vizcaino" (although some sources claim that he was not a native of the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(greater_region)" title="Basque Country (greater region)">
        Basque Country
       </a>
       , but was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Santo%C3%B1a" title="Santoña">
        Santoña
       </a>
       , Cantabria).
      </p>
      <p>
       Thereafter, the Basques began to come regularly and are distributed throughout the region. Due to this presence, the Colombian department of Antioquia has been considered a major route of the Basque-
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       emigration, mainly during the colonial era, when hundreds of Basques migrated to be linked to the Spanish colonization companies.
      </p>
      <p>
       People who were interested in investigating the presence of the Basque People in the department of Antioquia and Colombia have been troubled by the question that relates to the use and retention of the Basque language in their current territories.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is estimated, for example, for smaller Antioquia, a region where hundreds of Spaniards arrived, of which a good portion were Basque, some limited aspects of the culture and traditions were brought by Basque settlers, though without any mention of their particular language, thus tracking the use of Basque in the current Antioquia and Colombia. However, this is partly due to the Basque language always having been an outcast, which apparently left no written evidence in Antioquia.
      </p>
      <p>
       In this regard, it is hardly likely that the Spanish crown—in order to maintain the monopoly of overseas companies, to maintain policies that restrict citizens not belonging to the then Spanish rule—would allow its colonies to speak languages other than Spanish. Those Basques invited to participate in the colonization companies, and foreigners in general, had to learn the official language, i.e., Spanish, hence the dominance of Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite these restrictions, it is still possible to trace the history of Colombia's present references to the ancient language of the Basques. A reference that has very ancient use of
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Euskara
       </a>
       in Colombian territory, occurred in relation to
       <a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Aguirre" title="Lope de Aguirre">
        Lope de Aguirre
       </a>
       , a native of
       <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
        Gipuzkoa
       </a>
       nicknamed "The Madman". Aguirre's rebellion defied the Spanish empire, carrying out acts against the subjects of the Spanish crown.
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_de_Urs%C3%BAa" title="Pedro de Ursúa">
        Pedro de Ursúa
       </a>
       , a Navarrese faithful to the Spanish king, who was also the founder of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pamplona,_Colombia" title="Pamplona, Colombia">
        Pamplona
       </a>
       in eastern Colombia, said that he could persuade the soldiers to be told of Aguirre's revolt, if they spoke in Euskara.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
        Spanish Civil War
       </a>
       , many Basque families migrated to Colombia. A number of these families were Basque-speakers and wrote works in Euskara and, likewise, translated from Spanish to Basque literary works of Colombian authors.

        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Antioquia_Basque_speech">
        Antioquia Basque speech
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Antioquia Basque speech">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The current Spanish dialect in Antioquia, closely observed, has obvious influences from Basque. Basque influence is evident in words such as 'ma' (mother), 'coscorria' (useless, inept) and 'tap' (tap), to name only a few cases.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Basque also influenced the pronunciation of the letter 's' apico-alveolar (transitional between 's' and 'sh'), so in the Antioquia, and the letter "ll" (double L) pronounced as an affricative, not to overlook the inclusion of the letter "a" before certain initial Rs: arrecostarse instead of recostarse, arrecoger instead of recoger and arrecordarse instead of recordarse.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spaniards_in_Antioquia">
        Spaniards in Antioquia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Spaniards in Antioquia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first Spaniard known to have visited the territory now known as Antioquia was
       <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_de_Bastidas" title="Rodrigo de Bastidas">
        Rodrigo de Bastidas
       </a>
       , who explored the area around the future site of
       <a href="/wiki/Dari%C3%A9n_Province" title="Darién Province">
        Darién
       </a>
       in 1500. Ten years later,
       <a href="/wiki/Alonso_de_Ojeda" title="Alonso de Ojeda">
        Alonso de Ojeda
       </a>
       founded
       <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n_de_Urab%C3%A1" title="San Sebastián de Urabá">
        San Sebastián de Urabá
       </a>
       , 2 km from the present-day town of
       <a href="/wiki/Necocl%C3%AD" title="Necoclí">
        Necoclí
       </a>
       , which would be destroyed later by the natives. The first Spanish military incursion into Antioquia, however, was not made until 1537, when an expedition commanded by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francisco_C%C3%A9sar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francisco César (page does not exist)">
        Francisco César
       </a>
       traveled the lands of the Native American chief Dabeiba, arriving at the
       <a href="/wiki/Cauca_River" title="Cauca River">
        Cauca River
       </a>
       and taking an important treasure from the indigenous people's tombs. In response, the warriors of the chief Nutibara harassed the Spaniards continually, and forced them to return to
       <a href="/wiki/Urab%C3%A1_Antioquia" title="Urabá Antioquia">
        Urabá
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1541, the conquistador
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Robledo_(conquistador)" title="Jorge Robledo (conquistador)">
        Jorge Robledo
       </a>
       departed from the site of the future (1542) Spanish town of Arma, a little below Aguadas in the south of Antioquia, to lead an expedition north on the Cauca River.
      </p>
      <p>
       Farther north, Robledo would found the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_de_Antioquia" title="Santa Fe de Antioquia">
        Santa Fe de Antioquia
       </a>
       , which in 1813 was declared the capital of the sovereign and independent state of Antioquia, and remained the seat of the governate until 1826 when Medellin was designated the capital.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other Spaniards who settled Antioquia included Extremadurans, Catalans, Andalusians, and Canarians; the Extremadurans and Catalans also influenced the pronunciation of the letter 's' apico-alveolar, like Basques, and Andalusians and Canarians influenced
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       in the Spanish dialect.
      </p>
      <h3>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       The reason behind the chosen name for the Department is not historically clear. The most accepted
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

          </span>

        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       explanation is that the name for the, then
       <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
        Greek
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">
        Syrian
       </a>
       (now
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkish
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic" title="Hellenistic">
        Hellenistic
       </a>
       city of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antioch_on_the_Orontes" title="Antioch on the Orontes">
        Antioch on the Orontes
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el">
        Antiochia
       </span>
       , Αντιοχεία,
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">

       <a href="/wiki/Antakya" title="Antakya">

       ) was used, as the region known as the Coffee Zone in Colombia, in which many towns and cities are named after cities in the middle east, has a very strong

       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_Antioquia_from_the_16th_to_the_21st_centuries">
        History of Antioquia from the 16th to the 21st centuries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antioquia_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History of Antioquia from the 16th to the 21st centuries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Due to its geographical isolation (as it is located among mountains), Antioquia suffered supply problems. Its topography did not allow for much agriculture, so Antioquia became dependent upon trade, especially of gold and gin for the colonization of new land, although much of this trade was due to reforms passed after a visit from an inspector of the Spanish crown,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Antonio_Mon_y_Velarde&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Antonio Mon y Velarde (page does not exist)">
        Juan Antonio Mon y Velarde
       </a>
       in 1785. The Antioquia became colonizers and traders, contributing to the Antioquian culture.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Wall Street Journal and Citi announced in the year 2013 that
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       , the capital of the Department of Antioquia, is the winner of the City of the Year competition, a global program developed in partnership with the Urban Land Institute to recognize the most innovative urban centers. Medellín beating out finalists Tel Aviv and New York City.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>
        (10.9%)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amerindian" title="Amerindian">
         Amerindian
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">
         Indigenous
        </a>
        (0.5%)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The local inhabitants of Antioquia are known as
       <i>
        antioqueños
       </i>
       . Of the five main regional groups in
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       , the predominant group in Antioquia are known as
       <i>
        paisa
       </i>
       , referring to those living in the
       <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">
        Paisa Region
       </a>
       , which covers most of Antioquia, as well as the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo_Department" title="Quindío Department">
        Quindío
       </a>
       .
      </p>
