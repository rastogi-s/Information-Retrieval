      <p>
       <b>
        Champenois
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        champaignat
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
         langues d'oïl
        </a>
       </i>
       language family spoken by a minority of people in
       <a href="/wiki/Champagne_(province)" title="Champagne (province)">
        Champagne
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele-de-France" title="Île-de-France">
        Île-de-France
       </a>
       provinces in
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , as well as in a handful of towns in southern
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       (chiefly the municipality of
       <a href="/wiki/Vresse-sur-Semois" title="Vresse-sur-Semois">
        Vresse-sur-Semois
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       While it is classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        regional language of France
       </a>
       , it also has recognized status as a
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional language
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       region of Belgium. Champenois is considered an endangered language by the
       <a href="/wiki/Red_Book_of_Endangered_Languages" title="Red Book of Endangered Languages">
        UNESCO Red Book of Endangered Languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Champenois_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The language of
       <a href="/wiki/Chr%C3%A9tien_de_Troyes" title="Chrétien de Troyes">
        Chrétien de Troyes
       </a>
       is marked by Champenois traits, but the earliest literature to survive consciously written in Champenois is noted from the end of the 16th century.
       <i>
        Le Bontemps de Carnaval de Chaumont
       </i>
       was updated and republished in 1660. The language used contrasts the French spoken by the king's messengers with the Champenois of the local inhabitants. A feature of 18th century Champenois literature was the
       <i>
        noëls
       </i>
       (Christmas chants), which wove contemporary and local references into pious texts.
      </p>
