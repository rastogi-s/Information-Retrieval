      <p>
       <b>
        Cyrene
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Κυρήνη
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Ancient_Greek" title="Romanization of Ancient Greek">
         translit.
        </a>
       </small>
       <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        Kyrēnē
       </i>
       ) was an
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        ancient Greek
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       city near present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Shahhat" title="Shahhat">
        Shahhat
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">
        Libya
       </a>
       . It was the oldest and most important of the five Greek cities in the region. It gave eastern Libya the classical name
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">
         Cyrenaica
        </a>
       </i>
       that it has retained to modern times. Located nearby is the ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Necropolis_of_Cyrene" title="Necropolis of Cyrene">
        Necropolis of Cyrene
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Cyrene lies in a lush
       <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">
        valley
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Jebel_Akhdar,_Libya" title="Jebel Akhdar, Libya">
        Jebel Akhdar
       </a>
       uplands. The city was named after a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere)" title="Spring (hydrosphere)">
        spring
       </a>
       , Kyre, which the Greeks consecrated to
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">
        Apollo
       </a>
       . It was also the seat of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaics" title="Cyrenaics">
        Cyrenaics
       </a>
       , a famous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" title="Greek philosophy">
        school of philosophy
       </a>
       in the 4th century BC, founded by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristippus" title="Aristippus">
        Aristippus
       </a>
       , a disciple of
       <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">
        Socrates
       </a>
       . It was then nicknamed the "
       <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
        Athens
       </a>
       of Africa".

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Summary_of_the_founding_of_Cyrene.2C_as_told_in_Herodotus">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Summary_of_the_founding_of_Cyrene,_as_told_in_Herodotus">
        Summary of the founding of Cyrene, as told in Herodotus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Summary of the founding of Cyrene, as told in Herodotus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Grinus, son of Aesanius a descendant of Theras, and king of the island of Thera, had visited the
       <a href="/wiki/Oracle" title="Oracle">
        Oracle
       </a>
       and offered a
       <a href="/wiki/Hecatomb" title="Hecatomb">
        hecatomb
       </a>
       to the Oracle on sundry matters. The Oracle had offered the advice to found a new city in
       <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">
        Libya
       </a>
       . Many years had passed and the advice was not taken, and Thera had succumbed to a horrific
       <a href="/wiki/Drought" title="Drought">
        drought
       </a>
       and all of the crops and trees had perished. They again sent to
       <a href="/wiki/Delphi" title="Delphi">
        Delphi
       </a>
       and were reminded that the Oracle had said several years before to settle in the country of Libya, but this time she specifically said to found a settlement in the land of
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">
        Cyrene
       </a>
       . Not knowing how to get to Libya they sent a messenger to
       <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">
        Crete
       </a>
       to find someone to lead them on their journey. They found a dealer in purple
       <a href="/wiki/Dye" title="Dye">
        dye
       </a>
       s named Corobius. He had once traveled to an island across from Libya called Platea [or Plataea, modern Jazirat Barda`ah].
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Grinus and Corobius sailed to Platea, when they reached their destination they left Corobius with months of supplies and Grinus went back to Thera to collect men to settle the newly made colony. After two years of settling the
       <a href="/wiki/Colony" title="Colony">
        colony
       </a>
       they had little success and went back to the Oracle to get advice. The Oracle had repeated her advice to move directly to the country of Libya instead of across from Libya. So they moved to a place called Aziris. They settled there for six years, and were very successful until the Libyans visited the settlement of Aziris to convince the people to move further inland. They were swayed by the Libyans to move and settled into what is now Cyrene. The current king of that time Battus reigned for 40 years, until he passed on and his son, Arcesilaus, took over and reigned for 16 years, with no more or less population change until the Oracle had told the third king, another Battus, to bring
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">
        Greek
       </a>
       citizens to the settlement and with that expansion the Libyans had lost a lot of land surrounding Cyrene.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_period">
        Greek period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greek period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg/220px-Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg/330px-Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg/440px-Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Temple_of_Zeus_-_Cyrene.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Temple of Zeus, Cyrene
        </div>

      </div>
      <p>
       According to Greek tradition, Cyrene was founded in 631 BC as a settlement of Greeks from the island of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thera" title="Thera">
        Thera
       </a>
       , traditionally led by
       <a href="/wiki/Battus_I_of_Cyrene" title="Battus I of Cyrene">
        Battus I
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at a site 16 kilometres (10 mi) from its associated port,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollonia,_Cyrenaica" title="Apollonia, Cyrenaica">
        Apollonia (Marsa Sousa)
       </a>
       . Traditional details concerning the founding of the city are contained in
       <a href="/wiki/Herodotus" title="Herodotus">
        Herodotus
       </a>
       '
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Histories_(Herodotus)" title="Histories (Herodotus)">
         Histories
        </a>
       </i>
       IV. Cyrene promptly became the chief town of
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Libya" title="Ancient Libya">
        ancient Libya
       </a>
       and established commercial relations with all the Greek cities, reaching the height of its prosperity under its own kings in the 5th century BC. Soon after 460 BC it became a
       <a href="/wiki/Republic" title="Republic">
        republic
       </a>
       . In 413 BC, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Peloponnesian_War" title="Peloponnesian War">
        Peloponnesian War
       </a>
       , Cyrene supplied
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spartan_Army" title="Spartan Army">
        Spartan
       </a>
       forces with two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triremes" title="Triremes">
        triremes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Maritime_pilot" title="Maritime pilot">
        pilots
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macedon" title="Macedon">
        of Macedon
       </a>
       (323 BC), the Cyrenian republic became subject to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">
        Ptolemaic dynasty
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Magas_as_king_of_Kyrene,_circa_282_or_275_to_261_BC.jpg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ophelas" title="Ophelas">
        Ophelas
       </a>
       , the general who occupied the city in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I" title="Ptolemy I">
        Ptolemy I
       </a>
       's name, ruled the city almost independently until his death, when Ptolemy's son-in-law
       <a href="/wiki/Magas_of_Cyrene" title="Magas of Cyrene">
        Magas
       </a>
       received governorship of the territory. In 276 BC Magas crowned himself king and declared
       <i>
        de facto
       </i>
       independence, marrying the daughter of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seleucid" title="Seleucid">
        Seleucid
       </a>
       king and forming with him an alliance in order to invade Egypt. The invasion was unsuccessful and in 250 BC, after Magas' death, the city was reabsorbed into Ptolemaic Egypt.
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaica" title="Cyrenaica">
        Cyrenaica
       </a>
       became part of the Ptolemaic empire controlled from Alexandria, and became
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Roman
       </a>
       territory in 96 BC when
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_Apion" title="Ptolemy Apion">
        Ptolemy Apion
       </a>
       bequeathed Cyrenaica to
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       . In 74 BC the territory was formally transformed into a
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">
        Roman province
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Roman_period">
        Roman period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Roman period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 74 BC Cyrene was created a Roman province; but, whereas under the Ptolemies the Jewish inhabitants had enjoyed equal rights, they were allegedly increasingly oppressed by the now autonomous and much larger Greek population. Tensions came to a head in the insurrection of the Jews of Cyrene under
       <a href="/wiki/Vespasian" title="Vespasian">
        Vespasian
       </a>
       (73 AD, the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Jewish%E2%80%93Roman_War" title="First Jewish–Roman War">
        First Jewish–Roman War
       </a>
       ) and especially
       <a href="/wiki/Trajan" title="Trajan">
        Trajan
       </a>
       (117 AD, the
       <a href="/wiki/Kitos_War" title="Kitos War">
        Kitos War
       </a>
       ). This revolt was quelled by
       <a href="/wiki/Marcius_Turbo" title="Marcius Turbo">
        Marcius Turbo
       </a>
       , but not before huge numbers of civilians had been brutally massacred by the Jewish rebels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">
        Eusebius
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Caesarea" title="Early centers of Christianity">
        of Caesarea
       </a>
       , the Jewish rebellion left Libya so depopulated to such an extent that a few years later new colonies had to be established there by the emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Hadrian" title="Hadrian">
        Hadrian
       </a>
       just to maintain the viability of continued settlement.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
        Plutarch
       </a>
       in his work
       <i>
        De mulierum virtutibus
       </i>
       ("On the Virtues of Women") describes how the tyrant of Cyrene,
       <a href="/wiki/Aretaphila_of_Cyrene" title="Aretaphila of Cyrene">
        Nicocrates
       </a>
       , was deposed by his wife
       <a href="/wiki/Aretaphila_of_Cyrene" title="Aretaphila of Cyrene">
        Aretaphila of Cyrene
       </a>
       around the year 50 BC
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The famous "Venus of Cyrene", a headless marble statue representing the goddess Venus, a Roman copy of a Greek original, was discovered by Italian soldiers here in 1913. It was transported to Rome, where it remained until 2008, when it was returned to Libya.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A large number of Roman sculptures and inscriptions were excavated at Cyrene by Captain
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Murdoch_Smith" title="Robert Murdoch Smith">
        Robert Murdoch Smith
       </a>
       and Commander Edwin A. Porcher during the mid nineteenth century and can now be seen in the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">
        British Museum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They include the Apollo of Cyrene and a unique bronze head of an African man.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">
        Christianity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Christianity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Christianity is reputed from its beginning to have links with Cyrene. All three
       <a href="/wiki/Synoptic_Gospels" title="Synoptic Gospels">
        synoptic Gospels
       </a>
       mention a
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_of_Cyrene" title="Simon of Cyrene">
        Simon of Cyrene
       </a>
       as having been forced to help carry the cross of Jesus. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">
        Acts of the Apostles
       </a>
       there is mention of people from Cyrene being in Jerusalem on the day of Pentecost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to the tradition of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coptic_Orthodox_Church" title="Coptic Orthodox Church">
        Coptic Orthodox Church
       </a>
       , its founder,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Mark" title="Saint Mark">
        Saint Mark
       </a>
       was a native of Cyrene and ordained the first bishop of Cyrene. The
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Martyrology" title="Roman Martyrology">
        Roman Martyrology
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       mentions under 4 July a tradition that in the persecution of
       <a href="/wiki/Diocletian" title="Diocletian">
        Diocletian
       </a>
       a bishop Theodorus of Cyrene was scourged and had his tongue cut out. Earlier editions of the Martyrology mentioned what may be the same person also under 26 March. Letter 67 of
       <a href="/wiki/Synesius" title="Synesius">
        Synesius
       </a>
       tells of an irregular episcopal ordination carried out by a bishop Philo of Cyrene, which was condoned by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Athanasius" title="Athanasius">
        Athanasius
       </a>
       . The same letter mentions that a nephew of this Philo, who bore the same name, also became bishop of Cyrene. Although Cyrene was by then ruined, a bishop of Cyrene name Rufus was at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robber_Council_of_Ephesus" title="Robber Council of Ephesus">
        Robber Council of Ephesus
       </a>
       in 449. And there was still a bishop of Cyrene, named Leontius, at the time of
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Greek_Orthodox_Patriarchs_of_Alexandria" title="List of Greek Orthodox Patriarchs of Alexandria">
        Greek Patriarch
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eulogius_of_Alexandria" title="Eulogius of Alexandria">
        Eulogius of Alexandria
       </a>
       (580-607).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janin_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Janin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Known bishops of the town, then include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_Luke" title="Saint Luke">
         Saint Luke
        </a>
        by tradition
       </li>
       <li>
        Theodoro (
        <a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">
         fl.
        </a>
        302)
       </li>
       <li>
        Filo I (
        <a href="/wiki/Floruit" title="Floruit">
         fl.
        </a>
        370 circa)
       </li>
       <li>
        Filo II (fl.370 circa)
       </li>
       <li>
        Rufo (fl.449)
       </li>
       <li>
        Leontius (fl.600 circa)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       No longer a residential bishopric, Cyrene is today listed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
        Catholic Church
       </a>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Titular_see" title="Titular see">
        titular see
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_Orthodox_Church" title="Greek Orthodox Church">
        Greek Orthodox Church
       </a>
       has also treated it as a titular see.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Janin_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Janin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">
        Decline
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Decline">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Cyrene's chief local export through much of its early history was the medicinal herb
       <a href="/wiki/Silphium" title="Silphium">
        silphium
       </a>
       , used as an
       <a href="/wiki/Abortifacient" title="Abortifacient">
        abortifacient
       </a>
       ; the herb was pictured on most Cyrenian coins. Silphium was in such demand that it was harvested to extinction
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ; this, in conjunction with commercial competition from
       <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">
        Carthage
       </a>
       and Alexandria, resulted in a reduction in the city's trade. Cyrene, with its port of Apollonia (Marsa Susa), remained an important urban center until the
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        earthquake
       </a>
       of 262, which damaged the
       <a href="/wiki/Extramural_Sanctuary_of_Demeter_and_Persephone_at_Cyrene,_Libya" title="Extramural Sanctuary of Demeter and Persephone at Cyrene, Libya">
        Sanctuary of Demeter and Persephon
       </a>
       in Cyrene. After the disaster, the emperor
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_II" title="Claudius II">
        Claudius Gothicus
       </a>
       restored Cyrene, naming it
       <i>
        Claudiopolis
       </i>
       , but the restorations were poor and precarious. Natural catastrophes and a profound economic decline dictated its death, and in 365 another particularly devastating earthquake destroyed its already meager hopes of recovery.
       <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">
        Ammianus Marcellinus
       </a>
       described it in the 4th century as a deserted city, and
       <a href="/wiki/Synesius" title="Synesius">
        Synesius
       </a>
       , a native of Cyrene, described it in the following century as a vast ruin at the mercy of the nomads. Ultimately, the city fell under
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab_conquest" title="Arab conquest">
        Arab conquest
       </a>
       in 643, by which time little was left of the opulent Roman cities of Northern Africa; the ruins of Cyrene are located near the modern village of
       <a href="/wiki/Shahhat" title="Shahhat">
        Shahhat
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Philosophy">
        Philosophy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Philosophy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Cyrene contributed to the intellectual life of the Greeks, through renowned philosophers and mathematicians. Philosophy flourished at the Cyrenaican plateau, the School of Cyrene, known as
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrenaics" title="Cyrenaics">
        Cyrenaics
       </a>
       developed here, a minor
       <a href="/wiki/Socratic_method" title="Socratic method">
        Socratic
       </a>
       school founded by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristippus" title="Aristippus">
        Aristippus
       </a>
       (perhaps the friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Socrates" title="Socrates">
        Socrates
       </a>
       , though according to some accounts a grandson of Aristippus with the same name). Cyrene was the birthplace of
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       , who later went to
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">
        Alexandria
       </a>
       . Statues of philosophers, poets, and
       <a href="/wiki/The_Nine_Muses" title="The Nine Muses">
        The Nine Muses
       </a>
       , and a bust of
       <a href="/wiki/Demosthenes" title="Demosthenes">
        Demosthenes
       </a>
       were found in Cyrene.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Others included, Aristippus successor and daughter
       <a href="/wiki/Arete_of_Cyrene" title="Arete of Cyrene">
        Arete
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">
        Callimachus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carneades" title="Carneades">
        Carneades
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemais_of_Cyrene" title="Ptolemais of Cyrene">
        Ptolemais of Cyrene
       </a>
       , and Synesius, a bishop of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemais_(Cyrenaica)" title="Ptolemais (Cyrenaica)">
        Ptolemais
       </a>
       in the 4th century AD.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrene_in_the_Bible">
        Cyrene in the Bible
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cyrene in the Bible">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Early_centers_of_Christianity#Cyrene" title="Early centers of Christianity">
        Early centers of Christianity § Cyrene
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cyrene is referred to in the
       <a href="/wiki/Deuterocanonical_books" title="Deuterocanonical books">
        deuterocanonical
       </a>
       book
       <a href="/wiki/2_Maccabees" title="2 Maccabees">
        2 Maccabees
       </a>
       . The book of 2 Maccabees itself is said by its author to be an abridgment of a five-volume work by a Hellenized Jew by the name of
       <a href="/wiki/Jason_of_Cyrene" title="Jason of Cyrene">
        Jason of Cyrene
       </a>
       who lived around 100 BC.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cyrene is also mentioned in the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
        New Testament
       </a>
       . A Cyrenian named
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_of_Cyrene" title="Simon of Cyrene">
        Simon
       </a>
       carried the cross of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">
        Christ
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">
        Mark
       </a>
       15:21 and parallels). See also
       <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">
        Acts
       </a>
       2:10 where Jews from Cyrene heard the disciples speaking in their own language in Jerusalem on the day of
       <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">
        Pentecost
       </a>
       ; 6:9 where some Cyrenian Jews disputed with a disciple named Stephen; 11:20 tells of Jewish Christians originally from Cyrene who (along with believers from Cyprus) first preached the Gospel to non-Jews; 13:1 names Lucius of Cyrene as one of several to whom the Holy Spirit spoke, instructing them to appoint Barnabas and Saul (later Paul) for missionary service.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present">
        Present
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyrene,_Libya&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Present">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Necropolis_of_Cyrene" title="Necropolis of Cyrene">
        Necropolis of Cyrene
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cyrene is now an archeological site near the village of
       <a href="/wiki/Shahhat" title="Shahhat">
        Shahhat
       </a>
       . One of its more significant features is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_temple" title="Greek temple">
        temple
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo" title="Apollo">
        Apollo
       </a>
       which was originally constructed as early as 7th century BC. Other ancient structures include a temple to
       <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">
        Demeter
       </a>
       and a partially unexcavated temple to
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       There is a large
       <a href="/wiki/Necropolis" title="Necropolis">
        necropolis
       </a>
       approximately 10 km between Cyrene and its ancient port of Apollonia. Since 1982, it has been a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UNESCO_World_Heritage_Site" title="UNESCO World Heritage Site">
        UNESCO World Heritage Site
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2005, Italian archaeologists from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Urbino" title="University of Urbino">
        University of Urbino
       </a>
       discovered 76 intact Roman statues at Cyrene from the 2nd century AD. The statues remained undiscovered for so long because “during the earthquake of 375 AD, a supporting wall of the temple fell on its side, burying all the statues. They remained hidden under stone, rubble and earth for 1,630 years. The other walls sheltered the statues, so we were able to recover all the pieces, even works that had been broken."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Beginning in 2006,
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Heritage_Fund" title="Global Heritage Fund">
        Global Heritage Fund
       </a>
       , in partnership with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_University_of_Naples" title="Second University of Naples">
        Second University of Naples
       </a>
       (SUN, Italy), the
       <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">
        Libyan
       </a>
       Department of Antiquities, and the Libyan Ministry of Culture, has been working to preserve the ancient site through a combination of holistic conservation practices and training of local skilled and unskilled labor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-unesco-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Apart from conducting ongoing emergency conservation on a theater inside the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sanctuary_of_Apollo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sanctuary of Apollo (page does not exist)">
        Sanctuary of Apollo
       </a>
       through the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Anastylosis" title="Anastylosis">
        anastylosis
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Heritage_Fund" title="Global Heritage Fund">
        GHF
       </a>
       -led team is in the process of developing a comprehensive master site management plan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unesco_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-unesco-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007 Muammar Gaddafi's son planned safeguarding Libya's archaeological sites and to prevent overdevelopment of Mediterranean coastlines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In May 2011, a number of objects excavated from Cyrene in 1917 and held in the vault of the National Commercial Bank in
       <a href="/wiki/Benghazi" title="Benghazi">
        Benghazi
       </a>
       were stolen. Looters tunnelled into the vault and broke into two safes that held the artefacts which were part of the so-called 'Benghazi Treasure'. The whereabouts of these objects are currently unknown.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2017
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       added Cyrene to its
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_World_Heritage_in_Danger" title="List of World Heritage in Danger">
        List of World Heritage in Danger
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
