      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       is distinct from
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        Peninsular Spanish
       </a>
       and Spanish spoken elsewhere, such as in Africa and Asia. Linguistically, this grouping is somewhat arbitrary, akin to having a term for "overseas English" encompassing variants spoken in the United States, Canada, Australia, India, New Zealand and Ireland, but not the Island of Britain. There is great diversity among the various Latin American vernaculars, and it would be hard to point to one trait shared by all of them which is not also in existence in one or more of the variants of Spanish used in Spain. Of the more than
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanosphere" title="Hispanosphere">
        469 million people
       </a>
       who speak Spanish as their native language, more than 418 million are in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are numerous regional particularities and idiomatic expressions within Spanish. In Latin American Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       directly from English are relatively more frequent, and often foreign spellings are left intact. One notable trend is the higher abundance of loan words taken from English in Latin America as well as words derived from English. The Latin American Spanish word for "computer" is
       <i>
        computadora
       </i>
       , whereas the word used in Spain is
       <i>
        ordenador
       </i>
       , and each word sounds foreign in the region where it is not used. Some differences are due to Iberian Spanish having a stronger French influence than Latin America, where, for geopolitical reasons, the United States influence has been predominant throughout the twentieth century.
      </p>

      <p>
       Pronunciation varies from country to country and from region to region, just as English pronunciation varies from one place to another. In general terms, the speech of the Americas shows many common features akin to southern Spanish variants, especially to western Andalusia (Seville, Cádiz) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . Coastal language vernaculars throughout Hispanic America show particularly strong similarities to Atlantic-Andalusian speech patterns while inland regions in Mexico and Andean countries are not similar to any particular dialect in Spain.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Most Spaniards pronounce ⟨z⟩ and ⟨c⟩ (before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front unrounded vowel">
          e
         </a>
         /
        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">
          i
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ) as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
          θ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        (called
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distinci%C3%B3n" title="Distinción">
          distinción
         </a>
        </i>
        ). Conversely, most Hispanic Americans have
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
          seseo
         </a>
        </i>
        , lacking a distinction between this phoneme and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        . However,
        <i>
         seseo
        </i>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusian_people" title="Andalusian people">
         Andalusians
        </a>
        and all
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canarian_people" title="Canarian people">
         Canary islanders
        </a>
        . Andalusia's and the Canary Islands' predominant position in the conquest and subsequent immigration to Hispanic America from Spain is thought to be the reason for the absence of this distinction in most American Spanish dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        Most of Spain, particularly the regions that have a distinctive
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">

         /
        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

          s̺
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , with a weak "hushing" sound reminiscent of
        <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_consonant" title="Retroflex consonant">
         retroflex
        </a>
        fricatives. To a Hispanic American speaker, the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        like English ⟨sh⟩ as in
        <i>
         she
        </i>
        . However, this apico-alveolar realization of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        is not uncommon in some Latin American Spanish dialects which lack
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

          θ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

         </a>

         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative#Voiced_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced alveolar fricative">
          z
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        . The alveolar approximant realization is particularly associated with an indigenous substrate and it is quite common in Andean regions, especially in inland Ecuador, Peru, most of Bolivia and in parts of northern Argentina and Paraguay.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Belize, Puerto Rico, and
        <a href="/wiki/Archipelago_of_San_Andr%C3%A9s,_Providencia_and_Santa_Catalina" title="Archipelago of San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina">
         Colombian islands of San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina
        </a>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
          ɾ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
          r
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
          l
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
          r
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        can be realized as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
          ɹ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , an influence of
        <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
         American English
        </a>
        to Puerto Rican dialect and
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         British English
        </a>
        to Belizean dialect and Colombian dialect of Archipelago of San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina (in the case of the latter three, it is not exclusive to Colombians whose ancestors traced back to Spanish period before British invasion, under British territorial rule, and recovery of Spanish control, but is also used by
        <a href="/wiki/Raizal" title="Raizal">
         Raizals
        </a>
        , whites of British descent, and descendants of mainland Colombians);
        <i>
         "verso"'
        </i>
        (verse) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbeɹso]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbeɾso]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈberso]
        </span>
        , or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbelso]
        </span>
        , "
        <i>
         invierno
        </i>
        " (winter) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjeɹno]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjeɾno]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjerno]
        </span>
        , or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [imˈbjelno]
        </span>
        , and "
        <i>
         escarlata
        </i>
        " (scarlet) becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ehkaɹˈlata]
        </span>
        , aside from
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ehkaɾˈlata]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ehkarˈlata]
        </span>
        , or [
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         ehkaˈlata
        </span>
        ]. In word-final position,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
          r
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        will usually be one of these:
        <ul>
         <li>
          a trill, a tap, approximant,
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
            l
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          , or elided when followed by a consonant or a pause, as in
          <i>
           amo
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [r ~ ɾ ~ ɹ ~ l]
          </span>
          <i>
           paterno
          </i>
          'paternal love',
          <i>
           amor
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [aˈmo]
          </span>
          ,
         </li>
         <li>
          a tap, approximant, or
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
            l
           </a>
           ]
          </span>
          when the followed by a vowel-initial word, as in
          <i>
           amo
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ɾ ~ ɹ ~ l]
          </span>
          <i>
           eterno
          </i>
          'eternal love').
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
         voiced
        </a>
        consonants
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">
          b
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiced dental and alveolar stops">
          d
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_stop" title="Voiced velar stop">
          ɡ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosive" title="Plosive">
         plosives
        </a>
        after and sometimes before any consonant in most of
        <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Spanish" title="Colombian Spanish">
         Colombian Spanish
        </a>
        dialects (rather than the
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
         approximant
        </a>
        that is characteristic of most other dialects):
        <i>
         pardo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpaɾ
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         o]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         barba
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbaɾ
         <b>
          b
         </b>
         a]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         algo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈal
         <b>
          ɡ
         </b>
         o]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         peligro
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈli
         <b>
          ɡ
         </b>
         ɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         desde
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdez
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         e/ˈdeh
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         e]
        </span>
        —rather than the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpaɾ
         <b>
          ð
         </b>
         o]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbaɾ
         <b>
          β
         </b>
         a]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈal
         <b>
          ɣ
         </b>
         o]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈli
         <b>
          ɣ
         </b>
         ɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdez
         <b>
          ð
         </b>
         e/ˈdeh
         <b>
          ð
         </b>
         e]
        </span>
        of Spain and the rest of Spanish America. A notable exception is the
        <a href="/wiki/Nari%C3%B1o_Department" title="Nariño Department">
         Department of Nariño
        </a>
        and most
        <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Spanish#Caribbean_dialect" title="Colombian Spanish">
         <i>
          Costeño
         </i>
         speech (Atlantic coastal dialects)
        </a>
        which feature the soft, fricative realizations common to all other Hispanic American and European dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
          n
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        is velar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
          ŋ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        in much Latin American Spanish speech; this means a word like
        <i>
         pan
        </i>
        (bread) is often articulated
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         ['paŋ]
        </span>
        . To an English-speaker, those speakers that have a velar nasal for word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
          n
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        make
        <i>
         pan
        </i>
        sound like
        <i>
         pang
        </i>
        . Velarization of word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
          n
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        is so widespread in the Americas that it is easier to mention those regions that maintain an alveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
          n
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : most of Mexico, Colombia (except for coastal dialects) and Argentina (except for some northern regions). Elsewhere, velarization is common, although alveolar word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
          n
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        can appear among some educated speakers, especially in the media or in singing. Velar word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
          ŋ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        is also frequent in Spain, especially in southern Spanish dialects (Andalusia and the Canary Islands) and in the Northwest: Galicia, Asturias and León.
       </li>
      </ul>
