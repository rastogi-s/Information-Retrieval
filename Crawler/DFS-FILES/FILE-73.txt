        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single_phase" title="Single phase">
         single phase
        </a>
        , 3Φ for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_phase" title="Three phase">
         three phase
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        A common symbol for the parametrization of a surface in vector
        <a href="/wiki/Calculus" title="Calculus">
         calculus
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacanian_algebra" title="Lacanian algebra">
         Lacanian algebra
        </a>
        , Φ stands for the imaginary phallus and also represents phallic signification; -Φ stands in for castration.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diameter_symbol" title="Diameter symbol">
        diameter symbol
       </a>

       is 14

      <p>
       In ordinary Greek text, the character U+03C6 φ is used exclusively, although this character has considerable glyphic variation, sometimes represented with a glyph more like the representative glyph shown for U+03C6 (φ, the "loopy" or "open" form) and less often with a glyph more like the representative glyph shown for U+03D5 (ϕ, the "stroked" or "closed" form). Unicode makes an effort to distinguish the two by generally calling the loopy form "small letter phi" or "small phi", and by calling the stroked form "phi symbol", but this isn't exclusively true on all variants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Unicode represents a character in an abstract way, the choice between glyphs is purely a matter of font design. While some Greek typefaces, most notably in the
       <a href="/wiki/Porson_(typeface)" title="Porson (typeface)">
        Porson
       </a>
       family (used widely in editions of classical Greek texts), have a "stroked" glyph in this position (
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_phi_Porson.svg">
        <img alt="Greek phi Porson.svg" data-file-height="56" data-file-width="35" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Greek_phi_Porson.svg/10px-Greek_phi_Porson.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Greek_phi_Porson.svg/15px-Greek_phi_Porson.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Greek_phi_Porson.svg/20px-Greek_phi_Porson.svg.png 2x" width="10"/>
       </a>
       ), most other typefaces have "loopy" glyphs. This goes for the "Didot" (or "
       <i>
        apla
       </i>
       ") typefaces employed in most Greek book printing (
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_phi_Didot.svg">
        <img alt="Greek phi Didot.svg" data-file-height="56" data-file-width="40" height="16" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_phi_Didot.svg/11px-Greek_phi_Didot.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_phi_Didot.svg/17px-Greek_phi_Didot.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/Greek_phi_Didot.svg/23px-Greek_phi_Didot.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
       </a>

       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Greek_phi_Neohellenic.svg">
