      <p>
       <b>
        <i>
         Rioplatense
        </i>
        Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español rioplatense
        </span>
       </i>
       , locally
       <i>
        castellano rioplatense
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a dialect
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken mainly in the areas in and around the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata_Basin" title="Río de la Plata Basin">
        Río de la Plata Basin
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also referred to as
       <i>
        River Plate Spanish
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Argentine Spanish
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Being the most prominent dialect to employ
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       in both speech and writing, many features of Rioplatense are also shared with the varieties spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Media_Luna" title="Media Luna">
        Eastern Bolivia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       . This dialect is often spoken with an intonation resembling that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan
       </a>
       language of Southern Italy, but there are exceptions. The usual word employed to name the Spanish language in this region is
       <i>
        castellano
       </i>
       (English:
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        Castilian
       </span>
       ) and seldom
       <i>
        español
       </i>
       (English:
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        Spanish
       </span>
       ). See
       <a href="/wiki/Names_given_to_the_Spanish_language" title="Names given to the Spanish language">
        names given to the Spanish language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the fact that Rioplatense is merely a peculiar dialect and not a language, there are no credible figures for a total number of speakers. Generally, native speakers of Spanish who were raised in Uruguay or the majority of Argentina are assumed to speak Rioplatense (at least informally) just as, for instance, Americans would be assumed to speak
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       , rather than any other variety of English. The total population of these areas would amount to some 25–30 million, depending on the definition and expanse.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location">
        Location
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Rioplatense is mainly based in the cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rosario,_Santa_Fe" title="Rosario, Santa Fe">
        Rosario
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe,_Argentina" title="Santa Fe, Argentina">
        Santa Fe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/La_Plata" title="La Plata">
        La Plata
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mar_del_Plata" title="Mar del Plata">
        Mar del Plata
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%ADa_Blanca" title="Bahía Blanca">
        Bahía Blanca
       </a>
       in Argentina, and
       <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">
        Montevideo
       </a>
       in Uruguay, the most populated cities in the dialectal area, along with their respective suburbs and the areas in between. This regional form of Spanish is also found in other areas, not geographically close but culturally influenced by those population centers (e.g., in parts of Paraguay and in all of Patagonia). Rioplatense is the standard in audiovisual media in Argentina and Uruguay. To the north, and northeast exists the hybrid
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riverense_Portu%C3%B1ol" title="Riverense Portuñol">
        Riverense Portuñol
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_the_language">
        Influences on the language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influences on the language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spaniards brought their language to the area during the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">
        Spanish colonization
       </a>
       in the region. Originally part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_Peru" title="Viceroyalty of Peru">
        Viceroyalty of Peru
       </a>
       , the Río de la Plata basin had its status lifted to
       <a href="/wiki/Viceroyalty_of_the_R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata">
        Viceroyalty of the Río de la Plata
       </a>
       in 1776.
      </p>
      <p>
       Until the massive
       <a href="/wiki/Immigration_to_Argentina" title="Immigration to Argentina">
        immigration to the region
       </a>
       started in the 1870s, the language of the Río de la Plata had virtually no influence from other languages and varied mainly by localisms. Argentines and Uruguayans often state that their populations, like those of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       , comprise people of relatively recent European descent, the largest immigrant groups coming from Spain and Italy.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="European_immigration">
        European immigration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: European immigration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Several languages, and especially Italian, influenced the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Criollo_(people)" title="Criollo (people)">
         criollo
        </a>
       </i>
       Spanish of the time, because of the diversity of settlers and immigrants to Argentina and Uruguay:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        1870–1890: mainly
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
         Basque
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
         Galician
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">
         Northern Italian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        speakers and some from
        <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
         France
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
         Germany
        </a>
        , and other
        <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
         European
        </a>
        countries.
       </li>
       <li>
        1910–1945: again from Spain,
        <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">
         Southern Italy
        </a>
        , Portugal and in smaller numbers from across Europe;
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
         Jewish
        </a>
        immigration—mainly from
        <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
         Russia
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
         Poland
        </a>
        from the 1910s until after
        <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
         World War II
        </a>
        —was also significant.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_speakers" title="English speakers">
         English speakers
        </a>
        —from
        <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
         Britain
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Ireland" title="Republic of Ireland">
         Ireland
        </a>
        —were not as numerous as the Italians (40% of Argentines have Italian ancestry), but were influential in industry, business, education and agriculture. English immigrants were influential within the upper middle class.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_of_indigenous_populations_in_Argentina">
        Influence of indigenous populations in Argentina
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence of indigenous populations in Argentina">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       European settlement decimated Native American populations before 1810, and also during the expansion into Patagonia (after 1870). However, the interaction between Spanish and several of the native languages has left visible traces. Words from
       <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">
        Guarani
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">
        Quechua
       </a>
       and others were incorporated into the local form of Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some words of Amerindian origin commonly used in Rioplatense Spanish are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        From
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">
         Quechua
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         guacho
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         guacha
        </i>
        (orig.
        <i>
         wakcha
        </i>
        "poor person, vagabond, orphan"); the term for the native cowboys of the Pampas,
        <i>
         gaucho
        </i>
        , may be related.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        pochoclo
       </i>
       (pop + choclo, from choqllo, corn) -- popcorn in Argentina
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        From
        <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">
         Guaraní
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         pororó
        </i>
        —popcorn in Uruguay (but is also more commonly called pop or palomitas), Paraguay and some Argentine provinces.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         See
         <a href="/wiki/Influences_on_the_Spanish_language" title="Influences on the Spanish language">
          Influences on the Spanish language
         </a>
         for a more comprehensive review of borrowings into all dialects of Spanish.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Differences between dialects of Spanish are numerous; about 9,000 Rioplatense words
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       are not used or, in many cases, even understood elsewhere. These include many terms from the basic vocabulary, such as words for fruits, garments, foodstuffs, car parts, etc., as well as local slang.
      </p>
      <p>
       Rioplatense vocabularies continue to diverge from Peninsular Spanish: Rioplatense Spanish tends to borrow (or
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       ) technical words from American English, while Peninsular Spanish tends to borrow or calque them from British English or from French.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Rioplatense Spanish distinguishes itself from other dialects of Spanish by the pronunciation of certain consonants.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Like many other dialects, Rioplatense features
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísmo
        </a>
        : the sounds represented by
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        (historically the palatal lateral
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        ) and
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        (historically the palatal approximant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʝ/
        </span>
        ) have fused into one. Thus, in Rioplatense,
        <i>
         se cayó
        </i>
        "he fell down" is homophonous with
        <i>
         se calló
        </i>
        "he became silent". This merged phoneme is generally pronounced as a postalveolar fricative, either
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         voiced
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʒ]
        </span>
        (as in English
        <i>
         mea
         <u>
          s
         </u>
         ure
        </i>
        or the French
        <i>
         j
        </i>
        ) in the central and western parts of the dialect region (this phenomenon is called
        <i>
         zheísmo
        </i>
        ) or
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         voiceless
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        (as in the French
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        ) in and around Buenos Aires (called
        <i>
         sheísmo
        </i>
        ).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In addition sheísmo is also present in areas such as the Río de Plata and Uruguay.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In rare cases,
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        can be realized as a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced alveolar sibilant">
         voiced alveolar sibilant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [z]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        As in most American dialects, also, Rioplatense Spanish has
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        (traditional
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        ). That is,
        <i>
         casa
        </i>
        ("house") is homophonous with
        <i>
         caza
        </i>
        ("hunt").
        <i>
         Seseo
        </i>
        is common to other dialects of
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
         Spanish in Latin America
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
         Canarian Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian Spanish
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In popular speech, the fricative
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        has a tendency to become 'aspirated' before another consonant (the resulting sound depending on what the consonant is, although stating it is a
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         voiceless glottal fricative
        </a>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        , would give a clear idea of the mechanism) or simply in all syllable-final positions in less educated speech
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        . This change may be realized only at the word level or it may also cross word boundaries. That is,
        <i>
         esto es lo mismo
        </i>
        "this is the same" is pronounced something like
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈehto ˈeɦ lo ˈmiɦmo]
        </span>
        , but in
        <i>
         las águilas azules
        </i>
        "the blue eagles",
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        in
        <i>
         las
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         águilas
        </i>
        might remain
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        as no consonant follows:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [las ˈaɣilas aˈsules]
        </span>
        , or become
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        ; the pronunciation is largely an individual choice.
       </li>
       <li>
        The phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        (written as ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩, and as ⟨j⟩ elsewhere) is never glottalised to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        in the Atlantic coast.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        This phenomenon is common to other coastal dialects in Latin American Spanish, as well as Caribbean, Canarian, and Andalusian dialects, but not in Uruguayan dialect. Uruguayan speakers always realize it as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        , like people in Northern and Central Spain. Argentine speakers realize it both as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        . Uruguayans never realize it as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        , but
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        is a possible phonetic realization of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        when it is followed by two or more consonants or at the end of a phrase.
       </li>
       <li>
        In some areas, speakers tend to drop the final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        sound in verb infinitives and the final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        in most words.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        This elision is considered a feature of uneducated speakers in some places, but it is widespread in others, at least in rapid speech.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Aspiration of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , together with loss of final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       and some common instances of diphthong simplification,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
          <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (December 2014)">
           dubious
          </span>
         </a>
         <span class="metadata">
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:Rioplatense_Spanish#Diphthong_simplification" title="Talk:Rioplatense Spanish">
           discuss
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       tend to produce a noticeable simplification of the syllable structure, giving Rioplatense informal speech a distinct fluid consonant-vowel-consonant-vowel rhythm:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Si querés irte, andate. Yo no te voy a parar.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "If you want to go, then go. I'm not going to stop you."
       </dd>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Intonation">
        Intonation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Intonation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Preliminary research has shown that Rioplatense Spanish, and particularly the speech of the city of Buenos Aires, has
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       patterns that resemble those of
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">
        Italian dialects
       </a>
       . This correlates well with immigration patterns. Argentina has received huge numbers of Italian settlers since the 19th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to a study conducted by
       <a href="/wiki/National_Scientific_and_Technical_Research_Council" title="National Scientific and Technical Research Council">
        National Scientific and Technical Research Council
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Buenos Aires and Rosario residents speak with an intonation most closely resembling
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan
       </a>
       . The researchers note this as a relatively recent phenomenon, starting in the beginning of the 20th century with the main wave of Southern Italian immigration. Before that, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Porte%C3%B1o" title="Porteño">
         porteño
        </a>
       </i>
       accent was more similar to that of Spain, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns_and_verb_conjugation">
        Pronouns and verb conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:317px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voseo-extension-real1.png">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         In this map of
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">

          </a>
          countries
         </i>
         , the spread of the
         <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">

         </a>
         is clearly illustrated.
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
           Argentina
          </a>
         </b>
         ,
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
           Uruguay
          </a>
         </b>
         and
         <b>
          <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">

          </a>

         <b>
          Rioplatense Spanish
         </b>
         is spoken in these regions.
         <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
          Argentina
         </a>
         is the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Largest_country" title="Largest country">
          largest country
         </a>
         that uses the
         <i>
          voseo
         </i>
         , and it is associated as exclusively belonging to that
         <a href="/wiki/Nation" title="Nation">
          nation
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       One of the features of the Argentine and Uruguayan speaking style is the
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </b>
       : the usage of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       for the second person singular, instead of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . In other Spanish-speaking regions where
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       is used, such as in Chile and Colombia, the use of voseo has at times been considered a nonstandard lower speaking style; whereas in Argentina and Uruguay it is standard.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second person plural pronoun, which is
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       in Spain, is replaced with
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       in Rioplatense, as in most other Latin American dialects. While
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is the formal second person singular pronoun, its plural
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       has a neutral connotation and can be used to address friends and acquaintances as well as in more formal occasions (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       ).
       <i>
        Ustedes
       </i>
       takes a grammatically third- person plural verb.
      </p>

       <i>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the old times,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was used as a respectful term. In Rioplatense, as in most other dialects which employ
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       , this pronoun has become informal, supplanting the use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       (compare
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/You" title="You">
         you
        </a>
       </i>
       in English, which used to be formal singular but has replaced and obliterated the former informal singular pronoun
       <i>
        thou
       </i>
       ). It is used especially for addressing friends and family members (regardless of age), but may also include most acquaintances, such as co-workers, friends of one's friends, etc.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_tenses">
        Usage of tenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Usage of tenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although literary works use the full spectrum of verb inflections, in Rioplatense (as well as many other Spanish dialects), the future tense tends to use a verbal phrase (
       <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">
        periphrasis
       </a>
       ) in the spoken language.
      </p>
      <p>
       This verb phrase is formed by the verb
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       ("to go") followed by the preposition
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ("to") and the main verb in the infinitive. This resembles the English phrase
       <i>
        to be going to
       </i>
       + infinitive verb. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Creo que descansaré un poco
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Creo que voy a descansar un poco
        </i>
        (I think I will rest a little → I think I am going to rest a little)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mañana me visitará mi madre
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Mañana me va a visitar mi vieja
        </i>
        (Tomorrow my mother will visit me → Tomorrow my mother is going to visit me)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La visitaré mañana
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         La voy a visitar mañana
        </i>
        (I will visit her tomorrow → I am going to visit her tomorrow)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       (Spanish: Pretérito perfecto compuesto), just like
       <i>
        pretérito anterior
       </i>
       , is rarely used: the simple past replaces it. However, the Present Perfect is still used in Northwestern Argentina, particularly in the province of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n" title="Tucumán">
        Tucumán
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Juan no ha llegado todavía
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Juan no llegó todavía
        </i>
        (Juan has not arrived yet → Juan did not arrive yet)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         El torneo ha comenzado
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         El torneo empezó
        </i>
        (The tournament has begun → The tournament began)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellas no han votado
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         Ellas no votaron
        </i>
        (They have not voted → They did not vote)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       But, in the subjunctive mood, the
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       is still widely used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         No creo que lo hayan visto ya
        </i>
        (I don't believe they have already seen him)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Espero que lo hayas hecho ayer
        </i>
        (I hope you did it yesterday)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       a reflexive form of verbs is often used - "
       <i>
        se viene
       </i>
       " instead of "
       <i>
        viene''
       </i>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Chilean_Spanish">
        Influence on Chilean Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rioplatense_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influence on Chilean Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Spanish" title="Chilean Spanish">
        Chilean Spanish
       </a>
       there is plenty of lexical influence from the Argentine dialects suggesting a "masked prestige" otherwise not expressed, since the image of Argentine things is usually negative. Influences run across the different social strata of Chile. Argentine tourism in Chile during summer and Chilean tourism in Argentina would influence the speech of the upper class. The middle classes would have Argentine influences by watching football in
       <a href="/wiki/Cable_television" title="Cable television">
        cable television
       </a>
       and by watching Argentine programs in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_television" title="Broadcast television">
        broadcast television
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Cuarta" title="La Cuarta">
         La Cuarta
        </a>
       </i>
       , a "popular"
       <a href="/wiki/Tabloid_(newspaper_format)" title="Tabloid (newspaper format)">
        tabloid
       </a>
       , regularly employs
       <a href="/wiki/Lunfardo" title="Lunfardo">
        lunfardo
       </a>
       words and expressions. Usually Chileans do not recognize the Argentine borrowings as such, claiming they are Chilean terms and expressions. The relation between Argentine dialects and Chilean Spanish is one of "asymmetric permeability", with Chilean Spanish adopting sayings of the Argentine variants but usually not the other way around.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
