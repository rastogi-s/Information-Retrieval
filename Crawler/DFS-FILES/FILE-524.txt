   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Isochrony
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Isochrony
       </b>
       is the postulated
       <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">
        rhythmic
       </a>
       division of time into equal portions by a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       . Rhythm is an aspect of
       <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">
        prosody
       </a>
       , others being
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tempo_of_speech" title="Tempo of speech">
        tempo of speech
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Three alternative ways in which a language can divide time are postulated:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        The duration of every syllable is equal (syllable-timed);
       </li>
       <li>
        The duration of every
        <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">
         mora
        </a>
        is equal (mora-timed).
       </li>
       <li>
        The interval between two stressed
        <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
         syllables
        </a>
        is equal (stress-timed).
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       The idea as such was first expressed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_L._Pike" title="Kenneth L. Pike">
        Kenneth L. Pike
       </a>
       in 1945, though the concept of language naturally occurring in chronologically and rhythmically equal measures is found at least as early as 1775 (in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Prosodia_Rationalis" title="Prosodia Rationalis">
         Prosodia Rationalis
        </a>
       </i>
       ). This has implications for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_typology" title="Language typology">
        language typology
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/David_Abercrombie_(linguist)" title="David Abercrombie (linguist)">
        D. Abercrombie
       </a>
       claimed "As far as is known, every language in the world is spoken with one kind of rhythm or with the other ... French, Telugu and Yoruba ... are syllable-timed languages, ... English, Russian and Arabic ... are stress-timed languages."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While many linguists find the idea of different rhythm types appealing, empirical studies have not been able to find acoustic correlates of the postulated types, calling into question the validity of these types.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, when viewed as a matter of degree, relative differences in the variability of syllable duration across languages have been found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_timing">
        Syllable timing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isochrony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Syllable timing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a syllable-timed language, every syllable is perceived as taking up roughly the same amount of time, though the absolute length of time depends on the
       <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">
        prosody
       </a>
       . Syllable-timed languages tend to give syllables approximately equal prominence and generally lack
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction#Weakening_of_vowel_articulation" title="Vowel reduction">
        reduced vowels
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , Brazilian
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">
        Cantonese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">
        Georgian
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       are commonly quoted as examples of syllable-timed languages. This type of rhythm was originally metaphorically referred to as "machine-gun rhythm" because each underlying rhythmical unit is of the same duration, similar to the transient bullet noise of a machine-gun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the 1950s, speech scientists have tried to show the existence of equal syllable durations in the acoustic speech signal without success. More recent research claims that the duration of consonantal and vocalic intervals is responsible for syllable-timed perception.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mora_timing">
        Mora timing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isochrony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mora timing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gilbertese_language" title="Gilbertese language">
        Gilbertese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Luganda" title="Luganda">
        Ganda
       </a>
       also have regular pacing but are
       <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">
        mora
       </a>
       -timed rather than syllable-timed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Japanese, a V or CV syllable takes up one timing unit. Japanese does not have
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">
        vowel length
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongs
       </a>
       but
       <i>
        double
       </i>
       vowels, so CVV takes twice the time as CV. A final /N/ also takes as much time as a CV syllable and, at least in poetry, so does the extra length of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate" title="Geminate">
        geminate consonant
       </a>
       . However, colloquial language is less settled than poetic language, and the rhythm may vary from one region to another or with time.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Vedic Sanskrit">
        Vedic Sanskrit
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       were also strictly mora-timed.
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       probably was as well.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stress_timing">
        Stress timing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isochrony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Stress timing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a
       <i>
        stress-timed language
       </i>
       , syllables may last different amounts of time, but there is perceived to be a fairly constant amount of time (on average) between consecutive stressed syllables. Consequently, unstressed syllables between stressed syllables tend to be compressed to fit into the time interval: if two stressed syllables are separated by a single unstressed syllable, as in 'COME for TEA', the unstressed syllable will be relatively long, while if a larger number of unstressed syllables intervenes, as in 'COME and have some TEA', the unstressed syllables will be shorter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Stress-timing is sometimes called
       <i>
        Morse-code rhythm
       </i>
       , but any resemblance between the two is only superficial. Stress-timing is strongly related to
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
        vowel reduction
       </a>
       processes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">
        Thai
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
        Danish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
        Faroese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       , European
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       are typical stress-timed languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some stress-timed languages, for example
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , retain unreduced vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Degrees_of_durational_variability">
        Degrees of durational variability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isochrony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Degrees of durational variability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Despite the relative simplicity of the classifications above, in the real world languages do not fit quite so easily into such precise categories. Languages exhibit degrees of durational variability both in relation to other languages and to other standards of the same language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There can be varying degrees of stress-timing within the various standards of a language. Some southern dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , a syllable-timed language, are effectively stress-timed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       English, a stress-timed language, has become so widespread over the globe that some standards tend to be more syllable-timed than the
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">
        North American
       </a>
       standards, an effect which comes from the influence of other languages spoken in the relevant region.
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_English" title="Indian English">
        Indian English
       </a>
       , for example, tends toward syllable-timing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This does not necessarily mean the language standard itself is to be classified as syllable-timed, of course, but rather that this feature is more pronounced. A subtle example is that to a native English speaker, for example, some accents from Wales may sound more syllable-timed.
      </p>
      <p>
       A better-documented case of these varying degrees of stress-timing in a language comes from Portuguese.
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
       is more stress-timed than the
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian standard
       </a>
       . The latter has mixed characteristics
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and varies according to speech rate, sex and dialect. At fast speech rates, Brazilian Portuguese is more stress-timed, while in slow speech rates, it can be more syllable-timed. The accents of rural, southern
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">
        Rio Grande do Sul
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">
        Northeast
       </a>
       (especially
       <a href="/wiki/Bahia" title="Bahia">
        Bahia
       </a>
       ) are considered to sound more syllable-timed than the others, while the southeastern dialects such as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mineiro" title="Mineiro">
         mineiro
        </a>
       </i>
       , in central
       <a href="/wiki/Minas_Gerais" title="Minas Gerais">
        Minas Gerais
       </a>
       , the
       <i>
        paulistano
       </i>
       , of the northern coast and eastern regions of
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(state)" title="São Paulo (state)">
        São Paulo
       </a>
       , and the
       <i>
        fluminense
       </i>
       , along
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_(state)" title="Rio de Janeiro (state)">
        Rio de Janeiro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Esp%C3%ADrito_Santo" title="Espírito Santo">
        Espírito Santo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zona_da_Mata_(Minas_Gerais)" title="Zona da Mata (Minas Gerais)">
        eastern Minas Gerais
       </a>
       as well the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brazilian_Federal_District" title="Brazilian Federal District">
        Federal District
       </a>
       , are most frequently essentially stress-timed. Also, male speakers of Brazilian Portuguese speak faster than female speakers and speak in a more stress-timed manner.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ladefoged has proposed (citing work by Grabe and Low
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) that, since languages differ from each other in terms of the amount of difference between the durations of vowels in adjacent syllables, it is possible to calculate a Pairwise Variability Index (PVI) from measured vowel durations to quantify the differences. The data show that, for example, Dutch (traditionally classed as a stress-timed language) exhibits a higher PVI than Spanish (traditionally a syllable-timed language).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="The_stress-timing.E2.80.93syllable-timing_distinction_as_a_continuum">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_stress-timing–syllable-timing_distinction_as_a_continuum">
        The stress-timing–syllable-timing distinction as a continuum
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Isochrony&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The stress-timing–syllable-timing distinction as a continuum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Given the lack of solid evidence for a clear-cut categorical distinction between the two rhythmical types, it seems reasonable to suggest instead that
       <i>
        all
       </i>
       languages (and all their accents) display
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       types of rhythm to a greater or lesser extent. T. F. Mitchell claimed that there is no language which is totally syllable-timed or totally stress-timed; rather, all languages display both sorts of timing. Languages will, however, differ in which type of timing predominates.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This view was developed by Dauer
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in such a way that a metric was provided allowing researchers to place any language on a scale from maximally stress-timed to maximally syllable-timed. Examples of this approach in use are Dimitrova's study of Bulgarian
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Olivo's study of the rhythm of Ashanti Twi.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
