   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Helmert transformation
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Helmert transformation
       </b>
       (named after
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Robert_Helmert" title="Friedrich Robert Helmert">
        Friedrich Robert Helmert
       </a>
       , 1843–1917) is a transformation method within a three-dimensional space. It is frequently used in
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy
       </a>
       to produce distortion-free transformations from one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       to another. The Helmert transformation is also called a
       <b>
        seven-parameter transformation
       </b>
       and is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Similarity_transformation_(geometry)" title="Similarity transformation (geometry)">
        similarity transformation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variations">
        Variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helmert_transformation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is not always necessary to use the seven parameter transformation, sometimes it is sufficient to use the
       <b>
        five parameter transformation
       </b>
       , composed of three translations, one rotation (about the Z-axis) and one change of scale.
      </p>
      <p>
       A special case is the two-dimensional Helmert transformation. Here, only four parameters are needed (two translations, one scaling, one rotation). These can be determined from two known points; if more points are available then checks can be made.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Restrictions">
        Restrictions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helmert_transformation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Restrictions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Helmert transformation only uses one scale factor, so it is not suitable for:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The manipulation of measured drawings and
        <a href="/wiki/Photography" title="Photography">
         photographs
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        The comparison of paper deformations while
        <a href="/wiki/Image_scanner" title="Image scanner">
         scanning
        </a>
        old plans and maps.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In these cases, use another
       <a href="/wiki/Affine_transformation" title="Affine transformation">
        affine transformation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Application">
        Application
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helmert_transformation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Application">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Helmert transformation is used, among other things, in
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy
       </a>
       to transform the coordinates of the point from one coordinate system into another. Using it, it becomes possible to convert regional
       <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
        surveying
       </a>
       points into the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       locations used by
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        GPS
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the process, the
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Kr%C3%BCger_coordinate_system" title="Gauss–Krüger coordinate system">
        Gauss–Krüger coordinate
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       , plus the height,
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       , are converted into 3D values in steps:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Calculation of the ellipsoidal latitude, longitude and height (
        <i>
         W
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         L
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         H
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Calculation of
        <i>
         X
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Y
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         Z
        </i>
        relative to the
        <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
         reference ellipsoid
        </a>
        of surveying
       </li>
       <li>
        7-parameter transformation (where
        <i>
         X
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Y
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         Z
        </i>
        change almost evenly, a few hundred metres at most, and the distances change a few mm per km).
       </li>
       <li>
        Because of this, terrestrially measured positions can be compared with GPS data; these can then be brought into the surveying as new points — transformed in the opposite order.
       </li>
      </ol>

           </semantics>
          </math>

       For the reverse transformation, each element is multiplied by -1.
      </p>
      <p>
       The seven parameters are determined for each region with three or more "identical points" of both systems. To bring them into agreement, the small inconsistencies (usually only a few cm) are
       <a href="/wiki/Least_squares_adjustment" title="Least squares adjustment">
        adjusted
       </a>
       using the method of
       <a href="/wiki/Least_squares" title="Least squares">
        least squares
       </a>
       – that is, eliminated in a statistically plausible manner.
      </p>

      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>

This is not precise enough for surveying, and the Ordnance Survey supplements these results by using a lookup table of further translations in order to reach 1 cm accuracy.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calculating_the_parameters">
        Calculating the parameters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helmert_transformation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Calculating the parameters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       If the transformation parameters are unknown, they can be calculated with reference points (that is, points whose coordinates are known before and after the transformation. Since a total of seven parameters (three translations, one scale, three rotations) have to be determined, at least two points and one coordinate of a third point (for example, the Z-coordinate) must be known. This gives a
       <a href="/wiki/System_of_linear_equations" title="System of linear equations">
        system of linear equations
       </a>
       with seven equations and seven unknowns, which can be solved.
      </p>
      <p>
       In practice, it is best to use more points. Through this correspondence, more accuracy is obtained, and a statistical assessment of the results becomes possible. In this case, the calculation is adjusted with the Gaussian
       <a href="/wiki/Least_squares" title="Least squares">
        least squares
       </a>
       method.
      </p>
      <p>
       A numerical value for the accuracy of the transformation parameters is obtained by calculating the values at the reference points, and weighting the results relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Centroid" title="Centroid">
        centroid
       </a>
       of the points.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the method is mathematically rigorous, it is entirely dependent on the rigour of the parameters that are used. In practice, these parameters are computed from the inclusion of at least three known points in the networks. However the accuracy of these will affect the following transformation parameters, as these points will contain observation errors. Therefore, a "real-world" transformation will only be a best estimate and should contain a statistical measure of its quality.
      </p>
