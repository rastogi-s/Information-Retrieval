      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As in most languages swear words tend to come from semantic domains considered
       <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">
        taboo
       </a>
       such as the domains of human excretions, sexuality, and religion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As in most languages, in Spanish swearing serves several functions in discourse, as emphatic interjections expressing emotion, as expressions of interpersonal
       <a href="/wiki/Stance_(linguistics)" title="Stance (linguistics)">
        stances
       </a>
       such as aggression or as expression of gender identity, and as forms of linguistic play.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon39_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon39-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish insults are often of a sexual nature, taking the form of implying a lack of sexual decency if the insulted person is a woman (e.g.
       <i>
        puta
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ramera
       </i>
       "whore",
       <i>
        perra
       </i>
       "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bitch_(insult)" title="Bitch (insult)">
        bitch
       </a>
       ") or implying a lack of masculinity if the insulted person is male (e.g.
       <i>
        maricón
       </i>
       "faggot",
       <i>
        puto
       </i>
       "male prostitute").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimes_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grimes-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A particularly forceful Spanish insult is any mention of someone else's mother, (called
       <i>
        mentar la madre
       </i>
       ), including also in its strongest form insinuations or mentions of sexual relations (e.g.
       <i>
        ¡chinga tu madre!
       </i>
       "fuck your mother!").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Emphatic exclamations, not aimed to insult but to express strong emotion, often include words for sexual relations (e.g.
       <i>
        ¡joder!
       </i>
       "fuck!",
       <i>
        ¡chingados!
       </i>
       "fuckers!") or to excretions or sexual organs (
       <i>
        ¡mierda!
       </i>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Shit" title="Shit">
        shit
       </a>
       !",
       <i>
        ¡carajo!
       </i>
       "penis!"). Sexual taboo words that describe a masculine sexuality may be used in a positive sense (e.g.
       <i>
        cabrón
       </i>
       "male goat",
       <i>
        gallo
       </i>
       "rooster",
       <i>
        chingón
       </i>
       "fucker").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grimes_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grimes-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         <span class="unicode haudio">
          <span class="fn">
           <span style="white-space:nowrap">

            </a>
           </span>

            Play
           </a>
          </span>
          <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">
           (
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help">
            <span style="cursor:help;">
             help
            </span>
           </a>
           ·
           <a href="/wiki/File:Shave_and_a_Haircut_in_C.mid" title="File:Shave and a Haircut in C.mid">

            </span>

          </small>
         </span>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The official definition of this word in Spain is "an adult male goat".
      </p>
      <p>
       The modern Spanish version, including Mexican Spanish, has different connotations depending on the place or situation, but is commonly accepted as the Spanish equivalent for the English profanity "
       <a href="/wiki/Motherfucker" title="Motherfucker">
        motherfucker
       </a>
       ". As an adjective it is equivalent to "tough," as in, "It is tough" (
       <i>
        Está cabrón
       </i>
       ). In offensive mode it means "asshole".
      </p>
      <p>
       The seven-note musical flourish known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Shave_and_a_Haircut" title="Shave and a Haircut">
        shave and a haircut
       </a>
       (two bits), commonly played on
       <a href="/wiki/Vehicle_horn" title="Vehicle horn">
        car horns
       </a>
       , is associated with the seven-syllable phrase
       <i>
        ¡Chinga tu madre, cabrón!
       </i>
       ("Fuck your mother, asshole!"). Playing the jingle on a car horn can result in a hefty fine for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Traffic_violation" title="Traffic violation">
        traffic violation
       </a>
       if done in the presence of police, or
       <a href="/wiki/Road_rage" title="Road rage">
        road rage
       </a>
       if aimed at another driver or a pedestrian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In Mexico,
       <i>
        cabrón
       </i>
       refers to a man whose wife cheats on him without protest from him, or even with his encouragement. In Puerto Rico, it has a similar connotation, but it and
       <i>
        cabrona
       </i>
       (for women) are used regardless of whether the cheating victim knows about his/her wife/husband cheating or not, and whether the couple is separated or not.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chingada">
        <i>
         Chingada
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Chingada">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This term has several acceptations, including the action of
       <i>
        chingar
       </i>
       meaning cutting-off long tails of animals. As a vulgar or profane term it has connotations related to disgrace, difficult, miserable, or unfortunate events or conditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Though the negative expression "
       <i>
        hijo de la chingada
       </i>
       " literally translates to "son of the
       <i>
        chingada
       </i>
       " it would be erroneous to attribute the term "la chingada" to a woman or a personification of a female.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Puerto Rico, "chingada" means sex—"chingada" is similar in meaning to the term "fuck" in English, "una chingada" means "one sexual act" and "la chingada" usually means "the girl who had sex". Likewise, the term "chingo" means "had sex" or "will have sex" (as in "yo te chingo a tu esposa"—"I have-or will have-sex with your wife").
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pendejo">
        <i>
         Pendejo
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Pendejo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="391" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
         Look up
         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pendejo#Spanish" title="wiktionary:pendejo">
            pendejo
           </a>
          </b>
         </i>
         in
         <a href="/wiki/Wiktionary" title="Wiktionary">
          Wiktionary
         </a>
         , the free dictionary.
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <i>
        Pendejo
       </i>
       (according to the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diccionario_de_la_lengua_espa%C3%B1ola_de_la_Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Diccionario de la lengua española de la Real Academia Española">
         Diccionario de la lengua española de la Real Academia Española
        </a>
       </i>
       , lit. "a pubic hair'
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Chicano" title="Chicano">
        Chicano
       </a>
       poet
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Antonio_Burciaga" title="José Antonio Burciaga">
        José Antonio Burciaga
       </a>
       , "basically describes someone who is stupid or does something stupid."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Burciaga said that the word is often used while not in polite conversation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Pendejo
       </i>
       is equivalent to the English expression
       <i>
        dumb-ass
       </i>
       , which literally means, "as dumb as a donkey".
      </p>
      <p>
       Burciaga said that
       <i>
        pendejo
       </i>
       "is probably the least offensive" of the various Spanish profanity words beginning in "p", but that calling someone a
       <i>
        pendejo
       </i>
       is "stronger" than calling someone
       <i>
        estúpido
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Burciaga said "Among friends it can be taken lightly, but for others it is better to be angry enough to back it up."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Mexico, "pendejo" most commonly refers to a "fool", "idiot" or "asshole". In Mexico there are many proverbs that refer to
       <i>
        pendejos
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon40_15-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon40-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pinche">
        <i>
         Pinche
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pinche">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="391" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
         Look up
         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pinche#Spanish" title="wiktionary:pinche">
            pinche
           </a>
          </b>
         </i>
         in
         <a href="/wiki/Wiktionary" title="Wiktionary">
          Wiktionary
         </a>
         , the free dictionary.
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <i>
        Pinche
       </i>
       has different meanings:
      </p>
      <p>
       In Spain, where Castilian Spanish is the national language, the word may refer to a sous chef.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon39_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon39-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, its main and usual definition is that of a restaurant chef assistant or a kitchen helper who helps prepare the ingredients, assists in cooking the food and cleans the utensils. Some restaurants in Spain have the name "El Pinche", to the great amusement of Mexican and Chicano tourists.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon39_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon39-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In Mexico, the word can be inappropriate or offensive depending on tone and context. Furthermore, it is often used as an
       <a href="/wiki/Expletive_attributive" title="Expletive attributive">
        expletive attributive
       </a>
       (much like the English terms "damn", "fucking", or
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Bloody" title="Bloody">
        bloody
       </a>
       "), as in
       <i>
        estos pinches aguacates están podridos…
       </i>
       ("These damn avocados are rotten…");
       <i>
        Pinche Mario ya no ha venido…
       </i>
       ("Fucking Mario hasn't come yet"); or
       <i>
        ¿¡Quieres callarte la pinche boca!?
       </i>
       ("Would you shut your bloody mouth?"). Another meaning is used as an insult, as in
       <i>
        pinche güey
       </i>
       ("loser"), or to describe an object of poor quality,
       <i>
        está muy pinche
       </i>
       ("It really sucks").
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Therefore, it can be said in front of adults, but possibly not children, depending on one's moral compass. Sometimes
       <i>
        pinchudo(a)
       </i>
       is said instead.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It refers to a mean-spirited person.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GladsteinChacon39_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-GladsteinChacon39-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In El Salvador, the term means "tiny" or "very little" and it is not necessarily considered vulgar.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Nicaragua, the term means "stingy" or "cheapskate" and it is not vulgar.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Puta">
        <i>
         Puta
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Puta">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        puta
       </i>
       (uniform in all Hispanic nations), similar to the Portuguese derives from the Latin
       <i>
        putus
       </i>
       meaning boy and
       <i>
        puta
       </i>
       , girl. It has roughly the same meaning as English
       <i>
        whore
       </i>
       , but in some regions is considered very offensive when specifically directed at a woman. It can also translate as "bitch". The male version,
       <i>
        puto
       </i>
       can be used to refer to a male whore, but in Mexico it is a derrogatory term for a homosexual man i.e. "gay whore".
      </p>
      <p>
       It is, however, considered less offensive when used as an adjective, in which case it is equal to the English "fucking," similar to the Mexican word
       <i>
        pinche
       </i>
       ; for example,
       <i>
        dame las putas llaves
       </i>
       ("give me the fucking keys") or
       <i>
        es puto idiota
       </i>
       (he's a fucking idiot).
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition, the phrase,
       <i>
        hijo de puta
       </i>
       , literally means "son of a whore", (which translates to "son of a bitch" in English ) can often mean "motherfucker."
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Joder">
        <i>
         Joder
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_profanity&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Joder">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The verb
       <i>
        joder/joderse
       </i>
       means "to fuck," and follows the same rules as its English equivalent: "to fuck somebody" e.g.
       <i>
        anoche, Juan y su novia se jodieron
       </i>
       ("last night Juan and his girlfriend fucked"),
       <i>
        no me jodas
       </i>
       (don't fuck with me), or
       <i>
        lo has jodido
       </i>
       (you've fucked up). It cannot be used as an adjective, like the English "fucking", but is often used as a light interjection:
       <i>
        ¡Joder! Olvidé mi abrigo
       </i>
       ("Shit! I've forgotten my coat").
      </p>
      <p>
       Alternative ways demonstrating the meaning in the first context are:
       <i>
        follar, cogerse
       </i>
       (Argentina), and
       <i>
        echar un polvo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
