   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lexical similarity
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        lexical similarity
       </b>
       is a measure of the degree to which the word sets of two given
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       are similar. A lexical similarity of 1 (or 100%) would mean a total overlap between vocabularies, whereas 0 means there are no common words.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are different ways to define the lexical similarity and the results vary accordingly. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue
       </a>
       's method of calculation consists in comparing a standardized set of wordlists and counting those forms that show similarity in both form and meaning. Using such a method,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       was evaluated to have a lexical similarity of 60% with
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       and 27% with
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lexical similarity can be used to evaluate the degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        genetic relationship
       </a>
       between two languages. Percentages higher than 85% usually indicate that the two languages being compared are likely to be related
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The lexical similarity is only one indication of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutual intelligibility
       </a>
       of the two languages, since the latter also depends on the degree of phonetical, morphological, and syntactical similarity. It is worth noting that the variations due to differing wordlists weigh on this. For example, lexical similarity between French and English is considerable in lexical fields relating to culture, whereas their similarity is smaller as far as basic (function) words are concerned. Unlike mutual intelligibility, lexical similarity can only be symmetrical.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

         Sardinian
        </a>
        variety the lexical similarity was calculated. The answer could drastically change the data, since Ethnologue controversially considers Sardinian to be a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrolanguage" title="Macrolanguage">
         macrolanguage
        </a>
        by adding also two Sardo-Corsican transitional lects spoken in Sardinia (Corsican is in fact the closest lect to Tuscan and Italian of all in the Neolatin panorama). Based on the studies of Roberto Bolognesi and Wilbert Heeringa, the average divergence between the Sardinian language (without considering the aforementioned Corso-Sardinian lects) and Italian is around 48,7%, ranging from a minimum dialectal degree of divergence being 46,6% to the highest one of 51,1%.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bolognesi_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bolognesi-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        That would make the various dialects of Sardinian slightly more divergent from Italian than Spanish (with an average degree of divergence from Italian being around 46,0%) is.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bolognesi_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bolognesi-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        "-" denotes that comparison data are not available.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the case of English-French lexical similarity, at least two other studies
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        estimate the number of English words directly inherited from French at 28.3% and 41% respectively, with respectively 28.24% and 15% of other English words derived from Latin, putting English-French lexical similarity at around 0.56, with reciprocally lower English-German lexical similarities. Another study estimates the number of English words with an Italic origin at 51%, consistent with the two previous analyses.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
