      <p>
       <b>
        Marinus of Tyre
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Μαρῖνος ὁ Τύριος
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Marînos o Týrios
       </i>
       ;
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          AD
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
       70–130) was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_people" title="Greek people">
        Greek
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization" title="Hellenistic civilization">
        Hellenized
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_geographers" title="Greek geographers">
        geographer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartographer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematician
       </a>
       , who founded mathematical geography and provided the underpinnings of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">
        Claudius Ptolemy
       </a>
       's influential
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
        Life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinus_of_Tyre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Marinus was originally from
       <a href="/wiki/Tyre,_Lebanon" title="Tyre, Lebanon">
        Tyre
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_province" title="Roman province">
        province
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Syria" title="Roman Syria">
        Syria
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He and his work were a precursor to that of the great geographer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">
        Claudius Ptolemy
       </a>
       , who used Marinus's work as a source for his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       and acknowledges his great obligations to him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HarleyWoodward_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HarleyWoodward-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ptolemy said, "Marinus says of the merchant class generally that they are only intent on their business, and have little interest in exploration, and that often through their love of boasting they magnify distances."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later, Marinos was also cited by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arab" title="Arab">
        Arab
       </a>
       geographer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_al-Hasan_%27Al%C4%AB_al-Mas%27%C5%ABd%C4%AB" title="Abu al-Hasan 'Alī al-Mas'ūdī">
        al-Masʿūdī
       </a>
       . Beyond this, little is known of his life.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">
        Legacy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Marinus_of_Tyre&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legacy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Marinus's geographical treatise is lost and known only from Ptolemy's remarks. He introduced improvements to the construction of maps and developed a system of nautical charts. His chief legacy is that he first assigned to each place a proper
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . His
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zero_meridian" title="Zero meridian">
        zero meridian
       </a>
       ran through the westernmost land known to him, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isles_of_the_Blessed" title="Isles of the Blessed">
        Isles of the Blessed
       </a>
       around the location of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Verde_Islands" title="Cape Verde Islands">
        Cape Verde Islands
       </a>
       . He used the parallel of
       <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">
        Rhodes
       </a>
       for measurements of latitude. Ptolemy mentions several revisions of Marinus's geographical work, which is often dated to
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         AD
        </span>
       </span>
       114 although this is uncertain. Marinus estimated a length of 180,000
       <a href="/wiki/Stadion_(unit)" title="Stadion (unit)">
        stadia
       </a>
       for the equator, roughly corresponding
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to a circumference of the Earth of 33,300 km, about 17% less than the actual value.
      </p>
      <p>
       He also carefully studied the works of his predecessors and the diaries of travelers. His maps were the first in the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       to show
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       . He also invented
       <a href="/wiki/Equirectangular_projection" title="Equirectangular projection">
        equirectangular projection
       </a>
       , which is still used in map creation today. A few of Marinus's opinions are reported by Ptolemy. Marinus was of the opinion that the
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">
        World Ocean
       </a>
       was separated into an eastern and a western part by the continents of
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       . He thought that the inhabited world stretched in latitude from
       <a href="/wiki/Thule" title="Thule">
        Thule
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       ) to
       <a href="/wiki/Agisymba" title="Agisymba">
        Agisymba
       </a>
       (around the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       ) and in longitude from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isles_of_the_Blessed" title="Isles of the Blessed">
        Isles of the Blessed
       </a>
       (around the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canaries
       </a>
       ) to Shera (
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       ). Marinus also coined the term
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       , referring to the opposite of the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">
        Arctic Circle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
