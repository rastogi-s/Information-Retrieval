      <p>
       <b>
        Largo di Torre Argentina
       </b>
       is a square in
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , with four
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">
        Roman Republican
       </a>
       temples and the remains of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompey%27s_Theatre" title="Pompey's Theatre">
        Pompey's Theatre
       </a>
       . It is in the ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Campus_Martius" title="Campus Martius">
        Campus Martius
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longhi1960_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Longhi1960-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name of the square comes from the
       <i>
        Torre Argentina
       </i>
       , which takes its name from the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">
        Strasbourg
       </a>
       whose Latin name was
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Argentoratum" title="Argentoratum">
         Argentoratum
        </a>
       </i>
       . In 1503, the Papal Master of Ceremonies
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Johannes_Burckardt" title="Johannes Burckardt">
        Johannes Burckardt
       </a>
       , who came from Strasbourg and was known as "Argentinus", built in
       <i>
        via del Sudario
       </i>
       a palace (now at number 44), called
       <i>
        Casa del Burcardo
       </i>
       , to which the tower is annexed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The other tower in the square is not the one giving the name to the place but the medieval
       <i>
        Torre del Papito
       </i>
       ("Little Pope's Tower"), attributed by tradition to
       <a href="/wiki/Antipope_Anacletus_II" title="Antipope Anacletus II">
        Antipope Anacletus II
       </a>
       Pierleoni, allegedly not a tall person.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:MapTheatreofPompey.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2121" data-file-width="2472" height="189" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MapTheatreofPompey.jpg/220px-MapTheatreofPompey.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MapTheatreofPompey.jpg/330px-MapTheatreofPompey.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/MapTheatreofPompey.jpg/440px-MapTheatreofPompey.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:MapTheatreofPompey.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A map showing Pompey's Theatre and other Roman structures in black and modern structures in red.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">
        Italian unification
       </a>
       , it was decided to reconstruct part of Rome (1909), demolishing the zone of Torre Argentina. However, during the demolition work in 1927, the colossal head and arms of a marble statue were discovered. The archeological investigation brought to light the presence of a
       <i>
        holy area
       </i>
       , dating to the Republican era, with four temples and part of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pompey%27s_Theater" title="Pompey's Theater">
        Pompey's Theater
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       was
       <a href="/wiki/Assassination_of_Julius_Caesar" title="Assassination of Julius Caesar">
        killed
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Curia" title="Curia">
        Curia
       </a>
       of the Theatre of Pompey, and the spot he was believed to be assassinated is in the square.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coarelli2014_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Coarelli2014-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Largo_di_Torre_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Roman temples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="960" data-file-width="1280" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg/250px-PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg/375px-PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/12/PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg/500px-PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PICT0510_-_Largo_di_Torre_Argentina.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Temple A
         </i>
         or Temple of Juturna
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg/250px-Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg/375px-Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg/500px-Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Largo_di_Torre_Argentina_Temple_B_4.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Temple B
         </i>
         , dedicated to
         <i>
          Fortuna Huiusce Diei
         </i>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The four temples, originally designated by the letters
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        B
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        C
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        D
       </i>
       , front onto a paved street, which was reconstructed in the imperial era, after the fire of AD 80. The area was delineated to the North by the
       <i>
        Hecatostylum
       </i>
       (one-hundred columns porch) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Baths_of_Agrippa" title="Baths of Agrippa">
        Baths of Agrippa
       </a>
       , and to the South by the buildings related to the
       <a href="/wiki/Circus_Flaminius" title="Circus Flaminius">
        Circus Flaminius
       </a>
       , to the East by the great porched square of
       <i>
        Porticus Minucia Frumentaria
       </i>
       , and to the West by the
       <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_Pompey" title="Theatre of Pompey">
        Theatre of Pompey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Longhi1930_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Longhi1930-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jr1992_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jr1992-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Temple A
       </i>
       was built in the 3rd century BC, and is probably the
       <i>
        Temple of
        <a href="/wiki/Juturna" title="Juturna">
         Juturna
        </a>
       </i>
       built by
       <a href="/wiki/Gaius_Lutatius_Catulus" title="Gaius Lutatius Catulus">
        Gaius Lutatius Catulus
       </a>
       after his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Aegates_Islands" title="Battle of the Aegates Islands">
        victory
       </a>
       against the
       <a href="/wiki/Carthage" title="Carthage">
        Carthaginians
       </a>
       in 241 BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was later rebuilt into a church, whose apse is still present.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Temple B
       </i>
       , a circular temple
       <i>
        (
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tholos_(building)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tholos (building) (page does not exist)">
         tholus
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       with six columns remaining, was built by
       <a href="/wiki/Quintus_Lutatius_Catulus" title="Quintus Lutatius Catulus">
        Quintus Lutatius Catulus
       </a>
       in 101 BC in
       <a href="/wiki/Votum" title="Votum">
        fulfillment of his vow
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vercellae" title="Battle of Vercellae">
        Battle of Vercellae
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The temple
       <i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Glossary_of_ancient_Roman_religion#aedes" title="Glossary of ancient Roman religion">
         aedes
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       was devoted to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fortuna_Huiusce_Diei" title="Fortuna Huiusce Diei">
         Fortuna Huiusce Diei
        </a>
       </i>
       , "the
       <a href="/wiki/Fortuna" title="Fortuna">
        Fortune
       </a>
       of This Day." The colossal statue found during excavations and now kept in the
       <a href="/wiki/Capitoline_Museums" title="Capitoline Museums">
        Capitoline Museums
       </a>
       was the statue of the goddess herself. Only the head, the arms, and the legs were made of marble: the other parts, covered by the dress, were of other materials, probably a wooden frame. This is known as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acrolithic" title="Acrolithic">
        acrolithic
       </a>
       statue.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2492" data-file-width="2464" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg/220px-Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg/330px-Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg/440px-Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fortuna_huiusce_diei_-_Head.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Detail of portrait head of Fortuna huiusce diei
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Temple C
       </i>
       is the most ancient of the three, dating back to 4th or 3rd century BC, and was probably devoted to
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Feronia_(goddess)" title="Feronia (goddess)">
         Feronia
        </a>
       </i>
       the ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Italic_peoples" title="Ancient Italic peoples">
        Italic
       </a>
       goddess of fertility. After the fire of 80 AD, this temple was restored, and the white and black mosaic of the inner temple cell dates back to this restoration.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Temple D
       </i>
       is the largest of the four, dates back to the 2nd century BC with Late Republican restorations, and was devoted to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lares" title="Lares">
         Lares
        </a>
        Permarini
       </i>
       (Lares who protect sailors), but only a small part of it has been excavated (a street covers the most of it). It was vowed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Praetor" title="Praetor">
        praetor
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lucius_Aemilius_Regillus" title="Lucius Aemilius Regillus">
        Lucius Aemilius Regillus
       </a>
       , while engaged in a naval battle with the fleet of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antiochus_the_Great" title="Antiochus the Great">
        Antiochus the Great
       </a>
       in 190 B.C., and dedicated by
       <a href="/wiki/Marcus_Aemilius_Lepidus_(consul_187_BC)" title="Marcus Aemilius Lepidus (consul 187 BC)">
        M. Aemilius Lepidus
       </a>
       , when
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_censor" title="Roman censor">
        censor
       </a>
       , on 22 December, 179.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the doors of the temple was a dedicatory inscription in
       <a href="/wiki/Saturnian_(poetry)" title="Saturnian (poetry)">
        Saturnian metre
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is recorded as standing
       <i>
        in porticu Minucia
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and therefore its exact site depends on that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Portico" title="Portico">
        porticus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="18th_century_opera_house">
        18th century opera house
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Largo_di_Torre_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 18th century opera house">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Teatro_Argentina" title="Teatro Argentina">
        Teatro Argentina
       </a>
       was an 18th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Opera_house" title="Opera house">
        opera house
       </a>
       and theatre located in the square. The premieres of many notable operas took place there. They include
       <a href="/wiki/Gioachino_Rossini" title="Gioachino Rossini">
        Gioachino Rossini
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Barber_of_Seville" title="The Barber of Seville">
         The Barber of Seville
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1816 and
       <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Verdi" title="Giuseppe Verdi">
        Giuseppe Verdi
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/I_due_Foscari" title="I due Foscari">
         I due Foscari
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1844 and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_battaglia_di_Legnano" title="La battaglia di Legnano">
         La battaglia di Legnano
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1849.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cat_shelter">
        Cat shelter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Largo_di_Torre_Argentina&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cat shelter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Located in
       <i>
        Largo Argentina
       </i>
       is the Torre Argentina Cat Sanctuary, a
       <a href="/wiki/No-kill_shelter" title="No-kill shelter">
        no-kill shelter
       </a>
       because in Italy there is a no-kill law for homeless cats (of which Rome has many), and is guided by a group of volunteers who sterilize stray cats in Rome.
      </p>
