      <p>
       <b>
        Grammatical gender in Spanish
       </b>
       refers to the classification of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_nouns" title="Spanish nouns">
        Spanish nouns
       </a>
       to form
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        grammatical gender
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_determiners" title="Spanish determiners">
        Spanish determiners
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_adjectives" title="Spanish adjectives">
        Spanish adjectives
       </a>
       . All Spanish nouns have a lexical gender of either masculine or feminine, and most nouns referring to male humans are grammatically masculine, while most referring to females are feminine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        markedness
       </a>
       , the masculine is unmarked and the feminine is marked in Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Compared to other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Kinship_terminology" title="Kinship terminology">
        kinship terminology
       </a>
       derives more female terms from male terms: for example, uncle/aunt is
       <i>
        tío
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        tía
       </i>
       in Spanish but
       <i>
        oncle
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        tante
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ; brother/sister is
       <i>
        hermano
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        hermana
       </i>
       in Spanish but
       <i>
        fratello
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        sorella
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        Spanish pronouns
       </a>
       also uniquely retain feminine forms of the first and second person plural (
       <i>
        nosotras
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vosotras
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenberg_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eisenberg-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most common genders are called masculine and feminine, while some
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        Spanish pronouns
       </a>
       are considered to have neuter gender. A few nouns are said to be of "ambiguous" gender, meaning that they are sometimes treated as masculine and sometimes as feminine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, the terms "common gender" and "epicene gender" are used to classify ways in which grammatical gender interacts (or not) with "natural gender" (the sex of a person or animal). Many adjectives ending in
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       may be masculine or feminine, while adjectives ending in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       are almost always masculine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Masculine">
        Masculine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Masculine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The masculine (
       <i>
        masculino
       </i>
       ): As a general rule, nouns ending in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        libro
       </i>
       'book',
       <i>
        zapato
       </i>
       'shoe') and nouns which refer to males (
       <i>
        profesor
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        padre
       </i>
       'father') are masculine. Exceptionally,
       <i>
        mano
       </i>
       ('hand') is feminine. Also some colloquial shortened forms of feminine nouns end with
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        la foto(grafía)
       </i>
       ('photograph'),
       <i>
        la disco(teca)
       </i>
       ('discothèque'),
       <i>
        la moto(cicleta)
       </i>
       ('motorcycle'),
       <i>
        la radio(difusión)
       </i>
       ('radio [broadcasting]').
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Feminine">
        Feminine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Feminine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The feminine (
       <i>
        femenino
       </i>
       ): As a general rule, nouns ending in
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        casa
       </i>
       'house',
       <i>
        boca
       </i>
       'mouth') and nouns which refer to females (
       <i>
        madre
       </i>
       'mother',
       <i>
        mujer
       </i>
       'woman, wife') are feminine. Similarly, the endings
       <i>
        -ción
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -sión
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -dad
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -tad
       </i>
       indicate feminine gender. Exceptionally,
       <i>
        día
       </i>
       ('day') is masculine. Likewise, nouns of Greek origin ending in
       <i>
        -ma
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        drama
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        problema
       </i>
       ) or "-ta" ("planeta", "profeta") are masculine. (These "Greek" nouns can often be identified by their derived adjectives ending in
       <i>
        -mático
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Neuter">
        Neuter
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Neuter">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The neuter (
       <i>
        neutro
       </i>
       ): The pronoun
       <i>
        ello
       </i>
       ('it, the aforementioned concept') and the demonstrative pronouns
       <i>
        esto
       </i>
       ('this [idea or unnamed thing]'),
       <i>
        eso
       </i>
       ('that'), and
       <i>
        aquello
       </i>
       ('that') are said to have neuter gender because they do not have a gendered noun as their antecedent, but rather refer to a whole idea, a clause, or an object that has not been named in the discourse. The neuter article
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (not to be confused with the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        masculine/neuter object (clitic) pronoun
       </a>
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       ), is not used with nouns, since there are no neuter nouns. It is used with adjectives to create abstract "nouns":
       <i>
        lo bueno
       </i>
       , the good part (of it);
       <i>
        lo importante
       </i>
       , what is important (about it). Contrast
       <i>
        el bueno/la buena
       </i>
       , the good person or thing.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Common">
        Common
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Common">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       "Common gender" (
       <i>
        común
       </i>
       ) is the term applied to those nouns, referring to persons, that keep the same form regardless of the sex of the person, but which change their grammatical gender. For example,
       <i>
        el violinista
       </i>
       ('the male violinist'),
       <i>
        la violinista
       </i>
       ('the female violinist'),
       <i>
        el mártir
       </i>
       ('the male martyr'),
       <i>
        la mártir
       </i>
       ('the female martyr'),
       <i>
        el testigo
       </i>
       ('the male witness'),
       <i>
        la testigo
       </i>
       ('the female witness'),
       <i>
        el espía
       </i>
       ('the male spy'),
       <i>
        la espía
       </i>
       ('the female spy'), etc. To this gender belong
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        present participles
       </a>
       derived from active verbs and used as nouns, such as
       <i>
        el estudiante
       </i>
       ('the male student'),
       <i>
        la estudiante
       </i>
       ('the female student'),
       <i>
        el atacante
       </i>
       ('the male attacker'),
       <i>
        la atacante
       </i>
       ('the female attacker'),
       <i>
        el presidente
       </i>
       ('the male president'),
       <i>
        la presidente
       </i>
       ('the female president'—although
       <i>
        la presidenta
       </i>
       is also often used), etc.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Epicene">
        Epicene
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Epicene">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       "Epicene gender" (
       <i>
        epiceno
       </i>
       ) is the term applied to those nouns that have only one grammatical gender, masculine or feminine, but can refer to a living creature of either sex. Most animal names are of this type. E.g.:
       <i>
        el ratón
       </i>
       ('mouse'),
       <i>
        la rata
       </i>
       ('rat'),
       <i>
        la rana
       </i>
       ('frog'),
       <i>
        la comadreja
       </i>
       ('weasel'),
       <i>
        la liebre
       </i>
       ('hare'),
       <i>
        la hormiga
       </i>
       ('ant'),
       <i>
        el búho
       </i>
       ('owl'),
       <i>
        el escarabajo
       </i>
       ('beetle'),
       <i>
        el buitre
       </i>
       ('vulture'),
       <i>
        el delfín
       </i>
       ('dolphin'),
       <i>
        el cóndor
       </i>
       ('condor'),
       <i>
        la paloma
       </i>
       ('dove'),
       <i>
        la llama
       </i>
       ('llama'). To specify sex, a modifying word is added, with no change of gender:
       <i>
        el delfín macho
       </i>
       ('the male dolphin'),
       <i>
        el delfín hembra
       </i>
       ('the female dolphin'),
       <i>
        la comadreja macho
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la comadreja hembra
       </i>
       (male and female weasels respectively).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguous">
        Ambiguous
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ambiguous">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Ambiguous nouns (
       <i>
        ambiguo
       </i>
       ) whose grammatical gender varies in usage are said to be of "ambiguous" gender. Often the change of gender brings about a change of connotation. E.g.:
       <i>
        el mar
       </i>
       ('the sea'),
       <i>
        la mar
       </i>
       ('the sea', poetic or among sailors),
       <i>
        el calor
       </i>
       ('heat'),
       <i>
        la calor
       </i>
       (archaic),
       <i>
        el azúcar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la azúcar
       </i>
       ('sugar').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Changes">
        Changes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Changes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the recent
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Spanish_language" title="History of the Spanish language">
        history of the Spanish language
       </a>
       , there is a unidirectional tendency for words with unusual gender to be regularized by analogy to other words of their class. For example, in some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects" title="Spanish dialects">
        Spanish dialects
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        la tribu
       </i>
       ("tribe"), which is the only word ending in
       <i>
        -u
       </i>
       to be feminine, has been changed to
       <i>
        el tribo
       </i>
       . The word
       <i>
        idioma
       </i>
       ("language"), which is masculine in
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        standard Spanish
       </a>
       , has become feminine in some dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Feminism">
        Feminism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Feminism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_neutrality_in_languages_with_grammatical_gender" title="Gender neutrality in languages with grammatical gender">
        Gender neutrality in languages with grammatical gender
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Feminist_movements_and_ideologies" title="Feminist movements and ideologies">
        feminist movements and ideologies
       </a>
       have criticized certain grammatical rules in Spanish which privilege the masculine over the feminism. These include the rule that a masculine plural must be used for any group with at least one male; the use of the masculine definite article for infinitives (e.g.
       <i>
        el amar
       </i>
       , not
       <i>
        la amar
       </i>
       ); and the permissibility of using Spanish male pronouns for female referents but not vice versa (e.g.
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       includes women,
       <i>
        la que
       </i>
       does not include men). There also exist only the masculine
       <a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">
        apocope
       </a>
       forms
       <i>
        al
       </i>
       ("to him", from
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        él
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        del
       </i>
       ("of him", from
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        él
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        algún
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        alguno
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        buen
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        bueno
       </i>
       ). Some early proposals for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_neutrality_in_Spanish_and_Portuguese" title="Gender neutrality in Spanish and Portuguese">
        gender neutrality in Spanish and Portuguese
       </a>
       have included extending the use of the gender-neutral
       <i>
        -es
       </i>
       ending for plural nouns, so that
       <i>
        mis hijos
       </i>
       ("my children" or "my boys") becomes
       <i>
        mis hijes
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eisenberg_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eisenberg-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
