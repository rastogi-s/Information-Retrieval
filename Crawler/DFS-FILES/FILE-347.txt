     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        ISO 639-3:2007
       </b>
       ,
       <i>
        Codes for the representation of names of languages – Part 3: Alpha-3 code for comprehensive coverage of languages
       </i>
       , is an international standard for
       <a href="/wiki/Language_code" title="Language code">
        language codes
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639" title="ISO 639">
        ISO 639
       </a>
       series. It defines three-letter codes for identifying languages. The standard was published by
       <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
        ISO
       </a>
       on 1 February 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO_status_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ISO_status-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       ISO 639-3 extends the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2" title="ISO 639-2">
        ISO 639-2
       </a>
       alpha-3 codes with an aim to cover all known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">
        natural languages
       </a>
       . The extended language coverage was based primarily on the language codes used in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
         Ethnologue
        </a>
       </i>
       (volumes 10-14) published by
       <a href="/wiki/SIL_International" title="SIL International">
        SIL International
       </a>
       , which is now the
       <a href="/wiki/Registration_authority" title="Registration authority">
        registration authority
       </a>
       for ISO 639-3.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It provides an enumeration of languages as complete as possible, including living and extinct, ancient and constructed, major and minor, written and unwritten.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO_status_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ISO_status-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, it does not include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reconstructed_language" title="Reconstructed language">
        reconstructed languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       ISO 639-3 is intended for use as
       <a href="/wiki/Metadata" title="Metadata">
        metadata
       </a>
       codes in a wide range of applications. It is widely used in computer and information systems, such as the Internet, in which many languages need to be supported. In archives and other information storage, they are used in cataloging systems, indicating what language a resource is in or about. The codes are also frequently used in the linguistic literature and elsewhere to compensate for the fact that language names may be obscure or ambiguous.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because it provides comprehensive language coverage, giving equal opportunity for all languages, and because of its wide adoption in information technologies, ISO 639-3 provides an important technology component addressing the
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_divide" title="Digital divide">
        digital divide
       </a>
       problem.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_codes">
        Language codes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Language codes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_ISO_639-3_codes" title="List of ISO 639-3 codes">
        List of ISO 639-3 codes
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       ISO 639-3 includes all languages in
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-1" title="ISO 639-1">
        ISO 639-1
       </a>
       and all individual languages in
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2" title="ISO 639-2">
        ISO 639-2
       </a>
       . ISO 639-1 and ISO 639-2 focused on major languages, most frequently represented in the total body of the world's literature. Since ISO 639-2 also includes language collections and Part 3 does not, ISO 639-3 is not a superset of ISO 639-2. Where
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2#B_and_T_codes" title="ISO 639-2">
        B and T codes
       </a>
       exist in ISO 639-2, ISO 639-3 uses the T-codes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples:
      </p>

      <p>
       As of April 2012
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , the standard contains 7776 entries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Data-Files_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Data-Files-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The inventory of languages is based on a number of sources including: the individual languages contained in 639-2, modern languages from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue
       </a>
       , historic varieties, ancient languages and
       <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">
        artificial languages
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguist_List" title="Linguist List">
        Linguist List
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as languages recommended within the annual public commenting period.
      </p>
      <p>
       Machine-readable data files are provided by the registration authority.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Data-Files_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Data-Files-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mappings from ISO 639-1 or ISO 639-2 to ISO 639-3 can be done using these data files.
      </p>
      <p>
       ISO 639-3 is intended to assume distinctions based on criteria that are not entirely subjective.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not intended to document or provide identifiers for dialects or other sub-language variations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, judgments regarding distinctions between languages may be subjective, particularly in the case of oral language varieties without established literary traditions, usage in education or media, or other factors that contribute to language conventionalization.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Code_space">
        Code space
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Code space">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since the code is three-letter alphabetic, one upper bound for the number of languages that can be represented is 26 × 26 × 26 = 17576. Since ISO 639-2 defines special codes (4), a reserved range (520) and B-only codes (23), 547 codes cannot be used in part 3. Therefore, a stricter upper bound is 17576 − 547 = 17029.
      </p>
      <p>
       The upper bound gets even stricter if one subtracts the language collections defined in 639-2 and the ones yet to be defined in
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-5" title="ISO 639-5">
        ISO 639-5
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Macrolanguages">
        Macrolanguages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Macrolanguages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639_macrolanguage" title="ISO 639 macrolanguage">
        ISO 639 macrolanguage
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       There are 56 languages in ISO 639-2 which are considered, for the purposes of the standard, to be "macrolanguages" in ISO 639-3.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of these
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrolanguage" title="Macrolanguage">
        macrolanguages
       </a>
       had no individual language as defined by ISO 639-3 in the code set of ISO 639-2, e.g. 'ara' (Generic Arabic). Others like 'nor' (Norwegian) had their two individual parts ('nno' (
       <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">
        Nynorsk
       </a>
       ), 'nob' (
       <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">
        Bokmål
       </a>
       )) already in ISO 639-2.
      </p>
      <p>
       That means some languages (e.g. 'arb', Standard Arabic) that were considered by ISO 639-2 to be dialects of one language ('ara') are now in ISO 639-3 in certain contexts considered to be individual languages themselves.
      </p>
      <p>
       This is an attempt to deal with varieties that may be linguistically distinct from each other, but are treated by their speakers as two forms of the same language, e.g. in cases of
       <a href="/wiki/Diglossia" title="Diglossia">
        diglossia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="external free" href="http://www-01.sil.org/iso639-3/documentation.asp?id=ara" rel="nofollow">
         http://www-01.sil.org/iso639-3/documentation.asp?id=ara
        </a>
        (Generic Arabic, 639-2)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external free" href="http://www-01.sil.org/iso639-3/documentation.asp?id=arb" rel="nofollow">
         http://www-01.sil.org/iso639-3/documentation.asp?id=arb
        </a>
        (Standard Arabic, 639-3)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       See
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for the complete list.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collective_languages">
        Collective languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Collective languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2#Collective_language_codes" title="ISO 639-2">
        ISO 639-2 § Collective language codes
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-5" title="ISO 639-5">
        ISO 639-5
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       "A collective language code element is an identifier that represents a group of individual languages that are not deemed to be one language in any usage context."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These codes do not precisely represent a particular language or macrolanguage.
      </p>
      <p>
       While ISO 639-2 includes three-letter identifiers for collective languages, these codes are excluded from ISO 639-3. Hence ISO 639-3 is not a superset of ISO 639-2.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-5" title="ISO 639-5">
        ISO 639-5
       </a>
       defines 3-letter collective codes for language families and groups, including the collective language codes from ISO 639-2.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Special_codes">
        Special codes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Special codes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <span id="Non-language_codes">
       </span>
       <span id="Generic_codes">
       </span>
       Four codes are set aside in
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-2#Special_situations" title="ISO 639-2">
        ISO 639-2
       </a>
       and ISO 639-3 for cases where none of the specific codes are appropriate. These are intended primarily for applications like databases where an ISO code is required regardless of whether one exists.
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <td>
         mis
        </td>
        <td>
         uncoded languages
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         mul
        </td>
        <td>
         multiple languages
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         und
        </td>
        <td>
         undetermined languages
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         zxx
        </td>
        <td>
         no linguistic content / not applicable
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <code>
         mis
        </code>
        (originally an abbreviation for 'miscellaneous') is intended for languages which have not (yet) been included in the ISO standard.
       </li>
       <li>
        <code>
         mul
        </code>
        is intended for cases where the data includes more than one language, and (for example) the database requires a single ISO code.
       </li>
       <li>
        <code>
         und
        </code>
        is intended for cases where the language in the data has not been identified, such as when it is mislabeled or never had been labeled. It is not intended for cases such as
        <a href="/wiki/Trojan_language" title="Trojan language">
         Trojan
        </a>
        where an unattested language has been given a name.
       </li>
       <li>
        <code>
         zxx
        </code>
        is intended for data which is not a language at all, such as animal calls.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition, 520 codes in the range
       <code>
        qaa
       </code>
       –
       <code>
        qtz
       </code>
       are 'reserved for local use'. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguist_List" title="Linguist List">
        Linguist List
       </a>
       uses them for
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_ISO_639-3_language_codes_reserved_for_local_use&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="List of ISO 639-3 language codes reserved for local use (page does not exist)">
        extinct languages
       </a>
       . Linguist List has assigned one of them a generic value:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <td>
         qnp
        </td>
        <td>
         unnamed proto-language (Linguist List only)
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       This is used for proposed intermediate nodes in a family tree that have no name.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Maintenance_processes">
        Maintenance processes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Maintenance processes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The code table for ISO 639-3 is open to changes. In order to protect stability of existing usage, the changes permitted are limited to:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        modifications to the reference information for an entry (including names or categorizations for type and scope),
       </li>
       <li>
        addition of new entries,
       </li>
       <li>
        deprecation of entries that are duplicates or spurious,
       </li>
       <li>
        merging one or more entries into another entry, and
       </li>
       <li>
        splitting an existing language entry into multiple new language entries.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The code assigned to a language is not changed unless there is also a change in denotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morey-Post-Friedman_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morey-Post-Friedman-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Changes are made on an annual cycle. Every request is given a minimum period of three months for public review.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ISO 639-3 Web site has pages that describe "scopes of denotation"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/languoid" title="wikt:languoid">
        languoid
       </a>
       types) and types of languages,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which explain what concepts are in scope for encoding and certain criteria that need to be met. For example, constructed languages can be encoded, but only if they are designed for human communication and have a body of literature, preventing requests for idiosyncratic inventions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The registration authority documents on its Web site instructions made in the text of the ISO 639-3 standard regarding how the code tables are to be maintained.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It also documents the processes used for receiving and processing change requests.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A change request form is provided, and there is a second form for collecting information about proposed additions. Any party can submit change requests. When submitted, requests are initially reviewed by the registration authority for completeness.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a fully documented request is received, it is added to a published Change Request Index. Also, announcements are sent to the general LINGUIST discussion list at Linguist List and other lists the registration authority may consider relevant, inviting public review and input on the requested change. Any list owner or individual is able to request notifications of change requests for particular regions or language families. Comments that are received are published for other parties to review. Based on consensus in comments received, a change request may be withdrawn or promoted to "candidate status".
      </p>
      <p>
       Three months prior to the end of an annual review cycle (typically in September), an announcement is set to the LINGUIST discussion list and other lists regarding Candidate Status Change Requests. All requests remain open for review and comment through the end of the annual review cycle.
      </p>
      <p>
       Decisions are announced at the end of the annual review cycle (typically in January). At that time, requests may be adopted in whole or in part, amended and carried forward into the next review cycle, or rejected. Rejections often include suggestions on how to modify proposals for resubmission. A public archive of every change request is maintained along with the decisions taken and the rationale for the decisions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
        Criticism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=ISO_639-3&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Linguists Morey, Post and Friedman raise various criticisms of ISO 639, and in particular ISO 639-3:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morey-Post-Friedman_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morey-Post-Friedman-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The three-letter codes themselves are problematic, because while officially arbitrary technical labels, they are often derived from mnemonic abbreviations for language names, some of which are pejorative. For example,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yemsa_language" title="Yemsa language">
         Yemsa
        </a>
        was assigned the code [jnj], from pejorative "Janejero". These codes may thus be considered offensive by native speakers, but codes in the standard, once assigned, cannot be changed.
       </li>
       <li>
        The administration of the standard is problematic because SIL is a missionary organization with inadequate transparency and accountability. Decisions as to what deserves to be encoded as a language are made internally. While outside input may or may not be welcomed, the decisions themselves are opaque, and many linguists have given up trying to improve the standard.
       </li>
       <li>
        Permanent identification of a language is incompatible with language change.
       </li>
       <li>
        Languages and dialects often cannot be rigorously distinguished, and
        <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
         dialect continua
        </a>
        may be subdivided in many ways, whereas the standard privileges one choice. Such distinctions are often based instead on social and political factors.
       </li>
       <li>
        ISO 639-3 may be misunderstood and misused by authorities that make decisions about people's identity and language, abrogating the right of speakers to identify or identify with their speech variety. Though SIL is sensitive to such issues, this problem is inherent in the nature of an established standard, which may be used (or mis-used) in ways that ISO and SIL do not intend.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Haspelmath" title="Martin Haspelmath">
        Martin Haspelmath
       </a>
       agrees with four of these points, but not the point about language change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Haspelmath_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Haspelmath-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He disagrees because any account of a language requires identifying it, and we can easily identify different stages of a language. He suggests that linguists may prefer to use a codification that is made at the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/languoid" title="wikt:languoid">
        languoid
       </a>
       level since "it rarely matters to linguists whether what they are talking about is a language, a dialect or a close-knit family of languages." He also questions whether an ISO standard for language identification is appropriate since ISO is an industrial organization, while he views language documentation and nomenclature as a scientific endeavor. He cites the original need for standardized language identifiers as having been "the economic significance of translation and
       <a href="/wiki/Language_localisation" title="Language localisation">
        software localization
       </a>
       ," for which purposes the ISO 639-1 and 639-2 standards were established. But he raises doubts about industry need for the comprehensive coverage provided by ISO 639-3, including as it does "little-known languages of small communities that are never or hardly used in writing and that are often in danger of extinction".
      </p>
