    Alhambra Decree
   </h1>

         A service in a Spanish
         <a href="/wiki/Synagogue" title="Synagogue">
          synagogue
         </a>
         , from the
         <i>
          Sister Haggadah
         </i>
         (c. 1350). The Alhambra Decree would bring Spanish Jewish life to an end.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Alhambra Decree
       </b>
       (also known as the
       <b>
        Edict of Expulsion
       </b>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Decreto de la Alhambra, Edicto de Granada
        </span>
       </i>
       ) was an edict issued on 31 March 1492, by the joint
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">
        Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella I of Castile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand II of Aragon
       </a>
       ) ordering the expulsion of practising Jews from the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdoms of Castile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       and its territories and possessions by 31 July of that year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decree-translation_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Decree-translation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The primary purpose was to eliminate their influence on Spain's large
       <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">
        converso
       </a>
       population and ensure they did not revert to Judaism. Over half of Spain's Jews had converted as a result of the religious persecution and pogroms which occurred in 1391,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and as such were not subject to the Decree or to expulsion. A further number of those remaining chose to avoid expulsion as a result of the edict. As a result of the Alhambra decree and persecution in prior years, over 200,000 Jews converted to Catholicism and between 40,000 and 100,000 were expelled, an indeterminate number returning to Spain in the years following the expulsion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup>
        :17
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The edict was formally and symbolically revoked on 16 December 1968,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       following the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Vatican_Council" title="Second Vatican Council">
        Second Vatican Council
       </a>
       and a full century after Jews had once more been allowed to openly practice their religion in Spain and synagogues had been allowed to be used as places of worship under Spain's Laws of Religious Freedom.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1924, the regime of Primo de Rivera granted Spanish citizenship to the entire Sephardic Jewish diaspora. In 2014, the government of Spain passed a law allowing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_citizenship" title="Dual citizenship">
        dual citizenship
       </a>
       to Jewish descendants who apply, in order to "compensate for shameful events in the country's past."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus,
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardi Jews
       </a>
       who are descendants of those Jews expelled from Spain due to the Alhambra Decree, and can prove it, can "become Spaniards without leaving home or giving up their present nationality."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Spain" title="History of the Jews in Spain">
        History of the Jews in Spain
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Beginning in the 8th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        Muslims
       </a>
       had conquered and settled most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       , who had lived in these regions since
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman times
       </a>
       , were considered "
       <a href="/wiki/People_of_the_Book" title="People of the Book">
        People of the Book
       </a>
       " and given special status and often thrived.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The tolerance of the Muslim
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moorish
       </a>
       rulers of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
         al-Andalus
        </a>
       </i>
       compared to the repressive Visigothic kingdom which preceded them led to Jewish enclaves in Muslim Iberian cities flourishing as places of learning and commerce. Although Jews never enjoyed equal status to Muslims, in some
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">
        Taifas
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Granada" title="Taifa of Granada">
        Granada
       </a>
       , Jewish men were appointed to very high offices, including Grand Vizier.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
         Reconquista
        </a>
       </i>
       , the gradual reconquest of Muslim Iberia by the
       <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
        Christian
       </a>
       kingdoms, was driven by a powerful religious motivation: to reclaim Iberia for
       <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">
        Christendom
       </a>
       following the
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        Umayyad conquest of Hispania
       </a>
       centuries before. By the 14th century, most of the Iberian Peninsula (present-day Spain and
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       ) had been conquered by the Christian kingdoms of
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Castile
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        León
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Galicia" title="Kingdom of Galicia">
        Galicia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Overt hostility against Jews became more pronounced, finding expression in brutal episodes of violence and oppression. Thousands of Jews sought to escape these attacks by converting to Christianity; they were commonly called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">
         conversos
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Christian" title="New Christian">
        New Christians
       </a>
       , or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Marrano" title="Marrano">
         marranos
        </a>
       </i>
       . At first these conversions seemed an effective solution to the cultural conflict: many
       <i>
        converso
       </i>
       families met with social and commercial success. But eventually their success made these new Catholics unpopular with some of the clergy of the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">
        Church
       </a>
       and royal hierarchies.
      </p>
      <p>
       These suspicions on the part of Christians were only heightened by the fact that some of the coerced conversions were undoubtedly insincere. Some, but not all,
       <i>
        conversos
       </i>
       had understandably chosen to salvage their social and commercial positions or their lives by the only option open to them – baptism and embrace of Christianity – while privately adhering to their Jewish practice and faith. These secret practitioners are commonly referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Crypto-Judaism" title="Crypto-Judaism">
        crypto-Jews
       </a>
       or
       <i>
        marranos
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The existence of crypto-Jews was a provocation for secular and
       <a href="/wiki/Ecclesiology" title="Ecclesiology">
        ecclesiastical
       </a>
       leaders who were already hostile toward Spain's Jewry. The uncertainty over the sincerity of Jewish converts added fuel to the fire of
       <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">
        antisemitism
       </a>
       in 15th century Spain.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="European_context">
        European context
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: European context">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_Europe" title="History of the Jews in Europe">
        History of the Jews in Europe
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Expulsion_judios-en.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="570" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Expulsion_judios-en.svg/300px-Expulsion_judios-en.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Expulsion_judios-en.svg/450px-Expulsion_judios-en.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Expulsion_judios-en.svg/600px-Expulsion_judios-en.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Expulsion_judios-en.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Expulsions of Jews in Europe from 1100 to 1600
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the 13th to the 16th centuries European countries expelled the Jews from their territory on at least 15 occasions. Spain was preceded by England, France and some German states, among many others, and succeeded by at least five more expulsions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-isbn0-7065-1327-4_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-isbn0-7065-1327-4-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teacher's_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Teacher's-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ferdinand_and_Isabella">
        Ferdinand and Isabella
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ferdinand and Isabella">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">
        Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The hostility toward Jews was brought to a climax by the "Catholic Monarchs"
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella
       </a>
       , whose marriage in 1469 formed a
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">
        personal union
       </a>
       of the crowns of
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Castile
       </a>
       , with coordinated policies between their distinct kingdoms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ferdinand and Isabella were disturbed at reports that most Jewish converts to Christianity were insincere in their conversion, continued to practice Judaism in secret (see
       <a href="/wiki/Crypto-Judaism" title="Crypto-Judaism">
        Crypto-Judaism
       </a>
       ), and were trying to draw other
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Converso" title="Converso">
         conversos
        </a>
       </i>
       back into the Jewish fold. In 1478, Ferdinand and Isabella made formal application to
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">
        Rome
       </a>
       for a tribunal of the Inquisition in Castile to investigate these and other suspicions. In 1487, King Ferdinand promoted the establishment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">
        Spanish Inquisition
       </a>
       Tribunals in Castille; in Aragon, it was first instituted in 13th century to combat the Albigense heresy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The independent Islamic
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">
        Emirate of Granada
       </a>
       had been a
       <a href="/wiki/Tributary_state" title="Tributary state">
        tributary state
       </a>
       to Castile since 1238. In 1491, in preparation for an imminent transition to Castilian territory, the
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Granada_(1491)" title="Treaty of Granada (1491)">
        Treaty of Granada
       </a>
       was signed by Emir
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_XII_of_Granada" title="Muhammad XII of Granada">
        Muhammad XII
       </a>
       and the Queen of Castile, protecting the religious freedoms of the Muslims there. In 1492, Ferdinand and Isabella completed the Catholic
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       of the Iberian Peninsula from Islamic
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        al-Andalus
       </a>
       by victory in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Granada" title="Battle of Granada">
        Battle of Granada
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decree">
        Decree
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decree">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alhambra_Decree.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="698" data-file-width="500" height="419" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Alhambra_Decree.jpg/300px-Alhambra_Decree.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Alhambra_Decree.jpg/450px-Alhambra_Decree.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9e/Alhambra_Decree.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alhambra_Decree.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A signed copy of the Edict of Expulsion
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The king and queen issued the Alhambra Decree less than three months after the surrender of Granada. This was primarily a decision of Isabella, not her husband Fernando.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       That her
       <a href="/wiki/Confessor" title="Confessor">
        confessor
       </a>
       had just changed from the tolerant
       <a href="/wiki/Hernando_de_Talavera" title="Hernando de Talavera">
        Hernando de Talavera
       </a>
       to the very intolerant
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Jim%C3%A9nez_de_Cisneros" title="Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros">
        Francisco Jiménez de Cisneros
       </a>
       suggests that Cisneros may well have had a role in Isabel's decision.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the decree, Jews were accused of trying "to subvert their holy Catholic faith and trying to draw faithful Christians away from their beliefs."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decree-translation_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Decree-translation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These measures were
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_I_of_England#Finances.2C_Parliament_and_the_expulsion_of_Jews" title="Edward I of England">
        not new in Europe
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some Jews were only given four months and ordered to convert to Christianity or leave the country. Under the edict, Jews were promised royal "protection and security" for the effective three-month window before the deadline. They were permitted to take their belongings with them – except "...gold or silver or minted money or other things prohibited by the laws of our kingdoms...".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decree-translation_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Decree-translation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The punishment for any Jew who did not convert or leave by the deadline was
       <a href="/wiki/Summary_execution" title="Summary execution">
        summary execution
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Decree-translation_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Decree-translation-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dispersal">
        Dispersal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dispersal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Spanish Jews
       </a>
       who chose to leave Spain instead of converting,
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_diaspora" title="Jewish diaspora">
        dispersed
       </a>
       throughout the region of North Africa known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       . In those regions, they often intermingled with the already existing
       <a href="/wiki/Mizrahi_Jews" title="Mizrahi Jews">
        Mizrahi
       </a>
       Arabic or Berber speaking communities, becoming the ancestors of the Moroccan, Algerian, Tunisian and Libyan Jewish communities.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Spanish Jews also fled to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       , where they were given refuge. Sultan
       <a href="/wiki/Bayezid_II" title="Bayezid II">
        Bayezid II
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       , learning about the expulsion of Jews from Spain, dispatched the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Navy" title="Ottoman Navy">
        Ottoman Navy
       </a>
       to bring the Jews safely to Ottoman lands, mainly to the cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Thessaloniki" title="Thessaloniki">
        Thessaloniki
       </a>
       (currently in
       <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
        Greece
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%B0zmir" title="İzmir">
        İzmir
       </a>
       (currently in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of these Jews also settled in other parts of the Balkans ruled by the Ottomans such as the areas that are now Bulgaria, Serbia and Bosnia. Bayezid II was alleged to have quoted that: "Those who say that Ferdinand and Isabella are wise are indeed fools; for he gives me, his enemy, his national treasure, the Jews."
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout history, scholars have given widely differing numbers of Jews expelled from Spain. However, the figure is likely to be below the 100,000 Jews who had not yet converted to Christianity by 1492, possibly as low as 40,000. Many went to
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Portugal" title="Kingdom of Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       , gaining
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Jews_from_Portugal" title="Expulsion of the Jews from Portugal">
        only a few years of respite from persecution
       </a>
       . The Jewish community in Portugal (perhaps then some 10% of that country's population)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       were then declared Christians by royal decree unless they left.
      </p>
      <p>
       Such figures exclude the significant number of Jews who returned to Spain due to the hostile reception they received in their countries of refuge, notably
       <a href="/wiki/Fez" title="Fez">
        Fez
       </a>
       (Morocco). The situation of returnees was legalized with the Ordinance of the 10 of November 1492 which established that civil and church authorities should be witness to baptisim and, in the case that they were baptized before arrival, proof and witnesses of baptism were required. Furthermore, all property could be recovered by returnees at the same price at which it was sold. Returnees are documented as late as 1499. On the other hand, the Provision of the Royal Council of 24 of October 1493 set harsh sanctions for those who slandered these New Christians with insulting terms such as
       <i>
        tornadizos
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup>
        :115
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conversions">
        Conversions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Conversions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A majority of Spain's Jewish population had converted to Christianity during the waves of religious persecutions prior to the Decree—a total of 200,000 converts according to Joseph Pérez.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ensuring the definite conversion of such a large convert population was one of the main objectives of the expulsion of practicing Jews. Of the 100,000 Jews that remained true to their faith by 1492, an additional number chose to convert and join the converso community rather than face expulsion. Recent conversos were subject to additional suspicion by the Inquisition, which persecuted religious heresy with a strong focus on Judaism. Additionally, Limpieza de Sangre Statutes instituted legal discrimination against converso descendants, barring them from certain positions and forbidding them from emigrating to the Americas. Such measures slowly faded away as converso identity was forgotten and this community merged into Spain's dominant Catholic culture. There were, however, a few exceptions, most notably the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chuetas" title="Chuetas">
        Chuetas
       </a>
       of the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       , whose discrimination lasted into early 20th Century.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Y_chromosome" title="Y chromosome">
        Y chromosome
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DNA_testing" title="DNA testing">
        DNA test
       </a>
       conducted by the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Leicester" title="University of Leicester">
        University of Leicester
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Pompeu_Fabra_University" title="Pompeu Fabra University">
        Pompeu Fabra University
       </a>
       has indicated an average of nearly 20% for Spaniards having some direct patrilineal descent from populations from the Near East which colonized the region either in historical times, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">
        Phoenicians
       </a>
       , or during earlier prehistoric
       <a href="/wiki/Neolithic" title="Neolithic">
        Neolithic
       </a>
       migrations. On the other hand, genetic studies have dispelled local beliefs in the American South West that Spanish Americans are the descendants of Conversos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FloresMaca-Meyer2004_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FloresMaca-Meyer2004-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">

        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">

        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Spanish_policy">
        Modern Spanish policy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Alhambra_Decree&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modern Spanish policy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spanish government has actively pursued a policy of reconciliation with the descendants of its expelled Jews. In 1924, the regime of Primo de Rivera granted Spanish citizenship to the entire Sephardic Jewish diaspora. In 1992, in a ceremony marking the 500th anniversary of the Edict of Expulsion,
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Carlos_I_of_Spain" title="Juan Carlos I of Spain">
        King Juan Carlos
       </a>
       (wearing a
       <a href="/wiki/Kippah" title="Kippah">
        yarmulke
       </a>
       ) prayed alongside Israeli president
       <a href="/wiki/Chaim_Herzog" title="Chaim Herzog">
        Chaim Herzog
       </a>
       and members of the Jewish community in the
       <a href="/wiki/Beth_Yaacov_Synagogue_(Madrid,_Spain)" title="Beth Yaacov Synagogue (Madrid, Spain)">
        Beth Yaacov Synagogue
       </a>
       . The King said: "
       <a href="/wiki/Sepharad" title="Sepharad">
        Sefarad
       </a>
       (the Hebrew name for Spain) isn't a nostalgic memory anymore; it is a place where it must not be said that Jews should simply 'feel' at home there, for indeed Hispano-Jews are at home in Spain... What matters is not accountability for what we may have done wrong or right, but the willingness to look to the future, and analyze the past in light of our future."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From November 2012 Sephardi Jews have had the right to automatic Spanish nationality without the requirement of residence in Spain. Prior to November 2012, Sephardi Jews already had the right to obtain Spanish citizenship after a reduced residency period of two years (versus ten years for foreigners but natural from Philippines, Equatorial Guinea, Brazil and about other 20 American republics than also require 2 years.). While their citizenship is being processed, Sephardi Jews are entitled to the consular protection of the Kingdom of Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This makes Spain unique among European nations as the only nation that currently grants automatic citizenship to the descendants of Jews expelled during the
       <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_Jews_from_Spain" title="Expulsion of Jews from Spain">
        European medieval evictions
       </a>
       . As of November 2015, 4300 Sephardi Jews have benefited from this law and acquired Spanish citizenship, swearing allegiance to the Spanish Constitution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2013, the number of Jews in Spain was estimated to range between 40,000 and 50,000 people.
      </p>
