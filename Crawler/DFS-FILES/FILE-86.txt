        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Eastern Hemisphere
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geographical
       </a>
       term for the half of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        earth
       </a>
       which is east of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prime_meridians_an_(Greenwich)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prime meridians an (Greenwich) (page does not exist)">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       (which crosses
       <a href="/wiki/Greenwich" title="Greenwich">
        Greenwich
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       ) and west of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimeridian" title="Antimeridian">
        antimeridian
       </a>
       . It is also used to refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">
        Australia
       </a>
       , in contrast with the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">
        Western Hemisphere
       </a>
       , which includes
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       . This hemisphere may also be called the "Oriental Hemisphere". In addition, it may be used in a
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_geography" title="Cultural geography">
        cultural
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Geopolitics" title="Geopolitics">
        geopolitical
       </a>
       sense as a synonym for the "
       <a href="/wiki/Old_World" title="Old World">
        Old World
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Hemisphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The line demarcating the Eastern and Western Hemispheres is an arbitrary convention, unlike the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       (an imaginary line encircling the earth, equidistant from its
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">
        poles
       </a>
       ), which divides the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern
       </a>
       Hemispheres. The
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       at 0°
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antimeridian" title="Antimeridian">
        antimeridian
       </a>
       , at 180° longitude, are the conventionally accepted boundaries, since they divide eastern longitudes from western longitudes. This convention was established (predominantly by Europeans and Americans) in 1884 at the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference" title="International Meridian Conference">
        International Meridian Conference
       </a>
       held in
       <a href="/wiki/Washington,_D.C." title="Washington, D.C.">
        Washington, D.C.
       </a>
       where the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_time" title="Standard time">
        standard time
       </a>
       concepts of Canadian railroad engineer Sir
       <a href="/wiki/Sandford_Fleming" title="Sandford Fleming">
        Sandford Fleming
       </a>
       were adopted. Using this demarcation puts portions of
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Europe" title="Western Europe">
        Western Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">
        West Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">
        Oceania
       </a>
       , and extreme northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       in the Western Hemisphere, thereby diluting its usefulness as a geopolitical construct, since all of
       <a href="/wiki/Eurasia" title="Eurasia">
        Eurasia
       </a>
       and Africa are typically included in the Eastern Hemisphere. Consequently, the meridians of
       <a href="/wiki/20th_meridian_west" title="20th meridian west">
        20°W
       </a>
       and the diametrically opposed
       <a href="/wiki/160th_meridian_east" title="160th meridian east">
        160°E
       </a>
       are often used,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which includes all of the European and African mainlands, but also includes a small portion of northeast
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       (typically reckoned as part of
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       ) and excludes more of eastern Russia and Oceania (e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       ). Prior to the global adoption of standard time, numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridians
       </a>
       were decreed by various countries where time was defined by local noon (thereby, local meridian).
      </p>
      <p>
       The center of the Eastern Hemisphere is located in the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian Ocean
       </a>
       at the intersection of the equator and the
       <a href="/wiki/90th_meridian_east" title="90th meridian east">
        90th meridian east
       </a>
       , 910km west of
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ninety_East_Ridge" title="Ninety East Ridge">
        Ninety East Ridge
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The land mass of the Eastern Hemisphere is larger than that of the Western Hemisphere and has a wide variety of
       <a href="/wiki/Habitat" title="Habitat">
        habitats
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
        Demographics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eastern_Hemisphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       82% of
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        humans
       </a>
       live in the Eastern Hemisphere, compared to 18% in Western Hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
