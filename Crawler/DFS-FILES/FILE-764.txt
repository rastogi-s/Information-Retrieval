      <p>
       A
       <b>
        homophone
       </b>
       is a word that is
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronounced
       </a>
       the same (to varying extent) as another word but differs in meaning. A homophone may also differ in spelling. The two words may be
       <a href="/wiki/Spelling" title="Spelling">
        spelled
       </a>
       the same, such as
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rose#Noun" title="wikt:rose">
        <i>
         rose
        </i>
       </a>
       (flower) and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rose#Verb" title="wikt:rose">
        <i>
         rose
        </i>
       </a>
       (past tense of "rise"), or differently, such as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carat_(unit)" title="Carat (unit)">
         carat
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Caret" title="Caret">
         caret
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Carrot" title="Carrot">
         carrot
        </a>
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        to
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        two
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        too
       </i>
       . The term "homophone" may also apply to units longer or shorter than words, such as phrases, letters, or groups of letters which are pronounced the same as another phrase, letter, or group of letters.
      </p>
      <p>
       Homophones that are spelled the same are also both
       <a href="/wiki/Homograph" title="Homograph">
        homographs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">
        homonyms
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Homophones that are spelled differently are also called
       <b>
        heterographs
       </b>
       . A word or unit with this property is said to be "homophonous".
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       "Homophone" derives from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        homo-
       </i>
       (ὁμο‑), "same", and
       <i>
        phōnḗ
       </i>
       (φωνή), "voice, utterance".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_wordplay_and_games">
        In wordplay and games
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In wordplay and games">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2268" data-file-width="3134" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg/220px-Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg/330px-Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg/440px-Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Auction-storage-no-trespassing-sign-tn1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A sign reads, "Trespassers will be shot on site [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Sic" title="Sic">
           sic
          </a>
         </i>
         ]." Note that "site" is a homophone of "sight".
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Homophones are often used to create
       <a href="/wiki/Pun" title="Pun">
        puns
       </a>
       and to deceive the reader (as in
       <a href="/wiki/Crossword" title="Crossword">
        crossword puzzles
       </a>
       ) or to suggest multiple meanings. The last usage is common in
       <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
        poetry
       </a>
       and creative
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       . An example of this is seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Dylan_Thomas" title="Dylan Thomas">
        Dylan Thomas
       </a>
       's radio play
       <i>
        Under Milk Wood
       </i>
       : "The shops in mourning" where
       <i>
        mourning
       </i>
       can be heard as
       <i>
        mourning
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        morning
       </i>
       . Another vivid example is
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Hood" title="Thomas Hood">
        Thomas Hood
       </a>
       's use of "birth" and "berth" and "told" and "toll'd" (tolled) in his poem "Faithless Sally Brown":
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        His death, which happen'd in his berth,
       </dd>
       <dd>
        At forty-odd befell:
       </dd>
       <dd>
        They went and told the sexton, and
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The sexton toll'd the bell.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In some accents, various sounds have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        merged
       </a>
       in that they are no longer distinctive, and thus words that differ only by those sounds in an accent that maintains the distinction (a
       <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
        minimal pair
       </a>
       ) are homophonous in the accent with the merger. Some examples from
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       are:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         pin
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         pen
        </i>
        in many
        <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">
         southern American accents
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         merry
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         marry
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         Mary
        </i>
        in most American accents.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The pairs
        <i>
         do
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         due
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         forward
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         foreword
        </i>
        are homophonous in most American accents but not in most English accents.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The pairs
        <i>
         talk
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         torque
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         court
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         caught
        </i>
        are distinguished in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">
         rhotic
        </a>
        accents such as
        <a href="/wiki/Scottish_English" title="Scottish English">
         Scottish English
        </a>
        and most dialects of American English, but are homophones in many
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">
         non-rhotic
        </a>
        accents such as British
        <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">
         Received Pronunciation
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Wordplay is particularly common in English because the multiplicity of linguistic influences offers considerable complication in spelling and meaning and pronunciation compared with other languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Malapropism" title="Malapropism">
        Malapropisms
       </a>
       , which often create a similar comic effect, are usually near-homophones. See also
       <a href="/wiki/Eggcorn" title="Eggcorn">
        Eggcorn
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Oronyms">
        Oronyms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oronyms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Homophones of multiple words or phrases (as sometimes seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Word_game" title="Word game">
        word games
       </a>
       ) are also known as "oronyms". This term was coined by
       <a href="/wiki/Gyles_Brandreth" title="Gyles Brandreth">
        Gyles Brandreth
       </a>
       and first published in his book
       <i>
        The Joy of Lex
       </i>
       (1980), and it was used in the
       <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
        BBC
       </a>
       programme
       <i>
        Never Mind the Full Stops
       </i>
       , which also featured Brandreth as a guest.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of "oronyms" (which may only be true homophones in certain dialects of English) include:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "ice cream" vs. "I scream" (as in the popular song "I scream. You scream. We all scream for ice cream.")
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "euthanasia" vs. "
        <a href="/wiki/Youth_in_Asia" title="Youth in Asia">
         Youth in Asia
        </a>
        "
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "depend" vs. "deep end"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Gemini" vs. "Jim and I" vs. "Jem in eye"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "the sky" vs. "this guy" (most notably as a

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are sites, for example,
       <a class="external free" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20160825095711/http://people.sc.fsu.edu/~jburkardt/fun/wordplay/multinyms.html" rel="nofollow">
        https://web.archive.org/web/20160825095711/http://people.sc.fsu.edu/~jburkardt/fun/wordplay/multinyms.html
       </a>
       , which have lists of homonyms or rather homophones and even 'multinyms' which have as many as seven spellings. There are differences in such lists due to dialect pronunciations and usage of old words. In English, there are approximately 88 triples; 24 quadruples; 2 quintuples; 1 sextet and 1 septet. The septet is:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         raise
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         rays
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         rase
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         raze
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         rehs
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         res
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         réis
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Other than the three common words (
       <i>
        raise
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        rays
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        raze
       </i>
       ), there are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         rase
        </i>
        – a verb meaning "to erase";
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         rehs
        </i>
        – the plural of
        <i>
         reh
        </i>
        , a mixture of sodium salts found as an efflorescence in India;
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         res
        </i>
        – the plural of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Re_(musical_note)" title="Re (musical note)">
         <i>
          re
         </i>
        </a>
        , a name for one step of the musical scale;
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         réis – the plural of real, a currency unit of Portugal and Brazil.
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If proper names are allowed then a nonet is
       <i>
        Ayr
       </i>
       (Scottish town),
       <i>
        Aire
       </i>
       (Yorkshire River),
       <i>
        Eyre
       </i>
       (legal term and various geographic locations),
       <i>
        heir
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        air
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        err
       </i>
       (some speakers),
       <i>
        ere
       </i>
       (poetic "before"),
       <i>
        e'er
       </i>
       (poetic "ever", some speakers),
       <i>
        are
       </i>
       (unit of area; some speakers).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
        Japanese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Japanese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are a large number of homophones in Japanese, due to the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Sino-Japanese_vocabulary" title="Sino-Japanese vocabulary">
        Sino-Japanese vocabulary
       </a>
       , where borrowed words and morphemes from Chinese are widely used in Japanese, but many sound differences, such as tones, are lost. These are to some extent disambiguated via Japanese pitch accent, or from context, but many of these words are primarily or almost exclusively used in writing, where they are easily distinguished as they are written with different
       <a href="/wiki/Kanji" title="Kanji">
        kanji
       </a>
       ; others are used for puns, which are frequent in Japanese. An extreme example is
       <i>
        kikō
       </i>
       , which is the pronunciation of at least 22 words (some quite rare or specialized, others common; all these examples are two-character compounds), including: 機構 (organization/mechanism), 紀行 (travelogue), 稀覯 (rare), 騎行 (horseback riding), 貴校 ((school (respectful)), 奇功 (outstanding achievement), 貴公 (word for "you" used by men addressing male equals or inferiors), 起稿 (draft), 奇行 (eccentricity), 機巧 (contrivance), 寄港 (stopping at port), 帰校 (returning to school), 気功 (breathing exercise/qigong), 寄稿 (contribute an article/written piece), 機甲 (armor, e.g. of a tank), 帰航 (homeward voyage), 奇効 (remarkable effect), 季候 (season/climate), 気孔 (stoma), 起工 (setting to work), 気候 (climate), 帰港 (returning to port).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Korean">
        Korean
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Korean">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Korean language contains a combination of words that strictly belong to Korean and words that are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       from Chinese. Due to Chinese being pronounced with varying tones and Korean's removal of those tones, and because the modern Korean writing system, Hangeul, has a more finite number of phonemes than, for example, Latin-derived alphabets such as English's, there is a large number of homonyms with both same spelling and pronunciation. For example, '화장(化粧)하다': 'to put on makeup' and '화장(火葬)하다': 'to cremate'. Also, '유산(遺産)': 'inheritance' and '유산(流産)': 'miscarriage'. '방구': 'fart', and '방구(防具)': 'guard'. There are heterographs, but far fewer, contrary to the tendency in English. For example, '학문(學問)': 'learning', and '항문(肛門)': 'anus'. Using
       <a href="/wiki/Hanja" title="Hanja">
        hanja
       </a>
       (한자,漢字), which are Chinese characters, such words are written differently.
      </p>
      <p>
       Implicit above, like in other languages, Korean homonyms can be used to make puns. The context in which the word is used indicates which meaning is intended by the speaker or writer.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pseudo-homophones">
        Pseudo-homophones
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pseudo-homophones">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Pseudo-homophones are
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudoword" title="Pseudoword">
        pseudowords
       </a>
       that are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
        phonetically
       </a>
       identical to a word. For example, groan/grone and crane/crain are pseudo-homophone pairs, whereas plane/plain is a homophone pair since both letter strings are recognised words. Both types of pairs are used in
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_decision_task" title="Lexical decision task">
        lexical decision tasks
       </a>
       to investigate
       <a href="/wiki/Word_recognition" title="Word recognition">
        word recognition
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_as_ambiguous_information">
        Use as ambiguous information
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Homophone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Use as ambiguous information">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Homophones, specifically heterographs, where one spelling is of a threatening nature and one is not (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       slay/sleigh, war/wore) have been used in studies of
       <a href="/wiki/Anxiety" title="Anxiety">
        anxiety
       </a>
       as a test of cognitive models that those with high anxiety tend to interpret ambiguous information in a threatening manner.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
