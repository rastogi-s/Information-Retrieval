      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        flap
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        tap
       </b>
       is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonantal
       </a>
       sound, which is produced with a single contraction of the muscles so that one articulator (such as the tongue) is thrown against another.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrast_with_stops_and_trills">
        Contrast with stops and trills
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Contrast with stops and trills">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main difference between a flap and a
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       is that in a flap there is no buildup of air pressure behind the
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       and consequently no
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Release_(phonetics)" title="Release (phonetics)">
        release
       </a>
       burst. Otherwise a flap is similar to a brief stop.
      </p>
      <p>
       Flaps also contrast with
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trills
       </a>
       , where the airstream causes the articulator to vibrate. Trills may be realized as a single contact, like a flap, but are variable, whereas a flap is limited to a single contact. When a trill is brief and made with a single contact it is sometimes erroneously described as an (allophonic) flap, but a true flap is an active articulation whereas a trill is a passive articulation. That is, for a tap or flap the tongue makes an active gesture to contact the target place of articulation, whereas with a trill the contact is due to the vibration caused by the airstream rather than any active movement.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tap_vs._flap">
        Tap vs. flap
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Tap vs. flap">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguists" title="Linguists">
        linguists
       </a>
       use the terms
       <i>
        tap
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        flap
       </i>
       indiscriminately.
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Peter Ladefoged
       </a>
       proposed for a while that it might be useful to distinguish between them. However, his usage was inconsistent and contradicted itself even between different editions of the same text.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One proposed version of the distinction was that a tap strikes its point of contact directly, as a very brief
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       , but a flap strikes the point of contact tangentially: "Flaps are most typically made by retracting the tongue tip behind the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
        alveolar ridge
       </a>
       and moving it forward so that it strikes the ridge in passing."
      </p>
      <p>
       Later, however, he used the term
       <i>
        flap
       </i>
       in all cases.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Subsequent work on the labiodental flap has clarified the issue: flaps involve retraction of the active articulator, and a forward-striking movement.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For linguists who make the distinction, the
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       tap is transcribed as a fish-hook ar,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       , and the flap can be transcribed as a small capital dee,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ᴅ]
       </span>
       , which is not recognized by the IPA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In IPA terms the retroflex flap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ]
       </span>
       symbol captures the initial retraction and subsequent forward movement of the tongue tip involved. Otherwise, alveolars are typically called
       <i>
        taps
       </i>
       , and other
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        articulations
       </a>
       are called
       <i>
        flaps
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       No language has been confirmed to contrast a tap and a flap at the same place of articulation. However, such a distinction has been claimed for Norwegian in which the alveolar apical tap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       and the post-alveolar/retroflex apical flap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɽ/
       </span>
       have the same place of articulation for some speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alveolar_flaps">
        Alveolar flaps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alveolar flaps">

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
        Alveolar flap
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       features a good illustration of an alveolar flap, contrasting it with a
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trill
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        pe
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        o
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈpeɾo/
       </span>
       "but" vs.
       <i>
        pe
        <b>
         rr
        </b>
        o
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈpero/
       </span>
       "dog". Among the
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       , this
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophone
       </a>
       occurs in American and Australian English and in Northern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_German_language" title="Low German language">
        Low Saxon
       </a>
       . In American and Australian English it tends to be an allophone of intervocalic /t/ (as in "bu
       <b>
        tt
       </b>
       er," "la
       <b>
        t
       </b>
       er," "fa
       <b>
        tt
       </b>
       est" and "to
       <b>
        t
       </b>
       al") – see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intervocalic_alveolar_flapping" title="Intervocalic alveolar flapping">
        intervocalic alveolar flapping
       </a>

       <i>
        bä
        <b>
         d
        </b>
        en
       </i>
       /beeden/ →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        gah to Be
        <b>
         dd
        </b>
        e!
       </i>
       /gaa tou bede/ →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˌɡɑːtoʊˈbeɾe]
       </span>
       ‘go to bed!’,
       <i>
        Wa
        <b>
         t
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       /vaater/ →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈvɑːɾɜ]
       </span>
       ‘water’,
       <i>
        Va
        <b>
         dd
        </b>
        er
       </i>
       /fater/ →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈfaɾɜ]
       </span>
       ‘father’. (In some dialects this has resulted in reanalysis and a shift to /r/; thus
       <i>
        bären
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbeːrn]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        to Berre
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [toʊˈbere]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>

       </i>

        [ˈfarɜ]

       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">

       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">

       In
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       , a flap often appears instead of a former
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
         l
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       . This is part of a wider phenomenon called
       <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism_(sound_change)" title="Rhotacism (sound change)">
        rhotacism
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Retroflex_flaps">
        Retroflex flaps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Retroflex flaps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indic_language" title="Indic language">
        Indic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       have retroflex flaps. In
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       there are three, a simple retroflex flap as in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [bɐɽɑː]
       </span>
       <i>
        big,
       </i>
       a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">
        murmured
       </a>
       retroflex flap as in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [koɽʱiː]
       </span>
       <i>
        leper,
       </i>
       and a retroflex
       <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">
        nasal
       </a>
       flap in the Hindicized pronunciation of
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [mɐɽ̃i]
       </span>
       <i>
        ruby.
       </i>
       Some of these may be
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophonic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A retroflex flap is also common in
       <a href='/wiki/Norwegian_dialects#Retroflex_"R"' title="Norwegian dialects">
        Norwegian dialects
       </a>
       and some
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_dialects" title="Swedish dialects">
        Swedish dialects
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lateral_flaps">
        Lateral flaps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lateral flaps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many of the languages of Africa, Asia, and the Pacific that don't distinguish [r] from [l] may have a lateral flap. However, it is also possible that many of these languages do not have a lateral–central contrast at all, so that even a consistently neutral articulation may be perceived as sometimes lateral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɺ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       , sometimes central
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       . This has been suggested to be the case for
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       , for example.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996243_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996243-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Iwaidja_language" title="Iwaidja language">
        Iwaidja language
       </a>
       of Australia has both alveolar and
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_lateral_flap" title="Retroflex lateral flap">
        retroflex lateral flaps
       </a>
       . These contrast with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_approximant" title="Lateral approximant">
        lateral approximants
       </a>
       at the same positions, as well as a retroflex tap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɽ]
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_tap" title="Alveolar tap">
        alveolar tap
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_approximant" title="Retroflex approximant">
        retroflex approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɻ]
       </span>
       . However, the flapped, or tapped, laterals in Iwaidja are distinct from 'lateral flaps' as represented by the corresponding IPA symbols (see below). These phones consist of a flap component followed by a lateral component, whereas In Iwaidja the opposite is the case. For this reason, current IPA transcriptions of these sounds by linguists working on the language consist of an alveolar lateral followed by a superscript alveolar tap and a retroflex lateral followed by a superscript retroflex tap.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velar_lateral_flap" title="Velar lateral flap">
        velar lateral flap
       </a>
       may exist as an allophone in a few languages of New Guinea, according to
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Peter Ladefoged
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">
        Ian Maddieson
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transcription">
        Transcription
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Transcription">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The retroflex lateral flap does not have an officially recognized symbol in the IPA. However, an
       <i>
        ad hoc
       </i>
       symbol based on the alveolar lateral flap may occasionally be seen:
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lateral_flaps.svg">
        <img alt="Lateral flaps.svg" data-file-height="164" data-file-width="195" height="164" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Lateral_flaps.svg/195px-Lateral_flaps.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Lateral_flaps.svg/293px-Lateral_flaps.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Lateral_flaps.svg/390px-Lateral_flaps.svg.png 2x" width="195"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       Such derived symbols are becoming more frequent now that font-editing software is widely accessible. Note that besides not being sanctioned by the IPA, there is no
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       value for it. However, the retroflex lateral flap may be written in Unicode-compliant fashion as a digraph of the alveolar lateral flap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɺ]
       </span>
       with the right-tail diacritic,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɺ̢]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The palatal and velar lateral flaps may be represented with a short diacritic over the letter for the homorganic approximant, although the diacritic would need to appear under the palatal due to its ascender:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʎ̮, ʟ̆]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-coronal_flaps">
        Non-coronal flaps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-coronal flaps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The only common non-
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal
       </a>
       flap is the
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_flap" title="Labiodental flap">
        labiodental flap
       </a>
       , found throughout central Africa in languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Margi_language" title="Margi language">
        Margi
       </a>
       . In 2005, the IPA adopted a right-hook v,
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Labiodental_flap_(Gentium).svg">
         <img alt="Labiodental flap (Gentium).svg" data-file-height="85" data-file-width="101" height="25" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg/30px-Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg/45px-Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg/60px-Labiodental_flap_%28Gentium%29.svg.png 2x" width="30"/>
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       for this sound. (Supported by some fonts:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ⱱ]
       </span>
       .) Previously, it had been transcribed with the use of the breve diacritic,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v̆]
       </span>
       , or other
       <i>
        ad hoc
       </i>
       symbols.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other flaps are much less common. They include an
       <a href="/wiki/Epiglottal_flap" title="Epiglottal flap">
        epiglottal flap
       </a>
       ; a
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_flap" title="Bilabial flap">
        bilabial flap
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Banda_languages" title="Banda languages">
        Banda
       </a>
       , which may be an
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophone
       </a>
       of the labiodental flap; and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velar_lateral_flap" title="Velar lateral flap">
        velar lateral flap
       </a>
       as an allophone in
       <a href="/wiki/Kanite_language" title="Kanite language">
        Kanite
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melpa_language" title="Melpa language">
        Melpa
       </a>
       . These are often transcribed with the breve diacritic, as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w̆, ʟ̆]
       </span>
       . Note here that, like a velar
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trill
       </a>
       , a central velar flap or tap is not possible because the tongue and
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">
        soft palate
       </a>
       cannot move together easily enough to produce a sound.
      </p>
      <p>
       If other flaps are found, the breve diacritic could be used to represent them, but would more properly be combined with the symbol for the corresponding voiced stop. A
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">
        uvular
       </a>
       flap, which unlike a velar flap is believed to be articulatorily possible, could be represented this way (by
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[ɟ̆, ɢ̆~ʀ̆]
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nasal_flaps">
        Nasal flaps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Nasal flaps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        Nasal consonants
       </a>
       include flaps, although these are rarely phonemic. Many West African languages have a nasal flap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ̃]
       </span>
       (or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n̆]
       </span>
       ) as an allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       before a nasal vowel;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
        Pashto
       </a>
       , however, has a phonemic nasal
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_lateral_flap" title="Retroflex lateral flap">
        retroflex lateral flap
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tapped_fricatives">
        <span id="Fricative">
        </span>
        Tapped fricatives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Flap_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tapped fricatives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Voiced and voiceless tapped alveolar fricatives have been reported from a few languages. Flapped fricatives are possible but do not seem to be used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_tapped_fricative" title="Voiced alveolar tapped fricative">
        voiced alveolar tapped fricative
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_tapped_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar tapped fricative">
        voiceless alveolar tapped fricative
       </a>
       .
      </p>
