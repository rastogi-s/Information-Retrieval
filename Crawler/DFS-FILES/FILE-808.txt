      <p>
       <b>
        Hispanicisation
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        hispanisation
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        castilianization
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        castilianisation
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        castellanización
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       refers to the process by which a place or person becomes influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">
        Hispanic
       </a>
       culture or a process of cultural and/or linguistic change in which something non-Hispanic becomes Hispanic. Hispanicization is illustrated by spoken Spanish, production and consumption of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_cuisine" title="Hispanic cuisine">
        Hispanic food
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       music, and participation in Hispanic festivals and holidays.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the former Spanish colonies, the term is also used in the narrow linguistic sense of the Spanish language replacing indigenous languages.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spain">
        Spain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanicization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Within
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       , the term hispanization can refer to the cultural and linguistic absorption of the Berber Guanches, the indigenous people of the Canary Islands in the century following their conquest in the 15th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is much more rarely used as a synonym for castilianisation (castellanización) i.e. the historical process whereby speakers of minority Spanish languages such as Basque, Catalan, Galician, Asturian, Leonese or Aragonese are linguistically assimilated and progressively abandon their language for Spanish. Since all of the aforementioned languages are co-official national languages together with Castilian Spanish, the term castilianisation is preferred.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
        United States
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanicization&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: United States">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the
       <a href="/wiki/2000_United_States_Census" title="2000 United States Census">
        2000 United States Census
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       about 75% of all Hispanics spoke Spanish in the home. Hispanic retention rates are so high in parts of Texas and New Mexico and along the border because the percentage of Hispanics living there is also very high.
       <a href="/wiki/Laredo,_Texas" title="Laredo, Texas">
        Laredo, Texas
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Chimayo,_New_Mexico" title="Chimayo, New Mexico">
        Chimayo, New Mexico
       </a>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/Nogales,_Arizona" title="Nogales, Arizona">
        Nogales, Arizona
       </a>
       , for example, all have Hispanic populations greater than 90 percent. Furthermore, in these places have had a Hispanic-majority population since the time of the Spanish conquest and colonization of the area in the 15th and 16th centuries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       In Spanish America it is also used to refer to the imposition of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       in the former Spanish colonies such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       and its adoption by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_people" title="Indigenous people">
        indigenous peoples
       </a>
       . This refers to Spain's influence which began in the late 15th century and the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       beginning in the colonization of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       in 1402 which is now part of Spain. Later the landing of Christopher Columbus in 1492 in the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       then
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       . All these countries were Hispanicised; however, there are still many people there who hold a culture that still has its origins in the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Indigenous peoples of the Americas
       </a>
       . Until recently, castilianization has been official policy by the governments of many
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic American
       </a>
       countries. Only recently programs of
       <a href="/wiki/Intercultural_bilingual_education" title="Intercultural bilingual education">
        intercultural bilingual education
       </a>
       have been introduced to a substantial extent.
      </p>

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       archipelago was ruled from
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       as a territory of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       , from 1565 to 1821 and as a province of Spain until 1898. Since the late 16th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Spain" title="Culture of Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_culture" title="Hispanic culture">
        Hispanic culture
       </a>
       has intemperately influenced, shaped, and became the foundation of modern Filipino cultural landscape. Derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austronesian" title="Austronesian">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian
       </a>
       influences, modern Filipino culture is described
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2011)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       as a blend of Eastern and Western (mostly Spanish) traditions. Although most Filipinos speak an Austronesian language, the languages of the Philippines have thousands of Spanish loanwords. Furthermore, a number of Filipinos to the south speak a Spanish-based Creole known as
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       . This is also true with the Chamorro language in Guam, which lived side by side with Spanish for over 300 years. Many people
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2011)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       consider these languages of the Pacific, Hispano-Austronesian languages, because of the heavy influence of Spanish on the language.
      </p>
