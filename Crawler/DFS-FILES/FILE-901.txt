      <p>
       <b>
        Cantabrian
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        cántabru
       </i>
       , in Cantabrian) is a group of dialects belonging to
       <a href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_languages" title="Astur-Leonese languages">
        Astur-Leonese
       </a>
       . It is indigenous to the territories in and surrounding the Autonomous Community of
       <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">
        Cantabria
       </a>
       , in Northern
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Traditionally, some dialects of this group have been further grouped by the name
       <b>
        Montañés
       </b>
       ('from the Mountain'),
       <i>
        La Montaña
       </i>
       ('the Mountain') being a traditional name for Cantabria due to its mountainous topography.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
        Distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantabrian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="374" data-file-width="578" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg/280px-Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg/420px-Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg/560px-Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg.png 2x" width="280"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lleng%C3%BCes_d%27Espanya.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column">
          <span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid black; background-color: #008000; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">
          </span>
          Extension of Leonese variants in Spain
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       These dialects belong to the Northwestern Iberian
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       , and have been classified as belonging to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_language" title="Astur-Leonese language">
        Astur-Leonese domain
       </a>
       by successive research works carried out through the 20th century, the first of them, the famous work
       <i>
        El dialecto Leonés
       </i>
       , by Menéndez Pidal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This dialect group spans the whole territory of Cantabria. In addition, there is historical evidence of traits (such as toponyms, or certain constructions) linking the speech of some nearby areas to the Cantabrian Astur-Leonese group:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The western part of
        <a href="/wiki/Enkarterri" title="Enkarterri">
         Las Encartaciones
        </a>
        , in
        <a href="/wiki/Biscay" title="Biscay">
         Biscay
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Bordering areas with
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Burgos" title="Province of Burgos">
         Burgos
        </a>
        : especially the upper valleys of Espinosa de los Monteros, where Pasiegu dialect was spoken.
       </li>
       <li>
        Bordering areas with
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Palencia" title="Province of Palencia">
         Palencia
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Valleys of Peñamellera and Ribadedeva, in the easternmost part of
        <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">
         Asturias
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some of this areas had historically been linked to Cantabria before the
       <a href="/wiki/1833_territorial_division_of_Spain" title="1833 territorial division of Spain">
        1833 territorial division of Spain
       </a>
       , and the creation of the Province of Santander (with the same territory as modern-day's Autonomous Community).
      </p>

      <p>
       However, based on linguistic evidence, R. Molleda proposed what is today the habitual division of dialectal areas in Cantabria. Molleda proposed to take the isogloss of the masculine plural gender morphology, which seems to surround a large portion of Eastern Cantabria, running from the mouth of the Besaya River in the North, and along the Pas-Besaya watershed. He then proceeded to name the resulting areas Western and Eastern, depending on the location to the West or East of the isogloss. This division has gained strength due to the fact that, even though masculine morphology is not a very important difference, many other isoglosses draw the same line.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_description">
        Linguistic description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cantabrian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are some features in common with Spanish, the main of which is the set of consonants which is nearly identical to that of Northern Iberian Spanish. The only important difference is preservation of the voiceless glottal fricative (/h/) as an evolution of Latin's word initial f- as well as the [x-h] mergers; both feature are common in many Spanish dialects, especially those from Southern Spain and parts of Latin America.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        preservation of the voiceless glottal fricative
       </b>
       was usual in Middle Spanish, before the /h/ in words like /humo/, from Latin
       <i>
        fumus
       </i>
       , resulted in Modern Spanish /umo/. Every Cantabrian dialect keeps /f/ before consonants such as in /'fɾi.u/ (cold), just as Spanish and Astur-Leonese do.

      <p>
       Other features are common to most Astur-Leonese dialects; some of these are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Use of /u/ as masculine singular gender morpheme
        </b>
        : most dialects use a closed central rounded vowel [ʉ], as masculine morpheme, although only eastern dialects have shown [ʉ] - [u] contrast.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Opposition between singular and plural masculine gender morphemes
        </b>
        . The dialectal boundaries of this feature are usually used to represent the western and eastern dialects:
        <ul>
         <li>
          Western Dialects oppose /u/ masculine singular marker to /os/ masculine plural marker. E.g.
          <i>
           perru
          </i>
          (dog) but
          <i>
           perros
          </i>
          (dogs).
         </li>
         <li>
          Eastern Dialects used to oppose /ʉ/+metaphony (masc. sing.) to /us/ (masc. plural). E.g.
          <i>
           pirru
          </i>
          ['pɨ.rʉ] (dog) but
          <i>
           perrus
          </i>
          (dogs). This opposition is nearly lost and only few speakers of the Pasiegu dialect still use it. Nowadays, the most common situation is the no-opposition, using /u/ as a masculine morpheme both in singular and plural.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Mass neuter
        </b>
        : this feature marks uncountableness in nouns, pronouns, articles, adjectives and quantifiers. As in general Astur-Leonese, the neuter morpheme is /o/, rendering an opposition between
        <i>
         pelo
        </i>
        (the hair) and
        <i>
         pelu
        </i>
        (one strand of hair), however the actual development of this feature changes from dialect to dialect:
        <ul>
         <li>
          Most western dialects have recently lost this distinction in nominal and adjectival morphology, merging masculine and neuter morphology (
          <i>
           pelu
          </i>
          for both previous examples), although keeping this distinction in pronouns, quantifiers and articles, so
          <i>
           lo
          </i>
          (it, neuter) would refer to
          <i>
           pelu
          </i>
          (the hair, uncountable), but
          <i>
           lu
          </i>
          (it, masculine) would refer to '
          <i>
           pelu
          </i>
          (hair strand, countable).
         </li>
         <li>
          Eastern dialects show a more complex behaviour, with vowel harmony as the main mechanism for neuter distinction. Due to this, word-final morphology was not so important, and the mutations in stressed and previous syllables play a more important role. Thus, these would have ['pɨ.lʉ] (strand of hair, countable) and ['pe.lu] (the hair, uncountable), the same applied for adjectives. Likewise, eastern dialects modified their pronoun systems in order to avoid misunderstandings, replacing
          <i>
           lu
          </i>
          with
          <i>
           li
          </i>
          (originally dative pronoun) as third person singular accusative pronoun, and using
          <i>
           lu
          </i>
          for mass neuter. However, this distinction has been gradually lost and is now only retained in some older speakers of Pasiegu dialect. A unique feature of these dialects is the use of feminine agreeing quantifiers with neuter nouns, such as:
          <i>
           mucha pelu
          </i>
          (lots of hair).
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Dropping of the -r from verb infinitives
        </b>
        when clitic pronouns are appended. This results in
        <i>
         cantar
        </i>
        (to sing) +
        <i>
         la
        </i>
        (it, feminine) =
        <i>
         cantala
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Preference of simple verbal tenses
        </b>
        over complex (compound) tenses, e.g. "ya acabé" (I already finished) rather than "ya he acabáu" (I have already finished).
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       The following notes only apply for the Cantabrian derivatives, but might as well occur in other
       <a href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_languages" title="Astur-Leonese languages">
        Astur-Leonese varieties
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_inter_yA">
         <a href="#ref_inter_yA">
          <b>
           <sup>
            A
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        Many verbs keep the
        <b>
         etymological -h- or -d-
        </b>
        as an internal -y-. This derivation is most intense in the Pasiegan Dialect.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_mbgroupB">
         <a href="#ref_mbgroupB">
          <b>
           <sup>
            B
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <b>
         Latin -MB-
        </b>
        group is only retained in the derivatives of a group containing few, but very used, Latin etyma:
        <i>
         lumbum
        </i>
        (loin),
        <i>
         camba
        </i>
        (bed),
        <i>
         lambere
        </i>
        (lick), etc. however, it has not been retained during other more recent word derivations, such as
        <i>
         tamién
        </i>
        (also), which comes from the -mb- reduction of
        <i>
         también
        </i>
        a compound of
        <i>
         tan
        </i>
        (as) and
        <i>
         bien
        </i>
        (well).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_metaC">
         <a href="#ref_metaC">
          <b>
           <sup>
            C
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        In Pasiegan dialect, all masculine singular nouns, adjectives and some adverbs retain an ancient vowel mutation called
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Metaphony" title="Metaphony">
          Metaphony
         </a>
        </i>
        , thus:
        <i>
         lumu
        </i>
        (one piece of loin) but
        <i>
         lomu
        </i>
        (uncountable, loin meat), the same applies for
        <i>
         juigu
        </i>
        (a fire/campfire) and
        <i>
         juegu
        </i>
        (fire, uncountable) and
        <i>
         muistru
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         muestru
        </i>
        (our, masculine singular and uncountable, respectively).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_latinf-jD">
         <a href="#ref_latinf-jD">
          <b>
           <sup>
            D
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        Most
        <b>
         Western Cantabrian Dialects retain the ancient initial F-
        </b>
        as an aspiration (IPA [h]), so: FACERE &gt; /haθer/. This feature is still productive for all etyma starting with [f]. An example of this is the Greek root
        <i>
         phōs
        </i>
        (light) which, through Spanish
        <i>
         foto
        </i>
        (photo) derives in
        <i>
         jotu
        </i>
        (IPA: [hotu])(photography).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_latinf-hD">
         <a href="#ref_latinf-hD">
          <b>
           <sup>
            D
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <b>
         All Eastern Dialects have mostly lost Latin initial F-
        </b>
        , and only keep it on certain lexicalized vestiges, such as: jumu (IPA: [xumu]). Thus: FACERE &gt; /aθer/.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_prothesisE">
         <a href="#ref_prothesisE">
          <b>
           <sup>
            E
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <a href="/wiki/Prothesis_(linguistics)" title="Prothesis (linguistics)">
         <b>
          Prothesis
         </b>
        </a>
        : some words derive from the addition of an extra letter (usually /a/) at the beginning of the word.
        <i>
         arradiu
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         amotu
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         amutu
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         afutu
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_yeismoF">
         <a href="#ref_yeismoF">
          <b>
           <sup>
            F
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          Yeísmo
         </a>
        </b>
        : Most Cantabrian dialects do not distinguish between the /ʝ/ (written y) and /ʎ/ (written ll) fonemes, executing both with a single sound [ʝ]. Thus, rendering
        <i>
         poyu
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         pullu
        </i>
        (stone seat and chicken, respectively) homophones.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_lleismoG">
         <a href="#ref_lleismoG">
          <b>
           <sup>
            G
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <b>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lle%C3%ADsmo" title="Lleísmo">
          Lleísmo
         </a>
        </b>
        : Pasiegan Dialect is one of the few Cantabrian Dialects which does distinguish /ʝ/ and /ʎ/. Thus,
        <i>
         puyu
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         pullu
        </i>
        (stone seat and chicken, respectively) are both written and pronounced differently.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="citation wikicite" id="endnote_palatalizationH">
         <a href="#ref_palatalizationH">
          <b>
           <sup>
            H
           </sup>
          </b>
         </a>
        </span>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
          Palatalization
         </a>
        </b>
        : Cantabrian Dialects do mostly not palatalize Latin L-, however, some vestiges might be found in Eastern Cantabrian Dialects, in areas bordering Asturias (Asturian a very palatalizing language). This vestiges are often camuflated due to the strong
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         Yeísmo
        </a>
        . Palatalization of Latin N- is more common, and words such as ñudu (from Latin nudus), or ñublu (from Latin nubĭlus) are more common.
       </dd>
      </dl>

Pero encontó, casi agraecí el testarazu, pues las mis novillucas, que dispués de la estorregá debían haber quedau soterrás, cuasi no se mancaron. ¡Total: unas lijaduras de poco más de na!

Pero aún así, casi agradecí el cabezazo, pues mis novillas, que después de la caída deberían haber quedado para enterrar, casi no se lastimaron. ¡Total: unas rozaduras de nada!
       </i>
