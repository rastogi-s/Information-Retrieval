      <p>
       <b>
        Huelva
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈwelβa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        locally
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈwɛɹβa]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a city in southwestern Spain, the capital of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huelva_(province)" title="Huelva (province)">
        province of Huelva
       </a>
       in the autonomous region of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andaluc%C3%ADa" title="Andalucía">
        Andalucía
       </a>
       . It is located along the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_C%C3%A1diz" title="Gulf of Cádiz">
        Gulf of Cádiz
       </a>
       coast, at the confluence of the Odiel and
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_(river)" title="Rio Tinto (river)">
        Tinto
       </a>
       rivers. The city has been inhabited since 3000 BC. According to the 2010
       <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">
        census
       </a>
       , the city had a population of 149,410. Huelva is home to
       <a href="/wiki/Recreativo_de_Huelva" title="Recreativo de Huelva">
        Recreativo de Huelva
       </a>
       , the oldest football club in Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       A maritime town between the rivers Anas, (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Guadiana" title="Guadiana">
        Guadiana
       </a>
       ) and Baetis (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">
        Guadalquivir
       </a>
       ), it was situated on the estuary of the River
       <a href="/wiki/Odiel" title="Odiel">
        Luxia
       </a>
       (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Odiel" title="Odiel">
        Odiel
       </a>
       ), and on the road from the mouth of the
       <a href="/wiki/Guadiana" title="Guadiana">
        Anas
       </a>
       to Augusta Emerita (modern
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9rida,_Spain" title="Mérida, Spain">
        Mérida
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Huelva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:22_Feb._2009_(Carabelas)_019.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg/220px-22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg/330px-22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg/440px-22_Feb._2009_%28Carabelas%29_019.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:22_Feb._2009_(Carabelas)_019.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Pinta_(ship)" title="Pinta (ship)">
          Pinta
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%B1a" title="Niña">
          Niña
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_(ship)" title="Santa María (ship)">
          Santa María
         </a>
         ship docked in the "Muelle de Carabelas" in Palos de la Frontera, Huelva
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The city may be the site of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tartessus" title="Tartessus">
        Tartessus
       </a>
       ; it was called
       <b>
        Onoba
       </b>
       by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenicians" title="Phoenicians">
        Phoenicians
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks" title="Greeks">
        Greeks
       </a>
       kept the name and rendered it
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Ὄνοβα
       </span>
       . It was in the hands of the
       <a href="/wiki/Turdetani" title="Turdetani">
        Turdetani
       </a>
       at the time of conquest by
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       , and before the conquest it issued silver coins with Iberian legends. It was called both
       <b>
        Onoba Aestuaria
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        Onuba
       </b>
       (used on coinage) during Roman times, or, simply,
       <b>
        Onoba
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The city was incorporated into the Roman province of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Baetica" title="Hispania Baetica">
        Hispania Baetica
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">
        Arabs
       </a>
       then called it
       <b>
        Walbah
       </b>
       and ruled between 714-1250. It suffered substantial damage in the
       <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">
        1755 Lisbon earthquake
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are still some Roman remains. The city had a mint; and many coins have been found there bearing the name of the town as Onuba.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Modern inhabitants are called Onubenses in Spanish. Part of a large wooden wheel that was originally used to drain a copper mine in Huelva was discovered in the late nineteenth century. Dating to the Roman times, it was donated by the British mining company Rio Tinto to the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Museum" title="British Museum">
        British Museum
       </a>
       in 1889.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mines in the countryside send
       <a href="/wiki/Copper" title="Copper">
        copper
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrite" title="Pyrite">
        pyrite
       </a>
       to the port for export. From about 1873, the most important company in the area was
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Tinto_Group" title="Rio Tinto Group">
        Rio Tinto
       </a>
       , the British mining firm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mining operations caused severe sulfur dioxide pollution and were frequently accompanied by protests of local farmers, peasants and miners, allied under the anarchist syndicalist leader Maximiliano Tornet. On 4 February 1888, the Pavi Regiment of the Spanish Army opened fire on demonstrators at the village plaza of Rio Tinto. Historians estimate the number of deaths between 100 and 200.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Environmentalists from the nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Nerva,_Spain" title="Nerva, Spain">
        Nerva
       </a>
       village referred to 1888 as the "year of shots" a hundred years later in their protests against the province government's plans to site a large waste dump in a disused mine in the 1990s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated3_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated3-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       , the city was a hub of espionage activities led by members of the large British and German communities. German activity centered on reporting British shipping moving in and out of the Atlantic. Most famously, the city was the location where
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Mincemeat" title="Operation Mincemeat">
        Operation Mincemeat
       </a>
       allowed a body carrying false information to wash ashore.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The body of
       <a href="/wiki/Glyndwr_Michael" title="Glyndwr Michael">
        Glyndwr Michael
       </a>
       , the fictional
       <a href="/wiki/Operation_Mincemeat" title="Operation Mincemeat">
        "Major William Martin, Royal Marines,"
       </a>
       of the espionage operation is buried in the San Marco section of the cemetery of Nuestra Senora under a headstone that reads:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        William Martin, born 29 March 1907, died 24 April 1943, beloved son of John Glyndwyr and the late Antonia Martin of Cardiff, Wales, DULCE ET DECORUM EST PRO PATRIA MORI, R.I.P.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_War_Graves_Commission" title="Commonwealth War Graves Commission">
        Commonwealth War Graves Commission
       </a>
       in January 1998 added an inscription to the gravestone,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which reads:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Glyndwr Michael served as Major William Martin.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       On 11 October 2005,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Vince_(2005)" title="Hurricane Vince (2005)">
        Hurricane Vince
       </a>
       made landfall in Huelva as a tropical depression.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The Port of Huelva is divided in two sectors: the inner port (in the city) and the outer port (the main one):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <b>
         Inner Port
        </b>
        (one wharf). Constructed in 1972, the East Wharf, replaced constructed harbour facilities of inferior quality between 1900 and 1910. At the moment it is the wharf used for smaller traffic including tourist boats.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <b>
         Outer Port
        </b>
        (six wharves) was built in 1965, and is located to the south of the River Tinto.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transportation">
        Transportation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       Huelva is home to Grupo Damas, a provincial bus company. Huelva´s train station is now a shadow of its former self, and exists on a spur line. There are no trains to Portugal. Huelva´s port hosts Naviera Armas´ ferry
       <i>

       </i>
       , on which one can travel weekly to
       <a href="/wiki/Arrecife" title="Arrecife">

       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
        Demographics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Huelva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Demographics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Huelva had a population of 149,410 in 2010. The city experienced a population boom in the nineteenth century, due to the exploitation of mineral resources in the area, and another with the construction of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Polo_de_Desarrollo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Polo de Desarrollo (page does not exist)">
        Polo de Desarrollo
       </a>
       in the 1960s. It had a population of 5,377 inhabitants in 1787, which had risen to only 8,519 by 1857. From 1887, the city experienced rapid growth, reaching 21,539 residents in 1900, 56,427 in 1940, and 96,689 in 1970. Rapid expansion occurred in the following decades and the population reached 141,479 by 1991.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the last ten years, immigration both from abroad and from the surrounding area have sustained population growth. In 2007, the city reached the 145,000 mark, while the metropolitan area had nearly 232,000 inhabitants, encompassing the surrounding areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Aljaraque" title="Aljaraque">
        Aljaraque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moguer" title="Moguer">
        Moguer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/San_Juan_del_Puerto,_Spain" title="San Juan del Puerto, Spain">
        San Juan del Puerto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Punta_Umbr%C3%ADa" title="Punta Umbría">
        Punta Umbría
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gibrale%C3%B3n" title="Gibraleón">
        Gibraleón
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Palos_de_la_Frontera" title="Palos de la Frontera">
        Palos de la Frontera
       </a>
       . The 2006 census recorded a foreign population of almost 5,000 people in the urban centre, the majority of whom were of
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Moroccan
       </a>
       origin.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Huelva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Huelva and its metropolitan area have a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subtropical" title="Subtropical">
        Subtropical
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_climate" title="Mediterranean climate">
        Mediterranean climate
       </a>
       , characterized by very mild and wet winters and long warm to hot and dry summers. The average annual temperature is 23.9 °C (75.0 °F) during the day and 12.4 °C (54.3 °F) at night. The average annual precipitation is 525 mm (20.7 in) per year, there are about 52 rainy days per year. Extreme temperatures have been 43.8 °C (110.8 °F) recorded on 25 July 2004 and −3.2 °C (26.2 °F) recorded on 28 January 2005 at Ronda Este.

       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monasterio_de_La_R%C3%A1bida_(La_Rabida_Monastery)" title="Monasterio de La Rábida (La Rabida Monastery)">
        Rábida Monastery
       </a>
       . La Rábida is where Columbus sought the aid of the Franciscan brothers in advancing his project of discovery. They introduced him to local rich sailors (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Pinzon_Brothers" title="The Pinzon Brothers">
        Pinzón brothers
       </a>
       ), and, eventually, arranged a meeting in
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Artists">
        Artists
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Huelva&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Artists">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most well-known artists in Huelva have been: the poet and winner of the Nobel Prize in Literature
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Ram%C3%B3n_Jim%C3%A9nez" title="Juan Ramón Jiménez">
        Juan Ramón Jiménez
       </a>
       , the sculptor
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Le%C3%B3n_Ortega" title="Antonio León Ortega">
        Antonio León Ortega
       </a>
       , the writer
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Tenorio_Cerero" title="Nicolas Tenorio Cerero">
        Nicolas Tenorio Cerero
       </a>
       and the painter
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_V%C3%A1zquez_D%C3%ADaz" title="Daniel Vázquez Díaz">
        Daniel Vázquez Díaz
       </a>
       .
       <br/>
       Other outstanding artists from Huelva include the painters José Caballero, Pedro Gómez y Gómez, Antonio Brunt, Mateo Orduña Castellano, Pablo Martínez Coto, Manuel Moreno Díaz, Juan Manuel Seisdedos Romero, Francisco Doménech, Esperanza Abot, José María Labrador, Sebastián García Vázquez, Pilar Barroso, Juan Carlos Castro Crespo, Lola Martín, Antonio Gómez Feu, Rafael Aguilera, and Florencio Aguilera Correa. Miguel Biez, called
       <i>
        el Litri
       </i>
       , is perhaps the town's most famous artist; he was gored in a bullfight in 1929.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
