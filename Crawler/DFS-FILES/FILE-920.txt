      <p>
       <b>
        Provençal
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'v' in 'vie'">
            v
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="oc" xml:lang="oc">
         Provençau
        </span>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <span lang="oc" xml:lang="oc">
         Prouvençau
        </span>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Occitan" title="Help:IPA/Occitan">
         [pʀuveⁿˈsaw]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variety
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       spoken by a minority of people in southern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , mostly in
       <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">
        Provence
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       -speaking world, the term Provençal has historically also been used to refer to all of Occitan, but is now mainly understood to refer to the variety spoken in Provence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Provençal is also the customary name given to the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Occitan" title="Old Occitan">
        older version of the Occitan language
       </a>
       used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Troubadour" title="Troubadour">
        troubadours
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval" title="Medieval">
        medieval
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       , while
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       or the
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         langue d'oïl
        </span>
       </i>
       was limited to the northern areas of France. Thus the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-3" title="ISO 639-3">
        ISO 639-3
       </a>
       code for Old Occitan is [pro].
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007, all the
       <a href="/wiki/ISO_639-3" title="ISO 639-3">
        ISO 639-3
       </a>
       codes for Occitan dialects, including [prv] for Provençal, were retired and merged into [oci] Occitan.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdialects">
        Subdialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Proven%C3%A7al_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Subdialects">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main subdialects of Provençal are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Rodanenc
        </i>
        (in French
        <i>
         Rhodanien
        </i>
        ) around the lower
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhone" title="Rhone">
         Rhone
        </a>
        river,
        <a href="/wiki/Arles" title="Arles">
         Arles
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">
         Avignon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/N%C3%AEmes" title="Nîmes">
         Nîmes
        </a>
        .
        <ul>
         <li>
          A Rodanenc subvariety, the
          <i>
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shuadit_language" title="Shuadit language">
            Shuadit
           </a>
          </i>
          (or Judeo-Provençal), has been considered extinct since 1977. It was spoken by the Jewish community around
          <a href="/wiki/Avignon" title="Avignon">
           Avignon
          </a>
          . When Jews were granted freedom of residence in France the dialect declined.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Maritim
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Centrau
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Mediterranèu
        </i>
        (Maritime or Central or Mediterranean) around
        <a href="/wiki/Aix-en-Provence" title="Aix-en-Provence">
         Aix-en-Provence
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">
         Marseille
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">
         Toulon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Cannes" title="Cannes">
         Cannes
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Antibes" title="Antibes">
         Antibes
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Grasse" title="Grasse">
         Grasse
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Forcalquier" title="Forcalquier">
         Forcalquier
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Castellane" title="Castellane">
         Castellane
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Draguignan" title="Draguignan">
         Draguignan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ni%C3%A7ard_dialect" title="Niçard dialect">
          Niçard
         </a>
        </i>
        in the lower
        <a href="/wiki/County_of_Nice" title="County of Nice">
         County of Nice
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaro-Alpine" title="Vivaro-Alpine">
         Gavòt
        </a>

       (in French
       <i>
        Gavot
       </i>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digne" title="Digne">

       <a href="/wiki/Sisteron" title="Sisteron">

        Barcelonnette
       </a>
       and the upper
       <a href="/wiki/County_of_Nice" title="County of Nice">
        County of Nice
       </a>
       , but also in a part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ard%C3%A8che" title="Ardèche">
        Ardèche
       </a>
       , is not exactly a subdialect of Provençal, but rather a closely related Occitan dialect, also known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaro-Alpine" title="Vivaro-Alpine">
        Vivaro-Alpine
       </a>
       . So is the dialect spoken in the upper valleys of
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       (Val Maira, Val Varacha, Val d'Estura, Entraigas, Limon, Vinai, Pignerol, Sestriera).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some people view Gavòt as a variety of Provençal since a part of the Gavot area (near Digne and Sisteron) belongs to historical Provence.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
        Orthography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Proven%C3%A7al_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orthography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When written in the Mistralian norm ("normo mistralenco"), definite articles are
       <i>
        lou
       </i>
       in the masculine singular,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       in the feminine singular and
       <i>
        li
       </i>
       in the masculine and feminine plural (
       <i>
        lis
       </i>
       before vowels). Nouns and adjectives usually drop the Latin masculine endings, but
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       remains; the feminine ending is
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       . Nouns do not inflect for number, but all adjectives ending in vowels (
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       ) become
       <i>
        -i
       </i>
       , and all plural adjectives take
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       before vowels:
       <i>
        lou boun ami
       </i>
       "the good friend" (masculine),
       <i>
        la bouno amigo
       </i>
       "the good friend" (feminine),
       <i>
        li bouns ami
       </i>
       "the good friends" (masculine),
       <i>
        li bounis amigo
       </i>
       "the good friends" (feminine).
      </p>
      <p>
       When written in the classical norm ("norma classica"), definite articles are masculine
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       , feminine
       <i>
        la,
       </i>
       and plural
       <i>
        lis
       </i>
       . Nouns and adjectives usually drop the Latin masculine endings, but
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       remains; the feminine ending is
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       . Nouns inflect for number, all adjectives ending in vowels (
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       ) become
       <i>
        -i
       </i>
       , and all plural adjectives take
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        lo bon amic
       </i>
       "the good friend" (masc.),
       <i>
        la bona amiga
       </i>
       "the good friend" (fem.),
       <i>
        lis bons amics
       </i>
       "the good friends" (masc.),
       <i>
        lis bonis amigas
       </i>
       "the good friends" (fem.).
      </p>
      <p>
       Pronunciation remains the same in both norms (Mistralian and classical), which are only two different ways to write the same language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Proven%C3%A7al_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Modern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proven%C3%A7al_literature" title="Provençal literature">
        Provençal literature
       </a>
       was given impetus by Nobel laureate
       <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Mistral" title="Frédéric Mistral">
        Frédéric Mistral
       </a>
       and the association
       <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9librige" title="Félibrige">
        Félibrige
       </a>
       he founded with other writers, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_Aubanel" title="Théodore Aubanel">
        Théodore Aubanel
       </a>
       . The beginning of the 20th century saw other authors like
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_d%27Arbaud" title="Joseph d'Arbaud">
        Joseph d'Arbaud
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Batisto_Bonnet" title="Batisto Bonnet">
        Batisto Bonnet
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Val%C3%A8re_Bernard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Valère Bernard (page does not exist)">
        Valère Bernard
       </a>
       . It has been enhanced and modernized since the second half of the 20th century by writers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Rob%C3%A8rt_Lafont" title="Robèrt Lafont">
        Robèrt Lafont
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_Pessemesse&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre Pessemesse (page does not exist)">
        Pierre Pessemesse
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Claude_Barsotti&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Claude Barsotti (page does not exist)">
        Claude Barsotti
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Max-Philippe_Delavou%C3%ABt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Max-Philippe Delavouët (page does not exist)">
        Max-Philippe Delavouët
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Philippe_Gardy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Philippe Gardy (page does not exist)">
        Philippe Gardy
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Florian_Vernet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Florian Vernet (page does not exist)">
        Florian Vernet
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Danielle_Julien&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Danielle Julien (page does not exist)">
        Danielle Julien
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%B2rgi_Gr%C3%B2s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jòrgi Gròs (page does not exist)">
        Jòrgi Gròs
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=S%C3%A8rgi_Bec&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sèrgi Bec (page does not exist)">
        Sèrgi Bec
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bernat_Gi%C3%A9ly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bernat Giély (page does not exist)">
        Bernat Giély
       </a>
       , and many others.
      </p>
