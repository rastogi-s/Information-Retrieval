      <p>
       <b>
        Cocoliche
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Macaronic_language" title="Macaronic language">
        mixed language or pidgin
       </a>
       that was spoken by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_settlement_in_Argentina" title="Italian settlement in Argentina">
        Italian immigrants
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       (especially in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gran_Buenos_Aires" title="Gran Buenos Aires">
        Greater Buenos Aires
       </a>
       ) between 1870 and 1970. In the last decades of the 20th century, it has transferred / evolved in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunfardo" title="Lunfardo">
        Lunfardo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cocoliche&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Between 1880 and 1910, Argentina and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       received a large number of Italian immigrants, mostly poor country folk who arrived with little or no schooling in the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       and often were only semi-literate or totally illiterate in their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_languages" title="Italian languages">
        Italian languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As those immigrants strove to communicate with the local
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Criollo_peoples" title="Spanish Criollo peoples">
         criollos
        </a>
       </i>
       , they produced a variable mixture of Spanish with Italian and Italian dialects, which was given the derogatory name
       <i>
        Cocoliche
       </i>
       by the locals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        <span class="citation-needed-content" style="padding-left:0.1em; padding-right:0.1em; color:#595959; border:1px solid #DDD;">
         <i>
          Another example of a language influence is Cocoliche, which takes its name from Antonio Cuculicchio, a theater worker in the Podestá theater company established in Argentina and Uruguay towards the end of the nineteenth century. An Italian immigrant, Cuculicchio’s accent was apparently often mocked by others, giving rise to the comical caricature of a figure called “Cocolicchio”, representing a southern Italian. Cocoliche is a hybrid language that arose from the meeting of Spanish in Argentina and Italian brought to that country by immigrants around the turn of the twentieth century. The result was a
         </i>
         pidgin
         <i>
          — an oral form of communication that blended elements of two languages to foster communication between diverse groups of people, in some cases simplifying the grammar and lexicon of each language.
         </i>
        </span>
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Translation Blog, Transpanish
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Italian proper never took hold very well in Argentina, especially because most immigrants used their local
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       , and were not proficient in the standard language. This prevented the development of an Italian-language culture. Since the children of the immigrants grew up speaking Spanish at school, work, and military service, Cocoliche remained confined mostly to the first and second generation of Italian immigrants, and slowly fell out of use. The pidgin was depicted humorously in literary works and in the Argentine
       <a href="/wiki/Sainete" title="Sainete">
        sainete
       </a>
       theater, e.g. by
       <a href="/wiki/Dario_Vittori" title="Dario Vittori">
        Dario Vittori
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The area where most Italian emigrants and their descendants spoke the Cocoliche was near the port of
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       , mainly in the
       <i>
        barrios
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Belgrano,_Buenos_Aires" title="Belgrano, Buenos Aires">
        Belgrano
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Palermo,_Buenos_Aires" title="Palermo, Buenos Aires">
        Palermo
       </a>
       . These two areas where 70% speaking "Cocoliche" at the beginning of the 20th century.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
        Influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cocoliche&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many Cocoliche words were transferred to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunfardo" title="Lunfardo">
        Lunfardo
       </a>
       (a local Spanish dialect that some linguists
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (October 2015)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       think it is a mixture of Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ). For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         manyar
        </i>
        (to eat) from
        <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
         Venetian
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
         Lombard
        </a>
        "magnar" (
        <i>
         mangiare
        </i>
        in Italian)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lonyipietro
        </i>
        (fool)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fungi
        </i>
        (mushroom) -&gt; in Lunfardo: hat
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         vento
        </i>
        (wind) -&gt; in Lunfardo: money
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         matina
        </i>
        (morning) from Italian "mattina"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         mina
        </i>
        (girl) from
        <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
         Lombard
        </a>
        "mina" (
        <i>
         busty woman
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         prostitute
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         laburar
        </i>
        (to work) from Italian "lavorare"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         minga
        </i>
        (nothing) from
        <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
         Lombard
        </a>
        "minga" (negative particle like
        <i>
         not
        </i>
        in english or
        <i>
         ne pas
        </i>
        in french)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         yeta
        </i>
        (bad luck) from
        <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
         Neapolitan
        </a>
        "
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/iettatore" title="wikt:iettatore">
         iettatore
        </a>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         yira
        </i>
        (to go for a walk) from Italian "girare"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         salute
        </i>
        (cheers) from Italian "salute"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fiaca
        </i>
        (laziness) from Italian "fiacca"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some of these words show a characteristic codialect evolution, for example in the case of
       <i>
        manyar
       </i>
       , the word
       <i>
        manyar
       </i>
       does exist with the same meaning in Spanish even though it is considered jargon and not proper Spanish, being derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       <i>
        manjer
       </i>
       and reinforced by the Italian
       <i>
        mangiare
       </i>
       .
      </p>
