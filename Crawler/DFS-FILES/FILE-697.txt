      <p>
       The
       <b>
        imperative mood
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        grammatical mood
       </a>
       that forms a command or request.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       used in the imperative mood is the
       <a href="/wiki/English_grammar" title="English grammar">
        English
       </a>
       sentence "Please be quiet". Such imperatives imply a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        second-person
       </a>
       subject (
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       ), but some other languages also have first- and third-person imperatives, with the meaning of "let's (do something)" or "let him/her/them (do something)" (the forms may alternatively be called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cohortative" title="Cohortative">
        cohortative
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jussive" title="Jussive">
        jussive
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Imperative mood can be denoted by the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        glossing abbreviation
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          IMP
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       . It is one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Irrealis_mood" title="Irrealis mood">
        irrealis moods
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Imperative mood is often expressed using special
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugated
       </a>
       verb forms. Like other
       <a href="/wiki/Finite_verb" title="Finite verb">
        finite verb
       </a>
       forms, imperatives often inflect for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . Second-person imperatives (used for ordering or requesting performance directly from the person being addressed) are most common, but some languages also have imperative forms for the first and third persons (alternatively called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cohortative" title="Cohortative">
        cohortative
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jussive" title="Jussive">
        jussive
       </a>
       respectively).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , the imperative is formed using the bare
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       form of the verb (see
       <a href="/wiki/English_verbs" title="English verbs">
        English verbs
       </a>
       for more details). This is usually also the same as the second-person present
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicative" title="Indicative">
        indicative
       </a>
       form, except in the case of the verb
       <i>
        to be
       </i>
       , where the imperative is
       <i>
        be
       </i>
       while the indicative is
       <i>
        are
       </i>
       . (The present
       <a href="/wiki/English_subjunctive" title="English subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       always has the same form as the imperative, although it is
       <a href="/wiki/Negation" title="Negation">
        negated
       </a>
       differently – the imperative is negated using
       <i>
        do not
       </i>
       , as in "Don't touch me!"; see
       <a href="/wiki/Do-support" title="Do-support">
        <i>
         do
        </i>
        -support
       </a>
       .) The imperative form is understood as being in the second person (the subject
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronoun
       </a>
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       is usually omitted, although it can be included for emphasis), with no explicit indication of singular or plural. First and third person imperatives are expressed
       <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">
        periphrastically
       </a>
       , using a construction with the imperative of the verb
       <i>
        let
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Let us
        </b>
        (
        <b>
         Let's
        </b>
        ) have a drink! (equivalent to a first person plural imperative)
       </li>
       <li>
        Let him/her/them be happy! (equivalent to a third person imperative; constructions with
        <a href="/wiki/English_modal_verbs#may" title="English modal verbs">
         <i>
          may
         </i>
        </a>
        are also used)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other languages such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       have a greater variety of
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflected
       </a>
       imperative forms, marked for person and number, their formation often depending on a verb's conjugation pattern. Examples can be found in the specific language sections below. In languages that make a
       <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
        T–V distinction
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        vous
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        du
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        Sie
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        você
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       , etc.) the use of particular forms of the second person imperative may also be dependent on the degree of familiarity between the speaker and the addressee, as with other verb forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second person singular imperative often consists of just the stem of the verb, without any ending – this is the case in the
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , for example.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax_and_negation">
        Syntax and negation
        <span id="Prohibitive">
         <span id="Vetative">
         </span>
         <span class="mw-editsection">
          <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
           [
          </span>
          <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Syntax and negation">
           edit
          </a>
          <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
           ]
          </span>
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Imperative sentences sometimes use different
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       than declarative or other types of clauses. There may also be differences of syntax between affirmative and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negation_(grammar)" title="Negation (grammar)">
        negative
       </a>
       imperative sentences. In some cases the imperative form of the verb is itself different when negated. A distinct negative imperative form is sometimes said to be in
       <b>
        prohibitive
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        vetative
       </b>
       mood (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          PROH
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many languages, even not normally
       <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">
        null-subject languages
       </a>
       , omit the subject pronoun in imperative sentences, as usually occurs in English (see
       <a href="#English">
        below
       </a>
       ). Details of the syntax of imperative sentences in certain other languages, and of differences between affirmative and negative imperatives, can be found in some of the other specific language sections below.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Imperatives are used principally for ordering, requesting or advising the listener to do (or not to do) something: "Put down the gun!"; "Pass me the sauce"; "Don't go too near the tiger." They are also often used for giving instructions as to how to perform a task ("Install the file, then restart your computer"). They can sometimes be seen on signs giving orders or warnings ("Stop"; "Give way"; "Do not enter").
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of the imperative mood may be seen as impolite, inappropriate or even offensive in certain circumstances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In polite speech, orders or requests are often phrased instead as questions or statements, rather than as imperatives:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Could you come here for a moment? (more polite than "Come here!")
       </li>
       <li>
        It would be great if you made us a drink. (for "Make us a drink!")
       </li>
       <li>
        I have to ask you to stop. (for "Stop!")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Politeness" title="Politeness">
        Politeness
       </a>
       strategies (for instance,
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_act#Indirect_speech_acts" title="Speech act">
        indirect speech acts
       </a>
       ) can seem more appropriate in order not to threaten a conversational partner in their needs of self-determination and territory: the partner's
       <i>
        negative face
       </i>
       should not appear threatened.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As well as the replacement of imperatives with other sentence types as discussed above, there also often exist methods of phrasing an imperative in a more polite manner, such as the addition of a word like
       <i>
        please
       </i>
       or a phrase like
       <i>
        if you could
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Imperatives are also used for speech acts whose function is essentially not to make an order or request, but to give an invitation, give permission, express a wish, make an apology, etc.:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Come to the party tomorrow! (invitation)
       </li>
       <li>
        Eat the apple if you want. (permission)
       </li>
       <li>
        Have a nice trip! (wish)
       </li>
       <li>
        Pardon me. (apology)
       </li>
       <li>
        Visit Estonia and Armenia! (advertisement)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When written, imperative sentences are often, but not always, terminated with an
       <a href="/wiki/Exclamation_mark" title="Exclamation mark">
        exclamation mark
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       First person plural imperatives (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cohortative" title="Cohortative">
        cohortatives
       </a>
       ) are used mainly for suggesting an action to be performed together by the speaker and the addressee (and possibly other people): "Let's go to Barbados this year"; "Let us pray". Third person imperatives (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jussive" title="Jussive">
        jussives
       </a>
       ) are used to suggest or order that a third party or parties be permitted or made to do something: "Let them eat cake"; "Let him be executed".
      </p>
      <p>
       There is an additional imperative form that is used for general prohibitions, consisting of the word "no" followed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerund
       </a>
       form. The best known examples are "No Smoking" and "No Parking". This form does not have a positive form; that is, "Parking" by itself has no meaning unless used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       when it tells that parking is permitted.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Imperatives_in_particular_languages">
        Imperatives in particular languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Imperatives in particular languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For more details on imperatives in the languages listed below, and in languages that are not listed, see the articles on the grammar of the specific languages.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       English usually omits the subject pronoun in imperative sentences:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        You work hard. (indicative)
       </li>
       <li>
        Work hard! (imperative; subject pronoun
        <i>
         you
        </i>
        omitted)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, it is possible to include the
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       in imperative sentences for emphasis.
      </p>
      <p>
       English imperatives are negated using
       <i>
        don't
       </i>
       (as in "Don't work!") This is a case of
       <a href="/wiki/Do-support" title="Do-support">
        <i>
         do
        </i>
        -support
       </a>
       as found in indicative clauses; however in the imperative it applies even in the case of the verb
       <i>
        be
       </i>
       (which does not use
       <i>
        do
       </i>
       -support in the indicative):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        You
        <b>
         are not
        </b>
        late. (indicative)
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Don't be
        </b>
        late! (imperative)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It is also possible to use
       <i>
        do
       </i>
       -support in affirmative imperatives, for emphasis or (sometimes) politeness: "Do be quiet!"; "Do help yourself!".
      </p>
      <p>
       The subject
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       may be included for emphasis in negated imperatives as well ("You don't touch these!") There is also a fairly common construction where
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       (not necessarily emphasized) follows
       <i>
        don't
       </i>
       : "Don't you touch these!"
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latin">
        Latin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Latin regular imperatives include
       <i>
        amā
       </i>
       (2nd pers. singular) and
       <i>
        amāte
       </i>
       (2nd pers. plural), from the
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       <i>
        amāre
       </i>
       ("to love"); similarly
       <i>
        monē
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        monēte
       </i>
       from
       <i>
        monēre
       </i>
       ("to advise/warn");
       <i>
        audī
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        audīte
       </i>
       from
       <i>
        audīre
       </i>
       ("to hear"), etc. The negative imperative is formed with the infinitive of the verb, preceded by the imperative of
       <i>
        nōlle
       </i>
       ("to not want"):
       <i>
        nōlī stāre
       </i>
       ("don't stand", 2nd pers. singular) and
       <i>
        nōlīte stāre
       </i>
       (2nd pers. plural); compare the positive imperative
       <i>
        stā
       </i>
       ("stand", 2nd pers. singular) and
       <i>
        stāte
       </i>
       (2nd pers. plural).
      </p>
      <p>
       For third-person imperatives, the subjunctive mood is used instead.
      </p>
      <p>
       Latin also has a future imperative form. The corresponding forms are
       <i>
        amātō
       </i>
       (singular) and
       <i>
        amātōte
       </i>
       (plural),
       <i>
        monētō
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        monētōte
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        audītō
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        audītōte
       </i>
       . Unlike the present imperative, the future imperative also has special forms for the third person (
       <i>
        amantō
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        monentō
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        audiuntō
       </i>
       ). See
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_conjugation" title="Latin conjugation">
        Latin conjugation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Germanic_languages">
        Germanic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Germanic languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch">
        Dutch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dutch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A peculiar feature of
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       is, that it can form an imperative mood in the past tense. Its use is fairly common:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Had gebeld!
        </i>
        (“You should have called!”)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Was gekomen!
        </i>
        (“You should have come!”)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="German">
        German
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: German">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       German verbs have a singular and a plural imperative. The singular imperative is equivalent to the bare stem or the bare stem +
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       . (In most verbs, both ways are correct.) The plural imperative is the same as the second-person plural of the present tense.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         sing!
        </i>
        or:
        <i>
         singe!
        </i>
        — said to one person: “sing!”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         singt!
        </i>
        — said to a group of persons: “sing!”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In order to emphasize their addressee, German imperatives can be followed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       personal pronouns
       <i>
        du
       </i>
       (“thou; you [sg.]”) or
       <i>
        ihr
       </i>
       (“you [pl.]”), respectively. For example:
       <i>
        Geh weg!
       </i>
       –
       <i>
        Geh
        <u>
         du
        </u>
        doch weg!
       </i>
       (“Go away!” – “Why,
       <u>
        you
       </u>
       go away!”).
      </p>
      <p>
       German has
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T/V_distinction" title="T/V distinction">
        T/V distinction
       </a>
       , which means that the pronouns
       <i>
        du
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ihr
       </i>
       are used chiefly towards persons with whom one is privately acquainted, which holds true for the corresponding imperatives. (For details see
       <a href="/wiki/German_grammar" title="German grammar">
        German grammar
       </a>
       .) Otherwise, the social-distance pronoun
       <i>
        Sie
       </i>
       (“you”) is used for both singular and plural. Since there exists no actual imperative corresponding to
       <i>
        Sie
       </i>
       , the form is paraphrased with the third-person plural of the present
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       followed by the pronoun:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         singen Sie!
        </i>
        — said to one or more persons: “sing!”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         seien Sie still!
        </i>
        — said to one or more persons: “be quiet!”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       German can theoretically form past imperatives and passive imperatives, simply by using the imperative form of the respective
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verbs
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        habe gesungen!
       </i>
       (“[you shall] have sung!”),
       <i>
        werde geliebt!
       </i>
       (“[you shall] be loved!”),
       <i>
        sei geliebt worden!
       </i>
       (“[you shall] have been loved!”). None of such forms is really current, however.
      </p>
      <p>
       Like English, German features many constructions that express commands, wishes, etc. They are thus
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantically
       </a>
       related to imperatives without being imperatives grammatically:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         lasst uns singen!
        </i>
        (“let’s sing!”)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         mögest du singen!
        </i>
        (“may you sing!”)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         du sollst singen!
        </i>
        (“you shall sing!”)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="French">
        French
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: French">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Examples of regular imperatives in French are
       <i>
        mange
       </i>
       (2nd pers. singular),
       <i>
        mangez
       </i>
       (2nd pers. plural) and
       <i>
        mangeons
       </i>
       (1st person plural, "let's eat"), from
       <i>
        manger
       </i>
       ("to eat") – these are similar or identical to the corresponding present indicative forms, although there are some irregular imperatives that resemble the present subjunctives, such as
       <i>
        sois
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        soyez
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        soyons
       </i>
       , from
       <i>
        être
       </i>
       ("to be"). A third person imperative can be formed using a subjunctive clause with the conjunction
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , as in
       <i>
        qu'ils mangent de la brioche
       </i>
       ("
       <a href="/wiki/Let_them_eat_cake" title="Let them eat cake">
        let them eat cake
       </a>
       ").
      </p>
      <p>
       French uses different word order for affirmative and negative imperative sentences:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Donne-le-leur.
        </i>
        ("Give it to them.")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ne le leur donne pas.
        </i>
        ("Don't give it to them.")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The negative imperative (prohibitive) has the same word order as the indicative. See
       <a href="/wiki/French_personal_pronouns#Clitic_order" title="French personal pronouns">
        French personal pronouns § Clitic order
       </a>
       for detail. Like in English, imperative sentences often end with an exclamation mark, e.g. to emphasize an order.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">
        Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In Spanish, imperatives for the familiar singular second person (
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       ) are usually identical to indicative forms for the singular third person. However, there are irregular verbs for which unique imperative forms for
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       exist.
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        alternative to
        <i>

      <p>
       If an imperative takes a pronoun as an object, it is appended to the verb; for example,
       <i>
        Dime
       </i>
       ("Tell me"). Pronouns can be stacked like they can in indicative clauses:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Me lo dices. ("You tell me it" or "You tell it to me", can also mean "You tell me" as
        <i>
         lo
        </i>
        usually isn't translated)
       </li>
       <li>
        Dímelo. ("Tell me it", "Tell it to me", "Tell me")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Imperatives can be formed for
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (singular formal second person),
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       (plural second person), and
       <i>
        nosotros
       </i>
       (plural first person) from the respective present subjunctive form. Negative imperatives for these pronouns (as well as
       <i>

       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       ) are also formed this way, but are negated by
       <i>
        no
       </i>
       (e.g.
       <i>
        No cantes
       </i>
       , "Don't sing").
      </p>

       has imperative forms for present, aorist, and perfect tenses for the active, middle, and passive voices. Within these tenses, forms exist for second and third persons, for singular, dual, and plural subjects. Subjunctive forms with μή are used for negative imperatives in the aorist.

       <i>

       </i>
       "let me not hear that again").

       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">

       </a>
       , लोट् लकार् (
       <i>
        lōṭ lakār
       </i>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">
        Finnish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Finnish">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , there are two ways of forming a first-person plural imperative. A standard version exists, but it is typically replaced colloquially by the impersonal tense. For example, from
       <i>
        mennä
       </i>
       ("to go"), the imperative "let's go" can be expressed by
       <i>
        menkäämme
       </i>
       (standard form) or
       <i>
        mennään
       </i>
       (colloquial).
      </p>
      <p>
       Forms also exist for second (sing.
       <i>
        mene
       </i>
       , plur.
       <i>
        menkää
       </i>
       ) and third (sing.
       <i>
        menköön
       </i>
       , plur.
       <i>
        menkööt
       </i>
       ) person. Only first person singular doesn't have an imperative.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew_and_Arabic">
        Hebrew and Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Hebrew and Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Generally, in
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       , every word belongs to a word-family, and is, actually, a conjugation of word-family's three consonant
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_root" title="Semitic root">
        roots
       </a>
       . The various conjugations are made by adding vowels to the root consonants and by adding prefixes, in front or after the root consonant. For example, the conjugations of the root K.T.B (כ.ת.ב. ك.ت.ب), both in Hebrew and in Arabic, are words that have something to do with writing. Nouns like
       <i>
        a reporter
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        a letter
       </i>
       and verbs like
       <i>
        to write
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        to dictate
       </i>
       are conjugations of the root K.T.B. The verbs are further conjugated to bodies, times, and so on.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both in classic Hebrew and in classic Arabic, there is a form for positive imperative. It exists for singular and plural, masculine and feminine second-person. The imperative conjugations look like shortages of the future ones. However, in modern Hebrew, the imperative is used very colloquial, and often, the future tense is used in its place.
      </p>
      <p>
       The negative imperative in those languages is more complicated. In modern Hebrew, for instance, it contains a synonym of the word "no", that is used only in negative imperative (אַל), and is followed by the future tense.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Turkish">
        Turkish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Imperative_mood&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Turkish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       For the most common imperative form, the second person singular,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       uses the bare verb stem without the infinitive ending
       <i>
        -mek
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -mak
       </i>
       . Other imperative forms use various suffixes. In the second person plural there are two forms: the formal imperative with the suffix
       <i>
        -in
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -ın
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -un
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -ün
       </i>
       , and the public imperative used for notices and advice, which uses the suffix
       <i>
        -iniz
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -ınız
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -unuz
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        -ünüz
       </i>
       . All Turkish imperative suffixes change depending on the verb stem according to the rules of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
        vowel harmony
       </a>
       . For the verb
       <i>
        içmek
       </i>
       ("to drink", also "to smoke" a cigarette or similar):
      </p>
      <table border="1" class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         The verb
         <i>
          içmek
         </i>
         ("to drink")
        </th>
        <th>
         1st person singular
        </th>
        <th>
         1st person plural
        </th>
        <th>
         2nd person singular/informal
        </th>
        <th>
         2nd person plural/formal
        </th>
        <th>
         2nd person plural/public advice
        </th>
        <th>
         3rd person singular
        </th>
        <th>
         3rd person plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Imperative form
        </th>
        <td>
         <i>
          içeyim
         </i>
         ("let me drink")
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          içelim
         </i>
         ("let us drink")
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          iç
         </i>
         ("Drink!")
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          için
         </i>
         ("Drink!")
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          içiniz
         </i>
         ("Drink!", e.g.
         <i>
          Soğuk içiniz
         </i>
         "Drink cold" on soft drinks)
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          içsin
         </i>
         ("let him/her drink")
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          içsinler
         </i>
         ("let them drink")
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Negative imperative forms are made in the same way, but using a negated verb as the base. For example, the second person singular imperative of
       <i>
        içmemek
       </i>
       ("not to drink") is
       <i>
        içme
       </i>
       ("Don't drink!"). Other
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic languages
       </a>
       construct imperative forms similarly to Turkish.
      </p>
