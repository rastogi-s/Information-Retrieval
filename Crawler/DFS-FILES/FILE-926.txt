      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Gallo-Romance
       </b>
       branch of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       includes
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sensu" title="Sensu">
         sensu stricto
        </a>
       </i>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        Oïl languages
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and its closest relatives such as
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal language
       </a>
       (Arpitan).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, other definitions are far broader, variously encompassing the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitano-Romance_languages" title="Occitano-Romance languages">
        Occitano-Romance
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
        Rhaeto-Romance
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The Gallo-Romance group includes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
         Langues d'oïl
        </a>
        , or Oïl languages. These include
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         Standard French
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
         Picard
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
         Walloon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
         Lorrain
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
         Normand
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Poitevin_dialect" title="Poitevin dialect">
         Poitevin
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_language_(O%C3%AFl)" title="Burgundian language (Oïl)">
         Bourgignon
        </a>
        . These are the most phonologically innovative Romance varieties.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
         Arpitan
        </a>
        language, also known as Franco-Provençal, of southeastern France, western Switzerland, and
        <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">
         Aosta Valley
        </a>
        region of northwestern Italy. Formerly thought of as a dialect of either Oïl or Occitan, it is linguistically a language on its own, or rather a separate group of languages, as many of its dialects have little mutual comprehensibility. It shares features of both
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Proven%C3%A7al_dialect" title="Provençal dialect">
         Provençal dialect of Occitan
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other language families which are sometimes included in Gallo-Romance:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Occitano-Romance_languages" title="Occitano-Romance languages">
         Occitano-Romance languages
        </a>
        of Southern France and neighbouring areas, includes
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         Occitan
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        . Occitano-Romance can be classified as Gallo-Romance,
        <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">
         Iberian Romance
        </a>
        , or as a branch of the
        <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
         Western Romance languages
        </a>
        .
        <ul>
         <li>
          The
          <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
           Occitan language
          </a>
          , or langue d'oc, has dialects such as
          <a href="/wiki/Proven%C3%A7al_dialect" title="Provençal dialect">
           Provençal
          </a>
          , and
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gascon_dialect" title="Gascon dialect">
           Gascon-Aranese
          </a>
          .
         </li>
         <li>
          The
          <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
           Catalan language
          </a>
          has standard forms of Catalan and
          <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
           Valencian
          </a>
          . The inclusion of Catalan in Gallo-Romance is disputed by some linguists
          <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
           [
           <i>
            <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
             <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2015)">
              who?
             </span>
            </a>
           </i>
           ]
          </sup>
          who prefer to group it with
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibero-Romance_languages" title="Ibero-Romance languages">
           Iberian Romance
          </a>
          , since although Old Catalan is close to Old Occitan, it later adjusted its lexicon to some degree to align with Spanish.
          <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

           ]

          In general however, modern Catalan, especially grammatically, remains closer to modern Occitan than to either Spanish or Portuguese.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">

        </a>
        , including
        <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">

        </a>

        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">

        <a href="/wiki/Dolomites" title="Dolomites">

        <a href="/wiki/Friulian_language" title="Friulian language">

        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">

        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">

        </a>
        . Rhaeto-Romance is a diverse group, with the Italian varieties influenced by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetan_language" title="Venetan language">
         Venetan
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">

        </a>
        and Romansh by Franco-Provençal.

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard" title="Eastern Lombard">

       In the view of some linguists (
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bec" title="Pierre Bec">
        Pierre Bec
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Andreas_Schorta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Andreas Schorta (page does not exist)">
        Andreas Schorta
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Schmid" title="Heinrich Schmid">

        Geoffrey Hull
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Istriot" title="Istriot">
        Istriot
       </a>
       dialects, whose Italianate features are deemed to be superficial and secondary in nature.

      <p>
       How far the Gallo-Romance languages spread varies a great deal depending on which languages are included in the group. Those included in its narrowest definition (i.e. the Langues d'oïl and Arpitan) were historically spoken in the north of
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , parts of
       <a href="/wiki/French_Flanders" title="French Flanders">
        Flanders
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alsace" title="Alsace">
        Alsace
       </a>
       , part of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lorraine_(region)" title="Lorraine (region)">
        Lorraine
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       region of
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Channel_Islands" title="Channel Islands">
        Channel Islands
       </a>
       , parts of Switzerland, and northern Italy.
      </p>
      <p>
       Today, a single Gallo-Romance language (French) dominates much of this geographic region (including the formerly non-Romance areas of France) and has also spread overseas.
      </p>
      <p>
       At its broadest, the area also encompasses southern France,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">
        Valencia
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balearic_islands" title="Balearic islands">
        Balearic islands
       </a>
       in eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       , and much of northern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="General_characteristics">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h2>

      <p>
       The Gallo-Romance languages are generally considered the most innovative (least conservative) among the Romance languages. Northern France (the medieval area of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
        langue d'oïl
       </a>
       , from which modern French developed) was the epicentre. Characteristic Gallo-Romance features generally developed earliest and appear in their most extreme manifestation in the langue d'oïl, gradually spreading out from there along riverways and roads. The earliest vernacular Romance writing occurred in Northern France, as the development of vernacular writing in a given area was forced by the almost total inability of Romance speakers to understand Classical Latin, still the vehicle of writing and culture.
      </p>
      <p>
       Gallo-Romance languages are usually characterised by the loss of all unstressed final vowels other than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-a/
       </span>
       (most significantly, final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-o/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-e/
       </span>
       were lost). However, when the loss of a final vowel would result in an impossible final cluster (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tr/
       </span>
       ), a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prop_vowel" title="Prop vowel">
        prop vowel
       </a>
       appears in place of the lost vowel, usually
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       . Generally, the same changes also occurred in final syllables closed by a consonant.
      </p>
      <p>
       Furthermore, loss of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       in a final syllable was early enough in Primitive Old French that the Classical Latin third singular
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       was often preserved:
       <i>
        venit
       </i>
       "he comes" &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈvɛːnet/
       </span>
       (Romance vowel changes) &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈvjɛnet/
       </span>
       (diphthongization) &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈvjɛned/
       </span>
       (lenition) &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈvjɛnd/
       </span>
       (Gallo-Romance final vowel loss) &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈvjɛnt/
       </span>
       (final devoicing). Elsewhere, final vowel loss occurred later or unprotected
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       was lost earlier (perhaps under Italian influence).
      </p>
      <p>
       Other than southern Occitano-Romance, the Gallo-Romance languages are quite innovative, with French and some of the Gallo-Italian languages rivaling each other for the most extreme phonological changes compared with more conservative languages. For example, French
       <i>
        sain, saint, sein, ceint, seing
       </i>
       meaning "healthy, holy, breast, (he) girds, signature" (Latin
       <i>
        sānum
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sanctum
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sinum
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cinget
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        signum
       </i>
       ) are all pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sɛ̃/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In other ways, however, the Gallo-Romance languages are conservative. The older stages of many of the languages are famous for preserving a two-case system consisting of nominative and oblique, fully marked on nouns, adjectives and determiners, inherited almost directly from the Latin nominative and accusative cases and preserving a number of different declensional classes and irregular forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the opposite of the normal pattern, the languages closest to the oïl epicentre preserve the case system the best, and languages at the periphery (near languages that had long before lost the case system except on pronouns) lost it early. For example, the case system was preserved in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Occitan" title="Old Occitan">
        Old Occitan
       </a>
       until around the 13th century but had already been lost in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Old Catalan
       </a>
       , despite the fact that there were very few other differences between the two.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Occitan group is known for an innovatory
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       ending on many subjunctive and preterite verbs and an unusual development of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð]
       </span>
       (Latin intervocalic -d-), which, in many varieties, merges with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dz]
       </span>
       (from intervocalic palatalised -c- and -ty-).
      </p>

      <p>
       These are the notable characteristics of the Gallo-Romance languages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Early loss of all final vowels other than
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        is the defining characteristic, as noted above.
       </li>
       <li>
        Further reductions of final vowels in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
         langue d'oïl
        </a>
        and many
        <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
         Gallo-Italic languages
        </a>
        , with the feminine
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prop_vowel" title="Prop vowel">
         prop vowel
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        merging into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ə/
        </span>
        , which is often subsequently dropped.
       </li>
       <li>
        Early, heavy reduction of unstressed vowels in the interior of a word (another defining characteristic). That and final vowel reduction are most of the extreme phonemic differences between the Northern and the Central Italian dialects, which otherwise share a great deal of vocabulary and syntax.
       </li>
       <li>
        Loss of final vowels phonemicised the long vowels that had been automatic concomitants of stressed open syllables. The phonemic long vowels are maintained directly in many Northern Italian dialects. Elsewhere, phonemic length was lost, but many of the long vowels had been diphthongised, resulting in a maintenance of the original distinction. The langue d'oïl branch was again at the forefront of innovation, with no less than five of the seven long vowels diphthongising (only high vowels were spared).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Front_rounded_vowel" title="Front rounded vowel">
         Front rounded vowels
        </a>
        are present in all four branches.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /u/
        </span>
        usually fronts to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /y/
        </span>
        , and mid-front rounded vowels often develop from long
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /oː/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɔː/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Extreme lenition (repeated lenition) occurs in many languages, especially in langue d'oïl and many
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italian_languages" title="Gallo-Italian languages">
         Gallo-Italian languages
        </a>
        . Examples from French:
        <i>
         ˈvītam
        </i>
        &gt;
        <i>
         vie
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /vi/
        </span>
        "life"; *
        <i>
         saˈpūtum
        </i>
        &gt;
        <i>
         su
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /sy/
        </span>
        "known"; similarly
        <i>
         vu
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /vy/
        </span>
        "seen" &lt; *
        <i>
         vidūtum
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         pu
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /py/
        </span>
        "been able" &lt; *
        <i>
         potūtum
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         eu
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /y/
        </span>
        "had" &lt; *
        <i>
         habūtum
        </i>
        . Examples from Lombard: *"căsa" &gt; "cà"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ka/
        </span>
        "home, house"
       </li>
       <li>
        Langue d'oïl, Swiss
        <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
         Rhaeto-Romance languages
        </a>
        and many northern dialects of Occitan have a secondary
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
         palatalization
        </a>
        of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /a/
        </span>
        , producing different results from the primary Romance palatalisation:
        <i>
         centum
        </i>
        "hundred" &gt;
        <i>
         cent
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /sɑ̃/
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         cantum
        </i>
        "song" &gt;
        <i>
         chant
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃɑ̃/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Other than
        <a href="/wiki/Occitano-Romance_languages" title="Occitano-Romance languages">
         Occitano-Romance languages
        </a>
        , most Gallo-Romance languages are subject-obligatory (whereas all the rest of the Romance languages are
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-drop" title="Pro-drop">
         pro-drop
        </a>
        languages). That is a late development triggered by progressive phonetic erosion: Old French was still a null-subject language until the loss of secondarily final consonants in Middle French.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Gallo-Italian languages have a number of features in common with the other Italian languages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Loss of final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        , which triggers raising of the preceding vowel (more properly, the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        "
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Debuccalize" title="Debuccalize">
         debuccalizes
        </a>
        " to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        , which is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monophthongize" title="Monophthongize">
         monophthongized
        </a>
        into a higher vowel):
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /-as/
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /-e/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /-es/
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /-i/
        </span>
        , hence
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
         Standard Italian
        </a>
        plural
        <i>
         cani
        </i>
        &lt;
        <i>
         canes
        </i>
        , subjunctive
        <i>
         tu canti
        </i>
        &lt;
        <i>
         tu cantes
        </i>
        , indicative
        <i>
         tu cante
        </i>
        &lt;
        <i>
         tu cantas
        </i>
        (now
        <i>
         tu canti
        </i>
        in Standard Italian, borrowed from the subjunctive);
        <i>
         amiche
        </i>
        "female friends" &lt;
        <i>
         amicas
        </i>
        . The
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
         palatalisation
        </a>
        in the masculine
        <i>
         amici
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aˈmitʃi/
        </span>
        , compared with the lack of palatalisation in
        <i>
         amiche
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aˈmike/
        </span>
        , shows that feminine
        <i>
         -e
        </i>
        cannot come from Latin
        <i>
         ae
        </i>
        , which became
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɛː/
        </span>
        by the first-century AD, and could certainly have triggered palatalisation.
       </li>
       <li>
        Use of nominative
        <i>
         -i
        </i>
        for masculine plurals instead of accusative
        <i>
         -os
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
