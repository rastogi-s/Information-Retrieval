      <p>
       <b>
        Emilian
       </b>
       is a group of
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol language
       </a>
       spoken in the area historically called
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia_(region_of_Italy)" title="Emilia (region of Italy)">
        Emilia
       </a>
       , the western portion of today's
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       region in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no standardised version of Emilian.
      </p>
      <p>
       The default word order is
       <a href="/wiki/Subject%E2%80%93verb%E2%80%93object" title="Subject–verb–object">
        subject–verb–object
       </a>
       . There are two
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        genders
       </a>
       as well as a distinction between plural and singular. Emilian has a strong
       <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
        T–V distinction
       </a>
       to distinguish varying levels of politeness, social distance, courtesy, familiarity or insult. Its alphabet uses a considerable number of
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        diacritics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emilian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Emilian is a dialect of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol language
       </a>
       , one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic languages
       </a>
       . There is a high degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutual intelligibility
       </a>
       between the various varieties of Emilian and the other dialect,
       <a href="/wiki/Romagnol_dialect" title="Romagnol dialect">
        Romagnol
       </a>
       . The Gallo-Italic family has Emilian-Romagnol,
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">
        Dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emilian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Linguasphere_Observatory" title="Linguasphere Observatory">
        Linguasphere Observatory
       </a>
       recognises the following dialects:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Mantovano
        </b>
        , spoken in all but the very north of the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Mantua" title="Province of Mantua">
         Province of Mantua
        </a>
        in Lombardy. It has a strong Lombard influence.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vogherese (Pavese-Vogherese)
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pavia" title="Province of Pavia">
         Province of Pavia
        </a>
        in Lombardy. It is closely related phonetically and morphologically to Piacentino. It is also akin to Tortonese.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
           <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (October 2013)">
            clarification needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Piacentino
        </b>
        , spoken west of the
        <a href="/wiki/Taro_(river)" title="Taro (river)">
         River Taro
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Piacenza" title="Province of Piacenza">
         Province of Piacenza
        </a>
        and on the border with the province of Parma. The variants of Piacentino are strongly influenced by Lombard, Piedmontese, and Ligurian.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Parmigiano_dialect" title="Parmigiano dialect">
          Parmigiano
         </a>
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Parma" title="Province of Parma">
         Province of Parma
        </a>
        . Those from the area refer to the Parmigiano spoken outside of Parma as Arioso or Parmense, although today's urban and rural dialects are so mixed that only a few speak the original. The language spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Casalmaggiore" title="Casalmaggiore">
         Casalmaggiore
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cremona" title="Province of Cremona">
         Province of Cremona
        </a>
        to the north of Parma is closely related to Parmigiano.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Reggiano
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Reggio_Emilia" title="Province of Reggio Emilia">
         Province of Reggio Emilia
        </a>
        , although the northern parts (such as
        <a href="/wiki/Guastalla" title="Guastalla">
         Guastalla
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Luzzara" title="Luzzara">
         Luzzara
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Reggiolo" title="Reggiolo">
         Reggiolo
        </a>
        ) of the province are not part of this group and closer to Mantovano.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Modenese
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Modena" title="Province of Modena">
         Province of Modena
        </a>
        , although Bolognese is more widespread in the Castelfranco area. In the northern part of the province of Modena, the lowlands around the town of Mirandola, a Mirandolese sub-dialect of Modenese is spoken.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Bolognese_dialect" title="Bolognese dialect">
          Bolognese
         </a>
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Bologna" title="Province of Bologna">
         Province of Bologna
        </a>
        and in around
        <a href="/wiki/Castelfranco_Emilia" title="Castelfranco Emilia">
         Castelfranco Emilia
        </a>
        , Modena.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Ferrarese
        </b>
        , spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Ferrara" title="Province of Ferrara">
         Province of Ferrara
        </a>
        , southern
        <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">
         Veneto
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Comacchio" title="Comacchio">
         Comacchio
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other definitions include the following:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Carrarese
        </b>
        and the Lunigiano dialect, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Carrara" title="Carrara">
         Carrara
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lunigiana" title="Lunigiana">
         Lunigiana
        </a>
        , in almost all of the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Massa_and_Carrara" title="Province of Massa and Carrara">
         Province of Massa and Carrara
        </a>
        and a good portion of the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_La_Spezia" title="Province of La Spezia">
         Province of La Spezia
        </a>
        , i.e. northwestern
        <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
         Tuscany
        </a>
        . Historically, this region has been part of both Tuscany and the
        <a href="/wiki/Duchy_of_Parma" title="Duchy of Parma">
         Duchy of Parma
        </a>
        at different times, so has a close economic relationship with the Emilian area and is geographically proximate due to the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magra_(river)" title="Magra (river)">
         Magra
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Vara_(river)" title="Vara (river)">
         Vara
        </a>
        rivers.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Massese
        </b>
        (mixed with some Tuscanian features)
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Casalasco
        </b>
        , spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Cremona" title="Cremona">
         Cremona
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">
         Lombardy
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">
        Writing system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Emilian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Writing system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Emilian is written using a
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       that has never been standardised. As a result, spelling varies widely across the dialects. The dialects are largely oral and rarely written. However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
        Bible
       </a>
       was published in Emilian-Romagnol in 1865; the work has since been lost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
