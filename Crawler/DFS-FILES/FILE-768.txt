      <p>
       <b>
        Inverted question marks
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        ¿
       </b>
       ) and
       <b>
        exclamation marks
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="English in the Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth English
       </a>
       ) or
       <b>
        exclamation points
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       ) (
       <b>
        ¡
       </b>
       ) are
       <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">
        punctuation
       </a>
       marks used to begin interrogative and exclamatory sentences (or clauses), respectively, in written
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and sometimes also in languages which have cultural ties with Spanish, such as in older standards of
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       (now it is optional and not recommended) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Waray_language" title="Waray language">
        Waray language
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They can also be combined in several ways to express the combination of a question and surprise or disbelief. The initial marks are normally mirrored at the end of the sentence or clause by the common marks (
       <b>
        ?
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        !
       </b>
       ) used in most other languages. Unlike the ending marks, which are printed along the
       <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(typography)" title="Baseline (typography)">
        baseline
       </a>
       of a sentence, the inverted marks (¿ and ¡)
       <a href="/wiki/Descender" title="Descender">
        descend below the line
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Inverted marks were originally recommended by the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
         Real Academia Española
        </a>
       </i>
       (Spanish Royal Academy) in 1754, and adopted gradually over the next century
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On computers, inverted marks are supported by various standards, including
       <a href="/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-1" title="ISO/IEC 8859-1">
        ISO-8859-1
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/SGML_entity" title="SGML entity">
        HTML
       </a>
       . They can be entered directly on keyboards designed for Spanish-speaking countries, or via alternative methods on other keyboards.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">
        Spanish orthography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The inverted
       <a href="/wiki/Question_mark" title="Question mark">
        question mark
       </a>
       (¿) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Punctuation" title="Punctuation">
        punctuation
       </a>
       mark written before the first letter of an interrogative sentence or clause to indicate that a question follows. It is an inverted form of the standard symbol "?" recognized by speakers of languages written with the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
        Latin alphabet
       </a>
       . In most languages, a single question mark is used, and only at the end of an interrogative sentence: "How old are you?" This was once true of the Spanish language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The inverted question mark was adopted long
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
          <span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (October 2017)">
           specify
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia" title="Real Academia">
        Real Academia
       </a>
       's decision, published in the second edition of the
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <i>
          Ortografía de la lengua castellana
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       (Orthography of the Castilian language) in 1754
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       recommending it as the symbol indicating the beginning of a question in written Spanish—e.g.
       <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <i>
         ¿Cuántos años tienes?
        </i>
       </span>
       ("How old are you?"). The Real Academia also ordered the same inverted-symbol system for statements of exclamation, using the symbols "¡" and "!". This helps to recognize questions and exclamations in long sentences. "Do you like summer?" and "You like summer." are translated respectively as "¿Te gusta el verano?" and "Te gusta el verano." (There is no difference between the wording of a
       <a href="/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question" title="Yes–no question">
        yes–no question
       </a>
       and the corresponding statement in Spanish as there is in English.) These new rules were slowly adopted; there exist nineteenth-century books in which the writer does not use either opening symbol, neither the "¡" nor the "¿".
      </p>
      <p>
       In sentences that are both declarative and interrogative, the clause that asks a question is isolated with the starting-symbol inverted question mark, for example:
       <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <i>
         Si no puedes ir con ellos, ¿quieres ir con nosotros?
        </i>
       </span>
       ("If you cannot go with them, would you like to go with us?")
      </p>
      <p>
       Some writers omit the inverted question mark in the case of a short unambiguous question such as:
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <i>
          Quién viene?
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("Who comes?"). This is the criterion in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain Catalan-language authorities, such as Joan Solà, insist that both the opening and closing question marks be used for clarity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some Spanish-language writers, among them Nobel laureate
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">
        Pablo Neruda
       </a>
       (1904–1973), refuse to use the inverted question mark.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is common in Internet chat rooms and instant messaging now to use only the single "?" as an ending symbol for a question, since it saves typing time. Multiple closing symbols are used for emphasis:
       <i>
        Por qué dices eso??
       </i>
       , instead of the standard
       <i>
        ¿Por qué dices eso?
       </i>
       ("Why do you say that?"). Some may also use the ending symbol for both beginning and ending, giving
       <i>
        ?Por qué dices eso?
       </i>
       Given the informal setting, this might be unimportant; however, teachers see this as a problem, fearing and claiming that contemporary young people are inappropriately and incorrectly extending the practice to academic homework and essays. (See
       <a href="/wiki/Internet_linguistics#Educational_perspective" title="Internet linguistics">
        Internet linguistics: Educational perspective
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-notice" role="presentation">

      <p>
       In 1668,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Wilkins" title="John Wilkins">
        John Wilkins
       </a>
       proposed using the inverted exclamation mark "¡" as a symbol at the end of a sentence to
       <a href="/wiki/Irony_punctuation" title="Irony punctuation">
        denote irony
       </a>
       . He was one of many, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus" title="Desiderius Erasmus">
        Desiderius Erasmus
       </a>
       , who felt there was a need for such a punctuation mark, but Wilkins' proposal, as was true of the other attempts, failed to take hold.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houston2013_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Houston2013-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-irony_mark_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-irony_mark-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mixtures">
        Mixtures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mixtures">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although it has now become rare, it is correct usage in Spanish to begin a sentence with an opening inverted
       <a href="/wiki/Exclamation_mark" title="Exclamation mark">
        exclamation mark
       </a>
       ("¡") and end it with a question mark ("?"), or vice versa, for statements that are questions but also have a clear sense of exclamation or surprise such as:
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <i>
          ¡Y tú quién te crees?
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("Who do you think you are?!"). Normally, four signs are used, always with one type in the outer side and the other in the inner side (nested) (
       <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <i>
         ¿¡Y tú quién te crees!?
        </i>
       </span>
       ,
       <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <i>
         ¡¿Y tú quién te crees?!
        </i>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       )
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode#Versions" title="Unicode">
        Unicode 5.1
       </a>
       also includes
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">
         U+2E18
        </span>
       </span>
       <span style="font-size:125%">
        ⸘
       </span>
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">
        INVERTED INTERROBANG
       </span>
       , which is an
       <a href="/wiki/Interrobang#Inverted_interrobang" title="Interrobang">
        inverted version of the interrobang
       </a>
       (also known as a "gnaborretni"), a nonstandard punctuation mark used to denote both excitement and a question in just one glyph.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Encodings">
        Encodings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Encodings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       "¡" and "¿" are both located within the Unicode Common block, and are both inherited from ISO-8859-1. "¡" has Unicode codepoint U+00A1 (decimal entity reference
       <code>
        &amp;#161;
       </code>
       ) and HTML named entity reference
       <code>
        &amp;iexcl;
       </code>
       . "¿" has Unicode codepoint U+00BF (decimal entity reference
       <code>
        &amp;#191;
       </code>
       ) and has HTML named entity reference
       <code>
        &amp;iquest;
       </code>
       . In both cases, the "i" in the named entity reference is an initialism for "inverted".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Microsoft_Word" title="Microsoft Word">

       </a>

       <kbd class="keyboard-key nowrap" style="border: 1px solid #aaa; -moz-border-radius: 0.2em; -webkit-border-radius: 0.2em; border-radius: 0.2em; -moz-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); -webkit-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); background-color: #f9f9f9; background-image: -moz-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -o-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -webkit-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: linear-gradient(to bottom, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); padding: 0.1em 0.3em; font-family: inherit; font-size: 0.85em;">

       </kbd>
       and type 173 on the numeric keypad.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Input_methods_for_LaTeX">
        Input methods for LaTeX
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Input methods for LaTeX">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/LaTeX" title="LaTeX">
        LaTeX
       </a>
       documents, the "¿" is written as
       <code>
        ?`
       </code>
       (question mark, backtick) or
       <code>
        \textquestiondown
       </code>
       , and "¡" as
       <code>
        !`
       </code>
       (exclamation point, backtick) or
       <code>
        \textexclamdown
       </code>
       . (This may require including "\usepackage[utf8]{inputenc}" at the top of the document.)
       <a href="/wiki/XeTeX" title="XeTeX">
        XeLaTeX
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/LuaTeX" title="LuaTeX">
        LuaLaTeX
       </a>
       have full Unicode support, and literal ¿ and ¡ characters may be input.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use_in_filenames">
        Use in filenames
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Use in filenames">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Windows, an inverted question mark is valid in a file or directory name, whereas the normal is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reserved_character" title="Reserved character">
        reserved character
       </a>
       which cannot be so used.
      </p>
