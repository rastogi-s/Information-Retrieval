      <p>
       <b>
        African French
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         français africain
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is the generic name of the varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       spoken by an estimated 120 million (2010) people in
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       spread across 24
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophone
       </a>
       countries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007_report_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2007_report-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This includes those who speak French as a
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       in these 31 francophone African countries (dark blue on the map), but it does not include French speakers living in non-francophone African countries. Africa is thus the continent with the most French speakers in the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007_report_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-2007_report-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       French arrived in Africa as a
       <a href="/wiki/French_colonial_empire" title="French colonial empire">
        colonial
       </a>
       language. These African French speakers are now a large part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Francophonie" title="Francophonie">
        Francophonie
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In Africa, French is often spoken alongside indigenous languages, but in some areas it has become a first language, such as in the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">
        Abidjan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">
        Ivory Coast
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Libreville" title="Libreville">
        Libreville
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">
        Gabon
       </a>
       . In some countries it is a first language among some
       <a href="/wiki/Social_class" title="Social class">
        classes
       </a>
       of the population, such as in
       <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">
        Tunisia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">
        Algeria
       </a>
       where French is a first language among the
       <a href="/wiki/Upper_class" title="Upper class">
        upper classes
       </a>
       (many people in the upper classes are
       <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_bilingualism" title="Simultaneous bilingualism">
        simultaneous bilinguals
       </a>
       in Arabic/French), but only a second language among the general population.
      </p>
      <p>
       In each of the francophone African countries, French is spoken with local specificities in terms of pronunciation and vocabulary.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
        Varieties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Varieties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are many different varieties of African French, but they can be broadly grouped into four categories:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the French spoken by people in
        <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">
         West
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">
         Central
        </a>
        Africa – spoken altogether by about 75 million people as either a
        <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
         first
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
         second language
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        the French variety spoken by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghrebi_people" title="Maghrebi people">
         Maghrebis
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_people" title="Berber people">
         Berbers
        </a>
        in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northwest_Africa" title="Northwest Africa">
         Northwest Africa
        </a>
        (see
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghreb_French" title="Maghreb French">
         Maghreb French
        </a>
        ), which has about 36 million first and second language speakers.
       </li>
       <li>
        the French variety spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Djibouti" title="Djibouti">
         Djibouti
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Horn_of_Africa" title="Horn of Africa">
         Horn of Africa
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        the French variety spoken by
        <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">
         Creoles
        </a>
        in the
        <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean_Commission" title="Indian Ocean Commission">
         Indian Ocean
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A9union" title="Réunion">
         Réunion
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Mauritius" title="Mauritius">
         Mauritius
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">
         Seychelles
        </a>
        ), which has around 1.6 million first and second language speakers. The French spoken in this region is not to be confused with the
        <a href="/wiki/French-based_creole_languages" title="French-based creole languages">
         French-based creole languages
        </a>
        , which are also spoken in the area.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       All the African French varieties differ from
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">
        standard French
       </a>
       both in terms of pronunciation and vocabulary, but the formal African French used in education, media and legal documents is based on standard French vocabulary.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the colonial period, a vernacular form of
       <a href="/wiki/French-based_creole_languages" title="French-based creole languages">
        creole
       </a>
       French known as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Petit_n%C3%A8gre" title="Petit nègre">
         Petit nègre
        </a>
       </i>
       was also present in West Africa. The term has since, however, become a pejorative term for poorly spoken African French.
      </p>
      <p>
       V.Y. Mudimbe describes African French as possessing "approximate pronunciation, repressed syntax, bloated or tortured vocabulary, intonation, rhythm and accent stuck in the original African language flow; many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
        phonetic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphologic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexical
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Africanisms" title="Africanisms">
        africanisms
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The differences from European French are due to influence from the mother tongues and the complexity of French grammatical rules, which inhibit its learning by most non-native speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The difficulty linguists have in describing African French comes from variations, such as the "pure" language used by many African intellectuals and writers versus the mixtures between French and African languages. For this, the term "creolization" is used, often in a pejorative way, and especially in the areas where French is on the same level with one or more local languages. According to G. Manessy, "The consequences of this concurrency may vary according to the social status of the speakers, to their occupations, to their degree of acculturation and thus to the level of their French knowledge."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manessy_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-manessy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        Code-switching
       </a>
       , or the alternation of languages within a single conversation, takes place in both Senegal and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congo-Kinshasa" title="Congo-Kinshasa">
        Congo-Kinshasa
       </a>
       , the latter having four "national" languages – Kikongo, Lingala, Ciluba and Swahili – which are in a permanent opposition to French. Code-switching has been studied since colonial times by different institutions of linguistics. One of these, located in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dakar,_Senegal" title="Dakar, Senegal">
        Dakar, Senegal
       </a>
       , already spoke of the creolization
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">
          <span title="This statement is inconsistent with other parts of the article. (May 2012)">
           inconsistent
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       of French in 1968, naming the result "franlof": a mix of French and Wolof (the language most spoken in Senegal) which spreads by its use in urban areas and through schools, where teachers often speak Wolof in the classroom despite official instructions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The omnipresence of local languages in francophone African countries – along with insufficiencies in education – has given birth to a new linguistic concept:
       <i>
        le petit français
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-manessy_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-manessy-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Le petit français
       </i>
       is the result of a superposition of the structure of a local language with a narrowed lexical knowledge of French. The specific structures, though very different, are juxtaposed, marking the beginning of the creolization process.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Fran.C3.A7ais_populaire_africain">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Français_populaire_africain">
        Français populaire africain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Français populaire africain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the urban areas of francophone Africa, another type of French has emerged:
       <i>
        Français populaire africain
       </i>
       ("Popular African French") or FPA. It is used in the entirety of sub-Saharan Africa, but especially in African cities such as Abidjan, Ivory Coast;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ouagadougou,_Burkina_Faso" title="Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso">
        Ouagadougou, Burkina Faso
       </a>
       ; Dakar, Senegal;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cotonou,_Benin" title="Cotonou, Benin">
        Cotonou, Benin
       </a>
       ; and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lom%C3%A9,_Togo" title="Lomé, Togo">
        Lomé, Togo
       </a>
       . At its emergence, it was marginalized and associated with the ghetto;
       <i>
        Angèle Bassolé-Ouedraogo
       </i>
       describes the reaction of the scholars:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         Administration and professors do not want to hear that funny-sounding and barbarian language that seems to despise articles and distorts the sense of words. They see in it a harmful influence to the mastery of good French.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bassole_8-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-bassole-8">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       However, FPA has begun to emerge as a second language among the upper class. It has also become a symbol of social acceptance.
      </p>
      <p>
       FPA can be seen as a progressive evolution of Ivorian French. After diffusing out of Ivory Coast, it became Africanized under the influence of young Africans (often students) and cinema, drama, and dance.
      </p>
      <p>
       FPA has its own grammatical rules and lexicon. For example,
       <i>
        "Il ou elle peut me tuer!"
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        "Il ou elle peut me dja!"
       </i>
       can either mean "This person annoys me very much" or "I'm dying for him/her" depending on the circumstances.
       <i>
        "Il ou elle commence à me plaire"
       </i>
       signifies a feeling of exasperation (whereupon it actually means "he or she starts to appeal to me"), and friendship can be expressed with
       <i>
        "c'est mon môgô sûr"
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        "c'est mon bramôgo.
        <span style="margin-right:1.5px">
         "
        </span>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bassole_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-bassole-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       FPA is mainly composed of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metaphors" title="Metaphors">
        metaphors
       </a>
       and images taken from African languages. For example, the upper social class is called
       <i>
        "les en-haut d'en-haut"
       </i>
       (the above from above) or
       <i>
        "les môgôs puissants"
       </i>
       (the powerful môgôs).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">
        Pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Pronunciation in the many varieties of African French can be quite varied. There are nonetheless some trends among African French speakers; for instance, the letter
       <a href="/wiki/R" title="R">
        R
       </a>
       tends to be pronounced as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
        alveolar trill
       </a>
       instead of the standard dialect's
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" title="Uvular trill">
        uvular trill
       </a>
       or '
       <a href="/wiki/Guttural_R" title="Guttural R">
        guttural R
       </a>
       .' The
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_uvular_fricative" title="Voiced uvular fricative">
        voiced uvular fricative
       </a>
       , the sound represented by ⟨غ⟩ in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       word مغرب
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        <i>
         Ma
         <b>
          gh
         </b>
         rib
        </i>
       </a>
       , is another common alternative. Pronunciation of the letters [d], [t], [l] and [n] may also vary, and
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       may differ from standard French.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In terms of vocabulary, there exist three phenomena in African French. First, the presence of words which do not exist in standard French. These words were either coined locally or borrowed from local African languages. As a consequence, each regional variety of African French has its own local words that are not the same as in other varieties of African French, although this local vocabulary only constitutes a small part of the overall vocabulary which for the most part is identical to standard French. When talking to people from other regions or countries, African French speakers often switch to a more standard form of French avoiding this local vocabulary. However, there also exist some African French words that are found across many African countries (see for example
       <i>
        chicotter
       </i>
       in the Abidjan French vocabulary section below)
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A second phenomenon is the use of some words with a meaning different from standard French. For example, the word
       <i>
        présentement
       </i>
       (which means "at the moment" in standard French) is used a lot in
       <a href="/wiki/Sub-Saharan_Africa" title="Sub-Saharan Africa">
        sub-Saharan Africa
       </a>
       (but not in the
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       ) with the meaning of "as a matter of fact", "as it were" and not "at the moment".
      </p>
      <p>
       A third phenomenon is
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       , which is found especially among the educated and upper classes of sub-Saharan Africa. Educated people there tend to speak a very formal sort of French which may sound a bit old-fashioned and conservative to European and North American French speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The local African French vocabulary not found in standard French ranges from slang frowned upon by educated people, to colloquial usage, to words that have entered the formal usage (such as
       <i>
        chicotter
       </i>
       ). The French spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">
        Abidjan
       </a>
       , the largest city of
       <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">
        Ivory Coast
       </a>
       , offers a good example of these contrasting
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        registers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Abidjan_French_vocabulary">
        Abidjan French vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=African_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Abidjan French vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="614" data-file-width="819" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG/300px-Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG/450px-Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG/600px-Abidjan-Plateau1.JPG 2x" width="300"/>

          </a>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="225" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG/300px-Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG/450px-Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG/600px-Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pharmacieroutebassam.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         French language signs outside a pharmacy in
         <a href="/wiki/Port-Bou%C3%ABt" title="Port-Bouët">
          Port-Bouët
         </a>
         , Abidjan, in 2009.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       According to some estimates, French is spoken by 75 to 99 percent of
       <a href="/wiki/Abidjan" title="Abidjan">
        Abidjan
       </a>
       's population,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       either alone or alongside indigenous African languages. There are three sorts of French spoken in Abidjan. A formal French is spoken by the educated classes. Most of the population, however, speaks a colloquial form of French known as
       <i>
        français de Treichville
       </i>
       (after a working-class district of Abidjan) or
       <i>
        français de Moussa
       </i>
       (after a character in chronicles published by the magazine
       <i>
        Ivoire Dimanche
       </i>
       which are written in this colloquial Abidjan French). Finally, an Abidjan French slang called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouchi" title="Nouchi">
         nouchi
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken by people in gangs and also by young people copying them. New words usually appear in
       <i>
        nouchi
       </i>
       and then make their way into colloquial Abidjan French after some time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abidjan_varieties_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abidjan_varieties-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2012, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crowdsourced" title="Crowdsourced">
        crowdsourced
       </a>
       dictionary of Nouchi is being written using mobile phones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Here are some examples of words used in the African French variety spoken in Abidjan (the spelling used here conforms to
       <a href="/wiki/French_orthography" title="French orthography">
        French orthography
       </a>
       , except ô which should be read as -aw in the English word "law"):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         une go
        </i>
        is a slang word meaning a girl or a girlfriend. It is a
        <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
         loanword
        </a>
        either from the
        <a href="/wiki/Mandinka_language" title="Mandinka language">
         Mandinka language
        </a>
        or from
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        ("girl"). It is also French hip-hop slang for a girl.
        <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.dictionnairedelazone.fr/index.php?page=definition&amp;index=resultat&amp;type_recherche=&amp;mots=go&amp;photo=&amp;audio=&amp;video=&amp;illustration=&amp;terme=go" rel="nofollow">
         [1]
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         un maquis
        </i>
        is a colloquial word meaning a street-side eatery, a working-class restaurant serving African food. This word exists in standard French, but its meaning is "
        <a href="/wiki/Maquis_shrubland" title="Maquis shrubland">
         maquis shrubland
        </a>
        ", and by extension "guerrilla", see
        <a href="/wiki/Maquis_(World_War_II)" title="Maquis (World War II)">
         Maquis (World War II)
        </a>
        . It is not known exactly how this word came to mean street-side restaurant in Ivory Coast.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         un bra-môgô
        </i>
        is a slang word equivalent to "bloke" or "dude" in English. It is a loanword from the Mandinka language.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         chicotter
        </i>
        is a word meaning to whip, to beat, or to chastise (children). It is a loanword from
        <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
         Brazilian Portuguese
        </a>
        where it meant "to whip (the black slaves)". It has now entered the formal language of the educated classes.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         le pia
        </i>
        is a slang word meaning money. It comes perhaps from the standard French word
        <i>
         pièce
        </i>
        ("coin") or
        <i>
         pierre
        </i>
        ("stone"), or perhaps
        <i>
         piastre
        </i>
        (dollar, buck).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When speaking in a formal context, or when meeting French speakers from outside Ivory Coast, Abidjan speakers would replace these local words with the French standard words
       <i>
        une fille
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        un restaurant
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        une cantine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        un copain
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        battre
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        l'argent
       </i>
       respectively. Note that some local words are used across several African countries. For example,
       <i>
        chicotter
       </i>
       is attested not only in Ivory Coast but also in
       <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">
        Senegal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mali" title="Mali">
        Mali
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">
        Niger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Burkina_Faso" title="Burkina Faso">
        Burkina Faso
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">
        Chad
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">
        Central African Republic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">
        Benin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">
        Togo
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">
        Democratic Republic of the Congo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abidjan_varieties_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abidjan_varieties-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As already mentioned, these local words range from slang to formal usage, and their use therefore varies depending on the context. In Abidjan, this is how the sentence "The girl stole my money." is constructed depending on the
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        register
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Abidjan_varieties_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Abidjan_varieties-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        formal Abidjan French of the educated people:
        <i>
         La fille m'a subtilisé mon argent.
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        colloquial Abidjan French (
        <i>
         français de Moussa
        </i>
        <span style="margin-left:1px">
         )
        </span>
        :
        <i>
         Fille-là a prend mon l'argent.
        </i>
        (in standard French, the grammatically correct sentence should be "Cette fille (là) m'a pris l'argent.")
       </li>
       <li>
        Abidjan French slang (
        <i>
         nouchi
        </i>
        <span style="margin-left:3px">
         )
        </span>
        :
        <i>
         La go a momo mon pia.
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         momo
        </i>
        is an Abidjan slang word meaning "to steal")
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       With more than 8 million inhabitants,
       <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">
        Kinshasa
       </a>
       is the second largest francophone city in the world after
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       . It is the capital of the most populous francophone country in the world, the
       <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">
        Democratic Republic of the Congo
       </a>
       , where an estimated 24 million people (40% of the total population) can speak French (essentially as a second language).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007_report_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2007_report-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Contrary to Abidjan where French is the first language of a large part of the population, in Kinshasa French is only a second language, and its status of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
         lingua franca
        </a>
       </i>
       is shared with
       <a href="/wiki/Lingala" title="Lingala">
        Lingala
       </a>
       . Kinshasa French also differs from other African French variants, for it has some
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        Belgian French
       </a>
       influences, due to colonisation. People of different African mother tongues living in Kinshasa usually speak Lingala to communicate with each other in the street, but French is the language of businesses, administrations, schools, newspapers and televisions. French is also the predominant written language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to its widespread presence in Kinshasa, French has become a local language with its own pronunciation and some local words borrowed for the most part from Lingala. Depending on their social status, some people may mix French and Lingala, or
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code switch
       </a>
       between the two depending on the context. Here are examples of words particular to Kinshasa French. As in Abidjan, there exist various
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        registers
       </a>
       and the most educated people may frown upon the use of slangish/lingala terms.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cadavéré
        </i>
        means broken, worn out, exhausted, or dead. It is the local pronunciation of the standard French word
        <i>
         cadavre
        </i>
        whose meaning in standard French is "corpse". The word
        <i>
         cadavéré
        </i>
        has now spread to other African countries due to the popularity of Congolese music in Africa.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         makasi
        </i>
        means strong, resistant. It is a loanword from Lingala.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         anti-nuit
        </i>
        are sunglasses worn by partiers at night. It is a word coined locally and whose literal meaning in standard French is "anti-night". It is one of the many Kinshasa slang words related to nightlife and partying. A reveler is known locally as
        <i>
         un ambianceur
        </i>
        , from standard French
        <i>
         ambiance
        </i>
        which means atmosphere.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         casser le bic
        </i>
        , literally "to break the
        <a href="/wiki/Bic_Cristal" title="Bic Cristal">
         Bic
        </a>
        ", means to stop going to school. "
        <a href="/wiki/Bic_Cristal" title="Bic Cristal">
         Bic
        </a>
        " is colloquially used to refer to a ballpoint pen in
        <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
         Belgian French
        </a>
        and Kinshasa French, but not in standard French.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         merci mingi
        </i>
        means "thank you very much". It comes from standard French
        <i>
         merci
        </i>
        ("thank you") and Lingala
        <i>
         mingi
        </i>
        ("a lot").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         un zibolateur
        </i>
        is a bottle opener. It comes from the Lingala verb
        <i>
         kozibola
        </i>
        which means "to open something that is blocked up or bottled", to which was added the standard French ending
        <i>
         -ateur
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         un tétanos
        </i>
        is a rickety old taxi. In standard French
        <i>
         tétanos
        </i>
        means "
        <a href="/wiki/Tetanus" title="Tetanus">
         tetanus
        </a>
        ".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         moyen tê vraiment
        </i>
        means "absolutely impossible". It comes from
        <i>
         moyen tê
        </i>
        ("there's no way"), itself made up of standard French
        <i>
         moyen
        </i>
        ("way") and Lingala
        <i>
         tê
        </i>
        ("not", "no"), to which was added standard French
        <i>
         vraiment
        </i>
        ("really").
       </li>
      </ul>
