      <p>
       <b>
        Mozarabic
       </b>
       , more accurately
       <b>
        Andalusi Romance
       </b>
       , was a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        continuum
       </a>
       of closely related
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance dialects
       </a>
       spoken in the Muslim-controlled areas of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       , known as
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       . Mozarabic descends from
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
        Late Latin
       </a>
       and early Romance dialects spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">
        Hispania
       </a>
       from the 5th to the 8th centuries and was spoken until the 13th century when it was displaced, mostly by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castilian_language" title="Castilian language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       (what became modern
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This set of Latin dialects came to be called the
       <i>
        Mozarabic language
       </i>
       by 19th-century Spanish scholars who studied medieval Al-Andalus, though there never was a common language standard. The term is inaccurate, because it refers to the Christians who spoke
       <b>
        Andalusi Romance
       </b>
       , as a part of the Romance dialectic linguistic
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        continuum
       </a>
       in the Iberian Peninsula, but it was also spoken by Jews, and Muslims, as large parts of the population converted to Islam. The word
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarab" title="Mozarab">
        Mozarab
       </a>
       is a loanword from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusi_Arabic" title="Andalusi Arabic">
        Andalusi Arabic
       </a>
       <i>
        musta'rab
       </i>
       ,
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        مُستَعرَب
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Classical Arabic
       </a>
       <i>
        musta'rib
       </i>
       , meaning "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabized" title="Arabized">
        who adopts the ways of the Arabs
       </a>
       ".
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Native_name">
        Native name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozarabic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Native name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The name
       <i>
        Mozarabic
       </i>
       is today used for many medieval Romance dialects, no longer spoken, such as those of
       <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The native name (autonym or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endonym" title="Endonym">
        endonym
       </a>
       ) of the language was not "Muzarab" or "Mozarab" but "Latina" (Latin). In Iberia, as in much of Western Europe, the various Romance languages including Mozarabic were for many centuries thought of simply as dialects of Latin and so their speakers referred to their languages as
       <i>
        Latin
       </i>
       , including the Mozarabs. They did not call themselves "Mozarabs" either.
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        At times between persecution, Christian communities prospered in Muslim Spain; these Christians are now usually referred to as
        <i>
         Mozárabes
        </i>
        , although the term was not in use at the time (Hitchcock 1978)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It was only in the 19th century that Spanish historians started to use the words "Mozarabs" and "Mozarabic" to refer to those Christian people and their language who lived under Muslim rule in the Iberian Peninsula in the Middle Ages. Another very common Arab
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exonym" title="Exonym">
        exonym
       </a>
       for this language was
       <i>
        al-ajamiya
       </i>
       ("stranger/foreign") that had the meaning of Romance language in
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       . So the words "Mozarabic" or "ajamiya" are exonyms and not autonyms of the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Roger Wright, in his book about the evolution of early Romance languages in France and in the Iberian Peninsula
       <i>
        Late Latin and Early Romance in Spain and Carolingian France
       </i>
       , page 156, states:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The Early Romance of Moslem Spain was known to its users as
        <i>
         latinus
        </i>
        . This word can lead to confusion; the Visigothic scholars used it to contrast with Greek or Hebrew, and Simonet (1888: XXIII-IV, XXXV-VII) established that in Moslem Spain it was used to refer to the non-Arabic vernacular (as was Arabic
        <i>
         Al-Lathinī
        </i>
        )
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Also in the same book on page 158, the author states that:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The use of
        <i>
         latinus
        </i>
        to mean Latin-Romance, as opposed to Arabic, is also found north of the religious border
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This means that the word
       <i>
        Latinus
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Latino
       </i>
       had the meaning of spoken Romance language, and it was only contrasted with classical Latin (
       <i>
        lingua Latina
       </i>
       ) a few centuries later. Contemporary Romance speakers of the Iberian Peninsula of that time saw their vernacular spoken language as "Latin". This happened because classical Latin was seen as an educated speech, not as a different language. As Francisco Marcos-Marín (2015) has pointed out, following archaeological studies mainly by Juan Zozaya, Berber invaders could not have learnt to speak Arabic so soon. They used a continuum between Berber and Latin varieties. Latin was the cultural language of the Roman provinces of Africa before Arabic and continued in use (at least for some registers) until the 11th century. The interaction of these Afro-Romance varieties and Ibero-Romance has yet to be studied. Those African speakers also referred to their language as "Latine".
      </p>
      <p>
       Both
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
         Ladino
        </a>
       </i>
       , the name that Sephardic Jews gave to their spoken Romance language in Iberia, and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">
         Ladin
        </a>
       </i>
       , the name that an Alpine Romance speaking people, the Ladins, gave to their language, mean Latin.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Iberian Peninsula:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        The word
        <i>
         Ladino
        </i>
        (&lt; LATINUM) survived with the specific
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_meaning" title="Linguistic meaning">
         linguistic meaning
        </a>
        of "Spanish written by Jews" (Roger Wright 1982, p. 158)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This is one of the main reasons why Iberian Jews (
       <i>
        Sephardim
       </i>
       ) from central and southern regions called their everyday language
       <i>
        Ladino
       </i>
       - because this word had the sense of spoken Romance language (Ladino is today a Romance language more closely related to Spanish, mainly to Old Spanish, spoken by some Jews of Sephardic ancestry).
      </p>
      <p>
       For the same reason, speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">
        Ladin
       </a>
       , another Romance language (spoken in northern Italy in the Trentino Alto-Ádige/Südtirol and
       <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">
        Veneto
       </a>
       regions), call their own language
       <i>
        Ladin
       </i>
       i.e. "Latin".
      </p>
      <p>
       This word had the sense of spoken Romance language not only in Iberian Peninsula but also in other Romance language regions in early Middle Ages.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scripts">
        Scripts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozarabic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Scripts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because Mozarabic was not a language of high culture, it had no official script. Unlike most Romance languages, Mozarabic was primarily written in the
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">
        Arabic
       </a>
       rather than the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin
       </a>
       script, though it was also written in Latin and to a lesser extent in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">
        Hebrew alphabet
       </a>
       . Mozarab scholars wrote words of the Romance vernacular in alternative scripts in the margins or in the subtitles of Latin-language texts (glosses).
      </p>
      <p>
       The two languages of culture in Medieval Iberia were
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       in the north (although it was also used in the south by Mozarab scholars) and
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       in the south (which was the principal literary language of Mozarab scholars). These are the languages that constitute the great majority of written documents of the Peninsula at that time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mozarabic is first documented in writing in the Peninsula as choruses (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kharja" title="Kharja">
         kharjas
        </a>
       </i>
       ) (11th century) in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       lyrics called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Muwashshah" title="Muwashshah">
         muwashshahs
        </a>
       </i>
       . As these were written in the
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_script" title="Arabic script">
        Arabic script
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       had to be reconstructed when transliterating it into Latin script.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_phonetics">
        Morphology and phonetics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozarabic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology and phonetics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The phonology of Mozarabic is more archaic than the other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       in Spain, fitting with the general idea that language varieties in more isolated or peripheral areas act as "islands of conservatism". Based on the written documents that are identified as Mozarabic, some examples of these more archaic features are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The preservation of the Latin consonant clusters
        <i>
         cl
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         fl
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         pl
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The lack of
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         lenition
        </a>
        of intervocalic
        <i>
         p
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         t
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         c (k)
        </i>
        , as in the Mozarabic words
        <i>
         <span lang="mxi" xml:lang="mxi">
          lopa
         </span>
        </i>
        (she-wolf),
        <i>
         <span lang="mxi" xml:lang="mxi">
          toto
         </span>
        </i>
        (all) and
        <i>
         <span lang="mxi" xml:lang="mxi">
          formica
         </span>
        </i>
        (ant).
       </li>
       <li>
        The representation of Latin /kt/ as /ht/ (as in /nohte/ "night" &lt;
        <i>
         <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
          noctem
         </span>
        </i>
        ), thought
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
           <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2014)">
            by whom?
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        to have been an intermediate stage in the transition /kt/ &gt; /jt/, but represented nowhere else (
        <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">
         Galician-Portuguese
        </a>
        finished the transition, as shown by
        <i>
         <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
          noite
         </span>
        </i>
        "night").
       </li>
       <li>
        The preservation of palatalized /k(e)/, /k(i)/ as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        (as in
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        ), rather than
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ts/
        </span>
        as elsewhere in
        <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
         Western Romance languages
        </a>
        (except
        <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
         Picard
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The preservation (at least in some areas) of original /au/, /ai/.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The morphology of some words is closer to Latin than other Iberian Romance or Romance languages in general. This Romance variety had a significant impact in the formation of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , especially
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       , which explains why this language has numerous words of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Arabic" title="Andalusian Arabic">
        Andalusian Arabic
       </a>
       origin.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was spoken by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarab" title="Mozarab">
        Mozarabs
       </a>
       (Christians living as
       <a href="/wiki/Dhimmi" title="Dhimmi">
        dhimmis
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Muladi" title="Muladi">
        Muladis
       </a>
       (the native Iberian population converted to Islam) and some layers of the ruling Arabs and Berbers. The cultural language of Mozarabs continued to be
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       , but as time passed, young Mozarabs studied and even excelled in Arabic. Due to the northward migration of Mozarabs, Arabic placenames occur in areas where Islamic rule did not last long. With the deepening of Islamization and the advance of the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       , Mozarabic was substituted either by Arabic or by Northern Romance varieties, depending on the area and century.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Documents_in_Andalusi_Romance_.28Old_Southern_Iberian_Romance.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Documents_in_Andalusi_Romance_(Old_Southern_Iberian_Romance)">
        Documents in Andalusi Romance (Old Southern Iberian Romance)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mozarabic_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Documents in Andalusi Romance (Old Southern Iberian Romance)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some texts found in manuscripts of poetry in Muslim Iberian Peninsula (
       <i>
        Al-Andalus
       </i>
       ), although mainly written in Arabic, have however some stanzas in Andalusi Romance (
       <i>
        Latino
       </i>
       ) or in what seems to be Andalusi Romance. These are important texts because there are few examples of written Andalusi Romance. Proper Mozarabic (i.e. Christian) texts were written in Latin and are available in the accurate edition by Juan Gil.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        Late Latin and Early Romance
       </i>
       Roger Wright also makes an analysis of these poetry texts known as
       <i>
        kharjas
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        Muslim Spain has acquired philological interest for a further reason: the
        <i>
         kharjas
        </i>
        . These are apparently bilingual (Arabic-Romance) or macaronic final stanzas of some verses in the Hispano-Arabic
        <i>
         muwashshaha
        </i>
        form discovered in some Arabic and Hebrew manuscripts (...). Analyses of these have been hampered in the past by the belief that we know too little about
        <i>
         mozárabe
        </i>
        Romance to discuss the "Romance" element on a sound basis; but this is not entirely true. (...) The detailed investigations by Galmés de Fuentes (e.g. 1977, 1980) on later documents and toponyms have established the main features of
        <i>
         mozárabe
        </i>
        phonology, and many features of its morphology (...). The conclusion seems to be that
        <i>
         mozárabe
        </i>
        Romance is not particularly different from that of other parts of Iberia.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       However, a better knowledge of Andalusi Arabic, particularly due to the work of Federico Corriente, placed the
       <i>
        xarajat
       </i>
       (a term which should be preferred to
       <i>
        kharjas
       </i>
       ) in the framework of Arabic Literature, and the linguistic diglossic situation of al-Andalus. Most
       <i>
        xarajat
       </i>
       , actually, were written in Andalusi Arabic. Those with Andalusi Romance elements usually combine them with Andalusi Arabic forms.
      </p>
