      <p>
       <b>
        Canadian French
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         français canadien
        </span>
       </i>
       ) refers to a variety of dialects of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French language
       </a>
       generally spoken in Canada. In 2011, the total number of native French speakers in Canada was around 7.3 million (22% or the entire population), while another 2 million spoke it as a
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       . At
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">
        federal
       </a>
       level, it has official status alongside
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">
        English
       </a>
       . At
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Provinces" title="Canadian Provinces">
        provincial
       </a>
       level of government, French is the sole official language of
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       as well as one of two official languages of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">
        New Brunswick
       </a>
       , and jointly official (derived from its federal legal status) in
       <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">
        Nunavut
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">
        Yukon
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">
        Northwest Territories
       </a>
       . Government services are offered in French at select localities in
       <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
        Manitoba
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
        Ontario
       </a>
       (through the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Language_Services_Act" title="French Language Services Act">
        French Language Services Act
       </a>
       ), and to a lesser extent elsewhere in the country, depending largely on the proximity to
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       and/or Quebecois influence on any given region.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/New_England_French" title="New England French">
        New England French
       </a>
       , a dialect spoken in the northern
       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
        New England
       </a>
       is essentially a variety of Canadian French and exhibits no particular differences from the Canadian dialects, unlike
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_French" title="Louisiana French">
        Louisiana French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole" title="Louisiana Creole">
        Louisiana Creole
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ammon_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ammon-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
        Varieties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Varieties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
         Quebec French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       . Closely related varieties are spoken by francophone communities in
       <a href="/wiki/Ontario" title="Ontario">
        Ontario
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Canada" title="Western Canada">
        Western Canada
       </a>
       and in the
       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
        New England
       </a>
       region of the United States and differ from Quebec French primarily by their greater conservatism. The term
       <i>
        Laurentian French
       </i>
       has limited application as a collective label for all these varieties, and
       <i>
        Quebec French
       </i>
       has also been used for the entire dialect group. The overwhelming majority of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophone
       </a>
       Canadians speak this dialect.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">
         Acadian French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken by over 350,000
       <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">
        Acadians
       </a>
       in parts of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Provinces" title="Maritime Provinces">
        Maritime Provinces
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
        Newfoundland
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_Islands" title="Magdalen Islands">
        Magdalen Islands
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te-Nord" title="Côte-Nord">
        Lower North Shore
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gasp%C3%A9_peninsula" title="Gaspé peninsula">
        Gaspé peninsula
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_French" title="Métis French">
         Métis French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Manitoba" title="Manitoba">
        Manitoba
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Canada" title="Western Canada">
        Western Canada
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9tis_in_Canada" title="Métis in Canada">
        Métis
       </a>
       , descendants of
       <a href="/wiki/First_Nations" title="First Nations">
        First Nations
       </a>
       mothers and
       <a href="/wiki/Voyageurs" title="Voyageurs">
        voyageur
       </a>
       fathers during the
       <a href="/wiki/Fur_trade" title="Fur trade">
        fur trade
       </a>
       . Many Métis spoke
       <a href="/wiki/Cree_language" title="Cree language">
        Cree
       </a>
       in addition to French, and over the years they developed a unique mixed language called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michif_language" title="Michif language">
        Michif
       </a>
       by combining Métis French nouns, numerals, articles and adjectives with Cree verbs, demonstratives, postpositions, interrogatives and pronouns. Both the Michif language and the Métis dialect of French are
       <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">
        severely endangered
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_French" title="Newfoundland French">
         Newfoundland French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken by a small population on the
       <a href="/wiki/Port_au_Port_Peninsula" title="Port au Port Peninsula">
        Port-au-Port Peninsula
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador" title="Newfoundland and Labrador">
        Newfoundland
       </a>
       . It is endangered — both Quebec French and Acadian French are now more widely spoken among Newfoundland francophones than the distinctive peninsular dialect.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Brayon" title="Brayon">
         Brayon French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Beauce,_Quebec" title="Beauce, Quebec">
        Beauce
       </a>
       of Quebec; Edmundston, New Brunswick; and Madawaska, Maine. Although superficially a phonological descendant of Acadian French, analysis reveals it is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic" title="Morphosyntactic">
        morphosyntactically
       </a>
       identical to Quebec French.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is believed to have resulted from a localized levelling of contact dialects between Québécois and Acadian settlers.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_England_French" title="New England French">
         New England French
        </a>
       </i>
       is spoken in parts of
       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
        New England
       </a>
       in the United States. Essentially a local variant of Quebec French, it is one of three major forms of French that developed in what is now the U.S., the others being
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_French" title="Louisiana French">
        Louisiana French
       </a>
       and the nearly-extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri_French" title="Missouri French">
        Missouri French
       </a>
       . It is endangered, though its use is supported by bilingual education programs in place since 1987.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ammon_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ammon-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sub-varieties">
        Sub-varieties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sub-varieties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are two main sub-varieties of Canadian French.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Joual" title="Joual">
         Joual
        </a>
       </i>
       is an informal variety of French spoken in working-class neighbourhoods in the province of Quebec.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chiac" title="Chiac">
         Chiac
        </a>
       </i>
       is a blending of Acadian French syntax and vocabulary with numerous lexical borrowings from English.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_usage">
        Historical usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canadian_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        Canadian French
       </i>
       was formerly used to refer specifically to Quebec French and the closely related varieties of Ontario and Western Canada descended from it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is presumably because Canada and Acadia were distinct parts of New France, and also of British North America, until 1867. However, today the term
       <i>
        Canadian French
       </i>
       is not usually deemed to exclude Acadian French.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Phylogenetics" title="Phylogenetics">
        Phylogenetically
       </a>
       , Quebec French, Métis French and Brayon French are representatives of
       <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">
        koiné
       </a>
       French in the Americas whereas Acadian French, Cajun French, and Newfoundland French are derivatives of non-koinesized local dialects in France.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
