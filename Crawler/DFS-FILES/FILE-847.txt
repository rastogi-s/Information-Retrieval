      <p>
       <b>
        Dotdash
       </b>
       (formerly
       <b>
        About.com
       </b>
       ) is an American
       <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_market#Internet-based_knowledge_markets" title="Knowledge market">
        Internet-based network
       </a>
       of content that publishes articles and videos about various subjects on its "topic sites", of which there are nearly 1,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is operated by About, Inc. (formerly The Mining Company, MiningCo.com, Inc., and About.com, Inc.). The website competes with other online resource sites and encyclopedias. By March 2014, 61,428,000 unique visitors were registered by
       <a href="/wiki/ComScore" title="ComScore">
        comScore
       </a>
       for About.com, making it the 16th-most-visited online property during that month.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of August 2012, Dotdash is the property of
       <a href="/wiki/IAC_(company)" title="IAC (company)">
        IAC
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       owner of
       <a href="/wiki/Ask.com" title="Ask.com">
        Ask.com
       </a>
       and numerous other online brands, and its revenue is generated by advertising.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotdash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="1997.E2.80.932005:_launch.2C_renaming.2C_Primedia_acquisition">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1997–2005:_launch,_renaming,_Primedia_acquisition">
        1997–2005: launch, renaming, Primedia acquisition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotdash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 1997–2005: launch, renaming, Primedia acquisition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Founded in 1996 as The Mining Company, the site was launched on April 21, 1997, by
       <a href="/wiki/Scott_Kurnit" title="Scott Kurnit">
        Scott Kurnit
       </a>
       , owner of General Internet, Bill Day, and a group of other entrepreneurs in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The original goal was to maintain 1,800 topic areas, but after five years of operation, this number was reduced to around 700.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prim_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Prim-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The company changed its name to "About Inc.", and the website address from "miningco.com" to "about.com" in May 1999.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company was acquired by Primedia, Inc. in 2000 through a deal valued at US$690 million, whereby Primedia swapped 45.2 million shares for About, Inc.'s 18.1 million shares. At the time of the acquisition announcement, About Inc. was measured at US$133 million in cash and no debt (Sept. 30, 2000), while the Media Metrix company tallied 21 million unique monthly visitors—making it the seventh-most-visited "network of sites" at the time—a network of over 700 topic sites, sorted into 36 areas and 50,000 subjects, and approximately 4,000 advertisers. Following the purchase, which was finalized in the first quarter of 2001, the combined company was called "Primedia" and Kurnit remained
       <a href="/wiki/Chief_executive_officer" title="Chief executive officer">
        chief executive officer
       </a>
       (CEO).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Prim_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Prim-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="2005.E2.80.932012:_Times_Co._acquisition.2C_Abang.com.2C_About_en_Espa.C3.B1ol">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2005–2012:_Times_Co._acquisition,_Abang.com,_About_en_Español">
        2005–2012: Times Co. acquisition, Abang.com, About en Español
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotdash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 2005–2012: Times Co. acquisition, Abang.com, About en Español">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In February 2005,
       <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Company" title="The New York Times Company">
        The New York Times Company
       </a>
       (Times Co.) announced it was buying About.com,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allthingsd-about_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-allthingsd-about-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT20050218_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT20050218-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a purchase that was completed in the first half of the year for US$410 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">
        Google
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Yahoo!" title="Yahoo!">
        Yahoo
       </a>
       were reportedly among the other bidders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Following the Times Co. acquisition, Peter C. Horan was appointed as About Inc.'s president and CEO, but he was soon replaced by Scott Meyer in May 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In March 2007, About.com's patronage was measured at 33.5 million unique visitors. On May 7, 2007, About Inc. acquired ConsumerSearch.com—a site that generated 3 million unique monthly visitors during the first quarter of 2007—for US$33 million in cash following two other purchases that were made in the preceding eight-month period: UCompareHealthCare.com and Calorie-Count.com.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Initially conceived of in January 2007, About.com's first fully owned foreign venture, the China-based Abang.com, debuted in December 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the launch, the company had a Japan-based online entity, Allabout.co.jp, but it functioned under a licensing agreement. By January 2008, the China site consisted of around 25 employees, as well as 80 guides who were responsible for article production within seven categories: Fashion, Food, Health, Hobbies, Pets, Digital, and Travel. As part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_localization" title="Language localization">
        localization
       </a>
       process, the China initiative—led by Matt Roberts, who became the CEO of Abang.com, and Wen-Wei Wang, the vice president of technology for the launch—was named "Abang" because the Chinese character "bang" refers to the concept of a group or community.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The About Group generated US$102.7 million in 2007, which represented a 135-percent increase from the time of the Times Co. acquisition. Meyer stepped down from the chief executive role in March 2008 and was replaced by Cella Irvine, who previously worked for Hearst Corp. and Microsoft Corp.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In April 2011, Irvine launched the About en Español website, which was About's first-ever Spanish-language channel and initially featured nine topics, including movies and makeup, that were to be expanded by around 100 by the start of 2012. The launch was part of an overall strategy that included a redesigned About.com homepage, a doubling of the number of "how-to" and
       <a href="/wiki/Do_it_yourself" title="Do it yourself">
        do-it-yourself
       </a>
       videos on About.com's 24 channels, and new outlets on About.com for advertisers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The significant impact of the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Recession" title="Great Recession">
        global financial crisis
       </a>
       upon online advertising was experienced shortly after Irvine's appointment and, despite her focus on video content and
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">
        Hispanic
       </a>
       consumers, she was removed from the CEO role after three years and three consecutive quarters in which revenue decreased. Martin Nisenholtz, SVP of digital operations, temporarily replaced Irvine following her departure in May 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In July 2011, Darline Jean was named CEO of the About Group, after the company's second-quarter revenues totaled US$27.8 million. Jean previously served as About's
       <a href="/wiki/Chief_financial_officer" title="Chief financial officer">
        chief financial officer
       </a>
       (CFO) and her new appointment became effective on September 1, 2011.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="2012.E2.80.932017:_IAC_acquisition.2C_Neil_Vogel_appointment.2C_relaunch">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2012–2017:_IAC_acquisition,_Neil_Vogel_appointment,_relaunch">
        2012–2017: IAC acquisition, Neil Vogel appointment, relaunch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotdash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 2012–2017: IAC acquisition, Neil Vogel appointment, relaunch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A media report published in August 2012 indicated that
       <a href="/wiki/Answers.com" title="Answers.com">
        Answers.com
       </a>
       had reached a preliminary agreement to acquire About.com for US$270 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nyt-aboutsale_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nyt-aboutsale-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, on August 26,
       <a href="/wiki/Barry_Diller" title="Barry Diller">
        Barry Diller
       </a>
       's IAC announced that it would acquire About.com instead for US$300 million in cash.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A source for the
       <i>
        TechCrunch
       </i>
       publication later confirmed that Answers.com's offer was reportedly valued at US$270 million, but consisted of debt and equity in Answers.com.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tech_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tech-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the corresponding press release, IAC explained that the acquisition will help bolster and accompany its existing properties, such as the Ask.com
       <a href="/wiki/Web_search_engine" title="Web search engine">
        web search engine
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allthingsd-about_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-allthingsd-about-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jean fulfilled her role as chief executive during the transition period, while ownership was transferred to IAC, and then left About shortly after the sale was finalized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the IAC acquisition, which was signed on August 26, 2012, About.com consisted of nearly 1,000 topic sites and over three million unique articles, while, in traffic terms, Alexa ranked the site as number 37 in the U.S. and 80 in the global context.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tech_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tech-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On April 2, 2013, Neil Vogel became the new CEO of About.com. Up until March 2003, Vogel was a key executive member of the marketing and media company Alloy Inc., a role that he left to cofound the Recognition Media marketing business that is responsible for producing the annual
       <a href="/wiki/Webby_Award" title="Webby Award">
        Webby Award
       </a>
       event, as well as the Internet Week NY and Europe events.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vogel was selected by
       <i>
        Business Insider
       </i>
       for its 2012 "The Coolest People In New York Tech This Year" list, for his work as a Venture Partner of FirstMark Capital.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of May 2013, About.com was receiving about 84 million unique monthly visitors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-May_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-May-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By mid-July 2013, further executive appointments were made: Scott Kim was transferred from Ask.com to become About's
       <a href="/wiki/Chief_operating_officer" title="Chief operating officer">
        chief operating officer
       </a>
       (COO), Alex Ellerson, formerly of Google and Yahoo!, was appointed as
       <a href="/wiki/Vice_president#Officer_levels_in_business_administration" title="Vice president">
        senior vice president
       </a>
       (SVP) of Content; and Matthew Knell became
       <a href="/wiki/Vice_president" title="Vice president">
        vice president
       </a>
       (VP) of social media and community strategy after leaving
       <a href="/wiki/AOL" title="AOL">
        Aol.
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-July-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following his appointment, Vogel's overall plan for About focused on social, mobile, and user experience, with an emphasis on increasing the amount of time that users remain on the site, rather than attracting people in the first instance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-May_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-May-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vogel also stressed the importance of monetization in his numerous post-appointment interviews and he included a summary of About's revenue model as part of his discussion of the future with
       <i>
        Bloomberg
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        [We make money] one way, in general: we're advertising-supported. And that's monetized two ways: we do a lot of display advertising, and we do a lot of people clicking on links that we get paid for from Google and from others ... If we have all this traffic, and we have all this content, if we can make people engage content more, and spend more time here [About.com], and do more things, we're going to have more page views and much more of an opportunity to monetize About.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tv_33-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Tv-33">
          [33]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Vogel further explained that IAC has been able to create "compelling" content that manages to successfully engage Internet users, while About's content, in particular, is favored by the Google search engine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tv_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tv-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, Vogel stated that
       <a href="/wiki/Native_advertising" title="Native advertising">
        native advertising
       </a>
       is a marketing initiative that he would like to explore with caution, as "it’s a really big opportunity to let marketers talk to our audience in the voice that they’re already comfortable with."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-July_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-July-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In April 2014, Vogel revealed to the media that the About.com team had grown from 100 employees to 176, and the number of site contributors had increased by 20 percent. In terms of the website, the company's developers updated the backend technology so that it is capable of handling a greater degree of interactivity at the front end, while a major emphasis was placed on responsive web design, as the traffic from mobile devices and tablets was measured at nearly 40 percent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brand-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to About's online media kit,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint noexcerpt Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS" title="Wikipedia:NOTRS">
          <span title="This claim needs references to better sources. (April 2017)">
           better source needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       nearly 1,000 "Experts" (freelance writers) contribute to the site by writing on various topics, including healthcare and travel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The About.com website was relaunched in September 2014, following a significant redesign that expanded upon the new homepage that debuted in July 2014. Based on an analysis of the needs of both users and advertisers, the redesign sought to create an improved user experience and facilitate social sharing, including the addition of social media buttons that were not featured at the time of Vogel's appointment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_34-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brand-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company also plans to use a newly created video-production studio to deliver a larger volume of corresponding content in 2015. About's
       <a href="/wiki/Chief_revenue_officer" title="Chief revenue officer">
        chief revenue officer
       </a>
       (CRO) Brian Colbert explained to the media in September 2014: "We are judging the effectiveness and success of the redesign through engagement. We want people to visit more often, stick around longer and do more on the site."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="2017.E2.80.93present:_closure_and_rebranding">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2017–present:_closure_and_rebranding">
        2017–present: closure and rebranding
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dotdash&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 2017–present: closure and rebranding">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On May 2, 2017, IAC announced that they had renamed About.com to Dotdash,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after about a year of transition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dotdash-announced_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dotdash-announced-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       CEO Neil Vogel said that the company had lost
       <a href="/wiki/Mind_share" title="Mind share">
        mind share
       </a>
       , and needed to change their marketing strategy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-shut_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-verge-shut-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company elected to refocus on
       <a href="/wiki/Vertical_market" title="Vertical market">
        vertical markets
       </a>
       through its niche websites:
       <b>
        The Balance
       </b>
       (personal finance),
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Lifewire" title="Lifewire">
         Lifewire
        </a>
       </b>
       (consumer electronics),
       <b>
        The Spruce
       </b>
       (home and food),
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Verywell" title="Verywell">
         VeryWell
        </a>
       </b>
       (health),
       <b>
        TripSavvy
       </b>
       (travel), and
       <b>
        ThoughtCo
       </b>
       (education).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dotdash-announced_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-dotdash-announced-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-verge-shut_39-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-verge-shut-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
