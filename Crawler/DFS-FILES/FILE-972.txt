      <p>
       <b>
        Romagnol
       </b>
       (also known as
       <i>
        Rumagnol
       </i>
       ) is a group of closely related dialects of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol_language" title="Emilian-Romagnol language">
        Emilian-Romagnol language
       </a>
       spoken in the historical region of
       <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">
        Romagna
       </a>
       , which is today in the western part of
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       . The name itself is derived from the Lombard name for the region
       <i>
        Romania
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also spoken outside the province, particularly in the neighboring province of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pesaro_and_Urbino" title="Province of Pesaro and Urbino">
        Pesaro-Urbino
       </a>
       (part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">
        Le Marche
       </a>
       region) and in the independent country of
       <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">
        San Marino
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is classified as a threatened language, due to older generations having “neglected to pass on the dialect as a native tongue to the next generation”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While contemporaneous with modern Standard Italian, it is technically a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic branch
       </a>
       and more comparable to the “northern group” of Italian dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:5-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This includes the dialects
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian_dialect" title="Emilian dialect">
        Emilian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       . It is sometimes considered a subdialect of a larger Emilian-Romagnol language, which encompasses a broad continuum of dialects spanning the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Western_border">
        Western border
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Western border">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       West of
       <a href="/wiki/Romagna" title="Romagna">
        Romagna
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian language
       </a>
       is spoken. The border with Emilian-speaking areas is the Sillaro river, which runs 25 km east from
       <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">
        Bologna
       </a>
       to the west of (
       <a href="/wiki/Castel_San_Pietro_Terme" title="Castel San Pietro Terme">
        Castel San Pietro Terme
       </a>
       ). Emilian is spoken, to the east, in
       <a href="/wiki/Imola" title="Imola">
        Imola
       </a>
       , the language is Romagnol. In
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       , Emilian is spoken in all the rest of the region moving from the Sillaro river to the west, up to
       <a href="/wiki/Piacenza" title="Piacenza">
        Piacenza
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_border">
        Northern border
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Northern border">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reno_River" title="Reno River">
        Reno River
       </a>
       is the border between Romagnol and the dialect of Ferrara. Romagnol is spoken also in some villages northwards of the Reno River, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Argenta,_Emilia%E2%80%93Romagna" title="Argenta, Emilia–Romagna">
        Argenta, Emilia–Romagna
       </a>
       and Filo, where people of Romagnol origin live alongside people of Ferrarese origin.
       <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">
        Ferrara
       </a>
       goes into Emilian language territory.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_border">
        Southern border
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Southern border">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Outside Emilia-Romagna, Romagnol is spoken in the Republic of
       <a href="/wiki/San_Marino" title="San Marino">
        San Marino
       </a>
       ("Sammarinese"), in the Marecchia Valley, in the Conca Valley (
       <a href="/wiki/Montefeltro" title="Montefeltro">
        Montefeltro
       </a>
       ) and in all of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pesaro_e_Urbino" title="Pesaro e Urbino">
        Pesaro e Urbino
       </a>
       province.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Romagnol's first acknowledgement outside regional literature was in Dante Alighieri’s treatise
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_vulgari_eloquentia" title="De vulgari eloquentia">
         De vulgari eloquentia
        </a>
       </i>
       , wherein
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante
       </a>
       compares “the language of Romagna” to his native
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan dialect
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eventually, in 1629, the author
       <a href="/wiki/Adriano_Banchieri" title="Adriano Banchieri">
        Adriano Banchieri
       </a>
       wrote the treatise
       <i>
        Discorso della lingua Bolognese
       </i>
       , which countered Dante’s claim that the Tuscan dialect was better, arguing his belief that Bolognese (a subdialect of Romagnol that saw wide use in writing) was superior in “naturalness, softness, musicality, and usefulness.” Romagnol received more recognition after Romagna gained independence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Papal_States" title="Papal States">
        Papal States
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is also a large repertoire of folklore legends, myths, and fables in Romagnol, due to its role in local geopolitical history (e.g. Caesar crossing the
       <a href="/wiki/Rubicon" title="Rubicon">
        Rubicon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Theoderic_the_Great" title="Theoderic the Great">
        Theodoric’s
       </a>
       conquest and subsequent rule of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ostrogothic_Kingdom" title="Ostrogothic Kingdom">
        Ostrogothic Kingdom
       </a>
       ). Romagna’s geographic diversity was home to a variety of lifestyles and trade backgrounds, such as “the mountaineers of the Alps, the fisherman of the Adriatic, the farmers of the plains, and the city folk,” which in turn, allowed for a large range of topics and themes present in the literature. Darker themes, such as poverty and pessimism, are also known to be common subjects of Romagnol poetry, fables, and prose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="16th_to_19th_century">
        16th to 19th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 16th to 19th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The first appearance of a distinct Romagnol literary work is "Sonetto romagnolo" by
       <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_Gatti" title="Bernardino Gatti">
        Bernardino Catti
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">
        Ravenna
       </a>
       , printed 1502. It is written in a mixture of
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and Romagnol
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Romagnol
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">
        poem
       </a>
       dates back to the end of 16th century:
       <i>
        E Pvlon matt. Cantlena aroica
       </i>
       (Mad Nap), a
       <a href="/wiki/Mock-heroic" title="Mock-heroic">
        mock-heroic
       </a>
       poem based on
       <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Furioso" title="Orlando Furioso">
        Orlando Furioso
       </a>
       and written by an anonymous author from
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=San_Vittore_di_Cesena&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="San Vittore di Cesena (page does not exist)">
        San Vittore di Cesena
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Vittore_(Cesena)" title="it:San Vittore (Cesena)">
         it
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       . The original poem comprised twelve cantos, of which only the first four survived (1848 lines).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Romagnol poet to win fame was the
       <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">
        cleric
       </a>
       Pietro Santoni, (
       <a href="/wiki/Fusignano" title="Fusignano">
        Fusignano
       </a>
       , 1736–1823). He was the teacher of
       <a href="/wiki/Vincenzo_Monti" title="Vincenzo Monti">
        Vincenzo Monti
       </a>
       , one of the most famous Italian poets of his time.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1840 the first Romagnol-Italian Dictionary was published by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Antonio_Morri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Antonio Morri (page does not exist)">
        Antonio Morri
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Morri" title="it:Antonio Morri">
         it
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       , printed in
       <a href="/wiki/Faenza" title="Faenza">
        Faenza
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">
        20th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 20th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The 20th century saw a flourishing of Romagnol literature. Theatrical plays, poems and books of a high quality were produced. Some of the best known Romagnol authors are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Raffaello_Baldini" title="Raffaello Baldini">
         Raffaello Baldini
        </a>
        , who won in 1988 the "Premio Viareggio" and in 1995 the "Premio Bagutta," known for long pessimistic poems and prose
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-:3-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Tonino_Guerra" title="Tonino Guerra">
         Tonino Guerra
        </a>
        (1920–2012), wrote poems during his exile to WWII-era Germany, focusing on people of suffering and poverty
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-:3-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Olindo_Guerrini" title="Olindo Guerrini">
         Olindo Guerrini
        </a>
        , with "Sonetti romagnoli"
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aldo_Spallicci&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aldo Spallicci (page does not exist)">
         Aldo Spallicci
        </a>
        <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
         (
         <a class="extiw" href="https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldo_Spallicci" title="it:Aldo Spallicci">
          it
         </a>
         )
        </span>
        , an antifascist exiled from Romagna. He wrote poems such as "Rumâgna" that were often descriptive of Romagna
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_9-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-:3-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Morphology">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h3>
      <p>

        ablaut
       </a>
       . In the case of feminine nouns and adjectives, "a" becomes "i" or deletes if after a consonant cluster or double consonant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:5-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">
        Syntax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Italian and Romagnol share much of the same features when it comes to verbs. Both languages are SVO in simple sentences. Verbs are conjugated according to
       <a href="/wiki/Tense%E2%80%93aspect%E2%80%93mood" title="Tense–aspect–mood">
        tense, mood, and person
       </a>
       . Romagnol also has 4 conjugations compared to Italian's 3: the 1st, êr; the 2nd, -ér; the 3rd, -ar; and the 4th, -ìr. One marked difference in syntax between Romagnol and Italian is that pronouns are obligatory, and some verbs in Romagnol use a
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_verb" title="Reflexive verb">
        reflexive construction
       </a>
       (even if the speaker is not the second argument of the verb) where Italian uses an
       <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">
        intransitive construction
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Romagnol
        </th>
        <th>
         Italian
        </th>
        <th>
         English
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Mè a'm so lavê
        </td>
        <td>
         (Io) mi sono lavato
        </td>
        <td>
         I washed myself
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Mè a sò
        </td>
        <td>
         (Io) sono
        </td>
        <td>
         I am
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Mè a j'ò
        </td>
        <td>
         (Io) ho
        </td>
        <td>
         I have
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Verbs that are
       <a href="/wiki/Impersonal_verb" title="Impersonal verb">
        impersonal
       </a>
       (lacking a canonical subject) in Romagnol use "avèr," in contrast with Italian which uses "essere." Even though the subject is null, an expletive pronoun inserts itself in the specifier position, much like English's "it".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Italian:
        <b>
         è
        </b>
        piovuto,
        <i>
         It rained
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Romagnol:
        <b>
         l
        </b>
        'à piuvù,
        <i>
         It rained
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Additionally, whereas Standard Italian and other Northern dialects omit the definite article before “singular names and names of relatives,” Romagnol does not do so.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Romagnol has
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_similarity" title="Lexical similarity">
        lexical
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic" title="Syntactic">
        syntactic
       </a>
       uniformity throughout its area. However, its
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronunciation
       </a>
       changes as one goes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Po_Valley" title="Po Valley">
        Po Valley
       </a>
       to the hills.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syllable_structure">
        Syllable structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Romagnol_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Syllable structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some words that in Latin were
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trisyllabic" title="Trisyllabic">
        trisyllabic
       </a>
       or tetrasyllabic (where 'u' is without
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       ) are reduced in Romagnol to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monosyllabic" title="Monosyllabic">

      <p>
       Romagnol, in addition to its larger inventory of vowels, also has more consonants compared to standard Italian. Additionally, consonants bear the following differences compared to Standard Italian:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In central dialects, word-final n is deleted and the preceding vowel is nasalized
       </li>
       <li>
        Consonants are doubled in length after a closed vowel.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d͡ʒ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t͡ʃ/
        </span>
        can occur word-finally, and is usually distinguished by the lack or presence of an "h" in orthography
       </li>
       <li>
        In subdialects that have the dental fricatives
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ð/
        </span>
        , voicing is contrastive
       </li>
       <li>
        In younger speakers /ʂ/ and /ʐ/ are sometimes realized as /s/ and /z/, out of italian influence
       </li>
      </ul>
