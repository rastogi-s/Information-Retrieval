         Pachuco culture is associated with the
         <a href="/wiki/Zoot_suit" title="Zoot suit">
          zoot suit
         </a>
         and the idea of making flamboyant appearances in public
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Pachuco
       </b>
       refers to a particular old school
       <a href="/wiki/Subculture" title="Subculture">
        subculture
       </a>
       of Chicanos and Mexican-Americans associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Zoot_suit" title="Zoot suit">
        zoot suits
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Street_gang" title="Street gang">
        street gangs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nightlife" title="Nightlife">
        nightlife
       </a>
       , and flamboyant public behavior. The idea of the pachuco – a zoot-suited, well-dressed, street-connected flamboyant
       <a href="/wiki/Playboy_(lifestyle)" title="Playboy (lifestyle)">
        playboy
       </a>
       of Hispanic/Latino heritage – originated in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Paso" title="El Paso">
        El Paso
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ciudad_Ju%C3%A1rez" title="Ciudad Juárez">
        Ciudad Juárez
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       , had moved north, following the line of migration of Mexican railroad workers ("
       <a href="/wiki/Traquero" title="Traquero">
        traqueros
       </a>
       ") into
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       , where it developed further.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        pachuca
       </b>
       is the female counterpart, often idealized as a beautiful Chicana woman in extravagant evening dress or a female version of the zoot suit, out with a pachuco boyfriend for a night on the town. Pachucas broke
       <a href="/wiki/Taboo" title="Taboo">
        taboos
       </a>
       of their time by wearing men's-style pants sometimes and appearing in public often with their pachuco boyfriends; at the time, a "good woman" was considered to have her place in the home. They defied male/female stereotypes and roles in Mexican-American culture in much the same way
       <a href="/wiki/Flapper" title="Flapper">
        flappers
       </a>
       had in European-American culture in the 1920s.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pachuco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The origin of the term "pachuco" is uncertain, but one theory connects it to the city of El Paso, Texas, which was sometimes referred to as "Chuco Town" or "El Chuco." People migrating to El Paso would say, in Spanish, that they were going "
       <i>
        pa' El Chuco.
       </i>
       " These migrants became known as pachucos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name "Pachuco" is quite possibly derived from the name of the city of Pachuca, the capital of Mexico's Hidalgo state. There have long been migrants from Hidalgo state living in Texas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another theory says that the word derives from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pocho" title="Pocho">
         pocho
        </a>
       </i>
       , a derogatory term for a Mexican born in the United States who has lost touch with the
       <a href="/wiki/Culture_of_Mexico" title="Culture of Mexico">
        Mexican culture
       </a>
       . The word is also said to mean "punk" or "troublemaker". Yet another theory is put forth by author Laura L. Cummings who postulates a possible indigenous origin of the term. The founder of the "Pachuco" subculture was a Mexican comedian and film actor named
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_Vald%C3%A9s" title="German Valdés">
        German Valdés
       </a>
       better-known by his Artistic name "Tin-Tan". He introduced the Pachuco dress and slang throughout his Golden age-era films.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Connections have also been found between "Pachucos" and mixed civilians who lived near the Mexican–American border during the turn of the century, and between "Pachucos" and the poor soldiers who fought in the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">
        Mexican Revolution
       </a>
       in the armies of
       <a href="/wiki/Pancho_Villa" title="Pancho Villa">
        Pancho Villa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TinTanStatueDF.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1200" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/TinTanStatueDF.JPG/220px-TinTanStatueDF.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/TinTanStatueDF.JPG/330px-TinTanStatueDF.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/TinTanStatueDF.JPG/440px-TinTanStatueDF.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TinTanStatueDF.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Statue of the Mexican actor
         <a href="/wiki/Germ%C3%A1n_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Germán Valdés">
          Germán Valdés
         </a>
         better known as Tin Tan
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Pachucos called their slang
       <a href="/wiki/Cal%C3%B3_(Chicano)" title="Caló (Chicano)">
        Caló
       </a>
       (sometimes called "pachuquismo"), a unique
       <a href="/wiki/Argot" title="Argot">
        argot
       </a>
       that drew on the original Spanish Gypsy Caló, Mexican Spanish, the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexican_Spanish" title="New Mexican Spanish">
        New Mexican dialect of Spanish
       </a>
       , and American English, employing words and phrases creatively applied. To a large extent, Caló went mainstream and is one of the last surviving vestige of the Pachuco, often used in the lexicon of some urban
       <a href="/wiki/Latino" title="Latino">
        Latinos
       </a>
       in the United States to this day. The influence of Valdés is responsible for the assimilation of several Caló terms into Mexican slang.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Mexican
       <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Literature" title="Nobel Prize in Literature">
        Nobel laureate
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Octavio_Paz" title="Octavio Paz">
        Octavio Paz
       </a>
       writes in the essay, "The
       <i>
        Pachuco
       </i>
       and Other Extremes" that the Pachuco phenomenon paralleled the
       <a href="/wiki/Zazou" title="Zazou">
        zazou
       </a>
       subculture in
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       -era Paris in style of clothing, music favored (
       <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">
        jazz
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swing_(genre)" title="Swing (genre)">
        swing
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Jump_blues" title="Jump blues">
        jump blues
       </a>
       ), and attitudes. Although there was no known link between the two subcultures, they both are most certainly derivative localized blends of American pop culture in the United States.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pachuco culture in America was at its height during
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       . The Wartime Productions Board in 1942 thought it necessary to cut back on fabric consumption, so they enacted regulations on the amount of fabric used for suits. This enactment targeted Pachucos in particular because of the excess fabric used in their zoot suits. Pachucos boldly chose not to follow these regulations, demonstrating rebellious attitudes and pride in their culture. Pachucos continued to flaunt zoot suits, now attained through bootleg tailors. As a result, these flashy zoot suits were seen as unpatriotic by other Americans. This controversial series of events helped shape Pachuco culture, and zoot suits became a symbol of cultural pride among Mexican-Americans. It didn't all end well, however, as this also led to rising tension between Pachucos and other Americans, playing a part in the start of the 1944
       <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Suit_Riots" title="Zoot Suit Riots">
        Zoot Suit Riots
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The pachuco subculture declined in the 1960s, evolving into the
       <a href="/wiki/Chicano" title="Chicano">
        Chicano
       </a>
       style. This style preserved some of the pachuco slang while adding a strong political element characteristic of the late 1960s American life.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the early 1970s, a recession and the increasingly violent nature of gang life resulted in an abandonment of anything that suggested
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dandyism" title="Dandyism">
        dandyism
       </a>
       . Accordingly, Mexican-American gangs adopted a uniform of
       <a href="/wiki/T-shirt" title="T-shirt">
        T-shirts
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Khaki" title="Khaki">
        khakis
       </a>
       derived from prison uniforms, and the pachuco style died out. However, the zoot suit remains a popular choice of formal wear for urban and rural Latino youths in heavily ethnic Mexican neighborhoods. It is typically worn at a prom, in weddings, parties or in some cases, at informal Latino university commencement ceremonies.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Costa_Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pachuco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Costa Rica">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The term "
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pachuco#Spanish" title="wikt:pachuco">
          pachuco
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       " is used in
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       to define Costa Rican slang. It nevertheless differs from the Mexican slang. In Costa Rica the term "pachuco" refers to someone who has common habits and who is often very rude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Costa Rica the word pachuco refers to a person who has manners that are socially unacceptable and often uses shocking language when speaking. Pachuco is also a pejorative name given to certain colloquial words and expressions. Some consider pachuco and its pachuquísmos to be Costa Rica's second language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Style">
        Style
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pachuco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Style">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Pachuco style was a dominating trend among Mexican-American youth in the 1930s-40s. Pachucos became known for their distinguished look, dialogue, and actions. Pachucos dressed in recognizable
       <a href="/wiki/Zoot_suit" title="Zoot suit">
        Zoot suits
       </a>
       , and often styled their hair into ducktails. Things like decorative chains and tattoos were also sometimes part of the pachuco look. The unique speech of pachucos was a very important element of their defined style. Consisting of creative phrases and some English words,
       <a href="/wiki/Cal%C3%B3_(Chicano)" title="Caló (Chicano)">
        Caló (Chicano)
       </a>
       was a very popular form of speech among pachucos. Pachucos were seen as gangsters in the eyes of other Americans, because of the activities they participated in. They often carried concealed weapons and involved in criminal activities such as vandalism and gang related misdemeanor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="La_Pachuca">
        La Pachuca
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pachuco&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: La Pachuca">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The "Pachuca," the female counterpart of the Pachuco, had an aesthetic sensibility as strong as the male zoot suiter. The Pachuca's hairstyle tended to be a high "coif" or
       <a href="/wiki/Bouffant" title="Bouffant">
        bouffant
       </a>
       , with the hair put up in some way (a more pronounced version of the typical hair style of the time), sometimes using hair grease. Her makeup was heavy, particularly the lipstick. The preferred colors of clothing were black and gray. Some pachucas wore the traditionally male zoot suit, albeit with modifications to fit the female form. This was very subversive at the time because of long-held
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_role" title="Gender role">
        gender roles
       </a>
       that dictated how a person should dress. Sometimes, she donned the standard heavy gold pocket chain. Another variation involved a
       <a href="/wiki/Sweater" title="Sweater">
        sweater
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Coat_(clothing)" title="Coat (clothing)">
        coat
       </a>
       - often a variant on the male zoot-suit finger-tip jacket - over knee-length
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skirts" title="Skirts">
        skirts
       </a>
       , plus
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fishnet_stockings" title="Fishnet stockings">
        fishnet stockings
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bobby_socks" title="Bobby socks">
        bobby socks
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Platform_shoes" title="Platform shoes">
        platform shoes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Extravagant dresses were and are also common among pachucas. Like the entire culture, the fashion aesthetic revolved around the concept of "see and be seen".
      </p>
      <p>
       This style was associated with gang membership and activity largely due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Zoot_Suit_Riots" title="Zoot Suit Riots">
        Zoot Suit Riots
       </a>
       that took place in
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_California" title="Southern California">
        Southern California
       </a>
       in 1943. The negative image of the male zoot suiter as a "violent gangster" naturally extended to the Pachuca as well. The promiscuous image came from the Pachuca's high public visibility and patronage of
       <a href="/wiki/Nightlife" title="Nightlife">
        nightlife
       </a>
       with pachucos. This image contrasted with the traditional Latino-American image of
       <a href="/wiki/Femininity" title="Femininity">
        femininity
       </a>
       at the time which held that the "good"
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_group" title="Minority group">
        minority
       </a>
       woman stayed in the home.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Pachuca's challenge to the dominant perception of femininity came during the period between the advent of
       <a href="/wiki/Women%27s_suffrage" title="Women's suffrage">
        women's suffrage
       </a>
       in 1920 and the upsurge in
       <a href="/wiki/Feminism" title="Feminism">
        feminist
       </a>
       activism of the 1960s and 1970s.
      </p>
