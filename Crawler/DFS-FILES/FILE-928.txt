      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Burgundian language
       </b>
       , also known by
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       names
       <b>
        Bourguignon-morvandiau,
       </b>
       <b>
        Bourguignon,
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        Morvandiau,
       </b>
       is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_language" title="Oïl language">
        Oïl language
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundy_(region)" title="Burgundy (region)">
        Burgundy
       </a>
       and particularly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Morvan" title="Morvan">
        Morvan
       </a>
       area of the region.
      </p>
      <p>
       The arrival of the
       <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">
        Burgundians
       </a>
       brought
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgundian_language_(Germanic)" title="Burgundian language (Germanic)">
        Germanic
       </a>
       elements into the Gallo-Romance speech of the inhabitants. The occupation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">
        Low Countries
       </a>
       by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dukes_of_Burgundy" title="Dukes of Burgundy">
        Dukes of Burgundy
       </a>
       also brought Burgundian into contact with Dutch; e.g., the word for
       <a href="/wiki/Gingerbread" title="Gingerbread">
        gingerbread
       </a>
       <i>
        couque
       </i>
       derives from Old Dutch
       <i>
        kooke
       </i>
       (cake).
      </p>
      <p>
       Dialects of the south along the
       <a href="/wiki/Sa%C3%B4ne" title="Saône">
        Saône
       </a>
       river, such as Brionnais-Charolais, have been influenced by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arpitan_language" title="Arpitan language">
        Arpitan language
       </a>
       , which is spoken mainly in a neighbouring area that approximates the heartland of the original
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Burgundy" title="Kingdom of Burgundy">
        Kingdom of Burgundy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Eugène de Chambure published a
       <i>
        Glossaire du Morvan
       </i>
       in 1878.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgundian_language_(O%C3%AFl)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Apart from songs dating from the eighteenth century, there is little surviving
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       from before the nineteenth century. In 1854 the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papal_Bull" title="Papal Bull">
        Papal Bull
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ineffabilis_Deus" title="Ineffabilis Deus">
         Ineffabilis Deus
        </a>
       </i>
       was translated into the Morvan dialect by the Abbé Jacques-François Baudiau, and into the
       <a href="/wiki/Dijon" title="Dijon">
        Dijon
       </a>
       dialect by the Abbé Lereuil. The Abbé Baudiau also transcribed storytelling.
      </p>
      <p>
       Folklorists collected
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular_literature" title="Vernacular literature">
        vernacular literature
       </a>
       from the mid-nineteenth century and by the end of the century a number of writers were establishing an original literature.
       <a href="/wiki/Achille_Millien" title="Achille Millien">
        Achille Millien
       </a>
       (1838–1927) collected songs from the
       <a href="/wiki/Oral_tradition" title="Oral tradition">
        oral tradition
       </a>
       in the Nivernais. Louis de Courmont, nicknamed the “
       <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9odore_Botrel" title="Théodore Botrel">
        Botrel
       </a>
       of the Morvan,” was a chansonnier who after a career in Paris returned to his native region. A statue was erected to him in
       <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2teau-Chinon_(Ville)" title="Château-Chinon (Ville)">
        Château-Chinon
       </a>
       . Emile Blin wrote a number of stories and monologues aimed at a tourist market; a collection was published in 1933 under the title
       <i>
        Le Patois de Chez Nous.
       </i>
       Alfred Guillaume published a large number of vernacular texts for use on picturesque postcards at the beginning of the twentieth century, and in 1923 published a book in Burgundian,
       <i>
        L’âme du Morvan.
       </i>
       More recently, Marinette Janvier published
       <i>
        Ma grelotterie
       </i>
       (1974) and
       <i>
        Autour d’un teugnon
       </i>
       (1989).
      </p>
