   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Gravitational field
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        gravitational field
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_model" title="Scientific model">
        model
       </a>
       used to explain the influence that a massive body extends into the space around itself, producing a force on another massive body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, a gravitational
       <a href="/wiki/Field_(physics)" title="Field (physics)">
        field
       </a>
       is used to explain
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravitational
       </a>
       phenomena, and is measured in
       <a href="/wiki/Newton_(unit)" title="Newton (unit)">
        newtons
       </a>
       per
       <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">
        kilogram
       </a>
       (N/kg). In its original concept,
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       was a
       <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
        force
       </a>
       between point
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        masses
       </a>
       . Following
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace" title="Pierre-Simon Laplace">
        Pierre-Simon Laplace
       </a>
       attempted to model gravity as some kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       field or
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid" title="Fluid">
        fluid
       </a>
       , and since the 19th century explanations for gravity have usually been taught in terms of a field model, rather than a point attraction.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a field model, rather than two particles attracting each other, the particles distort
       <a href="/wiki/Spacetime" title="Spacetime">
        spacetime
       </a>
       via their mass, and this distortion is what is perceived and measured as a "force". In such a model one states that matter moves in certain ways in response to the curvature of spacetime,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that there is either
       <i>
        no gravitational force
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or that gravity is a
       <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_force" title="Fictitious force">
        fictitious force
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_mechanics">
        Classical mechanics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classical mechanics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">
        classical mechanics
       </a>
       as in
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       , a gravitational field is a physical quantity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A gravitational field can be defined using
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">
        Newton's law of universal gravitation
       </a>
       . Determined in this way, the gravitational field
       <span class="texhtml">
        <b>
         g
        </b>
       </span>
       around a single particle of mass
       <span class="texhtml">
        <i>
         M
        </i>
       </span>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Vector_field" title="Vector field">
        vector field
       </a>
       consisting at every point of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vector_(geometry)" title="Vector (geometry)">
        vector
       </a>
       pointing directly towards the particle. The magnitude of the field at every point is calculated applying the universal law, and represents the force per unit mass on any object at that point in space. Because the force field is conservative, there is a scalar potential energy per unit mass,
       <span class="texhtml">
        <i>
         Φ
        </i>
       </span>
       , at each point in space associated with the force fields; this is called
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential" title="Gravitational potential">
        gravitational potential
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The gravitational field equation is

      <p>
       which contains
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_gravity" title="Gauss's law for gravity">
        Gauss's law for gravity
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Poisson%27s_equation#Newtonian_gravity" title="Poisson's equation">
        Poisson's equation for gravity
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Divergence_theorem" title="Divergence theorem">
        divergence theorem
       </a>
       . Poisson's equation is obtained by taking the
       <a href="/wiki/Divergence" title="Divergence">
        divergence
       </a>
       of both sides of the previous equation. These classical equations are
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_equation" title="Differential equation">
        differential
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Equations_of_motion" title="Equations of motion">
        equations of motion
       </a>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="General_relativity">
        General relativity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gravitational_field&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: General relativity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration#General_relativity" title="Gravitational acceleration">
        Gravitational acceleration § General relativity
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_potential#General_relativity" title="Gravitational potential">
        Gravitational potential § General relativity
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
        general relativity
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Christoffel_symbols" title="Christoffel symbols">
        Christoffel symbols
       </a>
       play the role of the gravitational force field and the
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity)" title="Metric tensor (general relativity)">
        metric tensor
       </a>
       plays the role of the gravitational potential.
      </p>
      <p>
       In general relativity, the gravitational field is determined by solving the
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_field_equations" title="Einstein field equations">
        Einstein field equations
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle \mathbf {G} ={\frac {8\pi G}{c^{4}}}\mathbf {T} }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mi mathvariant="bold">
                G
               </mi>
              </mrow>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mfrac>
                <mrow>
                 <mn>
                  8
                 </mn>
                 <mi>
                  π
                  <!-- π -->
                 </mi>
                 <mi>
                  G
                 </mi>
                </mrow>
                <msup>
                 <mi>
                  c
                 </mi>
                 <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                  <mn>
                   4
                  </mn>
                 </mrow>
                </msup>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mi mathvariant="bold">
                T
               </mi>
              </mrow>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle \mathbf {G} ={\frac {8\pi G}{c^{4}}}\mathbf {T} }
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="{\displaystyle \mathbf {G} ={\frac {8\pi G}{c^{4}}}\mathbf {T} }" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa193b07a46d514b28a6c81398314a32de7523a4" style="vertical-align: -2.171ex; width:12.216ex; height:5.676ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Here
       <span class="texhtml">
        <b>
         T
        </b>
       </span>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor" title="Stress–energy tensor">
        stress–energy tensor
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="texhtml">
        <b>
         G
        </b>
       </span>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_tensor" title="Einstein tensor">
        Einstein tensor
       </a>
       , and
       <span class="texhtml">
        <i>
         c
        </i>
       </span>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       ,
      </p>
      <p>
       These equations are dependent on the distribution of matter and energy in a region of space, unlike Newtonian gravity, which is dependent only on the distribution of matter. The fields themselves in general relativity represent the
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity#Spacetime_as_a_curved_Lorentzian_manifold" title="General relativity">
        curvature of spacetime
       </a>
       . General relativity states that being in a region of curved space is
       <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_principle#The_Einstein_equivalence_principle" title="Equivalence principle">
        equivalent
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
        accelerating
       </a>
       up the
       <a href="/wiki/Gradient" title="Gradient">
        gradient
       </a>
       of the field. By
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law" title="Newton's laws of motion">
        Newton's second law
       </a>
       , this will cause an object to experience a
       <a href="/wiki/Fictitious_force" title="Fictitious force">
        fictitious force
       </a>
       if it is held still with respect to the field. This is why a person will feel himself pulled down by the force of gravity while standing still on the Earth's surface. In general the gravitational fields predicted by general relativity differ in their effects only slightly from those predicted by classical mechanics, but there are a number of easily verifiable
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity#Predictions_of_general_relativity" title="General relativity">
        differences
       </a>
       , one of the most well known being the
       <a href="/wiki/General_relativity#bending_of_light" title="General relativity">
        bending of light
       </a>
       in such fields.
      </p>
