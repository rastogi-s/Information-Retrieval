      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        great circle
       </b>
       , also known as an
       <b>
        orthodrome
       </b>
       , of a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       is the intersection of the sphere and a
       <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
        plane
       </a>
       that passes through the
       <a href="/wiki/Centre_(geometry)" title="Centre (geometry)">
        center point
       </a>
       of the sphere. A great circle is the largest circle that can be drawn on any given sphere. Any
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       of any great circle coincides with a diameter of the sphere, and therefore all great circles have the same center and
       <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">
        circumference
       </a>
       as each other. This special case of a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_a_sphere" title="Circle of a sphere">
        circle of a sphere
       </a>
       is in opposition to a
       <i>
        small circle
       </i>
       , that is, the intersection of the sphere and a plane that does not pass through the center. Every
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circle
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">
        Euclidean 3-space
       </a>
       is a great circle of exactly one sphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       For most pairs of points on the surface of a sphere, there is a unique great circle through the two points. The exception is a pair of
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">
        antipodal
       </a>
       points, for which there are infinitely many great circles. The minor arc of a great circle between two points is the shortest surface-path between them. In this sense, the minor arc is analogous to “straight lines” in
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_geometry" title="Euclidean geometry">
        Euclidean geometry
       </a>
       . The length of the minor arc of a great circle is taken as the distance between two points on a surface of a sphere in
       <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_geometry" title="Riemannian geometry">
        Riemannian geometry
       </a>
       where such great circles are called
       <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_circle" title="Riemannian circle">
        Riemannian circles
       </a>
       . These great circles are the
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">
        geodesics
       </a>
       of the sphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       In higher dimensions, the great circles on the
       <a href="/wiki/N-sphere" title="N-sphere">
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        -sphere
       </a>
       are the intersection of the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -sphere with 2-planes that pass through the origin in the Euclidean space
       <b>
        R
       </b>
       <sup>
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        + 1
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

       which is a plane through the origin, i.e., the center of the sphere.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
        Applications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Great_circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some examples of great circles on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       include the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_horizon" title="Celestial horizon">
        celestial horizon
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_equator" title="Celestial equator">
        celestial equator
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . Great circles are also used as rather accurate approximations of
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesics_on_an_ellipsoid" title="Geodesics on an ellipsoid">
        geodesics
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's surface for air or sea
       <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_navigation" title="Great-circle navigation">
        navigation
       </a>
       (although it
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shape_of_the_Earth" title="Shape of the Earth">
        is not a perfect sphere
       </a>
       ), as well as on spheroidal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_bodies" title="Celestial bodies">
        celestial bodies
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       of the idealized earth is a great circle and any meridian and its opposite meridian form a great circle. Another great circle is the one that divides the
       <a href="/wiki/Land_and_water_hemispheres" title="Land and water hemispheres">
        land and water hemispheres
       </a>
       . A great circle divides the earth into two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hemisphere_of_the_Earth" title="Hemisphere of the Earth">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       and if a great circle passes through a point it must pass through its
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">
        antipodal point
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Funk_transform" title="Funk transform">
        Funk transform
       </a>
       integrates a function along all great circles of the sphere.
      </p>
