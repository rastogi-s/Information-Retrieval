      <p>
       <b>
        Caribbean Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        español caribeño
       </i>
       ) is the general name of the Spanish dialects spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean" title="Caribbean">
        Caribbean
       </a>
       region. It closely resembles the Spanish spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       and western
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       More precisely, the term refers to the Spanish language as spoken in the Caribbean islands of
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       as well as in
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       , and the Caribbean coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caribbean_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Frequently, word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       are dropped (as in
       <i>
        compás
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kõmˈpa(h)]
       </span>
       'beat',
       <i>
        mitad
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [miˈta]
       </span>
       'half'). Syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (as well as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       in any context) may also be
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalized
       </a>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       :
       <i>
        los amigos
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [lo(h‿)aˈmiɣo(h)]
       </span>
       ('the friends'),
       <i>
        dos
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈdo(h)]
       </span>
       ('two').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Similarly, syllable-final nasals and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       (or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       ) in the infinitival morpheme may also be dropped (
       <i>
        ven
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbẽ(n)]
       </span>
       'come',
       <i>
        comer
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [koˈme(l)]
       </span>
       'to eat');
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Guitart|1997|p=515_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Guitart|1997|p=515-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the dropping of final nasals does not result in further neutralization compared to other dialects since the nasalization of the vowel is maintained.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several neutralizations also occur in the syllable coda. The liquids
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       may neutralize to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       (Cibaeño Dominican
       <i>
        celda
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        cerda
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈsejða]
       </span>
       'cell'/'bristle'),
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        alma
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        arma
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈalma]
       </span>
       'soul'/'weapon',
       <i>
        comer
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [koˈme(l)]
       </span>
       'to eat'), or as complete regressive assimilation (
       <i>
        pulga
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        purga
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpuɡːa]
       </span>
       'flea'/'purge').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Guitart|1997|p=515_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Guitart|1997|p=515-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The deletions and neutralizations show variability in their occurrence, even with the same speaker in the same utterance, which implies that nondeleted forms exist in the underlying structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That is not to say that these dialects are on the path to eliminating coda consonants since such processes have existed for more than four centuries in these dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="#CITEREFGuitart1997">
        Guitart (1997)
       </a>
       argues that it is the result of speakers acquiring multiple phonological systems with uneven control, like that of second language learners.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are other features:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        is often deleted (at times causing diphthongs):
        <i>
         cansado
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kãnˈsao]
        </span>
        ('tired'),
        <i>
         nada
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈna]
        </span>
        ('nothing'), and
        <i>
         perdido
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [perˈði.o]
        </span>
        ('lost').
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2009)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is retracted to glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        is often pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        and aspirated, especially in Puerto Rico: e.g.
        <i>
         revolución
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [xʰeβoluˈsjõŋ]
        </span>
        ('revolution')
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final /n/ is realized as
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
         velar
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        , meaning
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        is an allophone of /n/ before velar consonants and word-final position:
        <i>
         consideran
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kõnsiˈðeɾãŋ]
        </span>
        ('they consider') and
        <i>
         Teherán
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [te(e)ˈɾãŋ]
        </span>
        ('
        <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">
         Tehran
        </a>
        '); in Venezuela, syllable-final /n/-velarisation, or /n/-assimilation prevails:
        <i>
         ambientación
        </i>
        ("atmosphere") becomes either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ãŋbjẽŋtaˈsjõŋ]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ãbjẽtaˈsjõ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The second-person subject pronouns,
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        (or
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
          vos
         </a>
        </i>
        in Central America) and
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        , are used more frequently than in other varieties of Spanish, contrary to the general Spanish tendency to omit them when meaning is clear from the context (see
        <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
         Pro-drop language
        </a>
        ). Thus,
        <i>
         <b>
          tú
         </b>
         estás hablando
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         estás hablando
        </i>
        . The tendency is strongest in the island countries and, on the mainland, in Nicaragua, where
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
        (rather than the use of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        for the second person singular familiar) is predominant.
       </li>
       <li>
        So-called
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wh-question" title="Wh-question">
         "
         <i>
          wh
         </i>
         -questions"
        </a>
        , which in standard Spanish are marked by subject/verb inversion, often appear without the inversion in Caribbean Spanish: "¿Qué tú quieres?" for standard "¿Qué quieres (tú)?" ("What do you want?").
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
