      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Spanish language in South America
       </b>
       varies within the different countries and regions of the continent. The term "
       <b>
        South American Spanish
       </b>
       " (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        español sudamericano
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        español suramericano
       </i>
       ) is sometimes used as a broad name for the dialects of Spanish spoken on the continent, but such a term is only
       <i>
        geographical
       </i>
       and has little or no
       <i>
        linguistic
       </i>
       relevance.
      </p>
      <p>
       The diverse Spanish dialects of the continent have no unifying feature to set them apart from non-South American varieties. The Spanish of the
       <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
        Andean highlands
       </a>
       is historically conservative, having some traits in common with the Spanish of central
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexico
       </a>
       , while varieties spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       share some phonological innovations with the Spanish spoken on
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean islands
       </a>
       . In some cases a single South American country—for example
       <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Spanish" title="Colombian Spanish">
        Colombia
       </a>
       —presents a broad spectrum of conservative and innovative dialects.
      </p>
