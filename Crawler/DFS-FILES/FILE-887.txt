      <p>
       <b>
        Bozal Spanish
       </b>
       is a possible extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">
        Spanish-based creole language
       </a>
       that may have been a mixture of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kikongo" title="Kikongo">
        Kikongo
       </a>
       , with
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       influences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clements_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-clements-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Attestation is insufficient to indicate whether Bozal Spanish was ever a single, coherent or stable language, or if the term merely referred to any idiolect of Spanish that included African elements. The Spanish distinguished
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Black_ladino" title="Black ladino">
         negros ladinos
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRAE_ladino_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DRAE_ladino-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ("Latinate Negros", those who spent more than a year in a Spanish-speaking territory) and
       <i>
        negros bozales
       </i>
       (wild, untamed
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Negroes; those just brought from Africa). Similarly, the Portuguese distinguished between "indios mansos" (tamed, domesticated
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Indians
       </a>
       ) and "indios bravos" (untamed, wild Indians).
      </p>
      <p>
       Bozal Spanish was spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">
        African slaves
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clements_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-clements-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and other areas of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       from the 17th century up until its possible extinction at around 1850.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipski_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lipski-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although Bozal Spanish is extinct as a language, its influence still exists.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipski_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lipski-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some Cuban folk religious rituals today, people speak what they call "Bozal".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
