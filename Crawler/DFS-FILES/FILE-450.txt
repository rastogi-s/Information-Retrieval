      <p>
       In linguistics,
       <b>
        grammatical number
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical category
       </a>
       of nouns, pronouns, and adjective and verb
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       that expresses count distinctions (such as "one", "two", or "three or more").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In many languages, including
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , the number categories are singular and
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        plural
       </a>
       . Some languages also have a
       <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">
        dual
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number#Trial" title="Grammatical number">
        trial
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number#Paucal" title="Grammatical number">
        paucal
       </a>
       number or other arrangements.
      </p>
      <p>
       The count distinctions typically, but not always, correspond to the actual count of the
       <a href="/wiki/Referent" title="Referent">
        referents
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Marker_(linguistics)" title="Marker (linguistics)">
        marked
       </a>
       noun or pronoun.
      </p>
      <p>
       The word "number" is also used in linguistics to describe the distinction between certain grammatical aspects that indicate the number of times an event occurs, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Semelfactive" title="Semelfactive">
        semelfactive
       </a>
       aspect, the iterative aspect, etc. For that use of the term, see "
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        Grammatical aspect
       </a>
       ".
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most languages of the world have formal means to express differences of number. One widespread distinction, found in English and many other languages, involves a simple two-way number contrast between singular and plural (
       <i>
        car
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        cars
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        child
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        children
       </i>
       , etc.). Discussion of other more elaborate systems of number appears below.
      </p>
      <p>
       Grammatical number is a morphological category characterized by the expression of
       <a href="/wiki/Quantity" title="Quantity">
        quantity
       </a>
       through inflection or agreement. As an example, consider the English sentences below:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         That apple on the table is fresh.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Those two apples on the table are fresh.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The number of apples is marked on the noun—"apple"
       <b>
        singular number
       </b>
       (one item) vs. "apples"
       <b>
        plural number
       </b>
       (more than one item)—on the demonstrative, "that/those", and on the verb, "is/are". In the second sentence, all this information is
       <a href="/wiki/Redundancy_(linguistics)" title="Redundancy (linguistics)">
        redundant
       </a>
       , since quantity is already indicated by the numeral "two".
      </p>
      <p>
       A language has grammatical number when its nouns are subdivided into
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological classes
       </a>
       according to the quantity they express, such that:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Every
        <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
         noun
        </a>
        belongs to a unique number class (nouns are partitioned into disjoint classes by number).
       </li>
       <li>
        Noun
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modifier_(linguistics)" title="Modifier (linguistics)">
         modifiers
        </a>
        (such as adjectives) and
        <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
         verbs
        </a>
        may also have different forms for each number class and be
        <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
         inflected
        </a>
        to match the number of the nouns to which they refer (number is an
        <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
         agreement category
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       This is partly the case in English: every noun is either singular or plural (a few forms, such as "fish", can be either, according to context), and at least some modifiers of nouns—namely the
       <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">
        demonstratives
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        articles
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       —are inflected to agree with the number of the nouns to which they refer: "this car" and "these cars" are correct, while "*this cars" or "*these car" are ungrammatical and, therefore, incorrect. However adjectives are not inflected, and most verb forms do not distinguish between singular and plural. Only count nouns can be freely used in the singular and in the plural. Mass nouns, like "milk", "silverware", and "wisdom", are normally used in only the singular form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (In some cases, a normally mass noun
       <i>
        <b>
         X
        </b>
       </i>
       may be used as a count noun to collect several distinct kinds of
       <i>
        <b>
         X
        </b>
       </i>
       into an enumerable group; for example, a cheesemaker might speak of goat, sheep, and cow milk as
       <i>
        milks
       </i>
       .) Many languages distinguish between count nouns and mass nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       Not all languages have number as a grammatical category. In those that do not, quantity must be expressed either directly, with
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
        numerals
       </a>
       , or indirectly, through optional
       <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(linguistics)" title="Quantifier (linguistics)">
        quantifiers
       </a>
       . However, many of these languages compensate
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       for the lack of grammatical number with an extensive system of
       <a href="/wiki/Measure_word" title="Measure word">
        measure words
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a hierarchy among number categories: no language distinguishes a trial (indicating the number 3) unless it has a dual, and no language has a dual without a plural.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGreenberg1972_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGreenberg1972-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographical_distribution">
        Geographical distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographical distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Obligatory plural marking of all nouns is found throughout
       <a href="/wiki/Eurasiatic_languages" title="Eurasiatic languages">
        western and northern Eurasia
       </a>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Africa" title="Languages of Africa">
        most parts of Africa
       </a>
       . The rest of the world presents a heterogeneous picture. Optional plural marking is particularly common in
       <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">
        Southeast and East Asia
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        Australia
       </a>
       , and complete lack of plural marking is particularly found in
       <a href="/wiki/Papuan_languages" title="Papuan languages">
        New Guinea
       </a>
       and Australia. In addition to the
       <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">
        areal correlations
       </a>
       , there also seems to be at least one correlation with
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_typology" title="Morphological typology">
        morphological typology
       </a>
       :
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isolating_languages" title="Isolating languages">
        isolating languages
       </a>
       appear to favor no or non-obligatory plural marking. This can be seen particularly in Africa, where optionality or absence of plural marking is found particularly in the isolating languages of West Africa.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Number_in_specific_languages">
        Number in specific languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Number in specific languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_plural" title="English plural">
        English plural
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       is typical of most world languages, in distinguishing only between singular and plural number. The plural form of a noun is usually created by adding the
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        suffix
       </a>
       <i>
        -(e)s
       </i>
       . The pronouns have irregular plurals, as in "I" versus "we", because they are ancient and frequently used words going back to when English had a well developed system of
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       . English verbs distinguish singular from plural number in the third person present tense ("He goes" versus "They go"). English treats zero with the plural number.
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       did contain dual grammatical numbers.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Finnish">
        Finnish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Finnish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_grammar#Plurals" title="Finnish grammar">
        Finnish grammar § Plurals
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish language
       </a>
       has a plural form of almost every noun case (except the comitative, which is formally only plural).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         talo
        </i>
        – house
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         talot
        </i>
        – houses
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         taloissa
        </i>
        – in the houses
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, when a number is used, or a word signifying a number (monta- many), the singular version of the partitive case is used.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         kolme taloa
        </i>
        – three houses
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       and where no specific number is mentioned, the plural version of the partitive case is used
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        taloja
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       and in the possessive (genitive)
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        talon ovi (the house's door)
       </li>
       <li>
        talojen ovet (the houses' doors)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="French">
        French
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: French">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In modern Romance languages, nouns, adjectives and articles are declined according to number (singular or plural only). Verbs are conjugated for number as well as person. French treats zero as using the singular number, not the plural.
      </p>
      <p>
       In its written form,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       declines nouns for number (singular or plural). In speech, however, the majority of nouns (and adjectives) are not declined for number. The typical plural suffix,
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -es
       </i>
       , is
       <a href="/wiki/Silent_letter" title="Silent letter">
        silent
       </a>
       , no longer indicating a change in pronunciation. Spoken number marking on the noun appears when
       <a href="/wiki/Liaison_(French)" title="Liaison (French)">
        liaison
       </a>
       occurs.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        some plurals do differ from the singular in pronunciation; for example, masculine singulars in
        <i>
         -al
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [al]
        </span>
        sometimes form masculine plurals in
        <i>
         -aux
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [o]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proper_nouns" title="Proper nouns">
         Proper nouns
        </a>
        are not pluralized, even in writing. (
        <i>
         Les voitures
        </i>
        , but
        <i>
         Les
         <a href="/wiki/Peugeot_404" title="Peugeot 404">
          Peugeot 404
         </a>
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Normally, the article or determiner is the primary indicator of number.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hebrew">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Modern
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semitic_language" title="Semitic language">
        Semitic language
       </a>
       , most nouns have only singular and plural forms, such as ספר
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsɛfɛʁ/
       </span>
       "book" and ספרים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sfaˈʁim/
       </span>
       "books", but some have distinct dual forms using a distinct dual suffix (largely nouns pertaining to numbers or time, such as אלפיים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /alˈpajim/
       </span>
       "two thousand" and שבועיים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃvuˈajim/
       </span>
       "two weeks"), some use this dual suffix for their regular plurals (largely body parts that tend to come in pairs, such as עיניים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eɪˈnajim/
       </span>
       "eyes", as well as some that do not, such as שיניים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃiˈnajim/
       </span>
       "teeth"), and some are inherently dual (such as מכנסיים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /mɪxnaˈsajim/
       </span>
       "pants" and אופניים
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ofaˈnajim/
       </span>
       "bicycle"). Adjectives, verbs, and pronouns agree with their subjects' or antecedents' numbers, but only have a two-way distinction between singular and plural; dual nouns entail plural adjectives, verbs, and pronouns.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">
        Russian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Russian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_declension#Numerals" title="Russian declension">
        Russian declension § Numerals
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Modern
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       has a singular vs plural number system, but the
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       of noun phrases containing numeral expressions follows complex rules. For example, "У меня есть одна книга/три книги/пять книг" ("I have one book-
       <i>
        nom.sing.
       </i>
       /three book-
       <i>
        gen.sing.
       </i>
       /five book-
       <i>
        gen.plur.
       </i>
       "). See
       <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)#Slavic_languages" title="Dual (grammatical number)">
        Dual number: Slavic languages
       </a>
       for a discussion of number phrases in Russian and other Slavic languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       The numeral "one" also has a plural form, used with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluralia_tantum" title="Pluralia tantum">
        pluralia tantum
       </a>
       : одни джинсы/одни часы "one pair of jeans, one clock".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELunt1982204_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELunt1982204-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The same form is used with countable nouns in meaning "only": Кругом одни идиоты "There are only idiots around".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Swedish">
        Swedish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Swedish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       inflects nouns in singular and plural. The plural of the noun is usually obtained by adding a suffix, according to the noun's declension. The suffixes are as follows: -or in the 1st declension (e.g. flicka - flickor), -ar in the 2nd (e.g. bil - bilar), -er in the 3rd (e.g. katt - katter), -n in the 4th (e.g. äpple - äpplen) and no inflectional suffix is added for the nouns in the 5th declension (e.g. bord - bord). Verbs in Swedish do not distinguish singular from plural number.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tokelauan">
        Tokelauan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tokelauan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Tokelau" title="Tokelau">
        Tokelau
       </a>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       Island consisting of three atolls. The
       <a href="/wiki/Tokelauan_language" title="Tokelauan language">
        Tokelauan
       </a>
       language has three types of personal pronouns - 1st person, 2nd person inclusive, 2nd person exclusive, and 3rd person exclusive. The language has unique ways to address multiple people in one conversation. For example, the generic greeting for ‘Hello’ is Tālofa or Mālō. To greet one person, ‘greetings to you’ would be the same as the common greeting. To address two people, Tālofa nī/Mālō nī Fakatālofa atu kia te koulua; koulua is the dual third person term. To address three or more people, Fakatālofa atu kia te koutou; koutou is the plural third person term.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wuvulu-Aua">
        Wuvulu-Aua
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Wuvulu-Aua">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wuvulu_language" title="Wuvulu language">
        Wuvulu
       </a>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       Island located in the
       <a href="/wiki/Manus_Province" title="Manus Province">
        Manus Province
       </a>
       of Papua New Guinea. The languages numbering system is multiplicative construction, where each number is based on multiplying pre-existing numbers smaller than five. Wuvulu is most similar to most
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Oceanic_language" title="Proto-Oceanic language">
        Proto-Oceanic languages
       </a>
       , and their numbering system is representative of some systems found in the Marshall Islands. For examples, the number two in Wuvulu is
       <i>
        roa
       </i>
       and the number four in both Proto-Oceanic language and Wuvulu is
       <i>
        fa
       </i>
       . Therefore, the number eight in Wuvulu the construction of two and four, resulting in
       <i>
        fainaroa
       </i>
       , translating into "four multiply two". Moreover, the Wuvulu language has different numerical systems for animate objects and inanimates objects. When referencing an inanimate object, the number seven is
       <i>
        oloompalo
       </i>
       ; however, if it is an animate object, the word changes to
       <i>
        oloromea
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The structure of a noun phrase looks like " NP=(ART/DEMONSTRATIVE+)(NUMBER/QUANTIFIER+)(PREMODIFIERS+)NOUN(+MODIFER.) As we can see, the number or quantifier appears in the middle of the noun phrase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Example:
      </p>
      <p>
       ʔi=na-tafi-ʔa oloroa wa
      </p>
      <p>
       3SG=REAL-carve-TR six canoe
      </p>
      <p>
       He carved six canoes.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_number">
        Types of number
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Types of number">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Singular_versus_plural">
        Singular versus plural
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Singular versus plural">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        Plural
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In most languages with grammatical number, nouns, and sometimes other parts of speech, have two forms, the singular, for one instance of a concept, and the plural, for more than one instance. Usually, the singular is the
       <a href="/wiki/Marker_(linguistics)" title="Marker (linguistics)">
        unmarked
       </a>
       form of a word, and the plural is obtained by
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflecting
       </a>
       the singular. This is the case in English:
       <i>
        car/cars, box/boxes, man/men
       </i>
       . There may be exceptional nouns whose plural is identical to the singular:
       <i>
        one sheep/two sheep
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Singulative_versus_collective">
        Singulative versus collective
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Singulative versus collective">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Singulative_number" title="Singulative number">
        Singulative number
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some languages differentiate between an
       <a href="/wiki/Null_morpheme" title="Null morpheme">
        unmarked
       </a>
       form, the collective, which is indifferent in respect to number, and a marked form for single entities, called the singulative in this context. For example, in Welsh,
       <i>
        moch
       </i>
       ("pigs") is a basic form, whereas a suffix is added to form
       <i>
        mochyn
       </i>
       ("pig"). It is the collective form which is more basic, and it is used as an adjectival modifier, e.g.
       <i>
        cig moch
       </i>
       ("pig meat", "pork"). The collective form is therefore similar in many respects to an English mass noun like "rice", which in fact refers to a collection of items which are logically countable. However, English has no
       <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(linguistics)" title="Productivity (linguistics)">
        productive
       </a>
       process of forming singulative nouns (just phrases such as "a grain of rice"). Therefore, English cannot be said to have a singulative number.
      </p>
      <p>
       In other languages, singulatives can be regularly formed from
       <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">
        collective nouns
       </a>
       ; e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
        Standard Arabic
       </a>
       حجر
       <i>
        ḥajar
       </i>
       "stone" → حجرة
       <i>
        ḥajara
       </i>
       "(individual) stone", بقر
       <i>
        baqar
       </i>
       "cattle" → بقرة
       <i>
        baqara
       </i>
       "(single) cow". In
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       , the suffix for forming singulative form is -ин-
       <i>
        -in-
       </i>
       ; e.g. град
       <i>
        grad
       </i>
       "hail" → градина
       <i>
        gradina
       </i>
       "hailstone", лёд
       <i>
        lyod
       </i>
       "ice" → льдина
       <i>
        l'dina
       </i>
       "block of ice". In both Russian and Arabic, the singulative form always takes on the feminine
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       . In Dutch, singulative forms of collective nouns are occasionally made by diminutives:
       <i>
        snoep
       </i>
       "sweets, candy" →
       <i>
        snoepje
       </i>
       "sweet, piece of candy". These singulatives can be pluralised like most other nouns:
       <i>
        snoepjes
       </i>
       "several sweets, pieces of candy".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dual">
        Dual
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Dual">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dual_(grammatical_number)" title="Dual (grammatical number)">
        Dual (grammatical number)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The distinction between a "singular" number (one) and a "plural" number (more than one) found in English is not the only possible classification. Another one is "singular" (one), "dual" (two) and "plural" (more than two). Dual number existed in
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       , persisted in many ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       that descended from it—
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
        Gothic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       for example—and can still be found in a few modern Indo-European languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKordić200163,_72–73_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKordić200163,_72–73-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many more modern Indo-European languages show residual traces of the dual, as in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       distinctions
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        all
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        either
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        any
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        neither
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        none
       </i>
       , and so on. (Note, however, that Norwegian
       <i>
        både
       </i>
       , for example, though cognate with English
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       , can be used with more than two things, as in
       <i>
        X sparer både tid, penger, og arbeid
       </i>
       , literally "X saves both time, money, and labour".)
      </p>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       also have dual number. For instance, in Arabic all nouns can have singular, plural, or dual forms. For non-
       <a href="/wiki/Broken_plural" title="Broken plural">
        broken plurals
       </a>
       , masculine plural nouns end with ون
       <i>
        -ūn
       </i>
       and feminine plural nouns end with ات
       <i>
        -āt
       </i>
       , whilst ان
       <i>
        -ān
       </i>
       , is added to the end of a noun to indicate that it is dual (even among nouns that have broken plurals).
      </p>
      <p>
       Pronouns in
       <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">
        Polynesian languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tahitian_language" title="Tahitian language">
        Tahitian
       </a>
       exhibit the singular, dual, and plural numbers.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trial">
        Trial
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Trial">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The trial number is a grammatical number referring to 'three items', in contrast to 'singular' (one item), 'dual' (two items), and 'plural' (four or more items). Several
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolomako" title="Tolomako">
        Tolomako
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lihir_language" title="Lihir language">
        Lihir
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Manam_language" title="Manam language">
        Manam
       </a>
       ; the
       <a href="/wiki/Kiwaian_languages" title="Kiwaian languages">
        Kiwaian languages
       </a>
       ; and the
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       -influenced creole languages
       <a href="/wiki/Bislama" title="Bislama">
        Bislama
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tok_Pisin" title="Tok Pisin">
        Tok Pisin
       </a>
       have the trial number in their pronouns. No language is known with trial number in its nouns.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="what source(s) say no language is known with trial number in its nouns? (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quadral">
        Quadral
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Quadral">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quadral" title="wikt:quadral">
        quadral
       </a>
       number, if it existed, would denote
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4_(number)" title="4 (number)">
        four
       </a>
       items together, as trial does three. No known natural language has it, nor is there any proof that any natural language ever did. It was once thought to exist in the pronoun systems of
       <a href="/wiki/Marshallese_language" title="Marshallese language">
        Marshallese
       </a>
       , spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Islands" title="Marshall Islands">
        Marshall Islands
       </a>
       in the Pacific Ocean,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in
       <a href="/wiki/Sursurunga_language" title="Sursurunga language">
        Sursurunga
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in Tangga,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in several other Austronesian languages. While not all of these languages are adequately attested, it turns out that Sursurunga instead has both a "lesser paucal" (labeled "trial", but in fact referring to small groups, with typically three or four members) and a "greater paucal" (misnamed the "quadral", as it has a minimum of four, e.g. a pair of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyadic_kin" title="Dyadic kin">
        dyadic kin
       </a>
       terms)—the distinction is along the lines of "a few" vs. "several";—and that what Marshallese actually has is a trial and a paucal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett200025–30,_40,_46,_224,_317,_358_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett200025–30,_40,_46,_224,_317,_358-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       None of them has a "quadral"; in at least two cases the field workers who originally suggested they did have a "quadral" were also the first to publish a peer-reviewed article contradicting that suggestion.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Paucal">
        Paucal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Paucal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Paucal number, for a few (as opposed to many) instances of the referent (e.g. in
       <a href="/wiki/Hopi_language" title="Hopi language">
        Hopi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Warlpiri_language" title="Warlpiri language">
        Warlpiri
       </a>
       , some
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_languages" title="Oceanic languages">
        Oceanic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Siwai_language" title="Siwai language">
        Motuna
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Number_particles">
        Number particles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Number particles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Plurality is sometimes marked by a specialized number particle (or number word). This is frequent in Australian and
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian languages
       </a>
       . An example from
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
        Tagalog
       </a>
       is the word
       <i>
        mga
       </i>
       [mɐˈŋa]: compare
       <i>
        bahay
       </i>
       "house" with
       <i>
        mga bahay
       </i>
       "houses". In
       <a href="/wiki/Kapampangan_language" title="Kapampangan language">
        Kapampangan
       </a>
       , certain nouns optionally denote plurality by secondary stress:
       <i>
        ing laláki
       </i>
       "man" and
       <i>
        ing babái
       </i>
       "woman" become
       <i>
        ding láláki
       </i>
       "men" and
       <i>
        ding bábái
       </i>
       "women".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classifiers_with_number_morphology">
        Classifiers with number morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Classifiers with number morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       and some other languages, number and case are fused category and there is concord for number between a noun and its
       <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">
        predicator
       </a>
       . Some languages however (for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">
        Assamese
       </a>
       ) lack this feature.
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages that show number inflection for a large enough corpus of nouns or allow them to combine directly with singular and plural numerals can be described as non-classifier languages. On the other hand, there are languages that obligatorily require a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counter_word" title="Counter word">
        counter word
       </a>
       or the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Classifier_(linguistics)" title="Classifier (linguistics)">
        classifier
       </a>
       for all nouns. For example, the category of number in Assamese is fused with the category of classifier, which always carries a definite/indefinite reading. The singularity or plurality of the noun is determined by the addition of the classifier
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        suffix
       </a>
       either to the noun or to the numeral. Number system in Assamese is either realized as numeral or as nominal inflection, but not both. Numerals [ek] 'one' and [dui] 'two', can be realized as both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_morpheme" title="Free morpheme">
        free morpheme
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clitics" title="Clitics">
        clitics
       </a>
       . When used with classifiers, these two numerals are cliticised to the classifiers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pingelapese is a Micronesian language spoken on the Pingelap atoll and on two of the eastern Caroline Islands, called the high island of Pohnpei. In Pingelapese, the meaning, use, or shape of an object can be expressed through the use of numerical classifiers. These classifiers combine and noun and a number that together can give more details about the object. There are at least five sets of numerical classifiers in Pingelapese. Each classifier has a numeral part and a classifier part that corresponds to the noun it is describing. The classifier follows the noun in a phrase. There is a separate set of numerical classifiers that is used when the object is not specified. Examples of this is the names of the days of the week.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Obligatoriness_of_number_marking">
        Obligatoriness of number marking
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Obligatoriness of number marking">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In many languages, such as English, number is obligatorily expressed in every grammatical context. Some limit number expression to certain classes of nouns, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animates
       </a>
       or referentially prominent nouns (as with proximate forms in most
       <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">
        Algonquian languages
       </a>
       , opposed to referentially less prominent obviative forms). In others, such as Chinese and Japanese, number marking is not consistently applied to most nouns unless a distinction is needed or already present.
      </p>
      <p>
       A very common situation is for plural number to not be marked if there is any other overt indication of number, as for example in
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        virág
       </i>
       "flower";
       <i>
        virágok
       </i>
       "flowers";
       <i>
        hat virág
       </i>
       "six flowers".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transnumeral">
        Transnumeral
        <span id="general_number">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Transnumeral">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">
        Indonesian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">
        Malay
       </a>
       , have optional number marking. In such cases, an unmarked noun is neither singular nor plural, but rather ambiguous as to number. This is called
       <i>
        transnumeral
       </i>
       or sometimes
       <i>
        general number,
       </i>
       abbreviated
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         <b>
          TRN
         </b>
        </span>
       </span>
       . Many such languages have optional number marking, which tends to be used for definite and highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Animacy_hierarchy" title="Animacy hierarchy">
        animate
       </a>
       referents, most notably first-person pronouns.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inverse_number">
        Inverse number
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Inverse number">

       <a href="/wiki/Tanoan_languages" title="Tanoan languages">

       <i>

       <a href="/wiki/Jemez_language" title="Jemez language">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Number_agreement">
        Number agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Number agreement">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">
        Verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        Grammatical conjugation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In many languages, verbs are conjugated according to number. Using French as an example, one says
       <i>
        je vois
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        I see
       </i>
       ), but
       <i>
        nous voyons
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        we see
       </i>
       ). The verb
       <i>
        voir
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        to see
       </i>
       ) changes from
       <i>
        vois
       </i>
       in the first person singular to
       <i>
        voyons
       </i>
       in the plural. In everyday English, this often happens in the third person (
       <i>
        she sees
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        they see
       </i>
       ), but not in other grammatical persons, except with the verb
       <i>
        to be
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives_and_determiners">
        Adjectives and determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Adjectives and determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        Adjectives
       </a>
       often agree with the number of the noun they modify. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , one says
       <i>
        un grand arbre
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [œ̃ ɡʁɑ̃t aʁbʁ]
       </span>
       "a tall tree", but
       <i>
        deux grands arbres
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dø ɡʁɑ̃ zaʁbʁ]
       </span>
       "two tall trees". The singular adjective
       <i>
        grand
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        grands
       </i>
       in the plural, unlike English "tall", which remains unchanged.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(linguistics)" title="Determiner (linguistics)">
        determiners
       </a>
       may agree with number. In English, the
       <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">
        demonstratives
       </a>
       "this", "that" change to "these", "those" in the plural, and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indefinite_article" title="Indefinite article">
        indefinite article
       </a>
       "a", "an" is either omitted or changes to "some". In French and German, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite articles
       </a>
       have
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender distinctions
       </a>
       in the singular but not the plural. In Italian, Spanish and Portuguese, both definite and indefinite articles are inflected for gender and number, e.g. Portuguese
       <i>
        o, a
       </i>
       "the" (singular, masc./fem.),
       <i>
        os, as
       </i>
       "the" (plural, masc./fem.);
       <i>
        um, uma
       </i>
       "a(n)" (singular, masc./fem.),
       <i>
        uns, umas
       </i>
       "some" (plural, masc./fem.),
       <i>
        dois, duas
       </i>
       "two" (plural, masc./fem.),
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       sentence
       <i>
        Yö
        <b>
         t
        </b>
        o
        <b>
         vat
        </b>
        pime
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        tä
       </i>
       "Nights are dark", each word referring to the plural noun
       <i>
        yöt
       </i>
       "nights" ("night" =
       <i>
        yö
       </i>
       ) is pluralized (night-
       <small>
        PL
       </small>
       is-
       <small>
        PL
       </small>
       dark-
       <small>
        PL
       </small>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Partitive" title="Partitive">
        partitive
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">
        Exceptions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Exceptions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Synesis" title="Synesis">
        Synesis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Plurale_tantum" title="Plurale tantum">
        Plurale tantum
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sometimes, grammatical number will not represent the actual quantity. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neuter_gender" title="Neuter gender">
        neuter
       </a>
       plurals took a singular verb.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The plural form of a pronoun may also be applied to a single individual as a sign of importance, respect or generality, as in the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluralis_majestatis" title="Pluralis majestatis">
         pluralis majestatis
        </a>
       </i>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Generic_you" title="Generic you">
        generic "you"
       </a>
       , found in many languages, or, in English, when using the
       <a href="/wiki/Singular_they" title="Singular they">
        singular "they"
       </a>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-neutrality" title="Gender-neutrality">
        gender-neutrality
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_grammar" title="Arabic grammar">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , the plural of a non-human noun (one that refers to an animal or to an
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        inanimate
       </a>
       entity regardless of whether the noun is grammatically masculine or feminine in the singular) is treated as feminine singular—this is called the inanimate plural. For example:

        </i>
        .

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collective_nouns">
        Collective nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Collective nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">
        Collective noun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A collective noun is a word that designates a group of objects or beings regarded as a whole, such as "flock", "team", or "corporation". Although many languages treat collective nouns as singular, in others they may be interpreted as plural. In
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       , phrases such as
       <i>
        the committee are meeting
       </i>
       are common (the so-called agreement
       <i>
        in sensu
       </i>
       "in meaning"; with the meaning of a noun, rather than with its form, see
       <a href="/wiki/Synesis" title="Synesis">
        constructio ad sensum
       </a>
       ). The use of this type of construction varies with dialect and level of formality.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cases, the number marking on a verb with a collective subject may express the degree of collectivity of action:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         The committee are discussing the matter
        </i>
        (the individual members are discussing the matter), but
        <i>
         the committee has decided on the matter
        </i>
        (the committee has acted as an indivisible body).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         The crowd is tearing down the fences
        </i>
        (a crowd is doing something as a unit), but
        <i>
         the crowd are cheering wildly
        </i>
        (many individual members of the crowd are doing the same thing independently of each other).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Semantic_vs._grammatical_number">
        Semantic vs. grammatical number
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Semantic vs. grammatical number">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All languages are able to specify the quantity of referents. They may do so by
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexical
       </a>
       means with words such as English
       <i>
        a few
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        some
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        two
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        five hundred
       </i>
       . However, not every language has a grammatical category of number. Grammatical number is expressed by
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic" title="Syntactic">
        syntactic
       </a>
       means. That is, it is indicated by certain grammatical elements, such as through
       <a href="/wiki/Affix" title="Affix">
        affixes
       </a>
       or number words. Grammatical number may be thought of as the indication of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">
        semantic
       </a>
       number through
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages that express quantity only by lexical means lack a grammatical category of number. For instance, in
       <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">
        Khmer
       </a>
       , neither nouns nor verbs carry any grammatical information concerning number: such information can only be conveyed by lexical items such as
       <i>
        khlah
       </i>
       'some',
       <i>
        pii-bey
       </i>
       'a few', and so on.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Auxiliary_languages">
        Auxiliary languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_number&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Auxiliary languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">
        Auxiliary languages
       </a>
       often have fairly simple systems of grammatical number. In one of the most common schemes (found, for example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" title="Ido (language)">
        Ido
       </a>
       ), nouns and pronouns distinguish between singular and plural, but not other numbers, and adjectives and verbs do not display any number agreement. In
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       , however, adjectives must agree in both number and case with the nouns that they qualify.
      </p>
