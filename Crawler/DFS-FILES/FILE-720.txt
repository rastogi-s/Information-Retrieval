      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Mirandese language
       </b>
       (autonym:
       <i>
        mirandés
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lhéngua mirandesa
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         mirandês
        </span>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          língua mirandesa
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is an
       <a href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_languages" title="Astur-Leonese languages">
        Astur-Leonese language
       </a>
       that is sparsely spoken in a small area of northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       in the municipalities of
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_do_Douro" title="Miranda do Douro">
        Miranda do Douro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mogadouro" title="Mogadouro">
        Mogadouro
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vimioso" title="Vimioso">
        Vimioso
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">
        Assembly of the Republic
       </a>
       granted it official recognition alongside
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       for local matters on 17 September 1998 with the law 7/99 of 29 January 1999.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mirandese has a distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       . It has its roots in the local
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       spoken in the northern
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Mirandese is a descendant of the Astur-Leonese variety spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        Kingdom of León
       </a>
       and has both archaisms and innovations that differentiate it from the modern varieties of Astur-Leonese spoken in Spain. In recognition of these differences, and due to its political isolation from the rest of the Astur-Leonese speaking territory, Mirandese has adopted a different written norm than the one used in Spain for Astur-Leonese. Lexically, it shares a lot of vocabulary with Portuguese.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">
        Speakers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirandese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Speakers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 19th century,
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Leite_de_Vasconcelos" title="José Leite de Vasconcelos">
        José Leite de Vasconcelos
       </a>
       described it as "the language of the farms, of work, home, and love between the Mirandese". Since 1986–1987 it has been taught to students between the ages of 10 and 11, and so it is recovering.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Today Mirandese retains fewer than 5,000 speakers (but the number can be up to 15,000 if counting second-language speakers) in the villages of the Municipality of
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_do_Douro" title="Miranda do Douro">
        Miranda do Douro
       </a>
       and in some eastern villages (such as Vilar Seco and
       <a href="/wiki/Angueira" title="Angueira">
        Angueira
       </a>
       ; in Caçarelhos, it is considered recently extinct) of the Municipality of
       <a href="/wiki/Vimioso" title="Vimioso">
        Vimioso
       </a>
       , and some linguistic influence can be observed at other villages of the municipality of Vimioso and the municipalities of
       <a href="/wiki/Mogadouro" title="Mogadouro">
        Mogadouro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Macedo_de_Cavaleiros" title="Macedo de Cavaleiros">
        Macedo de Cavaleiros
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a,_Portugal" title="Bragança, Portugal">
        Bragança
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variants">
        Variants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirandese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Variants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Three
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variants
       </a>
       of the Mirandese language exist: Border Mirandese (
       <i>
        Mirandés Raiano
       </i>
       ), Central Mirandese (
       <i>
        Mirandés Central
       </i>
       ) and Sendinese (
       <i>
        Sendinés
       </i>
       ). Most speakers of Mirandese also speak Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>
       The main differences between Mirandese in Portugal and the Astur-Leonese languages in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       are caused by the dominant languages in each region. Mirandese has been influenced phonetically and in lexicon by Portuguese and the Astur-Leonese languages in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       by Spanish (Castilian). All have distinctive orthography that phonetically reflects the respective main national languages. Another difference is that Mirandese and Leonese remain very conservative, while Asturian has undergone a greater amount of change.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">

        </a>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberia" title="Iberia">
         Iberia
        </a>
        .
       </dd>

      <dl>
       <dd>
        Portuguese spelling still distinguishes all seven and is identical to Mirandese spelling in this respect, but in pronunciation, Portuguese has reduced them to four
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s, z, ʃ, ʒ/
        </span>
        except in northern hinterland
        <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
         European Portuguese
        </a>
        dialects, including those of the area that Mirandese is spoken. Northern/central Peninsular Spanish has also reduced them to four but in quite a different way:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t͡ʃ, θ, s̺, x/
        </span>
        .
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian Spanish
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_Spanish" title="Latin American Spanish">
         Latin American Spanish
        </a>
        have further reduced them to three:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t͡ʃ, s̻, h/
        </span>
        .
       </dd>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">

        </a>

        </a>

         /pl/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

         Proto-Romance

        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirandese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As in Portuguese, Mirandese still uses the following synthetic tenses:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Synthetic
        <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">
         pluperfect
        </a>
        in
        <i>
         -ra
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_subjunctive" title="Future subjunctive">
         Future subjunctive
        </a>
        in
        <i>
         -r(e)
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Personal_infinitive&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Personal infinitive (page does not exist)">
         Personal infinitive
        </a>
        in
        <i>
         -r(e)
        </i>
        , which has the same endings as the future subjunctive but often differs because the personal infinitive always uses the infinitive stem, but the future subjunctive uses the past-tense stem.
       </li>
      </ul>

       Then a comparison of the previous text in three modern languages of the Asturo-leonese group:

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="National_attention">
        National attention
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mirandese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: National attention">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miranda09.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2000" data-file-width="3008" height="146" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Miranda09.jpg/220px-Miranda09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Miranda09.jpg/330px-Miranda09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Miranda09.jpg/440px-Miranda09.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miranda09.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Public sign with the history of the
         <a href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Miranda_do_Douro" title="Cathedral of Miranda do Douro">
          Cathedral of Miranda do Douro
         </a>
         , written in Mirandese.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Mirandese, given its status as second official language in Portugal after Portuguese, has been the subject in recent years of some publicity and attention in other parts of Portugal. A monthly chronicle in Mirandese, by researcher and writer Amadeu Ferreira, appears in the daily Portuguese national newspaper
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/P%C3%BAblico_(Portugal)" title="Público (Portugal)">
         Público
        </a>
       </i>
       . The first volume of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Asterix" title="Asterix">
         Adventures of Asterix
        </a>
       </i>
       , named
       <i>
        Asterix, L Goulés
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Asterix_the_Gaul" title="Asterix the Gaul">
         Asterix the Gaul
        </a>
       </i>
       ), was published in a Mirandese translation by Amadeu Ferreira in 2005, and sold throughout Portugal. Amadeu Ferreira also translated into Mirandese the epic poem by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cam%C3%B5es" title="Camões">
        Camões
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Os_Lus%C3%ADadas" title="Os Lusíadas">
         Os Lusíadas
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Ls Lusíadas
       </i>
       ), under his pseudonym Francisco Niebro and published it in 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2011, the four
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gospels" title="Gospels">
        Gospels
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
        Bible
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
        New Testament
       </a>
       were translated into Mirandese, and in 2013 the entire Bible was translated into the language by Domingos Augusto Ferreira.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
