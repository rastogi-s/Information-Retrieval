      <p>
       Some of the regional
       <b>
        varieties of the Spanish language
       </b>
       are quite divergent from one another, especially in pronunciation and vocabulary, and less so in grammar.
      </p>
      <p>
       While all
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       dialects adhere to approximately the same written standard, all spoken varieties differ from the written variety, to different degrees. There are differences between European Spanish (also called
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        Peninsular Spanish
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        Spanish of the Americas
       </a>
       , as well as many different dialect areas both within
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and within
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Prominent differences of pronunciation among dialects of Spanish include:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        the maintenance or loss of distinction between the phonemes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distinci%C3%B3n" title="Distinción">
          distinción
         </a>
        </i>
        vs.
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
          seseo
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
          ceceo
         </a>
        </i>
        );
       </li>
       <li>
        the maintenance or loss of distinction between phonemes represented orthographically by
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          yeísmo
         </a>
        </i>
        );
       </li>
       <li>
        the maintenance of syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        vs. its weakening to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        (called aspiration, or more precisely
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         debuccalization
        </a>
        ), or its loss; and
       </li>
       <li>
        the tendency, in areas of central Mexico and of the Andean highlands, to
        <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
         reduction
        </a>
        (especially
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
         devoicing
        </a>
        ), or loss, of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Unstressed_vowel" title="Unstressed vowel">
         unstressed vowels
        </a>
        , mainly when they are in contact with
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
         voiceless
        </a>
        consonants.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cotton-Sharp_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cotton-Sharp-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Among grammatical features, the most prominent variation among dialects is in the use of the second-person
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        pronouns
       </a>
       . In Hispanic America the only second-person plural pronoun, for both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        formal and informal
       </a>
       treatment, is
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ustedes#Spanish" title="wikt:ustedes">
         ustedes
        </a>
       </i>
       , while in most of Spain the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        informal
       </a>
       second-person plural pronoun is
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vosotros#Spanish" title="wikt:vosotros">
         vosotros
        </a>
       </i>
       with
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ustedes#Spanish" title="wikt:ustedes">
         ustedes
        </a>
       </i>
       used only in the formal treatment. For the second-person
       <i>
        singular
       </i>
       familiar pronoun, some Hispanic America dialects use
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/t%C3%BA#Spanish" title="wikt:tú">
         tú
        </a>
       </i>
       (and its associated
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_verbs" title="Spanish verbs">
        verb forms
       </a>
       ), while others use either
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vos#Spanish" title="wikt:vos">
         vos
        </a>
       </i>
       (see
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       ) or both
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vos#Spanish" title="wikt:vos">
         vos
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       (which, together with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       , can make for a possible three-tiered
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        distinction of formalities
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       There are significant differences in vocabulary among regional varieties of Spanish, particularly in the domains of food products, everyday objects, and clothes; and many Hispanic American varieties show considerable lexical influence from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Native American languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sets_of_variants">
        Sets of variants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Sets of variants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a broad sense, Hispanic American Spanish can be grouped into:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="which author supports this grouping? (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
         Mexican
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Spanish" title="Central American Spanish">
         Central American
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
         Caribbean
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Spanish" title="Cuban Spanish">
         Cuba
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
         Venezuela
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
         Puerto Rico

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">
        Pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Distinci.C3.B3n_vs._seseo_and_ceceo">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distinción_vs._seseo_and_ceceo">
        Distinción vs. seseo and ceceo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Distinción vs. seseo and ceceo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
        Ceceo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The distinction between the historical phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       is maintained in northern Spain (in all positions) and in south-central Spain (only in syllable onset), while the two phonemes are merged in Hispanic America, the Canary Islands, and much of Andalusia. The maintenance of phonemic contrast is called
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       in Spanish. The merged phoneme is typically realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       , though in parts of southern Andalusia the realization is closer to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       ; in Spain this latter variation is called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
         ceceo
        </a>
       </i>
       and the former one
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In dialects with
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       the words
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/casa#Spanish" title="wikt:casa">
         casa
        </a>
       </i>
       ("house") and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caza#Spanish" title="wikt:caza">
         caza
        </a>
       </i>
       ("hunt") are pronounced as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homophones" title="Homophones">
        homophones
       </a>
       (generally
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkasa]
       </span>
       ), whereas in dialects with
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       they are pronounced differently (as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkasa]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkaθa]
       </span>
       respectively). The symbol
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       stands for a voiceless sibilant like the
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       of English
       <i>
        sick
       </i>
       , while
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       represents a voiceless interdental fricative like the
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       of English
       <i>
        thick
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cases where the phonemic merger would render words
       <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
        homophonic
       </a>
       in Hispanic America, one member of the pair is frequently replaced by a
       <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">
        synonym
       </a>
       or derived form—e.g.
       <i>
        caza
       </i>
       replaced by
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cacer%C3%ADa#Spanish" title="wikt:cacería">
         cacería
        </a>
       </i>
       , or
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cocer#Spanish" title="wikt:cocer">
         cocer
        </a>
       </i>
       ("to boil"), homophonic with
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/coser#Spanish" title="wikt:coser">
         coser
        </a>
       </i>
       ("to sew"), replaced by
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cocinar#Spanish" title="wikt:cocinar">
         cocinar
        </a>
       </i>
       . For more on
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       , see González-Bueno.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Ye.C3.ADsmo">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Yeísmo">
        Yeísmo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Yeísmo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        Yeísmo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Traditionally Spanish had a phonemic distinction between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ/
       </span>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
        palatal lateral approximant
       </a>
       , written
       <i>
        ll
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
        voiced palatal fricative
       </a>
       , written
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       ). But for most speakers in Spain and the Americas, these two phonemes have been merged in the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>
       . This merger results in the words
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/call%C3%B3#Spanish" title="wikt:calló">
         calló
        </a>
       </i>
       ("silenced") and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cay%C3%B3#Spanish" title="wikt:cayó">
         cayó
        </a>
       </i>
       ("fell") being pronounced the same, whereas they remain distinct in dialects that have not undergone the merger. The use of the merged phoneme is called "
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       In Spain, the distinction is preserved in some rural areas and smaller cities of the north, while in South America the contrast is characteristic of bilingual areas where
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">
        Quechua languages
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages
       </a>
       that have the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ/
       </span>
       sound in their inventories are spoken (this is the case of inland Peru and Bolivia), and in Paraguay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>
       can be pronounced in a variety of ways, depending on the dialect. In most of the area where
       <i>
        yeísmo
       </i>
       is present, the merged phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ ~ ʝ/
       </span>
       is pronounced as the
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       or as the
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        glide
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">
         j
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , and also, in word-initial positions,
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        glide
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricates
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_affricate" title="Voiced palatal affricate">
         ɟʝ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced palato-alveolar affricate">
         dʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (the latter is also used in other positions as variants of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       ). In the area around the
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Río de la Plata
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       ), this phoneme is pronounced as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Palatoalveolar_consonant" title="Palatoalveolar consonant">
        palatoalveolar
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       fricative, either as voiced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       or, especially by young speakers, as voiceless
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Variants_of_.2Fs.2F">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_of_/s/">
        Variants of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Variants of /s/">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One of the most distinctive features of the Spanish variants is the pronunciation of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       when it is not
       <i>
        aspirated
       </i>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or elided. In northern and central Spain, and in
       <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">
        Paisa Region
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       , as well as some other isolated dialects (e.g. some inland areas of Peru and of Bolivia), the sibilant realization of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_retracted_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant">
        apico-alveolar retracted
       </a>
       fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
         s̺
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , a sound transitional between
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminodental
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . However, in most of Andalusia, in a few other areas in southern Spain, and in most of Hispanic America it is rather pronounced as a
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        lamino-alveolar
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       realization.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is allophonized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative#Voiced_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced alveolar fricative">
         z
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced apico-alveolar sibilant">
         z̺
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       before voiced consonants when it is not
       <i>
        aspirated
       </i>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or elided;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced apico-alveolar sibilant">
         z̺
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       is a sound transitional between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [z]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         ʒ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Debuccalization_of_coda_.2Fs.2F">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Debuccalization_of_coda_/s/">
        Debuccalization of coda
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Debuccalization of coda /s/">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In much of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       —especially in the Caribbean and in coastal and lowland areas of Central and South America—and in the southern half of Spain,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_coda" title="Syllable coda">
        syllable-final
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is either pronounced as a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
        voiceless glottal fricative
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ) (
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       , also frequently called "aspiration"), or not pronounced at all. In some varieties of Hispanic American Spanish (notably Honduran Spanish) this may also occur intervocalically within an individual word, as with
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/nosotros#Spanish" title="wikt:nosotros">
         nosotros
        </a>
       </i>
       . which may be pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [nohotroh]
       </span>
       . In southeastern Spain (eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       and part of
       <a href="/wiki/La_Mancha" title="La Mancha">
        La Mancha
       </a>
       ), the distinction between syllables with a now-silent
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       and those originally without
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       is preserved by pronouncing the syllables ending in
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Open_vowel" title="Open vowel">
        open vowels
       </a>
       (that is, the open/closed syllable contrast has been turned into a tense/lax vowel contrast); this typically affects the vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       , but in some areas even
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       are affected. For instance,
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/todos#Spanish" title="wikt:todos">
         todos
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/los#Spanish" title="wikt:los">
         los
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cisnes#Spanish" title="wikt:cisnes">
         cisnes
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/son#Spanish" title="wikt:son">
         son
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/blancos#Spanish" title="wikt:blancos">
         blancos
        </a>
       </i>
       ("all the swans are white"), can be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈt̪o̞ðo̞ʱ lo̞ʰ ˈθːiʱnːe̞ʰ ˈsõ̞ⁿ ˈblãŋko̞ʰ]
       </span>
       , or even
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈt̪ɔðɔ lɔ ˈθːɪ̃ˠnːɛ ˈsɔ̃ⁿ ˈblãŋkɔ]
       </span>
       (Standard Peninsular Spanish:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈto̞ðoz̠ los̠ ˈθiz̠ne‿ˈs̠ːõ̞m ˈblãŋkos̠]
       </span>
       , Latin American Spanish:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈto̞ðoz lo‿ˈsːizne‿ˈsːõ̞m ˈblãŋkos]
       </span>
       ). This open-closed vowel contrast is sometimes reinforced by
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
        vowel harmony
       </a>
       . For those areas of southeastern Spain where the deletion of final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is complete, and where the distinction between singular and plural of nouns depends entirely on vowel quality, the case has been made to claim that a set of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_split" title="Phonemic split">
        phonemic splits
       </a>
       has occurred, resulting in a system with eight vowel phonemes in place of the standard five.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowel_reduction">
        Vowel reduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Vowel reduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although the vowels of Spanish are relatively stable from one dialect to another, the phenomenon of vowel reduction—devoicing or even loss—of unstressed vowels in contact with voiceless consonants, especially
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       , can be observed in the speech of central Mexico (including Mexico City). For example, it can be the case that the words
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pesos#Spanish" title="wikt:pesos">
         pesos
        </a>
       </i>
       ("pesos [money]"),
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pesas#Spanish" title="wikt:pesas">
         pesas
        </a>
       </i>
       ("weights"), and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/peces#Spanish" title="wikt:peces">
         peces
        </a>
       </i>
       ("fish [pl.]") sound nearly the same, as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpe̞ss̩]
       </span>
       (with the second
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       much like a
       <a href="/wiki/Syllabic_consonant" title="Syllabic consonant">
        syllabic consonant
       </a>
       ). One may hear
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pues#Spanish" title="wikt:pues">
         pues
        </a>
       </i>
       ("well then") pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p̩s]
       </span>
       . Some efforts to explain this vowel reduction link it to the strong influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Native American languages
       </a>
       in Mexican Spanish.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation_of_j">
        Pronunciation of
        <i>
         j
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pronunciation of j">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the 16th century, as the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">
        Spanish colonization of the Americas
       </a>
       was beginning, the phoneme now represented by the letter
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       had begun to change its place of articulation from palato-alveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       to palatal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ç]
       </span>
       and to velar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , like German
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        Bach
       </i>
       (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Spanish#Modern_development_of_the_Old_Spanish_sibilants" title="History of Spanish">
        History of Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish language
       </a>
       ). In southern Spanish dialects and in those Hispanic American dialects strongly influenced by southern settlers (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       ), rather than the velar fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , the result was a softer glottal sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       , like English
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        hope
       </i>
       . Glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       is nowadays the standard pronunciation for
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       in Caribbean dialects (Cuban, Dominican, and Puerto Rican) as well as in mainland Venezuela, much of Central America, southern Mexico,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Canary Islands and western Andalusia in Spain; in the rest of the country,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       alternates with a "raspy" uvular fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [χ]
       </span>
       , sometimes accompanied by uvular vibration.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="vibration? (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In the rest of the Americas, the velar fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       is prevalent. In Chile,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       becomes the more frontal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiceless palatal fricative">
         ç
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (like German
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        ich
       </i>
       ) when it precedes palatal vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i], [e]
       </span>
       :
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/gente#Spanish" title="wikt:gente">
         gente
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈçẽ̞n̪t̪e]
       </span>
       ,
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/jinete#Spanish" title="wikt:jinete">
         jinete
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [çiˈne̞t̪e]
       </span>
       ; in other phonological environments it is pronounced either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Word-final_-n">
        Word-final
        <i>
         -n
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Word-final -n">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In standard European Spanish, as well as in many dialects in the Americas (e.g. standard Argentine or Rioplatense, Colombian, and Mexican), word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       is, by default (i.e. when followed by a pause or by an initial vowel in the following word), alveolar, like English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [n]
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        pen
       </i>
       . When followed by a consonant, it assimilates to that consonant's place of articulation, becoming dental, interdental, palatal, or velar. In some dialects, however, word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       without a following consonant is pronounced as a velar nasal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       (like the
       <i>
        -ng
       </i>
       of English
       <i>
        long
       </i>
       ), and may produce vowel nasalization. In these dialects, words such as
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pan#Spanish" title="wikt:pan">
         pan
        </a>
       </i>
       ('bread') and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bien#Spanish" title="wikt:bien">
         bien
        </a>
       </i>
       ('well') may sound like
       <i>
        pang
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        byeng
       </i>
       to English-speakers. Because of this pronunciation, loanwords based on English words with final
       <i>
        -ng
       </i>
       sound similar to their original pronunciation:
       <i>
        mitin
       </i>
       ('political rally', from Eng.
       <i>
        meeting
       </i>
       ) pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmitiŋ]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        ranking
       </i>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈraŋkiŋ]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        marketing
       </i>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmaɾketiŋ]
       </span>
       ; and
       <i>
        pudín
       </i>
       (Eng.
       <i>
        pudding
       </i>
       ) as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [puˈðiŋ]
       </span>
       . Velar
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       is common in many parts of Spain (Galicia, León, Asturias, Murcia, Extremadura, Andalusia, and Canary Islands). In the Americas, velar
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       is prevalent in all Caribbean dialects, Central American dialects, the coastal areas of Colombia, Venezuela, much of Ecuador, Peru, and northern Chile.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Loss of final
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       with strong nasalization of the preceding vowel is not infrequent in all those dialects where velar
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       exists. In much of Ecuador, Peru, Venezuela (except for the Andean region) and Dominican Spanish, any pre-consonantal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m/
       </span>
       can be realized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       ; thus, a word like
       <i>
        ambientación
       </i>
       can be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ãŋbjẽ̞ŋt̪aˈsjõ̞ŋ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="R_sounds">
        <i>
         R
        </i>
        sounds
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: R sounds">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       All varieties of Spanish distinguish between a "single-R" and a "double-R" phoneme. The single-R phoneme corresponds to the letter
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       written once (except when word-initial or following
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       ) and is pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       , an alveolar
       <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" title="Flap consonant">
        flap
       </a>
       —like American English
       <i>
        tt
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        better
       </i>
       —in virtually all dialects. The single-R/double-R contrast is
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme#Neutralization_and_archiphonemes" title="Phoneme">
        neutralized
       </a>
       in syllable-final position, and in some dialects these phonemes also lose their contrast with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       , so a word such as
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/artesan%C3%ADa#Spanish" title="wikt:artesanía">
         artesanía
        </a>
       </i>
       may sound like
       <i>
        altesanía
       </i>
       . This neutralization or "leveling" of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       is frequent in dialects of southern Spain, the Caribbean, Venezuela and coastal Colombia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The double-R phoneme is spelled
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       between vowels (as in
       <i>
        ca
        <b>
         rr
        </b>
        o
       </i>
       'car') and
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       word-initially (e.g.
       <i>
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ey
       </i>
       'king',
       <i>
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        opa
       </i>
       'clothes') or following
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       (e.g.
       <i>
        al
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ededor
       </i>
       'around',
       <i>
        en
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        iquecer
       </i>
       'enrich',
       <i>
        en
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ollar
       </i>
       'roll up',
       <i>
        hon
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        a
       </i>
       'honor',
       <i>
        Is
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ael
       </i>
       'Israel'). In most varieties it is pronounced as an alveolar
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       , and that is considered the prestige pronunciation. Two notable variants occur additionally: one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       and the other
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_consonant" title="Uvular consonant">
        uvular
       </a>
       . The trill is also found in lexical derivations (morpheme-initial positions), and prefixation with
       <i>
        sub
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ab
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        abrogado
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aβ.roˈɣa(ð)o]
       </span>
       (also pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aɸ.roˈɣa(ð)o]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˠ.rːoˈɣa(ð)o]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aʱ.rːoˈɣa(ð)o]
       </span>
       ), 'abrogated',
       <i>
        subrayar
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [suβ.raˈjar]
       </span>
       (also pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [suɸ.raˈjar]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [suˠ.raˈjar]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [suʱ.raˈjar]
       </span>
       ), 'to stress'. The same goes for compound word
       <i>
        ciudad
        <b>
         r
        </b>
        ealeño
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        Ciudad Real
       </i>
       ) However, after vowels, the initial
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       of the root becomes
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       in prefixed or compound words:
       <i>
        prorrogar, infrarrojo, autorretrato, puertorriqueño, Monterrey
       </i>
       . In syllable-final position, inside a word, the tap is more frequent, but the trill can also occur (especially in emphatic
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or oratorical
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       style) with no semantic difference, especially before
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       —thus
       <i>
        arma
       </i>
       ('weapon') may be either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈaɾma]
       </span>
       (tap) or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈarma]
       </span>
       (trill),
       <i>
        perla
       </i>
       ('pearl') may be either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpe̞ɾla]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpe̞rla]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        invierno
       </i>
       ('winter') may be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ĩmˈbje̞ɾno]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ĩmˈbje̞rno]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        verso
       </i>
       ("verse") may be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞ɾso]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞rso]
       </span>
       , and
       <i>
        verde
       </i>
       ("green")
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞ɾðe]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞rðe]
       </span>
       . In
       <i>
        word
       </i>
       -final position the rhotic will usually be: either a trill or a tap when followed by a consonant or a pause, as in
       <i>
        amo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r ~ ɾ]
       </span>
       <i>
        paterno
       </i>
       'paternal love'),
       <i>
        amo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r ~ ɾ]
       </span>
       ; or a tap when the followed by a vowel-initial word, as in
       <i>
        amo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       <i>
        eterno
       </i>
       'eternal love').
      </p>
      <p>
       The pronunciation of the double-R phoneme as a voiced strident (or sibilant)
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       is common in New Mexico, Guatemala, and Costa Rica; in highland areas of Colombia, Ecuador, Bolivia, and Chile; in western and northern Argentina, and in Paraguay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some linguists have attempted to explain the
       <a href="/wiki/Assibilation" title="Assibilation">
        assibilated
       </a>
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       as a result of influence from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Native American languages
       </a>
       , and it is true that in the Andean regions mentioned an important part of the population is bilingual in Spanish and one or another indigenous language. Nonetheless, other researchers have pointed out that sibilant
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       in the Americas may not be an autonomous innovation, but rather a pronunciation that originated in some northern Spanish dialects and then was exported to the Americas. Spanish dialects spoken in the Basque Country, Navarre, La Rioja, and northern Aragon
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       (regions that contributed substantially to Spanish-American colonization) show the fricative or
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       variant for
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       (especially for the word-initial
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       sound, as in
       <i>
        Roma
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        rey
       </i>
       ). In Andean regions, the alveolar trill is realized as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
        alveolar approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɹ]
       </span>
       or even as a voiced apico-alveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       , and it is quite common in inland Ecuador, Peru, most of Bolivia and in parts of northern Argentina and Paraguay. The alveolar approximant realization is particularly associated with a substrate from Native American languages. In Ecuador and Bolivia,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       are assibilated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Raised_alveolar_trill" title="Raised alveolar trill">
         r̝
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_tapped_fricative" title="Voiced alveolar tapped fricative">
         ɾ̞
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , respectively; also particularly associated with a substrate from Native American languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       The other major variant for the
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       phoneme—common in Puerto Rico and the Dominican Republic—is articulated at the back of the mouth, either as a glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       followed by a voiceless apical trill or, especially in Puerto Rico, with a posterior articulation that ranges variously "from a velar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       to a uvular trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʀ]
       </span>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Canfield transcribes the sound as uppercase
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [R]
       </span>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Ring_(diacritic)#Ring_below" title="Ring (diacritic)">
        ring
       </a>
       below,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [◌̥]
       </span>
       , evidently for a voiceless trill.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These realizations for
       <i>
        rr
       </i>
       maintain their contrast with the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       , as the latter tends to be realized as a soft glottal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       : compare
       <i>
        Ramón
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [xaˈmõ̞ŋ] ~ [ʀ̥aˈmõ̞ŋ]
       </span>
       ("Raymond") with
       <i>
        jamón
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [haˈmõ̞ŋ]
       </span>
       ("ham").
      </p>
      <p>
       In Puerto Rico, syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       can be realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         ɹ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (probably an influence of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       ), aside from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       , so that
       <i>
        verso
       </i>
       ("verse") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞ɹso]
       </span>
       , alongside
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞ɾso]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞rso]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbe̞lso]
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        invierno
       </i>
       ("winter") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ĩmˈbje̞ɹno]
       </span>
       , alongside
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ĩmˈbje̞ɾno]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ĩmˈbje̞rno]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [imˈbje̞lno]
       </span>
       ; and
       <i>
        parlamento
       </i>
       (parliament) becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [paɹlaˈmento]
       </span>
       , aside from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [paɾlaˈmento]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [parlaˈmento]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>
       . In word-final position, the realization of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>

      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Before a consonant or pause: a trill, a tap, an approximant, the lateral
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [l]
        </span>
        , or elided, as in
        <i>
         amo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        Belize

        Archipelago of San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina
       </a>
       , an influence of British English.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Paraguay, syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       is pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         ɹ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       before
       <i>
        l
       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

      </p>
      <p>
       In Chile, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /rn/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <i>

       </i>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [xoˈn̪ːaː]

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [hoˈn̪ːaː]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation_of_x">
        Pronunciation of
        <i>
         x
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Pronunciation of x">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The letter
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       usually represents the phoneme sequence
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       . An exception to the correspondence of
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       is the pronunciation of the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       in some place names, especially in Mexico, such as
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Oaxaca#Spanish" title="wikt:Oaxaca">
         Oaxaca
        </a>
       </i>
       and the name
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/M%C3%A9xico#Spanish" title="wikt:México">
         México
        </a>
       </i>
       itself, reflecting an older spelling (see
       <a href="/wiki/Name_of_Mexico#Phonetic_evolution" title="Name of Mexico">
        "Name of Mexico"
       </a>
       ). Some personal names, such as
       <i>
        Javier
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Jiménez
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Rojas
       </i>
       , etc., also are occasionally spelled with
       <i>
        X
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        Xavier
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Ximénez
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Roxas
       </i>
       , etc. and the pronunciation of
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       . A small number of words in Mexican Spanish retain the historical
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       pronunciation, e.g.
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Mexica#Spanish" title="wikt:Mexica">
         Mexica
        </a>
       </i>
       . In emphatic speech,
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ks]
       </span>
       , but in casual speech the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , being syllable-final, can
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        weaken
       </a>
       to a voiced fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or disappear altogether, especially when the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is followed by a consonant; the remaining
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       will be
       <i>
        aspirated
       </i>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or elided in dialects with this trait. The tendency to delete the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       element is generally stronger in Spain than in the Spanish of Hispanic America.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       When the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is followed by
       <i>
        ce
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ci
       </i>
       (e.g.
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/excelente#Spanish" title="wikt:excelente">
         excelente
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/excitar#Spanish" title="wikt:excitar">
         excitar
        </a>
       </i>
       ), in dialects using
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       (see above), the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       sequence merges with the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       that corresponds to the letter
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       , so
       <i>
        excelente
       </i>
       may be pronounced either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞kseˈlẽ̞nte]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞ɣseˈlẽ̞nte]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞ˠsːeˈlẽ̞n̪t̪e]
       </span>
       . But in those areas of northern and central Spain that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distinci%C3%B3n" title="Distinción">
        distinguish between the phonemes
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       , when the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is not aspirated to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       or elided,
       <i>
        excelente
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞s̺θeˈlẽ̞n̪t̪e]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞ɣs̺θeˈlẽ̞n̪t̪e]
       </span>
       . If the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is followed by consonants, it may be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       or may merge as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (e.g.
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/exclamar#Spanish" title="wikt:exclamar">
         exclamar
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞ksklaˈmar]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞(ɣ)sklaˈmar]
       </span>
       ); the latter pronunciation is true, because syllable-final position cannot be more than one consonant in informal sociolects, and in many cases, it is allowed even in the formal sociolects. Dialects that practice
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       of syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (see above) treat the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       of a syllable-final (letter)
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       in the same way, so that
       <i>
        exclamar
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞ˠkːlaˈmar/ɹ/l] ~ [ɛˠkːlaˈma(r)]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="very dubious that this pronunciation is usual (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adoption_of_the_affricates_tz_and_tl">
        Adoption of the affricates
        <i>
         tz
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tl
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Adoption of the affricates tz and tl">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       and some other Hispanic American dialects have adopted from the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        native languages
       </a>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
        voiceless alveolar affricate
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡s]
       </span>
       and the cluster
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tl]
       </span>
       (originally
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tɬ]
       </span>
       ) represented by the respective digraphs &lt;tz&gt; and &lt;tl&gt;, as in the names
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atzcapotzalco" title="Atzcapotzalco">
        Atzcapotzalco
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tlaxcala" title="Tlaxcala">
        Tlaxcala
       </a>
       . In these dialects, even words of Greek and Latin origin with &lt;tl&gt;, such as
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Atl%C3%A1ntico#Spanish" title="wikt:Atlántico">
         Atlántico
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/atleta#Spanish" title="wikt:atleta">
         atleta
        </a>
       </i>
       , are pronounced with the affricate:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈtlãn̪t̪iko̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈtle̞t̪a]
       </span>
       (compare
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aðˈle̞t̪a]
       </span>
       in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ). The
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡s]
       </span>
       sound also occurs in European Spanish in loanwords of Basque origin (but only learned loanwords, not those inherited from Roman times), as in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Abertzale" title="Abertzale">
         abertzale
        </a>
       </i>
       . In colloquial Castilian it may be replaced by
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivian_Spanish" title="Bolivian Spanish">
        Bolivian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_Spanish" title="Paraguayan Spanish">
        Paraguayan
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish" title="Peruvian Ribereño Spanish">
        Coastal Peruvian Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡s]
       </span>
       also occurs in loanwords of Japanese origin.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_loaned_phonetics">
        Other loaned phonetics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other loaned phonetics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish has a fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       for loanwords of
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        origins from native languages
       </a>
       in Mexican Spanish, loanwords of French, German and English origin in Chilean Spanish, loanwords of Italian, Galician, French, German and English origin in Rioplatense Spanish and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
        Venezuelan Spanish
       </a>
       , Chinese loanwords in
       <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish" title="Peruvian Ribereño Spanish">
        Coastal Peruvian Spanish
       </a>
       , Japanese loanwords in Bolivian Spanish, Paraguayan Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Peruvian_Ribere%C3%B1o_Spanish" title="Peruvian Ribereño Spanish">
        Coastal Peruvian Spanish
       </a>
       , Basque loanwords in Castilian Spanish (but only learned loanwords, not those inherited from Roman times), and English loanwords in
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
        Puerto Rican Spanish
       </a>
       and all dialects.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="entire section (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation_of_ch">
        Pronunciation of
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pronunciation of ch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Spanish digraph
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">
         t͡ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       .
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡ʃ/
       </span>
       is pronounced as a fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in some
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian
       </a>
       dialects,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexican_Spanish" title="New Mexican Spanish">
        New Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       , informal
       <a href="/wiki/Panamanian_Spanish" title="Panamanian Spanish">
        Panamanian Spanish
       </a>
       , and informal
       <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Spanish" title="Chilean Spanish">
        Chilean Spanish
       </a>
       . This type of pronunciation is viewed as undesirable in Chilean, while this is also pronounced by educated speakers of Panamanian dialect. This is pronounced as
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       affricate,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant affricate">
         t͡s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in Madrid and among upper and middle class Chilean speakers.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Open-mid_vowels">
        Open-mid vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Open-mid vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some dialects of southeastern Spain (Murcia, eastern Andalusia and a few adjoining areas) where the weakening of final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       leads to its disappearance, the "silent"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       continues to have an effect on the preceding vowel,
       <a href="/wiki/Open-mid_vowel" title="Open-mid vowel">
        opening the mid vowels
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɔ]
       </span>
       respectively, and fronting the low vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       toward
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [æ]
       </span>
       . Thus the singular/plural distinction in nouns and adjectives is maintained by means of the vowel quality:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         libro
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾo]
        </span>
        'book', but
        <i>
         libros
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾɔ]
        </span>
        'books'.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         libre
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾe̝ ]
        </span>
        'free,
        <i>
         singular
        </i>
        ', but
        <i>
         libres
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾɛ]
        </span>
        'free,
        <i>
         plural'
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         libra
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾa]
        </span>
        'pound', but
        <i>
         libras
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈli βɾæ]
        </span>
        'pounds'.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hualde_2008_130_20-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Hualde_2008_130-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Furthermore, this opening of final mid vowels can affect other vowels earlier in the word, as an instance of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
        vowel harmony
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         lobo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈlo βo]
        </span>
        'wolf', but
        <i>
         lobos
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈlɔ βɔ]
        </span>
        'wolves'.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hualde_2008_130_20-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Hualde_2008_130-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       (In the remaining dialects, the mid vowels have nondistinctive open and closed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allophones" title="Allophones">
        allophones
       </a>
       determined by the shape of the syllable or by contact with neighboring phonemes. See
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology#Exact_number_of_allophones" title="Spanish phonology">
        Spanish phonology
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Judaeo-Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Judaeo-Spanish (often called
       <i>
        Ladino
       </i>
       ) refers to the Romance dialects spoken by Jews whose ancestors were
       <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">
        expelled from Spain near the end of the 15th century
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       These dialects have important phonological differences compared to varieties of Spanish proper; for example, they have preserved the
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiced/voiceless distinction
       </a>
       among
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
        sibilants
       </a>
       as they were in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       . For this reason, the letter
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       , when written single between vowels, corresponds to a voiced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [z]
       </span>
       —e.g.
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rosa#Spanish" title="wikt:rosa">
         rosa
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈro̞za]
       </span>
       ("rose"). Where
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       is not between vowels and is not followed by a voiced consonant, or when it is written double, it corresponds to voiceless
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       —thus
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/assentarse#Spanish" title="wikt:assentarse">
         assentarse
        </a>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [asẽ̞nˈtarse̞]
       </span>
       ("to sit down"). And due to a phonemic neutralization similar to the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       of other dialects, the Old Spanish voiced
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dz]
       </span>
       and the voiceless
       <i>
        ç
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ts]
       </span>
       have merged, respectively, with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       —while maintaining the voicing contrast between them. Thus
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/fazer#Spanish" title="wikt:fazer">
         fazer
        </a>
       </i>
       ("to make") has gone from the medieval
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [faˈdze̞r]
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [faˈze̞r]
       </span>
       , and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pla%C3%A7a#Spanish" title="wikt:plaça">
         plaça
        </a>
       </i>
       ("town square") has gone from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈplatsa]
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈplasa]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A related dialect is
       <a href="/wiki/Haketia" title="Haketia">
        Haketia
       </a>
       , the Judaeo-Spanish of northern Morocco. This too tended to assimilate with modern Spanish, during the Spanish occupation of the region.
       <a href="/wiki/Tetuani_Ladino" title="Tetuani Ladino">
        Tetuani Ladino
       </a>
       was brought to
       <a href="/wiki/Oran" title="Oran">
        Oran
       </a>
       in Algeria.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variation_in_second-person_pronouns_and_verbs">
        Variation in second-person pronouns and verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Variation in second-person pronouns and verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish is a language with a "
       <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
        T–V distinction
       </a>
       " in the second person, meaning that there are different pronouns corresponding to "you" which express different degrees of formality. In most varieties, there are two degrees, namely "formal" and "familiar" (the latter is also called "informal").
      </p>
      <p>
       For the second person
       <i>
        formal
       </i>
       , virtually all Spanish dialects of Spain and the Americas use
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/usted#Spanish" title="wikt:usted">
         usted
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ustedes#Spanish" title="wikt:ustedes">
         ustedes
        </a>
       </i>
       (singular and plural respectively). But for the second person
       <i>
        familiar
       </i>
       , there is regional variation—between
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/t%C3%BA#Spanish" title="wikt:tú">
         tú
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vos#Spanish" title="wikt:vos">
         vos
        </a>
       </i>
       for the singular, and, separately, between
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vosotros#Spanish" title="wikt:vosotros">
         vosotros
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ustedes#Spanish" title="wikt:ustedes">
         ustedes
        </a>
       </i>
       for the plural. The use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (and its corresponding verb forms) rather than
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Each of the second-person pronouns has its historically corresponding verb forms, used by most speakers. Most
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/voseo#Spanish" title="wikt:voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       speakers use both the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and its historically corresponding verb forms (e.g.
       <i>
        vos
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ten%C3%A9s#Spanish" title="wikt:tenés">
         tenés
        </a>
       </i>
       , "you have"). But some dialects use the pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       with "
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       verb forms" (verbal
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       —
       <i>
        tú
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ten%C3%A9s#Spanish" title="wikt:tenés">
         tenés
        </a>
       </i>
       ), while others use
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       with "
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       verb forms" (pronominal
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       —
       <i>
        vos
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tienes#Spanish" title="wikt:tienes">
         tienes
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_person_singular">
        Second person singular
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Second person singular">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In most dialects the familiar second person singular pronoun is
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       (from Latin
       <i>
        tū
       </i>
       ), and the formal pronoun is
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (usually considered to originate from
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vuestra#Spanish" title="wikt:vuestra">
         vuestra
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/merced#Spanish" title="wikt:merced">
         merced
        </a>
       </i>
       , meaning "your grace" or, literally, "your mercy"). In a number of regions in the Americas,
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is replaced by another pronoun,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , and the verb conjugation changes accordingly (see details below). Spanish
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       comes from Latin
       <i>
        vōs
       </i>
       , the second person
       <i>
        plural
       </i>
       pronoun in Latin.
      </p>
      <p>
       In any case, there is wide variation as to when each pronoun (formal or familiar) is used. In Spain,
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is familiar (for example, used with friends), and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is formal (for example, used with older people). In recent times, there has been a noticeable tendency to extend the use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       even in situations previously reserved for
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . Meanwhile, in several countries (in parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_America_(Americas)" title="Middle America (Americas)">
        Middle America
       </a>
       , especially, Costa Rica and Colombia), the formal
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is also used to denote a closer personal relationship. Many Colombians and some Chileans, for instance, use
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       for a child to address a parent and also for a parent to address a child. Some countries, such as Cuba and the Dominican Republic, prefer the use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       even in very formal circumstances, and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is seldom used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Meanwhile, in other countries, the use of formal rather than familiar second-person pronouns denotes authority. In Peru, for example, senior military officers use
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       to speak to their subordinates, but junior officers use only
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       to address their superior officers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Using the familiar
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       , especially in contexts where
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       was to be expected, is called
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tuteo#Spanish" title="wikt:tuteo">
         tuteo
        </a>
       </i>
       . The corresponding verb is
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tutear#Spanish" title="wikt:tutear">
         tutear
        </a>
       </i>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">
        transitive verb
       </a>
       , the direct object being the person addressed with the pronoun). The verb
       <i>
        tutear
       </i>
       is used even in those dialects whose familiar pronoun is
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and means "to treat with the familiar second-person pronoun".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Pronominal
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       , the use of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       , is the prevalent form of the familiar second person singular pronoun in Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, Costa Rica, Paraguay, Argentina and Uruguay. In those countries, it is used by many to address others in all kinds of contexts, often regardless of social status or age, including by cultured/educated speakers and writers, in television, advertisements, and even in translations from other languages. In Guatemala and Uruguay,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       are used concurrently, but
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is much more common. Both pronouns use the verb forms normally associated with
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        vos
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quer%C3%A9s#Spanish" title="wikt:querés">
         querés
        </a>
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        tú querés
       </i>
       , "you want").
      </p>
      <p>
       The name
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
         Rioplatense
        </a>
       </i>
       is applied to the dialect of Spanish spoken around the mouth of the Río de la Plata and the lower course of the Paraná River, where
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , not
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       , is invariably used, with the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       verb forms (
       <i>
        vos tenés
       </i>
       ). The area comprises the most populous part of Argentina (the provinces of Buenos Aires and Santa Fe) as well as an important part of Uruguay, including Montevideo, the capital.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Ecuador,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is the most prominent form throughout the Sierra region of the country, though it does coexist with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       and the lesser-used
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . In this region,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is regarded as the conversational norm, but it is not used in public discourse or the mass media. The choice of pronoun to be used depends on the participants' likeness in age and/or social status. Based on these factors, speakers can assess themselves as being equal, superior, or inferior to the addressee, and the choice of pronoun is made on this basis, sometimes resulting in a three-tiered system. Ecuadorians of the Highlands thus generally use
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       among familiarized equals or by superiors (in both social status and age) to inferiors;
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       among unfamiliarized equals, or by a superior in age but inferior in social status; and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       by both familiarized and unfamiliarized inferiors, or by a superior in social status but inferior in age. In the more-populated coastal region, the form
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is used in most situations,
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       being used only for unfamiliar and/or superior addressees.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       can be heard throughout most of Chile, Bolivia, and a small part of Peru as well, but in these places it is regarded as substandard. It is also used as the conversational norm in the
       <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">
        Paisa Region
       </a>
       and the southwest region of Colombia, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zulia_State" title="Zulia State">
        Zulia State
       </a>
       (Venezuela), in Honduras, El Salvador, Costa Rica, Guatemala, and the state of Chiapas in Mexico.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Chile, even though
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is the prestige pronoun among educated speakers, the use of "verbal
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       ", i.e. «
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       + verb conjugation of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       » (e.g. "tú
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pod%C3%ADs#Spanish" title="wikt:podís">
        podís
       </a>
       ") is widespread. On the other hand, "pronominal voseo", the use of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       —pronounced with
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        aspiration
       </a>
       of the final
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       —is used derisively in informal speech between close friends as playful banter (usually among men) or, depending on the tone of voice, as an offensive comment.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Colombia, the choice of second person singular varies with location. In most of inland Colombia (especially the
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andean
       </a>
       region),
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is the pronoun of choice for all situations, even in speaking between friends or family; but in large cities (especially
       <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">
        Bogotá
       </a>
       ), the use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is becoming more accepted in informal situations, especially between young interlocutors of opposite sexes and among young women. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca" title="Valle del Cauca">
        Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Cali" title="Cali">
        Cali
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Medellín
       </a>
       ) and the Pacific coast, the pronouns used are
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . On the Caribbean coast (mainly
       <a href="/wiki/Barranquilla" title="Barranquilla">
        Barranquilla
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">
        Cartagena
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is used for practically all informal situations and many formal situations as well,
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       being reserved for the most formal environments. A peculiarity occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Boyac%C3%A1_Department" title="Boyacá Department">
        Boyacá
       </a>
       and among older speakers in Bogotá:
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sumerc%C3%A9#Spanish" title="wikt:sumercé">
         sumercé
        </a>
       </i>
       for formal situations (it is relatively easy to identify a
       <i>
        Boyacense
       </i>
       by his/her use of this pronoun).
       <i>
        Sumercé
       </i>
       comes from
       <i>
        su merced
       </i>
       ("your mercy").
      </p>
      <p>
       In parts of Spain, a child used to use not
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       but
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       to address a parent. Today, however, this usage is unusual. Among the factors for the ongoing replacement of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       by
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       are the new social relevance of youth and the reduction of social differences. In particular, it has been attributed to the egalitarianism of the right-wing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falange" title="Falange">
        Falange
       </a>
       party. By contrast, Spanish leftists of the early 20th century would address their comrades as
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       as a show of respect and workers' dignity.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Joan_Coromines" title="Joan Coromines">
        Joan Coromines
       </a>
       , by the 16th century, the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (as a second person
       <i>
        singular
       </i>
       pronoun) had been reduced to rural areas of Spain, which were a source of many emigrants to the New World, and so
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       became the unmarked form in many areas of Hispanic America.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A slightly different explanation is that in Spain, even if
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       (as a singular) originally denoted the high social status of those who were addressed as such (monarchs, nobility, etc.), the people never used the pronoun themselves since there were few or no people above them in society. Those who used
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       were people of the lower classes and peasants. When the waves of Spanish immigrants arrived to populate the New World, they primarily came from these lower classes. In the New World, wanting to raise their social status from what it was in Spain, they demanded to be addressed as
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Through the widespread use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       in the Americas, the pronoun was transformed into an indicator of low status not only for the addresser but also for the addressee. Conversely, in Spain,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is now considered a highly exalted archaism virtually confined to liturgy.
      </p>
      <p>
       Speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       still use
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       as it was used in the Middle Ages, to address people higher on the social ladder. The pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       had not been introduced to this dialect of Spanish when the Jews were expelled from Spain in 1492 so
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is still used in Ladino much as
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is used in modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       A variant of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vusted
       </i>
       , can be heard in Andean regions of South America. Other, less frequent forms analogous to
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       are
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vuecencia#Spanish" title="wikt:vuecencia">
         vuecencia
        </a>
       </i>
       (short for "
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vuestra#Spanish" title="wikt:vuestra">
        vuestra
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/excelencia#Spanish" title="wikt:excelencia">
        excelencia
       </a>
       "), and
       <i>
        usía
       </i>
       (from "vuestra
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/se%C3%B1or%C3%ADa#Spanish" title="wikt:señoría">
        señoría
       </a>
       ").
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a traditional assumption that Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms are derived from those corresponding to
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       . This assumption, however, has been challenged, in an article by
       <a href="#CITEREFBaqueroWestphal2014">
        Baquero &amp; Westphal (2014)
       </a>
       —in the theoretical framework of classical
       <a href="/wiki/Generative_grammar" title="Generative grammar">
        generative phonology
       </a>
       —as
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrony_and_diachrony" title="Synchrony and diachrony">
        synchronically
       </a>
       inadequate, on the grounds that it requires at least six different rules, including three
       <a href="/wiki/Monophthongization" title="Monophthongization">
        monophthongization
       </a>
       processes that lack phonological motivation. Alternatively, the article argues that the Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms are synchronically derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Underlying_representations" title="Underlying representations">
        underlying representations
       </a>
       that coincide with those corresponding to the
       <a href="/wiki/Honorifics_(linguistics)" title="Honorifics (linguistics)">
        non-honorific
       </a>
       second person singular
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . The proposed theory requires the use of only one special rule in the case of Chilean
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       . This rule—along with other rules that are independently justified in the language—makes it possible to derive synchronically all Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms in a straightforward manner. The article additionally solves the problem posed by the alternate verb forms of Chilean
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       such as the future indicative (e.g.
       <i>
        vay a bailar
       </i>
       "you will dance"), the present indicative forms of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        habih
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        hai
       </i>
       "you have"), and the present indicative of
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        soi
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eríh
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        erei
       </i>
       "you are"), without resorting to any ad hoc rules.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_person_plural">
        Second person plural
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Second person plural">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In Standard European Spanish the plural of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and the plural of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       . In Hispanic America
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is not used, and the plural of both
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       . This means that speaking to a group of friends a Spaniard will use
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       , while a Hispanic American Spanish-speaker will use
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       . Although
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is semantically a second-person form, it is treated grammatically as a third-person plural form because it originates from the term
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vuestras#Spanish" title="wikt:vuestras">
         vuestras
        </a>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mercedes#Spanish" title="wikt:mercedes">
         mercedes
        </a>
       </i>
       ("your [pl.] graces," sing.
       <i>
        vuestra merced
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The only vestiges of
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       in the Americas are
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/boso#Spanish" title="wikt:boso">
         boso
        </a>
       </i>
       /
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/bosonan#Spanish" title="wikt:bosonan">
         bosonan
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </a>
       and the use of
       <i>
        vuestro/a
       </i>
       in place of
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sus#Spanish" title="wikt:sus">
         sus
        </a>
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/de#Spanish" title="wikt:de">
         de
        </a>
        ustedes)
       </i>
       as second person plural possessive in the Cusco region of Peru.
      </p>
      <p>
       In very formal contexts, however, the
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       conjugation can still be found. An example is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexican_national_anthem" title="Mexican national anthem">
        Mexican national anthem
       </a>
       , which contains such forms as
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/apretad#Spanish" title="wikt:apretad">
         apretad
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/empapad#Spanish" title="wikt:empapad">
         empapad
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The plural of the Colombian (Cundi-Boyacense Plateau)
       <i>
        sumercé
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        sumercés/susmercedes
       </i>
       , from
       <i>
        sus mercedes
       </i>
       ("your mercies").
      </p>
      <p>
       In some parts of Andalusia (the lands around the
       <a href="/wiki/Guadalquivir" title="Guadalquivir">
        Guadalquivir
       </a>
       river and western Andalusia), the usage is what is called
       <i>
        ustedes-vosotros
       </i>
       : the pronoun
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is combined with the verb forms for
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       . However, this sounds extremely colloquial and most Andalusians prefer to use each pronoun with its correct form.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is still the only second person plural pronoun, since
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       does not exist.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Second-person_verb_forms">
        Second-person verb forms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Second-person verb forms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Each second-person pronoun has its historically corresponding verb forms. The formal
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       , although semantically second person, take verb forms identical with those of the third person, singular and plural respectively, since they are derived from the third-person expressions
       <i>
        vuestra merced
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vuestras mercedes
       </i>
       ("your grace", "your graces"). The forms associated with the singular
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       can generally be derived from those for the plural
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       by deleting the palatal
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        semivowel
       </a>
       of the ending (
       <i>
        vosotros hablá
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        s
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        vos hablás
       </i>
       , "you speak";
       <i>
        vosotros comé
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        s
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        vos comés
       </i>
       , "you eat").
      </p>
      <p>
       General statements about the use of
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       in different localities should be qualified by the note that individual speakers may be inconsistent in their usage, and that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isoglosses" title="Isoglosses">
        isoglosses
       </a>
       rarely coincide with national borders. That said, a few assertions can be made:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "Full"
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        (involving both pronoun and verb—
        <i>
         vos comés
        </i>
        , "you eat") is characteristic of two zones: that of Argentina, Paraguay and Uruguay and that of Central America and the Mexican state of Chiapas.
       </li>
       <li>
        "Full"
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        coexists with the use of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        and its verb forms (e.g.
        <i>
         tú comes
        </i>
        ) in Colombia and Ecuador, and in parts of Colombia also with
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        (with its standard verb forms) as a familiar form.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Chile there is coexistence of three usages:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           tú
          </i>
          and its verb forms (
          <i>
           tú comes
          </i>
          );
         </li>
         <li>
          "full"
          <i>
           voseo
          </i>
          with uniquely Chilean
          <i>
           voseo
          </i>
          verb endings (
          <i>
           -ái
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           -ís
          </i>
          , and
          <i>
           -ís
          </i>
          respectively for
          <i>
           -ar
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           -er
          </i>
          , and
          <i>
           -ir
          </i>
          verbs:
          <i>
           vos hablái
          </i>
          —"you speak",
          <i>
           vos comís
          </i>
          —"you eat",
          <i>
           vos vivís
          </i>
          —"you live"); and
         </li>
         <li>
          verbal
          <i>
           voseo
          </i>
          with the Chilean verb endings (
          <i>
           tú hablái
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           tú comís
          </i>
          , etc.).
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        "Full"
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        coexists with verbal
        <i>
         voseo
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         tú comés
        </i>
        ) in Uruguay.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Venezuela's
        <a href="/wiki/Zulia" title="Zulia">
         Zulia
        </a>
        State and parts of the state of
        <a href="/wiki/Falc%C3%B3n" title="Falcón">
         Falcón
        </a>
        there is no deletion of the palatal semivowel, so you have "vos coméis", "vos habláis", "vos seáis". In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trujillo_State" title="Trujillo State">
         Trujillo State
        </a>
        , the "voseo" is like that of Argentina, with the exception of the imperative mood, which is like that of the standard
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
         <a href="#cite_note-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Voseo
        </i>
        is absent from the Spanish of Spain, and from most of Mexico, Peru, and the islands of the Caribbean.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       As for the second person familiar
       <i>
        plural
       </i>
       , it can be said that northern and central Spain use
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and its verb forms (
       <i>
        vosotros habláis
       </i>
       , "you [pl.] speak"), while the rest of the Spanish-speaking world merges the familiar and formal in
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        ustedes hablan
       </i>
       ). Usage in western Andalusia includes the use of
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       with the traditional
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       verb form (
       <i>
        ustedes habláis
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       , the second-person pronouns are quite different from those of Spain and Hispanic America. The forms
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       had not yet appeared in 1492 when the Jews were expelled from Spain. Speakers of Ladino still use
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       as it was used in the Middle Ages (as a singular) to address people higher on the social ladder. And
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is the only second person plural pronoun. In Ladino the formal singular for "you speak" is
       <i>
        vos avláis
       </i>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈvlaʃ]
       </span>
       , and the same verb form serves for the plural, both formal and familiar:
       <i>
        vosotros avláis
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [voˈzotros aˈvlaʃ]
       </span>
       ). The subjunctive "that you lose" (formal singular) is
       <i>
        que vos pedráis
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ke vos peˈdraʃ]
       </span>
       ), while the plural (both formal and familiar) is
       <i>
        que vosotros pedráis
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ke voˈzotros peˈdraʃ]
       </span>
       ). The formal
       <i>
        singular
       </i>
       imperative ("come") is
       <i>
        venid
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        vení
       </i>
       , and the same form serves as the
       <i>
        plural
       </i>
       imperative, both formal and familiar.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verb_tenses_for_past_events">
        Verb tenses for past events
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Verb tenses for past events">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a broad sense, when expressing an action viewed as finished in the past, speakers (and writers) in most of Spain use the perfect tense—e.g.
       <i>
        he llegado
       </i>
       "I have arrived"—more often than their Hispanic American counterparts, while Spanish-speakers in the Americas more often use the preterite (
       <i>
        llegué
       </i>
       "I arrived").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">
        perfect
       </a>
       is also called the "
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       " and, in Spanish,
       <i>
        pasado perfecto
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pretérito perfecto compuesto
       </i>
       . It is described as a
       <a href="/wiki/Compound_verb" title="Compound verb">
        "compound" tense
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        compuesto
       </i>
       in Spanish) because it is formed with the
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verb
       </a>
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       plus a main verb.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
        preterite
       </a>
       , also called the "simple past" and, in Spanish,
       <i>
        pretérito indefinido
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pretérito perfecto simple
       </i>
       , is considered a "simple" tense because it is formed of a single word: the verb
       <a href="/wiki/Word_stem" title="Word stem">
        stem
       </a>
       with an
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflectional ending
       </a>
       for person, number, etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       The choice between preterite and perfect, according to
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        prescriptive grammars
       </a>
       from both Spain
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Hispanic America,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is based on the psychological time frame—whether expressed or merely implied—in which the past action is embedded. If that time frame includes the present moment (i.e. if the speaker views the past action as somehow related to the moment of speaking), then the recommended tense is the perfect (
       <i>
        he llegado
       </i>
       ). But if the time frame does not include the present—if the speaker views the action as only in the past, with little or no relation to the moment of speaking—then the recommended tense is the preterite (
       <i>
        llegué
       </i>
       ). This is also the real spontaneous usage in most of Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       Following this criterion, an explicit time frame such as
       <i>
        hoy
       </i>
       "today" or
       <i>
        este año
       </i>
       "this year" includes the present and thus dictates the compound tense:
       <i>
        Este año he cantado
       </i>
       "I have sung this year". Conversely, a time frame such as
       <i>
        ayer
       </i>
       "yesterday" or
       <i>
        la semana pasada
       </i>
       "last week" does not include the present and therefore calls for the preterite:
       <i>
        La semana pasada canté
       </i>
       "I sang last week".
      </p>
      <p>
       However, in most of Hispanic America, and in the Canary Islands, the preterite is used for all actions viewed as completed in the past. It tends to be used in the same way in those parts of Spain where the local languages and vernaculars do not have compound tenses, that is, the
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       -speaking area and the neighbouring
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese" title="Astur-Leonese">
        Astur-Leonese
       </a>
       -speaking area.
      </p>
      <p>
       In most of Spain, the compound tense is preferred in most cases when the action described is close to the present moment:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         He viajado a los Estados Unidos.
        </i>
        "I have (just) traveled to the USA."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando he llegado, la he visto.
        </i>
        "When I arrived, I saw her."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué ha pasado?
        </i>
        "What (has) happened?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Prescriptive norms would rule out the compound tense in a
       <i>
        cuando
       </i>
       -clause, as in the second example above.
      </p>
      <p>
       Meanwhile, in Galicia, León, Asturias, Canary Islands, and Hispanic America, speakers follow the opposite tendency, using the simple past tense in most cases, even if the action takes place at some time close to the present:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ya viajé a los Estados Unidos.
        </i>
        "I (have already) traveled to the USA."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando llegué, la vi.
        </i>
        "When I arrived, I saw her."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué pasó?
        </i>
        "What happened?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For some speakers of Hispanic American Spanish, the compound tense can sound affected, bookish, or foreign.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In Hispanic America one could say
       <i>
        He viajado a España varias veces
       </i>
       , "I have traveled to Spain several times", to express a repeated action, as in English. But to say
       <i>
        El año pasado he viajado a España
       </i>
       would sound ungrammatical (as it would also be in English to say "Last year, I have traveled to Spain", as "last year" implies that the relevant time period does not include the present). In Spain, speakers use the compound tense when the period of time considered has not ended, as in
       <i>
        He comprado un coche este año
       </i>
       "I have bought a car this year". Meanwhile, a Hispanic American Spanish-speaker is more likely to say
       <i>
        Compré un carro este año
       </i>
       meaning "I bought a car this year".
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_comprehension">
        Mutual comprehension
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_dialects_and_varieties&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Mutual comprehension">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The different
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">
        accents
       </a>
       do not block cross-understanding among the educated. Meanwhile, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilect" title="Basilect">
        basilects
       </a>
       have diverged more. The unity of the language is reflected in the fact that early imported
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">
        sound films
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
        dubbed
       </a>
       into one version for the entire Spanish-speaking market. Currently, films not originally in Spanish (usually
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       productions) are dubbed separately into two accents: one for Spain and one for Hispanic American (using a Mexican or Puerto Rican accent without regionalisms). Some high-budget productions, however, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_(film_series)" title="Harry Potter (film series)">
        Harry Potter film series
       </a>
       , have had dubs in three or more of the major accents. On the other hand, productions from another Spanish-language country are seldom dubbed. Exceptionally, the made-in-Spain animated features
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dogtanian_and_the_Three_Muskehounds" title="Dogtanian and the Three Muskehounds">
         Dogtanian and the Three Muskehounds
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_World_of_David_the_Gnome" title="The World of David the Gnome">
         The World of David the Gnome
        </a>
       </i>
       , as well as TV serials from the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Cone" title="Southern Cone">
        Southern Cone
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        Karkú
       </i>
       (Chile) and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lalola" title="Lalola">
         Lalola
        </a>
       </i>
       (Argentina), have had a Mexican dub. The popularity of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Telenovela" title="Telenovela">
         telenovelas
        </a>
       </i>
       and music familiarizes the speakers with other accents of Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">
        Prescription
       </a>
       and a common cultural and literary tradition, among other factors, have contributed to the formation of a loosely defined
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        register
       </a>
       which can be termed
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       (or "Neutral Spanish"), which is the preferred form in formal settings, and is considered indispensable in academic and literary writing, the media, etc. This standard tends to disregard local grammatical, phonetic and lexical peculiarities, and draws certain extra features from the commonly acknowledged
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/canon" title="wiktionary:canon">
        canon
       </a>
       , preserving (for example) certain verb tenses considered "bookish" or archaic in most other dialects.
      </p>
