   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Official language
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        official language
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       that is given a special legal status in a particular
       <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction_(area)" title="Jurisdiction (area)">
        country, state, or other jurisdiction
       </a>
       . Typically a country's official language refers to the language used within government (e.g., courts, parliament, administration).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since "the means of expression of a people cannot be changed by any law",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the term "official language" does not typically refer to the language used by a people or country, but by its government.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Worldwide, 178 countries have at least one official language, and 101 of these countries recognise more than one language. Many of the world's constitutions mention one or more official or
       <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">
        national languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some countries use the official language designation to empower indigenous groups by giving them access to the government in their native languages. In countries that do not formally designate an official language, a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
         de facto
        </a>
       </i>
       national language usually evolves.
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       is the most common official language, with recognized status in 51 countries.
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       are also widely recognized.
      </p>
      <p>
       An official language that is also an
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_language" title="Indigenous language">
        indigenous language
       </a>
       is called
       <i>
        endoglossic
       </i>
       , one that is not indigenous is
       <i>
        exoglossic
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An instance is
       <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">
        Nigeria
       </a>
       which has three endoglossic official languages. By this the country aims to protect the indigenous languages although at the same time recognising the English language as its lingua franca.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Around 500 BC, when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darius_the_Great" title="Darius the Great">
        Darius the Great
       </a>
       annexed
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Assyria" title="Achaemenid Assyria">
        Mesopotamia
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire" title="Achaemenid Empire">
        Persian Empire
       </a>
       , he chose a form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">
        Aramaic language
       </a>
       (the so-called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Official_Aramaic" title="Official Aramaic">
        Official Aramaic
       </a>
       or Imperial Aramaic) as the vehicle for written communication between the different regions of the vast empire with its different peoples and languages.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic_script" title="Aramaic script">
        Aramaic script
       </a>
       was widely employed from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Achaemenid_Egypt" title="History of Achaemenid Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       in the southwest to
       <a href="/wiki/Bactria" title="Bactria">
        Bactria
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sogdiana" title="Sogdiana">
        Sogdiana
       </a>
       in the northeast. Texts were dictated in the native dialects and written down in Aramaic, and then read out again in the native language at the places they were received.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Emperor_of_Qin" title="First Emperor of Qin">
        First Emperor of Qin
       </a>
       standardized the written language of China after unifying the country in 221 BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Chinese" title="Classical Chinese">
        Classical Chinese
       </a>
       would remain the standard written language for the next 2000 years. Standardization of the spoken language received less political attention, and
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin
       </a>
       developed on an
       <i>
        ad hoc
       </i>
       basis from the dialects of the various imperial capitals until being officially standardized in the early twentieth century.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">
        Statistics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Statistics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to an undated chart by the American pro-English-only organization known as
       <a href="/wiki/U.S._English_(organization)" title="U.S. English (organization)">
        U.S. English
       </a>
       , 178 countries have an official language at the national level. Among those,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       is the most common with
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_territorial_entities_where_English_is_an_official_language" title="List of territorial entities where English is an official language">
        67 nations giving it official status
       </a>
       . French is second with 29 countries, Arabic is third with 26 countries and Spanish is fourth with 19 countries,
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       is the official language of
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_territorial_entities_where_Portuguese_is_an_official_language" title="List of territorial entities where Portuguese is an official language">
        9 countries
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_territorial_entities_where_German_is_an_official_language" title="List of territorial entities where German is an official language">
        German is official in 6
       </a>
       . Some countries—like
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       —have no official language recognized as such at national level. On the other extreme,
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
        Bolivia
       </a>
       officially recognizes 37 languages, the most by any country in the world. Second to Bolivia is
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       with 23 official languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-economist.com-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_alternatives">
        Political alternatives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Political alternatives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_multilingual_countries_and_regions" title="List of multilingual countries and regions">
        List of multilingual countries and regions
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The selection of an official language (or no official language) is often contentious.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patten_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patten-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An alternative to having a single official language is "official
       <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">
        multilingualism
       </a>
       ", where a government recognizes multiple official languages. Under this system, all government services are available in all official languages. Each citizen may choose their preferred language when conducting business. Most countries are multilingual
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and many are officially multilingual.
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       are examples of official multilingualism. This has been described as controversial and, in some other areas where it has been proposed, the idea has been rejected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patten_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patten-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has also been described as necessary for the recognition of different groups
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or as an advantage for the country in presenting itself to outsiders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        Russian
       </a>
       is the official language of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_Federation" title="Russian Federation">
        Russian Federation
       </a>
       and in all
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_subjects_of_Russia" title="Federal subjects of Russia">
        federal subjects
       </a>
       , however many minority languages have official status in the areas where they are indigenous. One type of federal subject in Russia,
       <a href="/wiki/Republics_of_Russia" title="Republics of Russia">
        republics
       </a>
       , are allowed to adopt additional official languages alongside Russian in their own constitutions. Republics are often based around particular native ethnic groups, and are often areas where ethnic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_people" title="Russian people">
        Russians
       </a>
       and native Russian-language speakers are a minority.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="South_Africa">
        South Africa
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: South Africa">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_South_Africa" title="Languages of South Africa">
        Languages of South Africa
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       has eleven official languages that are mostly indigenous. Due to limited funding, however, the government rarely produces documents in most of the languages. Accusations of mismanagement and
       <a href="/wiki/Corruption_in_South_Africa" title="Corruption in South Africa">
        corruption
       </a>
       have been leveled
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (November 2017)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       against the
       <a href="/wiki/Pan_South_African_Language_Board" title="Pan South African Language Board">
        Pan South African Language Board
       </a>
       , which is in charge of maintaining the system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-economist.com_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-economist.com-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Switzerland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Switzerland" title="Languages of Switzerland">
        Languages of Switzerland
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ukraine">
        Ukraine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Ukraine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2012 debate over adopting
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       as a regional language in
       <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
        Ukraine
       </a>
       caused "an all-out brawl in
       <a href="/wiki/Verkhovna_Rada" title="Verkhovna Rada">
        Parliament
       </a>
       ", protests, and the resignation of a lawmaker in attempt to block the bill.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
        United States
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: United States">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_United_States" title="Languages of the United States">
        Languages of the United States
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The United States has no official language. At the state level, 32 of the 50
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._states" title="U.S. states">
        U.S. states
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and all six inhabited
       <a href="/wiki/Insular_area" title="Insular area">
        U.S. territories
       </a>
       have designated English as one, or the only, official language, while courts have found that residents in the 50 states do not have a right to government services in their preferred language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scholastic_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Scholastic-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Public debate in the last few decades has focused on whether
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       should be recognized by the government, or whether all business should be done in English.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patten_10-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patten-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
        California
       </a>
       allows people to take their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Driver%27s_license_in_the_United_States" title="Driver's license in the United States">
        driving test
       </a>
       in the following 32 languages: Amharic, Arabic, Armenian, Chinese, Croatian, English, French, German, Greek, Hebrew, Hindi, Hmong, Hungarian, Indonesian, Italian, Japanese, Khmer, Korean, Laotian,
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       , Polish, Portuguese, Punjabi, Romanian, Russian, Samoan, Spanish, Tagalog/Filipino, Thai, Tongan, Turkish, and Vietnamese.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
        New York
       </a>
       state provides voter-registration forms in the following five languages:
       <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">
        Bengali
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . The same languages are also on ballot papers in certain parts of the state (namely,
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/English-only_movement" title="English-only movement">
        English-only movement
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The pro-English-only website
       <a href="/wiki/U.S._English_(organization)" title="U.S. English (organization)">
        U.S. English
       </a>
       sees a multilingual government as one in which its "services actually encourage the growth of linguistic enclaves...[and] contributes to racial and ethnic conflicts".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Opponents of an official language policy in the United States argue that it would hamper "the government's ability to reach out, communicate, and warn people in the event of a natural or man-made disaster such as a hurricane, pandemic, or...another terrorist attack".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Scholastic_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Scholastic-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Professor of politics Alan Patten argues that disengagement (officially ignoring the issue) works well in religious issues but that it is not possible with language issues because it must offer public services in some language. Even if it makes a conscious effort not to establish an official language, a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
         de facto
        </a>
       </i>
       official language, or the "
       <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">
        national language
       </a>
       ", will nevertheless emerge.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patten_10-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patten-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Indeed, two-thirds of Americans believe that English is the United States' official language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Yugoslavia">
        Yugoslavia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Yugoslavia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sometimes an official language definition can be motivated more by national identity than by linguistic concerns. When
       <a href="/wiki/Socialist_Federal_Republic_of_Yugoslavia" title="Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia">
        Yugoslavia
       </a>
       dissolved in 1991, the country had four official languages—
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Macedonian_language" title="Macedonian language">
        Macedonian
       </a>
       . Serbo-Croatian was used as a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
         lingua franca
        </a>
       </i>
       for mutual understanding and was also the language of the military.
      </p>
      <p>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">
        Croatia
       </a>
       declared independence (1991) it defined its official language as
       <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">
        Croatian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
        Serbia
       </a>
       likewise defined
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
          <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (November 2017)">
           when?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       its official language as
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bosnia-Herzegovina" title="Bosnia-Herzegovina">
        Bosnia-Herzegovina
       </a>
       defined three official languages:
       <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_language" title="Bosnian language">
        Bosnian
       </a>
       , Croatian, and Serbian. The different "languages" are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       and linguists see them more as
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of Serbo-Croat rather than as distinct languages.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Critics allege that the Bosnian government chose to define three languages to reinforce
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Bosnia_and_Herzegovina" title="Ethnic groups in Bosnia and Herzegovina">
        ethnic differences
       </a>
       and keep the country divided.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The language used in
       <a href="/wiki/Montenegro" title="Montenegro">
        Montenegro
       </a>
       , traditionally considered a dialect of Serbian, became standardized as the
       <a href="/wiki/Montenegrin_language" title="Montenegrin language">
        Montenegrin language
       </a>
       upon Montenegro's declaration (2006) of independence.
      </p>
