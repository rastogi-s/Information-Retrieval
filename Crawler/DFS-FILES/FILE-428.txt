      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        stratum
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       for "layer") or
       <b>
        strate
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       that influences, or is influenced by another through
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        contact
       </a>
       . A
       <b>
        substratum
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        substrate
       </b>
       is a language that has lower power or prestige than another, while a
       <b>
        superstratum
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        superstrate
       </b>
       is the language that has higher power or prestige. Both substratum and superstratum languages influence each other, but in different ways. An
       <b>
        adstratum
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        adstrate
       </b>
       refers to a language that is in contact with another language in a neighbor population without having identifiably higher or lower prestige. The notion of "strata" has first been developed by the Italian linguist Graziadio Isaia Ascoli (1829–1907), and became known in the English-speaking world by two different authors in 1932.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus, both terms refer to a situation where an intrusive language establishes itself in the territory of another, typically as the result of
       <a href="/wiki/Human_migration" title="Human migration">
        migration
       </a>
       . Whether the superstratum case (the local language persists and the intrusive language disappears) or the substratum one (the local language disappears and the intrusive language persists) applies will normally only be evident after several generations, during which the intrusive language exists within a
       <a href="/wiki/Diaspora" title="Diaspora">
        diaspora
       </a>
       culture. In order for the intrusive language to persist (
       <i>
        substratum
       </i>
       case), the immigrant population will either need to take the position of a political
       <a href="/wiki/Elite" title="Elite">
        elite
       </a>
       or immigrate in significant numbers relative to the local population (i. e., the intrusion qualifies as an
       <a href="/wiki/Invasion" title="Invasion">
        invasion
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colonisation" title="Colonisation">
        colonisation
       </a>
       ; an example would be the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       giving rise to
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       outside Italy, displacing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       and many other
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       ). The
       <i>
        superstratum
       </i>
       case refers to elite populations that eventually adopt the language of the lower classes. An example would be the
       <a href="/wiki/Burgundians" title="Burgundians">
        Burgundians
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       in France, who eventually abandoned their Germanic dialects in favor of other Indo-European languages of the Romance branch, profoundly influencing the local speech in the process.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Substratum">
        Substratum
        <span id="Substratum">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Substratum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A substratum (plural: substrata) or substrate is a language that influences an intrusive language that supplants it. The term is also used of substrate interference; i.e. the influence the substratum language exerts on the replacing language. According to some classifications, this is one of three main types of
       <a href="/wiki/Language_transfer" title="Language transfer">
        linguistic interference
       </a>
       : substratum interference differs from both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adstratum" title="Adstratum">
        adstratum
       </a>
       , which involves no language replacement but rather mutual borrowing between languages of equal "value", and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superstratum" title="Superstratum">
        superstratum
       </a>
       , which refers to the influence a socially dominating language has on another, receding language that might eventually be relegated to the status of a substratum language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a typical case of substrate interference, a Language A occupies a given territory and another Language B arrives in the same territory (brought, for example, with migrations of population). Language B then begins to supplant language A: the speakers of Language A abandon their own language in favor of the other language, generally because they believe that it will help them achieve certain goals within government, the workplace, and in social settings. During the language shift, however, the receding language A still influences language B (for example, through the transfer of
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Place_name" title="Place name">
        place names
       </a>
       , or grammatical patterns from A to B).
      </p>
      <p>
       In most cases, the ability to identify substrate influence in a language requires knowledge of the structure of the substrate language. This can be acquired in numerous ways:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The substrate language, or some later descendant of it, still survives in a part of its former range;
       </li>
       <li>
        Written records of the substrate language may exist to various degree;
       </li>
       <li>
        The substrate language itself may be unknown entirely, but it may have surviving close relatives that can be used a base of comparison.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       One of the first-identified cases of substrate influence is an example of a substrate language of the second type: Gaulish, from the ancient Celtic people the Gauls. The
       <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">
        Gauls
       </a>
       lived in the modern French-speaking territory before the arrival of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_empire" title="Roman empire">
        Romans
       </a>
       , namely the invasion of Julius Caesar's army. Given the cultural, economic and political advantages that came with being a Latin speaker, the Gauls eventually abandoned their language in favor of the language brought to them by the Romans, which evolved in this region until eventually it took the form of the French language that is known today. The Gaulish speech disappeared in the late Roman era, but remnants of its vocabulary survive in some French words (approximately 200) as well as place-names of Gaulish origin. It is also posited that some structural changes in French were shaped at least in part by Gaulish influence
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including diachronic sound changes and
       <a href="/wiki/Sandhi" title="Sandhi">
        sandhi
       </a>
       phenomena due to the retention of Gaulish phonetic patterns after the adoption of Latin,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calques
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        aveugle
       </i>
       ("blind", literally without eyes, from Latin
       <i>
        ab oculis
       </i>
       , which was a calque on the Gaulish word
       <i>
        exsops
       </i>
       with the same semantic construction as modern French)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with other Celtic calques possibly including "oui", the word for yes,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while syntactic and morphological effects are also posited.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other examples of substrate languages are the influence of the now extinct
       <a href="/wiki/North_Germanic_languages" title="North Germanic languages">
        North Germanic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Norn_language" title="Norn language">
        Norn language
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
        Scots
       </a>
       dialects of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shetland_Islands" title="Shetland Islands">
        Shetland
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orkney_Islands" title="Orkney Islands">
        Orkney
       </a>
       islands. In the Arab
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
        North Africa
       </a>
       , colloquial
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       dialects, most especially
       <a href="/wiki/Levantine_Arabic" title="Levantine Arabic">
        Levantine
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Egyptian_Arabic" title="Egyptian Arabic">
        Egyptian
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghreb_Arabic" title="Maghreb Arabic">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       dialects, often exhibit significant substrata from other regional Semitic (especially
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       ), Iranian, and Berber languages.
       <a href="/wiki/Yemeni_Arabic" title="Yemeni Arabic">
        Yemeni Arabic
       </a>
       has
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_South_Arabian" title="Modern South Arabian">
        Modern South Arabian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Old_South_Arabian" title="Old South Arabian">
        Old South Arabian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Himyarite_language" title="Himyarite language">
        Himyaritic
       </a>
       substrata.
      </p>
      <p>
       Typically,
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        Creole languages
       </a>
       have multiple substrata, with the actual influence of such languages being indeterminate.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Unattested_substrata">
        Unattested substrata
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Unattested substrata">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the absence of all three lines of evidence mentioned above, linguistic substrata may be difficult to detect. Substantial indirect evidence is needed to infer the former existence of a substrate. The nonexistence of a substrate is
       <a href="/wiki/Evidence_of_absence" title="Evidence of absence">
        difficult to show
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic14_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic14-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and to avoid digressing into speculation,
       <a href="/wiki/Burden_of_proof_(philosophy)" title="Burden of proof (philosophy)">
        burden of proof
       </a>
       must lie on the side of the scholar claiming the influence of a substrate. The principle of
       <a href="/wiki/Uniformitarianism" title="Uniformitarianism">
        uniformitarianism
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and results from the study of
       <a href="/wiki/Human_evolutionary_genetics" title="Human evolutionary genetics">
        human genetics
       </a>
       suggest that many languages have formerly existed that have since then been replaced under
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Linguistic_expansion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Linguistic expansion (page does not exist)">
        expansive
       </a>
       language families, such as Indo-European, Afro-Asiatic, Uralic or Bantu. However, it is not a given that such expansive languages would have acquired substratum influence from the languages they have replaced.
      </p>
      <p>
       Several examples of this type of substratum have still been claimed. For example, the earliest form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       may have
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_substrate_hypothesis" title="Germanic substrate hypothesis">
        been influenced by a non-Indo-European language
       </a>
       , purportedly the source of about one quarter of the most ancient Germanic vocabulary. There are similar arguments for a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substrate_in_Vedic_Sanskrit" title="Substrate in Vedic Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit substrate
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Pre-Greek_substrate" title="Pre-Greek substrate">
        Greek one
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pre-Sami_substrate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pre-Sami substrate (page does not exist)">
        a substrate
       </a>
       underlaying the
       <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">
        Sami languages
       </a>
       . Relatively clear examples are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chude" title="Chude">
        Chude
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merya" title="Merya">
        Merya
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Muromian" title="Muromian">
        Muromian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meshchera" title="Meshchera">
        Meshcheran
       </a>
       languages: while unattested, their existence has been noted in medieval chronicles, and one or more of them have left substantial influence in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Russian_dialects" title="Northern Russian dialects">
        Northern Russian dialects
       </a>
       . By contrast more contentious cases are the
       <a href="/wiki/Vasconic_substratum_theory" title="Vasconic substratum theory">
        Vasconic substratum theory
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_European_hydronymy" title="Old European hydronymy">
        Old European hydronymy
       </a>
       , which hypothesize large families of substrate languages across western Europe. Some smaller-scale unattested substrates that remain under debate involve alleged extinct branches of the Indo-European family, such as "
       <a href="/wiki/Nordwestblock" title="Nordwestblock">
        Nordwestblock
       </a>
       " substrate in the Germanic languages, and a "Temematic" substrate in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balto-Slavic" title="Balto-Slavic">
        Balto-Slavic
       </a>
       (proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Georg_Holzer" title="Georg Holzer">
        Georg Holzer
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic14_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic14-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When a substrate language or its close relatives cannot be directly studied, their investigation is rooted in the study of
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_typology" title="Linguistic typology">
        linguistic typology
       </a>
       . The study of unattested substrata often begins from the study of
       <i>
        substrate words
       </i>
       , which lack a clear etymology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leschber16_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Leschber16-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such words can in principle still be native inheritance, lost everywhere else in the language family; but they might in principle also originate from a substrate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrijver97_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schrijver97-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The sound structure of words of unknown origin — their
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       — can often suggest hints in either direction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic14_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic14-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       So can their meaning: words referring to the natural landscape, in particular indigenous fauna and flora, have often been found especially likely to derive from substrate languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrijver97_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schrijver97-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic14_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic14-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leschber16_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Leschber16-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       None of these conditions, however, is sufficient by itself to claim any one word as originating from an unknown substratum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matasovic14_11-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matasovic14-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Occasionally words that have been proposed to be of substrate origin will be found out to have cognates in more distantly related languages after all, and therefore likely native: an example is Proto-Indo-European
       <i>
        *mori
       </i>
       'sea', found widely in the northern and western Indo-European languages, but in more eastern Indo-European languages only in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ossetic_language" title="Ossetic language">
        Ossetic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schrijver97_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schrijver97-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Superstratum">
        Superstratum
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Superstratum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A superstratum (plural: superstrata) or superstrate offers the counterpart to a substratum. When one language succeeds another, linguists label the succeeding language a superstratum and the earlier language a substratum.
      </p>
      <p>
       A superstrate may also represent an imposed linguistic element akin to what occurred with
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman
       </a>
       after the Norman Conquest of 1066 when use of the English language carried low prestige. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_scientific_vocabulary" title="International scientific vocabulary">
        international scientific vocabulary
       </a>
       coinages from Greek and Latin roots adopted by European languages (and subsequently by other languages) to describe scientific topics (sociology, zoology, philosophy, botany, medicine, all "
       <a href="/wiki/-logy" title="-logy">
        -logy
       </a>
       " words, etc.) can also be termed a superstratum, although for this last case, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adstratum" title="Adstratum">
        adstratum
       </a>
       " might be a better designation (despite the prestige of science and of its language). In the case of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       is the superstrate and
       <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       the substrate.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some linguists contend that
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       (and
       <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">
        Japonic languages
       </a>
       in general) consists of an Altaic superstratum projected onto an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Austronesian_language" title="Austronesian language">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       substratum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some scholars also argue for the existence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altaic" title="Altaic">
        Altaic
       </a>
       superstrate influences on
       <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Chinese" title="Varieties of Chinese">
        varieties of Chinese
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_and_southern_China" title="Northern and southern China">
        Northern China
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this case, however, the superstratum refers to influence, not language succession. Alternate views detect
       <i>
        sub
       </i>
       strate effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adstratum">
        Adstratum
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stratum_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Adstratum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An adstratum (plural: adstrata) or adstrate is a language that through its
       <a href="/wiki/Prestige_(sociolinguistics)" title="Prestige (sociolinguistics)">
        prestige
       </a>
       is a source of lexical borrowings to another. Generally, the term is used about languages in particular geolinguistic or geopolitical contexts. For example, early in
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       's history,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       contributed an adstrate to the lexical structure of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The phenomenon is less common today in standardized linguistic varieties and more common in colloquial forms of speech since modern nations tend to favour one single linguistic variety (often corresponding to the
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_(political)" title="Capital (political)">
        capital
       </a>
       and other important regions) over others. In
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       , where dozens of languages are widespread, many languages could be said to share an adstratal relationship, but
       <a href="/wiki/Hindi" title="Hindi">
        Hindi
       </a>
       is certainly a dominant adstrate in
       <a href="/wiki/North_India" title="North India">
        North India
       </a>
       . A different example would be the sociolinguistic situation in
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , where the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       languages have roughly the same status, and could justifiably be called adstrates to each other having each one provided a remarkable set of lexical specifications to the other.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term is also used to identify systematic influences or a layer of borrowings in a given language from another language independently of whether the two languages continue coexisting as separate entities. Many modern languages have an appreciable adstratum from English due to the cultural influence and economic preponderance of the United States on international markets and previously colonization by the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
        British Empire
       </a>
       which made English a global
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       . The Greek and Latin coinages adopted by European languages (including English and now languages worldwide) to describe scientific topics (sociology, medicine, anatomy, biology, all the '-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logy" title="Logy">
        logy
       </a>
       ' words, etc.) are also justifiably called adstrata. Another example is found in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       , which contain a heavy Semitic (particularly Arabic) adstratum; and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language" title="Yiddish language">
        Yiddish
       </a>
       , which is a linguistic variety of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_German" title="High German">
        High German
       </a>
       with adstrata from
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aramaic_language" title="Aramaic language">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       , mostly in the sphere of religion, and
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , by reason of the geopolitical contexts Yiddish speaking villages lived through for centuries before disappearing during the Holocaust.
      </p>
