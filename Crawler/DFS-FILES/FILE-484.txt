      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        assimilation
       </b>
       is a common
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological_process" title="Phonological process">
        phonological process
       </a>
       by which one sound becomes more like a nearby sound. This can occur either within a word or between words. It occurs in normal speech, and it becomes more common in more rapid speech. In rapid speech, for example, "handbag" is often pronounced
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /ˈhæmbæɡ/
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       . The pronunciations
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈhænˌbæɡ/
       </span>
       or /
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˈhændˌbæɡ
       </span>
       / are however common in normal speech whereas the word "cupboard", for example, is always pronounced /
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˈkʌbərd
       </span>
       /, never /
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˈkʌpˌbɔrd
       </span>
       /, and even in slow, highly articulated speech.
      </p>
      <p>
       As in these examples, sound segments typically assimilate to a following sound (this is called regressive or anticipatory assimilation), but they may also assimilate to a preceding one (progressive assimilation). While assimilation most commonly occurs between immediately adjacent sounds, it may occur between sounds separated by others ("assimilation at a distance").
      </p>
      <p>
       Assimilation can be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronic_analysis" title="Synchronic analysis">
        synchronic
       </a>
       —that is, an active process in a language at a given point in time—or
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        diachronic
       </a>
       —that is, a historical
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A related process is
       <a href="/wiki/Coarticulation" title="Coarticulation">
        coarticulation
       </a>
       , where one segment influences another to produce an allophonic variation, such as vowels acquiring the feature
       <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">
        nasal
       </a>
       before nasal consonants when the
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">
        velum
       </a>
       opens prematurely or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       becoming labialised as in "boot". This article describes both processes under the term
       <i>
        assimilation
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Concept">
        Concept
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Concept">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The physiological or psychological mechanisms of coarticulation are unknown; coarticulation is often loosely referred to as a segment being "triggered" by an assimilatory change in another segment. In assimilation, the phonological patterning of the language, discourse styles and accent are some of the factors contributing to changes observed.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are four configurations found in assimilations:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Between adjacent segments.
       </li>
       <li>
        Between segments separated by one or more intervening segments.
       </li>
       <li>
        Changes made in reference to a preceding segment
       </li>
       <li>
        Changes made in reference to a following segment
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although all four occur, changes in regard to a following adjacent segment account for virtually all assimilatory changes (and most of the regular ones).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Assimilations to an adjacent segment are vastly more frequent than assimilations to a non-adjacent one. These radical asymmetries might contain hints about the mechanisms involved, but they are unobvious.
      </p>
      <p>
       If a sound changes with reference to a following segment, it is traditionally called "regressive assimilation"; changes with reference to a preceding segment are traditionally called "progressive". Many
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       find these terms confusing, as they seem to mean the opposite of the intended meaning. Accordingly, a variety of alternative terms have arisen—not all of which avoid the problem of the traditional terms. Regressive assimilation is also known as right-to-left, leading, or anticipatory assimilation. Progressive assimilation is also known as left-to-right, perseveratory, preservative, lagging or lag assimilation. The terms anticipatory and lag are used here.
      </p>
      <p>
       Occasionally, two sounds (invariably adjacent) may influence one another in reciprocal assimilation. When such a change results in a single segment with some of the features of both components, it is known as coalescence or fusion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Assimilation occurs in two different types: complete assimilation, in which the sound affected by assimilation becomes exactly the same as the sound causing assimilation, and partial assimilation, in which the sound becomes the same in one or more features, but remains different in other features.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tonal_language" title="Tonal language">
        Tonal languages
       </a>
       may exhibit tone assimilation (tonal umlaut, in effect), while
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sign_languages" title="Sign languages">
        sign languages
       </a>
       also exhibit assimilation when the characteristics of neighbouring
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chereme" title="Chereme">
        cheremes
       </a>
       may be mixed.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipatory_assimilation_to_an_adjacent_segment">
        Anticipatory assimilation to an adjacent segment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Anticipatory assimilation to an adjacent segment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Anticipatory assimilation to an adjacent segment
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sihler_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sihler-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is the most common type of assimilation by far, and typically has the character of a conditioned sound change, i.e., it applies to the whole lexicon or part of it. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English_consonant_clusters#Assimilation" title="Phonological history of English consonant clusters">
        in English
       </a>
       , the place of articulation of
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasals
       </a>
       assimilates to that of a following
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        handkerchief
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [hæŋkɚtʃif]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        handbag
       </i>
       in rapid speech is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [hæmbæɡ]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , voiceless stops assimilate to a following
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        <i>
         octo
        </i>
        "eight" &gt; It.
        <i>
         otto
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         lectus
        </i>
        "bed" &gt;
        <i>
         letto
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         subtus
        </i>
        – pronounced
        <i>
         suptus
        </i>
        "under" &gt;
        <i>
         sotto
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anticipatory_assimilation_at_a_distance">
        Anticipatory assimilation at a distance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Anticipatory assimilation at a distance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Anticipatory assimilation at a distance is rare, and usually merely an accident in the history of a specific word.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
         Standard Slovene
        </a>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Jevnica" title="Jevnica">
          Jevnica
         </a>
        </i>
        (a toponym) &gt; Slovene dialect
        <i>
         Vevnica
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, the diverse and common assimilations known as
       <a href="/wiki/I-mutation" title="I-mutation">
        umlaut
       </a>
       , wherein the phonetics of a vowel are influenced by the phonetics of a vowel in a following syllable, are both commonplace and in the nature of sound laws. Such changes abound in the histories of
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_languages" title="Insular Celtic languages">
        Insular Celtic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       , and many others.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples: in the history of English, a back vowel becomes front if a high front vowel or semivowel (*i, ī, j) is in the following syllable, and a front vowel becomes higher, if it is not already high:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">
         Proto-Germanic
        </a>
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *
         <i>
          mūsiz
         </i>
        </span>
        "mice" &gt;
        <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
         Old English
        </a>
        <i>
         mýs
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /myːs/
        </span>
        &gt; Modern English
        <i>
         mice
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        PGmc
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *
         <i>
          batizōn
         </i>
        </span>
        "better" &gt; OE
        <i>
         bettre
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        PGmc
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *
         <i>
          fōtiz
         </i>
        </span>
        "feet" &gt; OE
        <i>
         fét
        </i>
        &gt; ME
        <i>
         feet
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Contrariwise, Proto-Germanic *
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       and *
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        e, o
       </i>
       respectively before *
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       in the following syllable (
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_a-mutation" title="Germanic a-mutation">
        Germanic a-mutation
       </a>
       ), although this had already happened significantly earlier:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        PGmc
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *
         <i>
          wurda-
         </i>
        </span>
        &gt; OE
        <i>
         word
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        PGmc
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">

         </i>
        </span>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lag_assimilation_to_an_adjacent_segment">
        Lag assimilation to an adjacent segment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lag assimilation to an adjacent segment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lag assimilation to an adjacent segment
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sihler_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sihler-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is tolerably common, and often has the nature of a sound law.
      </p>
      <p>
       Proto-Indo-European
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *-
        <i>
         ln
        </i>
        -
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        -
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        -
       </span>
       in both Germanic and Italic. Thus
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         ḱl̥nis
        </i>
       </span>
       "hill" &gt; PreLat.
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         kolnis
        </i>
       </span>
       &gt; Lat.
       <i>
        collis
       </i>
       ; &gt; PGmc
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         hulniz
        </i>
        , *
        <i>
         hulliz
        </i>
       </span>
       &gt; OE
       <i>
        hyll
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /hyl/
       </span>
       &gt;
       <i>
        hill
       </i>
       . The enclitic form of English
       <i>
        is
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
        eliding
       </a>
       the vowel, becomes voiceless when adjacent to a word-final voiceless non-sibilant. Thus
       <i>
        it is
       </i>
       [ɪtɪz],
       <i>
        that is
       </i>
       [ðætɪz] &gt;
       <i>
        it's
       </i>
       [ɪts],
       <i>
        that's
       </i>
       [ðæts].
      </p>
      <p>
       For many if not most speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /v/
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       after a voiceless obstruent:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         kwiat
        </i>
        'flower', pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kfjat]
        </span>
        instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kvjat]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         twarz
        </i>
        'face', pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tfaʂ]
        </span>
        instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tvaʂ]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Because of a similar process,
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-Iranian_language" title="Proto-Indo-Iranian language">
        Proto-Indo-Iranian
       </a>
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         sw
        </i>
       </span>
       became
       <i>
        sp
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       . E.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Persian" title="Old Persian">
        Old Persian
       </a>
       <i>
        aspa
       </i>
       'horse' corresponds to
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       <i>
        aśva
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lag_assimilation_at_a_distance">
        Lag assimilation at a distance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lag assimilation at a distance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lag assimilation at a distance is rare, and usually sporadic (except when part of something bigger, as in the Skt.
       <i>
        śaśa
       </i>
       - example, above): Greek
       <i>
        leirion
       </i>
       &gt; Lat.
       <i>
        līlium
       </i>
       "lily".
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
        vowel harmony
       </a>
       , a vowel's phonetics is often influenced by that of a preceding vowel. Thus for example most Finnish case markers come in two flavors, with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɑ/
       </span>
       (written
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /æ/
       </span>
       (written
       <i>
        ä
       </i>
       ) depending on whether the preceding vowel is back or front. However, it is a difficult to know where and how in the history of Finnish an actual
       <i>
        assimilatory change
       </i>
       took place. The
       <i>
        distribution
       </i>
       of pairs of endings in Finnish is just that, and is not in any sense the operation of an assimilatory innovation (though probably the outbirth of such an innovation in the past).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Coalescence_.28fusion.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coalescence_(fusion)">
        Coalescence (fusion)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Assimilation_(phonology)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Coalescence (fusion)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Italic_language" title="Proto-Italic language">
        Proto-Italic
       </a>
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         dw
        </i>
       </span>
       &gt; Latin
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       , as in *
       <i>
        dwis
       </i>
       "twice" &gt; Lat.
       <i>
        bis.
       </i>
       Also, Old Latin
       <i>
        duellum
       </i>
       &gt; Latin
       <i>
        bellum
       </i>
       "war".
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">
        Proto-Celtic
       </a>
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         sw
        </i>
       </span>
       shows up in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">
        Old Irish
       </a>
       in initial position as
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       , thus
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         swesōr
        </i>
       </span>
       "sister" &gt; OIr
       <i>
        siur
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */ʃuɾ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         spenyo
        </i>
        -
       </span>
       &gt; *
       <i>
        swinea
       </i>
       - &gt; *
       <i>
        swine
       </i>
       "nipple" &gt;
       <i>
        sine
       </i>
       . However, when preceded by a vowel, the *
       <i>
        sw
       </i>
       sequence becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       :
       <i>
        má fiur
       </i>
       "my sister",
       <i>
        bó tri-fne
       </i>
       "a cow with three teats". There is also the famous change in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-Celtic" title="P-Celtic">
        P-Celtic
       </a>
       of
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         kʷ
        </i>
       </span>
       -&gt;
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">
        Proto-Celtic
       </a>
       also underwent the change
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        *
        <i>
         gʷ
        </i>
       </span>
       -&gt;
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       .
      </p>
