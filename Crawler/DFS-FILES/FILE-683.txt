      <p>
       <b>
        Colombian Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        español colombiano
       </i>
       ) is a grouping of the varieties of Spanish spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       . The term is of more geographical than linguistic relevance, since the dialects spoken in the various regions of Colombia are quite diverse. The speech of coastal areas tends to exhibit phonological innovations typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       , while highland varieties have been historically more conservative. The
       <a href="/wiki/Caro_and_Cuervo_Institute" title="Caro and Cuervo Institute">
        Caro and Cuervo Institute
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">
        Bogotá
       </a>
       is the main institution in Colombia promoting the scholarly study of the language and literature of both Colombia and Spanish America generally. The educated speech of Bogotá, a generally conservative variety of Spanish, holds high popular prestige among Spanish-speakers throughout the Americas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <img alt="Exclamation mark with arrows pointing at each other" data-file-height="620" data-file-width="620" height="38" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/38px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/57px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ambox_contradict.svg/76px-Ambox_contradict.svg.png 2x" title="Accuracy dispute" width="38"/>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text">
         <div class="mbox-text-span">
          This section
          <b>
           appears to contradict itself
          </b>
          .
          <span class="hide-when-compact">
           Please see the
           <a href="/wiki/Talk:Colombian_Spanish" title="Talk:Colombian Spanish">
            talk page
           </a>
           for more information.
          </span>
          <small>
           <i>
            (April 2017)
           </i>
          </small>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is realized as
        <a href="/wiki/Glottal_consonant" title="Glottal consonant">
         glottal
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        "in all regions [of Colombia]",
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        in common with the pronunciation of southern Mexico, Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras, Nicaragua, the Caribbean coast of Venezuela, the Spanish-speaking islands of the Caribbean, the Canary Islands, and southern Spain, as well as occasionally in Ecuador, Peru, Chile, and Argentina.
       </li>
       <li>
        As in most American dialects, also, Colombian Spanish has
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísmo
        </a>
        (a merger of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
          ʎ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
          ʝ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        ). The exception to yeísmo is the traditional speech of Santander and around Pasto (inland Nariño), where
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
          ʎ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        can still be heard. Until the twentieth century, most Andean Colombian dialects maintained
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
          ʎ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        , including Bogotá (nowadays only some older speakers retain the traditional distinction).
       </li>
       <li>
        Common to all of Hispanic America, the Canary Islands and most of Andalusia, Colombia has
        <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives" title="Phonological history of Spanish coronal fricatives">
         seseo
        </a>
        (the merger of sibilants), making
        <i>
         cocer
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         coser
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         abrazar
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         abrasar
        </i>
        homophonous. Though seseo is general in Colombia, an apico-alveolar, Castilian-style /s/,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s̺]
        </span>
        , made with the tip of the tongue against the alveolar ridge, is current in many Andean regions, especially in
        <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
         Antioquia Department
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
         Medellín
        </a>
        ). This phonetic trait (unique in the Americas) is to be associated with a large number of northern Spanish settlers in Andean Colombia.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
         voiced
        </a>
        consonants
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        , and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
         stop consonants
        </a>
        after and sometimes before any consonant (rather than the
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
         approximant
        </a>
        that is characteristic of most other dialects). Thus
        <i>
         pardo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpardo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         barba
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbarba]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         algo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈalɡo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         peligro
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈliɡɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         desde
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdezde] (dialectally [ˈdeɦde] or [ˈdedːe])
        </span>
        —rather than the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈparðo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbarβa]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈalɣo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [peˈliɣɾo]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈdezðe] (dial. [ˈdeɦðe], [ˈdɛɦðe] or [ˈdɛðːe]
        </span>
        ) of Spain and the rest of Spanish America. A notable exception is
        <a href="/wiki/Nari%C3%B1o_Department" title="Nariño Department">
         Nariño Department
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-auto-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and most
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Espa%C3%B1ol_coste%C3%B1o_(Colombia)" title="es:Español costeño (Colombia)">
         <i>
          Costeño
         </i>
         speech
        </a>
        (Atlantic coastal dialects) which feature the soft, fricative realizations common to all other Hispanic American and European dialects.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Like most Spanish dialects, standard Colombian Spanish has five vowels: two high vowels (/
       <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">
        i
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">
        u
       </a>
       /), two mid vowels (/
       <a href="/wiki/Mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid front unrounded vowel">
        e
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Mid back rounded vowel">
        o
       </a>
       /) and one open vowel (/
       <a href="/wiki/Open_central_unrounded_vowel" title="Open central unrounded vowel">
        a
       </a>
       /). However,
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_shift" title="Vowel shift">
        vowel shifts
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
        reductions
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_breaking" title="Vowel breaking">
        breaking
       </a>
       have taken place in many of Colombia's populous regions, particularly in
       <a href="/wiki/Bogot%C3%A1" title="Bogotá">
        Bogotá
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">
        Paisa
       </a>
       region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This means that in Colombian Spanish there may also be an additional number of vowels, making it one of the most phonologically rich varieties of the Spanish language.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_pronouns">
        Personal pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The Spanish of a large part of the population in Colombia, especially in Bogota, is known for the use of "usted" (the second-person singular pronoun considered
        <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
         "formal"
        </a>
        in most varieties of Spanish) between friends, family members, and others whose relationship would indicate the use of "tú" or "vos" in most other dialects.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Characteristic regional usages of pronouns include
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
        (use of
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        ( which is non standard, therefore prohibited in schools teaching, is nowadays in decreasing usage only in informal conversations) the familiar singular "you", rather than the
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        of other dialects) in the
        <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region" title="Paisa Region">
         Paisa Region
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca_Department" title="Valle del Cauca Department">
         Valle del Cauca Department
        </a>
        , and the use of
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns#The_use_of_vusted_and_vuestra_merced" title="Spanish pronouns">
         <i>
          su merced
         </i>
        </a>
        (literally "your grace") in
        <a href="/wiki/Cundinamarca_Department" title="Cundinamarca Department">
         Cundinamarca
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Boyac%C3%A1_Department" title="Boyacá Department">
         Boyacá
        </a>
        Departments. In the Eastern Highland where Bogotá is located,
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
        was current until the 19th century, after which it began to decline.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The second person plural pronoun
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        and its corresponding verb forms (-
        <i>
         áis
        </i>
        /-
        <i>
         éis
        </i>
        ), which are common in Spain, are, in Colombia—as in all other Spanish-speaking countries in Latin America—considered
        <a href="/wiki/Archaism" title="Archaism">
         archaic
        </a>
        and are restricted to ecclesiastical language.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        There are marked differences in the use of subject personal pronouns (overt vs. null subjects) between the highland and coastal varieties. The highland varieties have overall pronominal rates of approximately 22-26%. The coastal varieties have higher pronominal rates. For instance the overall pronominal rate in Barranquilla is 34.2%.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diminutives">
        Diminutives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diminutives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In Colombian Spanish, the diminutive forms
        <i>
         -ico
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -ica
        </i>
        (rather than the more conventional
        <i>
         -ito
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -ita
        </i>
        ) are often used in words whose stem ends with "t":
        <i>
         gato
        </i>
        ("cat") →
        <i>
         gatico
        </i>
        ("kitty"). This is often seen in
        <a href="/wiki/Cuban_Spanish" title="Cuban Spanish">
         Cuban
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
         Venezuelan
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rican_Spanish" title="Costa Rican Spanish">
         Costa Rican
        </a>
        Spanish as well.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The diminutive form can be applied not only to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouns" title="Nouns">
         nouns
        </a>
        , as above, but also to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjectives" title="Adjectives">
         adjectives
        </a>
        , to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbs" title="Verbs">
         verbs
        </a>
        —in their
        <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
         gerundive
        </a>
        form, for example
        <i>
         corriendo
        </i>
        ("running") →
        <i>
         corriendito
        </i>
        ("scurrying"); to
        <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
         adverbs
        </a>
        —e.g.
        <i>
         ahora
        </i>
        ("now") →
        <i>
         ahorita
        </i>
        ("right now"); and even to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
         prepositions
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         junto a
        </i>
        ("next to") →
        <i>
         juntico a
        </i>
        ("right next to").
       </li>
       <li>
        Redundant diminutives: The diminutive ending can be applied to both the noun and the adjective in the same phrase:
        <i>
         el chocolate caliente
        </i>
        ("the hot cocoa") →
        <i>
         el chocolatico calientico
        </i>
        ("the nice little cup of hot chocolate").
       </li>
       <li>
        The emphatic diminutives: When two diminutive endings are applied to the same word, it gives more emphasis to the sentence. For example, with
        <i>
         ahora
        </i>
        ("now"):
        <i>
         Váyase ahora mismo
        </i>
        ("Get out right now") →
        <i>
         Váyase ahoritica mismo
        </i>
        ("Get the heck out right now!"). For another example, with
        <i>
         bueno
        </i>
        ("good"):
        <i>
         El carro está bueno
        </i>
        ("The car is in good condition") →
        <i>

        ("The car is in tip-top condition").

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Common_expressions">
        Common expressions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Common expressions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Paradoxically, in intra-family speech, it is common for husband and wife to address each other as
        <i>
         mijo
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         mija
        </i>
        (from
        <i>
         mi hijo
        </i>
        "my son" and
        <i>
         mi hija
        </i>
        "my daughter"). And sons and daughters are lovingly called
        <i>
         papito
        </i>
        ("daddy") and
        <i>
         mamita
        </i>
        ("mommy").
       </li>
       <li>
        A common greeting in Colombia is
        <i>
         ¿Quiubo?
        </i>
        (sometimes written as
        <i>
         qui'iubo
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         kiubo
        </i>
        ), a contraction of the older but still somewhat common greeting
        <i>
         ¿Que Hubo?
        </i>
        . This phrase uses the which among younger generations is usually contracted to
        <i>
         ¿Qu'iubo?
        </i>
        (sometimes written as
        <i>
         ¿Kiubo?
        </i>
        ). The phrase uses the
        <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
         preterite
        </a>
        form of the verb
        <i>
         haber
        </i>
        , whose present-tense form,
        <i>
         hay
        </i>
        , means "there is" or "there are." Thus,
        <i>
         ¿Que Hubo
        </i>
        translates literally to "what was there?" or "what has there been?" It is used in the same way as phrases like "what's up?" are used in English. It originated in the Paisa dialect but has spread throughout Colombia, and is considered throughout Latin America and the Spanish-speaking world to be a very stereotypically Colombian thing to say.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Paisa_dialect">
        Paisa dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Paisa dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Paisa_Region#Language" title="Paisa Region">
        Paisa dialect
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paisa_region" title="Paisa region">
        Paisa
       </a>
       dialect is spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colombian_coffee" title="Colombian coffee">
        Colombian coffee
       </a>
       production areas, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Antioquia_Department" title="Antioquia Department">
        Antioquia
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo" title="Quindío">
        Quindío
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas
       </a>
       . Paisa people speak Spanish with an
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apicoalveolar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̺]
       </span>
       , a sound transitional between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , like that of northern and central Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-auto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Paisa Spanish is a "
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseante
       </a>
       " dialect, meaning it often uses
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       rather than
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       for the familiar singular "you" pronoun. The role of this
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       usage in forming the distinct Paisa linguistic identity was reinforced by its use in the works of several Paisa writers, including
       <a href="/wiki/Tom%C3%A1s_Carrasquilla" title="Tomás Carrasquilla">
        Tomás Carrasquilla
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-auto1_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-auto1-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_Gonz%C3%A1lez_(escritor)" title="es:Fernando González (escritor)">
        Fernando González Ochoa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Mej%C3%ADa_Vallejo" title="Manuel Mejía Vallejo">
        Manuel Mejía Vallejo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fernando_Vallejo" title="Fernando Vallejo">
        Fernando Vallejo
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gonzalo_Arango" title="Gonzalo Arango">
        Gonzalo Arango
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cundiboyacense dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Cundiboyacense dialect is spoken mainly in the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Cundinamarca_Department" title="Cundinamarca Department">
        Cundinamarca
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Boyac%C3%A1_Department" title="Boyacá Department">
        Boyacá
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Altiplano_Cundiboyacense" title="Altiplano Cundiboyacense">
        Cundiboyacense High Plateau
       </a>
       ). This dialect also makes a strong use of the expression
       <i>
        sumercé
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        su merced
       </i>
       (literally "your grace") as a formal second-person singular pronoun. It is also an area of strong "ustedeo", that is, the use of the pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (considered formal in most other dialects) in informal speech (as
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are used in other dialects).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Caribbean_dialect">
        Caribbean dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Caribbean dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Caribbean or Coastal (
       <i>
        costeño
       </i>
       ) dialect is spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_Region_of_Colombia" title="Caribbean Region of Colombia">
        Caribbean Region of Colombia
       </a>
       . It shares many of the features typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       generally, and is phonologically similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       . Word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       is realized as
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
        velar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       . Syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       is typically pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ; thus
       <i>
        costa
       </i>
       ("coast") is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkohta]
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        rosales
       </i>
       ("roses") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [roˈsaleh]
       </span>
       . The most notable and distinguishable varieties of Atlantic-coast Colombian accents are: Barranquilla (Considered the most articulated Spanish in America and mostly rhotic in upper-class speakers), Cartagena (Mostly non-rhotic and fast-spoken) and Montería (Sinú Valley Accent, strictly non-rhotic, plosive and very marked wording similar to received pronunciation in UK English) all varieties show a notable R-lessness.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Island_dialect">
        Island dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Island dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       This is the dialect spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islands" title="Islands">
        Islands
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s,_Providencia_and_Santa_Catalina" title="San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina">
        San Andrés, Providencia and Santa Catalina
       </a>
       in Colombia's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caribbean_Region_of_Colombia" title="Caribbean Region of Colombia">
        Caribbean region
       </a>
       . It is marked by a mixture of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       with some features of
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       . Syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       can be realized—in addition to the
       <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" title="Flap consonant">
        flap
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       [l]—as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
        alveolar approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         ɹ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , the latter thought to be an influence of
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       . Thus
       <i>
        verso
       </i>
       ("verse") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbeɹso]
       </span>
       (alongside
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbeɾso]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈberso]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbelso]
       </span>
       );
       <i>
        invierno
       </i>
       ("winter") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [imˈbjeɹno]
       </span>
       (alongside
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [imˈbjeɾno]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [imˈbjerno]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [imˈbjelno]
       </span>
       ); and
       <i>
        escarlata
       </i>
       ("scarlet") becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ehkaɹˈlata]
       </span>
       (alongside
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ehkaɾˈlata]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ehkarˈlata]
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ehkaˈlata]
       </span>
       ). Word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       , when followed by a vowel-initial word, is usually realized as a tap, an approximant, or the lateral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        amo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ~ ɹ ~ l]
       </span>
       <i>
        eterno
       </i>
       ("eternal love"). And when followed by a consonant or a pause, it may be realized as any of these sounds, or as a trill, or elided, as in
       <i>
        amo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r ~ ɾ ~ ɹ ~ l]
       </span>
       <i>
        paterno
       </i>
       ("paternal love").
      </p>
      <p>
       This phonetic characteristic is not exclusive to Colombians whose ancestry is traced back to the Spanish period before the British invasion, under British territorial rule, and the recovery of Spanish control; it is also used by
       <a href="/wiki/Raizal" title="Raizal">
        Raizals
       </a>
       , by whites of British descent, and by descendants of mainland Colombians. The dialect of native Spanish speakers in the area is closer to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaraguan_Spanish" title="Nicaraguan Spanish">
        Nicaraguan dialect of the Caribbean coast
       </a>
       , reflecting the geographical location of the archipelago, off the coast of Nicaragua.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Valluno_dialect">
        Valluno dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Valluno dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Valluno dialect or
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Espa%C3%B1ol_vallecaucano&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Español vallecaucano (page does not exist)">
        español vallecaucano
       </a>
       is spoken in the valley of the
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Cauca_River" title="Cauca River">
         Cauca River
        </a>
       </b>
       between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cordillera_Occidental,_Colombia" title="Cordillera Occidental, Colombia">
        Western
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cordillera_Central,_Colombia" title="Cordillera Central, Colombia">
        Central
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera" title="Cordillera">
        cordilleras
       </a>
       . In Cali, the capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Valle_del_Cauca_Department" title="Valle del Cauca Department">
        Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       , there is strong use of
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       (use of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       where other dialects use
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       ), with its characteristic verb forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Valluno dialect has many words and phrases not used outside of the region. People commonly greet one another with the phrase
       <i>
        "¿Q'hubo vé, bien o qué?"
       </i>
       . Also, it is common to be asked
       <i>
        "¿Sí o no?"
       </i>
       when assessing agreement to even rhetorical statements. Thong sandals are referred to as
       <i>
        chanclas
       </i>
       , and plastic bags (
       <i>
        bolsas
       </i>
       elsewhere) are called
       <i>
        chuspas
       </i>
       . As in other areas, a
       <i>
        chocha
       </i>
       here is another crude word for "vagina". It is used the word
       <i>
        chucha
       </i>
       to refer to an opossum. A
       <i>
        pachanguero
       </i>
       is someone who dances/parties all night long. Andrés Caicedo was the main writer to depict the vernacular usage of language in an accurate manner.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pastuso_dialect">
        Pastuso dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Pastuso dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
        Andean Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pasto" title="Pasto">
        Pastuso
       </a>
       dialect is spoken in the southwest area of the country. One feature is
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apicoalveolar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̺]
       </span>
       , a sound transitional between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         s
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , like that of northern and central Spain. But unlike Paisa dialect, speakers of this dialect typically conserve the "ll"/"y" distinction (i.e. they do not practice
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       ), and in some areas the double-R phoneme is realized as a
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_voicing_and_devoicing" title="Consonant voicing and devoicing">
        voiced
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
        apical
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       . Contrary to a tendency in general Spanish to weaken or relax the sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       between vowels, Pastuso speakers tend to tense these sounds with more emphasis than in other dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Opita_dialect">
        Opita dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Opita dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Opita dialect is spoken mostly in the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Tolima_Department" title="Tolima Department">
        Tolima
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Huila_Department" title="Huila Department">
        Huila
       </a>
       , mostly in the central and southern parts of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magdalena_River_Valley" title="Magdalena River Valley">
        Magdalena River Valley
       </a>
       . This dialect is said to show strong influence of indigenous languages. It is noted for its slow tempo and unique intonation. The phonology is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         yeísta
        </a>
       </i>
       and (like all Spanish in the Americas)
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
         seseante
        </a>
       </i>
       . The dialect is traditionally characterized by the use of the second-person pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        vusted
       </i>
       in some rural areas) not only in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        formal circumstances
       </a>
       but also in familiar ones (where most other dialects would use
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       )—see "ustedeo" above—although
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is gaining ground among young people. There is little or no
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       in this area.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Santanderean_dialect">
        Santanderean dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Santanderean dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The dialect spoken mostly in the northeastern part of the country in the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Santander_Department" title="Santander Department">
        Santander
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norte_de_Santander_Department" title="Norte de Santander Department">
        Norte de Santander
       </a>
       . As in the neighbouring
       <a href="/wiki/Altiplano_Cundiboyacense" title="Altiplano Cundiboyacense">
        Cundiboyacense High Plateau
       </a>
       , there is a strong use of
       <i>
        ustedeo
       </i>
       (see above).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eastern_plains_or_Llanero_dialect">
        Eastern plains or Llanero dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Eastern plains or Llanero dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Llanero_Spanish" title="Llanero Spanish">
        Llanero Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The dialect spoken in this region covers a vast area of the country with less population density. It is spoken in the eastern plains of the country from the
       <a href="/wiki/Cordillera_Oriental_(Colombia)" title="Cordillera Oriental (Colombia)">
        Cordillera Oriental
       </a>
       (eastern mountain range of the
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andes
       </a>
       ). It has a characteristic influence of inland Colombian settlers.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Choc.C3.B3_or_Pacific_dialect">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chocó_or_Pacific_dialect">
        Chocó or Pacific dialect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Colombian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Chocó or Pacific dialect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This dialect extends beyond the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Department_of_Choc%C3%B3" title="Department of Chocó">
        Department of Chocó
       </a>
       throughout the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific_Region_of_Colombia" title="Pacific Region of Colombia">
        Pacific coast
       </a>
       and is said to reflect African influence in terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Isochrony" title="Isochrony">
        rhythm
       </a>
       . Characteristically, syllable-final /s/ is frequently "
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalized
       </a>
       " (pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ) or omitted, as in Colombia's Caribbean dialect (see above). Like Caribbean dialect, word-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       is realized as
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
        velar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       . The /d/ is replaced by /r/ in some words, and syllable-final
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       are often merged or interchanged in a way similar to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
