      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Paris Observatory
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr">
         <b>
          Observatoire de Paris
         </b>
         or
         <b>
          Observatoire de Paris-Meudon
         </b>
        </span>
       </i>
       ), a research institution of
       <a href="/wiki/PSL_Research_University" title="PSL Research University">
        PSL Research University
       </a>
       , is the foremost
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomical
       </a>
       observatory of
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , and one of the largest astronomical centres in the world. Its historic building is to be found on the Left Bank of the
       <a href="/wiki/Seine" title="Seine">
        Seine
       </a>
       in central
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       , but most of the staff work on a satellite campus in
       <a href="/wiki/Meudon" title="Meudon">
        Meudon
       </a>
       , a suburb southwest of Paris.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Constitution">
        Constitution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Constitution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:177px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1290" data-file-width="973" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg/175px-Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg/263px-Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg/350px-Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg 2x" width="175"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Obs-Paris-meridienne.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Meridian Room (or Cassini Room) at the Paris Observatory. The
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Meridian" title="Paris Meridian">
          Paris Meridian
         </a>
         is traced on the floor.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Administratively, it is a
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_%C3%A9tablissement" title="Grand établissement">
         grand établissement
        </a>
       </i>
       of the French
       <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_National_Education_(France)" title="Ministry of National Education (France)">
        Ministry of National Education
       </a>
       , with a status close to that of a public university. Its missions include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-home_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-home-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        research in astronomy and
        <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
         astrophysics
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        education (four graduate programs,
        <a href="/wiki/Doctor_of_Philosophy" title="Doctor of Philosophy">
         Ph.D.
        </a>
        studies);
       </li>
       <li>
        diffusion of knowledge to the public.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It maintains a solar observatory at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meudon,_France" title="Meudon, France">
        Meudon
       </a>
       (
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Paris_Observatory&amp;params=48_48_18.32_N_2_13_51.61_E_">
         <span class="geo-default">
          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           <span class="latitude">
            48°48′18.32″N
           </span>
           <span class="longitude">
            2°13′51.61″E
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          ﻿ / ﻿
         </span>
         <span class="geo-nondefault">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           48.8050889°N 2.2310028°E
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
           ﻿ /
           <span class="geo">
            48.8050889; 2.2310028
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ) and a radio astronomy observatory at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nan%C3%A7ay_Radio_Telescope" title="Nançay Radio Telescope">
        Nançay
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-home_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-home-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was also the home to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Time_Bureau" title="International Time Bureau">
        International Time Bureau
       </a>
       until its dissolution in 1987.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Paris Observatory Library, which was founded in 1785, provides the researchers with documentation and preserves the ancient books, archives, and heritage collections of the institution. Many collections are available on the
       <a class="external text" href="http://bibnum.obspm.fr" rel="nofollow">
        Paris Observatory digital library
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="392" data-file-width="580" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png/250px-Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png/375px-Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png/500px-Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Paris_Observatory_XVIII_century.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The observatory in the beginning of the eighteenth century with the wooden "
         <a href="/wiki/Machine_de_Marly" title="Machine de Marly">
          Marly Tower
         </a>
         " on the right, moved to the grounds by
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Cassini" title="Giovanni Cassini">
          Giovanni Cassini
         </a>
         , for the mounting of long-tubed telescopes and even longer tubeless
         <a href="/wiki/Aerial_telescope" title="Aerial telescope">
          aerial telescopes
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Its foundation lies in the ambitions of
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">
        Jean-Baptiste Colbert
       </a>
       to extend
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       's maritime power and international trade in the 17th century.
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">
        Louis XIV
       </a>
       promoted its construction, which was started in 1667
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eb-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and completed in 1671. It thus predates by a few years the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">
        Royal Greenwich Observatory
       </a>
       in England, which was founded in 1675. The
       <a href="/wiki/Architect" title="Architect">
        architect
       </a>
       of the Paris Observatory was
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_Perrault" title="Claude Perrault">
        Claude Perrault
       </a>
       whose brother,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Perrault" title="Charles Perrault">
        Charles
       </a>
       , was secretary to
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Colbert" title="Jean-Baptiste Colbert">
        Colbert
       </a>
       and superintendent of public works.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Optical instruments were supplied by
       <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Campani" title="Giuseppe Campani">
        Giuseppe Campani
       </a>
       . The buildings were extended in 1730, 1810, 1834, 1850, and 1951.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eb_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-eb-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The last extension incorporates the spectacular Meridian Room designed by
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Prouv%C3%A9" title="Jean Prouvé">
        Jean Prouvé
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The world's first national almanac, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Connaissance_des_Temps" title="Connaissance des Temps">
         Connaissance des temps
        </a>
        ,
       </i>
       was published by the observatory in 1679, using eclipses in
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's satellites to aid sea-farers in establishing
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . In 1863, the observatory published the first modern
       <a href="/wiki/Weather_map" title="Weather map">
        weather maps
       </a>
       . In 1882, a 33 cm (13 in)
       <a href="/wiki/Astrograph" title="Astrograph">
        astrographic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lens_(optics)" title="Lens (optics)">
        lens
       </a>
       was constructed, an instrument that catalysed what proved to be the over-ambitious international
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Carte_du_Ciel" title="Carte du Ciel">
         Carte du Ciel
        </a>
       </i>
       project.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In November 1913, the Paris Observatory, using the
       <a href="/wiki/Eiffel_Tower" title="Eiffel Tower">
        Eiffel Tower
       </a>
       as an
       <a href="/wiki/Antenna_(radio)" title="Antenna (radio)">
        antenna
       </a>
       , exchanged sustained wireless (radio) signals with the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Naval_Observatory" title="United States Naval Observatory">
        United States Naval Observatory
       </a>
       to determine the exact difference of longitude between the two institutions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meudon_83-cm_Great_Refractor">
        Meudon 83-cm Great Refractor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Meudon 83-cm Great Refractor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_optical_refracting_telescopes" title="List of largest optical refracting telescopes">
        List of largest optical refracting telescopes
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_Inch" title="Paris Inch">
        Paris Inch
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Meudon
       <a href="/wiki/Great_refractor" title="Great refractor">
        great refractor
       </a>
       (Meudon 83-cm) was a 83 cm (33 in) aperture refractor, which with September 20, 1909 observations by E.M. Antoniadi helped disprove the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_canals" title="Mars canals">
        Mars canals
       </a>
       theory. It was a double telescope completed in 1891, with secondary having 62 cm (24 in) aperture lens for photography. It was one of the largest active telescopes in Europe.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Directors">
        Directors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

       by a Royal brevet dated November 12, 1771.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the important role played by his grandfather and father in this institution during its first century actually gives them somewhat the role of director.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Saint-V.C3.A9ran">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saint-Véran">
        Saint-Véran
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paris_Observatory&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Saint-Véran">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Also known as the Observatoire du Pic de Château Renard, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_de_Saint-V%C3%A9ran" title="Observatoire de Saint-Véran">
        Observatoire de Saint-Véran
       </a>
       was built in 1974 on top of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pic_de_Ch%C3%A2teau_Renard&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pic de Château Renard (page does not exist)">
        Pic de Château Renard
       </a>
       (2,900 m or 9,500 ft), on the commune of
       <a href="/wiki/Saint-V%C3%A9ran" title="Saint-Véran">
        Saint-Véran
       </a>
       in the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haut_Queyras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haut Queyras (page does not exist)">
        Haut Queyras
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hautes_Alpes" title="Hautes Alpes">
        Hautes Alpes
       </a>
       <i>
        département
       </i>
       ). A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronograph" title="Coronograph">
        coronograph
       </a>
       was in operation there for ten years; the dome was moved there from the Perrault building of the Observatoire de Paris.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Nowadays, the AstroQueyras amateur astronomy association operates the facility, using a 60 cm (24 in) telescope on loan from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_de_Haute_Provence" title="Observatoire de Haute Provence">
        Observatoire de Haute Provence
       </a>
       . Numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       have been discovered there.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
