      <p>
       <b>
        Gallo
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        regional language of France
       </a>
       . It is not as commonly spoken as it once was, as the standard form of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       now predominates. Gallo is classified as one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        Oïl languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Gallo was originally spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Marches_of_Neustria" title="Marches of Neustria">
        Marches of Neustria
       </a>
       , which now corresponds to the border lands of Brittany and Normandy and its former heart in
       <a href="/wiki/Le_Mans" title="Le Mans">
        Le Mans
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Maine_(province)" title="Maine (province)">
        Maine
       </a>
       . Gallo was the shared spoken language of the leaders of the
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_conquest_of_England" title="Norman conquest of England">
        Norman conquest of England
       </a>
       , most of whom originated in
       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Brittany" title="Lower Brittany">
        Lower Brittany
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Normandy" title="Lower Normandy">
        Lower Normandy
       </a>
       . Thus Gallo was a vehicle for the subsequent transformation ("Gallicisation") of English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Gallo continued as the language of
       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Brittany" title="Lower Brittany">
        Lower Brittany
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Maine_(province)" title="Maine (province)">
        Maine
       </a>
       and some neighbouring portions of
       <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">
        Normandy
       </a>
       until the introduction of universal education across France, but today Gallo is spoken by only a small minority of the population, having been largely superseded by standard
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As an Oïl language, Gallo forms part of a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       which includes
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Poitevin_dialect" title="Poitevin dialect">
        Poitevin dialect
       </a>
       , among others. One of the features that distinguishes it from Norman is the absence of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       influence. There is some limited
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutual intelligibility
       </a>
       with adjacent varieties of the Norman language along the linguistic frontier and with
       <a href="/wiki/Guern%C3%A9siais" title="Guernésiais">
        Guernésiais
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%A8rriais" title="Jèrriais">
        Jèrriais
       </a>
       . However, as the dialect continuum shades towards
       <a href="/wiki/Mayenne" title="Mayenne">
        Mayennais
       </a>
       , there is a less clear
       <a href="/wiki/Isogloss" title="Isogloss">
        isogloss
       </a>
       . The clearest isogloss is that distinguishing Gallo from
       <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">
        Breton
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">
        Brittonic Celtic language
       </a>
       traditionally spoken in the western territory of Brittany.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the west, the vocabulary of Gallo has been influenced by contact with Breton, but remains overwhelmingly Latinate. The influence of Breton decreases eastwards across Gallo-speaking territory.
      </p>
      <p>
       As of 1980
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , Gallo's western extent stretches from
       <a href="/wiki/Plouha" title="Plouha">
        Plouha
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Plóha
       </i>
       ), in
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4tes-d%27Armor" title="Côtes-d'Armor">
        Côtes-d'Armor
       </a>
       , south of
       <a href="/wiki/Paimpol" title="Paimpol">
        Paimpol
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Paimpol
       </i>
       ), passing through
       <a href="/wiki/Ch%C3%A2telaudren" title="Châtelaudren">
        Châtelaudren
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Châtié
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Corlay" title="Corlay">
        Corlay
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Corlaè
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Loud%C3%A9ac" title="Loudéac">
        Loudéac
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Loudia
       </i>
       ), east of
       <a href="/wiki/Pontivy" title="Pontivy">
        Pontivy
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Pontivy
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Locmin%C3%A9" title="Locminé">
        Locminé
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Lominoec
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">
        Vannes
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Vannes
       </i>
       ) and ending in the south, east of the Rhuys peninsula, in
       <a href="/wiki/Morbihan" title="Morbihan">
        Morbihan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_boundary_of_Brittany" title="Linguistic boundary of Brittany">
        Linguistic boundary of Brittany
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        gallo
       </i>
       is sometimes spelled
       <i>
        galo
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        gallot
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Les_Cahiers_de_Sociolinguistique_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Les_Cahiers_de_Sociolinguistique-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also referred to as
       <i>
        langue gallèse
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        britto-roman
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">
        Brittany
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Les_Cahiers_de_Sociolinguistique_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Les_Cahiers_de_Sociolinguistique-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In south
       <a href="/wiki/Lower_Normandy" title="Lower Normandy">
        Lower Normandy
       </a>
       and in the west of
       <a href="/wiki/Pays_de_la_Loire" title="Pays de la Loire">
        Pays de la Loire
       </a>
       it is often referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Patois" title="Patois">
        patois
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though this is a matter of some contention.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Gallo
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">
        Breton
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        gall
       </i>
       , meaning “foreigner”, “French” or “non-Breton”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Celtic.2C_Latin_and_Germanic_roots">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Celtic,_Latin_and_Germanic_roots">
        Celtic, Latin and Germanic roots
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Celtic, Latin and Germanic roots">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">
        Celts
       </a>
       settled in
       <a href="/wiki/Armorica" title="Armorica">
        Armorica
       </a>
       toward the 8th century BCE. Some of early groups mentioned in the written records of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greeks
       </a>
       were the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Redones" title="Redones">
        Redones
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Namnetes" title="Namnetes">
        Namnetes
       </a>
       . They spoke dialects of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gaulish_language" title="Gaulish language">
        Gaulish language
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and maintained important economic ties with the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
        British Isles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ict.griffith.edu.au_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ict.griffith.edu.au-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       ’s invasion of Armorica in 56 BC led to a sort of
       <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">
        Romanization
       </a>
       of the population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gaulish continued to be spoken in this region until the 6th century CE,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       especially in less populated, rural areas. When the
       <a href="/wiki/Bretons" title="Bretons">
        Bretons
       </a>
       emigrated to Armorica around this time, they found a people who had retained their Celtic language and culture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Bretons were therefore able to integrate easily.
      </p>
      <p>
       In contrast to Armorica’s western countryside,
       <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">
        Nantes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">
        Rennes
       </a>
       were Roman cultural centres. Following the
       <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
        Migration Period
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ict.griffith.edu.au_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ict.griffith.edu.au-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       these two cities, as well as regions to the east of the
       <a href="/wiki/Vilaine" title="Vilaine">
        Vilaine
       </a>
       , including the town
       <a href="/wiki/Vannes" title="Vannes">
        Vannes
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       fell under
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Frankish
       </a>
       rule.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ict.griffith.edu.au_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ict.griffith.edu.au-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">
        Merovingian dynasty
       </a>
       , the population of Armorica was diverse, consisting of Gaulish tribes with assimilated Bretons, as well as Romanized cities and Germanic tribes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       War between the Frank and Breton kingdoms was constant between the 6th and 9th centuries,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ict.griffith.edu.au_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ict.griffith.edu.au-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which made the border between the two difficult to define. Before the 10th century, Breton was spoken by at least one third of the population
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       up to the cities of Pornic and Avranches.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decline_of_Gallo">
        Decline of Gallo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Decline of Gallo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Historically, France has been a nation with a high degree of linguistic diversity matched with relative tolerance, that is until the French Revolution.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gallo’s status as a tolerated regional language of France suffered as a direct consequence of the French Revolution. During the revolution, the Jacobins viewed regional languages as a way in which the structural inequalities of France were perpetuated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Accordingly, they sought to eradicate regional languages to free regional language speakers of their unconstitutional inequalities.
      </p>
      <p>
       The consequences of the revolution on Gallo stretch into recent history. Within the 1900s, government policy directly affected the use of minority languages in France. In 1962, Charles de Gaulle established the Haut Comité pour la défense et l’expansion de la langue française; this French Language policy’s purpose was to enforce the traditional dialect of French, to the detriment of minority languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, in 1994, the Loi Toubon declared that any governmental publications and advertisements must be in French.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, the use of Gallo declined due to direct government actions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Furthermore, Gallo speakers began to associate French as the language of intellectuals and social promotion, and their Gallo language as an impediment to their success.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result, the rate of children learning the language has diminished, since parents struggle to see the benefit of Gallo in their children’s future.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gallo_and_education">
        Gallo and education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gallo and education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Within recent history, the presence of Gallo has fluctuated in Brittany's school system. Shortly before World War II, the Regional Federation of Bretagne introduced the idea of rejuvenating Gallo’s presences in schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They were primarily motivated in increasing the linguistic competence of children.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:2-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1982, Gallo was officially adopted as an optional subject in high schools in Brittany, even appearing on France’s high school-exit exam, the Baccalaureat.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It took years for the Gallo language to actually be incorporated into the curriculum, but by the 1990s, the main focus of the curriculum was cultural awareness of the Gallo language and identity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in 2002, Gallo’s optional-subject status in high schools was withdrawn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:3_19-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:3-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In reaction to the 2002 decision, an effective and committed network of Gallo activists advanced Gallo’s status in Brittany schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gallo is now taught in Upper Brittany’s state schools, though the population of students enrolled in Gallo courses remains low. In the academic year of 2003 to 2004, there were 569 students learning Gallo at high school or college.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For comparison, in the same year, 3791 students were learning Breton in either high school or college.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_20-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:4-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">
        Status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="841" data-file-width="630" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg/220px-Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg/330px-Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg/440px-Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Loud%C3%A9ac_bilingual_sign_Gallo.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The town of
         <a href="/wiki/Loud%C3%A9ac" title="Loudéac">
          Loudéac
         </a>
         displays its Gallo name,
         <i>
          Loudia
         </i>
         , on signage
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       One of the metro stations of the Breton capital,
       <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">
        Rennes
       </a>
       , has bilingual signage in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and Gallo, but generally the Gallo language is not as visibly high-profile as the Breton language, even in its traditional heartland of the
       <i>
        Pays Gallo
       </i>
       , which includes the two historical capitals of
       <a href="/wiki/Rennes" title="Rennes">
        Rennes
       </a>
       (Gallo
       <i>
        Resnn
       </i>
       , Breton
       <i>
        Roazhon
       </i>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Nantes" title="Nantes">
        Nantes
       </a>
       (Gallo
       <i>
        Nauntt
       </i>
       , Breton
       <i>
        Naoned
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Different dialects of Gallo are distinguished, although there is a movement for standardisation on the model of the dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Upper_Brittany" title="Upper Brittany">
        Upper Brittany
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It is difficult to record the exact number of Gallo speakers today. Gallo and vernacular French share a social-linguistic landscape, so speakers have difficulty determining exactly which language they are speaking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This makes estimates of the number of speakers vary widely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gallo_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although a written literary tradition exists, Gallo is more noted for extemporised story-telling and theatrical presentations. Given Brittany's rich musical heritage, contemporary performers produce a range of music sung in Gallo (see
       <a href="/wiki/Music_of_Brittany" title="Music of Brittany">
        Music of Brittany
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The roots of written Gallo literature are traced back to
       <i>
        Le Livre des Manières
       </i>
       written in 1178 by Etienne de Fougères, a poetical text of 336 quatrains and the earliest known Romance text from Brittany, and to
       <i>
        Le Roman d'Aquin
       </i>
       , an anonymous 12th century chanson de geste transcribed in the 15th century but which nevertheless retains features typical of the mediaeval Romance of Brittany. In the 19th century oral literature was collected by researchers and folklorists such as Paul Sébillot, Adolphe Orain, Amand Dagnet and Georges Dottin. Amand Dagnet (1857-1933) also wrote a number of original works in Gallo, including a play
       <i>
        La fille de la Brunelas
       </i>
       (1901).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was in the 1970s that a concerted effort to promote Gallo literature started. In 1979 Alan J. Raude published a proposed standardised orthography for Gallo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
