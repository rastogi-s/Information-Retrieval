      <p>
       <b>
        Approximants
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
        speech sounds
       </a>
       that involve the
       <a href="/wiki/Speech_organ" title="Speech organ">
        articulators
       </a>
       approaching each other but not narrowly enough
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       nor with enough articulatory precision
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to create turbulent airflow. Therefore, approximants fall between
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricatives
       </a>
       , which do produce a turbulent airstream, and
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       , which produce no turbulence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martínez-Celdrán_2004_201_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Martínez-Celdrán_2004_201-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This class of sounds includes
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       approximants like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        less
       </i>
       ), non-lateral approximants like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɹ]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        rest
       </i>
       ), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semivowels" title="Semivowels">
        semivowels
       </a>
       like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        west
       </i>
       , respectively).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martínez-Celdrán_2004_201_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Martínez-Celdrán_2004_201-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Before
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Peter Ladefoged
       </a>
       coined the term "approximant" in the 1960s,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the term "frictionless continuant" referred to non-lateral approximants.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Semivowels">
        Semivowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Approximant_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Semivowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
        Semivowel
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some approximants resemble vowels in acoustic and articulatory properties and the terms
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Semivowel" title="Semivowel">
         semivowel
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        glide
       </i>
       are often used for these non-syllabic vowel-like segments. The correlation between semivowels and vowels is strong enough that cross-language differences between semivowels correspond with the differences between their related vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Vowels and their corresponding semivowels alternate in many languages depending on the phonological environment, or for grammatical reasons, as is the case with
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_ablaut" title="Indo-European ablaut">
        Indo-European ablaut
       </a>
       . Similarly, languages often avoid configurations where a semivowel precedes its corresponding vowel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A number of phoneticians distinguish between semivowels and approximants by their location in a syllable. Although he uses the terms interchangeably,
       <a href="#CITEREFMontreuil2004">
        Montreuil (2004
       </a>
       :104) remarks that, for example, the final glides of English
       <i>
        par
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        buy
       </i>
       differ from French
       <i>
        par
       </i>
       ('through') and
       <i>
        baille
       </i>
       ('tub') in that, in the latter pair, the approximants appear in the

       </span>

       <i>
        piano
       </i>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">

       <i>

        <a href="#CITEREFTrask1996">

      <p>
       In articulation and often diachronically,
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       approximants correspond to
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowels
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       approximants to
       <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">
        back vowels
       </a>
       , and labialized approximants to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rounded_vowel" title="Rounded vowel">
        rounded vowels
       </a>
       . In American English, the
       <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_consonant" title="Rhotic consonant">
        rhotic
       </a>
       approximant corresponds to the rhotic vowel. This can create alternations (as shown in the above table).
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to alternations, glides can be inserted to the left or the right of their corresponding vowels when they occur next to a hiatus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_language" title="Ukrainian language">
        Ukrainian
       </a>
       , medial
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       triggers the formation of an inserted
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       that acts as a syllable onset so that when the affix
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-ist/
       </span>
       is added to футбол ('football') to make футболіст 'football player', it is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [futbo̞ˈlist]
       </span>
       , but маоїст ('
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maoist" title="Maoist">
        Maoist
       </a>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [mao̞ˈ
        <b>
         j
        </b>
        ist]
       </span>
       with a glide.

        <a href="#cite_note-15">

        </a>
       </sup>

       </sup>
      </p>

       Similarly, vowels can be inserted next to their corresponding glide in certain phonetic environments.
       <a href="/wiki/Sievers%27_law" title="Sievers' law">
        Sievers' law
       </a>
       describes this behaviour for
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Non-high semivowels also occur. In colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/Nepali_language" title="Nepali language">
        Nepali
       </a>
       speech, a process of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Glide-formation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Glide-formation (page does not exist)">
        glide-formation
       </a>
       occurs, where one of two adjacent vowels becomes non-syllabic; the process includes mid vowels so that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʱo̯a]
       </span>
       ('cause to wish') features a non-syllabic mid vowel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish features a similar process and even nonsyllabic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       can occur so that
       <i>
        ahorita
       </i>
       ('right away') is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [a̯o̞ˈɾita]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not often clear, however, whether such sequences involve a semivowel (a consonant) or a diphthong (a vowel), and in many cases, it may not be a meaningful distinction.
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Central_vowel" title="Central vowel">
        central vowels
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɨ, ʉ]
       </span>
       , which lie between back/velar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɯ, u]
       </span>
       and front/palatal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i, y]
       </span>
       , there are few cases of a corresponding approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ ȷ̈]
       </span>
       . One is in the Korean diphthong
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ ȷ̈i]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɨ̯i]
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though it is more frequently analyzed as velar (as in the table above), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mapudungun" title="Mapudungun">
        Mapudungun
       </a>
       may be another, with three high vowel sounds,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɨ/
       </span>
       and three corresponding consonants,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       , and a third one is often described as a voiced unrounded velar fricative; some texts note a correspondence between this approximant and

        /w/
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>

       <i>
        liq

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Approximants_versus_fricatives">
        Approximants versus fricatives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Approximant_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Approximants versus fricatives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In addition to less turbulence, approximants also differ from fricatives in the precision required to produce them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When emphasized, approximants may be slightly fricated (that is, the airstream may become slightly turbulent), which is reminiscent of fricatives. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        ayuda
       </i>
       ('help') features a palatal approximant that is pronounced as a fricative in emphatic speech.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish can be analyzed as having a meaningful distinction between fricative, approximant, and intermediate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ ʝ˕ j/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, such frication is generally slight and intermittent, unlike the strong turbulence of fricative consonants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because voicelessness has comparatively reduced resistance to air flow from the lungs, the increased air flow creates more turbulence, making acoustic distinctions between voiceless approximants (which are extremely rare cross-linguistically
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and voiceless fricatives difficult.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is why, for example, the voiceless labialized velar approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w̥]
       </span>
       (also transcribed with the special letter ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ʍ
       </span>
       ⟩) has traditionally been labeled a fricative, and no language is known to contrast it with a voiceless labialized velar fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [xʷ]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged_1996_326_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged_1996_326-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly,
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">
        Standard Tibetan
       </a>
       has a voiceless
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       approximant,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l̥]
       </span>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_phonology" title="Welsh phonology">
        Welsh
       </a>
       has a voiceless lateral fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɬ]
       </span>
       , but the distinction is not always clear from descriptions of these languages. Again, no language is known to contrast the two.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladefoged_1996_326_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladefoged_1996_326-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Iaai_language" title="Iaai language">
        Iaai
       </a>
       is reported to have an unusually large number of voiceless approximants, with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l̥ ɥ̊ w̥/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For places of articulation further back in the mouth, languages do not contrast voiced fricatives and approximants. Therefore, the IPA allows the symbols for the voiced fricatives to double for the approximants, with or without a lowering
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        diacritic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Occasionally, the glottal "fricatives" are called approximants, since
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       typically has no more frication than voiceless approximants, but they are often
       <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
        phonations
       </a>
       of the glottis without any accompanying manner or place of articulation.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lateral_approximants">
        Lateral approximants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Approximant_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lateral approximants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In lateral approximants, the center of tongue makes solid contact with the roof of the mouth. However, the defining location is the side of the tongue, which only approaches the teeth.

         [l̥]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
         voiced alveolar lateral approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [l]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_lateral_approximant" title="Retroflex lateral approximant">
         retroflex lateral approximant

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʎ̥]

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voiceless_approximants">
        Voiceless approximants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Approximant_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Voiceless approximants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Voiceless approximants are rarely distinguished from voiceless fricatives.
       <a href="/wiki/Iaai_language" title="Iaai language">
        Iaai
       </a>
       has an unusually large number of them, with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l̥ ɥ̊ w̥/
       </span>
       contrasting with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l ɥ w/
       </span>
       (as well as a large number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_nasal" title="Voiceless nasal">
        voiceless nasals
       </a>
       ). Attested voiceless approximants are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral approximant">
         voiceless alveolar lateral approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [l̥]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_lateral_approximant" title="Voiceless velar lateral approximant">
         voiceless velar lateral approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʟ̥]

         [ʋ̥]
        </span>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nasal_approximants">
        Nasal approximants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Approximant_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Nasal approximants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       (Not to be confused with 'nasal continuant', which is a synonym for
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal consonant
       </a>
       )
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nasal_palatal_approximant" title="Nasal palatal approximant">
         nasal palatal approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j̃]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nasal_labialized_velar_approximant" title="Nasal labialized velar approximant">
         nasal labialized velar approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [w̃]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_nasal_glottal_approximant" title="Voiceless nasal glottal approximant">
         voiceless nasal glottal approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h̃]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        nasal glides
       </b>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j̃]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w̃]
       </span>
       historically became
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɲ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m/
       </span>
       in some words. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Edo_language" title="Edo language">
        Edo
       </a>
       , the nasalized allophones of the approximants
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       are nasal occlusives,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɲ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋʷ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       What are transcribed as nasal approximants may include non-syllabic elements of
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vowel" title="Nasal vowel">
        nasal vowels
       </a>
       or diphthongs.
      </p>
