   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Arabic language influence on the Spanish language
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Arabic influence on the Spanish language
       </b>
       overwhelmingly dates from the
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Muslim rule in the Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       between 711 and 1492. The influence results mainly from the large number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       loanwords and derivations in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , plus a few other less obvious effects.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="600" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/220px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/330px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/440px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Chronological map showing linguistic evolution in southwest Europe
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       , also called
       <i>
        Castilian
       </i>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       that evolved from the dialects of Roman
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       spoken in the Iberian peninsula. The first documents written in a language with some features specific of modern Spanish are ascribed to a number of documents from different monasteries in the area of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Burgos" title="Province of Burgos">
        Burgos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">
        La Rioja
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in what is now northern Spain. However,
       <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">
        Toledo
       </a>
       in central Spain, which became the capital of the early
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       during its southward expansion, is where Spanish began to appear in a written form recognizable today. The pre-existing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarabic" title="Mozarabic">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       dialect of this region (i.e. the Romance present during Muslim rule) is therefore likely to have also had an important formative influence on modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lexical influence of Arabic reached its greatest level during the Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       , when the emerging
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       conquered large territories from
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moorish
       </a>
       rulers particularly in the 11th, 12th and 13th centuries. These territories, which included the former
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Toledo" title="Taifa of Toledo">
        Taifa of Toledo
       </a>
       , had large numbers of Arabic speakers, as well as many who spoke local Romance dialects (
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic language
       </a>
       ) that were heavily influenced by Arabic, both influencing Castilian. It's possible that Arabic words and their derivatives had also been priorly brought into Castilian by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarab" title="Mozarab">
        Mozarab
       </a>
       Christians who emigrated northwards from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Andalus" title="Al Andalus">
        Al Andalus
       </a>
       in times of
       <a href="/wiki/Sectarian_violence" title="Sectarian violence">
        sectarian violence
       </a>
       , particularly during the times of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">
        Almohad
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almoravid" title="Almoravid">
        Almoravid
       </a>
       rule in the 12th and 13th centuries. As such, Arabic can be considered to have had a formative influence on the Spanish language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The degree to which the Arabic language percolated through the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       varied enormously from one area to another and is the subject of academic debate. However, it is generally agreed that Arabic was used among the local elites, Muslims and Christians, and that the prevalent vernacular in many areas was
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       , a continuum of Arabic-influenced local Romance dialects. Only the southern
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">
        Emirate of Granada
       </a>
       in the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/Nasrid_dynasty" title="Nasrid dynasty">
        Nasrid dynasty
       </a>
       , which had had a large influx of Arabic speakers as the Reconquista advanced, became totally Arabized, or at least no evidence of a local Romance in the late Middle Ages has been found.
      </p>
      <p>
       Much of the Arabic influence upon Spanish came through the various Arabized Romance dialects that were spoken in areas under Moorish rule, known today by scholars as
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       . This resulted in Spanish often having both Arabic and Latin derived words with the same meaning. For example,
       <i>
        aceituna
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oliva
       </i>
       (olive),
       <i>
        alacrán
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        escorpión
       </i>
       (scorpion),
       <i>
        jaqueca
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        migraña
       </i>
       (migraine),
       <i>
        alcancía
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        hucha
       </i>
       (piggy bank).
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of the Arabized Mozarabic and of Arabic itself is more noticeable in the Spanish dialects from regions with a longer history of Moorish domination than those where it was shorter-lived. For this reason, the dialects of the southern half of the country, known collectively as
       <i>
        castellano meridional
       </i>
       or Southern Castilian, seem collectively to show a higher degree of preference for Arabisms. Northern Spanish dialects tend to prefer Romance synonyms to terms of Arabic origin, such as the Romance
       <i>
        calendario
       </i>
       vs. Arabic
       <i>
        almanaque
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hucha
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        alcancía
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        espliego
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        alhucema
       </i>
       , etc. Because Canarian and all Latin American
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       are mainly derived from Southern Castilian, Spanish words of Arabic origin are common in most
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        varieties
       </a>
       of Modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of words were also borrowed from
       <a href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">
        Moroccan Arabic
       </a>
       principally as a result of Spain's protectorate over
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Morocco
       </a>
       in the 19th and 20th centuries, although these are of minor significance.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       has also adopted a small number of words from
       <a href="/wiki/Hassaniya_Arabic" title="Hassaniya Arabic">
        Hassaniya Arabic
       </a>
       , principally from Canarian sailors who fish in proximity to the Saharan coast as well as by those Canarians who returned from Western Sahara after the
       <a href="/wiki/Green_March" title="Green March">
        Green March
       </a>
       of 1975.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexical_influence">
        Lexical influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Lexical influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The influence of Arabic on the Spanish language is fundamentally lexical but its other influences are also briefly examined in this article. It is estimated that there are about one thousand Arabic
       <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
        roots
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dworkin83_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dworkin83-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and approximately three thousand
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derivation_(linguistics)" title="Derivation (linguistics)">
        derived words
       </a>
       , for a total of around four thousand words
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dworkin83_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dworkin83-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or 8%
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the Spanish dictionary - the second largest lexical influence on Spanish after Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Influences_on_the_Spanish_language#Arabic" title="Influences on the Spanish language">
        Influences on the Spanish language
       </a>
       for more on how the number of Arabisms in Spanish has been estimated. The exact number of words of Arabic origin and their derivatives in Spanish is not known, and many words not included on this list are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regionalism_(linguistics)" title="Regionalism (linguistics)">
        regionalisms
       </a>
       : words which are used in certain parts of Spain and/or Latin America but are generally unknown elsewhere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The high point of Arabic word use in Spanish was in late medieval times and has declined since then, but hundreds are still used in normal conversation. A larger majority of these words are nouns, with a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       derived directly from these nouns, e.g.
       <i>
        alquilar
       </i>
       (to rent) and
       <i>
        alquilado
       </i>
       (rented) from
       <i>
        alquiler
       </i>
       (rent), most of which are excluded from this list. There are also one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        hasta
       </i>
       ("until"), and one
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverb
       </a>
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       . There has been little influence on the basic grammatical structure of the language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_words_of_Arabic_origin">
        List of words of Arabic origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: List of words of Arabic origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This is an open list of Spanish words acquired
       <i>
        directly
       </i>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Classical
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusi_Arabic" title="Andalusi Arabic">
        Andalusi Arabic
       </a>
       , listed in alphabetical order. This list includes the Spanish meaning of the word as well as the Arabic
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       . No fixed standard of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_transliteration" title="Arabic transliteration">
        Arabic transliteration
       </a>
       is used.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Rationale for inclusion
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Due to the large influence of Arabic on Spanish vocabulary, this list is relatively restrictive:
       </i>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        This list has been edited to include only words which are considered to appertain to the Spanish language and the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hispanic_culture" title="Hispanic culture">
         Hispanic culture
        </a>
        and society. Arabic words which may be understood by Spanish speakers, but remain foreign to the Hispanic civilisation such as
        <a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">
         Ayatolá
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Jihad" title="Jihad">
         Yihad
        </a>
        , or
        <a href="/wiki/Shia_Islam" title="Shia Islam">
         Chiita
        </a>
        , are excluded from this list.
       </li>
       <li>
        Only words which have passed directly from Arabic are included. Arabic words which entered the Spanish language through other, non-
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula">
         Iberian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
         Indo-European languages
        </a>
        (such as
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ayatollah" title="Ayatollah">
          Ayatolá
         </a>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Beduino
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Sofá
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         sorbete
        </i>
        ) are not included. Included as exceptions to this rule are
        <i>
         álcali
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         álgebra
        </i>
        , words of Arabic origin which are thought to have entered Spanish through "
        <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
         Low Latin
        </a>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        —as suggested by their initial stress (the
        <a href="/wiki/Arabic_definite_article" title="Arabic definite article">
         Arabic definite article
         <i>
          al-
         </i>
        </a>
        is not normally borrowed as a stressed syllable).
       </li>
       <li>
        Generally, only Spanish root words are listed, derivations (including nouns, verbs or adjectives) not being included. For example,
        <i>
         aceite
        </i>
        (from
        <i>
         az-zeit
        </i>
        , oil) is included but not
        <i>
         aceitería
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         aceitero
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         aceitón
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         aceitoso
        </i>
        . On the other hand,
        <i>
         aceituna
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Olive" title="Olive">
         olive
        </a>
        ) is included since it derives not from
        <i>
         az-zeit
        </i>
        but from
        <i>
         az-zeituna
        </i>
        in Arabic, even though the root of the
        <i>
         Arabic
        </i>
        word is the same.
        <i>
         Aceituno
        </i>
        (olive tree), on the other hand, would not be included, since it shares the same root as
        <i>
         aceituna
        </i>
        . For this reason a significant number of verbs and adjectives are excluded from this list. An exception to this rule may be made when the derived word is much more commonly used than the
        <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
         root word
        </a>
        , when the meaning of the derivative has no evident connection with the root word or when it is not clear that one is derived from the other (e.g. horro and ahorrar).
       </li>
       <li>
        Words derived from
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarabic" title="Mozarabic">
         Mozarabic
        </a>
        are not included (Mozarabic being fundamentally a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
         Romance language
        </a>
        ), unless the Mozarabic word is itself derived from classical or Andalusi Arabic.
       </li>
       <li>
        Words acquired from
        <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">
         Berber
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_languages" title="Hebrew languages">
         Hebrew
        </a>
        (or other
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic_languages" title="Afro-Asiatic languages">
         Afro-Asiatic languages
        </a>
        ) are not included.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The etymology and meaning of most of these words can be verified on the site of the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.rae.es" rel="nofollow">
        Real Academia de la Lengua Española
       </a>
       , although a small minority are only available in other sources or past editions of this dictionary.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many of these words will be unfamiliar to many Spanish speakers because their use is restricted to certain regions of Spain or Spanish-speaking countries or they are no longer in regular use. For example, the word for Arabic-derived word for "jewel"
       <b>
        alhaja
       </b>
       is very common in Mexico whereas in Spain it is restricted to rural areas of the southern half of the country, the standard Spanish term
       <b>
        joya
       </b>
       being much more common. On the other hand the Arabic derived term for fruit juice
       <b>
        zumo
       </b>
       is the standard term in Spain whereas it is virtually unknown on Latin America, where the Latin-derived
       <b>
        jugo
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        agua
       </b>
       are generally used. The Arabic term
       <b>
        alberca
       </b>
       in Spain refers to agricultural water deposits, whereas in Mexico it is the common term used for swimming pool. as opposed to
       <b>
        piscina
       </b>
       in Spain or
       <b>
        pileta
       </b>
       in Argentina.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Words with a coincidental similarity to Arabic and false arabisms">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         el
        </b>
        : The Spanish definite
        <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
         articles
        </a>
        <i>
         el
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         la
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         lo
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         los
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         las
        </i>
        , like most definite articles in the
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
         Romance languages
        </a>
        , derive from the
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        demonstratives
        <i>
         ille
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         illa
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         illud
        </i>
        . The similarity to the Arabic article
        <i>
         al
        </i>
        is a mere coincidence.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         usted
        </b>
        : The formal second-person pronoun
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        is derived from a shortening of the old form of address
        <i>
         Vuestra merced
        </i>
        , as seen in dialectal Spanish
        <i>
         vosted
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        <i>
         vostè
        </i>
        , etc.
        <i>
         Usted
        </i>
        is the remaining form from a number of variants used in Renaissance Spanish, such as
        <i>
         Usté
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Uced
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Vuesa Merced
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Vuesarced
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Vusted
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Su Merced
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         Vuesasted
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Voaced
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The possibility of a link with the Arabic word
        <i>
         ustādh
        </i>
        ('mister'/'professor'/'doctor') seems very remote.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         paella
        </b>
        : It is commonly believed in certain Arabic countries that the rice dish
        <a href="/wiki/Paella" title="Paella">
         paella
        </a>
        comes from the Arabic baqiya (meaning leftovers). The Spanish pronunciation of paella is similar to the Arabic "baqiya", particularly where the latter is pronounced with a silent qaaf as in a number of eastern Arabic dialects. Nevertheless, the word paella is a Catalan word of Latin in origin and refers to the pan in which it is cooked, with Spanish, Italian and French cognates Padilla, Padella and Pôele.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_influences">
        Other influences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other influences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         The suffix í
        </b>
        . Arabic has a very common type of adjective, known as the nisba or relationship adjective, which is formed by adding the suffix -ī (masc.) or ية -iyya (fem.) to a noun. This has given Spanish the suffix -í (both masc. and fem.), creating adjectives from nouns which indicate relationship or belonging. Examples are Marbellí, Ceutí, Maghrebí, Zaragocí, Andalusí or Alfonsí.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Expressions
        </b>
        . A number of expressions such as "¡
        <i>
         Ole
        </i>
        !" (sometimes spelled "
        <i>
         olé
        </i>
        " ), possibly from
        <i>
         wa'llah
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         ojalá
        </i>
        , from
        <i>
         law sha'a Allah
        </i>
        , have been borrowed directly from Arabic. Furthermore, many expressions in Spanish might have been
        <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
         calqued
        </a>
        from their Arabic equivalent. Examples would be
        <i>
         si Dios quiere
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         que Dios guarde
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         bendito sea Dios
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Idafa
        </b>
        . The Idafa was a feature of the Mozarabic dialects which had a major formative on modern Spanish. Although this morphological structure is no longer in use, it is still widely present in toponyms throughout Spain including names of recent origin such as the suburban colonies of Ciudalcampo and Guadalmar in Madrid and Malaga respectively.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h2>
       <span id="Toponyms_.28place_names.29_in_Spain_of_Arabic_origin">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Toponyms_(place_names)_in_Spain_of_Arabic_origin">
        Toponyms (place names) in Spain of Arabic origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Toponyms (place names) in Spain of Arabic origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are hundreds if not thousands of place names derived from Arabic in the Iberian peninsula including provinces and regions, cities, towns, villages and even neighborhoods and streets. They also include geographical features such as mountains, mountain ranges, valleys and rivers. Toponyms derived from Arabic are common in all of Spain (including much of the North of the country) except for those regions which never came under Muslim rule or where it was particularly short-lived. These regions include
       <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
        Galicia
       </a>
       and the Northern coast (
       <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">
        Asturias
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">
        Cantabria
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
        Basque country
       </a>
       ) as well as much of
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       and northern
       <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       . Regions where place names of Arabic origin are particularly common are the Eastern Coast (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia_(autonomous_community)" title="Valencia (autonomous community)">
        Valencia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       ) and the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       . In Portugal, the frequency of Arabic toponyms increases as one travels south in the country.
      </p>
      <p>
       Those toponyms which maintained their pre-Islamic name during the Muslim period were generally Arabized, and the mark of either the old
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic_phonology" title="Arabic phonology">
        Arabic pronunciation
       </a>
       or the popular pronunciation from which it derived is noticeable in their modern names: e.g. Hispalis - Ishbiliya -
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sevilla" title="Sevilla">
        Sevilla
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Given_names_and_surnames">
        Given names and surnames
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Given names and surnames">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Given names
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Almudena_(disambiguation)" title="Almudena (disambiguation)">
        Almudena
       </a>
       (from the
       <a href="/wiki/Virgin_of_Almudena" title="Virgin of Almudena">
        Virgin of Almudena
       </a>
       , patroness of Madrid, Spain) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fatima_(name)" title="Fatima (name)">
        Fátima
       </a>
       (derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_F%C3%A1tima" title="Our Lady of Fátima">
        Our Lady of Fátima
       </a>
       ) are common Spanish names rooted in the country´s Roman Catholic tradition, but both of which share Arabic etymologies originating in place names of religious significance. Guadalupe, a name present throughout the Spanish-speaking world, but particularly in Mexico, also shares this feature.
      </p>
      <p>
       A few given names of Arab origin have become popular in the Spanish-speaking world. In Spain, this coincided with a more flexible attitude to non-Catholic names, which were highly discouraged during the first decades of the Francoist dictatorship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Arabic names which have been present in Spain for many decades include
       <a href="/wiki/Omar_(name)" title="Omar (name)">
        Omar
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Soraya_(disambiguation)" title="Soraya (disambiguation)">
        Soraya
       </a>
       . Zaida has also become somewhat common, perhaps after
       <a href="/wiki/Zaida_of_Seville" title="Zaida of Seville">
        Zaida of Seville
       </a>
       , mistress or wife of King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VI of Castile">
        Alfonso VI of Castile
       </a>
       in the 11th century. A number of streets throughout Spain bear the name of this Muslim princess. Zahira and Zaira have also become somewhat common.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Surnames
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Surnames of indirect Arabic origin, such as Medina, Almunia, Guadarrama or Alcaide are common, often referring to toponyms or professions, but they don't have Arabic origin properly speaking. Few Arabic surnames explicitly denote Muslim ascendance. The reason is that, in the 15th and 16th century it was required that religious minorities change their surnames upon baptism to escape persecution. Muslims specifically were compelled to convert and adopt Christian surnames by a series of royal decrees in the 16th century. 16th century Morisco leader
       <a href="/wiki/Aben_Humeya" title="Aben Humeya">
        Muhammad Ibn Ummaya
       </a>
       , for example, was born to the Christian name Fernando de Córdoba y Valor. Exceptions to this general rule, such as the surname "Muley" which is still present in the Spanish South East and was maintained due to its noble lineage,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are thus very rare.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Suggestions_for_further_research">
        Suggestions for further research
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arabic_language_influence_on_the_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Suggestions for further research">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the English language, search the online catalogs of United States university libraries using the Library of Congress (LC) subject heading, "Spanish language foreign elements".
      </p>
      <p>
       When searching Spanish language Web sites, use the subject term, "arabismos".
      </p>
