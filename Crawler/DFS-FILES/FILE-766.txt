      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin-based
       </a>
       orthographies of many European languages (including English), the letter ⟨g⟩ is used in different contexts to represent two distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       , often called
       <b>
        hard and soft ⟨g⟩
       </b>
       . The sound of a hard ⟨g⟩ (which often precedes the
       <a href="/wiki/Back_vowel" title="Back vowel">
        non-front vowels
       </a>
       ⟨a o u⟩) is usually the voiced velar plosive
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        gangrene
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        golf)
       </i>
       while the sound of a soft ⟨g⟩ (typically before ⟨i e y⟩) may be a
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricate
       </a>
       , depending on the language. In English, the sound of soft ⟨g⟩ is the affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        Genesis
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        giraffe
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        gymnasium.
       </i>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This
       <a href="/wiki/Alternation_(linguistics)" title="Alternation (linguistics)">
        alternation
       </a>
       has its origins in a historical
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        palatalization
       </a>
       of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       which took place in
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Latin" title="Late Latin">
        Late Latin
       </a>
       , and led to a change in the pronunciation of the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       before the
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowels
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later, other languages not
       <a href="/wiki/Daughter_language" title="Daughter language">
        descended
       </a>
       from Latin, such as English, inherited this feature as an
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthographic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Convention_(norm)" title="Convention (norm)">
        convention
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scandinavian_languages" title="Scandinavian languages">
        Scandinavian languages
       </a>
       , however, have undergone their shift independently.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">
        English orthography
       </a>
       , the pronunciation of hard ⟨g⟩ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       and that of soft ⟨g⟩ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       ; the French soft ⟨g⟩,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       , survives in a number of French loanwords (e.g.
       <i>
        regime
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        genre
       </i>
       ). In words of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greco
       </a>
       -
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latinate" title="Latinate">
        Latinate
       </a>
       origin, the soft ⟨g⟩ pronunciation occurs before ⟨e i y⟩ while the hard ⟨g⟩ pronunciation occurs elsewhere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Emerson|1997|p=266_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Emerson|1997|p=266-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some words of
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       origin (e.g.
       <i>
        get
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        give
       </i>
       ), loan words from other languages (e.g.
       <i>
        geisha
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pierogi
       </i>
       ), and irregular Greco-Latinate words (e.g.
       <i>
        gynecology
       </i>
       ), the hard pronunciation may occur before ⟨e i y⟩ as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       Digraphs and trigraphs, such as ⟨ng⟩, ⟨gg⟩, and ⟨dge⟩, have their own pronunciation rules.
      </p>
      <p>
       The orthography of soft ⟨g⟩ is fairly consistent: a soft ⟨g⟩ is almost always followed by ⟨e i y⟩. The notable exceptions are
       <i>
        gaol
       </i>
       (now more commonly spelled
       <i>
        jail
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        margarine
       </i>
       (a French borrowing whose original hard ⟨g⟩ mysteriously softened).
       <i>
        Algae
       </i>
       is sometimes cited as an exception but is actually conformant, ⟨ae⟩ being an alternate spelling for a vowel in the ⟨e i y⟩ family.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Emerson|1997|p=266_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Emerson|1997|p=266-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some words a soft ⟨g⟩ has lost its trailing ⟨e⟩ due to suffixing (e.g.
       <i>
        fledgling
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        judgment
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pledgor
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       While ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/C" title="C">
        c
       </a>
       ⟩, which also has hard and soft pronunciations, exists alongside ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/K" title="K">
        k
       </a>
       ⟩ (which always indicates a hard pronunciation), ⟨g⟩ has no
       <a href="/wiki/Analogy" title="Analogy">
        analogous
       </a>
       letter or letter combination which consistently indicates a hard ⟨g⟩ sound, even though English uses ⟨j⟩ consistently for the
       <i>
        soft
       </i>
       ⟨g⟩ sound (the rationale for the spelling change of "gaol" to "jail"). This leads to special issues regarding the "neatness" of orthography when suffixes are added to words that end in a hard-⟨g⟩ sound. This additionally leads to many words spelled with g⟨e i y⟩ and pronounced with a hard ⟨g⟩, including what may be the most common g⟨e i y⟩ word "get".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Suffixation">
        Suffixation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Suffixation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When suffixes are added to words ending with a hard or soft ⟨g⟩ (such as
       <i>
        -ed
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ing
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -est
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ism
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ist
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -edness
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ish(ness)
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ily
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -iness
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ier
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -iest
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ingly
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -edly
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -ishly
       </i>
       ), the sound is normally maintained. Sometimes the normal rules of spelling changes before suffixes can help signal whether the hard or soft sound is intended. For example, as an accidental byproduct of the rule that doubles consonants in this situation after a short vowel, a double ⟨gg⟩ will normally indicate the hard pronunciation (e.g.
       <i>
        bagged
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈbæɡd/
       </span>
       , not as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈbædʒd/
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       There are occasional exceptions where alternations between the hard and soft sound occur before different suffixes. Examples are
       <i>
        analogous
       </i>
       (hard) vs.
       <i>
        analogy
       </i>
       (soft); similarly,
       <i>
        prodigal
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        prodigy
       </i>
       . These are generally cases where the entire word, including the suffix, has been imported from Latin, and the general Romance-language pattern of soft ⟨g⟩ before front vowels, but hard ⟨g⟩ otherwise, is preserved.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes a silent letter is added to help indicate pronunciation. For example, a silent ⟨e⟩ usually indicates the soft pronunciation, as in
       <i>
        change
       </i>
       ; this may be maintained before a suffix to indicate this pronunciation (as in
       <i>
        changeable
       </i>
       ), despite the rule that usually drops this letter. A silent ⟨i⟩ can also indicate a soft pronunciation, particularly with the suffixes
       <i>
        -gion
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -gious
       </i>
       (as in
       <i>
        region
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        contagious
       </i>
       ). A silent ⟨u⟩ can indicate a hard pronunciation in words borrowed from French (as in
       <i>
        analogue
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        league
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        guide
       </i>
       ) or words influenced by French spelling conventions (
       <i>
        guess
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        guest
       </i>
       ); a silent ⟨h⟩ serves a similar purpose in Italian-derived words (
       <i>
        ghetto
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        spaghetti
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silent_E" title="Silent E">
        silent ⟨e⟩
       </a>
       can occur at the end of a word – or at the end of a component root word that is part of a larger word – after ⟨g⟩ as well as word-internally. In this situation, the ⟨e⟩ usually serves a
       <a href="/wiki/English_orthography#Marking_sound_changes_in_other_letters" title="English orthography">
        marking function
       </a>
       that helps to indicate that the ⟨g⟩ immediately before it is soft. Examples include
       <i>
        image
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        management
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        pigeon
       </i>
       . Such a silent ⟨e⟩ also indicates that the vowel before ⟨g⟩ is a historic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_vowel" title="Long vowel">
        long vowel
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        rage
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        oblige
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        range
       </i>
       . When adding one of the above suffixes, this silent ⟨e⟩ is often dropped and the soft pronunciation remains. While ⟨dge⟩ commonly indicates a soft pronunciation, the silent ⟨e⟩ may be dropped before another consonant while retaining the soft pronunciation in a number of words such
       <i>
        judgment
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        abridgment.
       </i>
       Also, the word
       <i>
        veg
       </i>
       , a clipped form of
       <i>
        vegetate
       </i>
       , retains the soft pronunciation despite being spelled without a silent ⟨e⟩ (i.e., pronounced as if spelled
       <i>
        vedge
       </i>
       ). Similarly, soft ⟨g⟩ is sometimes replaced by ⟨j⟩ in some names of commercial entities, such as with
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.enerjy.com/" rel="nofollow">
        Enerjy Software
       </a>
       , or "Majic 105.7" in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cleveland,_Ohio" title="Cleveland, Ohio">
        Cleveland, Ohio
       </a>
       and some names commonly spelled with ⟨j⟩ are given unusual soft ⟨g⟩ spellings such as
       <i>
        Genna
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Gennifer
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Letter_combinations">
        Letter combinations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Letter combinations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A number of two-letter combinations or
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraphs
       </a>
       follow their own pronunciation patterns and, as such, may not follow the hard/soft distinction of ⟨g⟩. For example, ⟨
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ng_(digraph)" title="Ng (digraph)">
        ng
       </a>
       ⟩ often represents
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋ/
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        ring
       </i>
       ) or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋɡ/
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        finger
       </i>
       . The letters ⟨nge⟩, when final, represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ndʒ/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        orange
       </i>
       ; when not final their pronunciation varies according to the word's etymology (e.g.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ndʒ/
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        danger
       </i>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋg/
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        anger
       </i>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋ/
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        banger
       </i>
       ). In most cases, ⟨gg⟩ represents
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        dagger
       </i>
       , but it may also represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡdʒ/
       </span>
       as in
       <i>
        suggest
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        exaggerate
       </i>
       . Other letter combinations that don't follow the paradigm include ⟨gh⟩, ⟨gn⟩, and ⟨gm⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       The digraph ⟨gu⟩ is sometimes used to indicate a hard ⟨g⟩ pronunciation before ⟨i e y⟩ (e.g.
       <i>
        guess
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        guitar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Guinness" title="Guinness">
         Guinness
        </a>
       </i>
       ), including cases where ⟨e⟩ is silent (
       <i>
        rogue
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        intrigue
       </i>
       , and, in Commonwealth spelling,
       <i>
        catalogue
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        analogue
       </i>
       ). In some cases, the intervening ⟨u⟩ is pronounced as /w/ (
       <i>
        distinguish
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        unguent
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">
        Other languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latin_script">
        Latin script
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latin script">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       All modern
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       make the hard/soft distinction with ⟨g⟩,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       except a few that have undergone spelling reforms such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
        Ladino
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Creole" title="Haitian Creole">
        Haitian Creole
       </a>
       . The hard ⟨g⟩ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       in almost all these languages (with the exception of Galician, which may instead be a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_pharyngeal_fricative" title="Voiceless pharyngeal fricative">
        voiceless pharyngeal fricative
       </a>
       ), though the soft ⟨g⟩ pronunciation, which occurs before ⟨i e y⟩, differs amongst them as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [d͡ʒ]
        </span>
        in Italian
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Hall|1944|p=82_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Hall|1944|p=82-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʒ]
        </span>
        in French and Portuguese
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [(d)ʒ]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        in Spanish
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Different languages use different strategies to indicate a hard pronunciation before front vowels:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_alphabet" title="Italian alphabet">
         Italian
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Hall|1944|p=82_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Hall|1944|p=82-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_orthography" title="Romanian orthography">
         Romanian
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        writing systems use ⟨gh⟩ (e.g. Italian
        <i>
         laghi
        </i>
        , Romanian
        <i>
         ghid
        </i>
        ),
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/French_orthography" title="French orthography">
         French
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_orthography" title="Catalan orthography">
         Catalan
        </a>
        ,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Wheeler|1979|p=7_10-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Wheeler|1979|p=7-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38_1-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_orthography" title="Portuguese orthography">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        orthographies use a silent ⟨u⟩ (e.g. French
        <i>
         guerre
        </i>
        , Catalan
        <i>
         guerra
        </i>
        , Spanish
        <i>
         guitarra
        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>
         guitarra
        </i>
        ); a
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%9C#U-diaeresis" title="Ü">
         trema
        </a>
        over ⟨u⟩ is used in an all of these languages - except Portuguese nowadays
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        - to indicate that it is not silent (e.g. Spanish
        <i>
         vergüenza
        </i>
        is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [berˈɣwenθa]
        </span>
        , with both a hard ⟨g⟩ and non-mute ⟨u⟩).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A soft pronunciation before non-front vowels is usually indicated by a silent ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩ (e.g. Italian
       <i>
        giorno
       </i>
       , French
       <i>
        mangeons
       </i>
       ), though Spanish, Portuguese, and Catalan use ⟨j⟩ as in
       <i>
        jueves
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Arnaud|1945|p=38-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Wheeler|1979|p=7_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Wheeler|1979|p=7-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Several
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nordic_languages" title="Nordic languages">
        Nordic languages
       </a>
       also make a hard/soft distinction. Again, the hard ⟨g⟩ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       in most of these languages, but the soft ⟨g⟩ differs as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        before ⟨e i y ä ö⟩
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
         Norwegian
        </a>
        before ⟨i y⟩
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡ʃ]
        </span>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
         Faroese
        </a>
        before ⟨e i y ey⟩, but not before ⟨ei⟩
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_orthography" title="Icelandic orthography">
        Icelandic orthography
       </a>
       is a bit more complicated by having lenited pronunciations of ⟨g⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       In German, the g is mostly a hard g, also before
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        geben
       </i>
       (to give),
       <i>
        Geld
       </i>
       (money),
       <i>
        Gier
       </i>
       (greed),
       <i>
        Gift
       </i>
       (poison, venom). Soft g occurs in loanwoards (
       <i>
        Girokonto
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Orange
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        rangieren
       </i>
       , but
       <i>
        agieren
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Generation
       </i>
       , for example, are pronounced with a hard g). Some pronunciations vary: The word
       <i>
        Giraffe
       </i>
       is pronounced with a soft G in Austria, but with a hard G in Germany. The
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        Magnet
       </i>
       is pronounced as a hard g, but the
       <i>
        gn
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        Champagner
       </i>
       is pronounced like the French
       <i>
        gn
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        champagne
       </i>
       . In the letter combination
       <i>
        ng
       </i>
       , the g is only weakly spoken, e.g., in the German word
       <i>
        Finger
       </i>
       , it is not as clearly audible as in the English word
       <i>
        finger
       </i>
       . The same is true for German
       <i>
        Sänger
       </i>
       and English
       <i>
        singer
       </i>
       . However, in the verb
       <i>
        fingieren
       </i>
       , the hard
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       is clearly audible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other languages typically have hard ⟨g⟩ pronunciations except possibly in loanwords where it may represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The orthography of
       <a href="/wiki/Luganda" title="Luganda">
        Ganda
       </a>
       is similar to Italian in having a soft ⟨g⟩ pronunciation before front vowels (namely ⟨i y⟩) and ⟨gy⟩ indicates this soft pronunciation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto_orthography" title="Esperanto orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography" title="Phonemic orthography">
        phonemic
       </a>
       , ⟨g⟩ always represents a hard g; a soft g is represented by the accented letter
       <a href="/wiki/%C4%9C" title="Ĝ">
        ⟨ĝ⟩
       </a>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_scripts">
        Other scripts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hard_and_soft_G&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other scripts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       , which uses the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       , the Greek letter
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gamma" title="Gamma">
         gamma
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Capital_letter" title="Capital letter">
        uppercase
       </a>
       : ⟨Γ⟩;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lower_case" title="Lower case">
        lowercase
       </a>
       : ⟨γ⟩) – which is ancestral to the Roman letters ⟨g⟩ and ⟨c⟩ – has "soft-type" and "hard-type" pronunciations, though Greek speakers do not use such a terminology. The "soft" pronunciation (that is, the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
        voiced palatal fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       ) occurs before ⟨αι⟩ and ⟨ε⟩ (both which represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e]
       </span>
       ), and before ⟨ει⟩, ⟨η⟩, ⟨ι⟩, ⟨οι⟩, and ⟨υι⟩ (which all represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       ). In other instances, the "hard" pronunciation (that is, the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_fricative" title="Voiced velar fricative">
        voiced velar fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       ) occurs.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_alphabet" title="Russian alphabet">
        Russian alphabet
       </a>
       (a variant of
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_script" title="Cyrillic script">
        Cyrillic
       </a>
       ), ⟨г⟩ represents both hard (твёрдый
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈtvʲo.rdɨj]
       </span>
       ) and soft (мягкий
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmʲæ.xʲkʲɪj]
       </span>
       ) pronunciations,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡʲ]
       </span>
       , respectively. The soft pronunciation of ⟨г⟩ occurs before any of the "softening" vowels ⟨е ё и ю я ь⟩ and the hard pronunciation occurs elsewhere.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Modern
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       , which uses the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">
        Hebrew alphabet
       </a>
       , the letter
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gimel" title="Gimel">
         gimel
        </a>
       </i>
       (⟨ג⟩) typically has the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       sound within Hebrew words, although in some
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Hebrew" title="Sephardi Hebrew">
        Sephardic
       </a>
       dialects, it represents
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       when written with a
       <a href="/wiki/Dagesh" title="Dagesh">
        dagesh
       </a>
       (i.e., a dot placed inside the letter: ⟨גּ⟩), and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       when without a dagesh. An apostrophe-like symbol called a
       <a href="/wiki/Geresh" title="Geresh">
        Geresh
       </a>
       can be added immediately to the left of a gimel (i.e., ⟨ג׳⟩) to indicate that the gimel represents an affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
