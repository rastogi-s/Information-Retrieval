      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Official_or_national_language">
        Official or national language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Official or national language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish is the majority language in 20 sovereign states and several dependent territories, totaling around 440 million people.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is additionally the main official language in Equatorial Guinea.
      </p>
      <p>
       In these countries and territories, Spanish is the main or mostly used language of communication of the vast majority of the population; official documents are written chiefly or solely in that language; and it is taught in schools and utilized as the primary medium of instruction as part of the official curriculum.
      </p>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_realm" title="Commonwealth realm">
        Commonwealth realm
       </a>
       where English is the official national language. However, the country shares land borders with Spanish-speaking Mexico and Guatemala and, per the 2010 Belizean census, Spanish is spoken by a sizable portion of the population; 30% claim Spanish as a mother tongue and about 50% of the population has working knowledge of the language.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
        United States
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: United States">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_United_States" title="Spanish language in the United States">
        Spanish language in the United States
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish has been spoken in the United States for several centuries in the
       <a href="/wiki/Southwestern_United_States" title="Southwestern United States">
        Southwest
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       , which were all once part of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       . However, today only a tiny minority of Spanish speakers in the US trace their language back to those times; the overwhelming majority of speakers come from recent immigration. Only in northern
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       and southern
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       has Spanish maintained speaking communities uninterruptedly since colonial times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canf1_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-canf1-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish is the most studied foreign language in United States schools and is spoken as a native tongue by 41 million people, plus an additional 11 million fluent second-language speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though not official, Spanish has a special status for education in the U.S. state of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With over 50 million native speakers and second language speakers, the United States now has the second largest Spanish-speaking population in the world after Mexico.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Más_'speak_spanish'_que_en_España_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Más_'speak_spanish'_que_en_España-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish is increasingly used alongside English nationwide in business and politics. In the United States, the language is regulated by the
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_Academy_of_the_Spanish_Language" title="North American Academy of the Spanish Language">
        North American Academy of the Spanish Language
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Philippines">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
        Spanish language in the Philippines
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Spanish" title="Philippine Spanish">
        Philippine Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish was an official language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       from the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Philippines_(1521%E2%80%931898)" title="History of the Philippines (1521–1898)">
        Hispanic period
       </a>
       in 1565 and through independence until a constitutional change in 1973. However,
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">
        President
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_Marcos" title="Ferdinand Marcos">
        Ferdinand Marcos
       </a>
       had Spanish redesigned as an official language under Presidential Decree No. 156, dated 15 March 1973 and Spanish remained official until 1987, when it was re-designated as a voluntary and optional auxiliary language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 8 August 2007,
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_Philippines" title="President of the Philippines">
        President
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gloria_Macapagal-Arroyo" title="Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo">
        Gloria Macapagal-Arroyo
       </a>
       announced that the Philippine government asked help from the Spanish Government in her plan to reintroduce Spanish as a required subject in the Philippine school system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SpanishGMA_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SpanishGMA-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 2012, the language was a compulsory subject at only a very select number of secondary schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In spite of government promotion of Spanish, less than 0.5% of the population are able to speak Spanish at least proficiently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While Spanish is designated as an optional government language in the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       , its usage is very limited and not present in everyday life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the country, Spanish is regulated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Academy_of_the_Spanish_Language" title="Philippine Academy of the Spanish Language">
        Philippine Academy of the Spanish Language
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">

       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChamRefGram_41-0">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Judeo-Spanish">
        Judeo-Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_countries_where_Spanish_is_an_official_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Judeo-Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Judaeo-Spanish (sometimes known as Ladino or other names) is a language derived from medieval Spanish; it is still spoken by some
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardi Jews
       </a>
       , mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
