      <p>
       <br/>
       <b>
        Possessive determiners
       </b>
       constitute a sub-class of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(linguistics)" title="Determiner (linguistics)">
        determiners
       </a>
       which modify a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       by attributing
       <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">
        possession
       </a>
       (or other sense of belonging) to someone or something. They are also known as
       <b>
        possessive adjectives
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although the latter term is sometimes used with a wider meaning.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       include possessive forms of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_personal_pronoun" title="English personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       , namely:
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        his
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        its
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        our
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        their
       </i>
       , but excluding those forms such as
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hers
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ours
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        theirs
       </i>
       that are used as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_pronoun" title="Possessive pronoun">
        possessive pronouns
       </a>
       but not as determiners. Possessive determiners may also be taken to include possessive forms made from
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        nouns
       </a>
       , from other
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       and from
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrases
       </a>
       , such as
       <i>
        John's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        the girl's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        somebody's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        the king of Spain's
       </i>
       , when used to modify a following noun.
      </p>
      <p>
       In many languages, possessive determiners are subject to
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       with the noun they modify, as in the
       <a href="/wiki/French_grammar" title="French grammar">
        French
       </a>
       <i>
        mon
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ma
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        mes
       </i>
       , respectively the masculine singular, feminine singular and plural forms corresponding to the English
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_determiners_and_adjectives">
        Comparison with determiners and adjectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Possessive_determiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Comparison with determiners and adjectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Possessive determiners (possessive adjectives) have features of both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(linguistics)" title="Determiner (linguistics)">
        determiners
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Possessive determiners, as used in English and some other languages, imply the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
         definite article
        </a>
        . For example,
        <i>
         my car
        </i>
        implies
        <i>
         <b>
          the
         </b>
         car that belongs to me/is used by me
        </i>
        . (However, "This is the car I have" implies that it is the only car you have, whereas "This is my car" does not imply that to the same extent. When applied to relatives other than parents or spouse, there is no implication of uniqueness – "my brother" can mean equally well "one of my brothers" as "the one brother I have".) It is not correct to precede possessives with an article (*
        <i>
         the my car
        </i>
        ) or (in today's English) other definite determiner such as a
        <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">
         demonstrative
        </a>
        (*
        <i>
         this my car
        </i>
        ), although they can combine with quantifiers in the same ways that
        <i>
         the
        </i>
        can (
        <i>
         all my cars
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         my three cars
        </i>
        , etc.; see
        <a href="/wiki/English_determiners" title="English determiners">
         English determiners
        </a>
        ). This is not the case in all languages; for example in
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        the possessive is usually preceded by another determiner such as an article, as in
        <i>
         la mia macchina
        </i>
        ("my car", literally "the my car").
       </li>
       <li>
        Possessive determiners may be modified with an
        <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
         adverb
        </a>
        , as adjectives are, although not as freely or as commonly as is the case with adjectives. Such modification is generally limited to such adverbs as
        <i>
         more
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         less
        </i>
        , or
        <i>
         as much ... as
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">
         comparative
        </a>
        ) or
        <i>
         mostly
        </i>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superlative" title="Superlative">
         superlative
        </a>
        ), for example in
        <i>
         This is more my team than your team
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         This is less my team than your team
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         This is as much my team as your team
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         This is mostly my team
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">
        Nomenclature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Possessive_determiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While some classify the words
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       , etc. as
       <b>
        possessive adjectives
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       others, due to the differences noted above, do not consider them adjectives – at least, not in English – and prefer
       <b>
        possessive determiners
       </b>
       . In some other languages the equivalent parts of speech behave more like true adjectives, however.
      </p>
      <p>
       The words
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       , etc. are sometimes classified, along with
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       etc., as
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_pronouns" title="Possessive pronouns">
         possessive pronouns
        </a>
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        genitive pronouns
       </b>
       , since they are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_case" title="Possessive case">
        possessive
       </a>
       (or
       <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
        genitive
       </a>
       ) forms of the ordinary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Personal_pronouns" title="Personal pronouns">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       etc. However, unlike most other
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       , they do not behave grammatically as stand-alone nouns, but instead qualify another noun – as in
       <i>
        my book
       </i>
       (contrasted with
       <i>
        that's mine
       </i>
       , for example, where
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       substitutes for a complete noun phrase such as
       <i>
        my book
       </i>
       ). For this reason, other authors restrict the term "possessive pronoun" to the group of words
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       etc. that substitute directly for a noun or noun phrase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some authors who classify both sets of words as "possessive pronouns" or "genitive pronouns" apply the terms
       <b>
        dependent/independent
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        weak/strong
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to refer, respectively, to
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       , etc. and
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       , etc. For example, under this scheme,
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       is termed a
       <b>
        dependent possessive pronoun
       </b>
       and
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       an
       <b>
        independent possessive pronoun
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possessive_determiners_in_English">
        Possessive determiners in English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Possessive_determiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Possessive determiners in English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The basic
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronominal
       </a>
       possessive determiners in modern English are
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        his
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        its
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        our
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        their
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        whose
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (as in
       <i>
        Whose coat is this?
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        the man whose car was stolen
       </i>
       ). As noted above, they indicate definiteness, like the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       . Archaic forms include
       <i>
        thy
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        mine/thine
       </i>
       (for
       <i>
        my/thy
       </i>
       before a vowel). For details, see
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        English personal pronouns
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Other possessive determiners (although they may not always be classed as such, though they play the same role in
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       ) are the words and phrases formed by attaching the
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitic
       </a>
       <i>
        -'s
       </i>
       (or sometimes just an
       <a href="/wiki/Apostrophe" title="Apostrophe">
        apostrophe
       </a>
       after
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       ) to other pronouns, to nouns and to
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrases
       </a>
       (sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner_phrase" title="Determiner phrase">
        determiner phrases
       </a>
       ). Examples include
       <i>
        Jane's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        heaven's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        the boy's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Jesus',
       </i>
       <i>
        the soldiers',
       </i>
       <i>
        those men's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        the king of England's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        one's
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        somebody's
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For more details of the formation and use of possessives in English, see
       <a href="/wiki/English_possessive" title="English possessive">
        English possessive
       </a>
       . For more details about the use of determiners generally, see
       <a href="/wiki/English_determiners" title="English determiners">
        English determiners
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_languages">
        Other languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Possessive_determiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Though in English the possessive determiners indicate definiteness, in other languages the definiteness needs to be added separately for grammatical correctness.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       the phrase "my book" would be
       <i>
        boka mi
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where
       <i>
        boka
       </i>
       is the definite form of the feminine noun
       <i>
        bok
       </i>
       (book), and
       <i>
        mi
       </i>
       (my) is the possessive pronoun following feminine singular nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       In most
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ) the gender of the possessive determiners agrees with the thing(s) owned, not with the owner. French, for example, uses
       <i>
        son
       </i>
       for masculine nouns and also for feminine noun phrases starting with a vowel,
       <i>
        sa
       </i>
       elsewhere; compare
       <i>
        Il a perdu son chapeau
       </i>
       ("He lost his hat") with
       <i>
        Elle a perdu son chapeau
       </i>
       ("She lost her hat"). In this respect the possessive adjectives in these languages resemble ordinary adjectives. French also correlates possessive determiners to both the plurality of the possessor and possessee, as in
       <i>
        notre voiture
       </i>
       (our car) and
       <i>
        nos voitures
       </i>
       (our cars).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , constructions such as
       <i>
        il tuo libro nero
       </i>
       ("the your book black ", rendered in English as "your black book") and
       <i>
        quel tuo libro nero
       </i>
       ("that your book black", rendered in English as "that black book of yours") are grammatically correct. In Italian, the possessive determiners behave in almost every respect like adjectives.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       , such as English and
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       , use different pronouns depending on the owner. English has the (uninflected) words
       <i>
        his
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       ; Dutch uses the (uninflected)
       <i>
        zijn
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        haar
       </i>
       . Other Germanic languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       and several
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_dialects" title="Dutch dialects">
        Dutch dialects
       </a>
       including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limburgish" title="Limburgish">
        Limburgish
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brabantian" title="Brabantian">
        Brabantian
       </a>
       , additionally use different forms depending on the grammatical gender of the object owned. German has
       <i>
        sein
       </i>
       (with inflected forms like
       <i>
        seine
       </i>
       ) for masculine and
       <i>
        ihr
       </i>
       (with inflected forms like
       <i>
        ihre
       </i>
       ) for feminine possessors; in German, the "hat" sentences above would be
       <i>
        Er hat seinen Hut verloren
       </i>
       (He lost his hat) and
       <i>
        Sie hat ihren Hut verloren
       </i>
       (She lost her hat) respectively. Brabantian also inflects
       <i>
        zijn
       </i>
       (his) and
       <i>
        haar
       </i>
       (her) according to the grammatical gender and number of the thing(s) owned.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages have no distinctive possessive adjectives, and express
       <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">
        possession
       </a>
       by declining personal pronouns in the
       <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
        genitive
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_case" title="Possessive case">
        possessive case
       </a>
       , or by using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_suffix" title="Possessive suffix">
        possessive suffixes
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">
        particles
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       , for example,
       <i>
        boku no
       </i>
       (a word for
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       coupled with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">
        genitive
       </a>
       particle
       <i>
        no
       </i>
       ), is used for
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       , the possessive adjective and possessive pronoun take the same form as each other: the form associated with
       <i>
        wǒ
       </i>
       ("I") is
       <i>
        wǒ de
       </i>
       ("my", "mine"), where
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       is the possessive particle.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages use the same word for both the possessive adjective and the matching possessive pronoun. For example, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finnish_(language)" title="Finnish (language)">
        Finnish
       </a>
       (informal)
       <i>
        meiän
       </i>
       can mean either
       <i>
        our
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ours
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, some
       <a href="/wiki/Micronesian_languages" title="Micronesian languages">
        Micronesian languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pohnpeian" title="Pohnpeian">
        Pohnpeian
       </a>
       have a large number of possessive classifiers that reflect both the possessor and the possessum:
       <i>
        nah pwihk
       </i>
       means "his (live) pig;"
       <i>
        ah pwihk
       </i>
       means "his (butchered) pig;" and
       <i>
        kene pwihk
       </i>
       means "pork; his pig (to eat)." As a further example,
       <i>
        tehnweren ohlo war
       </i>
       (
       <small>
        POSSESSIVECLASS:HONORIFIC-CANOE
       </small>
       -n that-man canoe) means "that man's canoe," referring to a person of high status.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Semantics">
        Semantics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Possessive_determiner&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Semantics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For possessive adjectives as elsewhere, the genitive does not always indicate strict
       <i>
        possession
       </i>
       , but rather a general sense of
       <i>
        belonging
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        close identification with
       </i>
       . Consider the following examples:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         my mother
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         my people
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Here, a person does not own his or her mother, but rather has a close relationship with her. The same applies to
        <i>
         my people
        </i>
        , which means
        <i>
         people I am closely associated with
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         people I identify with
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         his train
        </i>
        (as in "If Bob doesn't get to the station in ten minutes he's going to miss
        <i>
         his train
        </i>
        ")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Here, Bob most likely does not own the train and instead
        <i>
         his train
        </i>
        means
        <i>
         the train Bob plans to travel on
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         my CD
        </i>
        (as in "The kids are enjoying
        <i>
         my CD
        </i>
        ")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         my CD
        </i>
        could refer to a CD that I own, a CD owned by someone else but with music that I recorded as an artist, a CD that I have just given to someone here as a gift, or one with some other relation to me that would be identifiable in the context.
       </dd>
      </dl>
