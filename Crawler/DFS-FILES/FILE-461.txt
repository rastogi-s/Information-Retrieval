      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        modifier
       </b>
       is an optional element in
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase" title="Phrase">
        phrase
       </a>
       structure or
       <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">
        clause
       </a>
       structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template noprint Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:CIRCULAR" title="Wikipedia:CIRCULAR">
          <span title="This claim cites another Wikipedia article. Articles need references to reliable third-party sources. (March 2017)">
           circular reference
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       A modifier is so called because it is said to
       <i>
        modify
       </i>
       (change the meaning of) another element in the structure, on which it is dependent. Typically the modifier can be removed without affecting the grammar of the sentence. For example, in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       sentence
       <i>
        This is a red ball
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       <i>
        red
       </i>
       is a modifier, modifying the
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       <i>
        ball
       </i>
       . Removal of the modifier would leave
       <i>
        This is a ball
       </i>
       , which is grammatically correct and equivalent in structure to the original sentence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other terms used with a similar meaning are
       <i>
        qualifier
       </i>
       (the word
       <i>
        qualify
       </i>
       may be used in the same way as
       <i>
        modify
       </i>
       in this context),
       <i>
        attribute
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Adjunct_(grammar)" title="Adjunct (grammar)">
         adjunct
        </a>
       </i>
       . These concepts are often distinguished from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">
         complements
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Argument_(linguistics)" title="Argument (linguistics)">
         arguments
        </a>
       </i>
       , which may also be considered dependent on another element, but are considered an indispensable part of the structure. For example, in
       <i>
        His face became red
       </i>
       , the word
       <i>
        red
       </i>
       might be called a complement or argument of
       <i>
        became
       </i>
       , rather than a modifier or adjunct, since it cannot be omitted from the sentence.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Premodifiers_and_postmodifiers">
        Premodifiers and postmodifiers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_modifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Premodifiers and postmodifiers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Modifiers may come either before or after the modified element (the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
         head
        </a>
       </i>
       ), depending on the type of modifier and the rules of
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       for the language in question. A modifier placed before the head is called a
       <b>
        premodifier
       </b>
       ; one placed after the head is called a
       <b>
        postmodifier
       </b>
       . For example, in
       <i>
        land mines
       </i>
       , the word
       <i>
        land
       </i>
       is a premodifier of
       <i>
        mines
       </i>
       , whereas in the phrase
       <i>
        mines in wartime
       </i>
       , the phrase
       <i>
        in wartime
       </i>
       is a postmodifier of
       <i>
        mines
       </i>
       . A head may have a number of modifiers, and these may include both premodifiers and postmodifiers. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         that nice tall man from Canada whom you met
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In this noun phrase,
       <i>
        man
       </i>
       is the head,
       <i>
        nice
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tall
       </i>
       are premodifiers, and
       <i>
        from Canada
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        whom you met
       </i>
       are postmodifiers.
      </p>
      <p>
       Notice that in English, simple adjectives are usually used as premodifiers, with occasional exceptions such as
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/galore" title="wikt:galore">
         galore
        </a>
       </i>
       (which always appears after the noun) and the phrases
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/time_immemorial" title="wikt:time immemorial">
         time immemorial
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/court_martial" title="wikt:court martial">
         court martial
        </a>
       </i>
       (the latter comes from
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , where most adjectives are postmodifiers). Sometimes placement of the adjective after the noun entails a change of meaning: compare
       <i>
        a responsible person
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        the person responsible
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        the proper town
       </i>
       (the appropriate town) and
       <i>
        the town proper
       </i>
       (the area of the town as properly defined).
      </p>
      <p>
       It is sometimes possible for a modifier to be separated from its head by other words, as in
       <i>
        The man came who you bumped into in the street yesterday
       </i>
       , where the relative clause
       <i>
        who...yesterday
       </i>
       is separated from the word it modifies (
       <i>
        man
       </i>
       ) by the word
       <i>
        came
       </i>
       . This type of situation is especially likely in languages with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_word_order" title="Free word order">
        free word order
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_modifiers">
        Types of modifiers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_modifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of modifiers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The two principal types of modifiers are
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       (and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjectival_phrase" title="Adjectival phrase">
        adjectival phrases
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adjectival_clause" title="Adjectival clause">
        adjectival clauses
       </a>
       ), which modify nouns; and
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverbs
       </a>
       (and
       <a href="/wiki/Adverbial_phrase" title="Adverbial phrase">
        adverbial phrases
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adverbial_clause" title="Adverbial clause">
        adverbial clauses
       </a>
       ), which modify other parts of speech, particularly verbs, adjectives and other adverbs, as well as whole phrases or clauses. (Not all adjectives and adverbs are necessarily modifiers, however; an adjective will normally be considered a modifier when used
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Attributive_adjective" title="Attributive adjective">
        attributively
       </a>
       , but not when used predicatively – compare the examples with the adjective
       <i>
        red
       </i>
       at the start of this article.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Another type of modifier in some languages, including English, is the
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">
        noun adjunct
       </a>
       , which is a noun modifying another noun (or occasionally another part of speech). An example is
       <i>
        land
       </i>
       in the phrase
       <i>
        land mines
       </i>
       given above.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of the above types of modifiers, in English, are given below.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         It was
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         a
         <b>
          nice
         </b>
         house
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adjective modifying a noun, in a noun phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        [
        <i>
         The
         <b>
          swiftly flowing
         </b>
         waters
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         carried it away.
        </i>
        (adjectival phrase, in this case a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Participial_phrase" title="Participial phrase">
         participial phrase
        </a>
        , modifying a noun in a noun phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         She's
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         the woman
         <b>
          with the hat
         </b>
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adjectival phrase, in this case a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prepositional_phrase" title="Prepositional phrase">
         prepositional phrase
        </a>
        , modifying a noun in a noun phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         I saw
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         the man
         <b>
          whom we met yesterday
         </b>
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adjectival clause, in this case a
        <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">
         relative clause
        </a>
        , modifying a noun in a noun phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         His desk was in
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         the
         <b>
          faculty
         </b>
         office
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (noun adjunct modifying a noun in a noun phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        [
        <i>
         Put it
         <b>
          gently
         </b>
         in the drawer
        </i>
        ]. (adverb in verb phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         He was
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          very
         </b>
         gentle
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adverb in adjective phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         She set it down
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          very
         </b>
         gently
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adverb in adverb phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          Even
         </b>
         more
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         people were there.
        </i>
        (adverb modifying a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(class)" title="Determiner (class)">
         determiner
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         It ran
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          right
         </b>
         up the tree
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        (adverb modifying a prepositional phrase)
       </li>
       <li>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          Only
         </b>
         the dog
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         was saved.
        </i>
        (adverb modifying a noun phrase)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In some cases, noun phrases or
       <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(linguistics)" title="Quantifier (linguistics)">
        quantifiers
       </a>
       can act as modifiers:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          A few
         </b>
         more
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         workers are needed.
        </i>
        (quantifier modifying a determiner)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         She's
        </i>
        [
        <i>
         <b>
          two inches
         </b>
         taller than her sister
        </i>
        ]
        <i>
         .
        </i>
        {noun phrase modifying an adjective}
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguous_and_dangling_modifiers">
        Ambiguous and dangling modifiers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_modifier&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ambiguous and dangling modifiers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dangling_modifier" title="Dangling modifier">
        Dangling modifier
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sometimes it is not clear which element of the sentence a modifier is intended to modify. In many cases this is not important, but in some cases it can lead to genuine
       <a href="/wiki/Ambiguity" title="Ambiguity">
        ambiguity
       </a>
       . For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         He painted her sitting on the step.
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Here the participial phrase
       <i>
        sitting on the step
       </i>
       may be intended to modify
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       (meaning that the painting's subject was sitting on the step), or it may be intended to modify the verb phrase
       <i>
        painted her
       </i>
       or the whole clause
       <i>
        he painted her
       </i>
       (or just
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ), meaning in effect that it was the painter who was sitting on the step.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes the element which the modifier is intended to modify does not in fact appear in the sentence, or is not in an appropriate position to be associated with that modifier. This is often considered a grammatical or stylistic error. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Walking along the road, a vulture loomed overhead.
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Here whoever was "walking along the road" is not mentioned in the sentence, so the modifier (
       <i>
        walking along the road
       </i>
       ) has nothing to modify, except
       <i>
        a vulture
       </i>
       , which is clearly not the intention. Such a case is called a "dangling modifier", or more specifically, in the common case where (as here) the modifier is a participial phrase, a "dangling participle"
      </p>
