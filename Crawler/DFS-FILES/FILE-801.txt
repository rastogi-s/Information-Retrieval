   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Most common words in Spanish
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       Below are two estimates of the most common words in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_Spanish" title="Modern Spanish">
        Modern Spanish
       </a>
       . Each estimate comes from an analysis of a different
       <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">
        text corpus
       </a>
       . A
       <i>
        text corpus
       </i>
       is a large collection of samples of written and/or spoken language, that has been carefully prepared for linguistic analysis. To determine which words are the most common, researchers create a
       <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">
        database
       </a>
       of all the words found in the corpus, and categorise them based on the context in which they are used.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first table lists the 100 most common
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_form" title="Word form">
        word forms
       </a>
       from the Corpus de Referencia del Español Actual (CREA), a text corpus compiled by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       (RAE). The RAE is Spain's official institution for documenting,
       <a href="/wiki/Language_planning" title="Language planning">
        planning
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        standardising
       </a>
       the Spanish language. A
       <i>
        word form
       </i>
       is any of the grammatical variations of a word.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second table is a list of 100 most common
       <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">
        lemmas
       </a>
       found in a text corpus compiled by
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Davies_(linguist)" title="Mark Davies (linguist)">
        Mark Davies
       </a>
       and other language researchers at
       <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">
        Brigham Young University
       </a>
       in the United States. A
       <i>
        lemma
       </i>
       is the primary form of a word—the one that would appear in a dictionary. The Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tener#Spanish" title="wikt:tener">
         tener
        </a>
       </i>
       ("to have") is a lemma, while
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tiene#Spanish" title="wikt:tiene">
         tiene
        </a>
       </i>
       ("has")—which is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verb_conjugation" title="Verb conjugation">
        conjugation
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        tener
       </i>
       —is a word form.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Real_Academia_Espa.C3.B1ola">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Real_Academia_Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Most_common_words_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Real Academia Española">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The list below comes from "1000 formas más frecuentes" (English:
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        1000 most frequent word forms
       </span>
       )", a list published by the Real Academia Española (RAE) from analysis of more than 160 million
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_form" title="Word form">
        word forms
       </a>
       found in the Corpus de Referencia del Español Actual (English:
       <span lang="en" xml:lang="en">
        Reference Corpus of Current Spanish
       </span>
       ), or CREA. CREA is a computerised
       <a href="/wiki/Text_corpus" title="Text corpus">
        corpus
       </a>
       of texts written in Spanish, and of transcripts of spoken Spanish. It includes books, magazines, and newspapers with a wide variety of content, as well as transcripts of spoken language from radio and television broadcasts and other sources. All the works in the collection are from 1975 to 2004. CREA includes samples from all Spanish-speaking countries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CREA_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CREA-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The list of "1000 most frequent word forms" comes from an analysis of CREA
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Software_version" title="Software version">
        version
       </a>
       3.2.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        Plurals
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verb_conjugation" title="Verb conjugation">
        verb conjugations
       </a>
       , and other
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflections
       </a>
       are ranked separately.
       <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">
        Homonyms
       </a>
       , however, are not distinguished from one another. CREA 3.2 was published in June 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CREA_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CREA-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mark_Davies">
        Mark Davies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Most_common_words_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mark Davies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2006,
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Davies_(linguist)" title="Mark Davies (linguist)">
        Mark Davies
       </a>
       , an associate professor of
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Brigham_Young_University" title="Brigham Young University">
        Brigham Young University
       </a>
       , published his estimate of the 5000 most common words in Modern Spanish. To make this list, he compiled samples only from 20th-century sources—especially from the years 1970 to 2000. Most of the sources are from the 1990s. Of the 20 million words in the corpus, about one-third (~6,750,000 words) come from transcripts of spoken Spanish: conversations, interviews, lectures, sermons, press conferences, sports broadcasts, and so on. Among the written sources are novels, plays, short stories, letters, essays, newspapers, and the encyclopedia
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Encarta" title="Encarta">
         Encarta
        </a>
       </i>
       . The samples, written and spoken, come from Spain and at least 10 Latin American countries. Most of the samples were previously compiled for the Corpus del Español (2001), a 100 million-word corpus that includes works from the
       <a href="/wiki/13th_century" title="13th century">
        13th century
       </a>
       through the
       <a href="/wiki/20th_century" title="20th century">
        20th
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">

        </a>

      </p>
      <p>
       The 5000 words in Davies' list are
       <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">
        lemmas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A
       <i>
        lemma
       </i>
       is the form of the word as it would appear in a dictionary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Singular nouns and plurals, for example, are treated as the same word, as are
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitives
       </a>
       and verb conjugations. The table below includes the top 100 words from Davies' list of 5000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This list distinguishes between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite articles
       </a>
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       and the pronouns
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       ; all are ranked individually. The adjectives
       <i>
        ese
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        esa
       </i>
       are ranked together (as are
       <i>
        este
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        esta
       </i>
       ) ), but the pronoun
       <i>
        eso
       </i>
       is separate. All conjugations of a verb are ranked together.
      </p>
