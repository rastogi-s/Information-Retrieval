      <p>
       <b>
        Ethnology
       </b>
       (from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        ἔθνος
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ethnos
       </i>
       meaning "nation"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) is the branch of
       <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
        anthropology
       </a>
       that compares and analyses the characteristics of different
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peoples" title="Peoples">
        peoples
       </a>
       and the relationship between them (cf.
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">
        cultural
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Social_anthropology" title="Social anthropology">
        social
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Sociocultural_anthropology" title="Sociocultural anthropology">
        sociocultural anthropology
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oxford_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-oxford-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_discipline">
        Scientific discipline
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ethnology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scientific discipline">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnicity" title="Ethnicity">
        Ethnicity
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Compared to
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">
        ethnography
       </a>
       , the study of single groups through direct contact with the culture, ethnology takes the research that ethnographers have compiled and then compares and contrasts different cultures.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AFKollar_1779.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="470" data-file-width="423" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/AFKollar_1779.jpg/150px-AFKollar_1779.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/AFKollar_1779.jpg/225px-AFKollar_1779.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/AFKollar_1779.jpg/300px-AFKollar_1779.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AFKollar_1779.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Adam_Franti%C5%A1ek_Koll%C3%A1r" title="Adam František Kollár">
          Adam František Kollár
         </a>
         , 1779
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        ethnologia
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        ethnology
       </i>
       ) is credited to
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_Franti%C5%A1ek_Koll%C3%A1r" title="Adam František Kollár">
        Adam Franz Kollár
       </a>
       (1718-1783) who used and defined it in his
       <i>
        Historiae ivrisqve pvblici Regni Vngariae amoenitates
       </i>
       published in
       <a href="/wiki/Vienna" title="Vienna">
        Vienna
       </a>
       in 1783.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as: “the science of nations and peoples, or, that study of learned men in which they inquire into the origins, languages, customs, and institutions of various nations, and finally into the fatherland and ancient seats, in order to be able better to judge the nations and peoples in their own times.”
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Kollár's interest in linguistic and cultural diversity was aroused by the situation in his native multi-ethnic and multilingual
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Hungary" title="History of Hungary">
        Kingdom of Hungary
       </a>
       and his roots among its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovak_people" title="Slovak people">
        Slovaks
       </a>
       , and by the shifts that began to emerge after the gradual retreat of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       in the more distant
       <a href="/wiki/Balkans#Population_composition_by_nationality_and_religion" title="Balkans">
        Balkans
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the goals of ethnology have been the reconstruction of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_history" title="Human history">
        human history
       </a>
       , and the formulation of
       <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">
        cultural
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_(metaphysics)" title="Universal (metaphysics)">
        invariants
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Incest_taboo" title="Incest taboo">
        incest taboo
       </a>
       and culture change, and the formulation of generalizations about "
       <a href="/wiki/Human_nature" title="Human nature">
        human nature
       </a>
       ", a concept which has been criticized since the 19th century by various philosophers (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hegel" title="Hegel">
        Hegel
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marx" title="Marx">
        Marx
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">
        structuralism
       </a>
       , etc.). In some parts of the world ethnology has developed along independent paths of investigation and
       <a href="/wiki/Pedagogy" title="Pedagogy">
        pedagogical
       </a>
       doctrine, with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cultural_anthropology" title="Cultural anthropology">
         cultural anthropology
        </a>
       </i>
       becoming dominant especially in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Social_anthropology" title="Social anthropology">
         social anthropology
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       . The distinction between the three terms is increasingly blurry. Ethnology has been considered an academic field since the late 18th century especially in Europe and is sometimes conceived of as any comparative study of human groups.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Levi-strauss_260.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2008" data-file-width="3023" height="100" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Levi-strauss_260.jpg/150px-Levi-strauss_260.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Levi-strauss_260.jpg/225px-Levi-strauss_260.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Levi-strauss_260.jpg/300px-Levi-strauss_260.jpg 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Levi-strauss_260.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">
          Claude Lévi-Strauss
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The 15th-century exploration of America by European explorers had an important role in formulating new notions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">
        Occidental
       </a>
       , such as, the notion of the "
       <a href="/wiki/Other_(philosophy)" title="Other (philosophy)">
        Other
       </a>
       ". This term was used in conjunction with "savages", which was either seen as a brutal barbarian, or alternatively, as "
       <a href="/wiki/Noble_savage" title="Noble savage">
        noble savage
       </a>
       ". Thus,
       <a href="/wiki/Civilization" title="Civilization">
        civilization
       </a>
       was opposed in a dualist manner to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barbary" title="Barbary">
        barbary
       </a>
       , a classic opposition constitutive of the even more commonly shared
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnocentrism" title="Ethnocentrism">
        ethnocentrism
       </a>
       . The progress of ethnology, for example with
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">
        Claude Lévi-Strauss
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Structural_anthropology" title="Structural anthropology">
        structural anthropology
       </a>
       , led to the criticism of conceptions of a linear
       <a href="/wiki/Progress_(history)" title="Progress (history)">
        progress
       </a>
       , or the pseudo-opposition between "societies with histories" and "societies without histories", judged too dependent on a limited view of
       <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">
        history
       </a>
       as constituted by accumulative growth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Lévi-Strauss often referred to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montaigne" title="Montaigne">
        Montaigne
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Essays_(Montaigne)" title="Essays (Montaigne)">
        essay
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Cannibalism" title="Cannibalism">
        cannibalism
       </a>
       as an early example of ethnology. Lévi-Strauss aimed, through a
       <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">
        structural method
       </a>
       , at discovering universal invariants in human society, chief among which he believed to be the
       <a href="/wiki/Incest_taboo" title="Incest taboo">
        incest taboo
       </a>
       . However, the claims of such cultural
       <a href="/wiki/Universality_(philosophy)" title="Universality (philosophy)">
        universalism
       </a>
       have been criticized by various 19th and 20th century social thinkers, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marx" title="Marx">
        Marx
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nietzsche" title="Nietzsche">
        Nietzsche
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Michel_Foucault" title="Michel Foucault">
        Foucault
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Derrida" title="Derrida">
        Derrida
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Althusser" title="Althusser">
        Althusser
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deleuze" title="Deleuze">
        Deleuze
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The French school of ethnology was particularly significant for the development of the discipline since the early 1950s with
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rivet" title="Paul Rivet">
        Paul Rivet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Marcel_Griaule" title="Marcel Griaule">
        Marcel Griaule
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Germaine_Dieterlen" title="Germaine Dieterlen">
        Germaine Dieterlen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss" title="Claude Lévi-Strauss">
        Claude Lévi-Strauss
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Rouch" title="Jean Rouch">
        Jean Rouch
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
