          </li>

           <b>

           </b>

          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            Plato
           </b>
           was an influential
           <b>
            philosopher
           </b>
           in ancient
           <b>
            Greece
           </b>
           .
          </li>
          <li>
           Revel the
           <b>
            night
           </b>
           , rob, murder, and commit/The oldest
           <b>
            sins
           </b>
           the newest
           <b>
            kind
           </b>
           of
           <b>
            ways
           </b>
           ? Henry IV Part 2, act 4 scene 5
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          A noun can co-occur with an
          <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
           article
          </a>
          or an
          <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
           attributive adjective
          </a>
          . Verbs and adjectives cannot. In the following, an asterisk (*) in front of an example means that this example is ungrammatical.
         </p>
         <ol>
          <li>
           <b>
            the name
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            name
           </i>
           is a noun: can co-occur with a definite article
           <i>
            the
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            *the baptise
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            baptise
           </i>
           is a verb: cannot co-occur with a definite article.)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            constant circulation
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            circulation
           </i>
           is a noun: can co-occur with the attributive adjective
           <i>
            constant
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            *constant circulate
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            circulate
           </i>
           is a verb: cannot co-occur with the attributive adjective
           <i>
            constant
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            a fright
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            fright
           </i>
           is a noun: can co-occur with the indefinite article
           <i>
            a
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            *an afraid
           </b>
           (
           <i>
            afraid
           </i>
           is an adjective: cannot co-occur with the article
           <i>
            a
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            terrible fright
           </b>
           (The noun
           <i>
            fright
           </i>
           can co-occur with the adjective
           <i>
            terrible
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
          <li>
           <b>
            *terrible afraid
           </b>
           (The adjective
           <i>
            afraid
           </i>
           cannot co-occur with the adjective
           <i>
            terrible
           </i>
           .)
          </li>
         </ol>

          </ul>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        noun
       </b>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        nōmen
       </i>
       , literally meaning "name")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
        word
       </a>
       that functions as the name of some specific thing or set of things, such as living creatures, objects, places, actions, qualities, states of existence, or ideas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        Linguistically
       </a>
       , a noun is a member of a large,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_class_(linguistics)" title="Open class (linguistics)">
        open
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
        part of speech
       </a>
       whose members can occur as the main word in the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Clause" title="Clause">
        clause
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       , or the object of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lexical categories (parts of speech) are defined in terms of the ways in which their members combine with other kinds of expressions. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic" title="Syntactic">
        syntactic
       </a>
       rules for nouns differ from language to language. In
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , nouns are those words which can occur with
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        articles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        attributive adjectives
       </a>
       and can function as the
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
        <span id="Substantive_as_a_word_for_noun">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech#History" title="Part of speech">
        History of parts of speech
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_class" title="Word class">
        Word classes
       </a>
       (parts of speech) were described by
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       grammarians from at least the 5th century BC. In
       <a href="/wiki/Y%C4%81ska" title="Yāska">
        Yāska
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nirukta" title="Nirukta">
         Nirukta
        </a>
       </i>
       , the noun (
       <i>
        nāma
       </i>
       ) is one of the four main categories of words defined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matilal_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matilal-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       equivalent was
       <i>
        ónoma
       </i>
       (ὄνομα), referred to by
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cratylus_(dialogue)" title="Cratylus (dialogue)">
        <i>
         Cratylus
        </i>
        dialog
       </a>
       , and later listed as one of the eight parts of speech in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Grammar" title="The Art of Grammar">
         The Art of Grammar
        </a>
       </i>
       , attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Thrax" title="Dionysius Thrax">
        Dionysius Thrax
       </a>
       (2nd century BC). The term used in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_grammar" title="Latin grammar">
        Latin grammar
       </a>
       was
       <i>
        nōmen
       </i>
       . All of these terms for "noun" were also words meaning "name".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The English word
       <i>
        noun
       </i>
       is derived from the Latin term, through the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman
       </a>
       <i>
        noun
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The word classes were defined partly by the grammatical
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        forms
       </a>
       that they take. In Sanskrit, Greek and Latin, for example, nouns are categorized by
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       and inflected for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . Because
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       share these three
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical categories
       </a>
       , adjectives are placed in the same class as nouns.
       <br/>
       Similarly, the Latin
       <i>
        nōmen
       </i>
       includes both nouns (substantives) and adjectives, as originally did the English word
       <i>
        noun
       </i>
       , the two types being distinguished as
       <i>
        nouns substantive
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        nouns adjective
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        substantive nouns
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        adjective nouns
       </i>
       , or short
       <i>
        substantives
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        adjectives
       </i>
       ). (The word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nominal_(linguistics)" title="Nominal (linguistics)">
         nominal
        </a>
       </i>
       is now sometimes used to denote a class that includes both nouns and adjectives.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Many European languages use a
       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
        cognate
       </a>
       of the word
       <i>
        substantive
       </i>
       as the basic term for noun (for example, Spanish
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sustantivo" title="wikt:sustantivo">
         sustantivo
        </a>
       </i>
       , "noun"). Nouns in the dictionaries of such languages are demarked by the abbreviation
       <i>
        s.
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        sb.
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        n.
       </i>
       , which may be used for proper nouns or neuter nouns instead. In English, some modern authors use the word
       <i>
        substantive
       </i>
       to refer to a class that includes both nouns (single words) and
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrases
       </a>
       (multiword units, also called noun equivalents).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CMOS_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CMOS-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It can also be used as a counterpart to
       <i>
        attributive
       </i>
       when distinguishing between a noun being used as the
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head
       </a>
       (main word) of a noun phrase and a noun being used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_adjunct" title="Noun adjunct">
        noun adjunct
       </a>
       . For example, the noun
       <i>
        knee
       </i>
       can be said to be used substantively in
       <i>
        my knee hurts
       </i>
       , but attributively in
       <i>
        the patient needed knee replacement
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_of_nouns">
        Definitions of nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Definitions of nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nouns have sometimes been defined in terms of the grammatical categories to which they are subject (classed by gender, inflected for case and number). Such definitions tend to be language-specific, since nouns do not have the same categories in all languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nouns are frequently defined, particularly in informal contexts, in terms of their
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantic
       </a>
       properties (their meanings). Nouns are described as words that refer to a
       <i>
        person
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        place
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        thing
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        event
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        substance
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        quality
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        quantity
       </i>
       , etc. However this type of definition has been criticized by contemporary linguists as being uninformative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jackendoff_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jackendoff-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There have been offered several examples of English-language nouns which do not have any reference:
       <i>
        drought
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        enjoyment
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        finesse
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/behalf" title="wikt:behalf">
         behalf
        </a>
       </i>
       (as found in
       <i>
        on behalf of
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dint" title="wikt:dint">
         dint
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        in dint of
       </i>
       ), and
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/sake" title="wikt:sake">
         sake
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        for the sake of
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Moreover, there may be a relationship similar to reference in the case of other parts of speech: the verbs
       <i>
        to rain
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        to mother
       </i>
       ; many adjectives, like
       <i>
        red
       </i>
       ; and there is little difference between the adverb
       <i>
        gleefully
       </i>
       and the noun-based phrase
       <i>
        with glee
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [note 2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Placeholder_names&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Placeholder names (page does not exist)">
        placeholder names
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Legal_fiction" title="Legal fiction">
        legal fiction
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reasonable_person" title="Reasonable person">
         reasonable person
        </a>
       </i>
       (whose existence is not in question), an experimental
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Artifact_(error)" title="Artifact (error)">
         artifact
        </a>
       </i>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Anthropomorphism" title="Anthropomorphism">
        personifications
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gremlin" title="Gremlin">
         gremlin
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        Linguists
       </a>
       often prefer to define nouns (and other lexical categories) in terms of their formal properties. These include
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       information, such as what
       <a href="/wiki/Prefix" title="Prefix">
        prefixes
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        suffixes
       </a>
       they take, and also their
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       – how they combine with other words and expressions of particular types. Such definitions may nonetheless still be language-specific, since syntax as well as morphology varies between languages. For example, in English it might be noted that nouns are words that can co-occur with definite articles (as stated at the start of this article), but this would not apply in
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       , which has no definite articles.
      </p>
      <p>
       There have been several attempts, sometimes controversial, to produce a stricter definition of nouns on a semantic basis. Some of these are referenced in the
       <a href="#Further_reading">
        § Further reading
       </a>
       section below.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">
        Gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        Grammatical gender
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In some languages, genders are assigned to nouns, such as masculine, feminine and neuter (or other combinations). The gender of a noun (as well as its number and case, where applicable) will often entail
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       in words that modify or are related to it. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , the singular form of the definite article is
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       with masculine nouns and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       with feminines; adjectives and certain verb forms also change (with the addition of
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       with feminines). Grammatical gender often correlates with the form of the noun and the inflection pattern it follows; for example, in both
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       most nouns ending
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       are feminine. Gender can also correlate with the
       <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">
        sex
       </a>
       of the noun's referent, particularly in the case of nouns denoting people (and sometimes animals). Nouns arguably do not have gender in Modern English, although many of them denote people or animals of a specific sex, and pronouns that refer to nouns must take the appropriate gender for that noun. (The
       <b>
        girl
       </b>
       lost
       <b>
        her
       </b>
       spectacles.)
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification_of_nouns">
        Classification of nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification of nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proper_nouns_and_common_nouns">
        Proper nouns and common nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proper nouns and common nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_noun" title="Proper noun">
        Proper noun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <i>
        proper noun
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        proper name
       </i>
       is a noun representing unique entities (such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
         Pakistan
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pegasus" title="Pegasus">
         Pegasus
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
         Jupiter
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Confucius" title="Confucius">
         Confucius
        </a>
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pequod_(Moby-Dick)" title="Pequod (Moby-Dick)">
         Pequod
        </a>
       </i>
       ), as distinguished from common nouns which describe a class of entities (such as
       <i>
        country
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        animal
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        planet
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        person
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ship
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">

        </a>
       </sup>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Countable_and_uncountable_nouns">
        Countable and uncountable nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Countable and uncountable nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Count_noun" title="Count noun">
        Count noun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_noun" title="Mass noun">
        Mass noun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Count nouns
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        countable nouns
       </i>
       are common nouns that can take a
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        plural
       </a>
       , can combine with
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_system" title="Numeral system">
        numerals
       </a>
       or counting
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantifiers_(linguistics)" title="Quantifiers (linguistics)">
        quantifiers
       </a>
       (e.g.,
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        two
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        several
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        every
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        most
       </i>
       ), and can take an indefinite article such as
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        an
       </i>
       (in languages which have such articles). Examples of count nouns are
       <i>
        chair
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        nose
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        occasion
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Mass nouns
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        uncountable
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        non-count
       </i>
       )
       <i>
        nouns
       </i>
       differ from count nouns in precisely that respect: they cannot take plurals or combine with number words or the above type of quantifiers. For example, it is not possible to refer to
       <i>
        a furniture
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        three furnitures
       </i>
       . This is true even though the pieces of furniture comprising
       <i>
        furniture
       </i>
       could be counted. Thus the distinction between mass and count nouns should not be made in terms of what sorts of things the nouns refer to, but rather in terms of how the nouns
       <i>
        present
       </i>
       these entities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many nouns have both countable and uncountable uses; for example,
       <i>
        beer
       </i>
       is countable in "give me three beers", but uncountable in "he likes beer".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collective_nouns">
        Collective nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Collective nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">
        Collective noun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Collective nouns
       </i>
       are nouns that – even when they are inflected for the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        singular
       </a>
       – refer to
       <i>
        groups
       </i>
       consisting of more than one individual or entity. Examples include
       <i>
        committee
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        government
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        police
       </i>
       . In English these nouns may be followed by a singular or a plural verb and referred to by a singular or plural pronoun, the singular being generally preferred when referring to the body as a unit and the plural often being preferred, especially in British English, when emphasizing the individual members.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gowers_2014_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gowers_2014-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Examples of acceptable and unacceptable use given by Gowers in
       <i>
        Plain Words
       </i>
       include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gowers_2014_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gowers_2014-16">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "A committee
        <i>
         was
        </i>
        appointed to consider this subject." (singular)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "The committee
        <i>
         were
        </i>
        unable to agree." (plural)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        * "The committee were of one mind when I sat on them" (unacceptable use of plural)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Concrete_nouns_and_abstract_nouns">
        Concrete nouns and abstract nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Concrete nouns and abstract nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">
        Physical body
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_object" title="Abstract object">
        Abstract object
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Concrete nouns
       </i>
       refer to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_bodies" title="Physical bodies">
        physical entities
       </a>
       that can, in principle at least
       <i>
        (i.e. different schools of philosophy and sciences may question the assumption, but, for the most part, people agree to the existence of something. E.g. a rock, a tree, universe)
       </i>
       , be observed by at least one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sense" title="Sense">
        senses
       </a>
       (for instance,
       <i>
        chair
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        apple
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Janet
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        atom
       </i>
       ).
       <i>
        Abstract nouns
       </i>
       , on the other hand, refer to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abstract_object" title="Abstract object">
        abstract objects
       </a>
       ; that is, ideas or concepts (such as
       <i>
        justice
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        hatred
       </i>
       ). While this distinction is sometimes exclusive, some nouns have multiple senses, including both concrete and abstract ones: consider, for example, the noun
       <i>
        art
       </i>
       , which usually refers to a concept (e.g.,
       <i>
        Art is an important element of human culture.
       </i>
       ) but which can refer to a specific artwork in certain contexts (e.g.,
       <i>
        I put my daughter's art up on the fridge.
       </i>
       )
      </p>
      <p>
       Some abstract nouns developed etymologically by figurative extension from literal roots. These include
       <i>
        drawback
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        fraction
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        holdout
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        uptake
       </i>
       . Similarly, some nouns have both abstract and concrete senses, with the latter having developed by figurative extension from the former. These include
       <i>
        view
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        filter
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        structure
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        key
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, many abstract nouns are formed by adding a suffix (
       <i>
        -ness
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ity
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ion
       </i>
       ) to adjectives or verbs. Examples are
       <i>
        happiness
       </i>
       (from the adjective
       <i>
        happy
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        circulation
       </i>
       (from the verb
       <i>
        circulate
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        serenity
       </i>
       (from the adjective
       <i>
        serene
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alienable_vs._Inalienable_Nouns">
        Alienable vs. Inalienable Nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alienable vs. Inalienable Nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some world languages refer to nouns differently, depending on how ownership is being given for the given noun. This can be broken into two categories: Alienable and
       <a href="/wiki/Inalienable_possession" title="Inalienable possession">
        Inalienable
       </a>
       . An alienable noun is something that does not belong to a person indefinitely. Inalienable nouns, on the other hand, is something that is possessed definitely. Examples of alienable nouns would be a tree or a shirt or roads. Examples of inalienable nouns would be a father or shadow or hair.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pingelapese">
        Pingelapese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Pingelapese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Pingelapese_language" title="Pingelapese language">
        Pingelapese language
       </a>
       uses a distinction between nouns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are several classifier forms. the first is for objects which tend to be pretty large in size and not being a favorite possession (tree or shirt), the second is for small, controllable, favorite objects like dogs, books or spears. A third form would be set aside for food objects like bananas, oranges or fish. Drinks like water or coconut liquor also have a classifier forms. A fifth classifier would be designated for things that are to be chewed but not fully consumed. The only example of this was from the book
       <i>
        Papers in Kosraean and Ponapeic,
       </i>
       the fruit, pandanus, is chewed for the sweet/ bitter juice, but what remains after consuming the juice discarded. The 6th classifier forms are set aside for ways of transportation (bikes, canoes, and boats). The last two classifiers are designated for the land and the house.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_phrases">
        Noun phrases
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Noun phrases">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        Noun phrase
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A noun phrase is a phrase based on a noun, pronoun, or other noun-like word (nominal) optionally accompanied by modifiers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       . A noun phrase functions within a clause or sentence in a role such as that of
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       , or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complement_(grammar)" title="Complement (grammar)">
        complement
       </a>
       of a verb or preposition. For example, in the sentence "The black cat sat on a dear friend of mine", the noun phrase
       <i>
        the black cat
       </i>
       serves as the subject, and the noun phrase
       <i>
        a dear friend of mine
       </i>
       serves as the complement of the preposition
       <i>
        on
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">
        Pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        Pronoun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Nouns and noun phrases can typically be replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       , such as
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        which
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        those
       </i>
       , in order to avoid repetition or explicit identification, or for other reasons. For example, in the sentence
       <i>
        Gareth thought that he was weird
       </i>
       , the word
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       is a pronoun standing in place of the person's name. The word
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       can replace parts of noun phrases, and it sometimes stands in for a noun. An example is given below:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        John's car is newer than
        <i>
         the one
        </i>
        that Bill has.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       But
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       can also stand in for larger parts of a noun phrase. For example, in the following example,
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       can stand in for
       <i>
        new car
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        This new car is cheaper than
        <i>
         that one
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nominalization">
        Nominalization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nominalization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Nominalization" title="Nominalization">
        Nominalization
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Nominalization is a process whereby a word that belongs to another part of speech comes to be used as a noun. In French and Spanish, for example, adjectives frequently act as nouns referring to people who have the characteristics denoted by the adjective. This sometimes happens in English as well, as in the following examples:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        This legislation will have the most impact on the
        <i>
         poor
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The race is not to the
        <i>
         swift
        </i>
        , nor the battle to the
        <i>
         strong
        </i>
        .
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The Socialist
        <i>
         International
        </i>
        is a worldwide association of political parties.
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Further_reading">
        Further reading
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Noun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Further reading">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Laycock, Henry (2005). "
        <a class="external text" href="http://post.queensu.ca/%7Elaycockh/Mass%20nouns%20%20Count%20nouns%20%20Non-count%20nouns.pdf" rel="nofollow">
         Mass nouns, Count nouns and Non-count nouns
        </a>
        ", Draft version of entry in
        <i>
         Encyclopedia of Language and Linguistics
        </i>
        Oxford: Elsevier.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For definitions of nouns based on the concept of "identity criteria":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Geach, Peter. 1962.
        <i>
         Reference and Generality.
        </i>
        Cornell University Press.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For more on identity criteria:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Gupta, Anil. 1980,
        <i>
         The logic of common nouns.
        </i>
        New Haven and London: Yale University Press.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For the concept that nouns are "prototypically referential":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Croft, William. 1993. "A noun is a noun is a noun — or is it? Some reflections on the universality of semantics". Proceedings of the Nineteenth Annual Meeting of the Berkeley Linguistics Society, ed. Joshua S. Guenter, Barbara A. Kaiser and Cheryl C. Zoll, 369-80. Berkeley: Berkeley Linguistics Society.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For an attempt to relate the concepts of identity criteria and prototypical referentiality:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Baker, Mark. 2003, Lexical Categories: verbs, nouns, and adjectives. Cambridge University Press, Cambridge.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Understanding nouns in the context of WordNet:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.text-analytics101.com/2014/10/demystifying-nouns-in-wordnet.html" rel="nofollow">
