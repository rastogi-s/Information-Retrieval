      <p>
       <b>
        Judeo-Italian
       </b>
       , also referred to as
       <b>
        Italkian
       </b>
       , is an endangered
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_language" title="Jewish language">
        Jewish language
       </a>
       , with only about 200 speakers in Italy and 250 total speakers today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ethnologue_Data_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ethnologue_Data-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The language is one of
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Italian_dialects" title="Italian dialects">
        Italian dialects
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Italian_Dialect_or_Jewish_Language?_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Italian_Dialect_or_Jewish_Language?-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some words have Italian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prefixes" title="Prefixes">
        prefixes
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suffixes" title="Suffixes">
        suffixes
       </a>
       added to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       words as well as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       roots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hebrew_Study_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hebrew_Study-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="The_term_.22Judeo-Italian.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='The_term_"Judeo-Italian"'>
        The term "Judeo-Italian"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title='Edit section: The term "Judeo-Italian"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The glossonym type
       <i>
        giudeo-italiano
       </i>
       is of academic and relatively late coinage. In
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Judeo-Italian
       </i>
       was first used by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lazaro_Belleli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lazaro Belleli (page does not exist)">
        Lazaro Belleli
       </a>
       in 1904 for his article
       <i>
        Judæo-Greek and Judæo-Italian
       </i>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_Encyclopedia" title="Jewish Encyclopedia">
        Jewish Encyclopedia
       </a>
       (vol. 7, 310-313), describing the languages of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jew" title="Jew">
        Jews
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">
        Corfu
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Italian,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Giuseppe_Cammeo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Giuseppe Cammeo (page does not exist)">
        Giuseppe Cammeo
       </a>
       referred to a
       <i>
        Gergo giudaico-italiano
       </i>
       in his 1909 article
       <i>
        Studj dialettali
       </i>
       (Vessillo Israelitico 57 (1909); the term first appears on p. 169). That same year,
       <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Cassuto" title="Umberto Cassuto">
        Umberto Cassuto
       </a>
       used the term
       <i>
        giudeo-italiano
       </i>
       , in the following:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Actually, while the existence of a Judæo-German dialect is universally known, almost nobody
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transalpine" title="Transalpine">
         beyond the Alps
        </a>
        suspects that the Italian Jews have, or at least had, not to say a dialect of their own, but at least a way of speaking with peculiar features. True, in practice its importance, limited to the everyday use of some thousand people, is almost nothing versus that of Judæo-German, spoken by millions of individuals that often do not know any other language, and has
        <a href="/wiki/Yiddish_literature" title="Yiddish literature">
         its own literature
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_press" title="Yiddish press">
         its own journalism
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_theater" title="Yiddish theater">
         its own theater
        </a>
        , and thus, almost the importance of a real language... It is almost nothing, if you will, even compared with other Jewish dialects,
        <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
         Judæo-Spanish
        </a>
        for instance, that are more or less used literally; all this is true, but from the linguistic point of view, Judæo-German is worth as much as Judæo-Italian [
        <i>
         giudeo-italiano
        </i>
        ], to name it so, since for the
        <a href="/wiki/Philology" title="Philology">
         glottological science
        </a>
        the different forms of human speech are important in themselves and not by its number of speakers or the artistic forms they are used in. Moreover, a remarkable difference between Judæo-German and Judæo-Italian [
        <i>
         giudeo-italiano
        </i>
        ], that is also valuable from the scientific point of view, is that while the former is so different from German as to constitute an independent dialect, the latter by contrast is not essentially a different thing from the language of Italy, or from the individual dialects of the different provinces of Italy...
       </dd>
       <dd>
        256:
       </dd>
       <dd>
        ...It was natural that the Judæo-Italian jargon [
        <i>
         gergo giudeo-italiano
        </i>
        ] would disappear in a short while.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Cassuto" title="Umberto Cassuto">
         Umberto Cassuto
        </a>
        “Parlata ebraica.” Vessillo Israelitico 57 (1909): 255-256)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cassuto!_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Cassuto!-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biblical_Representations_of_Judeo-Italian">
        Biblical Representations of Judeo-Italian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Biblical Representations of Judeo-Italian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One of the most accessible ways to view the Judeo-Italian language is by looking at translations in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Biblical_texts&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Biblical texts (page does not exist)">
        biblical texts
       </a>
       such as the Torah and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hagiographia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hagiographia (page does not exist)">
        Hagiographia
       </a>
       . Today, there are two locations, the Oxford Bodleian Library and the Jewish Theological Seminary in New York, in which these translated texts can be found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Handbook_of_Jewish_Languages_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Handbook_of_Jewish_Languages-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_designations">
        Other designations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Other designations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Historically, Italian Jews referred to their vernaculars as "
        <i>
         La`az
        </i>
        " (לעז),
        <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
         Hebrew
        </a>
        for "foreign language" (
        <i>
         i.e.
        </i>
        , specifically, "non-Hebrew language"). The Italian Jewish rite is sometimes called
        <i>
         minhag ha-lo'azim
        </i>
        , and linguists use
        <i>
         lo'ez
        </i>
        as a description of words of Romance origin in
        <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">
         Yiddish
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This may be connected with the
        <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
         Germanic
        </a>
        use of the word
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walha" title="Walha">
          wälsch
         </a>
        </i>
        (literally, "foreign") for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_peoples" title="Romance peoples">
         Romance peoples
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
         languages
        </a>
        (as in "Welsh", "Walloon" and "Wallachian"): the Italian and Sephardic Hebrew script for Torah scrolls is known as "Velsh" or "Veilish".
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1587,
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_de_Pomi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David de Pomi (page does not exist)">
         David de Pomi
        </a>
        uses the word "italiano" in reference to the
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        glosses in his trilingual dictionary. The Hebrew title of the 1609 Venice Haggadah uses the word "italiano" for the language of
        <a href="/wiki/Leon_of_Modena" title="Leon of Modena">
         Leone Modena
        </a>
        's translation (u-fitrono bi-leshon iṭalyano; ופתרונו בלשון איטליאנו).
       </li>
       <li>
        Other historic descriptions are "
        <i>
         Latino
        </i>
        " and "
        <i>
         Volgare
        </i>
        ", both of which were commonly used in the Middle Ages to mean Italian in general.
       </li>
       <li>
        After the institution of the
        <a href="/wiki/Ghetto" title="Ghetto">
         Ghetto
        </a>
        forced Jewish communities throughout Italy into segregation, the term
        <b>
         ghettaiolo
        </b>
        was identified with local Jewish varieties of regional dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        Another native name type is
        <i>
         giudeesco
        </i>
        (e.g., Judeo-Florentine
        <i>
         iodiesco
        </i>
        ; &lt; *IUDÆĬSCU[M], or an assimilation of the hiatus
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aˈe/
        </span>
        *giudaesco &lt; *IUDAĬSCU[M]).
       </li>
       <li>
        The neologism
        <b>
         Italkian
        </b>
        was coined in 1942 by Solomon Birnbaum (
        <i>
         see
         <a href="#References">
          References
         </a>
        </i>
        ), who modelled the word on the modern Hebrew adjective ית-/אטלקי
        <i>
         italki(t)
        </i>
        , “Italian”, from the middle Hebrew adjective איטלקי (&lt; ITALICU[M]), “Italic” or “Roman”.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_Yiddish">
        Influence on Yiddish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Influence on Yiddish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to some scholars, there are some Judeo-Italian loan words that have found their way into
       <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">
        Yiddish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Italian_Dialect_or_Jewish_Language?_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Italian_Dialect_or_Jewish_Language?-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the word in Judeo-Italian for "synagogue" is
       <i>
        scola
       </i>
       , as opposed to "school," which is
       <i>
        scuola
       </i>
       . The use of words for "school" to mean "synagogue" dates back to the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       . The Judeo-Italian distinction between
       <i>
        scola
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        scuola
       </i>
       parallels the Standard Yiddish distinction between
       <i>
        shul/shil
       </i>
       for 'synagogue' and
       <i>
        shule
       </i>
       for 'school'. Another example is
       <i>
        iente
       </i>
       , from the Judeo-Italian yientile, as differentiated from the standard Italian
       <i>
        gentile
       </i>
       , meaning 'noble'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">
        Dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Judeo-Italian regional dialects (
       <i>
        ghettaioli
       </i>
       <i>
        giudeeschi
       </i>
       ), including:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Ferraran&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Ferraran (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Ferraran
        </a>
        (giudeo-ferrarese) from
        <a href="/wiki/Ferrara" title="Ferrara">
         Ferrara
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Florentine&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Florentine (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Florentine
        </a>
        (giudeo-fiorentino, iodiesco) from
        <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
         Florence
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Mantuan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Mantuan (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Mantuan
        </a>
        (giudeo-mantovano) from
        <a href="/wiki/Mantua" title="Mantua">
         Mantua
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Modenan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Modenan (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Modenan
        </a>
        (giudeo-modenese) from
        <a href="/wiki/Modena" title="Modena">
         Modena
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Piedmontese" title="Judaeo-Piedmontese">
         Judaeo-Piedmontese
        </a>
        (giudeo-piemontese) from the region of
        <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
         Piedmont
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Reggian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Reggian (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Reggian
        </a>
        (giudeo-reggiano) from the region of
        <a href="/wiki/Reggio_Emilia" title="Reggio Emilia">
         Reggio Emilia
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
         Emilia-Romagna
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judeo-Roman" title="Judeo-Roman">
         Judeo-Roman
        </a>
        (giudeo-romanesco) from
        <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
         Rome
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Venetian&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Judeo-Venetian (page does not exist)">
         Judeo-Venetian
        </a>
        (giudeo-veneziano) from
        <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
         Venice
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bagitto&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bagitto (page does not exist)">
         Bagitto
        </a>
        (giudeo-livornese) from
        <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">
         Livorno
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       At least two Judeo-Italian varieties, based on
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Salentino
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       varieties were also used in
       <a href="/wiki/Corfu" title="Corfu">
        Corfu
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (see relevant section in
       <a href="/wiki/Corfiot_Italians#Italkian_with_Venetian/Apulian_influences" title="Corfiot Italians">
        Corfiot Italians
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All of the spoken Judeo-Italian varieties used a unique (among
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_languages" title="Jewish languages">
        Jewish languages
       </a>
       , although there are arguably parallels in
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_English_languages" title="Jewish English languages">
        Jewish English
       </a>
       usage) combination of
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       verb stems with
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       conjugations (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , "אכלר
       <i>
        akhlare
       </i>
       ", to eat; "גנביר
       <i>
        gannaviare
       </i>
       ", to steal; "דברר
       <i>
        dabberare
       </i>
       ", to speak; "לכטיר
       <i>
        lekhtire
       </i>
       ", to go). Similarly there are abstract nouns such as "טובזה
       <i>
        tovezza
       </i>
       ", goodness.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also common are lexical incorporations from
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       , particularly those applicable to daily life. Terms from other Jewish languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Yiddish" title="Yiddish">
        Yiddish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       were also incorporated.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bagitto, the dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Livorno" title="Livorno">
        Livorno
       </a>
       , is particularly rich in
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       from Judaeo-Spanish and
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Portuguese" title="Judaeo-Portuguese">
        Judaeo-Portuguese
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was claimed by Cassuto that most Judeo-Italian dialects reflect the Italian dialect of places further to the south, due to the fact that since the expulsion of the Jews from the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">
        Kingdom of Naples
       </a>
       the general direction of Jewish migration in Italy has been northward.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visuals_of_the_Language">
        Visuals of the Language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Visuals of the Language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Judeo-Italian language can be found in a
       <a href="/wiki/Haggadah" title="Haggadah">
        Haggadah
       </a>
       , a Jewish prayer book typically used during a seder. Judeo-Italian is represented in this
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=1716_Venice_Haggadah.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="1716 Venice Haggadah. (page does not exist)">
        1716 Venice Haggadah.
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Venice_Haggadah_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Venice_Haggadah-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Library_of_Congress.2FISO_information">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Library_of_Congress/ISO_information">
        Library of Congress/ISO information
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Judeo-Italian_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Library of Congress/ISO information">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       "Italkian" is not used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
        Library of Congress
       </a>
       as a subject heading, neither does it figure as a reference to Judeo-Italian. The authorized subject heading is "Judeo-Italian language". Subheadings are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Judeo-Italian language: Glossaries, vocabularies, etc.
       </li>
       <li>
        Judeo-Italian language: Grammar.
       </li>
       <li>
        Judeo-Italian language: Italy Livorno Glossaries, vocabularies, etc.
       </li>
       <li>
        Judeo-Italian language: Texts.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The subject reference is: Judæo-Italian dialect. LC-MARC uses the following language codes : Judeo-Italian Assigned collective code [ita] (Italian).
      </p>
      <p>
       This is in compliance with the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
        International Organization for Standardization
       </a>
       language code ISO 639-2 code (roa).
      </p>

            Infatti, mentre è universalmente nota l’esistenza di un dialetto giudeo-tedesco, quasi nessuno sospetta oltr’alpe che gli ebrei italiani abbiano pure, o almeno abbiano avuto, non dirò un loro dialetto, ma almeno una loro parlata con peculiari caratteri. Certo, praticamente l’importanza di essa, limitata all’uso quotidiano di poche migliaia di persone, è pressoché nulla di fronte a quella del giudeo-tedesco, il quale è parlato da milioni di individui che bene spesso non conoscono altra lingua, ed ha una propria letteratura, un proprio giornalismo, un proprio teatro, sì da assumere quasi l’importanza di una vera e propria lingua a sé … è pressoché nulla, se si vuole, anche a paragone di altri dialetti giudaici, del giudeo-spagnuolo ad esempio, che sono più o meno usati letterariamente; è vero tutto questo, ma dal punto di vista linguistico tanto vale il giudeo-tedesco, quanto il giudeo-italiano, se così vogliamo chiamarlo, giacché di fronte alla scienza glottologica le varie forme del parlare umano hanno importanza di per sé e non per il numero di persone che le usano o per le forme d’arte in cui vengono adoperate.
