      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        pronoun
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <b>
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          PRO
         </span>
        </span>
       </b>
       ) is a word that substitutes for a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       . It is a particular case of a
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-form" title="Pro-form">
        pro-form
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Pronouns have traditionally been regarded as one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech" title="Part of speech">
        parts of speech
       </a>
       , but some modern theorists would not consider them to form a single class, in view of the variety of functions they perform. Subtypes include
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
        reflexive
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Reciprocal_pronoun" title="Reciprocal pronoun">
        reciprocal
       </a>
       pronouns,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_pronoun" title="Possessive pronoun">
        possessive pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstrative_pronoun" title="Demonstrative pronoun">
        demonstrative pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_pronoun" title="Relative pronoun">
        relative pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interrogative_pronoun" title="Interrogative pronoun">
        interrogative pronouns
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronoun" title="Indefinite pronoun">
        indefinite pronouns
       </a>
       .

        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of pronouns often involves
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
        anaphora
       </a>
       , where the meaning of the pronoun is dependent on an
       <a href="/wiki/Antecedent_(grammar)" title="Antecedent (grammar)">
        antecedent
       </a>
       . This applies especially to third-person personal pronouns and relative pronouns. For example, in the sentence
       <i>
        That poor man looks as if he needs a new coat
       </i>
       , the antecedent of the pronoun
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       is the noun phrase
       <i>
        that poor man
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       associated with
       <i>
        pronoun
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        <b>
         pronominal
        </b>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [A]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A pronominal is also a word or phrase that acts as a pronoun. For example, in
       <i>
        That's not the one I wanted
       </i>
       , the phrase
       <i>
        the one
       </i>
       (containing the
       <a href="/wiki/Prop-word" title="Prop-word">
        prop-word
       </a>
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       ) is a pronominal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal">
        Personal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Personal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        Personal pronoun
       </a>
      </div>

      <p>
       Personal pronouns may be classified by
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       . English has three persons (first, second and third) and two numbers (singular and plural); in the third person singular there are also distinct pronoun forms for male, female and neuter gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         52–53
        </span>
       </sup>
       Principal forms are shown in the adjacent table (see also
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        English personal pronouns
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       English personal pronouns have two cases,
       <i>
        subject
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        object
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_pronoun" title="Subject pronoun">
        Subject pronouns
       </a>
       are used in
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       position (
       <i>
        <b>
         I
        </b>
        like to eat chips, but
        <b>
         she
        </b>
        does not
       </i>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Object_pronoun" title="Object pronoun">
        Object pronouns
       </a>
       are used for the
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       of a verb or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        John likes
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        but not
        <b>
         her
        </b>
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         52–53
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other distinct forms found in some languages include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
         Second person informal and formal pronouns
        </a>
        (the T-V distinction), like
        <i>
         tu
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         vous
        </i>
        in French. There is no such distinction in standard modern English, though Elizabethan English marked the distinction with
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Thou" title="Thou">
          thou
         </a>
        </i>
        (singular informal) and
        <i>
         you
        </i>
        (plural or singular formal), and this is preserved in some dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_and_exclusive_we" title="Inclusive and exclusive we">
         Inclusive and exclusive first person plural pronouns
        </a>
        , which indicate whether or not the audience is included, that is, whether "we" means "you and I" or "they and I". There is no such distinction in English.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Intensive_pronoun" title="Intensive pronoun">
         Intensive (emphatic) pronouns
        </a>
        , which re-emphasize a noun or pronoun that has already been mentioned. English uses the same forms as the reflexive pronouns; for example:
        <i>
         I did it
         <b>
          myself
         </b>
        </i>
        (contrast reflexive use,
        <i>
         I did it to myself
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Direct and indirect object pronouns, such as
        <i>
         le
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         lui
        </i>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/French_personal_pronouns" title="French personal pronouns">
         French
        </a>
        . English uses the same form for both; for example:
        <i>
         Mary loves
         <b>
          him
         </b>
        </i>
        (direct object);
        <i>
         Mary sent
         <b>
          him
         </b>
         a letter
        </i>
        (indirect object).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Prepositional_pronoun" title="Prepositional pronoun">
         Prepositional pronouns
        </a>
        , used after a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
         preposition
        </a>
        . English uses ordinary object pronouns here:
        <i>
         Mary looked at
         <b>
          him
         </b>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Disjunctive_pronoun" title="Disjunctive pronoun">
         Disjunctive pronouns
        </a>
        , used in isolation or in certain other special grammatical contexts, like
        <i>
         moi
        </i>
        in French. No distinct forms exist in English; for example:
        <i>
         Who does this belong to?
         <b>
          Me
         </b>
         .
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Weak_pronoun" title="Weak pronoun">
         Strong and weak forms
        </a>
        of certain pronouns, found in some languages such as Polish.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some special uses of personal pronouns include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Generic_you" title="Generic you">
         Generic
         <i>
          you
         </i>
        </a>
        , where second person pronouns are used in an indefinite sense:
        <i>
         <b>
          You
         </b>
         can't buy good old-fashioned bulbs these days.
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_they" title="Generic they">
         Generic
         <i>
          they
         </i>
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         In China
         <b>
          they
         </b>
         drive on the right.
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-specific_and_gender-neutral_pronouns" title="Gender-specific and gender-neutral pronouns">
         Gender non-specific
        </a>
        uses, where a pronoun needs to be found to refer to a person whose sex is not specified. Solutions sometimes used in English include
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_he" title="Generic he">
         generic
         <i>
          he
         </i>
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Singular_they" title="Singular they">
         singular
         <i>
          they
         </i>
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
         Dummy pronouns
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_expletive" title="Syntactic expletive">
         expletive
        </a>
        pronouns), used to satisfy a grammatical requirement for a noun or pronoun, but contributing nothing to meaning:
        <i>
         <b>
          It
         </b>
         is raining.
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Resumptive_pronoun" title="Resumptive pronoun">
         Resumptive pronouns
        </a>
        , "intrusive" personal pronouns found (for example) in some relative clauses where a gap (
        <a href="/wiki/Trace_(linguistics)" title="Trace (linguistics)">
         trace
        </a>
        ) might be expected:
        <i>
         This is the girl that I don’t know what
         <b>
          she
         </b>
         said.
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reflexive_and_reciprocal">
        Reflexive and reciprocal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Reflexive and reciprocal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
        Reflexive pronoun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Reciprocal_pronoun" title="Reciprocal pronoun">
        Reciprocal pronoun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Reflexive pronouns are used when a person or thing acts on itself, for example,
       <i>
        John cut
        <b>
         himself
        </b>
        .
       </i>
       In English they all end in
       <i>
        -self
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -selves
       </i>
       and must refer to a noun phrase elsewhere in the same clause.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         55
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Reciprocal pronouns refer to a reciprocal relationship (
       <i>
        each other
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        one another
       </i>
       ). They must refer to a noun phrase in the same clause.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         55
        </span>
       </sup>
       An example in English is:
       <i>
        They do not like
        <b>
         each other
        </b>
        .
       </i>
       In some languages, the same forms can be used as both reflexive and reciprocal pronouns.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possessive">
        Possessive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Possessive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
        Possessive
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive_determiner" title="Possessive determiner">
        Possessive determiner
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Possessive pronouns are used to indicate
       <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">
        possession
       </a>
       (in a broad sense). Some occur as independent noun phrases:
       <i>
        mine
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hers
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ours
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        yours
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        theirs
       </i>
       . An example is:
       <i>
        Those clothes are
        <b>
         mine
        </b>
        .
       </i>
       Others must accompany a noun:
       <i>
        my
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        our
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        your
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        their
       </i>
       , as in:
       <i>
        I lost
        <b>
         my
        </b>
        wallet.
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        His
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        its
       </i>
       can fall into either category, although
       <i>
        its
       </i>
       is nearly always found in the second.) Those of the second type have traditionally also been described as possessive
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , and in more modern terminology as
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive_determiner" title="Possessive determiner">
        possessive determiners
       </a>
       . The term "possessive pronoun" is sometimes restricted to the first type. Both types replace
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
        possessive
       </a>
       noun phrases. As an example,
       <i>
        <b>
         Their
        </b>
        crusade to capture our attention
       </i>
       could replace
       <i>
        <b>
         The advertisers'
        </b>
        crusade to capture our attention.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         55–56
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstrative">
        Demonstrative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Demonstrative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demonstrative_pronoun" title="Demonstrative pronoun">
        Demonstrative pronoun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Demonstrative pronouns (in English,
       <i>
        this
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        that
       </i>
       and their plurals
       <i>
        these
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        those
       </i>
       ) often distinguish their targets by pointing or some other indication of position; for example,
       <i>
        I'll take
        <b>
         these
        </b>
        .
       </i>
       They may also be
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
         anaphoric
        </a>
       </i>
       , depending on an earlier expression for context, for example,
       <i>
        A kid actor would try to be all sweet, and who needs
        <b>
         that
        </b>
        ?
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         56
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indefinite">
        Indefinite
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indefinite">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronoun" title="Indefinite pronoun">
        Indefinite pronoun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Indefinite pronouns, the largest group of pronouns, refer to one or more unspecified persons or things. One group in English includes compounds of
       <i>
        some-
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        any-
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        every-
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        no-
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        -thing
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -one
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -body
       </i>
       , for example:
       <i>
        <b>
         Anyone
        </b>
        can do that.
       </i>
       Another group, including
       <i>
        many
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        more
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        most
       </i>
       , can appear alone or followed by
       <i>
        of
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         54–55
        </span>
       </sup>
       In addition,
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Distributive_pronoun" title="Distributive pronoun">
         Distributive pronouns
        </a>
        are used to refer to members of a group separately rather than collectively. (
        <i>
         To
         <b>
          each
         </b>
         his own.
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negation_(linguistics)" title="Negation (linguistics)">
         Negative pronouns
        </a>
        indicate the non-existence of people or things. (
        <i>
         <b>
          Nobody
         </b>
         thinks that.
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impersonal_pronoun" title="Impersonal pronoun">
         Impersonal pronouns
        </a>
        normally refer to a person, but are not specific as to first, second or third person in the way that the personal pronouns are. (
        <i>
         <b>
          One
         </b>
         does not clean
         <b>
          one's
         </b>
         own windows.
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relative">
        Relative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Relative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_pronoun" title="Relative pronoun">
        Relative pronoun
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Relative pronouns (
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        whom
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        whose
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        what
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        which
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        that
       </i>
       ) refer back to people or things previously mentioned:
       <i>
        People
        <b>
         who
        </b>
        smoke should quit now.
       </i>
       They are used in
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_clause" title="Relative clause">
        relative clauses
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         56
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interrogative">
        Interrogative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Interrogative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Interrogative_word" title="Interrogative word">
        Interrogative word
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Interrogative pronouns ask which person or thing is meant. In reference to a person, one may use
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       (subject),
       <i>
        whom
       </i>
       (object) or
       <i>
        whose
       </i>
       (possessive); for example,
       <i>
        <b>
         Who
        </b>
        did that?
       </i>
       In colloquial speech,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whom" title="Whom">
         whom
        </a>
       </i>
       is generally replaced by
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       . English non-personal interrogative pronouns (
       <i>
        which
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        what
       </i>
       ) have only one form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Borjars_2-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Borjars-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         56–57
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In English and many other languages (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       ), the sets of relative and interrogative pronouns are nearly identical. Compare English:
       <i>
        <b>
         Who
        </b>
        is that?
       </i>
       (interrogative) and
       <i>
        I know the woman
        <b>
         who
        </b>
        came
       </i>
       (relative). In some other languages, interrogative pronouns and indefinite pronouns are frequently identical; for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Chinese" title="Standard Chinese">
        Standard Chinese
       </a>
       什么
       <i>
        shénme
       </i>
       means "what?" as well as "something" or "anything".
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Antecedents">
        Antecedents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Antecedents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The use of pronouns often involves
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
        anaphora
       </a>
       , where the meaning of the pronoun is dependent on another referential element. The
       <a href="/wiki/Referent" title="Referent">
        referent
       </a>
       of the pronoun is often the same as that of a preceding (or sometimes following) noun phrase, called the
       <a href="/wiki/Antecedent_(grammar)" title="Antecedent (grammar)">
        antecedent
       </a>
       of the pronoun. The following sentences give examples of particular types of pronouns used with antecedents:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Third-person personal pronouns:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            That poor man
           </b>
           looks as if
           <b>
            he
           </b>
           needs a new coat.
          </i>
          (the noun phrase
          <i>
           that poor man
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           he
          </i>
          )
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            Julia
           </b>
           arrived yesterday. I met
           <b>
            her
           </b>
           at the station.
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           Julia
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           her
          </i>
          )
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           When
           <b>
            they
           </b>
           saw us,
           <b>
            the lions
           </b>
           began roaring
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           the lions
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           they
          </i>
          ; as it comes after the pronoun it may be called a
          <i>
           postcedent
          </i>
          )
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Other personal pronouns in some circumstances:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            Terry and I
           </b>
           were hoping no-one would find
           <b>
            us
           </b>
           .
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           Terry and I
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           us
          </i>
          )
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            You and Alice
           </b>
           can come if
           <b>
            you
           </b>
           like.
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           you and Alice
          </i>
          is the antecedent of the second – plural –
          <i>
           you
          </i>
          )
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Reflexive and reciprocal pronouns:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            Jack
           </b>
           hurt
           <b>
            himself
           </b>
           .
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           Jack
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           himself
          </i>
          )
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            We
           </b>
           were teasing
           <b>
            each other
           </b>
           .
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           we
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           each other
          </i>
          )
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Relative pronouns:
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           <b>
            The woman who
           </b>
           looked at you is my sister.
          </i>
          (
          <i>
           the woman
          </i>
          is the antecedent of
          <i>
           who
          </i>
          )
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some other types, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronoun" title="Indefinite pronoun">
        indefinite pronouns
       </a>
       , are usually used without antecedents. Relative pronouns are used without antecedents in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_relative_clause" title="Free relative clause">
        free relative clauses
       </a>
       . Even third-person personal pronouns are sometimes used without antecedents ("unprecursed") – this applies to special uses such as
       <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
        dummy pronouns
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generic_they" title="Generic they">
        generic
        <i>
         they
        </i>
       </a>
       , as well as cases where the referent is implied by the context.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_considerations">
        Theoretical considerations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pronoun&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Theoretical considerations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Pronouns
       <i>
        (antōnymía)
       </i>
       are listed as one of
       <a href="/wiki/Part_of_speech#Western_tradition" title="Part of speech">
        eight parts of speech
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Art_of_Grammar" title="The Art of Grammar">
         The Art of Grammar
        </a>
       </i>
       , a treatise on Greek grammar attributed to
       <a href="/wiki/Dionysius_Thrax" title="Dionysius Thrax">
        Dionysius Thrax
       </a>
       and dating from the 2nd century BC. The pronoun is described there as "a part of speech substitutable for a noun and marked for a person." Pronouns continued to be regarded as a part of speech in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       grammar (the Latin term being
       <i>
        pronomen
       </i>
       , from which the English name – through
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_French" title="Middle French">
        Middle French
       </a>
       – ultimately derives), and thus in the European tradition generally.
      </p>
      <p>
       In more modern approaches, pronouns are less likely to be considered to be a single
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Word_class" title="Word class">
        word class
       </a>
       , because of the many different syntactic roles that they play, as represented by the various different types of pronouns listed in the previous sections.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table border="1" class="wikitable floatright" style="text-align:center">
       <tr>
        <th>

        </th>

         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          our
         </i>

        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>

        </th>
        <td>

         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>

         </i>

        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>

        </td>

      <p>
       Certain types of pronouns are often identical or similar in form to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(class)" title="Determiner (class)">
        determiners
       </a>
       with related meaning; some English examples are given in the table on the right. This observation has led some linguists, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Postal" title="Paul Postal">
        Paul Postal
       </a>
       , to regard pronouns as determiners that have had their following noun or noun phrase deleted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Postal_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Postal-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (Such patterning can even be claimed for certain personal pronouns; for example,
       <i>
        we
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        you
       </i>
       might be analyzed as determiners in phrases like
       <i>
        we Brits
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        you tennis players
       </i>
       .) Other linguists have taken a similar view, uniting pronouns and determiners into a single class, sometimes called "determiner-pronoun", or regarding determiners as a subclass of pronouns or vice versa. The distinction may be considered to be one of
       <a href="/wiki/Subcategorization" title="Subcategorization">
        subcategorization
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Valency_(linguistics)" title="Valency (linguistics)">
        valency
       </a>
       , rather like the distinction between
       <a href="/wiki/Transitivity_(grammar)" title="Transitivity (grammar)">
        transitive and intransitive
       </a>
       verbs – determiners take a noun phrase
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complement_(grammar)" title="Complement (grammar)">
        complement
       </a>
       like transitive verbs do, while pronouns do not.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is consistent with the
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner_phrase" title="Determiner phrase">
        determiner phrase
       </a>
       viewpoint, whereby a determiner, rather than the noun that follows it, is taken to be the
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head
       </a>
       of the phrase.
      </p>
      <p>
       The grammatical behavior of certain types of pronouns, and in particular their possible relationship with their antecedents, has been the focus of studies in
       <a href="/wiki/Binding_(linguistics)" title="Binding (linguistics)">
        binding
       </a>
       , notably in the Chomskyan
       <a href="/wiki/Government_and_binding_theory" title="Government and binding theory">
        government and binding theory
       </a>
       . In this context, reflexive and reciprocal pronouns (such as
       <i>
        himself
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        each other
       </i>
       ) are referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anaphor" title="Anaphor">
        anaphors
