    <i>
     La Argentinita
    </i>
   </h1>

      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         La Argentinita
        </b>
       </i>
       is the stage name of
       <b>
        Encarnación López Júlvez
       </b>
       (Buenos Aires, March 3, 1898 – New York, September 24, 1945), a Spanish-Argentine dancer and singer.
      </p>
      <p>
       She was brought to the north coast of Spain at an early age, where she began to learn Spanish regional dances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eventually, she danced to the compositions of
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_de_Falla" title="Manuel de Falla">
        Manuel de Falla
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Joaqu%C3%ADn_Turina" title="Joaquín Turina">
        Joaquín Turina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Alb%C3%A9niz" title="Isaac Albéniz">
        Isaac Albéniz
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Granados" title="Enrique Granados">
        Enrique Granados
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">
        Maurice Ravel
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       She led in the development of Ballet Español.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Among her early performances was as the Butterfly in the 1920 premiere of
       <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">
        Federico García Lorca
       </a>
       's musical play
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_maleficio_de_la_mariposa" title="El maleficio de la mariposa">
         El maleficio de la mariposa
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       La Argentinita later performed as a
       <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
        flamenco
       </a>
       dancer. She staged several flamenco theatrical shows, including an adaption of Falla's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Amor_Brujo" title="El Amor Brujo">
        El Amor Brujo
       </a>
       (Love the Magician) in 1933, and
       <i>
        Las Calles de Cádiz
       </i>
       (The Streets of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cadiz" title="Cadiz">
        Cadiz
       </a>
       ) in 1933 and 1940.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter was inspired by "the love of her life, the bullfighter
       <a href="/wiki/Ignacio_S%C3%A1nchez_Mej%C3%ADas" title="Ignacio Sánchez Mejías">
        Ignacio Sánchez Mejías
       </a>
       (killed in the bull ring in 1935)."
       <i>
        Las Calles de Cádiz
       </i>
       presented an innovation in flamenco performance, in that each artist not only danced or sung, but played a role so that the streets of Cádiz "seemed to come alive on stage." It was "the first large scale theatrical attempt at authentic flamenco".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       She chose the name La Argentinita in deference to dancer
       <a href="/wiki/La_Argentina_(dancer)" title="La Argentina (dancer)">
        La Argentina
       </a>
       . The Spanish choreographer and ballerina
       <a href="/wiki/Pilar_L%C3%B3pez_J%C3%BAlvez" title="Pilar López Júlvez">
        Pilar López Júlvez
       </a>
       was her younger sister.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
