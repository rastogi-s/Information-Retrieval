   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    List of language regulators
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       This is a list of bodies that regulate
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard languages
       </a>
       , often called
       <b>
        language academies
       </b>
       . Language academies are motivated by, or closely associated with,
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_purism" title="Linguistic purism">
        linguistic purism
       </a>
       , and typically publish
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        prescriptive
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictionaries" title="Dictionaries">
        dictionaries
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which purport to officiate and prescribe the meaning of words and pronunciations. A language regulator may also be descriptive, however, while maintaining (but not imposing) a standard spelling. Many language academies are private institutions, although some are governmental bodies in different states, or enjoy some form of government-sanctioned status in one or more countries. There may also be multiple language academies attempting to regulate the same language, sometimes based in different countries and sometimes influenced by political factors.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/World_language" title="World language">
        world languages
       </a>
       have one or more language academies. However, the degree of control that the academies exert over these languages does not render the latter
       <a href="/wiki/Controlled_natural_language" title="Controlled natural language">
        controlled natural languages
       </a>
       in the sense that the various kinds of "
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Simple_English_(disambiguation)" title="Simple English (disambiguation)">
        simple English
       </a>
       " (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Basic_English" title="Basic English">
        Basic English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Simplified_Technical_English" title="Simplified Technical English">
        Simplified Technical English
       </a>
       ) or
       <a href="/wiki/George_Orwell" title="George Orwell">
        George Orwell
       </a>
       's fictional
       <a href="/wiki/Newspeak" title="Newspeak">
        Newspeak
       </a>
       are. They instead remain
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_language" title="Natural language">
        natural languages
       </a>
       to a considerable extent and are thus not
       <a href="/wiki/Formal_language" title="Formal language">
        formal languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Attempto_Controlled_English" title="Attempto Controlled English">
        Attempto Controlled English
       </a>
       . They have a degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">
        standardization
       </a>
       that allows them to function as
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard languages
       </a>
       (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">
        standard French
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_language_regulators&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Esperanto">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Apart from the
       <a href="/wiki/Akademio_de_Esperanto" title="Akademio de Esperanto">
        Akademio de Esperanto
       </a>
       , most auxiliary languages, also known as constructed languages (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conlangs" title="Conlangs">
        Conlangs
       </a>
       ) have no true linguistic regulators, language academies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ido_(language)" title="Ido (language)">
        Ido
       </a>
       have been
       <i>
        constructed
       </i>
       (or planned) by a person or small group, before being adopted and further developed by communities of users through
       <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_linguistics" title="Evolutionary linguistics">
        natural language evolution
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Bodies such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Akademio_de_Esperanto" title="Akademio de Esperanto">
        Akademio de Esperanto
       </a>
       look at questions of usage in the light of the original goals and principles of the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       See also :
       <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">
        International auxiliary languages
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_language_regulators&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Interlingua">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The auxiliary language
       <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </a>
       has no regulating body, as its vocabulary, grammar, and orthography are viewed as a product of ongoing social forces. In theory, Interlingua therefore evolves independent from any human regulator. Interlingua's vocabulary is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interlingua_and_eligibility_of_international_words" title="Interlingua and eligibility of international words">
        verified and recorded
       </a>
       by dynamically applying certain general principles to an existing set of natural languages and their etymologies. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Auxiliary_Language_Association" title="International Auxiliary Language Association">
        International Auxiliary Language Association
       </a>
       ceased to exist in 1954, and according to the secretary of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Union_Mundial_de_Interlingua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Union Mundial de Interlingua (page does not exist)">
        Union Mundial de Interlingua
       </a>
       "Interlingua doesn't need its Academy".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
