      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        stop
       </b>
       , also known as a
       <b>
        plosive
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        oral
        <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">
         occlusive
        </a>
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonant
       </a>
       in which the vocal tract is blocked so that all
       <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
        airflow
       </a>
       ceases.
      </p>
      <p>
       The occlusion may be made with the tongue blade (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiceless dental and alveolar stops">
         t
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiced dental and alveolar stops">
         d
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ) or body (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_stop" title="Voiceless velar stop">
         k
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_stop" title="Voiced velar stop">
         ɡ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ), lips (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_stop" title="Voiceless bilabial stop">
         p
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">
         b
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ), or glottis (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
         ʔ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ). Stops contrast with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_occlusive" title="Nasal occlusive">
        nasals
       </a>
       , where the vocal tract is blocked but airflow continues through the nose, as in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_nasal" title="Bilabial nasal">
         m
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
         n
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       , and with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricatives" title="Fricatives">
        fricatives
       </a>
       , where partial occlusion impedes but does not block airflow in the vocal tract.
      </p>

      <p>
       The terms
       <i>
        stop, occlusive,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        plosive
       </i>
       are often and inaccurately used interchangeably. Linguists who distinguish them may not agree on the distinction being made. The terms refer to different features of the consonant. "Stop" refers to the airflow that is stopped. "Occlusive" refers to the articulation, which occludes (blocks) the vocal tract. "Plosive" refers to the release burst (plosion) of the consonant. Therefore, a plosive is a stop that is released, typically into a more open speech sound such as a vowel. It is inaccurate to call an unreleased stop a plosive. Rather, such stops are "applosives".
      </p>
      <p>
       Either "occlusive" or "stop" may be used as a general term covering the other together with nasals. That is, 'occlusive' may be defined as
       <a href="/wiki/Oral_consonant" title="Oral consonant">
        oral
       </a>
       occlusives (stops/plosives) plus nasal occlusives (nasals such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_nasal" title="Bilabial nasal">
         m
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Dental,_alveolar_and_postalveolar_nasals#Alveolar" title="Dental, alveolar and postalveolar nasals">
         n
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ), or 'stop' may be defined as oral stops (plosives) plus nasal stops (nasals).
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Ladefoged
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ian_Maddieson" title="Ian Maddieson">
        Maddieson
       </a>
       (1996) prefer to restrict 'stop' to oral occlusives. They say,
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         what we call simply nasals are called nasal stops by some linguists. We avoid this phrase, preferring to reserve the term 'stop' for sounds in which there is a complete interruption of airflow.
        </i>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In addition, they use "plosive" for a
       <a href="/wiki/Pulmonic_consonant" title="Pulmonic consonant">
        pulmonic
       </a>
       stop; "stops" in their usage include
       <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">
        ejective
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">
        implosive
       </a>
       consonants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       If a term such as 'plosive' is used for oral obstruents, and nasals are not called nasal stops, then a
       <i>
        stop
       </i>
       may mean the
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
        glottal stop
       </a>
       ; 'plosive' may even mean non-glottal stop. In other cases, however, it may be the word 'plosive' that is restricted to the glottal stop. Note that, generally speaking, stops do not have plosion (a release burst). In English, for example, there are stops with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_audible_release" title="No audible release">
        no audible release
       </a>
       , such as the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        apt
       </i>
       . However, pulmonic stops do have plosion in other environments.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       , the term for stop was
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        ἄφωνον
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        áphōnon
       </i>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which means "unpronounceable", "voiceless", or "silent", because stops could not be pronounced without a vowel. This term was
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calqued
       </a>
       into
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          mūta
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , and from there borrowed into English as
       <i>
        mute
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Mute
       </i>
       was sometimes used instead for voiceless consonants, whether stops or fricatives, a usage that was later replaced with
       <i>
        surd
       </i>
       , from Latin
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         surdus
        </span>
       </i>
       "deaf" or "silent",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a term still occasionally seen in the literature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For more information on the Ancient Greek terms, see
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_phonology#Terminology" title="Ancient Greek phonology">
        Ancient Greek phonology § Terminology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Common_stops">
        Common stops
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Common stops">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All spoken natural languages in the world have stops,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and most have at least the voiceless stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       . However, there are exceptions: Colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
        Samoan
       </a>
       lacks the
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       , and several North American languages, such as the northern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquoian" title="Iroquoian">
        Iroquoian
       </a>
       and southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iroquoian" title="Iroquoian">
        Iroquoian
       </a>
       languages (i.e.,
       <a href="/wiki/Cherokee" title="Cherokee">
        Cherokee
       </a>
       ), lack the
       <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">
        labial
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p]
       </span>
       . In fact, the labial is the least stable of the voiceless stops in the languages of the world, as the unconditioned sound change
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p]
       </span>
       →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       (→
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       → Ø) is quite common in unrelated languages, having occurred in the history of
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Japanese_language" title="Classical Japanese language">
        Classical Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Classical Arabic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic_language" title="Proto-Celtic language">
        Proto-Celtic
       </a>
       , for instance. Formal
       <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
        Samoan
       </a>
       has only one word with
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       ; colloquial Samoan conflates
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ni%E2%80%98ihau" title="Ni‘ihau">
        Ni‘ihau
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       has
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       to a greater extent than Standard Hawaiian, but neither distinguish a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       from a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       . It may be more accurate to say that Hawaiian and colloquial Samoan do not distinguish velar and coronal stops than to say they lack one or the other.
      </p>
      <p>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Occlusive#Common_occlusives" title="Occlusive">
        Common occlusives
       </a>
       for the distribution of both stops and nasals.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Articulation">
        Articulation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Articulation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the articulation of the stop, three phases can be distinguished:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Catch
        </b>
        : The airway closes so that no air can escape (hence the name
        <i>
         stop
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Hold
        </b>
        or
        <b>
         occlusion
        </b>
        : The airway stays closed, causing a slight pressure difference to build up (hence the name
        <i>
         occlusive
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Release
        </b>
        or
        <b>
         burst
        </b>
        : The closure is opened. The released airflow produces a sudden impulse causing an audible sound, or
        <i>
         burst
        </i>
        (hence the name
        <i>
         plosive
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In many languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_language" title="Malay language">
        Malay
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       , word-final stops lack a release burst, even when followed by a vowel, or have a
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_release" title="Nasal release">
        nasal release
       </a>
       . See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/No_audible_release" title="No audible release">
        no audible release
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Nasal occlusives are somewhat similar. In the catch and hold, airflow continues through the nose; in the release, there is no burst, and final nasals are typically unreleased across most languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricates
       </a>
       , the catch and hold are those of a stop, but the release is that of a
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricative
       </a>
       . That is, affricates are stop–fricative
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Contour_(phonetics)" title="Contour (phonetics)">
        contours
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">
        Voice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Voice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_consonant" title="Voiced consonant">
         Voiced
        </a>
        stops
       </b>
       are pronounced with vibration of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">
        vocal cords
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless" title="Voiceless">
         voiceless
        </a>
        stops
       </b>
       without. Stops are commonly voiceless, and many languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       , have only voiceless stops. Others, such as most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        Australian languages
       </a>
       , are indeterminate: stops may vary between voiced and voiceless without distinction.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aspiration">
        Aspiration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aspiration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">
         aspirated
        </a>
        stops
       </b>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vocal_cords" title="Vocal cords">
        vocal cords
       </a>
       (vocal folds) are abducted at the time of release. In a prevocalic aspirated stop (a stop followed by a vowel or sonorant), the time when the vocal cords begin to vibrate will be delayed until the vocal folds come together enough for voicing to begin, and will usually start with breathy voicing. The duration between the release of the stop and the voice onset is called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voice_onset_time" title="Voice onset time">
         voice onset time
        </a>
       </i>
       (VOT) or the
       <i>
        aspiration interval
       </i>
       . Highly aspirated stops have a long period of aspiration, so that there is a long period of voiceless airflow (a phonetic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ) before the onset of the vowel. In
       <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_consonant" title="Tenuis consonant">
        tenuis
       </a>
       stops, the vocal cords come together for voicing immediately following the release, and there is little or no aspiration (a voice onset time close to zero). In English, there may be a brief segment of breathy voice that identifies the stop as voiceless and not voiced. In voiced stops, the vocal folds are set for voice before the release, and often vibrate during the entire hold, and in English, the voicing after release is not breathy. A stop is called "fully voiced" if it is voiced during the entire occlusion. In English, however, initial voiced stops like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /#b/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /#d/
       </span>
       may have no voicing during the period of occlusion, or the voicing may start shortly before the release and continue after release, though word-final stops tend to be fully voiced: In most dialects of English, the final
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       in the
       <i>
        bag
       </i>
       is likely to be fully voiced, whereas the initial
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       will only be voiced during part of its occlusion. Initial voiceless stops, like the
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        pie
       </i>
       , are aspirated, with a palpable puff of air upon release, whereas a stop after an
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       , as in
       <i>
        spy
       </i>
       , is
       <a href="/wiki/Tenuis_consonant" title="Tenuis consonant">
        tenuis
       </a>
       (unaspirated). When spoken near a candle flame, the flame will flicker more after the words
       <i>
        par, tar,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        car
       </i>
       are articulated, compared with
       <i>
        spar, star,
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        scar
       </i>
       . In the common pronunciation of
       <i>
        papa
       </i>
       , the initial
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       is aspirated whereas the medial
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       is not.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Length">
        Length
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Length">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consonant_length" title="Consonant length">
        geminate
       </a>
       or
       <b>
        long
       </b>
       consonant, the occlusion lasts longer than in simple consonants. In languages where stops are only distinguished by length (e.g., Arabic, Ilwana, Icelandic), the long stops may be held up to three times as long as the short stops.
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       is well known for its geminate stops, as the double
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       in the name
       <i>
        Vittoria
       </i>
       takes just as long to say as the
       <i>
        ct
       </i>
       does in English
       <i>
        Victoria
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       also prominently features geminate consonants, such as in the minimal pair 来た
       <i>
        kita
       </i>
       'came' and 切った
       <i>
        kitta
       </i>
       'cut'.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that there are many languages where the features voice, aspiration, and length reinforce each other, and in such cases it may be hard to determine which of these features predominates. In such cases, the terms
       <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">
        fortis
       </a>
       is sometimes used for aspiration or gemination, whereas
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenis" title="Lenis">
        lenis
       </a>
       is used for single, tenuous, or voiced stops. Be aware, however, that the terms
       <i>
        fortis
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        lenis
       </i>
       are poorly defined, and their meanings vary from source to source.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nasalization">
        Nasalization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nasalization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        Nasal consonant
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nasalization" title="Nasalization">
        Nasalization
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Simple
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_occlusive" title="Nasal occlusive">
        nasals
       </a>
       are differentiated from stops only by a lowered
       <a href="/wiki/Soft_palate" title="Soft palate">
        velum
       </a>
       that allows the air to escape through the nose during the occlusion. Nasals are acoustically
       <a href="/wiki/Sonorant" title="Sonorant">
        sonorants
       </a>
       , as they have a non-turbulent airflow and are nearly always voiced, but they are articulatorily
       <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">
        obstruents
       </a>
       , as there is complete blockage of the oral cavity. The term
       <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">
        occlusive
       </a>
       may be used as a cover term for both nasals and stops.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prenasalized_stop" title="Prenasalized stop">
        prenasalized stop
       </a>
       starts out with a lowered velum that raises during the occlusion. The closest examples in English are consonant clusters such as the [nd] in
       <i>
        candy
       </i>
       , but many languages have prenasalized stops that function phonologically as single consonants.
       <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">
        Swahili
       </a>
       is well known for having words beginning with prenasalized stops, as in
       <i>
        ndege
       </i>
       'bird', and in many languages of the South Pacific, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Fijian_language" title="Fijian language">
        Fijian
       </a>
       , these are even spelled with single letters:
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       [mb],
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       [nd].
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_release" title="Nasal release">
        postnasalized stop
       </a>
       begins with a raised velum that lowers during the occlusion. This causes an audible nasal
       <i>
        release
       </i>
       , as in English
       <i>
        sudden
       </i>
       . This could also be compared to the /dn/ cluster found in
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       and other Slavic languages, which can be seen in the name of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dnieper_River" title="Dnieper River">
        Dnieper River
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that the terms
       <i>
        prenasalization
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        postnasalization
       </i>
       are normally used only in languages where these sounds are phonemic: that is, not analyzed into sequences of stop plus nasal.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Airstream_mechanism">
        Airstream mechanism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Airstream mechanism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Stops may be made with more than one
       <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
        airstream mechanism
       </a>
       . The normal mechanism is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pulmonic_egressive" title="Pulmonic egressive">
        pulmonic egressive
       </a>
       , that is, with air flowing outward from the lungs. All languages have pulmonic stops. Some languages have stops made with other mechanisms as well:
       <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">
        ejective stops
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glottalic_egressive" title="Glottalic egressive">
        glottalic egressive
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Implosive_consonant" title="Implosive consonant">
        implosive stops
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Glottalic_ingressive" title="Glottalic ingressive">
        glottalic ingressive
       </a>
       ), or
       <a href="/wiki/Click_consonant" title="Click consonant">
        click consonants
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lingual_ingressive" title="Lingual ingressive">
        lingual ingressive
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tenseness">
        Tenseness
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stop_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Tenseness">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Tenseness" title="Tenseness">
        Tenseness
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Fortis_and_lenis" title="Fortis and lenis">
         fortis
        </a>
        stop
       </b>
       (in the narrow sense) is produced with more muscular tension than a
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lenis" title="Lenis">
         lenis
        </a>
        stop
       </b>
       (in the narrow sense). However, this is difficult to measure, and there is usually debate over the actual mechanism of alleged fortis or lenis consonants.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are a series of stops in the
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean language
       </a>
       , sometimes written with the IPA symbol for ejectives, which are produced using "
       <a href="/wiki/Stiff_voice" title="Stiff voice">
        stiff voice
       </a>
       ", meaning there is increased contraction of the glottis than for normal production of voiceless stops. The indirect evidence for stiff voice is in the following vowels, which have a higher fundamental frequency than those following other stops. The higher frequency is explained as a result of the glottis being tense. Other such
       <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
        phonation
       </a>
       types include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Breathy_voice" title="Breathy voice">
        breathy voice
       </a>
       , or murmur;
       <a href="/wiki/Slack_voice" title="Slack voice">
        slack voice
       </a>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/Creaky_voice" title="Creaky voice">
        creaky voice
       </a>
       .
      </p>
