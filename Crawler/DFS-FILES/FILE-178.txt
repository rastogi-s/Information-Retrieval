      <p>
       The
       <b>
        IERS Reference Meridian (IRM)
       </b>
       , also called the
       <b>
        International Reference Meridian
       </b>
       , is the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       (0° longitude) maintained by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">
        International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service
       </a>
       (IERS). It passes about 5.3
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcseconds
       </a>
       east of
       <a href="/wiki/George_Biddell_Airy" title="George Biddell Airy">
        George Biddell Airy
       </a>
       's 1851
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transit_circle" title="Transit circle">
        transit circle
       </a>
       or 102.478 metres (336.21 ft) at the
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Observatory,_Greenwich" title="Royal Observatory, Greenwich">
        Royal Observatory, Greenwich
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also the reference meridian of the
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        Global Positioning System
       </a>
       (GPS) operated by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_Defense" title="United States Department of Defense">
        United States Department of Defense
       </a>
       , and of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       and its two formal versions, the ideal
       <a href="/wiki/International_Terrestrial_Reference_System" title="International Terrestrial Reference System">
        International Terrestrial Reference System
       </a>
       (ITRS) and its realization, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Terrestrial_Reference_Frame" title="International Terrestrial Reference Frame">
        International Terrestrial Reference Frame
       </a>
       (ITRF).
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location">
        Location
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=IERS_Reference_Meridian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The reason for the 5.3 arcsecond offset between the IERS Reference Meridian and the Airy transit circle is that the observations with the transit circle were based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Vertical_deflection" title="Vertical deflection">
        local vertical
       </a>
       , while the IERS Reference is a
       <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_datum" title="Geodetic datum">
        geodetic longitude
       </a>
       , that is, the plane of the meridian contains the center of mass of the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Hydrographic_Organization" title="International Hydrographic Organization">
        International Hydrographic Organization
       </a>
       adopted an early version of the IRM in 1983 for all nautical charts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The IRM was adopted for air navigation by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Civil_Aviation_Organization" title="International Civil Aviation Organization">
        International Civil Aviation Organization
       </a>
       on 3 March 1989.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">
        Tectonic plates
       </a>
       slowly move over the surface of the Earth, so most countries have adopted for their maps an IRM version fixed relative to their own tectonic plate as it existed at the beginning of a specific year. Examples include the North American Datum 1983 (NAD83), the European Terrestrial Reference Frame 1989 (ETRF89), and the Geocentric Datum of Australia 1994 (GDA94). Versions fixed to a tectonic plate differ from the global version by at most a few centimetres.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, the IRM is not fixed to any point on Earth. Instead, all points on the European portion of the Eurasian plate, including the Royal Observatory, are slowly moving northeast about 2.5 cm per year relative to it. Thus this IRM is the weighted average (in the
       <a href="/wiki/Least_squares" title="Least squares">
        least squares
       </a>
       sense) of the reference meridians of the hundreds of ground stations contributing to the IERS network. The network includes GPS stations,
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_laser_ranging" title="Satellite laser ranging">
        Satellite Laser Ranging
       </a>
       (SLR) stations,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_laser_ranging" title="Lunar laser ranging">
        Lunar Laser Ranging
       </a>
       (LLR) stations, and the highly accurate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Very_Long_Baseline_Interferometry" title="Very Long Baseline Interferometry">
        Very Long Baseline Interferometry
       </a>
       (VLBI) stations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All stations' coordinates are adjusted annually to remove net rotation relative to the major tectonic plates. If Earth had only two hemispherical plates moving relative to each other around any axis which intersects their centres or their junction, then the longitudes (around any other rotation axis) of any two, diametrically opposite, stations must move in opposite directions by the same amount. The
       <a href="/wiki/180th_meridian" title="180th meridian">
        180th meridian
       </a>
       is opposite the IERS Reference Meridian and forms a
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       with it dividing the earth into
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">
        Western Hemisphere
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">
        Eastern Hemisphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        Universal Time
       </a>
       is notionally based on the WGS84 meridian. Because of changes in the rate of Earth's rotation, standard international time
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        UTC
       </a>
       can differ from the mean observed solar time at noon on the prime meridian by up to 0.9 second.
       <a href="/wiki/Leap_second" title="Leap second">
        Leap seconds
       </a>
       are inserted periodically to keep UTC close to Earth's angular position relative to the Sun; see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_solar_time" title="Mean solar time">
        mean solar time
       </a>
       .
      </p>
