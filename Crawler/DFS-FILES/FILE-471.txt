      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_typology" title="Linguistic typology">
        linguistic typology
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        null-subject language
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       whose
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       permits an
       <a href="/wiki/Independent_clause" title="Independent clause">
        independent clause
       </a>
       to lack an explicit
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ; such a clause is then said to have a
       <b>
        null subject
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Typically, null-subject languages express
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       , and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       with the
       <a href="/wiki/Coreference" title="Coreference">
        referent
       </a>
       on the verb, rendering a subject
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       redundant. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Principles_and_parameters" title="Principles and parameters">
        principles and parameters
       </a>
       framework, the null subject is controlled by the
       <b>
        pro-drop parameter
       </b>
       , which is either on or off for a particular language.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       the subject "she" can be either explicit or implicit:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <span lang="it">
          Maria non vuole mangiare.
         </span>
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Literal_translation" title="Literal translation">
         lit.
        </a>
        Maria not wants [to-]eat, "Maria does not want to eat".
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <span lang="it">
          Non vuole mangiare.
         </span>
        </i>
        lit.
        <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
         Subject
        </a>
        not wants [to-]eat, "[(S)he] does not want to eat."
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The subject "(s)he" of the second sentence is only implied in Italian.
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , on the other hand, require an explicit subject in this sentence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the thousands of languages in the world, a considerable number are null-subject languages, from a wide diversity of unrelated
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        language families
       </a>
       . They include
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">
        Berber
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">
        Hindi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">
        Punjabi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic languages
       </a>
       , as well as most languages related to these, and many others still. In fact it is rather
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        non-pro-drop
       </a>
       that is an
       <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">
        areal feature
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Average_European" title="Standard Average European">
        Standard Average European
       </a>
       including French, German, and English.

        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characterization">
        Characterization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characterization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the framework of
       <a href="/wiki/Government_and_binding_theory" title="Government and binding theory">
        government and binding theory
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       , the term
       <i>
        null subject
       </i>
       refers to an
       <a href="/wiki/Empty_category" title="Empty category">
        empty category
       </a>
       . The empty category in question is thought to behave like an ordinary
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronoun
       </a>
       with respect to
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
        anaphoric reference
       </a>
       and other grammatical behavior. Hence it is most commonly referred to as "
       <i>
        pro
       </i>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       This phenomenon is similar, but not identical, to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop languages
       </a>
       , which may omit pronouns, including subject pronouns, but also
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       pronouns. While all pro-drop languages are null-subject languages, not all null-subject languages are pro-drop.
      </p>
      <p>
       In null-subject languages that have
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       in which the verb inflects for person, the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        grammatical person
       </a>
       of the subject is reflected by the inflection of the verb and likewise for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The following examples come from
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "I'm going home" can be translated either as
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           vou para casa
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        or as
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           eu vou para casa
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , where
        <i>
         eu
        </i>
        means "I".
       </li>
       <li>
        "It's raining" can be translated as
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           está chovendo
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
         Brazilian Portuguese
        </a>
        ) or
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           está a chover
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
         European Portuguese
        </a>
        ). In Portuguese, as in the other Romance languages, there is no exact equivalent for the pronoun
        <i>
         it
        </i>
        . However, some elder persons say
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           Ele está a chover
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
         European Portuguese
        </a>
        ) which directly translates to "He is raining".
       </li>
       <li>
        "I'm going home. I'm going to watch TV" would not, except in exceptional circumstances, be translated as
        <i>
         <span lang="pt">
          <i>
           Eu vou para casa. Eu vou ver televisão.
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        At least the subject of the second sentence should be omitted in Portuguese unless one wishes to express emphasis, as to emphasize the
        <i>
         I
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       As the examples illustrate, in many null-subject languages,
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       exist and can be used for
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        emphasis
       </a>
       but are dropped whenever they can be inferred from the context. Some sentences do not allow a subject in any form while, in other cases an explicit subject without particular emphasis, would sound awkward or unnatural.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bantu_language" title="Bantu language">
        Bantu languages
       </a>
       are null-subject. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Luganda" title="Luganda">
        Ganda
       </a>
       , 'I'm going home' could be translated as
       <i>
        Ŋŋenze ewange
       </i>
       or as
       <i>
        Nze ŋŋenze ewange
       </i>
       , where
       <i>
        nze
       </i>
       means 'I'.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       is considered a null-subject language, as demonstrated by the following example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Arabic text: ساعد غيرك، يساعدك
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Transliteration:
        <i>
         sā‘id ghayrak, yusā‘iduk
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Literal translation: help other, he helps you.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Idiomatic translation: You help another, he helps you.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bulgarian">
        Bulgarian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bulgarian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Дойдох, видях, победих
       </i>
       ("
       <a href="/wiki/Veni,_vidi,_vici" title="Veni, vidi, vici">
        Veni, vidi, vici
       </a>
       ").
       <br/>
       <b>
        Literal translation:
       </b>
       came, saw, conquered.
       <br/>
       <b>
        Idiomatic translation:
       </b>
       I came, I saw, I conquered.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Catalan">
        Catalan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Catalan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Catalan, as in Spanish, Portuguese, Galician, etc., the subject is also encoded in the verb conjugation. Pronoun use is not obligatory.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Nosaltres) Anem a la platja
        </i>
        : We go to the beach.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Tu) Ets la meva amiga
        </i>
        : You are my friend.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Vostès/vosaltres) No són/sou benvinguts aquí
        </i>
        : You are not welcome here.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ells) Estan dormint
        </i>
        : They are asleep.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Jo) Necessito ajuda
        </i>
        : I need help.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ell) És a la seva habitació
        </i>
        : He is in his bedroom.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ella) Està cansada
        </i>
        : She is tired.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In Catalan, one may choose whether to use the subject or not. If used in an inclined tone, it may be seen as an added emphasis; however, in colloquial speaking, usage of a pronoun is optional. Even so, sentences with a null subject are used more frequently than sentences with a subject. In some cases, it is even necessary to skip the subject to create a grammatically correct sentence.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Emilian">
        Emilian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Emilian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian_dialect" title="Emilian dialect">
        Emilian
       </a>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic language
       </a>
       ) at least one pronoun (of two) is mandatory.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Nuàtar) A 'ndém in spiàgia
        </i>
        : We go to the beach.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Tè) At sē mè amìga
        </i>
        : You are my friend.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Vuàtar) A n sî minga bènvgnû chè
        </i>
        : You are not welcome here. → (you) you not are not welcome here.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Lōr) I èn drē a durmìr
        </i>
        : They are asleep. → (they) they are behind to sleep.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (I gat) I èn drē a durmìr
        </i>
        : Cats are asleep. → (the cats) they are behind to sleep.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Mè) A gh'ò biśógn 'd ajùt
        </i>
        : I need help.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Lò) 'L è in dla sò cambra
        </i>
        : He is in his bedroom. → (he) he is in of the his bedroom.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Śvan) 'L è in dla sò cambra
        </i>
        : John is in his bedroom. → (John) he is in of the his bedroom.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Lē) L'è stufa
        </i>
        : She is tired.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Greek">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hebrew">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       is considered a partially null-subject language, as demonstrated by the following example:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Hebrew text:
       </b>
       עזור לאחרים, יעזרו לך
       <br/>
       <b>
        Transliteration:
       </b>
       azor l'aherim, ya'azru lkha
       <br/>
       <b>
        Literal translation:
       </b>
       help others, will-help you
       <br/>
       <b>
        Idiomatic translation:
       </b>
       you help others, they will help you.
      </p>
      <p>
       Subjects can usually be omitted only when the verb is conjugated for grammatical person, as in the third-person plural in the example above. In Hebrew one can also construct null-subject sentences as in the Latin and Turkish language examples: "We/they are going to the beach" can be expressed as "holkhim la-yam" (הולכים לים), lit. "Are going to the beach." This is truly a null-subject construction.
      </p>
      <p>
       As in Spanish and Turkish though, Hebrew conjugates verbs in accordance with specific pronouns, so "we went to the beach" is technically just as much a null-subject construction as in the other languages, but in fact the conjugation does indicate the subject pronoun: "Halakhnu la-yam" (הלכנו לים), lit. "Went (we) to the beach." The word "halakhnu" means "we went", just as the Spanish and Turkish examples indicate the relevant pronoun as the subject in their conjugation. So these should perhaps not be considered to be true null-subject phrases. Potentially confusing the issue further, is the fact that Hebrew word order can also make some sentences appear to be null-subject, when the subject is in fact given after the verb. For instance, "it's raining" is expressed "yored geshem" (יורד גשם) which means "descends rain" - rain is the subject. The phrases meaning "It's snowing" and "It's hailing" are formed in the same way.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impersonal_constructions">
        Impersonal constructions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Impersonal constructions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Impersonal_verb" title="Impersonal verb">
        Impersonal verb
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In some cases (impersonal constructions), a proposition has no
       <a href="/wiki/Referent" title="Referent">
        referent
       </a>
       at all. Pro-drop languages deal naturally with these, whereas many non-pro-drop languages such as English and French have to fill in the syntactic gap by inserting a
       <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
        dummy pronoun
       </a>
       . "
       <i>
        *Rains
       </i>
       " is not a correct sentence; a dummy "it" has to be added: "
       <i>
        It rains
       </i>
       ", French "
       <i>
        Il pleut
       </i>
       ". In most Romance languages, however, "
       <i>
        Rains
       </i>
       " can be a sentence: Spanish "
       <i>
        Llueve
       </i>
       ", Italian "
       <i>
        Piove
       </i>
       ", Catalan "
       <i>
        Plou
       </i>
       ", Portuguese "
       <i>
        Chove
       </i>
       ", Romanian "
       <i>
        Plouă
       </i>
       ", etc. Uralic and Slavic languages also show this trait: Finnish "
       <i>
        Sataa
       </i>
       ", Hungarian "
       <i>
        Esik
       </i>
       "; Polish "
       <i>
        Pada
       </i>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       There are constructed languages that are not pro-drop but do not require this syntactic gap to be filled. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       , "He made the cake" would translate as
       <i>
        Li faris la kukon
       </i>
       (never
       <i>
        *Faris la kukon
       </i>
       ), but
       <i>
        It rained yesterday
       </i>
       would be
       <i>
        Pluvis hieraŭ
       </i>
       (not
       <i>
        *Ĝi pluvis hieraŭ
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Null_subjects_in_non-null-subject_languages">
        Null subjects in non-null-subject languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Null subjects in non-null-subject languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Other languages (sometimes called non-null-subject languages) require each sentence to include a subject: this is the case for most
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       , as well as many other languages (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , though a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       , also requires a subject). In some cases, colloquial expressions, particularly in English, less so in German, and occasionally in French, allow for the omission of the subject in the same way that languages such as Spanish and Russian allow using "correct" grammar:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "Bumped into George this morning." (I)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Agreed to have a snifter to catch up on old times." (We)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Told me what the two of you had been up to." (He)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Went down to Brighton for the weekend?" (You)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_imperative_form">
        The imperative form
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: The imperative form">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Even in such non-null-subject languages as English, it is standard for clauses in the
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperative mood
       </a>
       to lack explicit subjects; for example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "Take a break—you're working too hard."
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Shut up!"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Don't listen to him!"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       An explicit declaration of the pronoun in English in the imperative mood is possible, usually for emphasis but not necessary:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        "You stay away!"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        "Don't you listen to him!"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       French and German offer less flexibility with regards to null subjects. In French, it is neither grammatically correct nor possible to include the subject within the imperative form (the
       <i>
        vous
       </i>
       in the expression
       <i>
        taisez-vous
       </i>
       would stem from the fact that
       <i>
        se taire
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        to be silent
       </i>
       is a reflexive verb and is thus the object with similar meaning to "
       <i>
        yourself
       </i>
       " in an English imperative).
      </p>
      <p>
       In German, the informal form
       <i>
        du
       </i>
       may be added to the imperative in a colloquial manner for emphasis (
       <i>
        Mach du das
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you do it
       </i>
       ). The formal imperative requires the addition of the subject
       <i>
        Sie
       </i>
       (as in
       <i>
        Machen Sie das
       </i>
       ) because the formal, addressee-specific imperative form of a verb is morphologically identical to the infinitive, which when used by itself belongs in final position and indicates a "neutral" or addressee-nonspecific imperative (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       , "
       <i>
        Bitte nicht stören
       </i>
       " ["Please do not disturb"]).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pro-drop_in_infants">

      <p>
       Research shows
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

          </span>

       that until around three years old, children often omit subjects. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Drop bean.
       </li>
       <li>
        Brushing my teeth.
       </li>
       <li>
        Going to school.
       </li>
       <li>
        Want an apple.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Auxiliary_languages">
        Auxiliary languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Auxiliary languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/International_auxiliary_language" title="International auxiliary language">
        international auxiliary languages
       </a>
       , while not officially pro-drop, permit pronoun omission with some regularity.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Interlingua">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
        Interlingua
       </a>
       , pronoun omission is most common with the pronoun
       <i>
        il
       </i>
       , which means "it" when referring to part of a sentence or to nothing in particular. Examples of this word include
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Il pluvia.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         It's raining.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Il es ver que ille arriva deman.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         It is true that he arrives tomorrow.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       <i>
        Il
       </i>
       tends to be omitted whenever the contraction "it's" can be used in English. Thus,
       <i>
        il
       </i>
       may be omitted from the second sentence above: "Es ver que ille arriva deman". In addition, subject pronouns are sometimes omitted when they can be inferred from a previous sentence:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Illa audiva un crito. Curreva al porta. Aperiva lo.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         She heard a cry. Ran to the door. Opened it.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Null-subject_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Esperanto">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Similarly,
       <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
        Esperanto
       </a>
       sometimes exhibits pronoun deletion in casual use. This deletion is normally limited to subject pronouns, especially where the pronoun has been used just previously:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Ĉu vi vidas lin? Venas nun.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <small>
         QUESTION-PARTICLE
        </small>
        <i>
         you see him? Comes now.
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Do you see him?
        </i>
        He
        <i>
         is coming now.
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In "official" use, however, Esperanto admits of null-subject sentences in two cases only:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        (optional) in the 2nd person imperative
        <i>
         (N.B. The Esperanto imperative is often named "volitive" instead, since it can be conjugated with a subject in any person, and also used in subordinate clauses)
        </i>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Venu!
          <i>
           Come!
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          Vi venu!
          <i>
           You [there], come [with me]!
          </i>
          (pronoun added for emphasis)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </li>
       <li>
        For "impersonal verbs" which have no semantic subject. In English or French, an "empty" subject is nevertheless required:
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Pluvas.
          <i>
           <b>
            It
           </b>
           is raining.
          </i>
          FR:
          <i>
           <b>
            Il
           </b>
           pleut.
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          Estas nun somero.
          <i>
           <b>
            It
           </b>
           is summer now.
          </i>
          FR:
          <i>
           <b>
            C'
           </b>
           est l'été à présent.
          </i>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          Estas vere, ke li alvenos morgaŭ.
          <i>
           <b>
            It
           </b>
           is true that he will arrive tomorrow.
          </i>
          FR:
          <i>
           <b>
            C'
           </b>
           est vrai qu'il arrivera demain.
          </i>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <i>
             (In this latter case, the sentence is not really no-subject, since "ke li alvenos morgaŭ" ("that he will arrive tomorrow") is the subject.)
            </i>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Contrary to the Interlingua example above, and as in English, a repeated subject can normally be omitted only within a single sentence:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Ŝi aŭdis krion. Ŝi kuris al la pordo. Ŝi malfermis ĝin.
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           She heard a shout. She ran to the door. She opened it.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Ŝi aŭdis krion, kuris al la pordo kaj malfermis ĝin.
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           She heard a shout, ran to the door and opened it.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
