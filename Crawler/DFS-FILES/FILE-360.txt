       </a>

Between 1072 and 1157 it was again united with León, and after 1230 this union became permanent. Throughout this period the Castilian kings made extensive conquests in southern Iberia at the expense of the
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Islamic principalities
       </a>
       . Castile and León, with their southern acquisitions, came to be known collectively as the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Crown of Castile
       </a>
       , a term that also came to encompass overseas expansion.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="9th_to_11th_centuries:_The_beginnings">
        9th to 11th centuries: The beginnings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: 9th to 11th centuries: The beginnings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to the chronicles of
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias" title="Alfonso III of Asturias">
        Alfonso III of Asturias
       </a>
       ; the first reference to the name "Castile" (Castilla) can be found in a document written during AD 800. In
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       chronicles from the Cordoban Caliphate, the oldest sources refer to it as Al-Qila, or "the castled" high plains past the territory of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alava" title="Alava">
        Alava
       </a>
       , more south to it and the first encountered in their expeditions from
       <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">
        Zaragoza
       </a>
       . The name reflects its origin as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/March_(territory)" title="March (territory)">
        march
       </a>
       on the eastern frontier of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">
        Kingdom of Asturias
       </a>
       , protected by castles,
       <a href="/wiki/Tower" title="Tower">
        towers
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Castra" title="Castra">
        castra
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The County of Castile, bordered in the south by the northern reaches of the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Sistema_Central" title="Sistema Central">
        Sistema Central
       </a>
       mountain system, just north of modern-day Madrid province. It was re-populated by inhabitants of
       <a href="/wiki/Cantabri" title="Cantabri">
        Cantabria
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astur" title="Astur">
        Asturias
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vascones" title="Vascones">
        Vasconia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">
        Visigothic
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarab" title="Mozarab">
        Mozarab
       </a>
       origins. It had its own Romance dialect and customary laws.
      </p>
      <p>
       From the first half of the 9th century until the middle of the century, in which it came to be paid more closer attention to, its administration and defense by the monarchs of Leon - due the increased incursions from the
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Emirate of Córdoba">
        Emirate of Córdoba
       </a>
       - its first repopulation settlements were led by small abbots and local counts from the other side of the Cantabrian ridge neighbor valleys,
       <a href="/wiki/Trasmiera" title="Trasmiera">
        Trasmiera
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Primorias&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Primorias (page does not exist)">
        Primorias
       </a>
       and smaller ones, being its first settlers from the contiguous maritime valleys of Mena and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encartaciones" title="Encartaciones">
        Encartaciones
       </a>
       in nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Biscay" title="Biscay">
        Biscay
       </a>
       , some of whom had abandoned those exposed areas of the Meseta a few decades earlier, and taken refuge by the much dense and intractable woods of the Atlantic valleys, so they were not that foreign to them.
      </p>
      <p>
       A mix of settlers from the recently swelled with refugees Cantabrian and Basque coastal areas, were led under the protection of Abbot Vitulus and his brother, count Herwig, as registered in the local charters they signed around the first years of the 800's. The areas that they settled didn't extend far from the Cantabrian southeastern ridges, and not beyond the southern reaches of the high Ebro river valleys and canyon gores.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Count of a wider and more united Castile was
       <a href="/wiki/Rodrigo_of_Castile" title="Rodrigo of Castile">
        Rodrigo
       </a>
       in 850, under
       <a href="/wiki/Ordo%C3%B1o_I_of_Asturias" title="Ordoño I of Asturias">
        Ordoño I of Asturias
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Asturias" title="Alfonso III of Asturias">
        Alfonso III of Asturias
       </a>
       , who settled and fortified the ancient Cantabrian hill town of
       <a href="/wiki/Amaya_(Burgos)" title="Amaya (Burgos)">
        Amaya
       </a>
       , much farther west and south of the Ebro river to offer a more easy defense and command of the still functional Roman Empire main highway passing by, south of the Cantabrian ridge all the way to Leon, from the Muslim military expeditions. Subsequently, the region was subdivided, separate counts being named to Alava, Burgos, Cerezo &amp; Lantarón, and a reduced Castile. In 931 the County was reunified by Count
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fern%C3%A1n_Gonz%C3%A1lez" title="Fernán González">
        Fernán González
       </a>
       , who rose in rebellion against the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        Kingdom of León
       </a>
       , successor state to Asturias, and achieved an autonomous status, allowing the county to be inherited by his family instead of being subject to appointment by the Leonese king.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="11th_and_12th_centuries:_Expansion_and_union_with_the_Kingdom_of_Le.C3.B3n">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="11th_and_12th_centuries:_Expansion_and_union_with_the_Kingdom_of_León">
        11th and 12th centuries: Expansion and union with the Kingdom of León
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 11th and 12th centuries: Expansion and union with the Kingdom of León">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:427px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="574" data-file-width="827" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg/425px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg/638px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg/850px-Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg.png 2x" width="425"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_Iberian_Peninsula_1037-es.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Kingdom of Castile (Castilla) in 1037
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The minority of Count García Sánchez led Castile to accept
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sancho_III_of_Navarre" title="Sancho III of Navarre">
        Sancho III of Navarre
       </a>
       , married to the sister of Count García, as feudal overlord. García was assassinated in 1028 while in León to marry the princess Sancha, sister of
       <a href="/wiki/Bermudo_III_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Bermudo III of León">
        Bermudo III of León
       </a>
       . Sancho III, acting as feudal overlord, appointed his younger son (García's nephew)
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Ferdinand I of León">
        Ferdinand
       </a>
       as Count of Castile, marrying him to his uncle's intended bride, Sancha of León. Following Sancho's 1035 death, Castile returned to the nominal control of León, but Ferdinand, allying himself with his brother
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garc%C3%ADa_S%C3%A1nchez_III_of_Navarre" title="García Sánchez III of Navarre">
        García Sánchez III of Navarre
       </a>
       , began a war with his brother-in-law Bermudo. At the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Tamar%C3%B3n" title="Battle of Tamarón">
        Battle of Tamarón
       </a>
       Bermudo was killed, leaving no surviving offspring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In right of his wife, Ferdinand then assumed the royal title as king of León and Castile, for the first time associating the royal title with the rule of Castile.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When Ferdinand I died in 1065, the territories were divided among his children.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sancho_II_of_Castile" title="Sancho II of Castile">
        Sancho II
       </a>
       became King of Castile,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VI_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VI of Castile">
        Alfonso VI
       </a>
       , King of León and García, King of Galicia,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while his daughters were given towns,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Urraca_of_Zamora,_Infanta_of_Castile" title="Urraca of Zamora, Infanta of Castile">
        Urraca
       </a>
       , Zamora, and
       <a href="/wiki/Elvira_of_Toro" title="Elvira of Toro">
        Elvira
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Toro,_Zamora" title="Toro, Zamora">
        Toro
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sancho II allied himself with Alfonso VI of León and together they conquered, then divided Galicia. Sancho later attacked Alfonso VI and invaded León with the help of
       <a href="/wiki/El_Cid" title="El Cid">
        El Cid
       </a>
       , and drove his brother into exile, thereby reuniting the three kingdoms. Urraca permitted the greater part of the Leonese army to take refuge in the town of Zamora. Sancho laid siege to the town, but the Castilian king was assassinated in 1072 by Bellido Dolfos, a Galician nobleman. The Castilian troops then withdrew.
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result, Alfonso VI recovered all his original territory of León, and now also became the king of Castile and Galicia. This was the second union of León and Castile, although the two kingdoms remained distinct entities joined only in a
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_union" title="Personal union">
        personal union
       </a>
       . The [
       <a href="/wiki/El_Cid" title="El Cid">
        oath taken by El Cid
       </a>
       ] before Alfonso VI in Santa Gadea de Burgos regarding the innocence of Alfonso in the matter of the murder of his brother is well known.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under Alfonso VI, there was an approach to the rest of Europeans kingdoms, including
       <a href="/wiki/France_in_the_Middle_Ages" title="France in the Middle Ages">
        France
       </a>
       . He gave his daughters, Elvira, Urraca and Theresa, in marriage to Raymond of Toulouse, Raymond of Burgundy and Henry of Burgundy respectively. In the Council of Burgos in 1080 the traditional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarabic_rite" title="Mozarabic rite">
        Mozarabic rite
       </a>
       was replaced by the Roman one. Upon his death, Alfonso VI was succeeded by his daughter the widowed Urraca, who then married Alfonso I of Aragon, but they almost immediately fell out, and Alfonso tried unsuccessfully to conquer Urraca's lands, before he repudiated her in 1114. Urraca also had to contend with attempts by her son (offspring of her first marriage), the king of Galicia, to assert his rights. When Urraca died, this son became king of León and Castile as
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VII_of_Le%C3%B3n_and_Castile" title="Alfonso VII of León and Castile">
        Alfonso VII
       </a>
       . During his reign, Alfonso VII managed to annex parts of the weaker kingdoms of Navarre and Aragón which fought to secede after the death of Alfonso I of Aragon. Alfonso VII refused his right to conquer the Mediterranean coast for the new union of Aragón with the County of Barcelona (Petronila and Ramón Berenguer IV).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="12th_century:_A_link_between_Christianity_and_Islam">
        12th century: A link between Christianity and Islam
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 12th century: A link between Christianity and Islam">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The centuries of Moorish rule had confirmed the high central tableland of Castile as a vast sheep pasturage; the fact that the greater part of Spanish sheep-rearing terminology was drawn from Arabic underscores the debt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 8th and 9th centuries was preceded by a period of
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">
        Umayyad
       </a>
       conquests, as Arabs took control of previously Hellenized areas such as
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">
        Syria
       </a>
       in the 7th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At this point they first began to encounter Greek ideas, though from the beginning, many Arabs were hostile to classical learning.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ros-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because of this hostility, the religious Caliphs could not support scientific translations. Translators had to seek out wealthy business patrons rather than religious ones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ros-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Until Abassid rule in the 8th century, however, there was little work in translation. Most knowledge of Greek during Umayyad rule was gained from those scholars of Greek who remained from the Byzantine period, rather than through widespread translation and dissemination of texts. A few scholars argue that translation was more widespread than is thought during this period, but theirs remains the minority view.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ros-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The main period of translation was during Abbasid rule. The 2nd Abassid Caliph Al-Mansur moved the capital from Damascus to Baghdad.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-anti_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-anti-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Here he founded the great library with texts containing Greek Classical texts. Al-Mansur ordered this rich fund of world literature translated into Arabic. Under al-Mansur and by his orders, translations were made from Greek, Syriac, and Persian, the Syriac and Persian books being themselves translations from Greek or Sanskrit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEO'Leary1922107_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Leary1922107-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Owing to the legacy of the 6th century King of Persia, Anushirvan (Chosroes I) the Just had introducing many Greek ideas into his kingdom.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brick_1_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-brick_1-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Aided by this knowledge and juxtaposition of beliefs, the Abassids considered it valuable to look at Islam with Greek eyes, and to look at the Greeks with Islamic eyes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ros_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ros-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Abassid philosophers also pressed the idea that Islam had from the very beginning stressed the gathering of knowledge as important to the religion. These new lines of thought allowed the work of amassing and translating Greek ideas to expand as it never before had.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 12th century, Europe enjoyed a great advance in intellectual achievements sparked in part by the kingdom of Castile's conquest of the great cultural center of
       <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">
        Toledo
       </a>
       (1085). There Arabic classics were discovered, and contacts established with the knowledge and works of Muslim scientists. In the first half of the century a
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">
        program of translations
       </a>
       , traditionally called the "School of Toledo", was undertaken which rendered many philosophical and scientific works from the classical Greek and the Islamic worlds into Latin. Many European scholars, including
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_of_Morley" title="Daniel of Morley">
        Daniel of Morley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona" title="Gerard of Cremona">
        Gerard of Cremona
       </a>
       travelled to Toledo to gain further education.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Way_of_St._James" title="Way of St. James">
        Way of St. James
       </a>
       further enhanced the cultural exchange between the kingdoms of Castile and León and the rest of Europe.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 12th century saw the establishment of many new religious orders, after the European fashion, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Calatrava" title="Order of Calatrava">
        Calatrava
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Alc%C3%A1ntara" title="Order of Alcántara">
        Alcántara
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Santiago" title="Order of Santiago">
        Santiago
       </a>
       ; and the foundation of many
       <a href="/wiki/Cistercians" title="Cistercians">
        Cistercian abbeys
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Castile_and_Le.C3.B3n">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Castile_and_León">
        Castile and León
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Castile and León">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="13th_century:_Definitive_union_with_the_Kingdom_of_Le.C3.B3n">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="13th_century:_Definitive_union_with_the_Kingdom_of_León">
        13th century: Definitive union with the Kingdom of León
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: 13th century: Definitive union with the Kingdom of León">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VII" title="Alfonso VII">
        Alfonso VII
       </a>
       restored the royal tradition of dividing his kingdom among his children.
       <a href="/wiki/Sancho_III_of_Castile" title="Sancho III of Castile">
        Sancho III
       </a>
       became King of Castile and
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Ferdinand II of León">
        Ferdinand II
       </a>
       , King of León.
      </p>
      <p>
       The rivalry between both kingdoms continued until 1230 when
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_III_of_Castile" title="Ferdinand III of Castile">
        Ferdinand III of Castile
       </a>
       received the Kingdom of León from his father
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_IX_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Alfonso IX of León">
        Alfonso IX
       </a>
       , having previously received the Kingdom of Castile from his mother
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berenguela_of_Castile" title="Berenguela of Castile">
        Berenguela of Castile
       </a>
       in 1217. In addition, he took advantage of the decline of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almohad" title="Almohad">
        Almohad
       </a>
       empire to conquer the Guadalquivir Valley whilst his son
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_X_of_Castile" title="Alfonso X of Castile">
        Alfonso X
       </a>
       took the
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Murcia" title="Taifa of Murcia">
        taifa of Murcia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Courts from León and Castile merged, an event considered as the foundation of the Crown of Castile, consisting of the kingdoms of Castile, León, taifas and other domains conquered from the
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       , including the
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Taifa of Córdoba">
        taifa of Córdoba
       </a>
       , taifa of Murcia,
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Ja%C3%A9n" title="Taifa of Jaén">
        taifa of Jaén
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa_of_Seville" title="Taifa of Seville">
        taifa of Seville
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="14th_and_15th_centuries:_The_House_of_Trast.C3.A1mara">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="14th_and_15th_centuries:_The_House_of_Trastámara">
        14th and 15th centuries: The House of Trastámara
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 14th and 15th centuries: The House of Trastámara">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Trast%C3%A1maran_Castile" title="Trastámaran Castile">
        Trastámaran Castile
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:242px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crown_of_Castile_-_Map.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="365" data-file-width="330" height="265" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Crown_of_Castile_-_Map.gif/240px-Crown_of_Castile_-_Map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c0/Crown_of_Castile_-_Map.gif 1.5x" width="240"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crown_of_Castile_-_Map.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
          Crown of Castile
         </a>
         through the years
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Trast%C3%A1mara" title="House of Trastámara">
        House of Trastámara
       </a>
       was a lineage that ruled Castile from 1369 to 1504,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arag%C3%B3n" title="Aragón">
        Aragón
       </a>
       from 1412 to 1516, Navarre from 1425 to 1479, and Naples from 1442 to 1501.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its name was taken from the Count (or Duke) of Trastámara.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This title was used by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_II_of_Castile" title="Henry II of Castile">
        Henry II of Castile
       </a>
       , of the Mercedes, before coming to the throne in 1369, during the civil war with his legitimate brother, King
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Castile" title="Peter of Castile">
        Peter of Castile
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_II_of_Arag%C3%B3n" title="John II of Aragón">
        John II of Aragón
       </a>
       ruled from 1458 to 1479 and upon his death, his daughter became Queen
       <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_of_Navarre" title="Eleanor of Navarre">
        Eleanor of Navarre
       </a>
       and his son became King
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand II of Aragon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Union_of_the_Crowns_of_Castile_and_Aragon">
        Union of the Crowns of Castile and Aragon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Union of the Crowns of Castile and Aragon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The marriage of
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand II of Aragon
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Queen_Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Queen Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella I of Castile
       </a>
       , in 1469 at the
       <a href="/wiki/Palacio_de_los_Vivero" title="Palacio de los Vivero">
        Palacio de los Vivero
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Valladolid" title="Valladolid">
        Valladolid
       </a>
       , began a familial union of the two kingdoms. They became known as
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">
        the Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       <i>
        (los Reyes Católicos)
       </i>
       . Isabella succeeded her brother as Queen of Castile and Ferdinand became
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jure_uxoris" title="Jure uxoris">
         jure uxoris
        </a>
       </i>
       King of Castile in 1474. When Ferdinand succeeded his father as King of Aragon in 1479, the Crown of Castile and the various territories of the Crown of Aragon were united in a personal union creating for the first time since the 8th century a single political unit referred to as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
         España
        </a>
        (Spain)
       </i>
       . 'Los Reyes Católicos' started policies to diminish the power of the bourgeoisie and nobility in Castile, and greatly reduced the powers of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">
         Cortes
        </a>
       </i>
       (General Courts) to the point where they '
       <i>
        rubber-stamped
       </i>
       ' the monarch's acts, and brought the nobility to their side. In 1492 the Kingdom of Castile conquered the last Moorish state of Granada, thereby ending Muslim rule in Iberia and completing the Reconquista.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="16th_century">
        16th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 16th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On Isabella's death in 1504 her daughter,
       <a href="/wiki/Joanna_of_Castile" title="Joanna of Castile">
        Joanna I
       </a>
       , became Queen (in name) with her husband
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_I_of_Castile" title="Philip I of Castile">
        Philip I
       </a>
       as King (in authority). After his death Joanna's father was regent, due to her perceived
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mental_illness" title="Mental illness">
        mental illness
       </a>
       , as her son Charles I was only six years old. On Ferdinand II's death in 1516,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">
        Charles I
       </a>
       was proclaimed as king of Castile and of Aragon (in authority) jointly with his mother Joanna I as the Queen of Aragon (in name).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the first monarch to reign over both Castile and Aragon he may be considered as the first operational
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">
        King of Spain
       </a>
       . Charles I also became Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor in 1519.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Government:_Municipal_councils_and_parliaments">
        Government: Municipal councils and parliaments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kingdom_of_Castile&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Government: Municipal councils and parliaments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As with all medieval kingdoms, supreme power was understood to reside in the monarch "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/By_the_grace_of_God" title="By the grace of God">
        by the grace of God
       </a>
       ", as the legal formula explained. Nevertheless, rural and urban communities began to form assemblies to issue regulations to deal with everyday problems. Over time, these assemblies evolved into municipal councils, known as variously as
       <i>
        ayuntamientos
       </i>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Cabildo_(council)" title="Cabildo (council)">
        <i>
         cabildos
        </i>
       </a>
       , in which some of the inhabitants, the property-owning heads of
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_household" title="Medieval household">
        households
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vecino" title="Vecino">
         vecinos
        </a>
       </i>
       ), represented the rest. By the 14th century these councils had gained more powers, such as the right to elect municipal magistrates and officers (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alcalde" title="Alcalde">
         alcaldes
        </a>
       </i>
       , speakers, clerks, etc.) and representatives to the parliaments (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cortes_Generales" title="Cortes Generales">
         Cortes
        </a>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the increasing power of the municipal councils and the need for communication between these and the King,
       <i>
        cortes
       </i>
       were established in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        Kingdom of León
       </a>
       in 1188, and in Castile in 1250. In the earliest Leonese and Castilian Cortes, the inhabitants of the cities (known as "laboratores") formed a small group of the representatives and had no legislative powers, but they were a link between the king and the general population, something that was pioneered by the kingdoms of Castile and León. Eventually the representatives of the cities gained the right to vote in the Cortes, often allying with the monarchs against the great noble lords.
      </p>
      <h2>

        </span>
