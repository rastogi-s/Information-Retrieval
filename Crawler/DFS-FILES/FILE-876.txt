      <p>
       <b>
        Peruvian Spanish
       </b>
       is a family of dialects of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       that have been spoken in Peru since 1532. There are four varieties spoken in the country, by about 80% of the population.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The four Peruvian dialects are
       <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
        Andean Spanish
       </a>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peruvian_Coast_Spanish" title="Peruvian Coast Spanish">
        Peruvian Coast Spanish
       </a>
       ; Andean-Costal Spanish or
       <i>
        neolimeño;
       </i>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Amazonic_Spanish" title="Amazonic Spanish">
        Amazonic Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spanish language first arrived in what today is
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       in 1532. During colonial and early republican times, the Spanish spoken colloquially in the coast and in the cities of the highland possessed strong local features, but as a result of dialect leveling in favor of the standard language, the language of urban Peruvians today is more or less uniform in pronunciation throughout most of the country.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vestiges of the older dialect of the coast can be found in the speech of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Peruvians" title="Afro-Peruvians">
        black Peruvians
       </a>
       , which retains Andalusian features such as the aspiration or deletion of final /s/ and the deletion of final /r/. The dialect of Arequipa in its pure form is now extinct, although some elders are familiar with it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout most of the highland,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quechua_languages" title="Quechua languages">
        Quechua
       </a>
       continued to be the language of the majority until the mid 20th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mass migration (rural exodus) into Lima starting in the 1940s, and into other major cities and regional capitals later on, accompanied by discrimination and the growth of mass media, have reconfigured the linguistic demography of the country in favor of Spanish. The poor urban masses originating in this migration adopted the standardized dialect spoken in the cities, however with traces of Andean pronunciation and a simplified syntax.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Peruvian dialects">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Andean_Spanish">
        Andean Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Andean Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
        Andean Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Andean Spanish the most common dialect in the Andes (more marked in rural areas) and has many similarities with the "standard" dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
        Ecuador
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
        Bolivia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Principal_characteristics">
        Principal characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Principal characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The phonology of Andean Peruvian Spanish is distinguished by its slow time and unique rhythm (
       <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">
        grave accent
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Assibilation" title="Assibilation">
        assibilation
       </a>
       of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɾ/
       </span>
       , and an apparent confusion of the vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       . (In reality, they are producing a sound between /e/ and /i/, and between /o/ and /u/.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) Furthermore, the "s" (originally apical and without aspiration) is produced with more force than that of the coast; this is also generally true of the other consonants, at the loss of the vowels. Other distinctive features are the preservation of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ/
       </span>
       , sometimes hypercorrective realization of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʝ/
       </span>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʎ]
       </span>
       , and the realization of velar plosives as a fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic" title="Morphosyntactic">
        morphosyntactic
       </a>
       characteristics are typical:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Confusion or unification of gender and number
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          A ellas lo recibí bien.
         </b>
        </i>
        .
        <i>
         <b>
          La revista es caro.
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Confusion or unification of gender and number
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          esa es su trenza del carlos.
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Overuse of the diminutives –ito e –ita
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Vente aquicito.
         </b>
        </i>
        .
        <i>
         <b>
          Sí, señorita, ahí están sus hijos.
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lo%C3%ADsmo" title="Loísmo">
         Loísmo
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Lo echan la agua
         </b>
        </i>
        .
        <i>
         <b>
          Lo pintan la casa
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Duplication of the possessives and objects
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Su casa de Pepe.
         </b>
         .
         <b>
          Lo conozco a ella.
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The absence or redundant use of articles
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Plaza de Armas es acá. La María está loca.
         </b>
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Uncommon use of the preposition "en" in front of
        <a href="/wiki/Locative_adverb" title="Locative adverb">
         locative adverbs
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Todo caerá en su encima

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Peruvian_coast_Spanish">
        Peruvian coast Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Peruvian coast Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peruvian_Coast_Spanish" title="Peruvian Coast Spanish">
        Peruvian Coast Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Coastal Spanish is spoken throughout the coast. It has the reputation (in pronunciation) of being one of the "purest" dialects in all of coastal Latin America because it does not debuccalize
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       between vowels
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Cleanup" title="Wikipedia:Cleanup">
          <span title="Wikipedia:Cleanup (June 2015)">
           is "in syllable coda" what is meant?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and retains the fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [χ]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is the characteristic dialect as perceived abroad and has the reputation of being the base of "normal" or
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard
       </a>
       Peruvian Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The vowels are stable and clear.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɾ/
        </span>
        are pronounced clearly, without any fricativization.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is more often laminal than apical, and debuccalized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        in front of most consonants (though it is
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
          x
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        ). It is retained as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        in final position (as opposed to in
        <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
         Chile
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalucia" title="Andalucia">
         Andalucia
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        varies between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_fricative" title="Voiceless uvular fricative">
          χ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        ; it is never
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final nasals are
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
         velar
        </a>
        (not alveolar like in
        <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
         Mexico
        </a>
        or central Spain).
       </li>
       <li>
        The final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        is normally
        <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
         elided
        </a>
        , but sometimes devoiced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiceless dental and alveolar stops">
          t
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        in formal speech.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
         Yeísmo
        </a>
        exists, the phoneme occurring as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
          ʝ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">
          j
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        interchangeably, and as palato-alveolar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ]
        </span>
        in initial position by some speakers.
       </li>
       <li>
        The tendency to eliminate
        <a href="/wiki/Hiatus_(linguistics)" title="Hiatus (linguistics)">
         hiatus
        </a>
        in word with an
        <i>
         -ear
        </i>
        suffix.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       General Spanish phrases from the Americas are common but there are also phrases that originate in the Lima coastal area, such as frequent traditional terms and expressions; the most ingrained
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_words_from_indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas#Words_from_Quechua" title="List of English words from indigenous languages of the Americas">
        "quechuaism"
       </a>
       in common speech is the familiar
       <i>
        calato
       </i>
       , meaning "naked".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Andean-Costal_Spanish_or_neolime.C3.B1o">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Andean-Costal_Spanish_or_neolimeño">
        Andean-Costal Spanish or
        <i>
         neolimeño
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Andean-Costal Spanish or neolimeño">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Born in the most recent 30–50 years with a mixture of the speech of Andean migrants and the speech of Lima. This dialect is the speech that is most typical of Peru.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Amazonic_Spanish">
        Amazonic Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Peruvian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Amazonic Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Amazonic_Spanish" title="Amazonic Spanish">
        Amazonic Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       This dialect has developed uniquely, with contact from Andean Spanish and the Spanish of Lima with the
       <a href="/wiki/Amazonian_languages" title="Amazonian languages">
        Amazonian languages
       </a>
       . It has a distinctive tonal structure.
      </p>
      <p>
       Phonetically it is characterized by:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        resisting aspiration
       </li>
       <li>
        A confusion of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        (always bilabial)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        For example,
        <i>
         San Juan
        </i>
        becomes
        <i>
         San Fan
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        There is
        <a href="/wiki/Occlusive" title="Occlusive">
         occlusion
        </a>
        of the intervals
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b, d, g/
        </span>
        in tonal ascension with aspiration and lengthening of the vowels.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p, t, k/
        </span>
        are pronounced with aspiration
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʝ/
        </span>
        tends to become an affricate (as opposed to Coastal Peruvian Spanish)
       </li>
       <li>
        Also, there is assibilation and weak
        <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
         trills
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       On the other hand, the syntactic order most recognized is the prefixation of the genitive:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         De Antonio sus amigas
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       There are also disorders of agreement, gender, etc.
      </p>
