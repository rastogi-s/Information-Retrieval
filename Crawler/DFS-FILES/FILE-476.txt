      <p>
       <b>
        Aragonese
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="/ær/: 'arr' in 'marry'">
            ær
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">
            ɡ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <i>
        aragonés
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [aɾaɣoˈnes]
        </a>
       </span>
       in Aragonese) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       spoken in several dialects by 10,000 to 30,000 people in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       valleys of
       <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       , Spain, primarily in the
       <a href="/wiki/Comarca#Spain" title="Comarca">
        comarcas
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Somontano_de_Barbastro" title="Somontano de Barbastro">
        Somontano de Barbastro
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacetania" title="Jacetania">
        Jacetania
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alto_G%C3%A1llego" title="Alto Gállego">
        Alto Gállego
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sobrarbe" title="Sobrarbe">
        Sobrarbe
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ribagorza/Ribagor%C3%A7a" title="Ribagorza/Ribagorça">
        Ribagorza/Ribagorça
       </a>
       . It is the only modern language which survived from medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">
        Navarro-Aragonese
       </a>
       in a form distinctly different from
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Informally known as
       <i>
        fabla
       </i>
       ("talk" or "speech"), Aragonese is also commonly referred to by the names of its
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language#Dialects" title="Aragonese language">
        local dialects
       </a>
       such as
       <i>
        cheso
       </i>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Valle_de_Hecho" title="Valle de Hecho">
        Valle de Hecho
       </a>
       ) or
       <i>
        patués
       </i>
       (from the
       <a href="/wiki/Benasque" title="Benasque">
        Benasque
       </a>
       Valley).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="257" data-file-width="192" height="294" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/220px-Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/330px-Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/440px-Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Evoluci%C3%B3n_cheografica_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The gradual retreat of Aragonese under the pressure of Castilian.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Aragonese, which developed in portions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">
        Ebro
       </a>
       basin, can be traced back to the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       . It spread throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       to areas where languages similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       were previously spoken. The
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">
        Kingdom of Aragon
       </a>
       (formed by the counties of Aragon, Sobrarbe and Ribagorza) expanded southward from the mountains, pushing the
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       farther south in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
         Reconquista
        </a>
       </i>
       and spreading the Aragonese language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The union of the
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalan counties
       </a>
       and the Kingdom of Aragon which formed the 12th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
        Crown of Aragon
       </a>
       did not merge the languages of the two territories;
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       continued to be spoken in the east and
       <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">
        Navarro-Aragonese
       </a>
       in the west, with boundaries blurred by
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialectal continuity
       </a>
       . The Aragonese
       <i>
        Reconquista
       </i>
       in the south ended with the cession of
       <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_Aragon" title="James I of Aragon">
        James I of Aragon
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       as dowry for an Aragonese princess.
      </p>
      <p>
       The best-known proponent of the Aragonese language was
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Heredia" title="Juan Fernández de Heredia">
        Johan Ferrandez d'Heredia
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Master_of_the_Knights_Hospitaller" title="Grand Master of the Knights Hospitaller">
        Grand Master of the Knights Hospitaller
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Rhodes" title="Rhodes">
        Rhodes
       </a>
       at the end of the 14th century. He wrote an extensive catalog of works in Aragonese and translated several works from Greek into Aragonese (the first in medieval Europe).
      </p>
      <p>
       The spread of Castilian (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ), the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Castilian
       </a>
       origin of the
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Trast%C3%A1mara" title="House of Trastámara">
        Trastámara dynasty
       </a>
       , and the similarity between Castilian and Aragonese facilitated the recession of the latter. A turning point was the 15th-century coronation of the Castilian
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_I_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand I of Aragon">
        Ferdinand I of Aragon
       </a>
       , also known as Ferdinand of Antequeraa.
      </p>
      <p>
       In recent times, Aragonese was mostly regarded as a group of rural dialects of Spanish. Compulsory education undermined its already weak position; for example, pupils were punished for using it. However, the 1978
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_transition_to_democracy" title="Spanish transition to democracy">
        Spanish transition to democracy
       </a>
       heralded literary works and studies of the language.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_Aragonese">
        Modern Aragonese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern Aragonese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg">
         <img alt="Multicolored map of Aragon" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="625" data-file-width="625" height="270" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/270px-Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/405px-Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cb/Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg/540px-Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg.png 2x" width="270"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grupos_de_parlas_de_l%27aragon%C3%A9s.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Aragonese dialect map
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Aragonese is the native language of the Aragonese mountain ranges of the Pyrenees, in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Comarca" title="Comarca">
         comarcas
        </a>
       </i>
       of Somontano, Jacetania, Sobrarbe, and Ribagorza. Cities and towns in which Aragonese is spoken are
       <a href="/wiki/Huesca" title="Huesca">
        Huesca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Graus" title="Graus">
        Graus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Monz%C3%B3n" title="Monzón">
        Monzón
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Barbastro" title="Barbastro">
        Barbastro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bielsa" title="Bielsa">
        Bielsa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gista%C3%ADn" title="Gistaín">
        Chistén
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fonz" title="Fonz">
        Fonz
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Echo,_Arag%C3%B3n,_Spain" title="Echo, Aragón, Spain">
        Echo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Estadilla" title="Estadilla">
        Estadilla
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Benasque" title="Benasque">
        Benasque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Campo,_Aragon" title="Campo, Aragon">
        Campo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sabi%C3%B1%C3%A1nigo" title="Sabiñánigo">
        Sabiñánigo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jaca" title="Jaca">
        Jaca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plan" title="Plan">
        Plan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ans%C3%B3" title="Ansó">
        Ansó
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ayerbe" title="Ayerbe">
        Ayerbe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Broto" title="Broto">
        Broto
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/El_Grado" title="El Grado">
        El Grado
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It is spoken as a second language by inhabitants of
       <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">
        Zaragoza
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Huesca" title="Huesca">
        Huesca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ejea_de_los_Caballeros" title="Ejea de los Caballeros">
        Ejea de los Caballeros
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Teruel" title="Teruel">
        Teruel
       </a>
       . According to recent polls, there are about 25,500 speakers(2011)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       including speakers living outside the native area.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2009, the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_Act_of_Aragon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Languages Act of Aragon (page does not exist)">
        Languages Act of Aragon
       </a>
       recognized the "native language, original and historic" of
       <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       . The language received several
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_rights" title="Linguistic rights">
        linguistic rights
       </a>
       , including its use in public administration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Aragonese has many historical traits in common with Catalan. Some are conservative features that are also shared with the
       <a href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese_languages" title="Astur-Leonese languages">
        Astur-Leonese languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">
        Galician-Portuguese
       </a>
       , where Spanish innovated in ways that did not spread to nearby languages.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_with_Catalan">
        Shared with Catalan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Shared with Catalan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Romance initial F- is preserved, e.g. FILIUM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           fillo
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("son", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           hijo
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           fill
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Pt.
        <i>
         <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
          <i>
           filho
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Romance
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">
         palatal approximant
        </a>
        (GE-, GI-, I-) consistently became medieval
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ]
        </span>
        , as in medieval Catalan and Portuguese. This becomes modern
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ]
        </span>
        , as a result of the devoicing of sibilants (see below). In Spanish, the medieval result was either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ]
        </span>
        (modern
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        ),
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʝ]
        </span>
        or nothing, depending on the context. E.g. IUVENEM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           choven
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("young man", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           joven
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ˈxoβen/
        </span>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           jove
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ˈʒoβǝ/
        </span>
        ), GELARE &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           chelar
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("to freeze", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           helar
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /eˈlaɾ/
        </span>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           gelar
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʒǝˈla/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Romance groups -LT-, -CT- result in
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [jt]
        </span>
        , e.g. FACTUM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           feito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("done", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           hecho
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           fet
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Gal./Port.
        <i>
         <span lang="gl" xml:lang="gl">
          <i>
           feito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ), MULTUM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           muito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("many"/"much", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           mucho
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           molt
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Gal.
        <i>
         <span lang="gl" xml:lang="gl">
          <i>
           moito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Port.
        <i>
         <span lang="gl" xml:lang="gl">
          <i>
           muito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Romance groups -X-, -PS-, SCj- result in voiceless palatal fricative
        <i>
         ix
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        , e.g. COXU &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           coixo
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("crippled", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           cojo
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           coix
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Romance groups -Lj-, -C'L-, -T'L- result in palatal lateral
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʎ]
        </span>
        , e.g. MULIERE &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           muller
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("woman", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           mujer
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           muller
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ), ACUT'LA &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           agulla
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("needle", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           aguja
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           agulla
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_with_Catalan_and_Spanish">
        Shared with Catalan and Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Shared with Catalan and Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Open O, E from Romance result systematically in diphthongs
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [we]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [je]
        </span>
        , e.g. VET'LA &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           viella
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("old woman", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           vieja
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           vella
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ). This includes before a palatal approximant, e.g.
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         octō
        </span>
        &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           ueito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("eight", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           ocho
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           vuit
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ). Spanish diphthongizes except before yod, whereas Catalan
        <i>
         only
        </i>
        diphthongizes before yod.
       </li>
       <li>
        Loss of final unstressed -E but not -O, e.g. GRANDE &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           gran
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("big"), FACTUM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           feito
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("done"). Catalan loses both -O and -E; Spanish preserves -O and sometimes -E.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Shared_with_Spanish">
        Shared with Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Former voiced sibilants become voiceless (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [z] &gt; [s]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒ] &gt; [tʃ]
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>

        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>

          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ("he was sleeping", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           dormía
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           dormia
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        High Aragonese dialects (
        <i>
         alto aragonés
        </i>
        ) and some dialects of
        <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
         Gascon
        </a>
        have preserved the voicelessness of many intervocalic stop consonants, e.g. CLETAM &gt;
        <i>
         <span lang="an" xml:lang="an">
          <i>
           cleta
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>

        <i>

        <i>

          <i>
           claie
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ), CUCULLIATAM &gt;
        <i>
         cocullata
        </i>
        ("crested lark", Sp.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <i>
           cogujada
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        , Cat.
        <i>
         <span lang="ca" xml:lang="ca">
          <i>
           cogullada
          </i>
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Several Aragonese dialects maintain Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         -ll-
        </span>
        as
        <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">
         geminate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>
        .
       </li>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
        Orthography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Orthography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2010, the
       <a href="/wiki/Academia_de_l%27Aragon%C3%A9s" title="Academia de l'Aragonés">
        Academia de l'Aragonés
       </a>
       (founded in 2006) established an orthographic standard to modernize medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       and to make it more etymological. The new orthography is used by the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://an.wikipedia.org/wiki/" title="an:">
        Aragonese Wikipedia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Aragonese had two orthographic standards:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         grafía de Uesca
        </i>
        , codified in 1987 by the Consello d'a Fabla Aragonesa (CFA) at a convention in
        <a href="/wiki/Huesca" title="Huesca">
         Huesca
        </a>
        , is used by most Aragonese writers. It has a more uniform system of assigning letters to phonemes, with less regard for etymology; words traditionally written with ⟨v⟩ and ⟨b⟩ are uniformly written with ⟨b⟩ in the Uesca system. Similarly, ⟨ch⟩, ⟨j⟩, and ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩ are all written ⟨ch⟩. It uses letters associated with Spanish, such as ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%91" title="Ñ">
         ñ
        </a>
        ⟩.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         grafía SLA
        </i>
        , devised in 2004 by the
        <a href="/wiki/Sociedat_de_Ling%C3%BCistica_Aragonesa" title="Sociedat de Lingüistica Aragonesa">
         Sociedat de Lingüistica Aragonesa
        </a>
        (SLA), is used by some Aragonese writers. It uses etymological forms which are closer to Catalan, Occitan, and medieval Aragonese sources. In the SLA system ⟨v⟩, ⟨b⟩,⟨ch⟩, ⟨j⟩, and ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩ are distinct, and the
        <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
         digraph
        </a>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Ny_(digraph)" title="Ny (digraph)">
         ny
        </a>
        ⟩ replaces ⟨ñ⟩.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       During the 16th century, Aragonese
       <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">
        Moriscos
       </a>
       wrote
       <a href="/wiki/Aljamiado" title="Aljamiado">
        aljamiado
       </a>
       texts (Romance texts in Arabic writing), possibly because of their inability to write in
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       . The language in these texts has a mixture of Aragonese and Castilian traits, and they are among the last known written examples of the Aragonese formerly spoken in central and southern Aragon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Aragonese grammar is similar to that of other Iberian Romance languages, such as Spanish and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">
        <span id="Definite_article">
        </span>
        <span id="Article">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The definite article in Aragonese has undergone dialect-related changes, with definite articles in Old Aragonese similar to their present Spanish equivalents. The most-widespread articles in Aragonese are similar to those in Galician and Portuguese, since they lack the initial
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       :

      <p>
       Neighboring Romance languages have influenced Aragonese. Catalan and Occitan influenced Medieval Aragonese, and the Catalan influence continued under the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
        Crown of Aragon
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ribagor%C3%A7an_dialect" title="Ribagorçan dialect">
        Ribagorçan dialect
       </a>
       . Since the 15th century, Spanish has most influenced Aragonese; it was adopted throughout Aragon as the first language, limiting Aragonese to the northern region surrounding the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       has also influenced Aragonese; Italian loanwords have entered through other languages (such as Catalan), and Portuguese words have entered through Spanish. Germanic words came with the conquest of the region by Germanic peoples during the fifth century, and
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       has introduced a number of new words into the language.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       Words that were part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension#Second_declension_.28o_stems.29" title="Latin declension">
        Latin second declension
       </a>
       —as well as words that joined it later on—are usually masculine:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        FILIU(M) &gt;
        <i>
         fillo
        </i>
        (son)
       </li>
       <li>
        SCIURU + OLU(M) &gt;

        </i>

        <a href="/wiki/Squirrel" title="Squirrel">

      <p>
       Words that were part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension#First_declension_.28a_stems.29" title="Latin declension">
        Latin first declension
       </a>
       are usually feminine:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        FILIA(M) &gt;
        <i>
         filla
        </i>
        (daughter).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some Latin neuter plural nouns joined the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension#First_declension_.28a_stems.29" title="Latin declension">
        first declension
       </a>
       as singular feminine nouns:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        FOLIA &gt;
        <i>
         fuella
        </i>
        (leaf).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Words ending in
       <i>
        -or
       </i>
       are feminine:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         a honor
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a calor
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a color
        </i>
        , and (in Medieval Aragonese)
        <i>
         la amor
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>

       The names of fruit trees usually end in
       <i>
        -era
       </i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">
        Pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Unlike most other Ibero-Romance varieties, Aragonese has
       <a href="/wiki/Partitive" title="Partitive">
        partitive
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Locative_case" title="Locative case">
        locative
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        clitic
       </a>
       pronouns derived from the Latin
       <i>
        inde
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ibi
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        en
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        ne
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bi
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        ie
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Such pronouns are present in most major non-Iberian Romance languages (
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       <i>
        en
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        hi
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       <i>
        ne
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       , French
       <i>
        en
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       , and Italian
       <i>
        ne
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ci
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        vi
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        En/ne
       </i>
       is used for:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Partitive objects:
        <i>
         No n'he visto como aquello
        </i>
        ("I haven't seen anything like that", literally 'Not (of it) I have seen like that').
       </li>
       <li>
        Partitive subjects:
        <i>
         En fa tanto de mal
        </i>
        ("It hurts so much", literally '(of it) it causes so much of pain')
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ablative_case" title="Ablative case">
         Ablatives
        </a>
        , places from which movements originate:
        <i>
         Se'n va ra memoria
        </i>
        ("Memory goes away", literally '(away from [the mind]) memory goes')
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        Bi/i/ie
       </i>
       is used for:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Locatives, where something takes place:
        <i>
         N'ibi heba uno
        </i>
        ("There was one of them"), literally '(Of them) there was one')
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Allative_case" title="Allative case">
         Allatives
        </a>
        , places that movements go towards or end:
        <i>
         Vés-be
        </i>
        ('Go there (imperative)')
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        <span id="Medieval_Ages">
        </span>
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese-language_literature" title="Aragonese-language literature">
        Aragonese-language literature
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Aragonese was not written until the 12th and 13th centuries; the history
       <i>
        Liber Regum
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-google1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Razón feita d'amor
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-google1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Libre dels tres reys d'orient
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-google1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        Vida de Santa María Egipcíaca
       </i>
       date from this period,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google1_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-google1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there is also an Aragonese version of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_the_Morea#Aragonese_text" title="Chronicle of the Morea">
        Chronicle of Morea
       </a>
       , differing also in its content and written in the late 14th century called
       <i>
        Libro de los fechos et conquistas del principado de la Morea
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">
        <span id="Early_modern_period_literature">
        </span>
        Early modern period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Early modern period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Since 1500, Spanish was the cultural language of Aragon; many Aragonese wrote in Spanish, and during the 17th century the Argensola brothers went to Castile to teach Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Aragonese became a popular village language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the 17th century, popular literature in the language began to appear. In a 1650 Huesca literary contest, Aragonese poems were submitted by Matías Pradas, Isabel de Rodas and "Fileno, montañés".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_literature">
        Contemporary literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       The 19th and 20th centuries have seen a renaissance of Aragonese literature in several dialects. In 1844, Braulio Foz' novel
       <i>
        Vida de Pedro Saputo
       </i>
       was published in the Almudévar (southern) dialect. The 20th century featured Domingo Miral's
       <a href="/wiki/Costumbrismo" title="Costumbrismo">
        costumbrist
       </a>
       comedies and Veremundo Méndez Coarasa's poetry, both in Hecho (western) Aragonese; Cleto Torrodellas' poetry and Tonón de Baldomera's popular writings in the Graus (eastern) dialect and Arnal Cavero's costumbrist stories and Juana Coscujuela' novel
       <i>
        A Lueca, historia d'una moceta d'o Semontano
       </i>
       , also in the
       <a href="/wiki/Somontano_de_Barbastro" title="Somontano de Barbastro">
        southern
       </a>
       dialect.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aragonese_in_Modern_Education">
        Aragonese in Modern Education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Aragonese in Modern Education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The 1997 Aragonese Law of languages stipulated that Aragonese (and Catalan) speakers had a right to the teaching of and in their own language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Following this, Aragonese lessons started in schools in the academic year 1997/98.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was originally taught as an extra-curricular, non-evaluable voluntary subject in four schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, whilst legally schools can choose to use Aragonese as the language of instruction, as of the academic year 2013/14, there are no recorded instances of this option being taken in primary or secondary education.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact, the only current scenario in which Aragonese is used as the language of instruction is in the university course of Aragonese Philology, which is optional, taught over the summer and still only given in Aragonese in some of the lectures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pre-School_Education">
        Pre-School Education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Pre-School Education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In pre-school education, students, whose parents wish to be taught Aragonese, receive between thirty minutes to one hour of Aragonese lessons a week.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 2014/15 academic year there were 262 students recorded in pre-school Aragonese lessons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Primary_School_Education">
        Primary School Education
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aragonese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Primary School Education">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The subject of Aragonese now has a fully developed curriculum in primary education in Aragon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite this there are only seven Aragonese teachers in the region across both pre-primary and primary education and none hold permanent positions, whilst only 320 primary education students received Aragonese lessons in the academic year 2013/14.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_16-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
