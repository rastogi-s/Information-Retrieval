      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Eastern Romance languages
       </b>
       are a group of
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       that developed in
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       (specifically in the
       <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">
        Balkans
       </a>
       ) from the local variant of
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       . Today, the group consists of Romanian and three other related minor languages,
       <a href="/wiki/Aromanian_language" title="Aromanian language">
        Aromanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megleno-Romanian" title="Megleno-Romanian">
        Megleno-Romanian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Istro-Romanian_language" title="Istro-Romanian language">
        Istro-Romanian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some classifications include also the
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian languages
       </a>
       . When Italian is classified as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Romance" title="Western Romance">
        Western Romance
       </a>
       , Dalmatian generally remains in Eastern. However, this article is concerned only with Eastern Romance in the narrow sense, without Italian nor Dalmatian.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Eastern Romance languages, in their narrow conception, diverged from a common proto-language. They formed until recently a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       , spread across the Balkans and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Europe" title="Central Europe">
        Central Europe
       </a>
       . In the words of Latin origin, they share the same phonetic changes, with the exception of very few recent evolutions. So, their classification as separate languages is controversial and is rejected by a majority of Romanian linguists.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Eastern Romance languages are sometimes known as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vlachs#Language" title="Vlachs">
         Vlach
        </a>
        languages
       </i>
       , being so designated especially by the speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Western and Southern Slavic languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </h2>
      <p>
       An asymmetrical merger of Latin vowels, with /i/ merging with /ē/ but /u/ merging with /ū/, sets off Eastern Romance from the symmetrical merger of /u/ with /ō/ and /o/ found in Western Romance. However, while this persists today in only a few isolated dialects in western
       <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">
        Basilicata
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Castelmezzano_dialect" title="Castelmezzano dialect">
        Castelmezzano dialect
       </a>
       , as well as Dalmatian and the Romanian languages, there is evidence that it once occurred throughout southern Italy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Several hundred years after the Roman Empire's dominance of the region, the local form of
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       developed into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Romanian" title="Proto-Romanian">
        Proto-Romanian
       </a>
       , a language which had undergone most of the phonetic and grammatical changes characteristic to modern Romanian with respect to Latin. Subsequently Proto-Romanian diversified and developed into a dialect continuum. Due to foreign invasions
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2016)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       that displaced Romance speaking populations and submerged vast areas occupied by intermediate dialects (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romania_in_the_Dark_Ages" title="Romania in the Dark Ages">
        Romania in the Dark Ages
       </a>
       ), surviving Proto-Romanian eventually split into separate varieties:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        North-Danubian
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
           Romanian
          </a>
          , including the South-Danubian
          <a href="/wiki/Vlach_language_in_Serbia" title="Vlach language in Serbia">
           Vlach language in Serbia
          </a>
          and Bulgaria; Romanian is often called Daco-Romanian to distinguish it from the South-Danubian languages
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        South-Danubian languages
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Aromanian_language" title="Aromanian language">
           Aromanian
          </a>
          (
          <i>
           Vlachika
          </i>
          in
          <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
           Greece
          </a>
          ), called also Macedo-Romanian
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Megleno-Romanian_language" title="Megleno-Romanian language">
           Megleno-Romanian
          </a>
          (
          <i>
           Megleniotika
          </i>
          in Greece)
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Istro-Romanian_language" title="Istro-Romanian language">
           Istro-Romanian
          </a>
          (
          <i>
           Ćićiski
          </i>
          or
          <i>
           Ćiribirski
          </i>
          in former Yugoslavia)
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This classification is merely geographical. So, Emanuel Vasiliu classifies Istro-Romanian with Daco-Romanian, as a North-Danubian language. And most linguists estimate that Megleno-Romanian shows mixed features, being intermediate between the Daco-Romanian and Aromanian.
      </p>
      <p>
       As another consequence of the foreign invasions, the Proto-Romanian was influenced at various degrees by Slavic and other languages. The Slavic influence was more massive in the central part of the domain, from where originated the actual speakers of Megleno-Romanian, Istro-Romanian, Banat sub-dialect and, in a less measure, the Wallachian sub-dialect. Here and there, and especially in this central part, the Proto-Romanian became insular and eventually disappeared. Notorious examples of populations that once spoken Eastern Romance languages, entirely slavized today, are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The Vlachs from the Balkans, founders of the
        <a href="/wiki/Second_Bulgarian_Empire" title="Second Bulgarian Empire">
         Vlach-Bulgarian Empire
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        The North-Carpathian ethno-cultural groups of
        <a href="/wiki/Hutsuls" title="Hutsuls">
         Hutsuls
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Lemkos" title="Lemkos">
         Lemkos
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Boykos" title="Boykos">
         Boykos
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Gorals" title="Gorals">
         Gorals
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Moravian_Wallachia" title="Moravian Wallachia">
         Moravian Wallachians
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This process of assimilation continues until today, the South-Danubian languages and some varieties of the Daco-Romanian spoken outside Romania being highly endangered.
      </p>
      <p>
       The place where Proto-Romanian formed is still under debate; most historians put it to the north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Jire%C4%8Dek_Line" title="Jireček Line">
        Jireček Line
       </a>
       , separating the Latin speaking areas from the Greek speaking ones. (See:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Origin_of_Romanians" title="Origin of Romanians">
        Origin of Romanians
       </a>
       ). Proto-Romanian is not the only Romance idiom that would develop in the Balkan Peninsula, if the invasion of the Germans, Slavs and Hungarians wouldn't occur. According to Haralambie Mihăescu, Aquilea, Noricum and Pannonia Superior would have, as well as the Dalmatia, their own original Romance idiom, different from the Romanian. The latin loans in Greek and Albanian show phonetic changes foreign to those specific to Romanian, too. It remains to Proto-Romanian only the most eastern part of the Roman domain in the Balkan Peninsula. According to Alexandru Rosetti, the Proto-Romanian formed in Dacia, Moesia Superior and Inferior, Dardania and Pannonia Inferior. What is contested by some foreign historians
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , but is fervently supported with proofs by most of their Romanian colleagues, is the fact that the Proto-Romanian could have been formed in Dacia, after the retreat of the Roman army in the 3rd century.
      </p>

       </a>

      <p>
       The Proto-Romanian branch was one of the earliest language groups to be isolated from the larger Latin family. As such, the languages contain a few words that were replaced with Germanic borrowings in Western Romance languages, for example, the word for
       <i>
        white
       </i>
       is derived from Latin "albus" instead of Germanic "blank".
      </p>
      <p>
       They also share a few sound changes with the western Romance languages: some with Italian, such as [kl] &gt; [kj] (Lat.
       <b>
        cl
       </b>
       arus &gt; Rom.
       <b>
        chi
       </b>
       ar, Ital.
       <b>
        chi
       </b>
       aro) and also a few with
       <a href="/wiki/Dalmatian_language" title="Dalmatian language">
        Dalmatian
       </a>
       , such as [gn] &gt; [mn] (Lat. co
       <b>
        gn
       </b>
       atus &gt; Rom. cu
       <b>
        mn
       </b>
       at, Dalm. co
       <b>
        mn
       </b>
       ut). However, most of them are original, see:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_to_Romanian_sound_changes" title="Latin to Romanian sound changes">
        Latin to Romanian sound changes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The languages that are part of this group have some features that differentiate them from the other Romance languages, notable being the grammatical features shared within the
       <a href="/wiki/Balkan_sprachbund" title="Balkan sprachbund">
        Balkan language area
       </a>
       as well as some semantic peculiarities, such as
       <i>
        lume
       </i>
       ("world") being derived from Latin
       <i>
        lumen
       </i>
       ("light"),
       <i>
        inimă
       </i>
       ("heart") being derived from Latin
       <i>
        anima
       </i>
       ("soul"), etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       The languages also contain a
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Romance_substratum" title="Eastern Romance substratum">
        Paleo-Balkanic substrate
       </a>
       of a few hundreds of words, shared with
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
