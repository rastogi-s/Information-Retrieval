      <p>
       A
       <b>
        loanword
       </b>
       (also
       <b>
        loan word
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        loan-word
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
        word
       </a>
       adopted from one
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       (the donor language) and incorporated into another language without
       <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">
        translation
       </a>
       . This is in contrast to
       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
        cognates
       </a>
       , which are words in two or more languages that are similar because they share an
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymological
       </a>
       origin, and
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calques
       </a>
       , which involve translation.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples_and_related_terms">
        Examples and related terms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples and related terms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A loanword is distinguished from a
       <a href="/wiki/Calque" title="Calque">
        calque
       </a>
       (loan translation), which is a word or phrase whose
       <a href="/wiki/Meaning_(linguistics)" title="Meaning (linguistics)">
        meaning
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">
        idiom
       </a>
       is adopted from another language by
       <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">
        translation
       </a>
       into existing words or word-forming roots of the recipient language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of loanwords in the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       include
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Coffeehouse" title="Coffeehouse">
         café
        </a>
       </i>
       (from French
       <i>
        café
       </i>
       , which literally means "coffee"),
       <a href="/wiki/Bazaar" title="Bazaar">
        bazaar
       </a>
       (from Persian
       <i>
        bāzār
       </i>
       , which means "market"), and
       <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">
        kindergarten
       </a>
       (from German
       <i>
        Kindergarten
       </i>
       , which literally means "children's garden").
      </p>
      <p>
       In a bit of
       <a href="/wiki/Autological_word" title="Autological word">
        heterological
       </a>
       irony, the word
       <i>
        calque
       </i>
       is a loanword from the French
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       , derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       <i>
        calquer
       </i>
       (to trace, to copy);
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the word
       <i>
        loanword
       </i>
       is a calque of the
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        Lehnwort
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the phrase "loan translation" is a calque of the German
       <i>
        Lehnübersetzung
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Loans of multi-word phrases, such as the English use of the French term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9j%C3%A0_vu" title="Déjà vu">
         déjà vu
        </a>
       </i>
       , are known as adoptions, adaptations, or lexical borrowings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Strictly speaking, the term
       <i>
        loanword
       </i>
       conflicts with the ordinary meaning of
       <i>
        loan
       </i>
       in that something is taken from the donor language without it being something that is possible to return.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="From_the_arts">
        From the arts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: From the arts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most of the technical vocabulary of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_classical_music" title="European classical music">
        classical music
       </a>
       (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Concerto" title="Concerto">
        concerto
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allegro_(music)" title="Allegro (music)">
        allegro
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">
        tempo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aria" title="Aria">
        aria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Opera" title="Opera">
        opera
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Soprano" title="Soprano">
        soprano
       </a>
       ) is borrowed from
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that of
       <a href="/wiki/Ballet" title="Ballet">
        ballet
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_classification">
        Linguistic classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Linguistic classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The studies by Werner Betz (1949, 1939),
       <a href="/wiki/Einar_Haugen" title="Einar Haugen">
        Einar Haugen
       </a>
       (1950, also 1956), and
       <a href="/wiki/Uriel_Weinreich" title="Uriel Weinreich">
        Uriel Weinreich
       </a>
       (1953) are regarded as the classical theoretical works on loan influence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The basic theoretical statements all take Betz’s nomenclature as their starting point. Duckworth (1977) enlarges Betz’s scheme by the type “partial substitution” and supplements the system with English terms. A schematic illustration of these classifications is given below.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The expression "foreign word" used in the illustration below is, however, an incorrect translation of the German term
       <i>
        Fremdwort
       </i>
       , which refers to loanwords whose pronunciation, spelling, and possible inflection or gender have not yet been so much adapted to the new language that they cease to feel foreign. Such a separation of loanwords into two distinct categories is not used by linguists in English in talking about any language. In addition, basing such a separation mainly on spelling as described in the illustration is (or, in fact, was) not usually done except by German linguists and only when talking about German and sometimes other languages that tend to adapt foreign spellings, which is rare in English unless the word has been in wide use for a very long time.
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the linguist Suzanne Kemmer, the expression "foreign word" can be defined as follows in English: "[W]hen most speakers do not know the word and if they hear it think it is from another language, the word can be called a foreign word. There are many foreign words and phrases used in English such as bon vivant (French), mutatis mutandis (Latin), and Schadenfreude (German)."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is however not how the term is (incorrectly) used in this illustration:
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="floatnone">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Loanword_classification_tree_3.gif">
         <img alt="Loanword classification tree 3.gif" data-file-height="672" data-file-width="1008" height="672" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Loanword_classification_tree_3.gif" width="1008"/>
        </a>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       On the basis of an importation-substitution distinction, Haugen (1950: 214f.) distinguishes three basic groups of borrowings: “(1)
       <i>
        Loanwords
       </i>
       show morphemic importation without substitution.... (2)
       <i>
        Loanblends
       </i>
       show morphemic substitution as well as importation.... (3)
       <i>
        Loanshifts
       </i>
       show morphemic substitution without importation”. Haugen later refined (1956) his model in a review of Gneuss’s (1955) book on Old English loan coinages, whose classification, in turn, is the one by Betz (1949) again.
      </p>
      <p>
       Weinreich (1953: 47ff.) differentiates between two mechanisms of lexical interference, namely those initiated by simple words and those initiated by compound words and phrases. Weinreich (1953: 47) defines
       <i>
        simple words
       </i>
       “from the point of view of the bilinguals who perform the transfer, rather than that of the descriptive linguist. Accordingly, the category ‘simple’ words also includes compounds that are transferred in unanalysed form”. After this general classification, Weinreich then resorts to Betz’s (1949) terminology.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_and_learned_loanwords">
        Popular and learned loanwords
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Popular and learned loanwords">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is a distinction between "popular" and "learned" loanwords. Popular loanwords are transmitted orally. Learned loanwords are first used in written language, often for scholarly, scientific, or literary purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <p>
       Some English loanwords remain relatively faithful to the donor language's phonology even though a particular
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       might not exist or have contrastive status in English. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lava" title="Lava">
         <span class="unicode">
          ʻ
         </span>
         a
         <span class="unicode">
          ʻ
         </span>
         ā
        </a>
       </i>
       is used by geologists to specify lava that is relatively thick, chunky, and rough. The Hawaiian spelling indicates the two
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
        glottal stops
       </a>
       in the word, but the English pronunciation,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
          </span>
          (
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ʔ/: the catch in 'uh-oh'">
            ʔ
           </span>
          </span>
          )
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ɑː/: 'a' in 'father'">
            ɑː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       , contains at most one. In addition, the English spelling usually removes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CA%BBokina" title="ʻokina">
        ʻokina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Macron_(diacritic)" title="Macron (diacritic)">
        macron
       </a>
       diacritics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The majority of English affixes, such as
       <i>
        un-
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ing
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -ly
       </i>
       , were present in older forms in Old English. However, a few English affixes are borrowed. For example, the English verbal suffix
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/-ize" title="-ize">
        <i>
         -ize
        </i>
        (American English) or
        <i>
         ise
        </i>
        (British English)
       </a>
       comes from Greek -ιζειν (
       <i>
        -izein
       </i>
       ) via Latin
       <i>
        -izare
       </i>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transmission_in_the_Ottoman_Empire">
        Transmission in the Ottoman Empire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Transmission in the Ottoman Empire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       During more than 600 years of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       , the literary and administrative language of the empire was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Turkish_(language)" title="Turkish (language)">
        Turkish
       </a>
       , with many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persian_(language)" title="Persian (language)">
        Persian
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_(language)" title="Arabic (language)">
        Arabic
       </a>
       loanwords, called
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Turkish_language" title="Ottoman Turkish language">
        Ottoman Turkish
       </a>
       , considerably differing from the everyday spoken Turkish of the time. Many such words were exported to other languages of the empire, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_language" title="Serbian language">
        Serbian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
        Ladino
       </a>
       . After the empire fell after
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        World War I
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Republic_of_Turkey" title="Republic of Turkey">
        Republic of Turkey
       </a>
       was founded, the Turkish language underwent an extensive
       <a href="/wiki/Language_reform" title="Language reform">
        language reform
       </a>
       led by the newly founded
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_Language_Association" title="Turkish Language Association">
        Turkish Language Association
       </a>
       , during which
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_replaced_loanwords_in_Turkish" title="List of replaced loanwords in Turkish">
        many adopted words
       </a>
       were replaced with new formations derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic
       </a>
       roots. That was part of the ongoing cultural reform of the time, in turn a part in the broader framework of
       <a href="/wiki/Atat%C3%BCrk%27s_Reforms" title="Atatürk's Reforms">
        Atatürk's Reforms
       </a>
       , which also included the introduction of the new
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_alphabet" title="Turkish alphabet">
        Turkish alphabet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Turkish also has taken many words from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_(language)" title="French (language)">
        French
       </a>
       , such as
       <i>
        pantolon
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        trousers
       </i>
       (from French
       <i>
        pantalon
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        komik
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        funny
       </i>
       (from French
       <i>
        comique
       </i>
       ), most of them pronounced very similarly. Word usage in modern Turkey has acquired a political tinge:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Right-wing" title="Right-wing">
        right-wing
       </a>
       publications tend to use more Arabic or Persian originated words,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Left-wing" title="Left-wing">
        left-wing
       </a>
       ones use more adopted from European languages, while centrist ones use more native Turkish root words.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dutch_words_in_Indonesian">
        Dutch words in Indonesian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Dutch words in Indonesian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Almost 350 years of Dutch presence in what is now
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       have left significant linguistic traces. Though very few Indonesians have a fluent knowledge of Dutch, the Indonesian language inherited many words from Dutch, both in words for everyday life and as well in scientific or technological terminology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One scholar argues that 20% of
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">
        Indonesian
       </a>
       words can be traced back to Dutch words.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_aspects">
        Cultural aspects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cultural aspects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Hans_Henrich_Hock" title="Hans Henrich Hock">
        Hans Henrich Hock
       </a>
       and Brian Joseph, "languages and dialects ... do not exist in a vacuum": there is always linguistic contact between groups.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The contact influences what loanwords are integrated into the lexicon and which certain words are chosen over others.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Changes_in_meaning_and_pronunciation">
        Changes in meaning and pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Loanword&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Changes in meaning and pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some cases, the original meaning shifts considerably through unexpected logical leaps. The English word
       <i>
        Viking
       </i>
       became Japanese バイキング
       <i>
        baikingu
       </i>
       meaning 'buffet', because Imperial Viking was the first restaurant in Japan to offer
       <a href="/wiki/Buffet" title="Buffet">
        buffet
       </a>
       -style meals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
