      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Italic languages
       </b>
       are a subfamily of the
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European
       </a>
       language family, originally spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/Italic_peoples" title="Italic peoples">
        Italic peoples
       </a>
       . They include
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       and its descendants (the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       ) as well as a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">
        extinct languages
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">
        Italian Peninsula
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Umbrian_language" title="Umbrian language">
        Umbrian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Oscan_language" title="Oscan language">
        Oscan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Faliscan_language" title="Faliscan language">
        Faliscan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Picene_language" title="South Picene language">
        South Picene
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       With over 800 million native speakers, the Italic languages are the second most widely spoken branch of the Indo-European family, after the
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Iranian_languages" title="Indo-Iranian languages">
        Indo-Iranian languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the past, various definitions of "Italic" have prevailed. This article uses the classification presented by the
       <i>
        Linguist List
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Italic includes the Latin subgroup (Latin and the Romance languages) as well as the ancient Italic languages (Faliscan, Osco-Umbrian and two unclassified Italic languages,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aequian" title="Aequian">
        Aequian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vestinian_language" title="Vestinian language">
        Vestinian
       </a>
       ).
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetic" title="Venetic">
        Venetic
       </a>
       (the language of the ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Adriatic_Veneti" title="Adriatic Veneti">
        Veneti
       </a>
       ), as revealed by its inscriptions, shared some similarities with the Italic languages and is sometimes classified as Italic. However, since it also shares similarities with other Western Indo-European branches (particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       ), some linguists prefer to consider it as an independent Indo-European language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the extreme view, Italic did not exist, but the different groups descended directly from Indo-European and converged because of geographic contiguity. That view stems in part from the difficulty in identifying a common Italic homeland in
       <span class="nowrap">
        prehistory.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the intermediate view, the Italic languages are one of the ten or eleven major subgroups of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-European_language_family" title="Indo-European language family">
        Indo-European language family
       </a>
       and might therefore have had an ancestor, Common Italic or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">
        Proto-Italic
       </a>
       from which its
       <a href="/wiki/Daughter_language" title="Daughter language">
        daughter languages
       </a>
       descended. Moreover, there are similarities between major groups, but how the similarities are to be interpreted is one of the major debated issues in the historical linguistics of Indo-European. The linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Calvert_Watkins" title="Calvert Watkins">
        Calvert Watkins
       </a>
       went so far as to suggest, among the ten major groups, a four-way division of East, West, North and South Indo-European. He considered them to be "dialectical divisions within Proto-Indo-European which go back to a period long before the speakers arrived in their historical areas of attestation".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not to be considered a nodular grouping; in other words, there was not necessarily any common west Indo-European serving as a node from which the subgroups branched but a hypothesised similarity between the dialects of Proto-Indo-European that developed into the recognised families.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main debate concerning the origin of the Italic languages is the same as one that preoccupied Greek studies for the last half of the 20th century. The Indo-Europeanists for Greek had hypothesized (see
       <a href="/wiki/Dorian_invasion" title="Dorian invasion">
        Dorian invasion
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Greek" title="Proto-Greek">
        Proto-Greek
       </a>
       ) that Greek originated outside Greece and was brought in by invaders. Analysis of the lexical items of
       <a href="/wiki/Mycenaean_Greek" title="Mycenaean Greek">
        Mycenaean Greek
       </a>
       , an early form of Greek, raised the issue of whether Greek was formed within Greece from Indo-European elements brought in by migrants or by invaders, mixed with elements of indigenous languages. The issue was settled in favour of Greek being a language that developed from all of these elements but then also took its recognisable form all within Greece.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A Proto-Italic homeland outside Italy is just as elusive as the home of the hypothetical Greek-speaking invaders. No early form of Italic is available to match Mycenaean Greek. The Italic languages are first attested in writing from Umbrian and Faliscan inscriptions from the 7th century BC. The alphabets used are based on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Italic_alphabet" title="Old Italic alphabet">
        Old Italic alphabet
       </a>
       , which is itself based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       . The Italic alphabets themselves show minor influence from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etruscan_alphabet" title="Etruscan alphabet">
        Etruscan alphabet
       </a>
       and somewhat more from the Ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       . There is no guarantee that the intermediate phases between Italic and Indo-European will be found. The question of whether Italic originated outside Italy or developed by assimilation of Indo-European and other elements within Italy, approximately on or within its current range there, remains. Silvestri says:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sylvestri325_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sylvestri325-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ...Common Italic... is certainly not to be seen as a prehistoric language that can largely be reconstructed, but rather as a set of prehistoric and proto-historic processes of convergence.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Bakkum defines Proto-Italic as a "chronological stage" without an independent development of its own, but extending over late Proto-Indo-European and the initial stages of Proto-Latin and Proto-Sabellic. Meiser's dates of 4000 BC to 1800 BC, well before Mycenaean Greek, are described by him as "as good a guess as anyone's".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gray and Atkinson come up by using their Bayesian phylogenetic model that the Italic branch separated from the Germanic branch 5500 years ago, roughly the start of the Bronze Age.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gray-Atkinson2003_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gray-Atkinson2003-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">
        Branches
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Branches">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="990" data-file-width="2232" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png/400px-Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png/600px-Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png/800px-Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_Landscape_of_Central_Italy.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Languages of Central Italy at the beginning of Roman expansion
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Italic family has two known branches:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Latino-Faliscan_languages" title="Latino-Faliscan languages">
          Latino-Faliscan
         </a>
        </b>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Faliscan_language" title="Faliscan language">
           Faliscan
          </a>
          , which was spoken in the area around
          <a href="/wiki/Falerii" title="Falerii">
           Falerii
          </a>
          Veteres (modern
          <a href="/wiki/Civita_Castellana" title="Civita Castellana">
           Civita Castellana
          </a>
          ) north of the city of
          <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
           Rome
          </a>
          and possibly
          <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
           Sardinia
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
           Latin
          </a>
          , which was spoken in west-central Italy. The
          <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
           Roman
          </a>
          conquests eventually spread it throughout the peninsula and beyond, in the
          <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
           Roman Empire
          </a>
          .
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
             Romance languages
            </a>
            , the descendants of Latin (evolved dialects of Latin)
           </li>
          </ul>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Osco-Umbrian_languages" title="Osco-Umbrian languages">
          Osco-Umbrian
         </a>
        </b>
        or
        <b>
         Sabellian
        </b>
        :
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Oscan_language" title="Oscan language">
           Oscan
          </a>
          , which was spoken in the south-central region of the
          <a href="/wiki/Italian_Peninsula" title="Italian Peninsula">
           Italian Peninsula
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Umbrian_language" title="Umbrian language">
           Umbrian
          </a>
          (not to be confused with the modern Umbrian dialect of Italian), which was spoken in the north-central region
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Volscian_language" title="Volscian language">
           Volscian
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsian_language" title="Marsian language">
           Marsian
          </a>
          , the language of the
          <a href="/wiki/Marsi" title="Marsi">
           Marsi
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/South_Picene_language" title="South Picene language">
           South Picene
          </a>
          , in east-central
          <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
           Italy
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sabine_language" title="Sabine language">
           Sabine
          </a>
          in
          <a href="/wiki/Lazio" title="Lazio">
           Lazio
          </a>
          and the central
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apennines" title="Apennines">
           Apennines
          </a>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some other languages belong to the Italic branch, but too little is known for further classification:
       <a href="/wiki/Aequian_language" title="Aequian language">
        Aequian
       </a>
       , spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Aequi" title="Aequi">
        Aequi
       </a>
       just east of Rome, and
       <a href="/wiki/Vestinian_language" title="Vestinian language">
        Vestinian
       </a>
       , spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Vestini" title="Vestini">
        Vestini
       </a>
       in northeast Italy. It is unknown whether the language spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicels" title="Sicels">
        Sicels
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       was Italic or not.
      </p>
      <p>
       As the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Republic" title="Roman Republic">
        Roman Republic
       </a>
       extended its political dominion over the whole of the Italian peninsula, Latin became dominant over the other Italic languages, which ceased to be spoken perhaps sometime in the 1st century AD. From
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       , the Romance languages emerged.
      </p>
      <p>
       It has also been proposed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Lusitanian_language" title="Lusitanian language">
        Lusitanian language
       </a>
       belonged to the Italic family.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Italic_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:307px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Centum_Satem_map.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="267" data-file-width="500" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Centum_Satem_map.png/305px-Centum_Satem_map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Centum_Satem_map.png/458px-Centum_Satem_map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Centum_Satem_map.png 2x" width="305"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Centum_Satem_map.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map showing the approximate extent of the
         <i>
          centum
         </i>
         (blue) and
         <i>
          satem
         </i>
         (red)
         <a href="/wiki/Areal_feature" title="Areal feature">
          areals
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the point of view of Proto-Indo-European, the Italic languages are fairly conservative. In phonology, the Italic languages are
       <a href="/wiki/Centum_and_satem_languages" title="Centum and satem languages">
        centum languages
       </a>
       by merging the palatals with the velars (Latin
       <i>
        centum
       </i>
       has a /k/) but keeping the combined group separate from the labio-velars. In morphology, the Italic languages preserve six cases in the noun and the adjective (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative, ablative, vocative) with traces of a seventh (locative), but the dual of both the noun and the verb has completely disappeared. From the position of both morphological innovations and uniquely shared lexical items, Italic shows the greatest similarities with Celtic and Germanic, with some of the shared lexical correspondences also being found in Baltic and Slavic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
