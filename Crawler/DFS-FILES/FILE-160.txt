      <p>
       <b>
        Decimal degrees
       </b>
       (DD) express
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">
        geographic coordinates
       </a>
       as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decimal_fractions" title="Decimal fractions">
        decimal fractions
       </a>
       and are used in many
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        geographic information systems
       </a>
       (GIS),
       <a href="/wiki/Web_mapping" title="Web mapping">
        web mapping
       </a>
       applications such as
       <a href="/wiki/OpenStreetMap" title="OpenStreetMap">
        OpenStreetMap
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        GPS
       </a>
       devices. Decimal degrees are an alternative to using degrees, minutes, and seconds (DMS). As with latitude and longitude, the values are bounded by ±90° and ±180° respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       Positive latitudes are north of the equator, negative latitudes are south of the equator. Positive longitudes are east of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       , negative longitudes are west of the Prime Meridian. Latitude and longitude are usually expressed in that sequence, latitude before longitude.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Precision">
        Precision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Decimal_degrees&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Precision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The radius of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       is 6,378,137.0 meters
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       resulting in a circumference of 40,075,161.2 meters. The equator is divided into 360 degrees of longitude, so each degree at the equator represents 111,319.9 meters or approximately 111.32 km. As one moves away from the equator towards a pole, however, one degree of longitude is multiplied by the cosine of the latitude, decreasing the distance, approaching zero at the pole. The number of decimal places required for a particular precision at the equator is:

      <p>
       A value in decimal degrees to a precision of 4 decimal places is precise to 11.132 meters at the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . A value in decimal degrees to 5 decimal places is precise to 1.1132 meter at the equator. Elevation also introduces a small error. At 6,378 m elevation, the radius and surface distance is increased by 0.001 or 0.1%. Because the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        earth
       </a>
       is not flat, the precision of the longitude part of the coordinates increases the further from the equator you get. The precision of the latitude part does not increase so much, more strictly however, a
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">
        meridian arc
       </a>
       length per 1 second depends on latitude at point concerned. The discrepancy of 1 second meridian arc length between equator and pole is about 0.3 metres because the earth is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate spheroid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
