      <p>
       <b>
        Geographical distance
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Distance" title="Distance">
        distance
       </a>
       measured along the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        earth
       </a>
       . The formulae in this article calculate distances between points which are defined by geographical coordinates in terms of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . This distance is an element in solving the
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy#Geodetic_problems" title="Geodesy">
        second (inverse) geodetic problem
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">
        Introduction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geographical_distance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Introduction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Calculating the distance between geographical coordinates is based on some level of abstraction; it does not provide an
       <i>
        exact
       </i>
       distance, which is unattainable if one attempted to account for every irregularity in the surface of the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Common abstractions for the surface between two geographic points are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Flat surface;
       </li>
       <li>
        Spherical surface;
       </li>
       <li>
        Ellipsoidal surface.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       All abstractions above ignore changes in elevation. Calculation of distances which account for changes in elevation relative to the idealized surface are not discussed in this article.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flat-surface_formulae">
        Flat-surface formulae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geographical_distance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Flat-surface formulae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A planar approximation for the surface of the earth may be useful over small distances. The accuracy of distance calculations using this approximation become increasingly inaccurate as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The separation between the points becomes greater;
       </li>
       <li>
        A point becomes closer to a geographic pole.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The shortest distance between two points in plane is a straight line. The
       <a href="/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem" title="Pythagorean theorem">
        Pythagorean theorem
       </a>
       is used to calculate the distance between points in a plane.
      </p>
      <p>
       Even over short distances, the accuracy of geographic distance calculations which assume a flat Earth depend on the method by which the latitude and longitude coordinates have been
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        projected
       </a>
       onto the plane. The projection of latitude and longitude coordinates onto a plane is the realm of
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The formulae presented in this section provide varying degrees of accuracy.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spherical-surface_formulae">
        Spherical-surface formulae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geographical_distance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Spherical-surface formulae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_distance" title="Great-circle distance">
        Great-circle distance
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       If we are willing to accept a possible error of 0.5%, we can use formulas of
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry" title="Spherical trigonometry">
        spherical trigonometry
       </a>
       on the sphere that best approximates the surface of the earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The shortest distance along the surface of a sphere between two points on the surface is along the great-circle which contains the two points.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_distance" title="Great-circle distance">
        great-circle distance
       </a>
       article gives the formula for calculating the distance along a great-circle on a sphere about the size of the Earth. That article includes an example of the calculation.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsoidal-surface_formulae">
        Ellipsoidal-surface formulae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geographical_distance&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Ellipsoidal-surface formulae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesics_on_an_ellipsoid" title="Geodesics on an ellipsoid">
        Geodesics on an ellipsoid
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="504" data-file-width="512" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg/220px-Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg/330px-Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg/440px-Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Long_geodesic_on_an_oblate_ellipsoid.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Geodesic on an oblate ellipsoid
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An ellipsoid approximates the surface of the earth much better than a sphere or a flat surface does. The shortest distance along the surface of an ellipsoid between two points on the surface is along the
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesic" title="Geodesic">
        geodesic
       </a>
       . Geodesics follow more complicated paths than great circles and in particular, they usually don't return to their starting positions after one circuit of the earth. This is illustrated in the figure on the right where
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       is taken to be 1/50 to accentuate the effect. Finding the geodesic between two points on the earth, the so-called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inverse_geodetic_problem" title="Inverse geodetic problem">
        inverse geodetic problem
       </a>
       , was the focus of many mathematicians and geodesists over the course of the 18th and 19th centuries with major contributions by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexis_Claude_Clairaut" title="Alexis Claude Clairaut">
        Clairaut
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Adrien-Marie_Legendre" title="Adrien-Marie Legendre">
        Legendre
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel" title="Friedrich Bessel">
        Bessel
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Robert_Helmert" title="Friedrich Robert Helmert">
        Helmert
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rapp
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       provides a good summary of this work.
      </p>
      <p>
       Methods for computing the geodesic distance are widely available in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographical_information_systems" title="Geographical information systems">
        geographical information systems
       </a>
       , software libraries, standalone utilities, and online tools. The most widely used algorithm is by
       <a href="/wiki/Thaddeus_Vincenty" title="Thaddeus Vincenty">
        Vincenty
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who uses a series which is accurate to third order in the flattening of the ellipsoid, i.e., about 0.5 mm; however, the algorithm fails to converge for points that are nearly
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodes" title="Antipodes">
        antipodal
       </a>
       . (For details, see
       <a href="/wiki/Vincenty%27s_formulae" title="Vincenty's formulae">
        Vincenty's formulae
       </a>
       .) This defect is cured in the algorithm given by Karney,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who employs series which are accurate to sixth order in the flattening. This results in an algorithm which is accurate to full double precision and which converges for arbitrary pairs of points on the earth. This algorithm is implemented in GeographicLib.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The exact methods above are feasible when carrying out calculations on a computer. They are intended to give millimeter accuracy on lines of any length; we can use simpler formulas if we don't need millimeter accuracy, or if we do need millimeter accuracy but the line is short. Rapp,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rapp91_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rapp91-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chap. 6, describes the
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_Puissant" title="Louis Puissant">
        Puissant
       </a>
       method, the Gauss mid-latitude method, and the Bowring method.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bowring81_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-bowring81-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
