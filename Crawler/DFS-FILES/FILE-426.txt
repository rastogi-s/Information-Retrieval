      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        betacism
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
          UK
         </a>
         :
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
           <span title="/əm/: 'm' in 'rhythm'">
            əm
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
          US
         </a>
         :
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          -/
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       in which
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_plosive" title="Voiced bilabial plosive">
        voiced bilabial plosive
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        bane
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">
        voiced labiodental fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        vane
       </i>
       ) are confused. The final result of the process can be both /b/ &gt; [v] or /v/ &gt; [b]. Betacism is a fairly common phenomenon; it has taken place in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       and some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ibero-Roman" title="Ibero-Roman">
        Iberian Romances
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Classical Greek
       </a>
       , the letter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beta_(letter)" title="Beta (letter)">
        beta
       </a>
       &lt;β&gt; denoted
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       . As a result of betacism, it has come to denote
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       , a process which probably began during the
       <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek_phonology#Consonants" title="Koine Greek phonology">
        Koine Greek
       </a>
       period, approximately in the 1st century AD, along with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirantization" title="Spirantization">
        spirantization
       </a>
       of the other
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        δ and γ
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Modern (and earlier Medieval) Greek uses the digraph &lt;μπ&gt; to represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Indeed, this is the origin of the word
       <i>
        betacism
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Betacism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Perhaps the best known example of betacism is in the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       . The first traces of betacism in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       can be found in the third century C.E.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The results of the shift are most widespread in the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Romance_languages" title="Western Romance languages">
        Western Romance languages
       </a>
       , especially in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , where the letters &lt;b&gt; and &lt;v&gt; are now both pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
        voiced bilabial fricative
       </a>
       ) except phrase-initially and after
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [m]
       </span>
       when they are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       ; the two sounds (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       ) are now
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophones
       </a>
       . Betacism is one of the main features in which
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       and northern
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       diverge from southern Portuguese; in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       betacism features in many dialects, though not in central and southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencian_Language" title="Valencian Language">
        Valencian
       </a>
       or in the
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_dialect" title="Balearic dialect">
        Balearic dialect
       </a>
       . Other Iberian languages with betacism are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astur-Leonese" title="Astur-Leonese">
        Astur-Leonese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       (in fact, fort the latter there is a pronounciantion-based orthography changing all &lt;v&gt; into &lt;b&gt;).
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example of betacism is in
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan
       </a>
       , or in Maceratese (dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Macerata" title="Macerata">
        Macerata
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ) which uses &lt;v&gt; to denote betacism-produced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       , such that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        bucca
       </i>
       corresponds to Neapolitan
       <i>
        vocca
       </i>
       and to Maceratese "vocca", Latin
       <i>
        arborem
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        arvero
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        arvulo
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        barba
       </i>
       to Neapolitan
       <i>
        varva
       </i>
       and Maceratese "varba".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Betacism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hebrew">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Betacism occurred in
       <a href="/wiki/Biblical_Hebrew" title="Biblical Hebrew">
        Ancient Hebrew
       </a>
       ; the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       (denoted &lt;ב&gt;) changed to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       and eventually to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       except when
       <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">
        geminated
       </a>
       or when following a consonant or pause. As a result, the two sounds became
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophones
       </a>
       ; but, due to later sound changes, including the loss of gemination, the distinction became
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemic
       </a>
       again in
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">
        Modern Hebrew
       </a>
       .
      </p>
