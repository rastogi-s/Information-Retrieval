      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        consonant cluster
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        consonant sequence
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        consonant compound
       </b>
       is a group of
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonants
       </a>
       which have no intervening
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowel
       </a>
       . In English, for example, the groups
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /spl/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/
       </span>
       are consonant clusters in the word
       <i>
        splits
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some linguists
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (April 2015)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       argue that the term can only be properly applied to those consonant clusters that occur within one
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllable
       </a>
       . Others contend that the concept is more useful when it includes consonant sequences across syllable boundaries. According to the former definition, the longest consonant clusters in the word
       <i>
        extra
       </i>
       would be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tr/
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas the latter allows
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kstr/
       </span>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonotactics">
        Phonotactics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consonant_cluster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Phonotactics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Languages'
       <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">
        phonotactics
       </a>
       differ as to what consonant clusters they permit.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many languages are more restrictive than English in terms of consonant clusters. Many languages forbid consonant clusters entirely.
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        Hawaiian
       </a>
       , like most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian" title="Malayo-Polynesian">
        Malayo-Polynesian
       </a>
       languages, is of this sort.
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       is almost as strict, but allows a sequence of a
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximant
       </a>
       , plus another consonant, as in
       <i>
        Honshū
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [hoɴɕuː]
       </span>
       (the name of the largest island of Japan), and
       <i>
        Tokyo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [toːkjoː]
       </span>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Arabic" title="Standard Arabic">
        Standard Arabic
       </a>
       forbids initial consonant clusters and more than two consecutive consonants in other positions. So do most other
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       , although Modern Israeli Hebrew permits initial two-consonant clusters (e.g.
       <i>
        pkak
       </i>
       "cap";
       <i>
        dlaat
       </i>
       "pumpkin"), and
       <a href="/wiki/Moroccan_Arabic" title="Moroccan Arabic">
        Moroccan Arabic
       </a>
       , under
       <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">
        Berber
       </a>
       influence, allows strings of several consonants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Like most Mon–Khmer languages,
       <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">
        Khmer
       </a>
       permits only initial consonant clusters with up to three consonants in a row per syllable.
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       has initial consonant clusters natively only on South-Western dialects and on foreign loans, and only clusters of three inside the word are allowed. Most spoken languages and dialects, however, are more permissive. In
       <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">
        Burmese
       </a>
       , consonant clusters of only up to three consonants (the initial and two medials—two written forms of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-j-/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-w-/
       </span>
       ) at the initial onset are allowed in writing and only two (the initial and one medial) are pronounced. These clusters are restricted to certain letters. Some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burmese_dialects" title="Burmese dialects">
        Burmese dialects
       </a>
       allow for clusters of up to four consonants (with the addition of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /-l-/
       </span>
       medial, which can combine with the above-mentioned medials.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the other end of the scale, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Caucasian_languages" title="South Caucasian languages">
        Kartvelian
       </a>
       languages of Georgia are drastically more permissive of consonant clustering. Clusters in
       <a href="/wiki/Georgian_language" title="Georgian language">
        Georgian
       </a>
       of four, five or six consonants are not unusual—for instance,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /brtʼqʼɛli/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        flat
       </i>
       ),
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /mt͡sʼvrtnɛli/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        trainer
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /prt͡skvna/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        peeling
       </i>
       )—and if grammatical affixes are used, it allows an eight-consonant cluster:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡvbrdɣvnis/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        he's plucking us
       </i>
       ). Consonants cannot appear as syllable nuclei in Georgian, so this syllable is analysed as CCCCCCCCVC. The neighboring, but unrelated,
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian language
       </a>
       also allows for long consonant strings. For example, the classic transliterations
       <i>
        k̕rt̕mndzhal
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        khghchmtank̕
       </i>
       of քրթմնջալ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kʰɾtʰmnd͡ʒɑl/
       </span>
       ("to grumble") and խղճմտանք
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /χʁt͡ʃmtɑnkʰ/
       </span>
       ("conscience") start with eight consonants, though in the
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_alphabet" title="Armenian alphabet">
        Armenian alphabet
       </a>
       and the modern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISO_9985" title="ISO 9985">
        ISO 9985
       </a>
       transliteration words starting with more than six consonants are rare. Many
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       may manifest almost as formidable numbers of consecutive consonants, such as in the
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       words
       <i>
        štvrť
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃtvr̩tʲ/
       </span>
       ("quarter"), and
       <i>
        žblnknutie
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒbl̩ŋknutje/
       </span>
       ("clunk"; "flop") and the
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        skrbstvo
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /skrbstʋo/
       </span>
       ("welfare"). However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">
        liquid consonants
       </a>
       /r/ and /l/ can form syllable nuclei in West and South Slavic languages and behave phonologically as vowels in this case. An example of a true initial cluster is the
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        wszczniesz
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /fʂt͡ʂɲɛʂ/
       </span>
       ("you will initiate"). In the
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        opskrbljivanje
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɔpskr̩bʎiʋaɲɛ/
       </span>
       ("victualling") the ⟨lj⟩ and ⟨nj⟩ are
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraphs
       </a>
       representing single consonants:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʎ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɲ]
       </span>
       , respectively. Some
       <a href="/wiki/Salishan_languages" title="Salishan languages">
        Salishan languages
       </a>
       exhibit long words with no vowels at all, such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nux%C3%A1lk_language" title="Nuxálk language">
        Nuxálk
       </a>
       word
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /xɬpʼχʷɬtʰɬpʰɬːskʷʰt͡sʼ/
       </span>
       :
       <i>
        he had had in his possession a bunchberry plant
       </i>
       . It is extremely difficult to accurately classify which of these consonants may be acting as the syllable nucleus, and these languages challenge classical notions of exactly what constitutes a
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllable
       </a>
       . The same problem is encountered in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Berber_languages" title="Northern Berber languages">
        Northern Berber languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There has been a trend to reduce and simplify consonant clusters in
       <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">
        East Asian languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_language" title="Vietnamese language">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Chinese" title="Old Chinese">
        Old Chinese
       </a>
       was known to contain additional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_medial" title="Syllable medial">
        medials
       </a>
       such as /r/ and/or /l/, which yielded retroflexion in
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Chinese" title="Middle Chinese">
        Middle Chinese
       </a>
       and today's
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       . The word 江, read as
       <i>
        jiang
       </i>
       in Mandarin and
       <i>
        kong
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">
        Cantonese
       </a>
       , was most likely
       <i>
        klong
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        krung
       </i>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . Additionally, initial clusters such as "tk" and "sn" were analysed in recent reconstructions of Old Chinese, and some were developed as palatalised sibilants. Another element of consonant clusters in Old Chinese were analysed in coda and post-coda position. Some "departing tone" syllables have cognates in the "entering tone" syllables, which feature a -p, -t, -k in Middle Chinese and Southern Chinese varieties. The departing tone was analysed to feature a post-coda sibilant, "s". Clusters of -ps, -ts, -ks, were then formed at the end of syllables. These clusters eventually collapsed into "-ts" or "-s", before disappearing altogether, leaving elements of diphthongisation in more modern varieties. Old Vietnamese also had a rich inventory of initial clusters, but these were slowly merged with plain initials during Middle Vietnamese, and some have developed into the palatal nasal.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">
        Loanwords
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consonant_cluster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Loanwords">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Consonant clusters occurring in
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       do not necessarily follow the cluster limits set by the borrowing language's
       <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">
        phonotactics
       </a>
       . These limits are called restraints or constraints (see also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optimality_theory" title="Optimality theory">
        optimality theory
       </a>
       ). A loanword from
       <a href="/wiki/Adyghe_language" title="Adyghe language">
        Adyghe
       </a>
       in the extinct
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh language
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        psta
       </i>
       ('to well up'), violates Ubykh's limit of two initial consonants. Also, the English words
       <i>
        sphere
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsfɪər/
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        sphinx
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsfɪŋks/
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       loanwords, violate the rule that two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricatives
       </a>
       may not appear adjacently word-initially.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Consonant_cluster&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , the longest possible initial cluster is three consonants, as in
       <i>
        split
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsplɪt/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        strudel
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈʃtruːdəl/
       </span>
       , and "squirrel"
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈskwɪrəl/
       </span>
       , all beginning with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       and ending with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       , or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the longest possible final cluster is five consonants, as in
       <i>
        angsts
       </i>
       in some dialects
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈæŋksts/
       </span>
       , though that is rare (perhaps owing to the fact that it is a derivative of a recent German loanword
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AngstEtymology_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AngstEtymology-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), while final clusters of four consonants, as in
       <i>
        sixths
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsɪksθs/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        twelfths
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈtwɛlfθs/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        bursts
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈbɜːrsts/
       </span>
       (in
       <a href="/wiki/Rhoticity_in_English" title="Rhoticity in English">
        rhotic accents
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        glimpsed
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈɡlɪmpst/
       </span>
       , are more common. Within compound words, clusters of five consonants or more are possible (if cross-syllabic clusters are accepted), as in
       <i>
        handspring
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈhændspriŋ/
       </span>
       and in the Yorkshire place-name of
       <i>
        Hampsthwaite
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /hæmpsθweɪt/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It is important to distinguish clusters and
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraphs
       </a>
       . Clusters are made of two or more consonant
       <i>
        sounds
       </i>
       , while a digraph is a group of two consonant
       <i>
        letters
       </i>
       standing for a single sound. For example, in the word
       <i>
        ship
       </i>
       , the two letters of the digraph ⟨sh⟩ together represent the single consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       . Conversely, the letter ⟨x⟩ can produce the consonant clusters /ks/ (annex), /gz/ (exist), /kʃ/ (sexual), or /gʒ/ (some pronunciations of "luxury"). It is worth noting that ⟨x⟩ often produces sounds in two different syllables (following the general principle of saturating the subsequent syllable before assigning sounds to the preceding syllable). Also note a combination digraph and cluster as seen in
       <i>
        length
       </i>
       with two digraphs ⟨ng⟩, ⟨th⟩ representing a cluster of two consonants:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋθ/
       </span>
       (although it may be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋkθ/
       </span>
       instead, as ⟨ng⟩ followed by a voiceless consonant in the same syllable often does);
       <i>
        lights
       </i>
       with a silent digraph ⟨gh⟩ followed by a cluster ⟨t⟩, ⟨s⟩:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/
       </span>
       ; and compound words such as
       <i>
        sightscreen
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsaɪtskriːn/
       </span>
       or
       <i>
        catchphrase
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkætʃfreɪz/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
