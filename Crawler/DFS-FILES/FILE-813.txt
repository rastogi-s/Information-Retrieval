      <p>
       <b>
        Chamorro
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/tʃ/: 'ch' in 'China'">
            tʃ
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">
            ɔːr
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (Chamorro:
       <i>
        <span lang="ch" xml:lang="ch">
         Finu' Chamoru
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is an
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian language
       </a>
       spoken by about 47,000 people (about 35,000 people on
       <a href="/wiki/Guam" title="Guam">
        Guam
       </a>
       and about 12,000 in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Mariana_Islands" title="Northern Mariana Islands">
        Northern Mariana Islands
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is the native and spoken language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chamorro_people" title="Chamorro people">
        Chamorro people
       </a>
       who are the indigenous people of Guam and the Northern Mariana Islands,
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_territory" title="United States territory">
        US territories
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">
        Speakers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamorro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Speakers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG/220px-Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG/330px-Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG/440px-Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Welcome_to_Guam2.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         "Hafa Adai" sign at
         <a href="/wiki/Antonio_B._Won_Pat_International_Airport" title="Antonio B. Won Pat International Airport">
          Antonio B. Won Pat International Airport
         </a>
         in Guam
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Chamorro language is threatened, with a precipitous drop in language fluency over the past century. It is estimated that 75% of the population of Guam was literate in the Chamorro language around the time the United States captured the island during the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish%E2%80%93American_War" title="Spanish–American War">
        Spanish–American War
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (there are no similar language fluency estimates for other areas of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mariana_Islands" title="Mariana Islands">
        Mariana Islands
       </a>
       during this time). A century later, the 2000 U.S. Census showed that fewer than 20% of Chamorros living in Guam speak their heritage language fluently, and the vast majority of those were over the age of 55.
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of forces have contributed to the steep, post-World War II decline of Chamorro language fluency. A colonial legacy, beginning with the Spanish colonization of Guam in 1668, and eventually the American acquisition of the islands (whose power continues to this day), imposed power structures privileging the language of the region's colonizers. It is of worthy note that according to estimates, a large majority, as stated above (75%), maintained active knowledge of the Chamorro language even during the Spanish colonial era, but this was all to change with advent of American imperialism and enforcement of the English language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Guam, the language suffered additional suppression when the U.S. government banned the Chamorro language in schools in 1922. They collected and burned all Chamorro dictionaries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similar policies were undertaken by the Japanese government when they controlled the region during World War II. After World War II, when Guam was ceded back to the United States, the American administrators of the island continued to impose “no Chamorro” language restrictions in local schools, teaching only English and disciplining students for speaking their indigenous tongue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Even though these oppressive language policies were progressively lifted, the damage had been done. Subsequent generations were often raised in households where only the oldest family members were fluent. Lack of exposure made it increasingly difficult to pick up Chamorro as a second language. Within a few generations, English replaced Chamorro as the language of daily life.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a difference in the rate of Chamorro language fluency between Guam and the other Mariana Islands. On Guam (called
       <i>
        Guåhan
       </i>
       by Chamorro speakers, from the word
       <i>
        guaha
       </i>
       , meaning "have"; its English meaning is "We have" from the idea that they had everything they needed
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pdn_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pdn-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) the number of native Chamorro speakers has dwindled in the last decade or so. In the Northern Mariana Islands (NMI), young Chamorros speak the language fluently. Chamorro is common among Chamorro households in the Northern Marianas, but fluency has greatly decreased among Guamanian Chamorros during the years of American rule in favor of American English, which is commonplace throughout the inhabited Marianas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Today, the NMI Chamorros and Guamanian Chamorros disagree strongly on each other's linguistic fluency. An NMI Chamorro would say that Guamanian Chamorros do not speak the language correctly (or speak "broken Chamorro"), whereas the Guamanian Chamorros believe that the NMI Chamorros are using an archaic form of the language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Revitalization_efforts">
        Revitalization efforts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamorro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revitalization efforts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Representatives from Guam have unsuccessfully lobbied the United States to take action to promote and protect the language.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2013, "Guam will be instituting
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.guamlegislature.org/Public_Laws_31st/P.L.%2031-45%20SBill%20No.%2095-31.pdf" rel="nofollow">
        Public Law 31-45
       </a>
       , which increases the teaching of the Chamorro language and culture in Guam schools," extending instruction to include grades 7–10.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other efforts have been made in recent times, most notably Chamorro immersion schools. One example is the Huråo Guåhan Academy, at the Chamorro Village in Hagåtña, GU. This program is led by Ann Marie Arceo and her husband, Ray Arceo. According to Huråo's official YouTube page, "Huråo Academy is one if not the first Chamoru Immersion Schools that focus on the teaching of Chamoru language and Self-identity on Guam. Huråo was founded as a non-profit in June 2005."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The academy has been praised by many for the continuity of the Chamoru language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other creative ways to incorporate and promote the Chamorro language have been found in the use of applications for smartphones, internet videos and television. From Chamorro dictionaries,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to the most recent "Speak Chamorro" app,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       efforts are growing and expanding in ways to preserve and protect the Chamorro language and identity.
      </p>
      <p>
       On YouTube, a popular Chamorro soap opera
       <i>
        Siha
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       has received mostly positive feedback from native Chamorro speakers on its ability to weave dramatics, the Chamorro language, and island culture into an entertaining program. On TV,
       <i>
        Nihi! Kids
       </i>
       is a first-of-its-kind show, because it is targeted "for Guam’s nenis that aims to perpetuate Chamoru language and culture while encouraging environmental stewardship, healthy choices and character development."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamorro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Unlike most of its neighbors, Chamorro is not classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Micronesian_languages" title="Micronesian languages">
        Micronesian
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Polynesian_languages" title="Polynesian languages">
        Polynesian
       </a>
       language. Rather, like
       <a href="/wiki/Palauan_language" title="Palauan language">
        Palauan
       </a>
       , it constitutes a possibly independent branch of the
       <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Malayo-Polynesian languages">
        Malayo-Polynesian language family
       </a>
       . Its indigenous origins are thus somewhat obscure.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the time the Spanish rule over Guam ended, it was thought that Chamorro was a semi-Creole language, with a substantial amount of the vocabulary of Spanish origin and beginning to have a high level of mutual intelligibility with Spanish. It is reported that even in the early 1920s Spanish was reported to be a living language in Guam for commercial transactions, but the use of Spanish and Chamorro was rapidly declining as a result of English pressure.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish influences in the language exist due to three centuries of Spanish colonial rule. Many words in the Chamorro lexicon are of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       etymological origin via Spanish, but their use conforms with indigenous grammatical structures. Furthermore, indigenous pronunciation has "nativized" most words of foreign origin that haven't conformed to the ways that indigenous speakers of the language are accustomed to making sounds. By some it may be considered a
       <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">
        mixed language
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       under a historical point of view, even though it remains independent and unique. In his
       <i>
        Chamorro Reference Grammar
       </i>
       , Donald M. Topping states:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        "The most notable influence on Chamorro language and culture came from the Spanish. ... There was wholesale borrowing of Spanish words and phrases into Chamorro, and there was even some borrowing from the Spanish sound system. But this borrowing was linguistically superficial. The bones of the Chamorro language remained intact. ... In virtually all cases of borrowing, Spanish words were forced to conform to the Chamorro sound system. ... While Spanish may have left a lasting mark on Chamorro vocabulary, as it did on many Philippine and South American languages, it had virtually no effect on Chamorro grammar. ... Japanese influence on Chamorro was much greater than that of German, but much less than Spanish. Once again, the linguistic influence was restricted exclusively to vocabulary items, many of which refer to manufactured objects...
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChamRefGram_17-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ChamRefGram-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       In contrast, in the essays found in
       <i>
        Del español al chamorro. Lenguas en contacto en el Pacífico
       </i>
       (2009), Dr. Rafael Rodríguez-Ponga—the Complutense-trained linguist and Secretary-General of Spain's Instituto Cervantes—refers to modern Chamorro as a "mixed language" of "Hispanic-Austronesian" origins, while estimating that approximately 50% of the Chamorro lexicon comes from the Spanish language and that the contribution of this language goes far beyond loanwords.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rafael_Rodr%C3%ADguez-Ponga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rafael Rodríguez-Ponga (page does not exist)">
        Rafael Rodríguez-Ponga
       </a>
       considers Chamorro a Spanish-Austronesian or a Spanish-Austronesian mixed language or at least a language that has emerged from a process of contact and
       <i>
        creolization
       </i>
       on the island of Guam, since modern Chamorro is influenced in vocabulary, and it has in its grammar many elements of Spanish origin:
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       , articles,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
        numerals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(grammar)" title="Conjunction (grammar)">
        conjunctions
       </a>
       , etc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPonga_cd_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPonga_cd-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This process, which began in the 17th century and ended in the early 20th century, meant a profound change from the old Chamorro (paleo-Chamorro) to modern Chamorro (neo-Chamorro) in its
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        <a href="/wiki/Received_Pronunciation" title="Received Pronunciation">

       </li>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">

       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back unrounded vowel">
         close-mid back unrounded vowel
        </a>

       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">

      <p>
       Additionally, some letter combinations in Chamoru sometimes represent single phonemes. For instance, "ci+[vowel]" and "ti+[vowel]" are both pronounced [ʃ], as in "hustisia" (
       <i>
        justice
       </i>
       ) and the surname
       <i>
        Concepcion
       </i>
       (Spanish influence).
      </p>
      <p>
       The letter ⟨y⟩ is usually (though not always) pronounced more like
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       (an approximation of the regional Spanish pronunciation of
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       ); it is also sometimes used to represent the same sound as the letter
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       by Guamanian speakers. The phonemes represented by ⟨n⟩ and ⟨ñ⟩ as well as ⟨a⟩ and ⟨å⟩ are not always distinguished in print. Thus the Guamanian place name spelled
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Yona,_Guam" title="Yona, Guam">
         Yona
        </a>
       </i>
       is pronounced "Dzonia"/
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dzoɲa]
       </span>
       , not
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[jona]
       </span>
       as might be expected. ⟨Ch⟩ is usually pronounced like
       <i>
        ts
       </i>
       rather than like English
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       . Chamorro ⟨r⟩ is usually a flap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       , like Spanish
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       between vowels, and a retroflex approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɻ ]
       </span>
       , like English
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       , at the beginning of words.
      </p>
      <p>
       Chamorro has
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate_consonant" title="Geminate consonant">
        geminate consonants
       </a>
       which are written double (GG, DD, KK, MM, NGNG, PP, SS, TT), native
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongs
       </a>
       AI and AO, plus OI, OE, IA, IU, IE in loanwords;
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Penultimate_stress&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Penultimate stress (page does not exist)">
        penultimate stress
       </a>
       , except where marked otherwise, if marked at all in writing, usually with an
       <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">
        acute accent
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        asút
       </i>
       "blue" or
       <i>
        dángkulo
       </i>
       "big". Unstressed vowels are limited to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ə i u/
       </span>
       , though they are often spelled A E O. Syllables may be consonant-vowel-consonant, as in
       <i>
        che’lu
       </i>
       "sibling",
       <i>
        diskatga
       </i>
       "unload",
       <i>
        mamahlao
       </i>
       "shy", or
       <i>
        oppop
       </i>
       "lie face down",
       <i>
        gatus
       </i>
       (old word for 100),
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hag%C3%A5t%C3%B1a" title="Hagåtña">
         Hagåtña
        </a>
       </i>
       (Capital of Guam); B, D, and G are not distinguished from P, T, and K in that position.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
          <span title="This information is too vague. (April 2009)">
           vague
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Today, there is an ongoing issue on the Chamorro language orthography (example: Mt. Tapochau vs. Mt. Tapochao). Also note that "Ññ" and "Åå" are not always used, leading to further confusion for modern learners of the language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamorro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Chamorro is a VSO or
       <a href="/wiki/Verb%E2%80%93subject%E2%80%93object" title="Verb–subject–object">
        verb–subject–object
       </a>
       language. However, the word order can be very flexible and so change to SVO (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" title="Subject-verb-object">
        subject-verb-object
       </a>
       ), like English, if necessary to convey different types of relative clauses depending on context and stressing parts of what someone is trying to say or convey. Again, this is subject to debate as those on Guam believe the language is flexible whereas those in the CNMI do not.
      </p>
      <p>
       Chamorro is also an
       <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">
        agglutinative language
       </a>
       , grammatically allowing root words to be modified by a number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affixes" title="Affixes">
        affixes
       </a>
       . For example,
       <i>
        masanganenñaihon
       </i>
       "talked awhile (with/to)", passive marking prefix
       <i>
        ma-
       </i>
       , root verb
       <i>
        sangan
       </i>
       , referential suffix
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       "to" (forced
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphophonemic" title="Morphophonemic">
        morphophonemically
       </a>
       to change to
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       ) with excrescent consonant
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       , and suffix
       <i>
        ñaihon
       </i>
       "a short amount of time". Thus
       <i>
        Masanganenñaihon gue'
       </i>
       "He/she was told (something) for a while".
      </p>
      <p>
       Chamorro has many Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       and other words have Spanish etymological roots (e.g.,
       <i>
        tenda
       </i>
       "shop/store" from Spanish
       <i>
        tienda
       </i>
       ), which may lead some to mistakenly conclude that the language is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish-based_creole_languages" title="Spanish-based creole languages">
        Spanish Creole
       </a>
       : Chamorro very much uses its loanwords in a Micronesian way (e.g., bumobola "playing
       <a href="/wiki/Ball" title="Ball">
        ball
       </a>
       " from bola "ball, play ball" with verbalizing
       <a href="/wiki/Infix" title="Infix">
        infix
       </a>
       -um- and
       <a href="/wiki/Reduplication" title="Reduplication">
        reduplication
       </a>
       of first syllable of root).
      </p>

       Chamorro is a
       <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">
        predicate
       </a>
       -initial,

       </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers">
        Numbers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chamorro_studies">
        Chamorro studies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chamorro_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Chamorro studies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Chamorro is studied at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Guam" title="University of Guam">
        University of Guam
       </a>
       and in several academic institutions of Guam and the Northern Marianas.
      </p>
      <p>
       Researchers in several countries are studying aspects of Chamorro. In 2009, the Chamorro Linguistics International Network (CHIN) was established in Bremen, Germany. CHiN was founded on occasion of the Chamorro Day (27 September 2009) which was part of the programme of the Festival of Languages. The foundation ceremony was attended by people from Germany, Guam, the Netherlands, New Zealand, Spain, Switzerland, and the United States of America.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
