      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Hispanidad
        </b>
       </i>
       (Hispanicity) is an expression with several meanings, loosely alluding to the group of people, countries and communities sharing the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       and displaying a Spanish-related culture. The term traces back to the
       <a href="/wiki/Early_modern_period" title="Early modern period">
        early modern period
       </a>
       but was redefined by Spanish philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Unamuno" title="Miguel de Unamuno">
        Miguel de Unamuno
       </a>
       in 1909. The term "Hispanity" is a term that regards to a multicultural community of countries of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       According to the philosopher and writer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jul%C3%ADan_Mari%C3%A1s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Julían Mariás (page does not exist)">
        Julían Mariás
       </a>
       , the Spanish American territories were not just colonies but extensions of Spain, mixed with the native American peoples with whom Europeans intermarried, creating a multicultural society.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_use">
        Early use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term has been used in the early modern period and is in the
       <i>
        Tractado de orthographía y accentos en las tres lenguas principales
       </i>
       by Alejo Venegas, printed in 1531, to mean "style of linguistic expression". It was used, with a similar meaning, in the 1803 edition of the
       <i>
        Dictionary of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Royal_Academy" title="Spanish Royal Academy">
         Spanish Royal Academy
        </a>
       </i>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Synonym" title="Synonym">
        synonym
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Hispanismo
       </i>
       (Hispanism), which, in turn, was defined as "the peculiar speech of the Spanish language".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filosofía-Hispanidad_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Filosofía-Hispanidad-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Revival">
        Revival
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Revival">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the early 20th century, the term and the were revived, with several new meanings. Its reintroduction is attributed to Unamuno in 1909, who used the term again on 11 March 1910, in an article,
       <i>
        La Argentinidad
       </i>
       , published in a newspaper in
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Naci%C3%B3n" title="La Nación">
         La Nación
        </a>
       </i>
       . He compared the term to other similar expressions:
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/argentinidad#Spanish" title="wikt:argentinidad">
          argentinidad
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/americanidad#Spanish" title="wikt:americanidad">
          americanidad
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/espa%C3%B1olidad#Spanish" title="wikt:españolidad">
          españolidad
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/italianidad#Spanish" title="wikt:italianidad">
          italianidad
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filosofía-Hispanidad_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Filosofía-Hispanidad-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unamuno linked the concept to the multiplicity of peoples speaking the Spanish language, which encompassed in turn his idea of
       <a href="/wiki/La_Raza" title="La Raza">
        La Raza
       </a>
       , gave it an egalitarian substrate and questioned the very status of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motherland" title="Motherland">
        motherland
       </a>
       for Spain; he claimed the need of approaching Spanish American republics in terms of sisterhood (opposing "primacies" and "maternities").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Unamuno-Adelanto_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Unamuno-Adelanto-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="305" data-file-width="200" height="198" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg/130px-Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg/195px-Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/84/Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg 2x" width="130"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Zacarias_de_Vizcarra.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The priest Zacarías de Vizcarra spread the term en 1926
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Further development of the concept of had to wait for the 1920s, when a group of intellectuals was influenced by the ideas of an ultranationalist French thinker
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Maurras" title="Charles Maurras">
        Charles Maurras
       </a>
       and rescued the term.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColom_González20139_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColom_González20139-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a precedent, the Spanish writer José María Salaverría, who lived in Argentina between 1910 and 1913, would have implicitly the idea of an Hispanic community, comparable to Hispanidad, but the leading status of Spain in the community is however a moot point in his work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Allende200965–67_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Allende200965–67-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term was used by Spanish priest Zacarías de Vizcarra, who was living in
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERamón_Solans2014364_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERamón_Solans2014364-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He proposed in 1926 that the expression
       <i>
        Fiesta de la Raza
       </i>
       should be changed to
       <i>
        Fiesta de la Hispanidad
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Cuevas2003244Marcilhacy201475_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Cuevas2003244Marcilhacy201475-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the reign of King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_XIII" title="Alfonso XIII">
        Alfonso XIII
       </a>
       of Spain, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Virgin_of_Guadaloupe" title="Virgin of Guadaloupe">
        Virgin of Guadaloupe
       </a>
       was proclaimed "Queen of the Hispanidad" in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPastor2010259_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPastor2010259-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the later years of the decade, vanguard writer
       <a href="/wiki/Ernesto_Gim%C3%A9nez_Caballero" title="Ernesto Giménez Caballero">
        Ernesto Giménez Caballero
       </a>
       began to elaborate a neo-imperialist narrative of the Hispanidad in
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_Gaceta_Literaria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La Gaceta Literaria (page does not exist)">
         La Gaceta Literaria
        </a>
        <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
         (
         <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/La_Gaceta_Literaria" title="es:La Gaceta Literaria">
          es
         </a>
         )
        </span>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFriedman201138–39_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFriedman201138–39-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The doctrine of Hispanidad would also become a core tenet of the
       <a href="/wiki/Reactionary" title="Reactionary">
        reactionary
       </a>
       thought in Spain in the coming years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJuan_Navarro2006392_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJuan_Navarro2006392-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         </div>

         <i>
          Defensa de la Hispanidad
         </i>
         (1934), by Ramiro de Maeztu.
        </div>

      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Spanish_Republic" title="Second Spanish Republic">
        Second Spanish Republic
       </a>
       , Spanish monarchist author
       <a href="/wiki/Ramiro_de_Maeztu" title="Ramiro de Maeztu">
        Ramiro de Maeztu
       </a>
       , who had been the ambassador to Argentina between 1928 and 1930,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENúñez_Seixas2013870_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENúñez_Seixas2013870-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       considered the concept of Hispanidad, motivated by the interests aroused on him by Argentine-related topics,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartínez_de_Velasco_Farinós1981180_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartínez_de_Velasco_Farinós1981180-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the meetings between him and the attendants to the courses of Catholic culture as nationalist, Catholic and anti-liberal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Calleja2007612_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Calleja2007612-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Maeztu explained his doctrine of Hispanidad in his work
       <i>
        Defensa de la Hispanidad
       </i>
       (1934); he thought it was a spiritual world that united Spain and its former colonies by the Spanish language and Catholicism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPerfecto201265_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPerfecto201265-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He attributed the concept to Vizcarra, instead of Unamuno.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Cuevas2003244_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Cuevas2003244-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the Hispanidad of Maeztu, the Christian and humanist features that would identify Hispanic peoples would replace rationalism, liberalism and democracy, which he called alien to the Hispanic
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ethos" title="Ethos">
         ethos
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Calleja2007619_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzález_Calleja2007619-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His work "relentlessly" linked Catholicism and Hispanidad and was highly influential with Argentine nationalists
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaborido2007425–426_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaborido2007425–426-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Spanish far right, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francoism" title="Francoism">
        Francoism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERodríguez_Jiménez199445_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERodríguez_Jiménez199445-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although declaredly antiracist because of its Catholic origin, the sense of racial egalitarianism in the Maeztu's idea of the Hispanidad was restricted to the scope of heavenly salvation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEÁlvarez_Chillida2014111–112_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEÁlvarez_Chillida2014111–112-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goma_y_Thomas.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2454" data-file-width="1470" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Goma_y_Thomas.JPG/130px-Goma_y_Thomas.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Goma_y_Thomas.JPG/195px-Goma_y_Thomas.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Goma_y_Thomas.JPG/260px-Goma_y_Thomas.JPG 2x" width="130"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Goma_y_Thomas.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Primate Gomá defended the ideas of Vizcarra and Maeztu.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMartini201558_21-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMartini201558-21">
           [21]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>

      <p>
       Spanish Primate Isidro Gomá y Tomas issued in Argentina, on 12 October 1934, a Maeztu-inspired manifesto,
       <i>
        Apology of the Hispanidad
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "America is the work of Spain. This work by Spain is essentially of Catholic nature. Hence, there is a relation of equality between Hispanidad and Catholicism, and it is madness any attempt of Hispanidad disowing that relation".
        <br/>
        <br/>
        "
        <i>
         América es la obra de España. Esta obra de España lo es esencialmente de catolicismo. Luego hay relación de igualdad entre hispanidad y catolicismo, y es locura todo intento de hispanización que lo repudie.
        </i>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts200462Colom_González200664_22-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts200462Colom_González200664-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <a href="/wiki/Isidro_Goma_y_Tomas" title="Isidro Goma y Tomas">
          Isidro Gomá
         </a>
         , fragment of «Apología de la Hispanidad» (Buenos Aires, 1934), collected in
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Acci%C3%B3n_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Acción Española">
           Acción Española
          </a>
         </i>
         (1 November 1934).
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       According to Stephen G. H. Roberts, Gomá linked the ideas of Maeztu and the ideology that was developed by the dictatorship of Franco.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts200462_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts200462-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Francoist_Spain">
        Francoist Spain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Francoist Spain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       That narrative was heavily featured in Nationalist propaganda during the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
        Spanish Civil War
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPasamar2010197_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPasamar2010197-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       being used as war tool.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPardo_Sanz1992211_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPardo_Sanz1992211-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish philosopher and Francoist propagandist
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Garc%C3%ADa_Morente&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manuel García Morente (page does not exist)">
        Manuel García Morente
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_Garc%C3%ADa_Morente" title="es:Manuel García Morente">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       would made
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Francisco Franco
       </a>
       the saviour of the legacy of the Hispanidad from an "invisible army" that was sent by the
       <a href="/wiki/Communist_International" title="Communist International">
        Communist International
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">
        Moscow
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENicolás_Marín199839–40_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENicolás_Marín199839–40-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       García Morente would synthetize the essence of Hispanidad in the archaistic ideal of "
       <a href="/wiki/Miles_Christianus" title="Miles Christianus">
        Christian knight
       </a>
       ", half-monk and half-soldier;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEColom_González200666_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEColom_González200666-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that figure was used in the pages of student books during the beginning of the Francoist dictatorship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENúñez_Seixas2006205_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENúñez_Seixas2006205-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After the Spanish Civil War, the
       <a href="/wiki/Our_Lady_of_the_Pillar" title="Our Lady of the Pillar">
        Our Lady of the Pillar
       </a>
       became a symbol of Hispanidad in Spain and was linked to the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Catholicism" title="National Catholicism">
        National Catholicism
       </a>
       of the Franco´s regime to the ideas of patriotism and "Hispanic essences".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECenarro199792,_97_y_98_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECenarro199792,_97_y_98-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Franco created the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Council_of_the_Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Council of the Hispanidad (page does not exist)">
        Council of the Hispanidad
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consejo_de_la_Hispanidad" title="es:Consejo de la Hispanidad">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       on 2 November 1940.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPayne1987360Barbeito1989117_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPayne1987360Barbeito1989117-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was thought at first to be a sort of supranational institution,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarbeito1989118_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarbeito1989118-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and it ended up being a council of 74 members, charged with the task of coordinating the relations with Latin America.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPayne1987360_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPayne1987360-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Hispanidad became the source of an expansive nationalism (first imperialist and then cultural).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarcilhacy2014101_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarcilhacy2014101-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Besides its character both as national identitary element and as stalwart of Catholicism, Francoism would use the Hispanidad in international relations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECalle_Velasco2004170_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECalle_Velasco2004170-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Council of the Hispanidad would become the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Institute_of_Hispanic_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Institute of Hispanic Culture (page does not exist)">
        Institute of Hispanic Culture
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instituto_de_Cultura_Hisp%C3%A1nica" title="es:Instituto de Cultura Hispánica">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       in 1946 and change from a more
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Falangist" title="Falangist">
        Falangist
       </a>
       profile to a more Catholic one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFernández_de_Miguel2012360_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFernández_de_Miguel2012360-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That happened within a framework of a general change in the doctrine of the Hispanidad between 1945 and 1947, with
       <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Mart%C3%ADn-Artajo" title="Alberto Martín-Artajo">
        Alberto Martín-Artajo
       </a>
       at the helm of the Spanish Ministry of Foreign Affairs. The message then became more defensive and less aggressive, with fewer mentions of "empire" and "race" (biological).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESepúlveda_Muñoz2005174_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESepúlveda_Muñoz2005174-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Afterwards, later in the Francoist dictatorship, the regime, then less constrained by the international community, recovered more aggressive rhetorics, but it failed to reach the full extent of when Ramón Serrano Suñer was Minister of Foreign Affairs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESepúlveda_Muñoz2005174–175_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESepúlveda_Muñoz2005174–175-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1958, the Day of the Race was renamed to Day of the Hispanidad in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMarcilhacy2014100_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMarcilhacy2014100-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mexico">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Already in the 1930s, conservative Mexican writer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alfonso_Junco&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alfonso Junco (page does not exist)">
        Alfonso Junco
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfonso_Junco" title="es:Alfonso Junco">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       had become an active propagandist of the Hispanidad.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUrías_Horcasitas2010b615_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUrías_Horcasitas2010b615-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of the key parts of the ideology of
       <a href="/wiki/National_Action_Party_(Mexico)" title="National Action Party (Mexico)">
        <i>
         "Panista"
        </i>
        Mexican politician
       </a>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Efra%C3%ADn_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Efraín González Luna (page does not exist)">
        Efraín González Luna
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Efra%C3%ADn_Gonz%C3%A1lez_Luna" title="es:Efraín González Luna">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       , who strongly supported
       <a href="/wiki/Miscegenation" title="Miscegenation">
        miscegenation
       </a>
       , was the Hispanidad, which he conceived in terms of a united community of sovereign states that defended their own values from foreign threats like communism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGómez_Peralta2010172_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGómez_Peralta2010172-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other opponents of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Post-revolutionary_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Post-revolutionary Mexico (page does not exist)">
        post-revolutionary Mexico
       </a>
       , who spread the doctrine of the Hispanidad were
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Miguel_Palomar_y_Vizcarra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Miguel Palomar y Vizcarra (page does not exist)">
        Miguel Palomar y Vizcarra
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miguel_Palomar_y_Vizcarra" title="es:Miguel Palomar y Vizcarra">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jes%C3%BAs_Guisa_y_Azevedo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jesús Guisa y Azevedo (page does not exist)">
        Jesús Guisa y Azevedo
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jes%C3%BAs_Guisa_y_Azevedo" title="es:Jesús Guisa y Azevedo">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvador_Abascal_Infante" title="Salvador Abascal Infante">
        Salvador Abascal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Borrego" title="Salvador Borrego">
        Salvador Borrego
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUrías_Horcasitas2010a196_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUrías_Horcasitas2010a196-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/National_Synarchist_Union" title="National Synarchist Union">
        National Synarchist Union
       </a>
       saw in the Hispanidad a key component of the vitality of the Mexican nation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArd200344_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArd200344-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_exiles">
        Spanish exiles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Spanish exiles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The idea of Hispanidad was also featured with new meanings in authors of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Republic_in_exile" title="Spanish Republic in exile">
        Spanish Republic in exile
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fernando_de_los_R%C3%ADos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fernando de los Ríos (page does not exist)">
        Fernando de los Ríos
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fernando_de_los_R%C3%ADos" title="es:Fernando de los Ríos">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joaquin_Xirau_Palau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joaquin Xirau Palau (page does not exist)">
        Joaquín Xirau
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_Nicol" title="Eduardo Nicol">
        Eduardo Nicol
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9rico_Castro" title="Américo Castro">
        Américo Castro
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESánchez_Cuervo201417,_25_y_30_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESánchez_Cuervo201417,_25_y_30-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_de_Madariaga" title="Salvador de Madariaga">
        Salvador de Madariaga
       </a>
       , also exiled, defended the Hispanidad as a positive factor towards cultural ontogeny; he believed its miscegenation was much better than the Anglo-Saxon example.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERojas_Mix1997187_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERojas_Mix1997187-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Argentina">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In Argentina, one of the few countries with good relations with Francoist Spain after the end of
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       , President
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Domingo_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Domingo Perón">
        Juan Domingo Perón
       </a>
       defended the concept of Hispanidad by highlighting the Hispanic roots of Argentina. However,
       <a href="/wiki/Peronism" title="Peronism">
        Peronism
       </a>
       began to detach itself from the idea from 1950 to 1954 period to replace it with
       <i>
        Latinidad
       </i>
       (Latinity).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERein1991_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERein1991-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_countries">
        Other countries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanidad&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Other countries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
