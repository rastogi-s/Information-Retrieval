      <p>
       <b>
        Lambda, Λ, λ
       </b>
       (uppercase
       <b>
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         Λ
        </span>
       </b>
       , lowercase
       <b>
        <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
         λ
        </span>
       </b>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        λάμ(β)δα
       </span>
       <i>
        lám(b)da
       </i>
       ) is the 11th letter of the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek alphabet
       </a>
       . In the system of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_numerals" title="Greek numerals">
        Greek numerals
       </a>
       lambda has a value of 30. Lambda is related to the
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet" title="Phoenician alphabet">
        Phoenician letter
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lamed" title="Lamed">
        Lamed
       </a>
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoenician_lamedh.svg" title="Lamedh">
        <img alt="Lamedh" data-file-height="85" data-file-width="85" height="15" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Phoenician_lamedh.svg/15px-Phoenician_lamedh.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Phoenician_lamedh.svg/23px-Phoenician_lamedh.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Phoenician_lamedh.svg/30px-Phoenician_lamedh.svg.png 2x" width="15"/>
       </a>
       . Letters in other
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabets
       </a>
       that stemmed from lambda include the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/L" title="L">
        L
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrillic" title="Cyrillic">
        Cyrillic
       </a>
       letter
       <a href="/wiki/El_(Cyrillic)" title="El (Cyrillic)">
        El
       </a>
       (Л, л). The ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandrine_grammarians" title="Alexandrine grammarians">
        grammarians
       </a>
       and dramatists give evidence to the pronunciation as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">
         [laːbdaː]
        </a>
       </span>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        λάβδα
       </span>
       ) in Classical Greek times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek" title="Modern Greek">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       the name of the letter, Λάμδα, is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">
         [lamða]
        </a>
       </span>
       ; the spoken letter itself has the sound of "
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/L" title="L">
         l
        </a>
       </span>
       " as with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latinate" title="Latinate">
        Latinate
       </a>
       "L".
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epichoric_alphabets" title="Epichoric alphabets">
        early Greek alphabets
       </a>
       , the shape and orientation of lambda varied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most variants consisted of two straight strokes, one longer than the other, connected at their ends. The angle might be in the upper-left, lower-left ("Western" alphabets), or top ("Eastern" alphabets). Other variants had a vertical line with a horizontal or sloped stroke running to the right. With the general adoption of the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Greek_alphabet#Standardization_–_the_Ionic_alphabet" title="History of the Greek alphabet">
        Ionic alphabet
       </a>
       , Greek settled on an angle at the top; the Romans put the angle at the lower-left.
      </p>
      <p>
       The HTML 4 character entity references for the Greek capital and small letter lambda are "&amp;#923;" and "&amp;#955;", respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Unicode code points for lambda are U+039B and U+03BB.
      </p>

      <p>
       Examples of the symbolic use of uppercase lambda include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda_particle" title="Lambda particle">
         lambda particle
        </a>
        is a type of
        <a href="/wiki/Subatomic_particle" title="Subatomic particle">
         subatomic particle
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Physics#High_energy/particle_physics" title="Physics">
         subatomic particle physics
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda is the set of logical axioms in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axiomatic_method" title="Axiomatic method">
         axiomatic method
        </a>
        of logical deduction in
        <a href="/wiki/First-order_logic" title="First-order logic">
         first-order logic
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda was used as a
        <a href="/wiki/Aspis" title="Aspis">
         shield pattern
        </a>
        by the
        <a href="/wiki/Spartan_army" title="Spartan army">
         Spartan army
        </a>
        . This stood for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lacedaemon" title="Lacedaemon">
         Lacedaemon
        </a>
        , (
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%9B%CE%B1%CE%BA%CE%B5%CE%B4%CE%B1%CE%AF%CE%BC%CF%89%CE%BD" title="wikt:Λακεδαίμων">
          Λακεδαίμων
         </a>
         ,
         <i>
          Lakedaímōn
         </i>
        </span>
        ), the name of the
        <a href="/wiki/Polis" title="Polis">
         polis
        </a>
        of the Spartans, as opposed to the city itself.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda is the
        <a href="/wiki/Von_Mangoldt_function" title="Von Mangoldt function">
         von Mangoldt function
        </a>
        in mathematical
        <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">
         number theory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
         statistics
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samuel_Stanley_Wilks" title="Samuel Stanley Wilks">
         Wilks's lambda
        </a>
        is used in multivariate analysis of variance (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MANOVA" title="MANOVA">
         MANOVA
        </a>
        analysis) to compare group means on a combination of dependent variables.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_decomposition#Normal_matrices" title="Spectral decomposition">
         spectral decomposition of matrices
        </a>
        , lambda indicates the
        <a href="/wiki/Diagonal_matrix" title="Diagonal matrix">
         diagonal matrix
        </a>
        of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvalues" title="Eigenvalues">
         eigenvalues
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">
         matrix
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
         computer science
        </a>
        , lambda is the time window over which a process is observed for determining the working memory set for a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_computer" title="Digital computer">
         digital computer
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Virtual_memory" title="Virtual memory">
         virtual memory
        </a>
        management.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
         astrophysics
        </a>
        , lambda represents the likelihood that a small body will encounter a
        <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
         planet
        </a>
        or a
        <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
         dwarf planet
        </a>
        leading to a deflection of a significant magnitude. An object with a large value of lambda is expected to have
        <a href="/wiki/Clearing_the_neighbourhood" title="Clearing the neighbourhood">
         cleared its neighborhood
        </a>
        , satisfying the current
        <a href="/wiki/Definition_of_planet" title="Definition of planet">
         definition of a planet
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Crystal_optics" title="Crystal optics">
         crystal optics
        </a>
        , lambda is used to represent the period of a lattice.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/NATO" title="NATO">
         NATO
        </a>
        military operations, a chevron (a capital lambda symbol) is painted on the vehicles of this military alliance for identification.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
         chemistry
        </a>
        there are Δ (delta) and Λ (lambda) isomers, see:
        <a href="/wiki/Coordination_complex" title="Coordination complex">
         coordination complex
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">
         electrochemistry
        </a>
        , lambda denotes the "equivalent
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_conductance" title="Electrical conductance">
         conductance
        </a>
        " of an
        <a href="/wiki/Electrolyte" title="Electrolyte">
         electrolyte
        </a>
        solution.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">
         cosmology
        </a>
        , lambda is the symbol for the
        <a href="/wiki/Cosmological_constant" title="Cosmological constant">
         cosmological constant
        </a>
        , a term added to some dynamical equations to account for the
        <a href="/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe" title="Accelerating expansion of the universe">
         acceleration of the universe
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In optics, lambda denotes the grating pitch of a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bragg_reflector" title="Bragg reflector">
         Bragg reflector
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In block-handwritten
        <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
         Russian
        </a>
        , this letter represents
        <a href="/wiki/El_(Cyrillic)" title="El (Cyrillic)">
         Л
        </a>
        in both uppercase and lowercase.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       Examples of the symbolic use of lowercase lambda include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_algorithm" title="Evolutionary algorithm">
         evolutionary algorithms
        </a>
        , λ indicates the number of offspring that would be generated from μ current population in each generation. The terms μ and λ are originated from
        <a href="/wiki/Evolution_strategy" title="Evolution strategy">
         Evolution strategy
        </a>
        notation.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda indicates the
        <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
         wavelength
        </a>
        of any
        <a href="/wiki/Wave" title="Wave">
         wave
        </a>
        , especially in
        <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
         physics
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electronics_engineering" title="Electronics engineering">
         electronics engineering
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
         mathematics
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda indicates the
        <a href="/wiki/Exponential_decay" title="Exponential decay">
         radioactivity decay constant
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">
         nuclear physics
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">
         radioactivity
        </a>
        . This constant is very simply related (by a multiplicative constant) to the
        <a href="/wiki/Half-life" title="Half-life">
         half-life
        </a>
        of any radioactive material.
       </li>
       <li>
        In probability theory, lambda represents the density of occurrences within a time interval, as modeled by the
        <a href="/wiki/Poisson_distribution" title="Poisson distribution">
         Poisson distribution
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_logic" title="Mathematical logic">
         mathematical logic
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
         computer science
        </a>
        , lambda is used to introduce
        <a href="/wiki/Anonymous_function" title="Anonymous function">
         anonymous functions
        </a>
        expressed with the concepts of
        <a href="/wiki/Lambda_calculus" title="Lambda calculus">
         lambda calculus
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lambda_(unit)" title="Lambda (unit)">
         Lambda
        </a>
        is a unit of volume, synonymous with one
        <a href="/wiki/Micro-" title="Micro-">
         micro
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liter" title="Liter">
         liter
        </a>
        (1 μL), that is, one
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_millimetre" title="Cubic millimetre">
         cubic millimetre
        </a>
        (1 mm
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
        ). This use is currently deprecated.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda indicates an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eigenvalue" title="Eigenvalue">
         eigenvalue
        </a>
        in the mathematics of
        <a href="/wiki/Linear_algebra" title="Linear algebra">
         linear algebra
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In the physics of
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">
         electric fields
        </a>
        , lambda sometimes indicates the linear
        <a href="/wiki/Charge_density" title="Charge density">
         charge density
        </a>
        of a uniform line of
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
         electric charge
        </a>
        (measured in
        <a href="/wiki/Coulomb" title="Coulomb">
         coulombs
        </a>
        per meter).
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda denotes a
        <a href="/wiki/Lagrange_multiplier" title="Lagrange multiplier">
         Lagrange multiplier
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Multivariable_calculus" title="Multivariable calculus">
         multi-dimensional calculus
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Solid-state_electronics" title="Solid-state electronics">
         solid-state electronics
        </a>
        , lambda indicates the
        <a href="/wiki/Channel_length_modulation" title="Channel length modulation">
         channel length modulation
        </a>
        parameter of a
        <a href="/wiki/MOSFET" title="MOSFET">
         MOSFET
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        There is a
        <a href="/wiki/Lambda_phage" title="Lambda phage">
         lambda phage
        </a>
        virus in biology.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Ecology" title="Ecology">
         ecology
        </a>
        , lambda denotes the long-term intrinsic growth rate of a population. This value is often calculated as the dominant eigenvalue of the age/size class
        <a href="/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics)" title="Matrix (mathematics)">
         matrix (mathematics)
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formal_language_theory" title="Formal language theory">
         formal language theory
        </a>
        and in computer science, lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Empty_string" title="Empty string">
         empty string
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda is a nonstandard symbol in the
        <a href="/wiki/Obsolete_and_nonstandard_symbols_in_the_International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="Obsolete and nonstandard symbols in the International Phonetic Alphabet">
         International Phonetic Alphabet
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telecommunications" title="Telecommunications">
         telecommunications
        </a>
        , lambda indicates the wavelength in a
        <a href="/wiki/Wavelength-division_multiplexing" title="Wavelength-division multiplexing">
         wavelength-division multiplexing
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optical_communications" title="Optical communications">
         optical communications
        </a>
        system. This is actually a form of the long-established concept of
        <a href="/wiki/Frequency-division_multiplexing" title="Frequency-division multiplexing">
         frequency-division multiplexing
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Lebesgue_measure" title="Lebesgue measure">
         Lebesgue measure
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Set_theory" title="Set theory">
         mathematical set theory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Goodman_and_Kruskal%27s_lambda" title="Goodman and Kruskal's lambda">
         Goodman and Kruskal's lambda
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Statistics" title="Statistics">
         statistics
        </a>
        indicates the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proportional_reduction_in_error" title="Proportional reduction in error">
         proportional reduction in error
        </a>
        when one variable's values are used to predict the values of another variable.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_sensor" title="Oxygen sensor">
         oxygen sensor
        </a>
        in a vehicle that measures the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Air-fuel_ratio" title="Air-fuel ratio">
         air-to-fuel ratio
        </a>
        in the exhaust
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gases" title="Gases">
         gases
        </a>
        of an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal-combustion_engine" title="Internal-combustion engine">
         internal-combustion engine
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        A
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lambda_(rocket)" title="Lambda (rocket)">
         Lambda 4S
        </a>
        solid-fuel rocket was used to launch
        <a href="/wiki/%C5%8Csumi_(satellite)" title="Ōsumi (satellite)">
         Japan's first orbital satellite
        </a>
        in 1970.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Failure_rate" title="Failure rate">
         failure rate
        </a>
        of devices and systems in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reliability_theory" title="Reliability theory">
         reliability theory
        </a>
        , and it is measured in failure events per hour. Numerically, this lambda is also the reciprocal of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_time_between_failure" title="Mean time between failure">
         mean time between failures
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In criminology, lambda denotes an individual's frequency of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Offence_(law)" title="Offence (law)">
         offenses
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In cartography and navigation, lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
         longitude
        </a>
        of a location.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda denotes
        <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Langton" title="Christopher Langton">
         Christopher Langton
        </a>
        's parameter for the classification of
        <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Wolfram" title="Stephen Wolfram">
         Stephen Wolfram
        </a>
        's classes of
        <a href="/wiki/Cellular_automaton" title="Cellular automaton">
         cellular automata
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Electrochemistry" title="Electrochemistry">
         electrochemistry
        </a>
        , lambda also denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
         ionic
        </a>
        conductance of a given ion (The composition of the ion is generally shown as a subscript to the lambda character).
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neurobiology" title="Neurobiology">
         neurobiology
        </a>
        , lambda denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Length_constant" title="Length constant">
         length constant
        </a>
        (or exponential rate of decay) of the
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_potential" title="Electric potential">
         electric potential
        </a>
        across the
        <a href="/wiki/Cell_membrane" title="Cell membrane">
         cell membrane
        </a>
        along a length of a nerve cell's
        <a href="/wiki/Axon" title="Axon">
         axon
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In the science and technology of
        <a href="/wiki/Heat_transfer" title="Heat transfer">
         heat transfer
        </a>
        , lambda denotes the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization" title="Heat of vaporization">
         heat of vaporization
        </a>
        per mole of material (a.k.a. its "latent heat").
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the technology and science of celestial navigation, lambda denotes the longitude as opposed to the Roman letter "L", which denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
         latitude
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        King
        <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIII_of_France" title="Louis XIII of France">
         Louis XIII of France
        </a>
        used the letter lambda as his personal symbol rather than a
        <a href="/wiki/Monogram" title="Monogram">
         monogram
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Volvo" title="Volvo">
         Volvo
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
         automobiles
        </a>
        , lambda denotes an engine, fuel, or ignition system failure.
       </li>
       <li>
        A block style Lambda is used as a recurring symbol in the
        <a href="/wiki/Valve_Corporation" title="Valve Corporation">
         Valve Corporation
        </a>
        computer game series
        <i>

        ,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Litra_symbol">
        Litra symbol
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lambda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Litra symbol">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_units" title="Roman units">
        Roman
       </a>
       <i>
        libra
       </i>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine_units" title="Byzantine units">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       <i>
        lítra
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        λίτρα
       </span>
       ), which served as both the pound mass unit and liter volume unit, were abbreviated in Greek using lambda with modified forms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iota_subscript" title="Iota subscript">
        iota subscript
       </a>
       (as λͅ). These are variously encoded in Unicode. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_Numbers_(Unicode_block)" title="Ancient Greek Numbers (Unicode block)">
        Ancient Greek Numbers
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode_block" title="Unicode block">
        Unicode block
       </a>
       includes 10183
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         GREEK LITRA SIGN
        </span>
       </span>
       (𐆃) as well as 𐅢, which is described as 10162
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         GREEK ACROPHONIC HERMIONIAN TEN
        </span>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but was much more common as a form of the litra sign. A variant of the sign can be formed from 0338
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         COMBINING LONG SOLIDUS OVERLAY
        </span>
       </span>
       and either 039B
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         GREEK CAPITAL LETTER LAMDA
        </span>
       </span>
       (Λ̸) or 03BB
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         GREEK SMALL LETTER LAMDA
        </span>
       </span>
       (λ̸).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        An automotive
        <a href="/wiki/Oxygen_sensor" title="Oxygen sensor">
         oxygen sensor
        </a>
        (O
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        sensor) is also known as a lambda probe, sensor, sond, or sonde.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lambda is used in art and photography to refer to a digital
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_C_print" title="Type C print">
         Type C print
        </a>
        , or to the equipment that is used to produce it.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the study of gasoline engines, lambda sometimes refers to the air–fuel mixture going into the engine.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Character_encodings">
        Character encodings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
