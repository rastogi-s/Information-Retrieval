      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       uses
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(linguistics)" title="Determiner (linguistics)">
        determiners
       </a>
       in a similar way to English. The main difference is that they are modified by
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       (masculine/feminine).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstrative_determiners">
        Demonstrative determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Demonstrative determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spanish has three kinds of demonstrative, whose use typically depends on the distance (physical or metaphorical) between the speaker and the described entity, or sometimes it depends on the proximity to the three grammatical persons.
      </p>

      </table>
      <p>
       English also used to have a three-way system: "this hill (here)", "that hill (there)" or "yon hill (yonder)" — in Spanish, "esta colina", "esa colina", "aquella colina". Standard English lost the third level, so that the "that, there" series covers the ground of "yon, yonder".
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Este
       </i>
       usually refers to something near the speaker (the first person).
       <i>
        Ese
       </i>
       usually refers to something nearer the listener (the second person).
       <i>
        Aquel
       </i>
       usually refers to something away from both the speaker and the listener.
      </p>
      <p>
       The demonstrative determiners can also be used as pronouns, with the addition of the neutral singular forms
       <i>
        esto
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eso
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        aquello
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar three-way system of demonstratives is found in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_grammar#Demonstratives" title="Portuguese grammar">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_grammar#Demonstratives" title="Japanese grammar">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">
        Articles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Articles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definite_article">
        Definite article
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definite article">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The definite article in Spanish, corresponding to "the", is
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       . It inflects for gender and number as follows:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        Articles
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th rowspan="2">
        </th>
        <th colspan="2">
         Definite
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Singular
        </th>
        <th>
         Plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Masculine
        </th>
        <td>
         el
        </td>
        <td>
         los
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Feminine
        </th>
        <td>
         la
        </td>
        <td>
         las
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Neuter
        </th>
        <td>
         lo
        </td>
        <td>
         —
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         hombre
        </i>
        = "[the] man"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          los
         </b>
         hombres
        </i>
        = "[the] men"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         mujer
        </i>
        = "[the] woman"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          las
         </b>
         mujeres
        </i>
        = "[the] women"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The usually masculine form
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       is used instead of
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       before feminine nouns that begin with a stressed
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       sound:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         águila (pequeña)
        </i>
        = "the (small) eagle"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         agua (fresca)
        </i>
        = "the (fresh) water"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         hacha (afilada)
        </i>
        = "the (sharp) axe"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        La
       </i>
       is used, however, when
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       would imply a masculine noun:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         ácrata
        </i>
        (because
        <i>
         el ácrata
        </i>
        would be a male anarchist)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         árabe
        </i>
        (because
        <i>
         el árabe
        </i>
        would be a male Arab, or the Arabic language)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        El
       </i>
       is never used, however, before feminine adjectives that begin with a stressed
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         alta montaña
        </i>
        = "the high mountain"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         ancha calle
        </i>
        = "the wide street"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        Azúcar
       </i>
       is a very special case. Its
       <i>
        a-
       </i>
       is unstressed, and yet it usually takes
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       even when feminine; in addition,
       <i>
        azúcar
       </i>
       can be of both genders in Spanish (other words with double gender are
       <i>
        sal
       </i>
       (salt),
       <i>
        mar
       </i>
       (sea) and
       <i>
        sartén
       </i>
       (frying pan)):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         azúcar refinad
         <b>
          a
         </b>
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <b>
          el
         </b>
         azúcar refinad
         <b>
          o
         </b>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <b>
          la
         </b>
         azúcar refinad
         <b>
          a
         </b>
        </i>
        are also acceptable)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Feminine
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       does not have the same origin as the masculine
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       . The latter is from the Old Castilian
       <i>
        ele
       </i>
       , whereas the former is from
       <i>
        ela
       </i>
       , just as
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       is.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is also a "neuter article" that is used before adjectives and makes them work like nouns:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         bueno
        </i>
        = "the good, what is good"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         importante
        </i>
        = "the important thing"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         indefinible
        </i>
        = "the indefinable"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          lo
         </b>
         desconocido
        </i>
        = "the unknown"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indefinite_article">
        Indefinite article
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Indefinite article">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The indefinite article in Spanish, corresponding to "a/an", is
       <i>
        un
       </i>
       . It inflects for gender and number as follows:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        Articles
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th rowspan="2">
        </th>
        <th colspan="2">
         Indefinite
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Singular
        </th>
        <th>
         Plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Masculine
        </th>
        <td>
         un
        </td>
        <td>
         unos
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Feminine
        </th>
        <td>
         una
        </td>
        <td>
         unas
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Neuter
        </th>
        <td>
         uno
        </td>
        <td>
         —
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          un
         </b>
         hombre
        </i>
        = "a man"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          unos
         </b>
         hombres
        </i>
        = "some men"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          una
         </b>
         mujer
        </i>
        = "a woman"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          unas
         </b>
         mujeres
        </i>
        = "some women"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Near synonyms of
       <i>
        unos
       </i>
       include
       <i>
        unos cuantos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        algunos
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        unos pocos
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The same rules that apply to the use of feminine
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       apply to
       <i>
        una
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        un
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          un
         </b>
         ala
        </i>
        = "a wing"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          una
         </b>
         árabe
        </i>
        = "a female Arab"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          una
         </b>
         alta montaña
        </i>
        = "a high mountain"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       As in English, the plural indefinite article is not always required:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hay [unas] cosas en la mesa
        </i>
        = "There are [some] things on the table"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possessive_determiners">
        Possessive determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Possessive determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       These are often known as possessive or genitive determiners. They are used before the noun they possess (and before the rest of the whole noun phrase, for example when an adjective precedes the noun). They inflect for number and in some cases gender as well.
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        Possessive adjectives
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th>
        </th>
        <th>
         1st-person singular
        </th>
        <th>
         2nd-person singular
        </th>
        <th>
         3rd-person singular
        </th>
        <th>
         1st-person plural
        </th>
        <th>
         2nd-person plural
        </th>
        <th>
         3rd-person plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Masculine singular
        </th>
        <td>
         mi
        </td>
        <td>
         tu
        </td>
        <td>
         su
        </td>
        <td>
         nuestro
        </td>
        <td>
         vuestro
        </td>
        <td>
         su
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Feminine singular
        </th>
        <td>
         mi
        </td>
        <td>
         tu
        </td>
        <td>
         su
        </td>
        <td>
         nuestra
        </td>
        <td>
         vuestra
        </td>
        <td>
         su
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Masculine plural
        </th>
        <td>
         mis
        </td>
        <td>
         tus
        </td>
        <td>
         sus
        </td>
        <td>
         nuestros
        </td>
        <td>
         vuestros
        </td>
        <td>
         sus
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Feminine plural
        </th>
        <td>
         mis
        </td>
        <td>
         tus
        </td>
        <td>
         sus
        </td>
        <td>
         nuestras
        </td>
        <td>
         vuestras
        </td>
        <td>
         sus
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este es
         <b>
          mi
         </b>
         perro
        </i>
        = "This is my dog"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta es
         <b>
          tu
         </b>
         camisa
        </i>
        = "This is your shirt"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estos son
         <b>
          nuestros
         </b>
         libros
        </i>
        = "These are our books"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estas son
         <b>
          sus
         </b>
         casas
        </i>
        = "These are his/her/your/their houses"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Given the ambiguous meaning of "su/s", this is often avoided, and replaced by other forms that clearly state who owns the thing in question. So sentences like the following can be heard:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         la casa de él
        </i>
        = "his house" (lit. "the house of him")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         la casa de ella
        </i>
        = "her house" (lit. "the house of her")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         la casa de ellos
        </i>
        = "their house" (lit. "the house of them" i.e.: the house has more than one owner, and at least one of them is a man)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         la casa de usted
        </i>
        = "your house" (lit. "the house of you" (one possessor))
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Or even:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         su casa de usted
        </i>
        = "your house" (lit. "your house of you" (one possessor))
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         su casa de ustedes
        </i>
        = "your house" (lit. "your house of you" (more than one possessor))
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        There is no distinction according to the number (or gender) of possessors for the third person possessives (i.e. between "his/her/its" and "their").
       </li>
       <li>
        The possessive for
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ustedes
        </i>
        is
        <i>
         su(s)
        </i>
        as for other third-person pronouns. The ambiguity that this causes (especially considering that
        <i>
         su(s)
        </i>
        already covers "his", "her", "its" and "their") can be allieviated by treating
        <i>
         usted(es)
        </i>
        as a noun and thereby saying
        <i>
         la casa de ustedes
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         su casa
        </i>
        . It is also possible to disambiguate by saying
        <i>
         la casa de él
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         la casa de ella
        </i>
        , etc.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Dialectal variation:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The archaic pronoun
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        has the possessive form
        <i>
         vuestro
        </i>
        , just like
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        does. However, in modern dialectal
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         tu
        </i>
        is the possessive corresponding to
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        . Therefore, an Argentinian would say
        <i>
         Che, decime tu dirección
        </i>
        and never
        <i>
         decime vuestra dirección
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         dime tu dirección
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Dialectally,
        <i>
         usted/ustedes
        </i>
        may replace
        <i>
         tú/vosotros
        </i>
        without any intention to be formal. The corresponding possessive determiner
        <i>
         su(s)
        </i>
        is used. Therefore, a Colombian may say
        <i>
         Hijo, enséñeme sus deberes
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         Hijo, enséñame tus deberes
        </i>
        ("Son, show me your homework").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Combining_demonstratives_and_possessives">
        Combining demonstratives and possessives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Combining demonstratives and possessives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Demonstrative pronouns can be combined with possessives as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta
         <b>
          nuestra
         </b>
         tierra
        </i>
        = "This Earth of ours"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este
         <b>
          mi
         </b>
         amor
        </i>
        = "This love of mine"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Strictly speaking, the presence of the first determiner means that the possessive must be interpreted as an adjective rather than a determiner. Note however that the long adjectival form (
       <i>
        mío
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tuyo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        suyo
       </i>
       , etc.), which is identical to the corresponding
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns#Genitive_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        possessive pronoun
       </a>
       , is not used in this construction, which is rather uncommon.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is also possible to use the long adjectival form. In this case, it goes after the noun:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta tierra
         <b>
          nuestra
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This Earth of ours"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este amor
         <b>
          mío
         </b>
        </i>
        = "This love of mine"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Miscellaneous_determiners">
        Miscellaneous determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_determiners&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Miscellaneous determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are many more words that can be used as determiners in Spanish. They mostly end in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       and have the usual four forms (
       <i>
        -o, -a, -os, -as
       </i>
       ) to agree with their noun.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡
         <b>
          Otra
         </b>
         cerveza, por favor!
        </i>
        = "Another beer, please!"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Mucha
         </b>
         gente pasa por aquí
        </i>
        = "Many people pass through here"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No hay
         <b>
          tanta
         </b>
         gente como en verano
        </i>
        = "There are not as many people as in summer"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Ciertos
         </b>
         vinos son muy dulces
        </i>
        = "Certain wines are very sweet"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         He salido con
         <b>
          varias
         </b>
         chicas
        </i>
        = "I have gone out with several girls"
       </li>
      </ul>
