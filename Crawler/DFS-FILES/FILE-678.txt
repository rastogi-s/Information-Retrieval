      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        Spanish dialectology
       </a>
       , the terms
       <b>
        <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distinci%C3%B3n#Spanish" title="wikt:distinción">
          distinción
         </a>
        </i>
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/seseo#Spanish" title="wikt:seseo">
          seseo
         </a>
        </i>
       </b>
       , and
       <b>
        <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ceceo#Spanish" title="wikt:ceceo">
          ceceo
         </a>
        </i>
       </b>
       are used to describe the opposition between dialects that distinguish the
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       ), and those that exhibit
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merger_(phonology)" title="Merger (phonology)">
        merger
       </a>
       of the two sounds (
       <i>
        neutralización
       </i>
       ) into either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       ) or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_corono-dentoalveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless corono-dentoalveolar sibilant">
         s̄
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , a sibilant fricative similar to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Dialects that distinguish the two sounds, and thus pronounce the words
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/casa#Spanish" title="wikt:casa">
         casa
        </a>
       </i>
       ('house') and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caza#Spanish" title="wikt:caza">
         caza
        </a>
       </i>
       ('hunt') differently, are described as having
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       , whereas the dialects that lack this distinction and pronounce the two words as
       <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
        homophones
       </a>
       are described as having
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       if both words are pronounced with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       or
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       if both words are pronounced with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_corono-dentoalveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless corono-dentoalveolar sibilant">
         s̄
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Seseo
       </i>
       is typical of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        American
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian dialects
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Spanish" title="Philippine Spanish">
        Philippine
       </a>
       and some dialects of central
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       , whereas
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       is typical of most dialects in Spain, except in much of Andalusia
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and the Canary Islands.
       <i>
        Ceceo
       </i>
       is found in some dialects of Spain, in the southernmost part of Andalusia.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distinction">
        Distinction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distinction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Distinction (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         distinción
        </span>
       </i>
       ) refers to the differentiated pronunciation of the two Spanish phonemes written ⟨s⟩ and ⟨z⟩ or ⟨c⟩ (only before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩, the so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_C" title="Hard and soft C">
        "soft" ⟨c⟩
       </a>
       ):
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        ⟨s⟩ represents a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant">
         voiceless alveolar sibilant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        (either
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminal
        </a>
        as in English, or
        <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
         apical
        </a>
        );
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨z⟩ and soft ⟨c⟩ represent a
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         voiceless dental fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        (the ⟨th⟩ in
        <i>
         think
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       This pronunciation is the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard
       </a>
       on which Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       was based, and it is universal in Central and Northern parts of Spain, except for some bilingual speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       , according to
       <a href="#CITEREFHualde2005">
        Hualde (2005)
       </a>
       . Thus, in Spanish the choice between the spellings ⟨sa⟩, ⟨se⟩, ⟨si⟩, ⟨so⟩, ⟨su⟩ and ⟨za⟩, ⟨ce⟩, ⟨ci⟩, ⟨zo⟩, ⟨zu⟩ is determined by the pronunciation in most of Spain, unlike English, where it is often done according to
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       or orthographic
       <a href="/wiki/Convention_(norm)" title="Convention (norm)">
        conventions
       </a>
       (although in English, soft c is always
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and never
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       like s is, as with 'rise' vs. 'rice').
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mergers">
        Mergers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mergers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In many other Spanish-speaking regions and countries, however, the phonemic distinction between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       has been neutralized or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_differentiation#Phonemic_mergers" title="Phonemic differentiation">
        merged
       </a>
       . These varieties of Spanish are sometimes said to exhibit
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/neutralizaci%C3%B3n#Spanish" title="wikt:neutralización">
         neutralización
        </a>
       </i>
       ('neutralization') as opposed to
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/distinci%C3%B3n#Spanish" title="wikt:distinción">
         distinción
        </a>
       </i>
       . In this case, their pronunciation may or may not coincide with the English pronunciation.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ceceo">
        Ceceo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ceceo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Ceceo
       </i>
       is a phenomenon found in a few dialects of southern Spain in which the historical phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       are both realized as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_corono-dentoalveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless corono-dentoalveolar sibilant">
         s̄
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , a sibilant sounding somewhat like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       , but not identical.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Ceceo
       </i>
       is found primarily in some varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       , although Hualde reports that there is some evidence of it in parts of Central America. A publication of the University of Oviedo also notes that the ceceo can be found in Argentina and Chile.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other linguists have noticed the use of ceceo in parts of Puerto Rico, Honduras, and Venezuela.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is a largely rural pronunciation and is often
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        stigmatized
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Seseo">
        Seseo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Seseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Seseo
       </i>
       is the merger in the opposite direction: the original phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       are both pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       . For example, the words
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/casa#Spanish" title="wikt:casa">
         casa
        </a>
       </i>
       ('house') and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/caza#Spanish" title="wikt:caza">
         caza
        </a>
       </i>
       ('hunt') would be pronounced with the same
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       sound. This rentention of the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       can result in ambiguity but can usually be interpreted depending on the context of which the sentence is spoken.
       <i>
        Seseo
       </i>
       is the most widespread pronunciation among Spanish speakers worldwide (around 90% of native Spanish speakers) and occurs in nearly all speakers in
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       . While it is a minority pronunciation in Spain itself,
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       is considered standard in all varieties of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_Spanish" title="Latin American Spanish">
        Latin American Spanish
       </a>
       . It coexists with
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       in parts of Spain (e.g. in Canary Islands and in some areas of Andalusia). Traditional dialect atlases (e.g.,
       <a href="#CITEREFAlvar1991">
        Alvar (1991)
       </a>
       ) show one variant or another used in adjacent regions. In Spain,
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       is considered "more socially acceptable or perhaps 'less substandard' than
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The following table gives an example of the three pronunciation patterns discussed so far:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable" style="text-align:center; margin: 1em auto auto 2em">
       <tr>
        <th>
        </th>
        <th>
         <i>
          la casa
         </i>
         ('the house')
        </th>
        <th>
         <i>
          la caza
         </i>
         ('the hunt')
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         distinción
        </th>
        <td>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /la ˈkasa/
         </span>
        </td>
        <td>
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /la ˈkaθa/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         ceceo
        </th>
        <td colspan="2">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /la ˈkas̄a/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         seseo
        </th>
        <td colspan="2">
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /la ˈkasa/
         </span>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ceseo_or_seceo">
        Ceseo or seceo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Ceseo or seceo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many speakers of
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       dialects in Spain show
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics#Sociolinguistic_variables" title="Sociolinguistics">
        sociolinguistic variation
       </a>
       in usage. In some cases, this variation may arise when a
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       speaker more or less consciously attempts to use
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       in response to sociolinguistic pressure (
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       ). However, as, for instance, in the case of the variation between the standard velar nasal and alveolar pronunciation of the nasal in
       <i>
        -ing
       </i>
       in English (
       <i>
        walking
       </i>
       versus
       <i>
        walkin'
       </i>
       ), the switching may be entirely unconscious. It is perhaps evidence of the saliency of three-way
       <i>
        ceceo-seseo-distinción
       </i>
       variation that inconsistent use has elicited evaluative comments by some traditional Spanish dialectologists. For instance,
       <a href="#CITEREFDalbor1980">
        Dalbor (1980)
       </a>
       discussed it as "sporadic or chaotic switching [between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       ] and the use of intermediate sounds impossible to determine with precision".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="#CITEREFObaid1973">
        Obaid (1973)
       </a>
       proposes the synonymous terms
       <b>
        ceseo
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        seceo
       </b>
       to refer to these "mixed" patterns, and notes surprise at a speaker who produced all four possible pronunciations of
       <i>
        Zaragoza
       </i>
       within the space of a few minutes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact, sociolinguistic variation is typically highly structured in terms of how often each variant will appear given various social and linguistic independent variables. The Spanish spoken by the inhabitants of the Canary Islands is exclusively seseante, but exclusive seseo is quite rare in mainland Spain – even in areas, such as Seville, listed as being majority seseante.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Castilian_.27lisp.27">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Castilian_'lisp'">
        Castilian 'lisp'
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Castilian 'lisp'">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A persistent
       <a href="/wiki/Urban_legend" title="Urban legend">
        urban legend
       </a>
       claims that the prevalence of the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       in Spanish can be traced back to a Spanish king who spoke with a
       <a href="/wiki/Lisp" title="Lisp">
        lisp
       </a>
       , and whose pronunciation spread by
       <a href="/wiki/Sociolinguistics#Class_aspiration" title="Sociolinguistics">
        prestige borrowing
       </a>
       to the rest of the population. This myth has been discredited by scholars for lack of evidence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="#CITEREFLundeberg1947">
        Lundeberg (1947)
       </a>
       traces the origins of the legend back to a chronicle of
       <a href="/wiki/Pero_L%C3%B3pez_de_Ayala" title="Pero López de Ayala">
        Pero López de Ayala
       </a>
       stating that
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Castile" title="Peter of Castile">
        Peter of Castile
       </a>
       "lisped a little" ("
       <i>
        ceceaba un poco
       </i>
       ") however he reigned in the 14th century and the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       began to develop in the 16th century (see
       <a href="#Historical_evolution">
        below
       </a>
       ). Moreover, it is clear that a true
       <a href="/wiki/Lisp" title="Lisp">
        lisp
       </a>
       would not give rise to the systematic distinction between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       that characterizes Standard Peninsular pronunciation. For example, a lisp would lead one to pronounce both
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/siento#Spanish" title="wikt:siento">
         siento
        </a>
       </i>
       ('I feel') and
       <i lang="es" xml:lang="es">
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/ciento#Spanish" title="wikt:ciento">
         ciento
        </a>
       </i>
       ('hundred') the same (as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θjento]
       </span>
       ) whereas in Standard Peninsular Spanish they are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈsjento]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈθjento]
       </span>
       , respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       For native speakers of
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       varieties of Spanish, in which
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       is absent, the presence of this phoneme in European speakers does not appear strange. However, it might be striking for learners of Spanish in North America, where people are more familiar with
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       pronunciation, and indeed gives an impression of "lispiness". The
       <a href="/wiki/Misnomer" title="Misnomer">
        misnomer
       </a>
       "Castilian lisp" is used occasionally to refer to this aspect of Peninsular pronunciation (in both
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       varieties).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_evolution">
        Historical evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phonological_history_of_Spanish_coronal_fricatives&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Historical evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the 15th century, Spanish had developed a large number of
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilant
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       : seven by some accounts,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       eight by others
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (depending on whether
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       are considered contrasting)—in either case, more than any present-day variety of the language. During the 16th and early 17th centuries these phonemes merged differently as they evolved into those of the different modern dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris1969a_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris1969a-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There were four pairs of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless" title="Voiceless">
        voiceless
       </a>
       versus
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiced
       </a>
       sibilants:
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricates
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s/
       </span>
       vs.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡z/
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨c⟩ or ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%87" title="Ç">
        ç
       </a>
       ⟩ vs. ⟨z⟩); dental/alveolar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricatives
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨ss⟩ when intervocalic, ⟨s⟩ otherwise) vs.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z/
       </span>
       (intervocalic only, spelled ⟨s⟩);
       <a href="/wiki/Postalveolar_consonant" title="Postalveolar consonant">
        postalveolar
       </a>
       affricates
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡ʃ/
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨ch⟩ vs.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       ; and postalveolar fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       (spelled ⟨x⟩) vs.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       . Both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       were spelled
       <a href="/wiki/Hard_and_soft_G" title="Hard and soft G">
        ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩
       </a>
       ), and ⟨j⟩ elsewhere. It is likely that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       deaffricated and merged with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       before the year 1500.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main difference between the prestige dialect of north central Spain and dialects to the south (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       ) was that, in the north, the dental/alveolar continuants were more retracted than the affricates (the former pair can be represented as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̺/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̺/
       </span>
       and the latter as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s̪/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡z̪/
       </span>
       ), keeping their phonemic distinction, while in the south they were homorganic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first step away from that system was the deaffrication of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡z̪/
       </span>
       in the first quarter of the 16th century. Because of a differing
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       , this still contrasted with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̺/
       </span>
       in the prestige dialect of north central Spain, though it was a complete merger for southern dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris1969_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris1969-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The second step was the
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_voicing_and_devoicing" title="Consonant voicing and devoicing">
        devoicing
       </a>
       of voiced sibilants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris1969_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris1969-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the north,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̺/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       were lost, though
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /z̪/
       </span>
       remained contrastive as there had been no voiceless
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̪/
       </span>
       . This sound contrasted with two acoustically similar sounds: dentoalveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s̪/
       </span>
       and apicoalveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̺/
       </span>
       . By 1600,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s̪/
       </span>
       had deaffricated and merged with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s̪/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris1969_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris1969-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Subsequent changes to the sound system of Spanish retained the contrasts while enhancing the segments by increasing articulatory distance amongst their rather subtle acoustic contrasts, an appropriate step due to the high productivity of these phonemes in differentiating frequently used
       <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
        minimal pairs
       </a>
       . The dentoalveolar one was moved "forward" to interdental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ̟/
       </span>
       , losing its former sibilance in the process (which increased its acoustic distance to the remaining sibilant ⟨s⟩), and the prepalatal one was moved "backward" to
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       also losing its former sibilance. All in all resulting in the three-way distinction found in modern Standard Peninsular pronunciation:

      <p>
       In the south, the devoicing process and deaffrication of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡s/
       </span>
       gave rise to new fricatives that were indistinguishable from the existing ones. The process of increasing articulatory distance still applied, however, and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       retracted to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       in the south just as it did in the north.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harris1969a_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harris1969a-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a number of
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       areas (particularly the southernmost provinces like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz_(province)" title="Cádiz (province)">
        Cádiz
       </a>
       )
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       developed into a non-sibilant apico-dental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ̺]
       </span>
       , perceptually similar to the interdental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ̟/
       </span>
       used by Standard Peninsular speakers for orthographic ⟨c⟩/⟨z⟩. In
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       areas (particularly in the westernmost provinces like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seville_(province)" title="Seville (province)">
        Seville
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huelva_(province)" title="Huelva (province)">
        Huelva
       </a>
       ), the resulting phoneme developed a predorsal alveolar realization
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̻]
       </span>
       (like English ⟨s⟩), perceptually similar to the apicoalveolar
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̺]
       </span>
       used by Standard Peninsular speakers for orthographic ⟨s⟩. This
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       variety was the pronunciation that most impacted Latin America, as many emigrants to the Americas were from Andalusian and Canarian ports. In addition, several generations of Spanish speakers had lived and grown in the Americas before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       appeared in Castilian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </td>
        <td>
         ⟨j⟩ or ⟨g⟩ (before ⟨e⟩, ⟨i⟩)
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The development of the sibilants in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
        Ladino
       </a>
       (which split off from Castilian and other Peninsular varieties in the 15th century) was more conservative, resulting in a system closer to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
