      <p>
       The
       <b>
        geoid
       </b>
       is the shape that the surface of the oceans would take under the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth" title="Gravity of Earth">
        Earth's gravity
       </a>
       and rotation alone, in the absence of other influences such as winds and tides. This surface is extended through the continents (such as with very narrow hypothetical canals). All points on a geoid surface have the same
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_potential" title="Effective potential">
        effective potential
       </a>
       (the sum of
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravitational
       </a>
       potential energy and centrifugal potential energy). The geoid can be defined at any value of gravitational potential such as within the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's crust or far out in space, not just at
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       . The force of gravity acts everywhere perpendicular to the geoid, meaning that
       <a href="/wiki/Plumb_bob" title="Plumb bob">
        plumb lines
       </a>
       point perpendicular and
       <a href="/wiki/Spirit_level" title="Spirit level">
        water levels
       </a>
       parallel to the geoid if only gravity and rotational acceleration were at work.
      </p>
      <p>
       Specifically, the geoid is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equipotential_surface" title="Equipotential surface">
        equipotential surface
       </a>
       that would coincide with the mean ocean surface of Earth if the oceans and atmosphere were in equilibrium, at rest relative to the rotating Earth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conrad_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-conrad-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and extended through the continents (such as with very narrow canals). According to
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">
        Gauss
       </a>
       , who first described it, it is the "mathematical figure of Earth", a smooth but highly irregular surface whose shape results from the uneven distribution of mass within and on the surface of Earth. It does not correspond to the actual surface of Earth's crust, but to a surface which can only be known through extensive gravitational measurements and calculations. Despite being an important concept for almost two hundred years in the history of
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
        geophysics
       </a>
       , it has only been defined to high precision since advances in
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_geodesy" title="Satellite geodesy">
        satellite geodesy
       </a>
       in the late 20th century. It is often described as the true physical
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        figure of the Earth
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-conrad_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-conrad-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in contrast to the idealized geometrical figure of a
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The surface of the geoid is higher than the reference ellipsoid wherever there is a positive gravity anomaly (mass excess) and lower than the reference ellipsoid wherever there is a negative gravity anomaly (mass deficit).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1166" data-file-width="1686" height="152" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png/220px-Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png/330px-Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png/440px-Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geoid_height_red_blue_averagebw.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of the
         <a href="/wiki/Undulation_of_the_geoid" title="Undulation of the geoid">
          undulation of the geoid
         </a>
         , in meters (based on the
         <a href="/wiki/EGM96" title="EGM96">
          EGM96
         </a>
         gravity model and the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
          WGS84
         </a>
         reference ellipsoid).
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
          <a href="#cite_note-3">
           [3]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geoida.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="338" data-file-width="730" height="102" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Geoida.svg/220px-Geoida.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Geoida.svg/330px-Geoida.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Geoida.svg/440px-Geoida.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geoida.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1. Ocean
         <br/>
         2.
         <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
          Reference ellipsoid
         </a>
         <br/>
         3. Local
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumb-bob" title="Plumb-bob">
          plumb line
         </a>
         <br/>
         4. Continent
         <br/>
         5. Geoid
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The geoid surface is irregular, unlike the
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       which is a mathematical idealized representation of the physical Earth, but considerably smoother than Earth's physical surface. Although the physical Earth has excursions of +8,848 m (
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">
        Mount Everest
       </a>
       ) and −429 m (
       <a href="/wiki/Dead_Sea" title="Dead Sea">
        Dead Sea
       </a>
       ), the geoid's variation ranges from −106 to +85 m, less than 200 m total
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       compared to a perfect mathematical ellipsoid.
      </p>
      <p>
       If the ocean surface were
       <a href="/wiki/Isopycnic" title="Isopycnic">
        isopycnic
       </a>
       (of constant density) and undisturbed by tides, currents, or weather, it would closely approximate the geoid. The permanent deviation between the geoid and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
        mean sea level
       </a>
       is called
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_surface_topography" title="Ocean surface topography">
        ocean surface topography
       </a>
       . If the continental land masses were criss-crossed by a series of tunnels or canals, the sea level in these canals would also very nearly coincide with the geoid. In reality the geoid does not have a physical meaning under the continents, but
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodesist" title="Geodesist">
        geodesists
       </a>
       are able to derive the heights of continental points above this imaginary, yet physically defined, surface by a technique called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spirit_leveling" title="Spirit leveling">
        spirit leveling
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Being an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equipotential_surface" title="Equipotential surface">
        equipotential surface
       </a>
       , the geoid is by definition a surface to which the force of gravity is everywhere perpendicular. This means that when traveling by ship, one does not notice the undulations of the geoid; the local vertical (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plumb-bob" title="Plumb-bob">
        plumb line
       </a>
       ) is always perpendicular to the geoid and the local horizon
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tangential_component" title="Tangential component">
        tangential
       </a>
       to it. Likewise, spirit levels will always be parallel to the geoid.
      </p>
      <p>
       On a long voyage, spirit leveling indicates height variations even though the ship is always at sea level (neglecting the effects of tides). This is because GPS
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       , orbiting about the center of gravity of the Earth, can only measure heights relative to a geocentric reference ellipsoid. To obtain one's geoidal height, a raw GPS reading must be corrected. Conversely, height determined by spirit leveling from a tidal measurement station, as in traditional land surveying, will always be geoidal height. Modern GPS receivers have a grid implemented inside where they obtain the geoid (e.g. EGM-96) height over the
       <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">
        World Geodetic System
       </a>
       (WGS) ellipsoid from the current position. Then they are able to correct the height above WGS ellipsoid to the height above
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       geoid. When height is not zero on a ship, the discrepancy is due to other factors such as ocean tides, atmospheric pressure (meteorological effects) and local
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_surface_topography" title="Sea surface topography">
        sea surface topography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Simplified_example">
        Simplified example
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Simplified example">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The gravitational field of the earth is neither perfect nor uniform. A flattened ellipsoid is typically used as the idealized earth, but even if the earth were perfectly spherical, the strength of gravity would not be the same everywhere, because density (and therefore mass) varies throughout the planet. This is due to magma distributions, mountain ranges, deep sea trenches, and so on.
      </p>
      <p>
       If that perfect sphere were then covered in water, the water would not be the same height everywhere. Instead, the water level would be higher or lower depending on the particular strength of gravity in that location.
      </p>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gravity,_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1132" data-file-width="2202" height="113" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif/220px-Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif/330px-Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif/440px-Gravity%2C_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gravity,_geoid_anomaly_synthetic_cases_with_local_isostasy_2.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Gravity_anomaly" title="Gravity anomaly">
          Gravity
         </a>
         and Geoid anomalies caused by various crustal and lithospheric thickness changes relative to a reference configuration. All settings are under local
         <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
          isostatic
         </a>
         compensation.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Variations in the height of the geoidal surface are related to density anomalous distributions within the Earth. Geoid measures help thus to understand the internal structure of the planet. Synthetic calculations show that the geoidal signature of a thickened crust (for example, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orogen" title="Orogen">
        orogenic belts
       </a>
       produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">
        continental collision
       </a>
       ) is positive, opposite to what should be expected if the thickening affects the entire
       <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
        lithosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Time-variability">
        Time-variability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Time-variability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Recent satellite missions, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Field_and_Steady-State_Ocean_Circulation_Explorer" title="Gravity Field and Steady-State Ocean Circulation Explorer">
        GOCE
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_Recovery_and_Climate_Experiment" title="Gravity Recovery and Climate Experiment">
        GRACE
       </a>
       , have enabled the study of time-variable geoid signals. The first products based on GOCE satellite data became available online in June 2010, through the European Space Agency (ESA)’s Earth observation user services tools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ESA launched the satellite in March 2009 on a mission to map Earth's gravity with unprecedented accuracy and spatial resolution. On 31 March 2011, the new geoid model was unveiled at the Fourth International GOCE User Workshop hosted at the Technische Universität München in Munich, Germany.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Studies using the time-variable geoid computed from GRACE data have provided information on global hydrologic cycles,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       mass balances of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_sheet" title="Ice sheet">
        ice sheets
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postglacial_rebound" title="Postglacial rebound">
        postglacial rebound
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From postglacial rebound measurements, time-variable GRACE data can be used to deduce the
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscosity
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle" title="Earth's mantle">
        Earth's mantle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The concept of the geoid has been extended to other
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       and also
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moons
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The geoid of Mars has been measured using flight paths of satellite missions such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mariner_9" title="Mariner 9">
        Mariner 9
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Viking_program" title="Viking program">
        Viking
       </a>
       . The main departures from the ellipsoid expected of an ideal fluid are from the
       <a href="/wiki/Tharsis" title="Tharsis">
        Tharsis
       </a>
       volcanic plateau, a continent-size region of elevated terrain, and its antipodes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cattermole_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cattermole-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
