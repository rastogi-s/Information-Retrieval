      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lateral
       </b>
       is an
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       -like
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonant
       </a>
       in which the
       <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
        airstream
       </a>
       proceeds along the sides of the tongue, but it is blocked by the tongue from going through the middle of the mouth.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the most common laterals, the tip of the tongue makes contact with the upper teeth (see
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental consonant
       </a>
       ) or the upper gum (see
       <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_consonant" title="Alveolar consonant">
        alveolar consonant
       </a>
       ), but there are many other possible places for laterals to be made. The most common laterals are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approximant" title="Approximant">
        approximants
       </a>
       and belong to the class of
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid_consonant" title="Liquid consonant">
        liquids
       </a>
       , but lateral
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricatives
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricates
       </a>
       are also common in some parts of the world. Some languages, such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iwaidja" title="Iwaidja">
        Iwaidja
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ilgar" title="Ilgar">
        Ilgar
       </a>
       languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       , have
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_flap" title="Lateral flap">
        lateral flaps
       </a>
       , and others, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">
        Xhosa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">
        Zulu
       </a>
       languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , have
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_clicks" title="Lateral clicks">
        lateral clicks
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When pronouncing the
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodental
       </a>
       fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f] and [v]
       </span>
       , the lip blocks the airflow in the centre of the vocal tract, so the airstream proceeds along the sides instead. Nevertheless, they are not considered lateral consonants because the airflow never goes over the tongue. This is why no language makes a distinction between lateral and non-lateral labiodentals.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosive_consonant" title="Plosive consonant">
        Plosives
       </a>
       are never lateral, but they may have
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_release_(phonetics)" title="Lateral release (phonetics)">
        lateral release
       </a>
       . Normally,
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasals
       </a>
       are never lateral, but in some languages
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_nasal_click" title="Lateral nasal click">
        lateral nasal clicks
       </a>
       can occur. For consonants articulated in the throat (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laryngeal_consonants" title="Laryngeal consonants">
        laryngeals
       </a>
       ), the lateral distinction is meaningless, because there is no airflow over the tongue at these
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Places_of_articulation" title="Places of articulation">
        places of articulation
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        </li>

      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lateral_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       English has one lateral phoneme: the lateral approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       , which in many accents has two
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophones
       </a>
       . One, found before vowels as in
       <i>
        lady
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        fly
       </i>
       , is called
       <i>
        clear l
       </i>
       , pronounced as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
        alveolar lateral approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       with a "neutral" position of the body of the tongue. The other variant, so-called
       <i>
        dark l
       </i>
       found before consonants or word-finally, as in
       <i>
        bold
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        tell
       </i>
       , is pronounced as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velarized_alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Velarized alveolar lateral approximant">
        velarized alveolar lateral approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɫ]
       </span>
       with the tongue assuming a spoon-like shape with its back part raised, which gives the sound a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       - or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʟ]
       </span>
       -like resonance. In some languages, like
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       , those two sounds are different phonemes.
       <a href="/wiki/East_Slavic_languages" title="East Slavic languages">
        East Slavic languages
       </a>
       contrast
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɫ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [lʲ]
       </span>
       but do not have [l].
      </p>
      <p>
       In many British accents (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Cockney#Cockney_speech" title="Cockney">
        Cockney
       </a>
       ), dark
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɫ]
       </span>
       may undergo
       <a href="/wiki/L-vocalization" title="L-vocalization">
        vocalization
       </a>
       through the reduction and loss of contact between the tip of the tongue and the alveolar ridge, becoming a rounded back vowel or glide. This process turns
       <i>
        tell
       </i>
       into something like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tɛɰ]
       </span>
       , as must have happened with
       <i>
        talk
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tɔːk]
       </span>
       or
       <i>
        walk
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [wɔːk]
       </span>
       at some stage. A similar process happened during the development of many other languages, including
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       , in all three of these resulting in
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɰ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [w]
       </span>
       , whence
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_French" title="Modern French">
        Modern French
       </a>
       <i>
        sauce
       </i>
       as compared with
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <i>
        salsa
       </i>
       , or Polish
       <i>
        Wisła
       </i>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [viswa]
       </span>
       ) as compared with English
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vistula" title="Vistula">
         Vistula
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In central and Venice dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       , intervocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       has turned into a semivocalic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̯]
       </span>
       , so that the written word
       <i>
        ła bała
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [abae̯a]
       </span>
       . The orthography uses the letter
       <a href="/wiki/%C5%81" title="Ł">
        ł
       </a>
       to represent this phoneme (it specifically represents not the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̯]
       </span>
       sound but the phoneme that is, in some dialects,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̯]
       </span>
       and, in others,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many aboriginal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Australian_languages" title="Australian languages">
        Australian languages
       </a>
       have a series of three or four lateral approximants, as do various dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       . Rarer lateral consonants include the retroflex laterals that can be found in many
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_India" title="Languages of India">
        languages of India
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and in some
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_dialects" title="Swedish dialects">
        Swedish dialects
       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral fricative">
        voiceless alveolar lateral fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɬ/
       </span>
       , found in many
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Native North American languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_phonology" title="Welsh phonology">
        Welsh
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">
        Zulu
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Adyghe_language" title="Adyghe language">
        Adyghe
       </a>
       and some
       <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">
        Athabaskan languages
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/H%C3%A4n_language" title="Hän language">
        Hän
       </a>
       , both voiceless and voiced alveolar lateral fricatives occur, but there is no approximant. Many of these languages also have lateral
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affricate" title="Affricate">
        affricates
       </a>
       . Some languages have palatal or velar voiceless lateral fricatives or affricates, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Dahalo_language" title="Dahalo language">
        Dahalo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Zulu_language" title="Zulu language">
        Zulu
       </a>
       , but the IPA has no symbols for such sounds. However, appropriate symbols are easy to make by adding a lateral-fricative belt to the symbol for the corresponding lateral approximant (see below). Also, a devoicing diacritic may be added to the approximant.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nearly all languages with such lateral obstruents also have the approximant. However, there are a number of exceptions, many of them located in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Northwest" title="Pacific Northwest">
        Pacific Northwest
       </a>
       area of the United States. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">
        Tlingit
       </a>
       has
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tɬ, tɬʰ, tɬʼ, ɬ, ɬʼ/
       </span>
       but no
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other examples from the same area include
       <a href="/wiki/Nuu-chah-nulth_language" title="Nuu-chah-nulth language">
        Nuu-chah-nulth
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kutenai_language" title="Kutenai language">
        Kutenai
       </a>
       , and elsewhere,
       <a href="/wiki/Chukchi_language" title="Chukchi language">
        Chukchi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kabardian_language" title="Kabardian language">
        Kabardian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Tibetan" title="Standard Tibetan">
        Standard Tibetan
       </a>
       has a voiceless lateral approximant, usually romanized as
       <i>
        lh
       </i>
       , as in the name
       <a href="/wiki/Lhasa_(prefecture-level_city)" title="Lhasa (prefecture-level city)">
        Lhasa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_lateral_approximant" title="Uvular lateral approximant">
        uvular lateral approximant
       </a>
       has been reported to occur in some speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
        Pashto
       </a>
       has a
       <a href="/wiki/Retroflex_lateral_flap" title="Retroflex lateral flap">
        retroflex lateral flap
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are a large number of lateral
       <a href="/wiki/Click_consonant" title="Click consonant">
        click consonants
       </a>
       ; 17 occur in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/!X%C3%B3%C3%B5" title="!Xóõ">
        !Xóõ
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lateral
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trills
       </a>
       are also possible, but they do not occur in any known language. They may be pronounced by initiating
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɬ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɮ]
       </span>
       with an especially forceful airflow. There is no symbol for them in the IPA. They are sometimes used to imitate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bird_call" title="Bird call">
        bird calls
       </a>
       , and they are a component of
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Duck_talk" title="Donald Duck talk">
        Donald Duck talk
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguous_centrality">
        Ambiguous centrality
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lateral_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Ambiguous centrality">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The IPA requires sounds to be defined as to centrality, as either central or lateral. However, languages may be ambiguous as to some consonants' laterality. Transcribing them as either central or lateral is therefore misleading.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996243_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadefogedMaddieson1996243-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lateralized_consonants">
        Lateralized consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lateral_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lateralized consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A superscript ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˡ
       </span>
       ⟩ is defined as
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_release_(phonetics)" title="Lateral release (phonetics)">
        lateral release
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Consonants may also be pronounced with simultaneous lateral and central airflow. This is well-known from speech pathology with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lateral_lisp" title="Lateral lisp">
        lateral lisp
       </a>
       . However, it also occurs in nondisordered speech in some southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_dialects" title="Arabic dialects">
        Arabic dialects
       </a>
       and possibly some
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_South_Arabian_languages" title="Modern South Arabian languages">
        Modern South Arabian languages
       </a>
       , which have pharyngealized nonsibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʪˤ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʫˤ/
       </span>
       (simultaneous
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ͜ɬˤ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð͡ɮˤ]
       </span>
       ) and possibly a sibilant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʪ/
       </span>
       (simultaneous
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s͜ɬ]
       </span>
       ). Examples are
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θˡˤaim/
       </span>
       'pain' in the dialect of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Al-Rubu%27ah&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Al-Rubu'ah (page does not exist)">
        Al-Rubu'ah
       </a>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ðˡˤahr/
       </span>
       'back' and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ðˡˤabʕ/
       </span>
       'hyena' in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rijal_Alma%27&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rijal Alma' (page does not exist)">
        Rijal Alma'
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (Here the ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ˡ
       </span>
       ⟩ indicates simultaneous laterality rather than lateral release.)
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Arabic" title="Old Arabic">
        Old Arabic
       </a>
       has been analyzed as having the emphatic central–lateral fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ͜ɬˤ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð͡ɮˤ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ͡ɬˤ]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
