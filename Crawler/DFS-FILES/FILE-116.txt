       ) is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       now used to define the
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodetic
       </a>
       network in North America. A datum is a formal description of the shape of the Earth along with an "anchor" point for the coordinate system. In
       <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
        surveying
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Land-use_planning" title="Land-use planning">
        land-use planning
       </a>
       , two North American Datums are in use: the North American Datum of 1927 (NAD 27) and the North American Datum of 1983 (NAD 83). Both are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_reference_system" title="Geodetic reference system">
        geodetic reference systems
       </a>
       based on slightly different assumptions and measurements.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_North_American_Datum">
        First North American Datum
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: First North American Datum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1901 the
       <a href="/wiki/U.S._National_Geodetic_Survey" title="U.S. National Geodetic Survey">
        United States Coast and Geodetic Survey
       </a>
       adopted a national horizontal datum called the United States Standard Datum, based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ross_Clarke" title="Alexander Ross Clarke">
        Clarke
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid#Historical_Earth_ellipsoids" title="Earth ellipsoid">
        Ellipsoid
       </a>
       of 1866. It was fitted to data previously collected for regional datums, which by that time had begun to overlap. In 1913, Canada and Mexico adopted that datum, so it was also renamed the North American Datum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_Datum_of_1927">
        North American Datum of 1927
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: North American Datum of 1927">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As more data were gathered, discrepancies appeared, so the datum was recomputed in 1927, using the same spheroid and origin as its predecessor.
      </p>
      <p>
       The North American Datum of 1927 (NAD 27) was based on surveys of the entire continent from a common reference point that was chosen in 1901, because it was as near the center of the contiguous United States as could be calculated: It was based on a
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation_station" title="Triangulation station">
        triangulation station
       </a>
       at the junction of the transcontinental triangulation arc of 1899 on the
       <a href="/wiki/39th_parallel_north" title="39th parallel north">
        39th parallel north
       </a>
       and the triangulation arc along the
       <a href="/wiki/98th_meridian_west" title="98th meridian west">
        98th meridian west
       </a>
       that was near the
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_center_of_the_contiguous_United_States" title="Geographic center of the contiguous United States">
        geographic center of the contiguous United States
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The datum declares the
       <a href="/wiki/Meades_Ranch_Triangulation_Station" title="Meades Ranch Triangulation Station">
        Meades Ranch Triangulation Station
       </a>
       to be 39°13′26.686″ north latitude, 98°32′30.506″ west longitude (
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.ngs.noaa.gov/cgi-bin/ds_mark.prl?PidBox=kg0640" rel="nofollow">
        NGS data
       </a>
       ). NAD 27 is oriented by declaring the
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuth" title="Azimuth">
        azimuth
       </a>
       from Meades Ranch to
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.ngs.noaa.gov/cgi-bin/ds_mark.prl?PidBox=kg0648" rel="nofollow">
        Waldo
       </a>
       to be 255°28′14.52″ from north. The latitude and longitude of every other point in North America is then based on its distance and direction from Meades Ranch: If a point was X meters in azimuth Y degrees from Meades Ranch, measured on the
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ross_Clarke" title="Alexander Ross Clarke">
        Clarke
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid#Historical_Earth_ellipsoids" title="Earth ellipsoid">
        Ellipsoid
       </a>
       of 1866, then its latitude and longitude on that ellipsoid were defined and could be calculated.
      </p>

      </p>
      <p>
       These are the defining dimensions for NAD 27, but Clarke actually defined his 1866
       <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
        spheroid
       </a>
       as
       <i>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">

      </p>
      <p>
       Most
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
        USGS
       </a>
       topographic maps were published in NAD 27 and many major projects by the United States Army Corps of Engineers and other agencies were defined in NAD 27, so the datum remains important, despite more refined datums being available.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_Datum_of_1983">
        North American Datum of 1983
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: North American Datum of 1983">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because Earth deviates significantly from a perfect
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       , the ellipsoid that best approximates its shape varies region by region across the world. Clarke 1866, and North American Datum of 1927 with it, were surveyed to best suit North America as a whole. Likewise, historically, most regions of the world used ellipsoids measured locally to best suit the vagaries of Earth's shape in their respective locales. While ensuring the most accuracy locally, this practice makes integrating and disseminating information across regions troublesome.
      </p>
      <p>
       As
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       geodesy and
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        remote sensing
       </a>
       technology reached high precision and were made available for
       <a href="/wiki/Civilian" title="Civilian">
        civilian
       </a>
       applications, it became feasible to acquire information referred to a single global ellipsoid. This is because satellites naturally deal with Earth as a monolithic body. Therefore, the
       <a href="/wiki/GRS_80" title="GRS 80">
        GRS 80
       </a>
       ellipsoid was developed for best approximating the Earth as a whole, and it became the foundation for the North American Datum of 1983. Though GRS 80 and its close relative,
       <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">
        WGS 84
       </a>
       , are generally not the best fit for any given region, a need for the closest fit largely evaporates when a global survey is combined with computers, databases, and software able to compensate for local conditions.
      </p>
      <table border="1" cellspacing="0" style="text-align:center" width="cellpadding=3">

      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparing_NAD_27_to_NAD_83">
        Comparing NAD 27 to NAD 83
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Comparing NAD 27 to NAD 83">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="581" data-file-width="1115" height="115" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png/220px-Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png/330px-Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png/440px-Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Datum_Shift_Between_NAD27_and_NAD83.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Datum Shift Between NAD 27 and NAD 83
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A point having a given latitude and longitude in NAD 27 may be displaced on the order of many tens of meters from another point having the identical latitude and longitude in NAD 83, so it is important to specify the datum along with the coordinates. The North American Datum of 1927 is defined by the latitude and longitude of an initial point (
       <a href="/wiki/Meades_Ranch_Triangulation_Station" title="Meades Ranch Triangulation Station">
        Meades Ranch Triangulation Station
       </a>
       in Kansas), the direction of a line between this point and a specified second point, and two dimensions that define the spheroid. The North American Datum of 1983 is based on a newer defined spheroid (GRS 80); it is an Earth-centered (or "
       <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_model" title="Geocentric model">
        geocentric
       </a>
       ") datum having no initial point or initial direction.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">
        NOAA
       </a>
       provides a converter between the two systems.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The practical impact is that if you use current GPS device set to work in NAD 83 or WGS 84 to navigate to NAD 27 coordinates (as from a topo map) in Seattle, you would be off by about 95 meters (not far enough west), and you'd be about 47 meters off in Miami (not far enough north-northeast), whereas you would be much closer for points near Chicago.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="North_American_Datum_1983_and_WGS_84">
        North American Datum 1983 and WGS 84
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: North American Datum 1983 and WGS 84">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The initial definition of NAD 83(1986) was intended to match GRS 80 and WGS 84, so many older publications indicate no difference. Subsequent measurements found a difference on the order of a meter over much of the United States. Each datum has undergone refinements with more accurate and later measurements.
      </p>
      <p>
       NAD 83 is defined to remain constant over time for points on the
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_Plate" title="North American Plate">
        North American Plate
       </a>
       , whereas WGS 84 is defined with respect to the average of stations all over the world. Thus the two systems naturally diverge over time. For much of the United States the relative rate is on the order of 1 to 2 cm per year. Hawaii and the coastal portions of central and southern California west of the
       <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">
        San Andreas Fault
       </a>
       are not on the North American plate, so their divergence rate differs.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Latest_version_of_North_American_Datum">
        Latest version of North American Datum
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=North_American_Datum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Latest version of North American Datum">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The United States National Spatial Reference System NAD 83(2011/MA11/PA11) epoch 2010.00, is a refinement of the NAD 83 datum using data from a network of very accurate GPS receivers at Continuously Operating Reference Stations (CORS).
      </p>
