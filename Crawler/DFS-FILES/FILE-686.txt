      <p>
       <b>
        Grammatical person
       </b>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , is the grammatical distinction between
       <a href="/wiki/Deixis" title="Deixis">
        deictic
       </a>
       references to participant(s) in an event; typically the distinction is between the speaker (first person), the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Addressee" title="Addressee">
        addressee
       </a>
       (second person), and others (third person). Put in simple colloquial English, first person is that which includes the speaker, namely, "I," "we," "me," and "us," second person is the person or people to whom are spoken, literally, "you," and third person includes all that is not listed above.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Grammatical person typically defines a language's set of
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       . It also frequently affects
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       , and sometimes
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        nouns
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Genitive_case" title="Genitive case">
        possessive
       </a>
       relationships.
      </p>

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , first-, second-, and third-person pronouns are typically also marked for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        singular
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        plural
       </a>
       forms, and sometimes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_grammatical_number" title="Dual grammatical number">
        dual
       </a>
       form as well (
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        grammatical number
       </a>
       ). Some languages, especially European ones, distinguish degrees of formality and informality (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-V distinction
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Some other languages use different classifying systems, especially in the plural pronouns. One frequently found difference not present in most Indo-European languages is a contrast between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclusive_and_exclusive_we" title="Inclusive and exclusive we">
        inclusive and exclusive "we"
       </a>
       : a distinction of first-person plural pronouns between including or excluding the addressee.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some other languages have much more elaborate systems of formality that go well beyond the T-V distinction, and use many different pronouns and verb forms that express the speaker's relationship with the people they are addressing. Many
       <a href="/wiki/Malayo-Polynesian_languages" title="Malayo-Polynesian languages">
        Malayo-Polynesian languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">
        Javanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Balinese_language" title="Balinese language">
        Balinese
       </a>
       , are well known for their complex systems of
       <a href="/wiki/Honorific" title="Honorific">
        honorifics
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       also have similar systems to a lesser extent.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_on_verbs">
        Effect on verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Effect on verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In many languages, the
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       takes a form dependent on the person of the subject and whether it is singular or plural. In
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , this happens with the verb
       <i>
        to be
       </i>
       as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        I
        <i>
         am
        </i>
        (first-person singular)
       </li>
       <li>
        you
        <i>
         are
        </i>
        /thou
        <i>
         art
        </i>
        (second-person singular)
       </li>
       <li>
        he, she, one or it
        <i>
         is
        </i>
        (third-person singular)
       </li>
       <li>
        we
        <i>
         are
        </i>
        (first-person plural)
       </li>
       <li>
        you
        <i>
         are
        </i>
        /ye
        <i>
         are
        </i>
        (second-person plural)
       </li>
       <li>
        they
        <i>
         are
        </i>
        (third-person plural)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other verbs in English take the suffix
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       to mark the present tense third person singular.
      </p>
      <p>
       In many languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/French_conjugation" title="French conjugation">
        French
       </a>
       , the verb in any given tense takes a different suffix for any of the various combinations of person and number of the subject.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Additional_persons">
        Additional persons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_person&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Additional persons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The grammars of some languages divide the semantic space into more than three persons. The extra categories may be termed
       <i>
        <b>
         fourth person
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         fifth person
        </b>
       </i>
       , etc. Such terms are not absolute but can refer depending on context to any of several phenomena.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Algonquian_languages" title="Algonquian languages">
        Algonquian languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Salishan_languages" title="Salishan languages">
        Salishan languages
       </a>
       divide the category of third person into two parts:
       <i>
        proximate
       </i>
       for a more
       <a href="/wiki/Topic_and_comment" title="Topic and comment">
        topical
       </a>
       third person, and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Obviative" title="Obviative">
         obviative
        </a>
       </i>
       for a less topical third person. The obviative is sometimes called the fourth person.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice#The_fourth_person_in_Baltic-Finnic_languages" title="Grammatical voice">
        fourth person
       </a>
       is also sometimes used for the category of indefinite or generic referents, which work like
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       in English phrases such as "one should be prepared" or
       <i>
        people
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        people say that...
       </i>
       , when the grammar treats them differently from ordinary third-person forms.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The so-called "zero person"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laitinen_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Laitinen-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leinonen_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Leinonen-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       and related languages, in addition to passive
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(grammar)" title="Voice (grammar)">
        voice
       </a>
       may serve to leave the subject-referent open. Zero person subjects are sometimes translated as "one," but the problem with that is that English language constructions involving
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       , e.g. "One hopes that will not happen," are rare and could be considered expressive of an overly academic tone, while Finnish sentences like "Ei saa koskettaa" ("0 cannot touch" or "it is impossible to touch") are recognizable to and used by young children in both languages.
      </p>
