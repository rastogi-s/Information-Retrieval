      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Andalusian varieties of
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
       </b>
       (Spanish:
       <i>
        andaluz
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [andaˈluθ, -ˈlus]
        </a>
       </span>
       ; Andalusian:
       <i>
        andalú
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [ãndaˈluʰ, -ˈlʊ]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) are spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       . They include perhaps the most distinct of the southern variants of
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        peninsular Spanish
       </a>
       , differing in many respects from northern varieties, and also from
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       . Due to the large population of Andalusia, the Andalusian dialects are among the ones with more speakers in Spain. Within Spain, other southern dialects of Spanish share some core elements of Andalusian, mainly in terms of phonetics  –  notably Canarian Spanish, Extremaduran Spanish and Murcian Spanish as well as, to a lesser degree, Manchegan Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to massive emigration from Andalusia to the Spanish colonies in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       and elsewhere, most
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        American Spanish
       </a>
       dialects share some fundamental characteristics with Western Andalusian Spanish, such as the use of
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       for the second person plural, and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       . Many varieties of Spanish, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       and other Latin American Spanish dialects, including their
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard dialects
       </a>
       , are considered by most to be based on Andalusian Spanish.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Andalusian has a number of distinguishing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological" title="Phonological">
        phonological
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic" title="Syntactic">
        syntactic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_semantics" title="Lexical semantics">
        lexical
       </a>
       features. However, not all of these are unique to Andalusian, nor are all of these features found in all areas where Andalusian is spoken, but in any one area, most of these features will be present.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonological_features">
        Phonological features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonological features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="591" height="203" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG/300px-Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG/450px-Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/8a/Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Andaluc%C3%ADa_ceceante_y_seseante.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Areas of Andalusia in which
         <i>
          seseo
         </i>
         (green),
         <i>
          ceceo
         </i>
         (red), or the distinction of
         <i>
          c
         </i>
         /
         <i>
          z
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          s
         </i>
         (white) predominate. Note that the city of
         <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">
          Cádiz
         </a>
         has seseo.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Most Spanish dialects in Spain differentiate between the sounds represented in traditional spelling by ⟨z⟩ and ⟨c⟩ (before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩), pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        , and that of ⟨s⟩, pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        . However, in many Andalusian-speaking areas, the two phonemes have merged as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        , which is known as
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
          seseo
         </a>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /seˈseo/
        </span>
        . In other areas, the two phonemes have merged as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_corono-dentoalveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless corono-dentoalveolar sibilant">
          s̄
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        (a sound close, but not identical to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [θ]
        </span>
        ), which is known as
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ceceo" title="Ceceo">
          ceceo
         </a>
        </i>
        (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θeˈθeo/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In still other areas, the distinction is retained (
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /distinˈθjon/
       </span>
       ).
       <i>
        Ceceo
       </i>
       predominates in more southerly parts of Andalusia, including the provinces of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz_(province)" title="Cádiz (province)">
        Cádiz
       </a>
       , southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huelva_(province)" title="Huelva (province)">
        Huelva
       </a>
       , most of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga_(province)" title="Málaga (province)">
        Málaga
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seville_(province)" title="Seville (province)">
        Seville
       </a>
       (except the northern parts of both provinces and the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       ) and south-western
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granada_(province)" title="Granada (province)">
        Granada
       </a>
       . A common stereotype about
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       is that it is mostly found in backward rural areas, but the predominance of
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       in major cities such as
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A1laga" title="Málaga">
        Málaga
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Huelva" title="Huelva">
        Huelva
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Granada" title="Granada">
        Granada
       </a>
       (where, on the other hand one can also find
       <i>
        distinción
       </i>
       , depending on the neighbourhood) are enough proof to refute this.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Seseo
       </i>
       predominates in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%B3rdoba_(Spanish_province)" title="Córdoba (Spanish province)">
        Córdoba
       </a>
       , northern Seville and Málaga and western Huelva. Interestingly, the cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">
        Cádiz
       </a>
       are
       <i>
        seseante
       </i>
       , but entirely surrounded by
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       areas; the city of Cádiz is the only area in the entire province of Cádiz, along with San Fernando (
       <i>
        La Isla de León
       </i>
       ), that is not
       <i>
        ceceante
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Distinción
       </i>
       is mostly found in the provinces of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_(province)" title="Almería (province)">
        Almería
       </a>
       , eastern Granada,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ja%C3%A9n_(Spanish_province)" title="Jaén (Spanish province)">
        Jaén
       </a>
       , and the northern parts of Córdoba and Huelva. See map above for a detailed description of these zones. Outside Andalusia,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
       </i>
       also existed in parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       up to at least 1940. The standard distinction which predominates in Eastern Andalusia is now to be heard in many cultivated speakers of the West, especially among younger speakers in urban areas or in monitored speech. The influence of media and school is now strong in Andalusia and this is eroding traditional
       <i>
        seseo
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ceceo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">

         </a>
        </i>
        , that is the merging of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

          ɟʝ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>

       </li>
       <li>
        Intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>

        <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">

        </a>

        <i>
         pesao
        </i>
        for
        <i>
         pesado
        </i>
        ('heavy'), *
        <i>
         a menúo
        </i>
        for
        <i>
         a menudo
        </i>
        ('often'). This is especially common in the past participle; e.g.
        <i>
         he acabado
        </i>
        becomes *
        <i>
         he acabao
        </i>
        ('I have finished'). For the -
        <i>
         ado
        </i>
        suffix, this feature is common to all peninsular variants of Spanish, while in other positions it is widespread throughout most of the southern half of Spain. This is the continuation of the tendency of
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
         lenition
        </a>
        in Vulgar Latin which developed into the Romance languages. Compare Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         vīta
        </span>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈwiːta]
        </span>
        , Italian
        <i>
         vita
        </i>

        </span>

        </i>

        </span>
        with a fully occlusive
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [d]
        </span>

        <i>

        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <i>
         vida
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbiða]
        </span>
        with an interdental
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ð]
        </span>
        (as in English ⟨th⟩ in "this") and French
        <i>
         vie
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <i>
         vida
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbi.a]
        </span>
        'life').
       </li>

        <i>

        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">

        </a>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

        <a href="/wiki/Murcian_Spanish" title="Murcian Spanish">
         Murcian Spanish
        </a>

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Lloret|2007|p=24-25_1-0">

         </a>

        <i>
         la casa
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        </span>

       </li>

      <p>
       As a result, these varieties have five vowel phonemes, each with a tense allophone (roughly the same as the normal realization in northern Spanish;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [a]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [e̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [o̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [u]
       </span>
       ) and a lax allophone (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [æ̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɛ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i̞]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɔ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [u̞]
       </span>
       ). In addition to this, a process of
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_harmony" title="Vowel harmony">
        vowel harmony
       </a>
       may take place where tense vowels that precede a lax vowel may become lax themselves; e.g.
       <i>
        trébol
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈtɾe̞βo̞l]
       </span>
       ('clover, club') vs
       <i>
        tréboles
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈtɾɛβɔlɛ]
       </span>
       ('clovers, clubs').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harvcoltxt|Lloret|2007|p=24-25_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harvcoltxt|Lloret|2007|p=24-25-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       S-aspiration is general in all of the southern half of Spain, and now becoming common in the northern half too.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        As in standard Spanish, phonetic vowel nasalization occurs for vowels occurring between nasal consonants or when preceding a syllable-final nasal. However, contrary to standard Spanish (where syllable-final nasal consonants are retained), in Andalusian varieties utterance-final
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nasal_stop" title="Nasal stop">
         nasals
        </a>
        are often deleted; e.g.
        <i>
         bien
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbjẽ̞]
        </span>
        ('good').
       </li>
       <li>
        Final consonants are dropped in many instances. This does not cause the previous vowel to lower; e.g.
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          comer
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ko̞ˈme̞]
        </span>
        ('to eat'),
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          comercial
         </span>
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ko̞me̞rˈθja]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ko̞me̞rˈsja]
        </span>
        ('commercial'),
        <i>
         pared
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [paˈɾe̞]
        </span>
        ('wall'). This often gives rise to a situation where two different words sound exactly the same, as with the
        <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
         infinitive
        </a>
        <i>
         cortar
        </i>
        ('to cut'), the
        <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
         imperative
        </a>
        <i>
         ¡cortad!
        </i>
        ('cut [it]!') and the feminine past participle
        <i>
         cortada
        </i>
        , ('[a] cut thing'); which are all pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ko̞rˈta]
        </span>
        . The geographical extent of this consonant drop is variable, and in some cases, like final ⟨d⟩, common to most of Spain.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        is deaffricated to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        in Western Andalusia, including cities like
        <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
         Seville
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A1diz" title="Cádiz">
         Cádiz
        </a>
        ; e.g.
        <i>
         escucha
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɛˈkuʃa]
        </span>
        ('s/he listens').
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        may be
        <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism_(sound_change)" title="Rhotacism (sound change)">
         pronounced as
         <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
          /r/
         </span>
        </a>
        in syllable-final position, as in
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈarma]
        </span>
        instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈalma]
        </span>
        for
        <i>
         alma
        </i>
        ('soul') or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [e̞r]
        </span>
        instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [e̞l]
        </span>
        for
        <i>
         el
        </i>
        ('the'). The opposite may also happen; i.e.
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        becomes
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        (e.g.
        <i>
         sartén
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [salˈtẽ̞]
        </span>
        'frying pan').
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is usually aspirated or pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        except in some eastern Andalusian subvarieties (i.e.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ja%C3%A9n_(Spanish_province)" title="Jaén (Spanish province)">
         Jaén
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Granada_(province)" title="Granada (province)">
         Granada
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Almer%C3%ADa_(province)" title="Almería (province)">
         Almería
        </a>
        provinces), where the dorsal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x]
        </span>
        is retained. This also happens in most of
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        and parts of
        <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">
         Cantabria
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Before
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        (Western Andalusian),
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        can be pronounced in two ways: it may be elided, thus leaving only the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        or it may be retained, intensifying the aspirated sound of the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        . Thus,
        <i>
         virgen
        </i>
        ('virgin') becomes either
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbihẽ̞]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbirhẽ̞]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Words of Latin origin starting with ⟨h⟩ (originally ⟨
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         f
        </span>
        ⟩) in writing (that is, that have kept the etymological ⟨h⟩ in writing) are sometimes pronounced with an initial
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        sound; e.g. Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         fartvs
        </span>
        'stuffed, full' →
        <i>
         harto
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈharto̞]
        </span>
        (standard Spanish
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈarto̞]
        </span>
        'fed up'). This also occurs in the speech of
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        . However, this characteristic is limited to rural areas and the
        <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
         flamenco
        </a>
        culture.
       </li>
      </ul>

         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Morphology and syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Subject pronouns
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Many Western Andalusian speakers replace the informal second person plural
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       with the formal
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       (without the formal connotation, as happens in other parts of Spain). For example, the standard second person plural verb forms for
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       ('to go') are
       <i>
        vosotros vais
       </i>
       (informal) and
       <i>
        ustedes van
       </i>
       (formal), but in Western Andalusian one often hears
       <i>
        ustedes vais
       </i>
       for the informal version.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Object pronouns
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although mass media have generalised the use of
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       as a pronoun for the substitution of direct objects, many Andalusians still use the normative
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       , as in
       <i>
        lo quiero mucho
       </i>
       (instead of
       <i>
        le quiero mucho
       </i>
       ). Laísmo (the substitution of indirect pronoun
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       , as in the incorrect sentence *
       <i>
        la pegó una bofetada
       </i>
       ) does not exist. Though not correct, it is frequently heard on Radio and TV programmes.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Verbs
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The standard form of imperative, second person plural with a reflexive pronoun (
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       ) is
       <i>
        -aos
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        -aros
       </i>
       in informal speech, whereas in Andalusian, and other dialects, too,
       <i>
        -se
       </i>
       is used instead, so
       <i>
        ¡callaos ya! / ¡callaros ya!
       </i>
       ('shut up!') becomes
       <i>
        ¡callarse ya!
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ¡sentaos! / ¡sentaros!
       </i>
       ('sit down!') becomes
       <i>
        ¡sentarse!
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Gender
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       of some words may not match that of Standard Spanish, e.g.
       <i>
        la calor
       </i>
       not
       <i>
        el calor
       </i>
       ('the heat'),
       <i>
        el chinche
       </i>
       not
       <i>
        la chinche
       </i>
       ('the bedbug').
       <i>
        La mar
       </i>
       is also more frequently used than
       <i>
        el mar
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        La mar de
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tela de
       </i>
       are lexicalised expressions to mean
       <i>
        a lot of...
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many words of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozarabic" title="Mozarabic">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cal%C3%B3_(Spanish_Romani)" title="Caló (Spanish Romani)">
        Romani
       </a>
       and Old Castilian origin occur in Andalusian which are not found in other dialects in Spain (but many of these may occur in South American and, especially, in
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       dialects due to the greater influence of Andalusian there). For example:
       <i>
        chispenear
       </i>
       instead of standard
       <i>
        lloviznar
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        chispear
       </i>
       ('to drizzle'),
       <i>
        babucha
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        zapatilla
       </i>
       ('slipper'),
       <i>
        chavea
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        antié
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        anteayer
       </i>
       ('the day before yesterday'). A few words of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalusi_Arabic" title="Andalusi Arabic">
        Andalusi Arabic
       </a>
       origin that have become archaisms or unknown in general Spanish can be found, together with multitude of sayings: e.g.
       <i>
        haciendo morisquetas
       </i>
       (from the word
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">
         morisco
        </a>
       </i>
       , meaning pulling faces and gesticulating, historically associated with Muslim prayers). These can be found in older texts of Andalusi. There are some doublets of Arabic-Latinate synonyms with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       form being more common in Andalusian like Andalusian
       <i>
        alcoba
       </i>
       for standard
       <i>
        habitación
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        dormitorio
       </i>
       ('bedroom') or
       <i>
        alhaja
       </i>
       for standard
       <i>
        joya
       </i>
       ('jewel').
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">
        Influence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Andalusian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some words pronounced in the Andalusian way have entered general Spanish with a specific meaning. One example is
       <i>
        juerga
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juerga_DRAE_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-juerga_DRAE-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ("debauchery", or "partying"), the Andalusian pronunciation of
       <i>
        huelga
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-huelga_DRAE_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-huelga_DRAE-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (originally "period without work", now "
       <a href="/wiki/Strike_action" title="Strike action">
        work strike
       </a>
       "). The
       <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
        flamenco
       </a>
       lexicon incorporates many Andalusisms, for example,
       <i>
        cantaor
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tocaor
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        bailaor
       </i>
       , which are examples of the dropped "d"; in standard spelling these would be
       <i>
        cantador
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tocador
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        bailador
       </i>
       , while the same terms in more general Spanish may be
       <i>
        cantante
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        músico
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        bailarín
       </i>
       . Note that, when referring to the flamenco terms, the correct spelling drops the "d"; a flamenco
       <i>
        cantaor
       </i>
       is written this way, not
       <i>
        cantador
       </i>
       . In other cases, the dropped "d" may be used in standard Spanish for terms closely associated with Andalusian culture. For example,
       <i>
        pescaíto frito
       </i>
       ("little fried fish") is a popular dish in Andalusia, and this spelling is used in many parts of Spain when referring to this dish. For general usage, the spelling would be
       <i>
        pescadito frito
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Llanito" title="Llanito">
        Llanito
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_overseas_territory" title="British overseas territory">
        British overseas territory
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       , is based on Andalusian Spanish, with
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       and other influences.
      </p>
