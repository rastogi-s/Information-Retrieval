         <a href="/wiki/Saying" title="Saying">

         variety:
         <i>

         </a>
         carries the large bag."

      <p>
       The primary languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">
        Calabria
       </a>
       are the standard variety of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       and regional varieties of the
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian languages
       </a>
       collectively known as
       <b>
        Calabrian
       </b>
       (Italian:
       <i>
        calabrese
       </i>
       ). In addition, there are significant numbers of
       <a href="/wiki/Calabrian_Greek" title="Calabrian Greek">
        Calabrian Greek
       </a>
       speakers and pockets of
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Arb%C3%ABresh" title="Arbëresh">
        Arbëresh
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Calabrian_.28Calabrese.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calabrian_(Calabrese)">
        Calabrian (
        <i>
         Calabrese
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_of_Calabria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Calabrian (Calabrese)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Calabrian (it:
       <i>
        Calabrese
       </i>
       ) refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance varieties
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">
        Calabria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . The varieties of Calabria are part of a strong
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       that are generally recognizable as Calabrian, but that are usually divided into two different language groups:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In the southern two-thirds of the region, the Calabrian dialects are more closely related to
        <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
         Sicilian
        </a>
        , grouped as
        <b>
         Central-Southern Calabrian
        </b>
        , or simply
        <b>
         Calabro
        </b>
        , and are usually classified as part of Extreme Southern Italian (
        <i>
         Italiano meridionale-estremo
        </i>
        ) language group.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the northern one-third of the region, the Calabrian dialects are often classified typologically with
        <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
         Neapolitan language
        </a>
        (it:
        <i>
         Napoletano-Calabrese
        </i>
        ) and are called
        <b>
         Northern Calabrian
        </b>
        or just
        <b>
         Cosentino
        </b>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Amantea" title="Amantea">
        Amantea
       </a>
       -
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cir%C3%B2_(KR)" title="Cirò (KR)">
        Cirò
       </a>
       line is generally considered an approximate demarcation between the Neapolitan and Sicilian language groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       The linguistic division roughly corresponds with the historic administrative division already in place since medieval times:
       <i>
        Calabria Citeriore
       </i>
       (or Latin Calabria) and
       <i>
        Calabria Ulteriore
       </i>
       (or Greek Calabria). This is a broad generalization and many communities in the more central parts of the region exhibit features of both language groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       The dialects of Calabria have been extensively studied, catalogued and commented upon by German
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">
        philologist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gerhard_Rohlfs" title="Gerhard Rohlfs">
        Gerhard Rohlfs
       </a>
       . From the mid-1920s to the mid-1970s, he traveled the region extensively and assembled a very extensive, multi-volume dictionary.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Central-Southern_Calabrian">
        Central-Southern Calabrian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_of_Calabria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Central-Southern Calabrian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="floatright">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Dialetti_italiani_meridionali_estremi.jpg">
        <img alt="Dialetti italiani meridionali estremi.jpg" data-file-height="362" data-file-width="510" height="284" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Dialetti_italiani_meridionali_estremi.jpg/400px-Dialetti_italiani_meridionali_estremi.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/09/Dialetti_italiani_meridionali_estremi.jpg 1.5x" width="400"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The areas where
       <b>
        Central–Southern Calabrian
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        calabbrìsi
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        calavrìsi
       </i>
       , in Sicilian) is spoken corresponds generally to the provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Reggio_Calabria" title="Province of Reggio Calabria">
        Reggio Calabria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Vibo_Valentia" title="Province of Vibo Valentia">
        Vibo Valentia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Catanzaro" title="Province of Catanzaro">
        Catanzaro
       </a>
       and the southern part of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Crotone" title="Province of Crotone">
        Crotone
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Crotone" title="Crotone">
        Crotone
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Isola_di_Capo_Rizzuto" title="Isola di Capo Rizzuto">
        Isola di Capo Rizzuto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cutro" title="Cutro">
        Cutro
       </a>
       and vicinity). The term
       <i>
        Sicilian-Calabrian
       </i>
       is also used to distinguish the group from the Northern Calabrian group. It comprises
       <b>
        Central Calabrian
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        Southern Calabrian
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The primary roots of the dialects is
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Southern and Central Calabrian dialects are strongly influenced by a
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">
        substratum
       </a>
       and ensuing levels of Latin influence and other external Southern Italian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superstratum" title="Superstratum">
        superstrata
       </a>
       , in part hindered by geography, resulted in the many local variations found between the idioms of Calabria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nonetheless, the dialects have a rich and varied influence from other languages, thanks to the domination and influx of different cultures. As a result,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       have left a strong imprint.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Northern_Calabrian_.28Cosentino.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Calabrian_(Cosentino)">
        <span id="Cosentino">
        </span>
        Northern Calabrian (Cosentino)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_of_Calabria&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Northern Calabrian (Cosentino)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cosenza_mappa.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="694" data-file-width="700" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cosenza_mappa.png/150px-Cosenza_mappa.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cosenza_mappa.png/225px-Cosenza_mappa.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Cosenza_mappa.png/300px-Cosenza_mappa.png 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosenza_mappa.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cosenza" title="Province of Cosenza">
          Province of Cosenza
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Northern Calabrian dialects (
       <i>
        calabbrìse
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        calavrìse
       </i>
       , in Neapolitan) are similar to other
       <a href="/wiki/Neapolitan_language" title="Neapolitan language">
        Neapolitan varieties
       </a>
       and are significantly different from the dialects of southern Calabria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The dialects of the northern fringes of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cosenza" title="Province of Cosenza">
        Province of Cosenza
       </a>
       give way to
       <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">
        Campanian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">
        Lucanian
       </a>
       dialects; however, the majority of the province speaks the
       <b>
        Cosentino dialect
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        u cusindinu
       </i>
       ). It is also spoken in the northern portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Crotone" title="Province of Crotone">
        Province of Crotone
       </a>
       and in the extreme northwest of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Catanzaro" title="Province of Catanzaro">
        Province of Catanzaro
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       One aspect of Northern Calabrian is that the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
        preterite
       </a>
       is almost absent, in great contrast to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian varieties
       </a>
       of the south. In Cosentino, the norm is
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        haju) pigliatu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        signu jutu
       </i>
       (literally "I took" and "I went"); whereas the preterite (distant remote tense)
       <i>
        pigghiai
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ivi
       </i>
       would be more common in the rest of Calabria. Other aspects are the phonetic modifications similar to other Neapolitan dialects, such as where
       <i>
        -nt
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <i>
        -nd
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        praticamente
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        praticamende
       </i>
       ), and
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       is usually pronounced
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       . In addition, spoken Cosentino is noted for its "soft" truncation of end syllables at the termination of sentences, and for the typical T/C sound in words such as
       <i>
        ccjù
       </i>
       (more) or
       <i>
        cjanu
       </i>
       (slow). As a transitional dialect between Sicilian and Neapolitan, Cosentino shares many sounds, words and features unique to each of the Sicilian and Neapolitan groups.
      </p>
