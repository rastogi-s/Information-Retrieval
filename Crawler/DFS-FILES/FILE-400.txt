      <p>
       <b>
        Celtiberian
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Northeastern Hispano-Celtic
       </b>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Extinct_language" title="Extinct language">
        extinct
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European
       </a>
       language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       branch spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Celtiberians" title="Celtiberians">
        Celtiberians
       </a>
       in an area of the Iberian Peninsula lying between the headwaters of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duero" title="Duero">
        Duero
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tajo" title="Tajo">
        Tajo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/J%C3%BAcar" title="Júcar">
        Júcar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turia_(river)" title="Turia (river)">
        Turia
       </a>
       rivers and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">
        Ebro
       </a>
       river. This language is directly attested in nearly 200 inscriptions dated to the 2nd century BC and the 1st century BC, mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/Celtiberian_script" title="Celtiberian script">
        Celtiberian script
       </a>
       , a direct adaptation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Northeastern_Iberian_script" title="Northeastern Iberian script">
        northeastern Iberian script
       </a>
       , but also in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
        Latin alphabet
       </a>
       . The longest extant Celtiberian inscriptions are those on three
       <a href="/wiki/Botorrita_plaque" title="Botorrita plaque">
        Botorrita plaques
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bronze" title="Bronze">
        bronze
       </a>
       plaques from
       <a href="/wiki/Botorrita" title="Botorrita">
        Botorrita
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Zaragoza" title="Zaragoza">
        Zaragoza
       </a>
       , dating to the early 1st century BC, labelled Botorrita I, III and IV (Botorrita II is in the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin language
       </a>
       ). In the northwest was another Celtic language,
       <a href="/wiki/Gallaecian_language" title="Gallaecian language">
        Northwestern Hispano-Celtic
       </a>
       , that was somewhat related to Celtiberian.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Celtiberian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Enough has been preserved to show that the Celtiberian language could be called
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages#Classification" title="Celtic languages">
        Q-Celtic
       </a>
       (like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goidelic" title="Goidelic">
        Goidelic
       </a>
       ), and not
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-Celtic" title="P-Celtic">
        P-Celtic
       </a>
       like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For some, this has served to confirm that the legendary invasion of Ireland by the
       <a href="/wiki/Milesians_(Irish)" title="Milesians (Irish)">
        Milesians
       </a>
       , preserved in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lebor_Gab%C3%A1la_%C3%89renn" title="Lebor Gabála Érenn">
         Lebor Gabála Érenn
        </a>
       </i>
       , actually happened.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_language_(Celtic)" title="British language (Celtic)">
        Brythonic
       </a>
       is
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       -Celtic too, but as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Insular_Celtic_language" title="Insular Celtic language">
        Insular Celtic language
       </a>
       more closely related to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goidelic" title="Goidelic">
        Goidelic
       </a>
       than to Gaulish,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it follows that the
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        Q
       </i>
       division is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polyphyletic" title="Polyphyletic">
        polyphyletic
       </a>
       : the change from
       <i>
        kʷ
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       occurred in Brythonic and Gaulish at a time when they were already separate languages, rather than constituting a division that marked a separate branch in the "family tree" of the Celtic languages. A change from PIE
       <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
        <i>
         kʷ
        </i>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        q
       </i>
       ) to
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       also occurred in some
