      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish pronouns
       </b>
       in some ways work quite differently from their
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        English counterparts
       </a>
       . Subject pronouns are often omitted, and object pronouns can appear either as
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitics
       </a>
       that come before the verb or
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Enclitic" title="Clitic">
        enclitics
       </a>
       attached to the end of it in different linguistic environments. There is also regional variation in the use of pronouns, particularly the use of the informal second-person singular
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         vos
        </a>
       </i>
       and the informal second-person plural
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       .
      </p>

        Personal pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Personal pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_personal_pronouns" title="Spanish personal pronouns">
        Spanish personal pronouns
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        Personal pronouns
       </a>
       in Spanish have distinct forms according to whether they stand for a subject (
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       ), a direct object (
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative
       </a>
       ), an indirect object (
       <a href="/wiki/Dative_case" title="Dative case">
        dative
       </a>
       ), or a
       <a href="/wiki/Reflexive_pronoun" title="Reflexive pronoun">
        reflexive
       </a>
       object. Several pronouns further have special forms used after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       . Spanish is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pro-drop" title="Pro-drop">
        pro-drop
       </a>
       language with respect to subject pronouns. Like
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and other languages with the
       <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
        T–V distinction
       </a>
       , modern Spanish has a distinction in its second person pronouns that has no equivalent in modern English. Object pronouns are generally
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic#Proclitic" title="Clitic">
        proclitic
       </a>
       , and non-emphatic
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic_doubling" title="Clitic doubling">
        clitic doubling
       </a>
       is most often found with dative clitics. The
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronoun
       </a>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        vos
       </a>
       " is used in some areas of Latin America, particularly in Central America, Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, Chile, the state of Zulia in Venezuela, and the Andean regions of Colombia, Bolivia, Perú, and Ecuador.
      </p>
      <p>
       The table below shows a cumulative list of personal pronouns from Peninsular, Latin American and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
        Ladino Spanish
       </a>
       . Ladino or Judaeo-Spanish, spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardic Jews
       </a>
       , is different from Latin American and Peninsular Spanish in that it retains rather archaic forms and usage of personal pronouns.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demonstrative_pronouns">
        Demonstrative pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demonstrative pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Near the speaker ("this"):
        <i>
         éste, ésta, esto, éstos, éstas
        </i>
        (from the Latin
        <small>
         ISTE, ISTA, ISTVD
        </small>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Near the listener ("that"):
        <i>
         ése, ésa, eso, ésos, ésas
        </i>
        (from the Latin
        <small>
         IPSE, IPSA, IPSVM
        </small>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        Far from both speaker and listener ("that (over there)"):
        <i>
         aquél, aquélla, aquello, aquéllos, aquéllas
        </i>
        (from the Latin
        <small>
         *ECCVM ILLE, *ECCVM ILLA, *ECCVM ILLVD
        </small>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       According to a decision by the
       <i>
        Real Academia
       </i>
       in the 1960s, the accents on these forms are only to be used when necessary to avoid ambiguity with the demonstrative
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_determiners" title="Spanish determiners">
        determiners
       </a>
       . However, the normal educated standard is still as above. Foreign learners may safely adhere to either standard. There is furthermore never an accent on the neuter forms
       <i>
        esto
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eso
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        aquello
       </i>
       , which do not have determiner equivalents.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relative_pronouns">
        Relative pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relative pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_pronoun" title="Relative pronoun">
        relative pronoun
       </a>
       in Spanish is
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , from Latin
       <small>
        QVID
       </small>
       . Others include
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        quien
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        donde
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Que">
        <i>
         Que
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Que">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Que
       </i>
       covers "that", "which", "who", "whom" and the null pronoun in their functions of subject and direct-object relative pronouns:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         La carta
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         te envié era larga
        </i>
        = "The letter [that] I sent you was long" (restrictive relative pronoun referring to direct object)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La carta,
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         te envié, era larga
        </i>
        = "The letter, which I did send you, was long" (non-restrictive relative pronoun referring to direct object)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La gente
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         no sabe leer ni escribir se llama analfabeta
        </i>
        = "People who cannot read or write are called illiterate" (relative pronoun referring to subject)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esa persona,
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         conozco muy bien, no es de fiar
        </i>
        = "That person, whom I know very well, is not to be trusted" (non-restrictive relative pronoun referring to direct object)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note from the last example that unlike with other relative pronouns, personal
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       does not have to be used with
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       when used as a personal direct object.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="El_que">
        <i>
         El que
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: El que">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       When
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       is used as the object of a preposition, the definite article is added to it, and the resulting form (
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       ) inflects for number and gender, resulting in the forms
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la que
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los que
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        las que
       </i>
       and the neuter
       <i>
        lo que
       </i>
       . Unlike in English, the preposition must go right before the relative pronoun "which" or "whom":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ella es la persona
         <u>
          a la que
         </u>
         le di el dinero
        </i>
        = "She is the person [that/whom] I gave the money to"/"She is the person to whom I gave the money"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es el camino
         <u>
          por el que
         </u>
         caminabais
        </i>
        = "It is the path [that] you all were walking along"/"It is the path along which you all were walking"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In some people's style of speaking, the definite article may be omitted after
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        con
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       in such usage, particularly when the antecedent is abstract or neuter:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         La aspereza
         <u>
          con [la] que
         </u>
         la trataba
        </i>
        = "The harshness with which he treated her"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No tengo nada
         <u>
          en [lo] que
         </u>
         creer
        </i>
        = "I have nothing to believe in"/"I have nothing in which to believe"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       After
       <i>
        en
       </i>
       , the definite article tends to be omitted if precise spatial location is not intended:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo hiciste de la misma forma
         <u>
          en que
         </u>
         lo hizo él
        </i>
        = "You did it [in] the same way [that/in which] he did it" (note also how "in" with the word
        <i>
         forma
        </i>
        is translated as
        <i>
         de
        </i>
        when used directly, but then changes to
        <i>
         en
        </i>
        when used with the relative pronoun)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La casa
         <u>
          en que
         </u>
         vivo
        </i>
        = "The house in which I live" (as opposed to
        <i>
         La casa
         <u>
          en la que
         </u>
         estoy encerrado
        </i>
        = "The house inside which I am trapped")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lo_que">
        <i>
         Lo que
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Lo que">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       When used without a precise antecedent,
       <i>
        lo que
       </i>
       has a slightly different meaning from that of
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       , and is usually used as the connotation of "that which" or "what":
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo que hiciste era malo
        </i>
        = "What you did was bad"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo que creí no es correcto
        </i>
        = "What I believed is not right"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="El_cual">
        <i>
         El cual
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: El cual">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The pronoun
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       can replace
       <i>
        [el] que
       </i>
       . It is generally more emphatic and formal than
       <i>
        [el] que
       </i>
       , and it always includes the definite article. It is derived from the Latin
       <small>
        QVALIS
       </small>
       , and it has the following forms:
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        la cual
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        los cuales
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        las cuales
       </i>
       , and the neuter
       <i>
        lo cual
       </i>
       . It can be used as a formal, emphatic replacement for
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       in non-defining clauses, for both subjects and direct objects, and it can also be used as a formal, emphatic replacement for
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       as the object of some prepositions. Moreover, it is often preferred to
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       entirely in certain contexts. In non-defining clauses, the fact that it agrees for gender and number can make it clearer to what it refers. The fact that it cannot be used as the subject or direct object in defining clauses also makes it clear that a defining clause is not intended:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Los niños y sus madres,
         <u>
          las cuales
         </u>
         eran de Valencia, me impresionaron
        </i>
        = "The children and their mothers, who were from Valencia, impressed me" (
        <i>
         los cuales
        </i>
        would have referred to the children as well and not just their mothers)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When used as a personal direct object, personal
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       must be used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esa persona,
         <u>
          a la cual
         </u>
         conozco yo muy bien, no es de fiar
        </i>
        = "That person, whom I know very well, is not to be trusted"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In such situations as well as the object of monosyllabic prepositions, the use of
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       is generally purely a matter of high style. This is used sparingly in Spanish, and foreigners should thus avoid over-using it:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es el asunto
         <u>
          al cual
         </u>
         se refería Vd.
        </i>
        = "It is the matter to which you were referring"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In more everyday style, this might be phrased as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es el asunto
         <u>
          al que
         </u>
         te referías
        </i>
        = "It is the matter to which you were referring"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       After multisyllabic prepositions and prepositional phrases (
       <i>
        a pesar de
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        debajo de
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        a causa de
       </i>
       , etc.), however,
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       is often preferred entirely:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Un régimen
         <u>
          bajo el cual
         </u>
         es imposible vivir
        </i>
        = "A régime under which it is impossible to live"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estas cláusulas,
         <u>
          sin perjuicio de las cuales
         </u>
         ...
        </i>
        = "These clauses, notwithstanding which..."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        El cual
       </i>
       is further generally preferred entirely when, as the object of a preposition, it is separated from its antecedent by intervening words. The more words that intervene, the more the use of
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       is practically obligatory:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es un billete con el que se puede viajar [...] pero
         <u>
          por el cual
         </u>
         se paga sólo 2€
        </i>
        = "It is a ticket with which you can travel [...] but for which you pay just €2"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cual">
        <i>
         Cual
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cual">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The bare form
       <i>
        cual
       </i>
       is used as the relative adjective ("in which sense", "with which people", etc.), which only inflects for number:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         en
         <u>
          cual
         </u>
         caso
        </i>
        = "in which case"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         a
         <u>
          cual
         </u>
         tiempo
        </i>
        = "at which time"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <u>
          cuales
         </u>
         cosas
        </i>
        = "which things"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quien">
        <i>
         Quien
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Quien">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       "Quien" redirects here. For for other uses, see
       <a class="mw-redirect mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Qui%C3%A9n_(disambiguation)" title="Quién (disambiguation)">
        Quién
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       The pronoun
       <i>
        quien
       </i>
       comes from the Latin
       <small>
        QVEM
       </small>
       , "whom", the accusative of
       <small>
        QVIS
       </small>
       , "who".
      </p>
      <p>
       It too can replace
       <i>
        [el] que
       </i>
       in certain circumstances. Like the English pronouns "who" and "whom", it can only be used to refer to people.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is invariable for gender, and was originally invariable for number. However, by analogy with other words, the form
       <i>
        quienes
       </i>
       was invented.
       <i>
        Quien
       </i>
       as a plural form survives as an archaism that is now considered non-standard.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="For_subjects">
        For subjects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: For subjects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       It can represent a subject. In this case, it is rather formal and is largely restricted to non-defining clauses.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike
       <i>
        el cual
       </i>
       , it does not inflect for gender, but it does inflect for number, and it also specifies that it does refer to a person:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Los niños con sus mochilas,
         <u>
          quienes
         </u>
         eran de Valencia, me impresionaron
        </i>
        = "The children with their rucksacks, who were from Valencia, impressed me" (the use of
        <i>
         quienes
        </i>
        makes it clear that
        <i>
         los niños
        </i>
        is referred to;
        <i>
         que
        </i>
        could refer to the rucksacks, the children, or both,
        <i>
         los cuales
        </i>
        would refer to either the children or both, and
        <i>
         las cuales
        </i>
        would refer only to the rucksacks)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="As_the_object_of_a_preposition">
        As the object of a preposition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: As the object of a preposition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Quien
       </i>
       is particularly common as the object of a proposition when the clause is non-defining, but is also possible in defining clauses:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ella es la persona
         <u>
          a quien
         </u>
         le di el dinero
        </i>
        = "She is the person to whom I gave the money"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         José,
         <u>
          gracias a quien
         </u>
         tengo el dinero, es muy generoso
        </i>
        = "José, thanks to whom I have the money, is very generous"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Donde.2C_a_donde.2C_como_and_cuando">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Donde,_a_donde,_como_and_cuando">
        <i>
         Donde
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a donde
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         como
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         cuando
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Donde, a donde, como and cuando">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Donde
       </i>
       is ultimately from a combination of the obsolete adverb
       <i>
        onde
       </i>
       ("whence" or "from where") and the preposition
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Onde
       </i>
       is from Latin
       <small>
        VNDE
       </small>
       , which also meant "whence" or "from where", and over the centuries it lost the "from" meaning and came to mean just "where". This meant that, to say "whence" or "where from", the preposition
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       had to be added, and this gave
       <i>
        d'onde
       </i>
       . The meaning of
       <i>
        d'onde
       </i>
       once again eroded over time until it came to mean just "where", and prepositions therefore had to be added once more. This gave rise to the modern usage of
       <i>
        donde
       </i>
       for "where" and
       <i>
        a donde
       </i>
       for "to where", among others. Note that all this means that, etymologically speaking,
       <i>
        de donde
       </i>
       is the rather redundant "from from where", and
       <i>
        a donde
       </i>
       is the rather contradictory "to from where". This tendency goes even further with the vulgar form
       <i>
        ande
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        adonde
       </i>
       ), which is often used to mean "where" as well. In the Ladino dialect of Spanish, the pronoun
       <i>
        onde
       </i>
       is still used, where
       <i>
        donde
       </i>
       still means "whence" or "where from", and in Latin America, isolated communities and rural areas retain this as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Como
       </i>
       is from
       <small>
        QVOMODO
       </small>
       , "how", the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablative" title="Ablative">
        ablative
       </a>
       of
       <small>
        QVI MODVS
       </small>
       , "what way".
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Cuando
       </i>
       is from
       <small>
        QVANDO
       </small>
       , "when".
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location_and_movement">
        Location and movement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Location and movement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Donde
       </i>
       can be used instead of other relative pronouns when location is referred to.
       <i>
        Adonde
       </i>
       is a variant that can be used when motion to the location is intended:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         El lugar
         <u>
          en que
         </u>
         /
         <u>
          en el que
         </u>
         /
         <u>
          en el cual
         </u>
         /
         <u>
          donde
         </u>
         estoy
        </i>
        = "The place where I am"/"The place in which I am"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Voy a[l lugar]
         <u>
          donde
         </u>
         está él
        </i>
        =
        <i>
         Voy al lugar
         <u>
          en el que
         </u>
         está él
        </i>
        = "I am going [to the place] where he is"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Iré [al lugar]
         <u>
          adonde
         </u>
         me lleven
        </i>
        =
        <i>
         Iré al lugar
         <u>
          al que
         </u>
         me lleven
        </i>
        = "I will go wherever they take me"/"I will go to whatever place to which they take me"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Manner">
        Manner
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Manner">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Como
       </i>
       can be used instead of other relative pronouns when manner is referred to:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         La forma/manera
         <u>
          en que
         </u>
         /
         <u>
          en la que
         </u>
         /
         <u>
          como
         </u>
         reaccionasteis
        </i>
        = "The way that/in which/how you reacted" (
        <i>
         en que
        </i>
        is the most common and natural, like "that" or the
        <a href="/wiki/Reduced_relative_clause" title="Reduced relative clause">
         null pronoun in English
        </a>
        ; but
        <i>
         como
        </i>
        is possible, as "how" is in English)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that
       <i>
        mismo
       </i>
       tends to require
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lo dijo del mismo modo
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         lo dije yo
        </i>
        = "She said it the same way [that] I did"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Time">
        Time
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Time">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Cuando
       </i>
       tends to replace the use of other relative pronouns when time is referred, usually in non-defining clauses.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Non-defining
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         En agosto,
         <u>
          cuando
         </u>
         la gente tiene vacaciones, la ciudad estará vacía
        </i>
        = "In August, when people have their holidays, the town will be empty"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Defining
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Sólo salgo los días
         <u>
          [en] que
         </u>
         no trabajo
        </i>
        = "I only go out the days that I am not working"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that just
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , or at the most
       <i>
        en que
       </i>
       , is normal with defining clauses referring to time.
       <i>
        En el que
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        cuando
       </i>
       are rarer.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cuyo">
        <i>
         Cuyo
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Cuyo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       "Cuyo" is the formal Spanish equivalent for the English pronoun "whose." However, "cuyo" inflects for gender and number (
       <i>
        cuyos
       </i>
       (m. pl.),
       <i>
        cuya
       </i>
       (f. sg.), or
       <i>
        cuyas
       </i>
       (f. pl.)) according to the word it precedes. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Alejandro es un estudiante cuy
         <u>
          as
         </u>
         calificacion
         <u>
          es
         </u>
         son siempre buenas
        </i>
        = "Alejandro is a student whose grades are always good"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       "cuyo" in this example has changed to "cuyas" in order to match the condition of the following word, "calificaciones" (f. pl.)
      </p>
      <p>
       In Old Spanish there were interrogative forms,
       <i>
        cúyo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cúya
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cúyos
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        cúyas
       </i>
       , which are no longer used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPDCúyo_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPDCúyo-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In practice,
       <i>
        cuyo
       </i>
       is reserved to formal language. A periphrasis like
       <i>
        Alejandro es un estudiante que tiene unas calificaciones siempre buenas
       </i>
       is more common.
       <i>
        Alejandro es un estudiante que sus calificaciones son siempre buenas
       </i>
       can also be found even if disapproved by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescriptivist" title="Prescriptivist">
        prescriptivists
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPD_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPD-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Cuyo
       </i>
       is from
       <small>
        CVIVS
       </small>
       , the genitive (possessive) form of
       <small>
        QVI
       </small>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notes_on_relative_and_interrogative_pronouns">
        Notes on relative and interrogative pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_pronouns&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Notes on relative and interrogative pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Relative pronouns often have corresponding interrogative pronouns. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿
         <b>
          Qué
         </b>
         es esto?
        </i>
        = "
        <b>
         What
        </b>
        is this?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Ese es el libro
         <b>
          que
         </b>
         me diste
        </i>
        = "That's the book
        <b>
         that
        </b>
        you gave me"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In the second line,
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       helps to answer what
       <i>
        qué
       </i>
       was asking for, a definition of "this".
      </p>
      <p>
       Below is a list of interrogative pronouns and phrases with the relative pronouns that go with them:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Qué
        </b>
        - what,
        <b>
         que
        </b>
        - that, which
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Quién
        </b>
        - who, whom (after prepositions),
        <b>
         quien
        </b>
        - who, whom (after prepositions)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <b>
           A quién
          </b>
          - whom (direct object), to whom,
          <b>
           a quien
          </b>
          - whom (direct object), to whom
         </li>
         <li>
          <b>
           De quién
          </b>
          - whose, of whom,
          <b>
           cuyo
          </b>
          - whose, of whom
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
