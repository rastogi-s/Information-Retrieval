      <p>
       <b>
        Claudius Ptolemy
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Κλαύδιος Πτολεμαῖος
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Klaúdios Ptolemaîos
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">
         [kláwdios ptolɛmɛ́ːos]
        </a>
       </span>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Claudius Ptolemaeus
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <span class="nowrap">
        <abbr title="circa">
         c.
        </abbr>
        AD 100 –
       </span>
       c.
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        170
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was a
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">
        Greco
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematician" title="Mathematician">
        mathematician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">
        geographer
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrologer" title="Astrologer">
        astrologer
       </a>
       , and poet of a single
       <a href="/wiki/Epigram" title="Epigram">
        epigram
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_Anthology" title="Greek Anthology">
        Greek Anthology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He lived in the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">
        Alexandria
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt_(Roman_province)" title="Egypt (Roman province)">
        Roman province of Egypt
       </a>
       , wrote in
       <a href="/wiki/Koine_Greek" title="Koine Greek">
        Koine Greek
       </a>
       , and held
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">
        Roman citizenship
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 14th-century astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Theodore_Meliteniotes" title="Theodore Meliteniotes">
        Theodore Meliteniotes
       </a>
       gave his birthplace as the prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">
        Greek
       </a>
       city
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemais_Hermiou" title="Ptolemais Hermiou">
        Ptolemais Hermiou
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Πτολεμαΐς ‘Ερμείου
       </span>
       ) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Thebaid" title="Thebaid">
        Thebaid
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Θηβαΐδα [Θηβαΐς]
       </span>
       ). This attestation is quite late, however, and, according to
       <a href="/wiki/Gerald_J._Toomer" title="Gerald J. Toomer">
        Gerald Toomer
       </a>
       , the translator of his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       into English, there is no reason to suppose he ever lived anywhere other than Alexandria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He died there around AD 168.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy wrote several scientific treatises, three of which were of importance to later
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantium" title="Byzantium">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_science" title="Islamic science">
        Islamic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        European
       </a>
       science. The first is the astronomical treatise now known as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       , although it was originally entitled the
       <i>
        Mathematical Treatise
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Μαθηματικὴ Σύνταξις
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Mathēmatikē Syntaxis
       </i>
       ) and then known as the
       <i>
        Great Treatise
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Ἡ Μεγάλη Σύνταξις
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Hē Megálē Syntaxis
       </i>
       ). The second is the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       , which is a thorough discussion of the geographic knowledge of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greco-Roman" title="Greco-Roman">
        Greco-Roman
       </a>
       world. The third is the astrological treatise in which he attempted to adapt
       <a href="/wiki/Horoscopic_astrology" title="Horoscopic astrology">
        horoscopic astrology
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotelian_physics" title="Aristotelian physics">
        Aristotelian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_philosophy" title="Natural philosophy">
        natural philosophy
       </a>
       of his day. This is sometimes known as the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apotelesmatika" title="Apotelesmatika">
         Apotelesmatika
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Ἀποτελεσματικά
       </span>
       ) but more commonly known as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tetrabiblos" title="Tetrabiblos">
         Tetrabiblos
        </a>
       </i>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Τετράβιβλος
       </span>
       ) meaning "Four Books" or by the Latin
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quadripartitum" title="Quadripartitum">
         Quadripartitum
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_urania.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="367" data-file-width="280" height="288" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Ptolemy_urania.jpg/220px-Ptolemy_urania.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Ptolemy_urania.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_urania.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Engraving of a crowned Ptolemy being guided by the muse Astronomy, from
         <i>
          Margarita Philosophica
         </i>
         by
         <a href="/wiki/Gregor_Reisch" title="Gregor Reisch">
          Gregor Reisch
         </a>
         , 1508. Although
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Ma%27shar_al-Balkhi" title="Abu Ma'shar al-Balkhi">
          Abu Ma'shar
         </a>
         believed Ptolemy to be one of the
         <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">
          Ptolemies
         </a>
         who ruled Egypt after the conquest of
         <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
          Alexander
         </a>
         the title ‘King Ptolemy’ is generally viewed as a mark of respect for Ptolemy's elevated standing in science.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_(name)" title="Ptolemy (name)">
         Ptolemaeus
        </a>
       </i>
       (Πτολεμαῖος –
       <i>
        Ptolemaios
       </i>
       ) is a Greek name. It occurs once in Greek mythology, and is of Homeric form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was common among the Macedonian upper class at the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       , and there were several of this name among Alexander's army, one of whom made himself King of Egypt in 323 BC:
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">
        Ptolemy I Soter
       </a>
       . All the kings (Pharaohs) after him, until Egypt became a Roman province in 30 BC, were also Greek
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_dynasty" title="Ptolemaic dynasty">
        Ptolemies
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The name
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_(gens)" title="Claudius (gens)">
         Claudius
        </a>
       </i>
       is a Roman
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">
         nomen
        </a>
       </i>
       ; the fact that Ptolemy bore it indicates he lived under the Roman rule of Egypt with the privileges and political rights of
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_citizenship" title="Roman citizenship">
        Roman citizenship
       </a>
       . It would have suited custom if the first of Ptolemy's family to become a citizen (whether he or an ancestor) took the
       <i>
        nomen
       </i>
       from a Roman called Claudius who was responsible for granting citizenship. If, as was common, this was the emperor, citizenship would have been granted between AD 41 and 68 (when
       <a href="/wiki/Claudius" title="Claudius">
        Claudius
       </a>
       , and then
       <a href="/wiki/Nero" title="Nero">
        Nero
       </a>
       , were Roman emperors). The astronomer would also have had a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_naming_conventions" title="Roman naming conventions">
         praenomen
        </a>
       </i>
       , which remains unknown.
      </p>
      <p>
       The 9th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">
        Persian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_astronomy" title="Islamic astronomy">
        astronomer
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Ma%27shar_al-Balkhi" title="Abu Ma'shar al-Balkhi">
        Abu Ma'shar
       </a>
       presents Ptolemy as a member of Egypt's royal lineage, stating that the ten kings of Egypt who followed
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander
       </a>
       were wise "and included Ptolemy the Wise, who composed the book of the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       ".
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Ma%27shar_al-Balkhi" title="Abu Ma'shar al-Balkhi">
        Abu Ma'shar
       </a>
       recorded a belief that a different member of this royal line "composed the book on astrology and attributed it to Ptolemy". We can evidence historical confusion on this point from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Ma%27shar_al-Balkhi" title="Abu Ma'shar al-Balkhi">
        Abu Ma'shar's
       </a>
       subsequent remark “It is sometimes said that the very learned man who wrote the book of astrology also wrote the book of the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       . The correct answer is not known”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is little evidence on the subject of Ptolemy's ancestry, apart from what can be drawn from the details of his name (see above); however, modern scholars refer to Abu Ma’shar’s account as erroneous,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and it is no longer doubted that the astronomer who wrote the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       also wrote the
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       as its astrological counterpart.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy wrote in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       and can be shown to have utilized
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomical_diaries" title="Babylonian astronomical diaries">
        Babylonian astronomical data
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a Roman citizen, but was ethnically either a
       <a href="/wiki/Greeks_in_Egypt" title="Greeks in Egypt">
        Greek
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-britannica.com_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-britannica.com-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz_184_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Katz_184-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ptolemy_2006_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ptolemy_2006-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Hellenization" title="Hellenization">
        Hellenized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egypt" title="Ancient Egypt">
        Egyptian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katz_184_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Katz_184-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sarton_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sarton-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was often known in later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       sources as "the
       <a href="/wiki/Upper_Egypt" title="Upper Egypt">
        Upper Egyptian
       </a>
       ",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggesting he may have had origins in southern
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bernal_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bernal-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomy_in_medieval_Islam" title="Astronomy in medieval Islam">
        Arabic astronomers
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_in_medieval_Islam" title="Geography in medieval Islam">
        geographers
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_in_medieval_Islam" title="Physics in medieval Islam">
        physicists
       </a>
       referred to him by his name in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       :
       <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        بَطْلُمْيوس
       </span>
       ‎
       <i>
        Batlamyus
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomy">
        Astronomy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Astronomy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
        Almagest
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1124" data-file-width="750" height="330" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg/220px-Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg/330px-Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c9/Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg/440px-Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_1476_with_armillary_sphere_model.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Ptolemy with an
          <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">
           armillary sphere
          </a>
          model
         </i>
         , by
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Joos_van_Ghent" title="Joos van Ghent">
          Joos van Ghent
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Berruguete" title="Pedro Berruguete">
          Pedro Berruguete
         </a>
         , 1476,
         <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">
          Louvre
         </a>
         , Paris
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       is the only surviving comprehensive ancient treatise on astronomy.
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">
        Babylonian astronomers
       </a>
       had developed arithmetical techniques for calculating astronomical phenomena; Greek astronomers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       had produced
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geometric_model" title="Geometric model">
        geometric models
       </a>
       for calculating celestial motions. Ptolemy, however, claimed to have derived his geometrical models from selected astronomical observations by his predecessors spanning more than 800 years, though astronomers have for centuries suspected that his models' parameters were adopted independently of observations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ptolemy presented his astronomical models in convenient tables, which could be used to compute the future or past position of the planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       also contains a
       <a href="/wiki/Star_catalogue" title="Star catalogue">
        star catalogue
       </a>
       , which is a version of a catalogue created by Hipparchus. Its list of forty-eight
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellations
       </a>
       is ancestral to the modern system of constellations, but unlike the modern system they did not cover the whole sky (only the sky Hipparchus could see). Across Europe, the Middle East and North Africa in the Medieval period, it was the authoritative text on astronomy, with its author becoming an almost mythical figure, called Ptolemy, King of Alexandria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       was preserved, like most of extant Classical Greek science, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       manuscripts (hence its familiar name). Because of its reputation, it was widely sought and was
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century" title="Latin translations of the 12th century">
        translated twice into Latin in the 12th century
       </a>
       , once in Sicily and again in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ptolemy's model, like those of his predecessors, was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geocentric" title="Geocentric">
        geocentric
       </a>
       and was almost universally accepted until the appearance of simpler
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliocentric" title="Heliocentric">
        heliocentric
       </a>
       models during the
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_revolution" title="Scientific revolution">
        scientific revolution
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       His
       <i>
        Planetary Hypotheses
       </i>
       went beyond the mathematical model of the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       to present a physical realization of the universe as a set of nested spheres,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which he used the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicycle" title="Epicycle">
        epicycles
       </a>
       of his planetary model to compute the dimensions of the universe. He estimated the Sun was at an average distance of 1,210 Earth radii, while the radius of the sphere of the fixed stars was 20,000 times the radius of the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy presented a useful tool for astronomical calculations in his
       <i>
        Handy Tables
       </i>
       , which tabulated all the data needed to compute the positions of the Sun, Moon and planets, the rising and setting of the stars, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eclipses" title="Eclipses">
        eclipses
       </a>
       of the Sun and Moon. Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Handy Tables
       </i>
       provided the model for later astronomical tables or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Zij" title="Zij">
         zījes
        </a>
       </i>
       . In the
       <i>
        Phaseis
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Risings of the Fixed Stars
       </i>
       ), Ptolemy gave a
       <i>
        parapegma
       </i>
       , a star
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">
        calendar
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Almanac" title="Almanac">
        almanac
       </a>
       , based on the hands and disappearances of stars over the course of the solar year.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Geography">
        The
        <i>
         Geography
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: The Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">
        Geography (Ptolemy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:La_Cosmographie_de_Claude_Ptolem%C3%A9e.djvu?page=9" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Geography
         </i>
         by Ptolemy, Latin manuscript of the early 15th century
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's other main work is his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geography_(Ptolemy)" title="Geography (Ptolemy)">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       (also called the
       <i>
        Geographia
       </i>
       ), a compilation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographical_coordinates" title="Geographical coordinates">
        geographical coordinates
       </a>
       of the part of the world known to the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       during his time. He relied somewhat on the work of an earlier geographer,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marinos_of_Tyre" title="Marinos of Tyre">
        Marinos of Tyre
       </a>
       , and on
       <a href="/wiki/Gazetteer" title="Gazetteer">
        gazetteers
       </a>
       of the Roman and ancient
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Iran" title="History of Iran">
        Persian Empire
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       He also acknowledged ancient astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       for having provided the elevation of the north pole for a few cities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first part of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       is a discussion of the data and of the methods he used. As with the model of the solar system in the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       , Ptolemy put all this information into a grand scheme. Following Marinos, he assigned
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate" title="Coordinate">
        coordinates
       </a>
       to all the places and geographic features he knew, in a
       <a href="/wiki/Grid_(spatial_index)" title="Grid (spatial index)">
        grid
       </a>
       that spanned the globe.
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        Latitude
       </a>
       was measured from the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , as it is today, but Ptolemy preferred
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to express it as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Climata" title="Climata">
        <i>
         climata
        </i>
        , the length of the longest day
       </a>
       rather than
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees of arc
       </a>
       : the length of the
       <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">
        midsummer
       </a>
       day increases from 12h to 24h as one goes from the equator to the
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">
        polar circle
       </a>
       . In books 2 through 7, he used degrees and put the
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
        meridian
       </a>
       of 0
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       at the most western land he knew, the "
       <a href="/wiki/Fortunate_Isles" title="Fortunate Isles">
        Blessed Islands
       </a>
       ", often identified as the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       , as suggested by the location of the six dots labelled the "FORTUNATA" islands near the left extreme of the blue sea of Ptolemy's map here reproduced.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PtolemyWorldMap.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1026" data-file-width="1500" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PtolemyWorldMap.jpg/300px-PtolemyWorldMap.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PtolemyWorldMap.jpg/450px-PtolemyWorldMap.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/PtolemyWorldMap.jpg/600px-PtolemyWorldMap.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PtolemyWorldMap.jpg" title="Enlarge">

         </div>

         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy_world_map" title="Ptolemy world map">

         </a>
         , reconstituted from Ptolemy's
         <i>
          Geography
         </i>
         (circa AD 150), indicating the countries of "
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seres" title="Seres">
          Serica
         </a>
         " and "
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinae" title="Sinae">
          Sinae
         </a>
         " (
         <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">
          China
         </a>
         ) at the extreme east, beyond the island of "Taprobane" (
         <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">

         </a>
         , oversized) and the "
         <a href="/wiki/Golden_Chersonese" title="Golden Chersonese">

         </a>
         " (
         <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">
          Malay Peninsula
         </a>
         ).

      </div>

        </a>

         </div>
         Prima Europe tabula. A 15th century copy of Ptolemy's map of Britain
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy also devised and provided instructions on how to create maps both of the whole inhabited world (
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oikoumen%C3%A8" title="Oikoumenè">
         oikoumenè
        </a>
       </i>
       ) and of the Roman provinces. In the second part of the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       , he provided the necessary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographic" title="Topographic">
        topographic
       </a>
       lists, and captions for the maps. His
       <i>
        oikoumenè
       </i>
       spanned 180 degrees of longitude from the Blessed Islands in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       to the middle of
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       , and about 80 degrees of latitude from
       <a href="/wiki/Shetland" title="Shetland">
        Shetland
       </a>
       to anti-Meroe (east coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       ); Ptolemy was well aware that he knew about only a quarter of the globe, and an erroneous extension of China southward suggests his sources did not reach all the way to the Pacific Ocean.
      </p>
      <p>
       The maps in surviving manuscripts of Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       , however, only date from about 1300, after the text was rediscovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Maximus_Planudes" title="Maximus Planudes">
        Maximus Planudes
       </a>
       . It seems likely that the topographical tables in books 2–7 are cumulative texts – texts which were altered and added to as new knowledge became available in the centuries after Ptolemy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This means that information contained in different parts of the Geography is likely to be of different dates.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="5813" data-file-width="8073" height="216" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg/300px-Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg/450px-Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg/600px-Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Claudius_Ptolemy-_The_World.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A printed map from the 15th century depicting Ptolemy's description of the
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">
           Ecumene
          </a>
         </i>
         , (1482, Johannes Schnitzer, engraver).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maps" title="Maps">
        Maps
       </a>
       based on scientific principles had been made since the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       , in the 3rd century BC, but Ptolemy improved
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        map projections
       </a>
       . It is known from a speech by
       <a href="/wiki/Eumenius" title="Eumenius">
        Eumenius
       </a>
       that a world map, an
       <i>
        orbis pictus
       </i>
       , doubtless based on the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       , was on display in a school in
       <a href="/wiki/Autun" title="Autun">
        Augustodunum
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">
        Gaul
       </a>
       in the third century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 15th century, Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       began to be printed with engraved maps; the earliest printed edition with engraved maps was produced in Bologna in 1477, followed quickly by a Roman edition in 1478 (Campbell, 1987). An edition printed at
       <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">
        Ulm
       </a>
       in 1482, including woodcut maps, was the first one printed north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
        Alps
       </a>
       . The maps look distorted when compared to modern maps, because Ptolemy's data were inaccurate. One reason is that Ptolemy estimated the size of the Earth as too small: while
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       found 700
       <i>
        stadia
       </i>
       for a great circle degree on the globe, Ptolemy uses 500
       <i>
        stadia
       </i>
       in the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       . It is highly probable that these were the same
       <i>
        stadion
       </i>
       , since Ptolemy switched from the former scale to the latter between the
       <i>
        Syntaxis
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       , and severely readjusted longitude degrees accordingly. See also
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_units_of_measurement" title="Ancient Greek units of measurement">
        Ancient Greek units of measurement
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_geodesy" title="History of geodesy">
        History of geodesy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Ptolemy derived many of his key latitudes from crude longest day values, his latitudes are erroneous on average by roughly a degree (2 degrees for Byzantium, 4 degrees for Carthage), though capable ancient astronomers knew their latitudes to more like a minute. (Ptolemy's own latitude was in error by 14'.) He agreed (
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       1.4) that longitude was best determined by simultaneous observation of lunar eclipses, yet he was so out of touch with the scientists of his day that he knew of no such data more recent than 500 years before (Arbela eclipse). When switching from 700 stadia per degree to 500, he (or Marinos) expanded longitude differences between cities accordingly (a point first realized by P.Gosselin in 1790), resulting in serious over-stretching of the Earth's east-west scale in degrees, though not distance. Achieving highly precise longitude remained a problem in geography until the application of
       <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">
        Galileo
       </a>
       's Jovian moon method in the 18th century. It must be added that his original topographic list cannot be reconstructed: the long tables with numbers were transmitted to posterity through copies containing many scribal errors, and people have always been adding or improving the topographic data: this is a testimony to the persistent popularity of this influential work in the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_cartography" title="History of cartography">
        history of cartography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astrology">
        Astrology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Astrology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrabiblos" title="Tetrabiblos">
        Tetrabiblos
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_16century.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1076" data-file-width="1000" height="237" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ptolemy_16century.jpg/220px-Ptolemy_16century.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ptolemy_16century.jpg/330px-Ptolemy_16century.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Ptolemy_16century.jpg/440px-Ptolemy_16century.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ptolemy_16century.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The mathematician Claudius Ptolemy 'the Alexandrian', as depicted by a 16th-century engraving
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-portraits_1-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-portraits-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy has been referred to as “a pro-astrological authority of the highest magnitude”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His astrological treatise, a work in four parts, is known by the Greek term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tetrabiblos" title="Tetrabiblos">
         Tetrabiblos
        </a>
       </i>
       , or the Latin equivalent
       <i>
        Quadripartitum
       </i>
       : ‘Four Books’. Ptolemy's own title is unknown, but may have been the term found in some Greek manuscripts:
       <i>
        Apotelesmatika
       </i>
       , roughly meaning 'Astrological Outcomes,' 'Effects' or ‘Prognostics’.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As a source of reference, the
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       is said to have "enjoyed almost the authority of a Bible among the astrological writers of a thousand years or more".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was first translated from Arabic into Latin by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plato_of_Tivoli" title="Plato of Tivoli">
        Plato of Tivoli
       </a>
       (Tiburtinus) in 1138, while he was in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       is an extensive and continually reprinted treatise on the ancient principles of
       <a href="/wiki/Horoscopic_astrology" title="Horoscopic astrology">
        horoscopic astrology
       </a>
       . That it did not quite attain the unrivaled status of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       was, perhaps, because it did not cover some popular areas of the subject, particularly
       <a href="/wiki/Electional_astrology" title="Electional astrology">
        electional astrology
       </a>
       (interpreting astrological charts for a particular moment to determine the outcome of a course of action to be initiated at that time), and
       <a href="/wiki/Medical_astrology" title="Medical astrology">
        medical astrology
       </a>
       , which were later adoptions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The great popularity that the
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       did possess might be attributed to its nature as an exposition of the art of astrology, and as a compendium of astrological lore, rather than as a manual. It speaks in general terms, avoiding illustrations and details of practice. Ptolemy was concerned to defend astrology by defining its limits,
       <a href="/wiki/Ephemeris" title="Ephemeris">
        compiling astronomical data
       </a>
       that he believed was reliable and dismissing practices (such as considering the
       <a href="/wiki/Numerology" title="Numerology">
        numerological
       </a>
       significance of names) that he believed to be without sound basis.
      </p>
      <p>
       Much of the content of the
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       was collected from earlier sources; Ptolemy's achievement was to order his material in a systematic way, showing how the subject could, in his view, be rationalized. It is, indeed, presented as the second part of the study of astronomy of which the
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       was the first, concerned with the influences of the celestial bodies in the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        sublunar
       </a>
       sphere. Thus explanations of a sort are provided for the astrological effects of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planets" title="Planets">
        planets
       </a>
       , based upon their combined effects of heating, cooling, moistening, and drying.
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's astrological outlook was quite practical: he thought that astrology was like
       <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">
        medicine
       </a>
       , that is
       <i>
        conjectural
       </i>
       , because of the many variable factors to be taken into account: the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Race_(classification_of_human_beings)" title="Race (classification of human beings)">
        race
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">
        country
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nurture" title="Nurture">
        upbringing
       </a>
       of a person affects an individual's personality as much as, if not more than, the positions of the Sun, Moon, and planets at the precise moment of their birth, so Ptolemy saw astrology as something to be used in life but in no way relied on entirely.
      </p>
      <p>
       A collection of one hundred
       <a href="/wiki/Aphorism" title="Aphorism">
        aphorisms
       </a>
       about astrology called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Centiloquium" title="Centiloquium">
         Centiloquium
        </a>
       </i>
       , ascribed to Ptolemy, was widely reproduced and commented on by Arabic, Latin and Hebrew scholars, and often bound together in medieval manuscripts after the
       <i>
        Tetrabiblos
       </i>
       as a kind of summation. It is now believed to be a much later
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudepigraph" title="Pseudepigraph">
        pseudepigraphical
       </a>
       composition. The identity and date of the actual author of the work, referred to now as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudo-Ptolemy" title="Pseudo-Ptolemy">
        Pseudo-Ptolemy
       </a>
       , remains the subject of conjecture.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
        Music
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Music">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ptolemy also wrote an influential work,
       <i>
        Harmonics
       </i>
       , on
       <a href="/wiki/Music_theory" title="Music theory">
        music theory
       </a>
       and the mathematics of music. After criticizing the approaches of his predecessors, Ptolemy argued for basing musical intervals on mathematical ratios (in contrast to the followers of
       <a href="/wiki/Aristoxenus" title="Aristoxenus">
        Aristoxenus
       </a>
       and in agreement with the followers of
       <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">
        Pythagoras
       </a>
       ), backed up by empirical observation (in contrast to the overly theoretical approach of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pythagoreans" title="Pythagoreans">
        Pythagoreans
       </a>
       ). Ptolemy wrote about how musical notes could be translated into mathematical equations and vice versa in
       <i>
        Harmonics
       </i>
       . This is called Pythagorean tuning because it was first discovered by Pythagoras. However, Pythagoras believed that the mathematics of music should be based on the specific ratio of 3:2, whereas Ptolemy merely believed that it should just generally involve
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrachord" title="Tetrachord">
        tetrachords
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Octave" title="Octave">
        octaves
       </a>
       . He presented his own divisions of the tetrachord and the octave, which he derived with the help of a
       <a href="/wiki/Monochord" title="Monochord">
        monochord
       </a>
       . His
       <i>
        Harmonics
       </i>
       never had the influence of his
       <i>
        Almagest
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Planetary Hypotheses
       </i>
       , but a part of it (Book III) did encourage
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler" title="Kepler">
        Kepler
       </a>
       in his own musings on the harmony of the world (Kepler,
       <i>
        Harmonice Mundi
       </i>
       , Appendix to Book V).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ptolemy's astronomical interests also appeared in a discussion of the "
       <a href="/wiki/Musica_universalis" title="Musica universalis">
        music of the spheres
       </a>
       ". See:
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_intense_diatonic_scale" title="Ptolemy's intense diatonic scale">
        Ptolemy's intense diatonic scale
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optics">
        Optics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ptolemy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Optics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Optics_(Ptolemy)" title="Optics (Ptolemy)">
        Optics (Ptolemy)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       His
       <i>
        Optics
       </i>
       is a work that survives only in a poor Arabic translation and in about twenty manuscripts of a Latin version of the Arabic, which was translated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eugene_of_Palermo" title="Eugene of Palermo">
        Eugene of Palermo
       </a>
       (c. 1154). In it Ptolemy writes about properties of
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_(physics)" title="Reflection (physics)">
        reflection
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refraction
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colour" title="Colour">
        colour
       </a>
       . The work is a significant part of the early
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_optics" title="History of optics">
        history of optics
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and influenced the more famous 11th-century
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Optics" title="Book of Optics">
         Book of Optics
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alhazen" title="Alhazen">
        Alhazen
       </a>
       (Ibn al-Haytham). It contains the earliest surviving table of refraction from air to water, for which the values (with the exception of the 60° angle of incidence), although historically praised as experimentally derived, appear to have been obtained from an arithmetic progression.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The work is also important for the early history of perception. Ptolemy combined the mathematical, philosophical and physiological traditions. He held an extramission-intromission theory of vision: the rays (or flux) from the eye formed a cone, the vertex being within the eye, and the base defining the visual field. The rays were sensitive, and conveyed information back to the observer’s intellect about the distance and orientation of surfaces. Size and shape were determined by the visual angle subtended at the eye combined with perceived distance and orientation. This was one of the early statements of size-distance invariance as a cause of perceptual size and shape constancy, a view supported by the Stoics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ptolemy offered explanations for many phenomena concerning illumination and colour, size, shape, movement and binocular vision. He also divided illusions into those caused by physical or optical factors and those caused by judgmental factors. He offered an obscure explanation of the sun or
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">
        moon illusion
       </a>
       (the enlarged apparent size on the horizon) based on the difficulty of looking upwards.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
