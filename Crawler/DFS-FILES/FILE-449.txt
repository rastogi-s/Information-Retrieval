      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        grammatical gender
       </b>
       is a specific form of
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_class" title="Noun class">
        noun-class
       </a>
       system in which the division of noun classes forms an agreement system with another aspect of the language, such as adjectives, articles, pronouns, or verbs. This system is used in approximately one quarter of the world's
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       . In these languages, most or all nouns inherently carry one value of the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical category
       </a>
       called
       <i>
        gender
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the values present in a given language (of which there are usually two or three) are called the
       <i>
        genders
       </i>
       of that language. According to one definition: "Genders are classes of nouns reflected in the behaviour of associated words."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hockett_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hockett-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett19914_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett19914-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Common gender divisions include masculine and feminine; masculine, feminine and neuter; or
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animate
       </a>
       and inanimate. In a few languages, the gender assignment of nouns is solely determined by their meaning or attributes, like biological sex,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       humanness, animacy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in most languages, this
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">
        semantic
       </a>
       division is only partially valid, and many nouns may belong to a gender category that contrasts with their meaning (e.g. the word for "manliness" could be of feminine gender).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this case, the gender assignment can also be influenced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       of the noun, or in some cases can be apparently arbitrary.
      </p>
      <p>
       Grammatical gender manifests itself when words related to a noun like
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       change their form (
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inflect" title="Inflect">
         inflect
        </a>
       </i>
       ) according to the gender of noun they refer to (
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        <i>
         agreement
        </i>
       </a>
       ). The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parts_of_speech" title="Parts of speech">
        parts of speech
       </a>
       affected by gender agreement, the circumstances in which it occurs, and the way words are marked for gender vary between languages. Gender inflection may interact with other grammatical categories like
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       . In some languages the
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       pattern followed by the noun itself will be different for different genders.
      </p>
      <p>
       Grammatical gender is found in many
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       (including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       —but not
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       , for example),
       <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">
        Afroasiatic languages
       </a>
       (which includes the
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Berber_languages" title="Berber languages">
        Berber languages
       </a>
       , etc.), and in other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_families" title="Language families">
        language families
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">
        Northeast Caucasian
       </a>
       , as well as several
       <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_languages" title="Australian Aboriginal languages">
        Australian Aboriginal languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Dyirbal_language" title="Dyirbal language">
        Dyirbal
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kala_Lagaw_Ya_language" title="Kala Lagaw Ya language">
        Kalaw Lagaw Ya
       </a>
       . Most
       <a href="/wiki/Niger%E2%80%93Congo_languages" title="Niger–Congo languages">
        Niger–Congo languages
       </a>
       also have extensive systems of noun classes, which can be grouped into several grammatical genders. Conversely, grammatical gender is usually absent from the
       <a href="/wiki/Koreanic_languages" title="Koreanic languages">
        Koreanic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japonic_languages" title="Japonic languages">
        Japonic
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tungusic_language" title="Tungusic language">
        Tungusic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolic_languages" title="Mongolic languages">
        Mongolic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sino-Tibetan_languages" title="Sino-Tibetan languages">
        Sino-Tibetan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic
       </a>
       and most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Native_American_languages" title="Native American languages">
        Native American
       </a>
       language families.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett19912_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett19912-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">
        Modern English
       </a>
       makes use of gender in pronouns, which are generally marked for natural gender, but lacks a system of gender concord within the
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       which is one of the central elements of grammatical gender in most other Indo-European languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-audring_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-audring-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gato_vs_gata,_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2575" data-file-width="1469" height="438" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg/250px-Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg/375px-Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg/500px-Gato_vs_gata%2C_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gato_vs_gata,_illustration_of_grammatical_gender_in_Spanish.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The grammatical gender of a noun affects the form of other words related to it. For example, in
         <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
          Spanish
         </a>
         , determiners, adjectives, and pronouns change their form depending on the noun to which they refer.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley200427,_52_11-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley200427,_52-11">
           [11]
          </a>
         </sup>
         Spanish nouns have two genders: masculine and feminine, represented here by the nouns
         <i>
          gato
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          gata
         </i>
         respectively.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In languages with grammatical gender, each
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       is assigned to one of the classes called
       <i>
        genders
       </i>
       , which form a closed set. Most such languages usually have from two to four different genders, but some are attested with up to 20.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hockett_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hockett-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dixon_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dixon-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sil-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The division into genders usually correlates to some degree, at least for a certain set of nouns (such as those denoting humans), with some property or properties of the things that particular nouns denote. Such properties include
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animacy
       </a>
       or inanimacy, "
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        humanness
       </a>
       " or non-humanness, and biological
       <a href="/wiki/Sex" title="Sex">
        sex
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Few or no nouns can occur in more than one class.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hockett_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-hockett-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dixon_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-dixon-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-sil-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Depending on the language and the word, this assignment might bear some relationship with the meaning of the noun (e.g. "woman" is usually feminine), or may be arbitrary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gender is considered an inherent quality of nouns, and it affects the forms of other related words, a process called
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       . Nouns may be considered the "triggers" of the process, whereas other words will be the "target" of these changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These related words can be, depending on the language:
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
        numerals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(linguistics)" title="Quantifier (linguistics)">
        quantifiers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
        possessives
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , past and passive
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        participles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        articles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverbs" title="Adverbs">
        adverbs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Complementizer" title="Complementizer">
        complementizers
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adposition" title="Adposition">
        adpositions
       </a>
       . Gender class may be marked on the noun itself, but will also always be marked on other constituents in a noun phrase or sentence. If the noun is explicitly marked, both trigger and target may feature similar alternations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dixon_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-dixon-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Common systems of gender division include:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         masculine–feminine
        </b>
        : here nouns that denote specifically male persons (or animals) are normally of masculine gender; those that denote specifically female persons (or animals) are normally of feminine gender; and nouns that denote something that does not have any sex, or do not specify the sex of their referent, have come to belong to one or other of the genders, in a way that may appear arbitrary.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578_15-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Examples of languages with such a system include most of the modern
        <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
         Romance languages
        </a>
        , the
        <a href="/wiki/Baltic_languages" title="Baltic languages">
         Baltic languages
        </a>
        , the
        <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
         Celtic languages
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">
         Hindustani
        </a>
        , and the
        <a href="/wiki/Afroasiatic_languages" title="Afroasiatic languages">
         Afroasiatic languages
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         masculine–feminine–neuter
        </b>
        : this is similar to the masculine–feminine system, except that there is a third available gender, so nouns with sexless or unspecified-sex referents may be either masculine, feminine, or neuter. There are also certain exceptional nouns whose gender does not follow the denoted sex, such as the German
        <i>
         Mädchen
        </i>
        , meaning "girl", which is neuter. This is because it is actually a diminutive of "Magd" and all diminutive forms with the suffix
        <i>
         -chen
        </i>
        are neuter. Examples of languages with such a system include later forms of
        <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
         Proto-Indo-European
        </a>
        (see
        <a href="#Indo-European">
         below
        </a>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
         Sanskrit
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
         Norwegian
        </a>
        in most dialects and in both written forms (Norwegian Bokmål and Norwegian Nynorsk),
        <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">
         Marathi
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Modern_Greek_grammar" title="Modern Greek grammar">
         Greek
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        , standard
        <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
         Dutch
        </a>
        and some dialects
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
         Slavic languages
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         animate–inanimate
        </b>
        : here nouns that denote animate things (humans and animals) generally belong to one gender, and those that denote inanimate things to another (although there may be some deviation from that principle). Examples include earlier forms of Proto-Indo-European and the earliest family known to have split off from it, the extinct
        <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_languages" title="Anatolian languages">
         Anatolian languages
        </a>
        (see
        <a href="#Indo-European">
         below
        </a>
        ). Modern examples include, to some extent,
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_grammar" title="Basque grammar">
         Basque
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Ojibwe_language" title="Ojibwe language">
         Ojibwe
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199120–21_19-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199120–21-19">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         common–neuter
        </b>
        : here a masculine–feminine–neuter system previously existed, but the distinction between masculine and feminine genders has been lost (they have merged into what is called
        <i>
         common gender
        </i>
        ). Thus nouns denoting people are usually of common gender, whereas other nouns may be of either gender. Examples include
        <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
         Danish
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        (see
        <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_Danish_and_Swedish" title="Gender in Danish and Swedish">
         Gender in Danish and Swedish
        </a>
        ), and to some extent Dutch (see
        <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_Dutch_grammar" title="Gender in Dutch grammar">
         Gender in Dutch grammar
        </a>
        ). The dialect of the old Norwegian capital
        <a href="/wiki/Bergen" title="Bergen">
         Bergen
        </a>
        also use common gender and neuter exclusively. The common gender in Bergen and in Danish is conjugated with the same articles and suffixes as the masculine gender in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Norwegian Bokmål">
         Norwegian Bokmål
        </a>
        . This makes some obviously feminine noun phrases like "a cute girl", "the well milking cow" or "the pregnant mares" sound strange to most Norwegian ears when spoken by Danes and people from Bergen since they are conjugated in a way that sounds like the masculine conjugations in South-Eastern Norwegian dialects. The same does not apply to
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        common gender, as the conjugations follow a different pattern than both the Norwegian written languages.
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Nynorsk" title="Norwegian Nynorsk">
         Norwegian Nynorsk
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Norwegian Bokmål">
         Norwegian Bokmål
        </a>
        and most spoken dialects retain masculine, feminine and neuter even if their Scandinavian neighbours have lost one of the genders. As shown, the merger of masculine and feminine in these languages and dialects can be considered a reversal of the original split in Proto-Indo-European (see
        <a href="#Indo-European">
         below
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other types of division or subdivision may be found in particular languages. These may sometimes be referred to as
       <i>
        classes
       </i>
       rather than genders; for some examples, see
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_class" title="Noun class">
        Noun class
       </a>
       . In some of the
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , for example, within the masculine and sometimes feminine and neuter genders, there is a further division between animate and inanimate nouns – and in
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_grammar" title="Polish grammar">
        Polish
       </a>
       , also sometimes between nouns denoting humans and non-humans. (For details, see
       <a href="#Slavic_languages">
        below
       </a>
       .) A human–non-human (or "rational–non-rational") distinction is also found in
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences_of_gender">
        Consequences of gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Consequences of gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The grammatical gender of a noun manifests itself in two principal ways: in the modifications that the noun itself undergoes, and in modifications of other related words (
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       ). These are described in the following sections.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_inflection">
        Noun inflection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Noun inflection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The gender of a noun may affect the modifications that the noun itself undergoes, particularly the way in which the noun
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflects
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       . For example, a language like
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       has a number of different declension patterns, and which pattern a particular noun follows may be highly correlated with its gender. For some instances of this, see
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        Latin declension
       </a>
       . A concrete example is provided by the German word
       <i>
        See
       </i>
       , which has two possible genders: when it is masculine (meaning "lake") its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitive" title="Genitive">
        genitive
       </a>
       singular form is
       <i>
        Sees
       </i>
       , but when it is feminine (meaning "sea"), the genitive is
       <i>
        See
       </i>
       , because feminine nouns do not take the genitive
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Gender is sometimes reflected in other ways. In
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       , gender marking is mostly lost on nouns; however, Welsh has
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_mutation#Celtic_languages" title="Consonant mutation">
        initial mutation
       </a>
       , where the first consonant of a word changes into another in certain conditions. Gender is one of the factors that can cause one form of mutation (soft mutation). For instance, the word
       <i>
        merch
       </i>
       "girl" changes into
       <i>
        ferch
       </i>
       after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       . This only occurs with feminine singular nouns:
       <i>
        mab
       </i>
       "son" remains unchanged. Adjectives are affected by gender in a similar way.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Additionally, in many languages, gender is often closely correlated with the basic unmodified form (
       <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">
        lemma
       </a>
       ) of the noun, and sometimes a noun can be modified to produce (for example) masculine and feminine words of similar meaning. See
       <a href="#Correlation_between_gender_and_the_form_of_a_noun">
        § Correlation between gender and the form of a noun
       </a>
       , below.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Agreement">
        Agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Agreement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        Agreement
       </a>
       , or concord, is a grammatical process in which certain words change their form so that values of certain
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical categories
       </a>
       match those of related words. Gender is one of the categories which frequently require agreement. In this case, nouns may be considered the "triggers" of the process, because they have an inherent gender, whereas related words that change their form to match the gender of the noun can be considered the "target" of these changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       These related words can be, depending on the language:
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Numeral_(linguistics)" title="Numeral (linguistics)">
        numerals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quantifier_(linguistics)" title="Quantifier (linguistics)">
        quantifiers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Possessive" title="Possessive">
        possessives
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , past and passive
       <a href="/wiki/Participle" title="Participle">
        participles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adverbs" title="Adverbs">
        adverbs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Complementizer" title="Complementizer">
        complementizers
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adposition" title="Adposition">
        adpositions
       </a>
       . Gender class may be marked on the noun itself, but can also be marked on other constituents in a noun phrase or sentence. If the noun is explicitly marked, both trigger and target may feature similar alternations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dixon_12-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-dixon-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572_14-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200572-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200578-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As an example, we consider
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , a language with two noun genders: masculine and feminine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley200418_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley200418-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Among other lexical items, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       changes its form according to the gender of the noun. In the singular, the article is:
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       (masculine),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       (feminine).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBradley200427_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBradley200427-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, nouns referring to male beings carry the masculine article, and female beings the feminine article (agreement).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       In the Spanish sentences
       <i>

       </i>
       "
       <i>

       </i>
       is a good actor" and
       <i>

       </i>
       "
       <i>

       </i>
       is a good actress", almost every word undergoes gender-related changes. The noun
       <i>

       </i>
       changes by replacing the masculine suffix

       </i>
       with the feminine suffix

        -riz
       </i>
       , the personal pronoun
       <i>
        él
       </i>
       "he" changes to
       <i>

       <i>

       </i>
       is added to the article (
       <i>
        un
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        una
       </i>
       ) and to the adjective (
       <i>
        buen
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        buena
       </i>
       ).
      </p>

       When a verb is used as a noun, it is almost always masculine.

       The word
       <i>

       <i>

       "shield". Because this noun was grammatically feminine, the adjectives
       <i>
        brade
       </i>
       "broad" and
       <i>
        tilu
       </i>
       "good", as well as the pronouns
       <i>
        seo
       </i>
       "the/that" and
       <i>
        hire
       </i>
       "her", which referred to
       <i>
        lind
       </i>
       , must also appear in their feminine forms. Old English had three genders, masculine, feminine and neuter, but gender inflections (like many other types of inflection in English) were later greatly simplified by
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound changes
       </a>
       , and then completely lost.
      </p>
      <p>
       In modern English, by contrast, the noun
       <i>
        shield
       </i>
       takes the neuter pronoun
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       , because it designates a sexless object. In a sense, the neuter gender has grown to encompass most nouns, including many that were masculine or feminine in Old English. If one were to replace the phrase "broad shield" above with
       <i>
        brave man
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        brave woman
       </i>
       , the only change to the rest of the sentence would be in the pronoun at the end, which would become
       <i>
        him
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       respectively.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_assignment">
        Gender assignment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gender assignment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:French_country_name_genders.svg">

         <i>
          Mexique
         </i>
         ,
         <i>

         </i>
         ,
         <i>

         </i>

         <i>

         <i>

         .

      <p>
       There are three main ways by which natural languages categorize nouns into genders: according to logical or symbolic similarities in their meaning (
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantic
       </a>
       ), by grouping them with other nouns that have similar form (
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       ), and through apparently arbitrary convention (lexical, possibly rooted in the language's history). In most languages that have grammatical gender, a combination of these three types of criteria is found, although one type may be more prevalent.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Strict_semantic_criteria">
        Strict semantic criteria
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Strict semantic criteria">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some languages, the gender of a noun is directly determined by its physical attributes (sex, animacy, etc.), and there are few or no exceptions to this rule. There are relatively few such languages. The
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       use this system as described
       <a href="#Dravidian">
        below
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dizi_language" title="Dizi language">
        Dizi language
       </a>
       , which has two asymmetrical genders. The feminine includes all living beings of female sex (e.g. woman, girl, cow...), and
       <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">
        diminutives
       </a>
       ; the masculine encompasses all other nouns (e.g. man, boy, pot, broom...). In this language, feminine nouns are always marked with
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -in
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199111_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199111-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another African language,
       <a href="/wiki/Defaka_language" title="Defaka language">
        Defaka
       </a>
       , has three genders: one for all male humans, one for all female humans, and a third for all the remaining nouns. Gender is only marked in personal pronouns. Standard English pronouns (see
       <a href="#English">
        below
       </a>
       ) are very similar in this respect, although the English gendered pronouns (
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       ) are used for domestic animals if the sex of the animal is known, and sometimes for certain objects such as ships,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199112_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199112-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       e.g. "What happened to the Titanic? She (or it) sank."
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mostly_semantic_criteria">
        Mostly semantic criteria
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Mostly semantic criteria">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some other languages, the gender of nouns can again mostly be determined by physical (semantic) attributes, although there remain some nouns whose gender is not assigned in this way (Corbett calls this "semantic residue").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199113_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199113-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The world view (e.g. mythology) of the speakers may influence the division of categories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199132_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199132-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An example is the
       <a href="/wiki/Zande_language" title="Zande language">
        Zande language
       </a>
       , which has four genders: male human, female human, animal, and inanimate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199114_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199114-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, there are about 80 nouns representing inanimate entities which are nonetheless animate in gender: heavenly objects (moon, rainbow), metal objects (hammer, ring), edible plants (sweet potato, pea), and non-metallic objects (whistle, ball). Many have a round shape or can be explained by the role they play in mythology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199114_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199114-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Ket_language" title="Ket language">
        Ket language
       </a>
       has three genders (masculine, feminine, and neuter), and most gender assignment is based on semantics, but there are many inanimate nouns outside the neuter class. Masculine nouns include male animates, most fish, trees, the moon, large wooden objects, most living beings and some religious items. Feminine nouns include female animates, three types of fish, some plants, the sun and other heavenly objects, some body parts and skin diseases, the soul, and some religious items. Words for part of a whole, as well as most other nouns that do not fall into any of the aforementioned classes, are neuter. The gender assignment of non-sex-differentiable things is complex. In general, those of no importance to the Kets are feminine, whereas objects of importance (e.g. fish, wood) are masculine. Mythology is again a significant factor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199119_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199119-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Alamblak_language" title="Alamblak language">
        Alamblak language
       </a>
       has two genders, masculine and feminine. However, the masculine also includes things which are tall or long and slender, or narrow (e.g. fish, snakes, arrows and slender trees), whereas the feminine gender has things which are short, squat or wide (e.g. turtles, houses, shields and squat trees).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett199132_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett199132-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The distinction between the gender of a noun and the gender of the object it refers to is clear when nouns of different genders can be used for the same object, e.g. French vélo (m.) = bicyclette (f.).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Correlation_between_gender_and_the_form_of_a_noun">
        Correlation between gender and the form of a noun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Correlation between gender and the form of a noun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In many other languages, nouns are assigned to gender largely without any semantic basis – that is, not based on any feature (such as animacy or sex) of the person or thing that a noun represents. However, in many languages there may be a correlation, to a greater or lesser degree, between gender and the form of a noun (such as the letter or syllable with which it ends).
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , nouns that end in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       or a consonant are mostly masculine, whereas those that end in
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       are mostly feminine, regardless of their meaning. (Nouns that end in some other vowel are assigned a gender either according to
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       , by analogy, or by some other convention.) These rules may override semantics in some cases: for example, the noun
       <i>
        membro
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        miembro
       </i>
       ("member") is always masculine, even when it refers to a girl or a woman, and
       <i>
        pessoa
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        persona
       </i>
       ("person") is always feminine, even when it refers to a boy or a man. (In other cases, though, meaning takes precedence: the noun
       <i>
        comunista
       </i>
       "communist" is masculine when it refers or could refer to a man, even though it ends with
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       .) In fact, nouns in Spanish and Portuguese (as in the other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       such as Italian and French) generally follow the gender of the Latin words from which they are derived. When nouns deviate from the rules for gender, there is usually an etymological explanation:
       <i>
        problema
       </i>
       ("problem") is masculine in Spanish because it was derived from a Greek noun of the neuter gender, whereas
       <i>
        radio
       </i>
       ("radio station") is feminine, because it is a shortening of
       <i>
        estación de radio
       </i>
       , a phrase whose
       <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
        head
       </a>
       is the feminine noun
       <i>
        estación
       </i>
       . (Most Spanish nouns in
       <i>
        -ión
       </i>
       are feminine; they derive from Latin feminines in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        Suffixes
       </a>
       often carry a specific gender. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Diminutive" title="Diminutive">
        diminutives
       </a>
       with the suffixes
       <i>
        -chen
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -lein
       </i>
       (meaning "little, young") are always neuter, even if they refer to people, as with
       <i>
        Mädchen
       </i>
       ("girl") and
       <i>
        Fräulein
       </i>
       ("young woman") (see
       <a href="#Natural_gender">
        below
       </a>
       ). Similarly, the suffix
       <i>
        -ling
       </i>
       , which makes
       <a href="/wiki/Count_noun" title="Count noun">
        countable nouns
       </a>
       from uncountable nouns (
       <i>
        Teig
       </i>
       "dough" →
       <i>
        Teigling
       </i>
       "piece of dough"), or personal nouns from abstract nouns (
       <i>
        Lehre
       </i>
       "teaching",
       <i>
        Strafe
       </i>
       "punishment" →
       <i>
        Lehrling
       </i>
       "apprentice",
       <i>
        Sträfling
       </i>
       "convict") or adjectives (
       <i>
        feige
       </i>
       "cowardly" →
       <i>
        Feigling
       </i>
       "coward"), always produces masculine nouns. And the German suffixes
       <i>
        -heit
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -keit
       </i>
       (comparable with
       <i>
        -hood
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ness
       </i>
       in English) produce feminine nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       , nouns ending in
       <i>
        -óir/-eoir
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ín
       </i>
       are always masculine, whereas those ending
       <i>
        -óg/-eog
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -lann
       </i>
       are always feminine.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , nouns whose singular form ends in a
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C4%81%CA%BE_marb%C5%AB%E1%B9%ADa" title="Tāʾ marbūṭa">
         tāʾ marbūṭa
        </a>
       </i>
       (traditionally a
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Alveolar" title="Voiceless dental and alveolar stops">
         t
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       , becoming
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Pausa" title="Pausa">
        pausa
       </a>
       ) are of feminine gender, the only significant exceptions being the word
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        خليفة
       </span>
       <i>
        khalīfah
       </i>
       ("
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">
        caliph
       </a>
       ") and certain masculine personal names (e.g.
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        أسامة
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Osama_(name)" title="Osama (name)">
        ʾUsāmah
       </a>
       ). However, many masculine nouns take a
       <i>
        tāʾ marbūṭa
       </i>
       in their plural; for example
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        أستاذ
       </span>
       <i>
        ustādh
       </i>
       ("male professor") has the plural
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        أساتذة
       </span>
       <i>
        asātidha
       </i>
       , which might be confused for a feminine singular noun. Gender may also be predictable from the type of
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">
        derivation
       </a>
       : for instance, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_verbal_noun" title="Arabic verbal noun">
        verbal nouns
       </a>
       of Stem II (e.g.
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        التفعيل
       </span>
       <i>
        al-tafʿīl
       </i>
       , from
       <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        فعّل، يفعّل
       </span>
       <i>
        faʿʿala, yufaʿʿil
       </i>
       ) are always masculine.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , nouns ending in
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       tend to be feminine, whereas others tend to be masculine, but there are many exceptions to this (e.g.
       <i>
        cadre
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        arbre
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        signe
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        meuble
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        nuage
       </i>
       are masculine as
       <i>
        façon
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        chanson
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        voix
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        main
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eau
       </i>
       are feminine). Certain suffixes are quite reliable indicators, such as
       <i>
        -age
       </i>
       , which when added to a verb (e.g.
       <i>
        garer
       </i>
       "to park" -&gt;
       <i>
        garage
       </i>
       ; nettoyer "to clean" -&gt;
       <i>
        nettoyage
       </i>
       "cleaning") indicates a masculine noun; however, when -
       <i>
        age
       </i>
       is part of the root of the word, it can be feminine, as in
       <i>
        plage
       </i>
       ("beach") or
       <i>
        image
       </i>
       . On the other hand, nouns ending in
       <i>
        -tion
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -sion
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -aison
       </i>
       are all feminine.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nouns can sometimes vary their form to enable the
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">
        derivation
       </a>
       of differently gendered
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cognate_(linguistics)" title="Cognate (linguistics)">
        cognate
       </a>
       nouns; for example, to produce nouns with a similar meaning but referring to someone of a different sex. Thus, in Spanish,
       <i>
        niño
       </i>
       means "boy", and
       <i>
        niña
       </i>
       means "girl". This paradigm can be exploited for making new words: from the masculine nouns
       <i>
        abogado
       </i>
       "lawyer",
       <i>
        diputado
       </i>
       "member of parliament" and
       <i>
        doctor
       </i>
       "doctor", it was straightforward to make the feminine equivalents
       <i>
        abogada
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        diputada
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        doctora
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the same way,
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_name" title="Personal name">
        personal names
       </a>
       are frequently constructed with affixes that identify the sex of the bearer. Common feminine suffixes used in English names are
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       , of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       origin (cf.
       <i>
        Robert
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Roberta
       </i>
       ); and
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       , of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       origin (cf.
       <i>
        Justin
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Justine
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Although gender inflection may be used to construct nouns and names for people of opposite sexes in languages that have grammatical gender, this alone does not constitute grammatical gender. Distinct words and names for men and women are also common in languages which do not have a grammatical gender system for nouns in general. English, for example, has feminine suffixes such as
       <i>
        -ess
       </i>
       (as in
       <i>
        actress
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        poetess
       </i>
       , etc.), and also distinguishes male and female personal names, as in the above examples.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_in_personal_names">
        Gender in personal names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Gender in personal names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="352" data-file-width="538" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png/220px-Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png/330px-Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png/440px-Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Spanish_names%26nouns_ending_in_a_Statistics.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Statistical data on the Spanish nouns and names ending in a
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Given names are proper nouns and they follow the same gender grammatical rules as common nouns. In most Indo-European languages female grammatical gender is created using an “a” or an “e” ending.
      </p>
      <p>
       Classical Latin typically made a grammatical feminine gender in “a” (silva - forest, aqua - water) and this was reflected in feminine names originating in that period, like Emilia. Romance languages preserved this characteristic. For example, Spanish has approximately 89% feminine nouns with an “a” ending and 98% given names with the same ending.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Germanic languages the female names have been Latinized by adding “e” and “a”: Brunhild, Kriemhild and Hroswith became Brunhilde, Kriemhilde and Hroswitha. Slavic feminine given names: Olga (Russian), Małgorzata (Polish), Tetiana (Ukrainian), Oksana (Belarusian), Eliška (Czech), Bronislava (Slovak), Milica (Serbian), Darina (Bulgarian), Lucja (Croatian), Lamija (Bosnian) and Zala (Slovenian).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_absence_of_criteria">
        Apparent absence of criteria
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Apparent absence of criteria">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some languages, any gender markers have been so eroded over time (possibly through
       <a href="/wiki/Deflexion_(linguistics)" title="Deflexion (linguistics)">
        deflexion
       </a>
       ) that they are no longer recognizable. Many German nouns, for example, do not indicate their gender through either meaning or form. In such cases a noun's gender must simply be memorized, and gender can be regarded as an integral part of each noun when considered as an entry in the speaker's
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       . (This is reflected in
       <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">
        dictionaries
       </a>
       , which typically indicate the gender of noun
       <a href="/wiki/Headword" title="Headword">
        headwords
       </a>
       where applicable.)
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second-language_learner" title="Second-language learner">
        Second-language learners
       </a>
       are often encouraged to memorize a modifier, usually a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       , in conjunction with each noun – for example, a learner of French may learn the word for "chair" as
       <i>
        la chaise
       </i>
       (meaning "the chair"); this carries the information that the noun is
       <i>
        chaise
       </i>
       , and that it is feminine (because
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       is the feminine singular form of the definite article).
      </p>
      <p>
       When learning a second language such as Spanish speakers are encouraged to memorize articles (either the "el" or the "la" in front of the word. Generally they correspond with "-o" being masculine and "-a" being feminine. However it is worth noting that there are exceptions to this rule. Examples of some words that are considered exceptions would be
       <i>
        el panda
       </i>
       (the panda),
       <i>
        el día
       </i>
       (the day),
       <i>
        la mano
       </i>
       (the hand),
       <i>
        el cura
       </i>
       (the priest), etc.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns_with_more_than_one_gender">
        Nouns with more than one gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Nouns with more than one gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It is relatively uncommon for a noun to have more than one possible gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hockett_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-hockett-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dixon_12-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-dixon-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sil_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-sil-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When this happens, it may be associated with a difference in the sex of the referent (as with nouns such as
       <i>
        comunista
       </i>
       in Spanish, which may be either masculine or feminine, depending on whether it refers to a male or a female), or with some other difference in the meaning of the word. For example, the German word
       <i>
        See
       </i>
       meaning "lake" is masculine, whereas the identical word meaning "sea" is feminine. In this sense it is used to disambiguate
       <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">
        homonyms
       </a>
       , especially when the different meanings are totally unrelated. One example is the Norwegian noun "ting". The masculine "en ting" is "a thing" whereas neutral "et ting" is an assembly: the parliament is the Storting, the other
       <i>
        tings
       </i>
       like Borgarting are the regional courts. Another example is the Bulgarian word
       <i>
        пръст
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        prəst
       </i>
       ) which means "finger" when masculine and "soil" when feminine. In even rarer cases the gender may be ambiguous, e.g. in Bulgarian the word
       <i>
        пу̀стош
       </i>
       , (
       <i>
        pustosh
       </i>
       , "wilderness") may be either masculine (definite form
       <i>
        пу̀стоша
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pustoshə
       </i>
       ) or feminine (definite form
       <i>
        пустошта̀
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        pustoshta
       </i>
       ) without any change in meaning and no preference in usage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes a noun's gender can change between plural and singular, as with the French words
       <i>
        amour
       </i>
       ("love"),
       <i>
        délice
       </i>
       ("delight") and
       <i>
        orgue
       </i>
       ("organ" as musical instrument), all of which are masculine in the singular but feminine in the plural. These anomalies may have a historical explanation (
       <i>
        amour
       </i>
       used to be feminine in the singular too) or result from slightly different notions (
       <i>
        orgue
       </i>
       in the singular is usually a
       <a href="/wiki/Barrel_organ" title="Barrel organ">
        barrel organ
       </a>
       , whereas the plural
       <i>
        orgues
       </i>
       usually refers to the collection of columns in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Church_organ" title="Church organ">
        church organ
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Disputed statement">
          <span title="This claim has reliable sources with contradicting facts (December 2013)">
           disputed
          </span>
         </a>
         <span class="metadata">
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:Grammatical_gender" title="Talk:Grammatical gender">
           discuss
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . Further examples are the Italian words
       <i>
        uovo
       </i>
       ("egg") and
       <i>
        braccio
       </i>
       ("arm"). These are masculine in the singular, but form the irregular plurals
       <i>
        uova
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        braccia
       </i>
       , which have the endings of the feminine singular, but have feminine plural agreement. (This is related to the forms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_declension" title="Latin declension">
        second declension
       </a>
       Latin neuter nouns from which they derive:
       <i>
        ovum
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bracchium
       </i>
       , with nominative plurals
       <i>
        ova
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bracchia
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       In Norwegian, many nouns could be either feminine or masculine according to the dialect, level of formality or whim of the speaker/writer. Even the two written forms of the language have many nouns whose gender is optional. Choosing the masculine gender will often seem more formal than using the feminine. This might be because before the creation of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Nynorsk" title="Norwegian Nynorsk">
        Norwegian Nynorsk
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Norwegian Bokmål">
        Norwegian Bokmål
       </a>
       in the late 19th century, Norwegians wrote Danish, which has lost the feminine gender, thus making use of the masculine gender (corresponding exactly to Danish common gender in conjugation in Norwegian Bokmål) more old-fashioned and formal sounding to modern Norwegians. "Woman" can be conjugated with either gender and "damen" (masculine) is more formal than "dama" feminine. The feminine form will not normally be used in official documents in bokmål or spoken in western Oslo (the "dialect" used by the royal family) or in Bergen.
      </p>
      <p>
       The noun "sun" can be another example. You might decline it masculine: "En sol, solen, soler, solene" or feminine "Ei sol, sola, soler, solene" in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norwegian_Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Norwegian Bokmål">
        Norwegian Bokmål
       </a>
       . The same goes for a lot of common words like "bok" (book), "dukke" (doll), "bøtte" (bucket) and so forth. Interestingly, many of the words where it is possible to choose gender are inanimate objects that one might suspect would be conjugated with the neuter gender. Nouns conjugated with the neuter gender can normally not be conjugated as feminine or masculine in Norwegian. There is also a slight tendency towards using the masculine indefinite article even when choosing the feminine conjugation of a noun in many eastern Norwegian dialects. For instance, girl: "En jente - jenta - jenter - jentene".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Genderless_nouns">
        Genderless nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Genderless nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some languages the gender is distinguished only in singular number but not in plural. In terms of linguistic
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        markedness
       </a>
       , these languages neutralize the gender opposition in the plural, itself a marked category. So adjectives and pronouns have three forms in singular (e.g. Bulgarian
       <i>
        червен
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        червена
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        червено
       </i>
       or German
       <i>
        roter
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        rote
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        rotes
       </i>
       ) but only one in plural (Bulgarian
       <i>
        червени
       </i>
       , German
       <i>
        rote
       </i>
       ) [all examples mean "red"]. As a consequence
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pluralia_tantum" title="Pluralia tantum">
        Pluralia tantum
       </a>
       nouns (lacking a singular form) cannot be assigned a gender. Example with Bulgarian:
       <i>
        клещи
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        kleshti
       </i>
       , "pincers"),
       <i>
        гащи
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        gashti
       </i>
       , "pants"),
       <i>
        очила
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        ochila
       </i>
       , "spectacles"),
       <i>
        хриле
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        hrile
       </i>
       , "gills"). The characteristic ending
       <i>
        -а
       </i>
       of
       <i>
        очил
        <b>
         а
        </b>
       </i>
       suggests a neuter noun, but there is no way to cross-check it and there are indeed a few masculine nouns using the same ending in their plural (
       <i>
        крак
        <b>
         а
        </b>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        рог
        <b>
         а
        </b>
       </i>
       are plurals of masculine
       <i>
        крак
       </i>
       "leg" and
       <i>
        рог
       </i>
       "horn"). However, the endings
       <i>
        -и
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -е
       </i>
       do not make any such indications because they are ambiguous themselves: although
       <i>
        -и
       </i>
       is the regular ending for masculine and feminine nouns, both are in fact used to form plurals of nouns of all three genders (e.g.
       <i>
        завод
        <b>
         и
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        жен
        <b>
         и
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        насеком
        <b>
         и
        </b>
       </i>
       from masculine
       <i>
        завод
       </i>
       "factory", feminine
       <i>
        жена
       </i>
       "woman" and neuter
       <i>
        насекомо
       </i>
       "insect" or
       <i>
        крал
        <b>
         е
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ръц
        <b>
         е
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        колен
        <b>
         е
        </b>
       </i>
       from masculine
       <i>
        крал
       </i>
       "king", feminine
       <i>
        ръка
       </i>
       "hand" and neuter
       <i>
        коляно
       </i>
       "knee").
      </p>
      <p>
       Other languages, e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       , allow doubly marked forms both for number and gender. In these languages, each noun has a definite gender no matter the number.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Names.2C_occupations.2C_and_nationalities">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Names,_occupations,_and_nationalities">
        Names, occupations, and nationalities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Names, occupations, and nationalities">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some languages, some names have two forms, a male and a female one. This can be true of occupations and nationalities. For example,
       <i>
        Andrew
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Andrea
       </i>
       in English, or
       <i>
        Κωνσταντίνος (Konstantinos)
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Κωνσταντίνα (Konstantina)
       </i>
       in Greek. Or
       <i>
        actor
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        actress
       </i>
       in English, but
       <i>
        doctor
       </i>
       for both, and
       <i>
        ηθοποιός (ithopios)
       </i>
       “actor” for both male and female in Greek and
       <i>
        γιατρός (giatros)
       </i>
       “doctor” again for both, but with informal female variants
       <i>
        γιατρίνα (giatrina)
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        γιάτραινα (giatraina)
       </i>
       too. Finally, in the case of a nationality, in Greek there are five forms: male and female for people (and sometimes for other living beings), and masculine, feminine, and neuter for nouns. For example,
       <i>
        English = Άγγλος (Agglos), Αγγλίδα (Agglida), αγγλικός (agglikos), αγγλική (aggliki), αγγλικό (aggliko)
       </i>
       . To complicate matters, the Greek language often offers additional less formal versions of these. The corresponding for English are the following:
       <i>
        Εγγλέζος (Egglezos), Εγγλέζα (Eggleza), εγγλέζικος (egglezikos), εγγλέζικη (eggleziki), εγγλέζικο (eggleziko)
       </i>
       . The formal forms come from the name
       <i>
        Αγγλία (Agglia)
       </i>
       “England”, while the less formal are derived from Italian
       <i>
        inglese
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_linguistic_concepts">
        Related linguistic concepts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Related linguistic concepts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_classes">
        Noun classes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Noun classes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_classes" title="Noun classes">
        Noun classes
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A noun may belong to a given class because of characteristic features of its
       <a href="/wiki/Referent" title="Referent">
        referent
       </a>
       , such as sex, animacy, shape, although in some instances a noun can be placed in a particular class based purely on its grammatical behavior. Some authors use the term "grammatical gender" as a synonym of "noun class", but others use different definitions for each.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many authors prefer "noun classes" when none of the inflections in a language relate to sex, such as when an
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animate
       </a>
       –inanimate distinction is made. Note however that the word "gender" derives from Latin
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">
         genus
        </a>
       </i>
       (also the root of
       <i>
        genre
       </i>
       ) which originally meant "kind", so it does not necessarily have a sexual meaning.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Noun_classifiers">
        Noun classifiers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Noun classifiers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_classifier" title="Noun classifier">
        Noun classifier
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A classifier, or
       <a href="/wiki/Measure_word" title="Measure word">
        measure word
       </a>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Word" title="Word">
        word
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morpheme
       </a>
       used in some languages together with a noun, principally to enable numbers and certain other
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       to be applied to the noun. They are not regularly used in English or other European languages, although they parallel the use of words such as
       <i>
        piece(s)
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        head
       </i>
       in phrases like "three pieces of paper" or "thirty head of cattle". They are a prominent feature of
       <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_languages" title="East Asian languages">
        East Asian languages
       </a>
       , where it is common for all nouns to require a classifier when being quantified – for example, the equivalent of "three people" is often "three
       <i>
        classifier
       </i>
       people". A more general type of classifier (
       <a href="/wiki/Classifier_handshape" title="Classifier handshape">
        classifier handshapes
       </a>
       ) can be found in
       <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
        sign languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Classifiers can be considered similar to genders or noun classes, in that a language which uses classifiers normally has a number of different ones, used with different sets of nouns. These sets depend largely on properties of the things that the nouns denote (for example, a particular classifier may be used for long thin objects, another for flat objects, another for people, another for abstracts, etc.), although sometimes a noun is associated with a particular classifier more by convention than for any obvious reason. However it is also possible for a given noun to be usable with any of several classifiers; for example, the Mandarin
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_classifier" title="Chinese classifier">
        Chinese classifier
       </a>
       个
       <i>
        gè
       </i>
       is frequently used as an alternative to various more specific classifiers.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_of_pronouns">
        Gender of pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Gender of pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gender_in_Pronouns.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="426" data-file-width="804" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gender_in_Pronouns.png/220px-Gender_in_Pronouns.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gender_in_Pronouns.png/330px-Gender_in_Pronouns.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gender_in_Pronouns.png/440px-Gender_in_Pronouns.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gender_in_Pronouns.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gender in Pronouns
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As noted above, pronouns may
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agree
       </a>
       in gender with the noun or noun phrase to which they refer (their
       <a href="/wiki/Antecedent_(grammar)" title="Antecedent (grammar)">
        antecedent
       </a>
       ). Sometimes, however, there is no antecedent – the referent of the pronoun is deduced indirectly from the context. In such cases, the pronoun is likely to agree with the
       <i>
        natural gender
       </i>
       of the referent. Examples of this can be in most European languages, including English (the
       <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       are used depending on whether the referent is male, female, or inanimate or non-human; this is in spite of the fact that English does not generally have grammatical gender). A parallel example is provided by the object suffixes of verbs in
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       , which correspond to object pronouns, and which also inflect for gender in the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        second person
       </a>
       (though not in the first):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "I love you", said to a male:
        <i>
         uḥibbuk
         <b>
          a
         </b>
        </i>
        (
        <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
         أُحِبُّكَ
        </span>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        "I love you", said to a female:
        <i>
         uḥibbuk
         <b>
          i
         </b>
        </i>
        (
        <span lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
         أُحِبُّكِ
        </span>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Not all languages have gendered pronouns. In languages that never had grammatical gender, there is normally just one word for "he" and "she", like
       <i>
        dia
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">
        Indonesian
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        ő
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       . These languages might only have different pronouns and inflections in the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        third person
       </a>
       to differentiate between people and inanimate objects, but even this distinction is often absent. (In written
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_grammar" title="Finnish grammar">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , for example,
       <i>
        hän
       </i>
       is used for "he" and "she" and
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       for "it", but in the colloquial language
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       is usually used for "he" and "she" as well.)
      </p>
      <p>
       For more on these different types of pronoun, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-specific_pronoun" title="Gender-specific pronoun">
        Gender-specific pronoun
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-neutral_pronoun" title="Gender-neutral pronoun">
        Gender-neutral pronoun
       </a>
       . Issues may arise in languages with gender-specific pronouns in cases when the gender of the referent is unknown or not specified; this is discussed under
       <a href="/wiki/Gender-neutral_language" title="Gender-neutral language">
        Gender-neutral language
       </a>
       , and in relation to English at
       <a href="/wiki/Singular_they" title="Singular they">
        Singular
        <i>
         they
        </i>
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cases the gender of a pronoun is not marked in the form of the pronoun itself, but is marked on other words by way of agreement. Thus the French word for "I" is
       <i>
        je
       </i>
       , regardless of who is speaking; but this word becomes feminine or masculine depending on the sex of the speaker, as may be reflected through adjective agreement:
       <i>
        je suis fort
        <b>
         e
        </b>
       </i>
       ("I am strong", spoken by a female);
       <i>
        je suis fort
       </i>
       (the same spoken by a male).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Null-subject_language" title="Null-subject language">
        null-subject languages
       </a>
       (and in some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ellipsis_(grammar)" title="Ellipsis (grammar)">
        elliptical
       </a>
       expressions in other languages), such agreement may take place even though the pronoun does not in fact appear. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "[I am] very grateful", said by a male:
        <i>
         muito obrigad
         <b>
          o
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        the same, said by a female:
        <i>
         muito obrigad
         <b>
          a
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The two sentences above mean literally "much obliged"; the adjective agrees with the natural gender of the speaker, that is, with the gender of the first person pronoun which does not appear explicitly here.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indefinite_and_dummy_pronouns">
        <span id="Indefinite_pronouns">
        </span>
        <span id="Dummy_pronouns">
        </span>
        Indefinite and dummy pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Indefinite and dummy pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
        dummy pronoun
       </a>
       is a type of pronoun used when a particular verb argument (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ) is nonexistent, but when a reference to the argument is nevertheless syntactically required. They occur mostly in non-
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop languages
       </a>
       , such as English (because in pro-drop languages the position of the argument can be left empty). Examples in English are the uses of
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       in "It's raining" and "It's nice to relax."
      </p>
      <p>
       When a language has gendered pronouns, the use of a particular word as a dummy pronoun may involve the selection of a particular gender, even though there is no noun to agree with. In languages with a neuter gender, a neuter pronoun is usually used, as in German
       <i>
        es regnet
       </i>
       ("it rains, it's raining"), where
       <i>
        es
       </i>
       is the neuter third person singular pronoun. (English behaves similarly, because the word
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       neuter gender.) In languages with only masculine and feminine genders, the dummy pronoun may be the masculine third person singular, as in the French for "it's raining":
       <i>
        il pleut
       </i>
       (where
       <i>
        il
       </i>
       means "he", or "it" when referring to masculine nouns); although some languages use the feminine, as in the equivalent
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       sentence:
       <i>
        mae hi'n bwrw glaw
       </i>
       (where the dummy pronoun is
       <i>
        hi
       </i>
       , which means "she", or "it" when referring to feminine nouns).
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar, apparently arbitrary gender assignment may need to be made in the case of
       <a href="/wiki/Indefinite_pronoun" title="Indefinite pronoun">
        indefinite pronouns
       </a>
       , where the referent is generally unknown. In this case the question is usually not which pronoun to use, but which gender to assign a given pronoun to (for such purposes as adjective agreement). For example, the French pronouns
       <i>
        quelqu'un
       </i>
       ("someone"),
       <i>
        personne
       </i>
       ("no-one") and
       <i>
        quelque chose
       </i>
       ("something") are all treated as masculine – this is in spite of the fact that the last two correspond to feminine nouns (
       <i>
        personne
       </i>
       meaning "person", and
       <i>
        chose
       </i>
       meaning "thing").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For other situations in which such a "default" gender assignment may be required, see
       <a href="#Mixed_and_indeterminate_gender">
        § Mixed and indeterminate gender
       </a>
       below.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammatical_vs._natural_gender">
        Grammatical vs. natural gender
        <span id="Natural_gender">
        </span>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Grammatical vs. natural gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        natural gender
       </i>
       of a noun, pronoun or noun phrase is a gender to which it would be expected to belong based on relevant attributes of its referent. This usually means masculine or feminine, depending on the referent's sex (or
       <a href="/wiki/Gender" title="Gender">
        gender
       </a>
       in the sociological sense). For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        mujer
       </i>
       ("woman") is feminine whereas
       <i>
        hombre
       </i>
       ("man") is masculine; these attributions occur solely due to the semantically inherent gender character of each noun.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        grammatical gender
       </i>
       of a noun does not always coincide with its natural gender. An example of this is the
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        Mädchen
       </i>
       ("girl"); this is derived from
       <i>
        Maid
       </i>
       "maiden",
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_Umlaut" title="Germanic Umlaut">
        umlauted
       </a>
       to "Mäd-" with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diminutive_suffix" title="Diminutive suffix">
        diminutive suffix
       </a>
       <i>
        -chen
       </i>
       , and this suffix always makes the noun grammatically neuter. Hence the grammatical gender of
       <i>
        Mädchen
       </i>
       is neuter, although its natural gender is feminine (because it refers to a female person).
      </p>
      <p>
       Other examples include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
         Old English
        </a>
        <i>
         wīf
        </i>
        (neuter) and
        <i>
         wīfmann
        </i>
        (masculine), meaning "woman"
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        <i>
         Weib
        </i>
        (neuter), meaning "woman" (the word is now pejorative and generally replaced with 'die Frau', originally 'lady', fem. of obsolete 'der
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Appendix:Proto-Germanic/frawj%C3%B4" title="wikt:Appendix:Proto-Germanic/frawjô">
         Fro
        </a>
        ', meaning 'lord')
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
         Irish
        </a>
        <i>
         cailín
        </i>
        (masculine) meaning "girl", and
        <i>
         stail
        </i>
        (feminine) meaning "stallion"
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic_language" title="Scottish Gaelic language">
         Scottish Gaelic
        </a>
        <i>
         boireannach
        </i>
        (masculine), meaning "woman"
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
         Slovenian
        </a>
        <i>
         dekle
        </i>
        (neuter), meaning "girl"
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
         Portuguese
        </a>
        <i>
         mulherão
        </i>
        (masculine), meaning "voluptuous woman"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Normally, such exceptions are a small minority.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a noun with conflicting natural and grammatical gender is the antecedent of a pronoun, it may not be clear which gender of pronoun to choose. There is a certain tendency to keep the grammatical gender when a close back-reference is made, but to switch to natural gender when the reference is further away. For example, in German, the sentences "The girl has come home from school. She is now doing her homework" can be translated in two ways:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Das
         <u>
          Mädchen
         </u>
         (n.) ist aus der Schule gekommen.
         <b>
          Es
         </b>
         (n.) macht jetzt
         <b>
          seine
         </b>
         (n.) Hausaufgaben.
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Das
         <u>
          Mädchen
         </u>
         (n.) ist aus der Schule gekommen.
         <b>
          Sie
         </b>
         (f.) macht jetzt
         <b>
          ihre
         </b>
         (f.) Hausaufgaben.
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Though the second sentence may appear grammatically incorrect (
       <a href="/wiki/Synesis" title="Synesis">
        constructio ad sensum
       </a>
       ), it is common in speech. With one or more intervening sentences, the second form becomes even more likely. However, a switch to the natural gender is never possible with articles and
       <i>
        attributive
       </i>
       pronouns or adjectives. Thus it can never be correct to say
       <i>
        *eine Mädchen
       </i>
       ("a girl" – with female indefinite article) or
       <i>
        *diese kleine Mädchen
       </i>
       ("this little girl" – with female demonstrative pronoun and adjective).
      </p>
      <p>
       This phenomenon is quite popular in Slavic languages: for example Polish
       <i>
        kreatura
       </i>
       (deprecative "creature") is feminine but can be used to refer both man (masculine gender), woman (feminine gender), child (neuter gender) or even animate nouns (e.g. a dog being masculine). Similarly with other deprecatory nouns as
       <i>
        pierdoła
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ciapa
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        łamaga
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        łajza
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        niezdara
       </i>
       ("wuss, klutz");
       <i>
        niemowa
       </i>
       ("mute") can used deprecatively as described previously, and then can be used for verbs marked for the male and female genders.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Animals">
        Animals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Animals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the case of languages which have masculine and feminine genders, the relation between biological sex and grammatical gender tends to be less exact in the case of animals than in the case of people. In Spanish, for instance, a cheetah is always
       <i>
        un guepardo
       </i>
       (masculine) and a zebra is always
       <i>
        una cebra
       </i>
       (feminine), regardless of their biological sex. To specify the sex of an animal, an adjective may be added, as in
       <i>
        un guepardo hembra
       </i>
       ("a female cheetah"), or
       <i>
        una cebra macho
       </i>
       ("a male zebra").
       <a href="/wiki/Suppletion" title="Suppletion">
        Different names
       </a>
       for the male and the female of a species are more frequent for common pets or farm animals, e.g. English
       <i>
        cow
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bull
       </i>
       , Spanish
       <i>
        vaca
       </i>
       "cow" and
       <i>
        toro
       </i>
       "bull".
      </p>
      <p>
       As regards the pronouns used to refer to animals, these generally agree in gender with the nouns denoting those animals, rather than the animals' sex (natural gender). In a language like English, which does not assign grammatical gender to nouns, the pronoun used for referring to objects (
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       ) is often used for animals also. However, if the sex of the animal is known, and particularly in the case of companion animals, the gendered pronouns (
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       ) may be used as they would be for a human.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       , a few general words such as
       <i>
        zwierzę
       </i>
       ("animal") or
       <i>
        bydlę
       </i>
       ("animal, one head of cattle") are neuter, but most species names are masculine or feminine. When the sex of an animal is known, it will normally be referred to using gendered pronouns consistent with its sex; otherwise the pronouns will correspond to the gender of the noun denoting its species.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_and_indeterminate_gender">
        Mixed and indeterminate gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Mixed and indeterminate gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are certain situations where the assignment of gender to a noun, pronoun or noun phrase may not be straightforward. This includes in particular:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        groups of mixed gender;
       </li>
       <li>
        references to people or things of unknown or unspecified gender.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In languages with masculine and feminine gender, the masculine is usually employed by default to refer to persons of unknown gender, and to groups of people of mixed gender. Thus, in French the feminine plural pronoun
       <i>
        elles
       </i>
       always designates an all-female group of people (or stands for a group of nouns all of feminine gender), but the masculine equivalent
       <i>
        ils
       </i>
       may refer to a group of males or masculine nouns, to a mixed group, or to a group of people of unknown genders. In such cases, one says that the feminine gender is
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        semantically marked
       </a>
       , whereas the masculine gender is unmarked.
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, the problem of gender determination does not arise in the plural, because gender in that language is reflected only in pronouns, and the plural pronoun
       <i>
        they
       </i>
       does not have gendered forms. In the singular, however, the issue frequently arises when a person of unspecified or unknown gender is being referred to. In this case it has been traditional to use the masculine (
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ), but other solutions are now often preferred – see
       <a href="/wiki/Gender-neutral_language" title="Gender-neutral language">
        Gender-neutral language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Singular_they" title="Singular they">
        Singular
        <i>
         they
        </i>
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In languages with a neuter gender, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       , the neuter is often used for indeterminate gender reference, particularly when the things referred to are not people. In some cases this may even apply when referring to people, particularly children. For example, in English, one may use
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       to refer to a child, particularly when speaking generically rather than about a particular child of known sex.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       (which preserves a masculine–feminine–neuter distinction in both singular and plural), the neuter is used for indeterminate or mixed gender reference even when talking about people. For example, the greeting
       <i>
        velkominn
       </i>
       ("welcome") is altered depending on who is being spoken to:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          inn
         </b>
        </i>
        (masculine singular) – to one male person
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          in
         </b>
        </i>
        (feminine singular) – to one female person
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          ið
         </b>
        </i>
        (neuter singular) – to someone whose gender is unknown
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          nir
         </b>
        </i>
        (masculine plural) – to a group of males
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          nar
         </b>
        </i>
        (feminine plural) – to a group of females
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         velkom
         <b>
          in
         </b>
        </i>
        (neuter plural) – to a mixed or indeterminate group
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Nevertheless, even in Icelandic, the feminine is considered somewhat more marked than the masculine.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       (which has an overall common–neuter gender system), masculinity may be argued to be a marked feature, because in the
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_grammar#Weak_inflection" title="Swedish grammar">
        weak adjectival declension
       </a>
       there is a distinct ending (
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       ) for naturally masculine nouns (as in
       <i>
        min lill
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        bror
       </i>
       , "my little brother"). In spite of this, the third-person singular masculine pronoun
       <i>
        han
       </i>
       would normally be the default for a person of unknown gender, although in practice the indefinite pronoun
       <i>
        man
       </i>
       and the reflexive
       <i>
        sig
       </i>
       or its possessive forms
       <i>
        sin/sitt/sina
       </i>
       usually make this unnecessary.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       , where a gender-like distinction is made in the plural between "masculine personal" and all other cases (see
       <a href="#Slavic_languages">
        below
       </a>
       ), a group is treated as masculine personal if it contains at least one male person.
      </p>
      <p>
       In languages which preserve a three-way gender division in the plural, the rules for determining the gender (and sometimes number) of a
       <a href="/wiki/Coordination_(linguistics)" title="Coordination (linguistics)">
        coordinated
       </a>
       noun phrase ("... and ...") may be quite complex.
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       is an example of such a language, with a division (in the plural) between masculine animate, masculine inanimate, feminine, and neuter. The rules
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for gender and number of coordinated phrases in that language are summarized at
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_declension#Gender_and_number_of_compound_phrases" title="Czech declension">
        Czech declension § Gender and number of compound phrases
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_correspondence_between_languages">
        Gender correspondence between languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Gender correspondence between languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nouns which have the same meanings in different languages need not have the same gender. This is particularly so in the case of things with no natural gender, such as sexless objects. There is nothing objective about a table, for example, which would cause it to be associated with any particular gender, and different languages' words for "table" are found to have various genders: feminine, as with the French
       <i>
        table
       </i>
       ; masculine, as with German
       <i>
        Tisch
       </i>
       ; or neuter, as with
       <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
        Norwegian
       </a>
       <i>
        bord
       </i>
       . (Even within a given language, nouns that denote the same concept may differ in gender – for example, of two German words for "car",
       <i>
        Wagen
       </i>
       is masculine whereas
       <i>
        Auto
       </i>
       is neuter.)
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Cognate" title="Cognate">
        Cognate
       </a>
       nouns in closely related languages are likely to have the same gender, because they tend to inherit the gender of the original word in the parent language. For instance, in the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , the words for "sun" are masculine, being derived from the Latin masculine noun
       <i>
        sol
       </i>
       , whereas the words for "moon" are feminine, being derived from the Latin feminine
       <i>
        luna
       </i>
       . (This contrasts with the genders found in German, where
       <i>
        Sonne
       </i>
       "sun" is feminine, and
       <i>
        Mond
       </i>
       "moon" is masculine.) However, there are exceptions to this principle. For instance,
       <i>
        arte
       </i>
       ("art") is feminine in Italian, like the Latin word
       <i>
        ars
       </i>
       from which it stems, but in French, the corresponding word
       <i>
        art
       </i>
       is masculine. Likewise, the word for "boat" is neuter in German (
       <i>
        das Boot
       </i>
       ), but common gender in Swedish (
       <i>
        en båt
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Some more examples of the above phenomena are given below. (These come mostly from the Slavic languages, where gender largely correlates with the noun ending.)
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The Russian word луна ("moon") is feminine, whereas месяц ("
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
         crescent moon
        </a>
        ", also meaning "month") is masculine. In Polish, another Slavic language, the word for moon is
        <i>
         księżyc
        </i>
        , which is masculine.
       </li>
       <li>
        Russian also has two words for "potato": картофель which is masculine, and картошка which is feminine.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Polish the loanword
        <i>
         tramwaj
        </i>
        ("tram") is masculine, whereas the cognate loanword in Czech,
        <i>
         tramvaj
        </i>
        , is feminine. In Romanian,
        <i>
         tramvai
        </i>
        is neuter.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Polish word
        <i>
         tysiąc
        </i>
        ("thousand") is masculine, whereas the cognate in Russian, тысяча, is feminine.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Spanish word
        <i>
         origen
        </i>
        ("origin") is masculine, but its close relatives
        <i>
         origem
        </i>
        (from Portuguese) and
        <i>
         orixe
        </i>
        (from Galician and Asturian) are feminine.
       </li>
       <li>
        The French word
        <i>
         équipe
        </i>
        ("team") is feminine, while the Spanish word
        <i>
         equipo
        </i>
        is masculine. The Spanish form contrasts with Brazilian Portuguese
        <i>
         equipe
        </i>
        and European Portuguese
        <i>
         equipa
        </i>
        , both of which are feminine.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Italian word
        <i>
         scimmia
        </i>
        ("ape") is feminine, whereas the Spanish word
        <i>
         simio
        </i>
        is masculine.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender_in_words_borrowed_from_one_language_by_another">
        Gender in words borrowed from one language by another
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Gender in words borrowed from one language by another">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Ibrahim identifies several processes by which a language assigns a gender to a newly borrowed word; these processes follow patterns by which even children, through their subconscious recognition of patterns, can often correctly predict a noun's gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIbrahim197361_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIbrahim197361-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        If the noun is animate, natural gender tends to dictate grammatical gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        The borrowed word tends to take the gender of the native word it replaces.
       </li>
       <li>
        If the borrowed word happens to have a suffix that the borrowing language uses as a gender marker, the suffix tends to dictate gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        If the borrowed word rhymes with one or more native words, the latter tend to dictate gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        The default assignment is the borrowing language's unmarked gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        Rarely, the word retains the gender it had in the donor language. This tends to happen more frequently in more formal language such as scientific terms, where some knowledge of the donor language can be expected.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Sometimes the gender of a word switches with time. For example, the Russian modern loanword
       <i>
        виски
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        viski
       </i>
       ) "whisky" was originally feminine,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       then masculine,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and today it has become neuter.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Useful_roles">
        Useful roles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Useful roles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ibrahim identified three possible useful roles of grammatical gender:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIbrahim197327–28_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIbrahim197327–28-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        In a language with explicit inflections for gender, it is easy to express the natural gender of animate beings.
       </li>
       <li>
        Grammatical gender "can be a valuable tool of disambiguation", rendering clarity about antecedents.
       </li>
       <li>
        In literature, gender can be used to "animate and personify inanimate nouns".
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Among these, role 2 is probably the most important in everyday usage.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Languages with gender distinction generally have fewer cases of ambiguity concerning, for example, pronominal reference. In the English phrase "
       <i>
        a flowerbed in the garden which I maintain
       </i>
       " only context tells us whether the relative clause (
       <i>
        which I maintain
       </i>
       ) refers to the whole garden or just the flowerbed. In German, gender distinction prevents such ambiguity. The word for "(flower) bed" (
       <i>
        Beet
       </i>
       ) is neuter, whereas that for "garden" (
       <i>
        Garten
       </i>
       ) is masculine. Hence, if a neuter relative pronoun is used, the relative clause refers to "bed", and if a masculine pronoun is used, the relative clause refers to "garden". Because of this, languages with gender distinction can often use pronouns where in English a noun would have to be repeated in order to avoid confusion. It does not, however, help in cases where the words are of the same grammatical gender. (There are often several synonymous nouns of different grammatical gender to pick from to avoid this, however.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Moreover, grammatical gender may serve to distinguish
       <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
        homophones
       </a>
       . It is a quite common phenomenon in language development for two
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       to merge, thereby making etymologically distinct words sound alike. In languages with gender distinction, however, these word pairs may still be distinguishable by their gender. For example, French
       <i>
        pot
       </i>
       ("pot") and
       <i>
        peau
       </i>
       ("skin") are homophones /po/, but disagree in gender:
       <i>
        le pot
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        la peau
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_culture">
        Influence on culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Influence on culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to research by
       <a href="/wiki/Lera_Boroditsky" title="Lera Boroditsky">
        Lera Boroditsky
       </a>
       , grammatical genders are among the aspects of languages that shape how people think (a hypothesis called "
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_relativity" title="Linguistic relativity">
        linguistic relativity
       </a>
       "). In one study by Boroditsky, in which native speakers of German and Spanish were asked to describe everyday objects in English, she found that they were more likely to use attributes conventionally associated with the genders of the objects in their native languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       For instance, German speakers more often described
       <i>
        <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
         Brücke
        </span>
        (f.)
       </i>
       "bridge" with words like 'beautiful', 'elegant', 'fragile', 'peaceful', 'pretty', and 'slender', whereas Spanish speakers, which use
       <i>
        puente (m.)
       </i>
       used terms like 'big', 'dangerous', 'long', 'strong', 'sturdy', and 'towering'.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also according to Boroditsky, the gender in which concepts are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphized" title="Anthropomorphized">
        anthropomorphized
       </a>
       in art is dependent, in 85% of all cases, on the grammatical gender of the concept in the artist's language. Therefore, in German art
       <i>
        Tod (m.)
       </i>
       "
       <a href="/wiki/Death" title="Death">
        death
       </a>
       " is generally portrayed as a man, but in Russian art
       <i>
        смерть (f.)
       </i>
       "death" is generally portrayed as a woman.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Edge_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Edge-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A problem with such arguments is that, as argued by
       <a href="/wiki/Ad%C3%A8le_Mercier" title="Adèle Mercier">
        Adèle Mercier
       </a>
       , in French and many other languages the same class of objects can be referred to by words of different grammatical gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Of interest are languages that lack grammatical gender for either the pronoun or the noun, such as Armenian. In a recent study by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rafik_Santrosyan&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rafik Santrosyan (page does not exist)">
        Rafik Santrosyan
       </a>
       , in which native speakers of Armenian were given
       <a href="/wiki/Epicene" title="Epicene">
        epicene
       </a>
       (gender-neutral) words denoting different occupations, those words that implied high agency (e.g. leader, mayor, doctor etc.) were predominantly associated with maleness while words denoting subservient and sexually objectified positions were associated with femaleness. Santrosyan concludes that the cultural understanding of gender in Contemporary Armenian is constructed around the axis of agency.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="By_language">
        By language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: By language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_languages_by_type_of_grammatical_genders" title="List of languages by type of grammatical genders">
        List of languages by type of grammatical genders
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Grammatical gender is a common phenomenon in the world's languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoley_&amp;_Van_Valin1984326_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoley_&amp;_Van_Valin1984326-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A typological survey of 174 languages revealed that over one fourth of them had grammatical gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENichols1992_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENichols1992-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Gender systems rarely overlap with numerical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_classifier" title="Noun classifier">
        classifier
       </a>
       systems. Gender and noun class systems are usually found in
       <a href="/wiki/Fusional_language" title="Fusional language">
        fusional
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agglutinating_language" title="Agglutinating language">
        agglutinating
       </a>
       languages, whereas classifiers are more typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Isolating_language" title="Isolating language">
        isolating languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200577_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200577-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Thus, these are main characteristics of gendered languages:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200577_44-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFranceschina200577-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        location in an area with languages featuring noun classes;
       </li>
       <li>
        preference for
        <a href="/wiki/Head_(linguistics)" title="Head (linguistics)">
         head
        </a>
        -marking morphology;
       </li>
       <li>
        moderate to high morphological complexity;
       </li>
       <li>
        non-accusative
        <a href="/wiki/Morphosyntactic_alignment" title="Morphosyntactic alignment">
         alignment
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indo-European">
        Indo-European
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Indo-European">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , but not English, provide archetypical examples of grammatical gender.
      </p>
      <p>
       Research indicates that the earliest stages of
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       had two genders (animate and inanimate), as did
       <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">
        Hittite
       </a>
       , the earliest attested Indo-European language. The classification of nouns based on animacy and inanimacy and the lack of gender are today characteristic of
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian
       </a>
       . According to the theory, the animate gender, which (unlike the inanimate) had independent vocative and accusative forms, later split into masculine and feminine, thus originating the three-way classification into masculine, feminine and neuter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Indo-European languages retained the three genders, including most
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       , Ancient and Modern
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       . In them, there is a high but not absolute correlation between grammatical gender and
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declensional
       </a>
       class. Many linguists believe that to be true of the middle and late stages of Proto-Indo-European.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, many languages reduced the number of genders to two. Some lost the neuter, leaving masculine and feminine like most
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin#Loss_of_neuter" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin § Loss of neuter
       </a>
       ; a few traces of the neuter remain, such as the distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        Spanish pronoun
       </a>
       <i>
        ello
       </i>
       and Italian nouns with so-called "mobile gender") and
       <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">
        Hindustani
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic languages
       </a>
       . Others merged feminine and the masculine into a common gender but retained the neuter, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Swedish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
        Danish
       </a>
       (and, to some extent,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       ; see
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_Danish_and_Swedish" title="Gender in Danish and Swedish">
        Gender in Danish and Swedish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_Dutch_grammar" title="Gender in Dutch grammar">
        Gender in Dutch grammar
       </a>
       ). Finally, some languages, such as English and
       <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">
        Afrikaans
       </a>
       , have nearly completely lost grammatical gender (retaining only some traces, such as the English pronouns
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">
        Bengali
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Assamese_language" title="Assamese language">
        Assamese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ossetian_language" title="Ossetian language">
        Ossetic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Odia_language" title="Odia language">
        Odia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Khowar_language" title="Khowar language">
        Khowar
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kalash_language" title="Kalash language">
        Kalasha
       </a>
       have lost it entirely.
      </p>
      <p>
       On the other hand, some
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       can be argued to have added new genders to the classical three (see
       <a href="#Slavic_languages">
        below
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_English" title="Gender in English">
        Gender in English
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although grammatical gender was a fully productive inflectional category in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       , Modern English has a much less pervasive gender system, primarily based on natural gender and reflected essentially in pronouns only.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are a few traces of gender marking in Modern English:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Some words take different
        <a href="/wiki/Morphological_derivation" title="Morphological derivation">
         derived
        </a>
        forms depending on the sex of the referent, such as
        <i>
         actor/actress
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         widow/widower
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The third-person singular
        <a href="/wiki/English_personal_pronouns" title="English personal pronouns">
         personal pronouns
        </a>
        (and their possessive forms) are gender specific:
        <i>
         he/him/his
        </i>
        (masculine gender, used for males),
        <i>
         she/her(s)
        </i>
        (feminine gender, for females), and
        <i>
         it/its
        </i>
        (neuter gender, mainly for objects, abstractions and animals). (There are also distinct personal and non-personal forms but no differentiation by sex in the case of certain interrogative and
        <a href="/wiki/English_relative_clauses" title="English relative clauses">
         relative pronouns
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         who/whom
        </i>
        for persons, corresponding to
        <i>
         he
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         she
        </i>
        ; and
        <i>
         which
        </i>
        corresponding to
        <i>
         it
        </i>
        .)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, these are relatively insignificant features compared with a typical language with full grammatical gender. English nouns are not generally considered to belong to gender classes in the way that French, German or Russian nouns are. There is no gender agreement in English between nouns and their modifiers (
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        articles
       </a>
       , other
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , with the occasional exception such as
       <i>
        blond/blonde
       </i>
       , a spelling convention borrowed from French). Gender agreement applies in effect only to pronouns, and the choice of pronoun is determined based on semantics (perceived qualities of the thing being referred to) rather than on any conventional assignment of particular nouns to particular genders.
      </p>
      <p>
       It should also be noted that only a relatively small number of English nouns have distinct male and female forms; many of them are
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       from non-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       (the suffixes
       <i>
        -ress
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -rix
       </i>
       in words such as
       <i>
        actress
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        aviatrix
       </i>
       , for instance, derive from Latin
       <i>
        -rix
       </i>
       , in the first case via the French
       <i>
        -rice
       </i>
       ). English has no live
       <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(linguistics)" title="Productivity (linguistics)">
        productive
       </a>
       gender
       <a href="/wiki/Marker_(linguistics)" title="Marker (linguistics)">
        markers
       </a>
       . An example of such a marker might be the suffix
       <i>
        -ette
       </i>
       (of French provenance), but this is seldom used today, surviving mostly in either historical contexts or with disparaging or humorous intent.
      </p>
      <p>
       The gender of an English pronoun, then, typically coincides with the sex (natural gender) of its referent, rather than with the grammatical gender of its
       <a href="/wiki/Antecedent_(grammar)" title="Antecedent (grammar)">
        antecedent
       </a>
       . The choice of
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       comes down to whether the pronoun is intended to designate a female, a male or something else. There are certain exceptions, however:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        With animals,
        <i>
         it
        </i>
        is usually used, but when the sex of the animal is known, it may be referred to as
        <i>
         he
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         she
        </i>
        (particularly when expressing an emotional connection with the animal, as with a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pet_animal" title="Pet animal">
         pet
        </a>
        ). See also
        <a href="#Animals">
         § Animals
        </a>
        above.
       </li>
       <li>
        Certain nonhuman things are referred to with the pronoun
        <i>
         she
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         her
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         hers
        </i>
        ), particularly countries and ships, and sometimes other vehicles or machines. See
        <a href="/wiki/Gender_in_English#Ships" title="Gender in English">
         Gender in English § Ships
        </a>
        . That usage is considered an optional
        <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_speech" title="Figure of speech">
         figure of speech
        </a>
        ; it is also in decline, and advised against by most journalistic style guides.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
         <a href="#cite_note-47">
          [47]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Problems arise when selecting a personal pronoun to refer to someone of unspecified or unknown gender (see also
       <a href="#Mixed_and_indeterminate_gender">
        § Mixed and indeterminate gender
       </a>
       above). Traditionally, the masculine has been used as the "default" gender in English. The use of the plural pronoun
       <i>
        they
       </i>
       with singular reference is common in practice. The neuter
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       may be used for a baby but not normally for an older child or adult. (Other genderless pronouns exist, such as the impersonal pronoun
       <i>
        one
       </i>
       , but they are not generally substitutable for a personal pronoun.) For more information see
       <a href="/wiki/Gender-neutral_language" title="Gender-neutral language">
        Gender-neutral language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Singular_they" title="Singular they">
        Singular
        <i>
         they
        </i>
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slavic_languages">
        Slavic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Slavic languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       mostly continue the Proto-Indo-European system of three genders, masculine, feminine and neuter. Gender correlates largely with noun endings (masculine nouns typically end in a consonant, feminines in
       <i>
        -a
       </i>
       and neuters in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       ) but there are many exceptions, particularly in the case of nouns whose stems end in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soft_consonant" title="Soft consonant">
        soft consonant
       </a>
       . However, some of the languages, including
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       , also make certain additional grammatical distinctions between
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animate
       </a>
       and inanimate nouns. In the case of Polish, in the plural, and Russian, in the accusative case, they differentiate between human and non-human nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Russian, the different treatment of animate nouns involves their accusative case (and that of adjectives qualifying them) being formed identically to the genitive rather than to the nominative. In the singular that applies to masculine nouns only, but in the plural, it applies in all genders. See
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_declension" title="Russian declension">
        Russian declension
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar system applies in Czech, but the situation is somewhat different in the plural (only masculine nouns are affected, and the distinctive feature is a distinct inflective ending for masculine animate nouns in the nominative plural and for adjectives and verbs agreeing with those nouns). See
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_declension" title="Czech declension">
        Czech declension
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Polish might be said to distinguish five genders: personal masculine (referring to male humans), animate non-personal masculine, inanimate masculine, feminine, and neuter. The animate–inanimate opposition for the masculine gender applies in the singular, and the personal–impersonal opposition, which classes animals along with inanimate objects, applies in the plural. (A few nouns denoting inanimate things are treated grammatically as animate and vice versa.) The manifestations of the differences are as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In the singular, masculine animates (in the standard declension) have an accusative form identical to the genitive, and masculine inanimates have accusative identical to the nominative. The same applies to adjectives qualifying these nouns, the same as in Russian and Czech. Also, Polish masculine animates always form their genitive in
        <i>
         -a
        </i>
        , whereas in the case of inanimates some use
        <i>
         -a
        </i>
        and some
        <i>
         -u
        </i>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          animate:
          <i>
           dobry klient
          </i>
          ("good customer"; nominative);
          <i>
           dobrego klienta
          </i>
          (accusative and genitive)
         </dd>
         <dd>
          animate:
          <i>
           dobry pies
          </i>
          ("good dog"; nominative);
          <i>
           dobrego psa
          </i>
          (accusative and genitive)
         </dd>
         <dd>
          inanimate:
          <i>
           dobry ser
          </i>
          ("good cheese"; nominative and accusative);
          <i>
           dobrego sera
          </i>
          (genitive only)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In the plural, masculine personal nouns (but not other animate nouns) take accusatives that are identical to the genitives; they also typically take different endings (e.g.
        <i>
         -i
        </i>
        rather than
        <i>
         -y
        </i>
        ) in the nominative: such endings also appear on adjectives and past tense verbs. The two features are analogous to features of Russian and Czech respectively, except that those languages make an animate/inanimate (not personal/impersonal) distinction. Examples of the Polish system:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          personal:
          <i>
           dobrzy klienci
          </i>
          ("good customers"; nominative);
          <i>
           dobrych klientów
          </i>
          (accusative and genitive)
         </dd>
         <dd>
          impersonal:
          <i>
           dobre psy
          </i>
          ("good dogs"; nominative and accusative);
          <i>
           dobrych psów
          </i>
          (genitive only)
         </dd>
         <dd>
          impersonal:
          <i>
           dobre sery
          </i>
          ("good cheeses"; nominative and accusative);
          <i>
           dobrych serów
          </i>
          (genitive only)
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       A few nouns have both personal and impersonal forms, depending on meaning (for example,
       <i>
        klient
       </i>
       may behave as an impersonal noun when it refers to a
       <a href="/wiki/Client_(computing)" title="Client (computing)">
        client
       </a>
       in the computing sense). For more information on the above inflection patterns, see
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_morphology" title="Polish morphology">
        Polish morphology
       </a>
       . For certain rules concerning the treatment of mixed-gender groups, see
       <a href="#Mixed_and_indeterminate_gender">
        § Mixed and indeterminate gender
       </a>
       above.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dravidian">
        Dravidian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Dravidian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       , nouns are classified primarily on the basis of their semantic properties. The highest-level classification of nouns is often described as being between "rational" and "nonrational".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECorbett19918–11_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECorbett19918–11-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nouns representing humans and
       <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">
        deities
       </a>
       are considered rational, and other nouns (those representing animals and objects) are treated as nonrational. Within the rational class there are further subdivisions into masculine, feminine and
       <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">
        collective nouns
       </a>
       . For further information, see
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_grammar" title="Tamil grammar">
        Tamil grammar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Constructed_languages">
        Constructed languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Constructed languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Constructed_language" title="Constructed language">
        constructed languages
       </a>
       have natural gender systems similar to that of English. Animate nouns can have distinct forms reflecting natural gender, and personal pronouns are selected according to natural gender. There is no gender agreement on modifiers.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Auxiliary_languages">
        Auxiliary languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Auxiliary languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Esperanto" title="Esperanto">
         Esperanto
        </a>
        has no grammatical gender. The female suffix
        <i>
         -in-
        </i>
        , sometimes quoted as an example, is simply one of many suffixes intended to simplify the vocabulary and make the language easier and faster to learn. There are no accompanying features of grammatical gender (such as different articles or markers applying to associated adjectives). Although it differentiates a small number of male and female nouns, such as
        <i>
         patro
        </i>
        (father) and
        <i>
         patrino
        </i>
        (mother) for the reason described above, most nouns are gender-neutral and the use of it is not necessary. For instance,
        <i>
         hundo
        </i>
        means either a male or female dog,
        <i>
         virhundo
        </i>
        means a male dog, and
        <i>
         hundino
        </i>
        means a female dog.
        <a href="/wiki/Esperanto_grammar#Personal_pronouns" title="Esperanto grammar">
         Personal pronouns
        </a>
        <i>
         li
        </i>
        (he) and
        <i>
         ŝi
        </i>
        (she) and their possessive forms
        <i>
         lia
        </i>
        (his) and
        <i>
         ŝia
        </i>
        (her) are used for male and female antecedents, and
        <i>
         ĝi
        </i>
        (it) and its possessive form
        <i>
         ĝia
        </i>
        (its) are used to refer to a nonpersonal antecedent.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ido_language" title="Ido language">
         Ido
        </a>
        has the masculine infix
        <i>
         -ul
        </i>
        and the feminine infix
        <i>
         -in
        </i>
        for animate beings. Both are optional and used only if it is necessary to avoid ambiguity:
        <i>
         kato
        </i>
        "a cat",
        <i>
         katulo
        </i>
        "a male cat",
        <i>
         katino
        </i>
        "a female cat". There are third-person singular and plural pronouns for all three genders,
        <i>
         masculine
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         feminine
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         neuter
        </i>
        , but also
        <i>
         gender-free
        </i>
        pronouns.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Interlingua" title="Interlingua">
         Interlingua
        </a>
        has no grammatical gender. It indicates only the natural gender, as in
        <i>
         matre
        </i>
        "mother" and
        <i>
         patre
        </i>
        "father". Interlingua speakers may use feminine endings. For example,
        <i>
         -a
        </i>
        may be used in place of
        <i>
         -o
        </i>
        in
        <i>
         catto
        </i>
        , producing
        <i>
         catta
        </i>
        "female cat".
        <i>
         Professora
        </i>
        may be used to denote a professor who is female, and
        <i>
         actrice
        </i>
        may be used to mean "actress". As in Ido, inflections marking gender are optional, but some gender-specific nouns such as
        <i>
         femina
        </i>
        , "woman", happen to end in
        <i>
         -a
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         -o
        </i>
        . Interlingua
        <a href="/wiki/Interlingua_grammar" title="Interlingua grammar">
         has feminine pronouns
        </a>
        , and its general pronoun forms are also used as masculine pronouns.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Wuvulu-Aua_language" title="Wuvulu-Aua language">
         Wuvulu-Aua language
        </a>
        , gender is often used when addressing someone related to you. For example, tafi means 'sister of female', ʔari means opposite-gender sibling, and wane means female's father's sister, female's brother's daughter.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
         <a href="#cite_note-49">
          [49]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The Wuvulu people have gender-specific chores.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
         <a href="#cite_note-50">
          [50]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       See also
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-neutrality_in_languages_with_grammatical_gender#International_auxiliary_languages" title="Gender-neutrality in languages with grammatical gender">
        Gender-neutrality in languages with grammatical gender: International auxiliary languages
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-specific_pronoun#Constructed_languages" title="Gender-specific pronoun">
        Gender-specific pronoun: Constructed languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Artistic_languages">
        Artistic languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_gender&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Artistic languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Klingon_language" title="Klingon language">
         Klingon language
        </a>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Marc_Okrand" title="Marc Okrand">
         Marc Okrand
        </a>
        divides nouns into
        <i>
         beings capable of using language
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         body parts
        </i>
        and others. Regular nouns in these categories form plurals with the endings
        <i>
         -pu
         <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
          '
         </span>

        ,
        <i>
         -Du
         <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
          '
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         -mey
        </i>
        respectively. The first category also has a separate possessive suffix in the first and second persons.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
         <a href="#cite_note-51">
          [51]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_Valyrian" title="High Valyrian">
         High Valyrian
        </a>
        , built by
        <a href="/wiki/David_J._Peterson" title="David J. Peterson">
         David J. Peterson
        </a>
        for the TV series
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Game_of_Thrones" title="Game of Thrones">
          Game of Thrones
         </a>
        </i>
        , has four grammatical genders, none of which are related to natural gender (see also
        <a href="/wiki/Valyrian_languages#Nouns" title="Valyrian languages">
         Valyrian languages#Nouns
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
