      <p>
       <b>
        Animacy
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semantic" title="Semantic">
        semantic
       </a>
       principle expressed in language based on how
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sentient" title="Sentient">
        sentient
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">
        alive
       </a>
       the referent of a
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       is. Widely expressed, animacy is one of the most elementary principles in languages around the globe, and is a distinction acquired as early as six months of age.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szewczyk_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Szewczyk-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Concepts of animacy constantly vary beyond a simple animate and inanimate binary; many languages function off of a
       <a href="/wiki/Implicational_hierarchy" title="Implicational hierarchy">
        hierarchical
       </a>
       General Animacy Scale that ranks animacy as a "matter of gradience."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamamoto_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yamamoto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Typically (with some variation of order and of where the cutoff for animacy occurs), the scale ranks humans above animals, then plants, natural forces, concrete objects, and abstract objects, in that order. In referring to humans, this scale contains a hierarchy of persons, ranking the first and second person
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       above the third person, partly a product of empathy, involving the speaker and
       <a href="/wiki/Interlocutor_(linguistics)" title="Interlocutor (linguistics)">
        interlocutor
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamamoto_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yamamoto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, the hierarchy tends to place singular persons over plural (as seen in the heavy difference between "I regret to inform you" and "We regret to inform you"), but it carries exceptions, such as thousands of people signing a petition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamamoto_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yamamoto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The distinction between
       <i>
        he
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she
       </i>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender-specific_and_gender-neutral_pronouns" title="Gender-specific and gender-neutral pronouns">
        other personal pronouns
       </a>
       , on one hand, and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       , on the other hand is a distinction in animacy in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       and in many
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       . The same can be said about distinction between
       <i>
        who
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        what
       </i>
       . Some languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoken_Finnish" title="Spoken Finnish">
        Spoken Finnish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       do not distinguish between
       <i>
        s/he
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , there is a distinction in animacy between
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/h%C3%A4n#Finnish" title="wikt:hän">
         hän
        </a>
       </i>
       "he/she" and
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/se#Finnish" title="wikt:se">
         se
        </a>
       </i>
       "it", but in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spoken_Finnish" title="Spoken Finnish">
        Spoken Finnish
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       can mean "he/she". English shows a similar lack of distinction between
       <i>
        they
       </i>
       animate and
       <i>
        they
       </i>
       inanimate in the plural but, as shown above, it has such a distinction in the singular.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is another example of how animacy plays some role in English. For example, the higher animacy a referent has, the less preferable it is to use the preposition
       <i>
        of
       </i>
       for possession (that can also be interpreted in terms of alienable or
       <a href="/wiki/Inalienable_possession" title="Inalienable possession">
        inalienable possession
       </a>
       ):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         My face
        </i>
        is correct while *
        <i>
         the face of me
        </i>
        would sound very strange.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         The man's face
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         the face of the man
        </i>
        are both correct, but the former is preferred.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         The clock's face
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         the face of the clock
        </i>
        are both correct, but the latter is preferred.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Examples of languages in which an animacy hierarchy is important include the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Totonac_language" title="Totonac language">
        Totonac language
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Athabaskan_languages" title="Southern Athabaskan languages">
        Southern Athabaskan languages
       </a>
       (such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Apache" title="Western Apache">
        Western Apache
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">
        Navajo
       </a>
       ) whose animacy hierarchy has been the subject of intense study. The
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil language
       </a>
       has a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        noun classification
       </a>
       based on animacy.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Indo-European_language">
        Proto-Indo-European language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Proto-Indo-European language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Because of the similarities in morphology of feminine and masculine
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender#Masculine,_feminine,_and_neuter" title="Grammatical gender">
        grammatical gender
       </a>
       inflections in
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , there is a theory that in an early stage, the
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European language
       </a>
       had only two grammatical genders: "
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender#Animate_and_inanimate" title="Grammatical gender">
        animate
       </a>
       " and "
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender#Common_and_neuter" title="Grammatical gender">
        inanimate/neuter
       </a>
       "; the most obvious difference being that inanimate/neuter nouns used the same form for the
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vocative_case" title="Vocative case">
        vocative
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_case" title="Noun case">
        noun cases
       </a>
       . The distinction was preserved in
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolian_languages" title="Anatolian languages">
        Anatolian languages
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/Hittite_language" title="Hittite language">
        Hittite
       </a>
       , all of which are now extinct.
      </p>
      <p>
       The animate gender would then later, after the separation of the Anatolian languages, have developed into the feminine and masculine genders. The plural of neuter/inanimate nouns is believed to have had the same ending as
       <a href="/wiki/Collective_noun" title="Collective noun">
        collective nouns
       </a>
       in the singular, and some words with the collective noun ending in singular were later to become words with the feminine gender. Traces can be found in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       in which the singular form of verbs was used when they referred to neuter words in plural. Still in many Indo-European languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic
       </a>
       , the plural ending of many neuter words in the merged nominative–accusative–vocative corresponds to the feminine singular nominative form.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Navajo">
        Navajo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Navajo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Like most other
       <a href="/wiki/Athabaskan_languages" title="Athabaskan languages">
        Athabaskan languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Athabaskan_languages" title="Southern Athabaskan languages">
        Southern Athabaskan languages
       </a>
       show various levels of animacy in their grammar, with certain nouns taking specific verb forms according to their rank in this animacy hierarchy. For instance,
       <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">
        Navajo
       </a>
       nouns can be ranked by animacy on a continuum from most animate (a human) to least animate (an abstraction) (Young &amp; Morgan 1987: 65-66):
      </p>
      <p>
       Human &gt; Infant/Big Animal &gt; Medium-sized Animal &gt; Small Animal &gt; Natural Force &gt; Abstraction
      </p>
      <p>
       Generally, the most animate noun in a sentence must occur first while the noun with lesser animacy occurs second. If both nouns are equal in animacy, either noun can occur in the first position. Both sentences (1) and (2) are correct. The
       <i>
        yi-
       </i>
       prefix on the verb indicates that the first noun is the subject and
       <i>
        bi-
       </i>
       indicates that the second noun is the subject.
      </p>

      <p>
       There is evidence suggesting that the word order, per se, is not the important factor. Instead, the verb construction usually interpreted as the passive voice (eg. "the girl was pecked by the bird") instead indicates that the more animate noun allowed the less animate noun to perform the action (eg. "the girl let herself be pecked by the bird"). The idea is that things ranked higher in animacy are presumed to be in control of the situation, and that the less-animate thing can only act if the more-animate thing permits it.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
        Japanese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Japanese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Although nouns in
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       are not marked for animacy, it has two
       <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
        existential
       </a>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Possessive_(linguistics)" title="Possessive (linguistics)">
        possessive
       </a>
       verbs; one for implicitly animate nouns (usually humans and animals) and one for implicitly inanimate nouns (often nonliving objects and plants). The verb
       <i>
        iru
       </i>
       (いる, also written 居る) is used to show the existence or possession of an animate noun. The verb
       <i>
        aru
       </i>
       (ある, sometimes written 在る when existential or 有る when possessive) is used to show the existence or possession of an inanimate noun.
      </p>
      <p>
       An animate noun, there 'cat,' is marked as the subject of the verb with the subject particle
       <i>
        ga
       </i>
       (が), but no
       <a href="/wiki/Topic-prominent_language" title="Topic-prominent language">
        topic
       </a>
       or location is marked. That implies the noun is indefinite and merely exists.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Russian">
        Russian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Russian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       has a somewhat complex hierarchy of animacy in which syntactically animate nouns may include both animate and inanimate objects (like mushrooms and dances).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Overall, the border between animate and inanimate places humans and animals in the former and plants, etc., in the latter, thus basing itself more so on sentience than life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Animacy functions as a subgender through which noun cases intersect in a phenomenon called
       <a href="/wiki/Syncretism_(linguistics)" title="Syncretism (linguistics)">
        syncretism
       </a>
       , which here can be either
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        nominative-accusative or genitive-accusative
       </a>
       . Inanimate nouns have accusative forms that take on the same forms as their nominative, with animate nouns marked by having their genitive forms resemble the nominative.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, syncretism conditioned by referential animacy results in forms like the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        NOM
        <i>
         stol
        </i>
        ‘table’ -&gt; ACC stol, like nom (exhibiting nom-acc syncretism);
       </li>
       <li>
        NOM
        <i>
         kot
        </i>
        ‘cat’ -&gt; ACC kota, like gen (exhibiting gen-acc syncretism).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       That syncretism also occurs when restricted by declension class, resulting in syncretism in multiple
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronominal forms
       </a>
       , such as the reflexive pronoun
       <i>
        sebja
       </i>
       , personal pronouns, and the indefinite interrogative and relative pronoun
       <i>
        kto
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In their plural forms, nouns of all genders may distinguish the categories of animate vs. inanimate by that syncretism, but only masculine nouns of the first declension (and their modifiers) show it in the singular (Frarie 1992:12), and other declensions and genders of nouns "restrict (morphological) expression of animacy to the plural" (Frarie 1992:47).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Masc nouns that show
        <b>
         acc-gen
        </b>
        (sg &amp; plural) syncretism: муж [muʂ] husband, сын [sɨn] son, лев [lʲef] lion, конь [konʲ] horse.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Fem animate nouns that show
        <b>
         acc-gen
        </b>
        (plural) syncretism: женщина [ˈʐɛnʲɕːɪnə] woman, лошадь [ˈɫoʂətʲ] horse.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Neut animate nouns that show
        <b>
         acc-nom
        </b>
        (sg) and
        <b>
         acc-gen
        </b>
        (plural) syncretism:
        <a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/животное#Russian">
         животное
        </a>
        'animal',
        <a class="external text" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/насекомое#Russian">
         насекомое
        </a>
        'insect'.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Elsewhere, animacy is displayed syntactically, such as in endings of modifiers for masc nouns of the second declension.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Animacy_as_a_.22subgender.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Animacy_as_a_"subgender"'>
        Animacy as a "subgender"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: Animacy as a "subgender"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       While animacy is viewed as primarily semantic when approached diachronically, a synchronic view suggests animacy as a sublevel of gender.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Syntactic gender is defined through patterns in agreement, not necessarily semantic value.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, Russian has "common gender" nouns that refer to traditionally masculine roles but act as syntactically feminine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Animacy occurs as a subgender of nouns and modifiers (and pronouns only when adjectival) and is primarily reflected in modifier-head agreement (as opposed to subject-predicate agreement).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Controversy">
        Controversy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Controversy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Some consider the system to be based on marking inanimacy in which case the gen-acc distinguishes a "non-inanimate" subgender of nouns and modifiers,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klenin_6-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Klenin-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and others claim that ultimately it is indeed animacy that is marked.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Frarie_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Frarie-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish">
        Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">
        Nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , the preposition
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       (meaning "to" or "at") has gained a second role as a marker of concrete animate direct objects:
      </p>
      <table>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <i>
          Veo esa catedral.
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         "I can see that cathedral."
        </td>
        <td>
         (inanimate direct object)
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <i>
          Veo a esa persona
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         "I can see that person."
        </td>
        <td>
         (animate direct object)
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <i>
          Vengo a España.
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         "I come to Spain."
        </td>
        <td>
         (
         <i>
          a
         </i>
         used in its literal sense)
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The usage is standard and is found around the Spanish-speaking world.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">
        Pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Spanish personal pronouns are generally omitted if the subject of the sentence, but when they are explicitly stated, they are used only with people or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanized_animals" title="Humanized animals">
        humanized animals
       </a>
       or things. There are no inanimate subject pronouns in Spanish, like
       <i>
        it
       </i>
       in English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish direct object pronouns (
       <i>
        me, te, lo, la, se, nos, os, los, las
       </i>
       ) do not differentiate between animate and inanimate entities, and only the third persons have a gender distinction. Thus, for example, the third person singular feminine pronoun,
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       , could refer to a woman, an animal (like
       <i>
        mariposa
       </i>
       , butterfly), or an object (like
       <i>
        casa
       </i>
       , house), if their genders are feminine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With pronouns, there is a tendency to use
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       (which is usually an indirect object pronoun, meaning "to him/her") as a direct-object pronoun, at the expense of the direct-object pronouns
       <i>
        lo/la
       </i>
       , if the referent is animate. That tendency is especially strong if (a) the pronoun is being used as a special second-person pronoun of respect, (b) the referent is male, (c) certain verbs are used, (d) the subject of the verb happens to be inanimate. There is great regional variation as regards that usage.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Arabic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Classical
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
        Modern Standard Arabic
       </a>
       and some other
       <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Arabic" title="Varieties of Arabic">
        varieties of Arabic
       </a>
       , animacy has a limited application in the
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       of plural and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dual_form" title="Dual form">
        dual
       </a>
       nouns with verbs and adjectives. Verbs follow nouns in plural agreement only when the verb comes after the subject. When a verb comes before an explicit subject, the verb is always singular. Also, only animate plural and dual nouns take plural agreement; inanimate plural and dual nouns are always analyzed as singular feminine for the purpose of agreement. Thus, Arabic
       <span lang="ar">
        المهندسون يطيرون إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Al-muhandisūn yuṭīrūn 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       , "The engineers fly to
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       ") is masculine plural agreement, but
       <span lang="ar">
        الطائرات تطير إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Al-ṭā'irāt tuṭīr 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       , "The planes fly to Germany") is feminine singular. Compare them to
       <span lang="ar">
        تطير المهندسات إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Tuṭīr al-muhandisat 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       ) and
       <span lang="ar">
        المهندسات يطرن إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Al-muhandisāt yuṭīrna 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       ) for "The [female] engineers fly to Germany."
      </p>
      <p>
       In general, Arabic divides animacy between
       <span lang="ar">
        عاقل
       </span>
       (thinking, or rational) and
       <span lang="ar">
        غير عاقل
       </span>
       (unthinking, or irrational). Animals fall in the latter category, but their status may change depending on the usage, especially with personification. Different writers might use
       <span lang="ar">
        الغربان يطيرون إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Al-ghurbān yuṭīrūn 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       ) or
       <span lang="ar">
        الغربان تطير إلى ألمانيا
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        Al-ghurbān tuṭīr 'ilā 'Almāniyā
       </i>
       ) for "The ravens fly to Germany."
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Animacy_hierarchy_and_morphosyntactic_alignment">
        Animacy hierarchy and morphosyntactic alignment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Animacy hierarchy and morphosyntactic alignment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Split_ergativity">
        Split ergativity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Split ergativity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Animacy can also condition the nature of the morphologies of
       <a href="/wiki/Split_ergativity" title="Split ergativity">
        split-ergative
       </a>
       languages. In such languages, participants more animate are more likely to be the
       <a href="/wiki/Theta_role" title="Theta role">
        agent
       </a>
       of the verb, and therefore are marked in an accusative pattern: unmarked in the agent role and marked in the patient or oblique role.
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise, less animate participants are inherently more patient-like, and take ergative marking: unmarked when in the patient role and marked when in the agent role. The hierarchy of animacy generally, but not always, is ordered:
      </p>
      <table>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <i>
          1st person
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          2nd person
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          3rd person
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          proper names
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          humans
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <div class="plainlist">
          <ul>
           <li>
            <i>
             non-humans
            </i>
           </li>
           <li>
            <i>
             animates
            </i>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td>
         &gt;
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          inanimates
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The location of the split (the line which divides the inherently agentive participants from the inherently patientive participants) varies from language to language, and, in many cases, the two classes overlap, with a class of nouns near the middle of the hierarchy being marked for both the agent and patient roles.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hierarchical_alignment">
        Hierarchical alignment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Hierarchical alignment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a
       <a href="/wiki/Direct%E2%80%93inverse_language" title="Direct–inverse language">
        direct–inverse language
       </a>
       clauses with
       <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">
        transitive verbs
       </a>
       can be expressed with either a direct or an inverse construction. The direct construction is used when the subject of the transitive clause outranks the object in salience or animacy. The inverse construction is used when the "notional object" outranks the "notional subject".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thematic_roles">
        Thematic roles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Animacy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Thematic roles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A noun essentially requires the traits of animacy in order to receive the role of Actor and Experiencer. Additionally, the Agent role is generally assigned to the NP with highest ranking in the animacy hierarchy – ultimately, only animate beings can function as true agents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szewczyk_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Szewczyk-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly, languages universally tend to place animate nouns earlier in the sentence than inanimate nouns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Szewczyk_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Szewczyk-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Animacy is a key component of agency – combined with other factors like “awareness of action”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamamoto_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yamamoto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Agency and animacy are intrinsically linked – with each as a “conceptual property” of the other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yamamoto_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yamamoto-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
