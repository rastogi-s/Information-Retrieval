      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Gulf of Guinea
       </b>
       is the northeasternmost part of the tropical
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Lopez" title="Cape Lopez">
        Cape Lopez
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">
        Gabon
       </a>
       , north and west to
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Palmas" title="Cape Palmas">
        Cape Palmas
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Liberia" title="Liberia">
        Liberia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iho_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iho-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The intersection of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       (zero degrees
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       ) is in the gulf.
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the many rivers that drain into the Gulf of Guinea are the
       <a href="/wiki/Niger_River" title="Niger River">
        Niger
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Volta_River" title="Volta River">
        Volta
       </a>
       . The coastline on the gulf includes the
       <a href="/wiki/Bight_of_Benin" title="Bight of Benin">
        Bight of Benin
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bight_of_Bonny" title="Bight of Bonny">
        Bight of Bonny
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      <p>
       The origin of the name Guinea is thought to be an area in the region, although the specifics are disputed. Bovill (1995) gives a thorough description:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The name Guinea is usually said to have been a corrupt form of the name Ghana, picked up by the Portuguese in the
        <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
         Maghrib
        </a>
        . The present writer finds this unacceptable. The name Guinea has been in use both in the Maghrib and in Europe long before Prince Henry's time. For example, on a map dated about 1320 by the Genoese cartographer Giovanni di Carignano, who got his information about Africa from a fellow-countryman in
        <a href="/wiki/Sijilmasa" title="Sijilmasa">
         Sijilmas
        </a>
        [ancient trading city in North Africa], we find Gunuia, and in the Catalan atlas of 1375 as Ginyia. A passage in Leo [Africanus] (vol. III, 822) points to Guinea having been a corrupt form of
        <a href="/wiki/Djenn%C3%A9" title="Djenné">
         Jenne
        </a>
        [2,000-year-old city in central Mali on Niger river], less famous than Ghana but nevertheless for many centuries famed in the Maghrib as a great market and a seat of learning. The relevant passage reads: "The Kingdom of Ghinea . . . called by the merchants of our nation Gheneoa, by the natural inhabitants thereof Genni and by the Portugals and other people of Europe Ghinea." But it seems more probable that Guinea derives from
        <i>
         aguinaou
        </i>
        , the Berber for Negro. Marrakech [city in southeastern Morocco] has a gate, built in the twelfth century, called the Bab Aguinaou, the Gate of the Negro (Delafosse, Haut-Sénégal-Niger, II, 277-278). The modern application of the name Guinea to the coast dates only from 1481. In that year the Portuguese built a fort,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Jorge_da_Mina" title="São Jorge da Mina">
         São Jorge da Mina
        </a>
        (modern day
        <a href="/wiki/Elmina" title="Elmina">
         Elmina
        </a>
        ), on the
        <a href="/wiki/Gold_Coast_(region)" title="Gold Coast (region)">
         Gold Coast region
        </a>
        , and their king, John II, was permitted by the Pope [Sixtus II or Innocent VIII] to style himself Lord of Guinea, a title that survived until the recent extinction of the monarchy.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The name "
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea_(region)" title="Guinea (region)">
        Guinea
       </a>
       " was also applied to south coast of
       <a href="/wiki/West_Africa" title="West Africa">
        West Africa
       </a>
       , north of the Gulf of Guinea, which became known as "Upper Guinea", and the west coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">
        Southern Africa
       </a>
       , to the east, which became known as "Lower Guinea".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The name "Guinea" is still attached to the names of three countries in Africa:
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea" title="Guinea">
        Guinea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">
        Guinea-Bissau
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
        New Guinea
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Melanesia" title="Melanesia">
        Melanesia
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Islands_in_the_Gulf_of_Guinea">
        Islands in the Gulf of Guinea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gulf_of_Guinea&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Islands in the Gulf of Guinea">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Gulf of Guinea contains a number of islands, the largest of which are in a southwest-northeast chain, forming part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_line" title="Cameroon line">
        Cameroon line
       </a>
       of volcanoes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Annob%C3%B3n" title="Annobón">
        Annobón
       </a>
       , also known as Pagalu or Pigalu, is an island that is part of
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bobowasi_Island" title="Bobowasi Island">
        Bobowasi Island
       </a>
       is an island off the west coast of Africa in the Gulf of Guinea that is part of Western region Ghana.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bioko" title="Bioko">
        Bioko
       </a>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Island" title="Island">
        island
       </a>
       off the west coast of Africa in the Gulf of Guinea that is part of Equatorial Guinea.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Corisco" title="Corisco">
        Corisco
       </a>
       is an island belonging to Equatorial Guinea.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Elobey_Grande" title="Elobey Grande">
        Elobey Grande
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Elobey_Chico" title="Elobey Chico">
        Elobey Chico
       </a>
       are two small islands belonging to Equatorial Guinea.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">
        São Tomé and Príncipe
       </a>
       (officially the Democratic Republic of São Tomé and Príncipe) is a Portuguese-speaking
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Island_nation" title="Island nation">
        island nation
       </a>
       in the Gulf of Guinea that became independent from
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       in 1975. It is located off the western equatorial coast of Africa and consists of two islands,
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_Island" title="São Tomé Island">
        São Tomé
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Príncipe">
        Príncipe
       </a>
       . They are located about 140 kilometres (87 mi) apart and about 250 and 225 kilometres (155 and 140 mi), respectively, off the northwestern coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Gabon" title="Gabon">
        Gabon
       </a>
       . Both islands are part of an
       <a href="/wiki/Cameroon_line" title="Cameroon line">
        extinct
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
        volcanic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">
        mountain range
       </a>
       . São Tomé, the sizeable southern island, is situated just north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       .
      </p>
