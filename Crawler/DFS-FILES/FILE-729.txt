      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <b>
         Llibre dels fets
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <small>
        Catalan pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Catalan" title="Help:IPA/Catalan">
         [ˈʎiβɾə ðəls ˈfets]
        </a>
       </span>
       . Original spelling:
       <i>
        <b>
         Libre dels feyts
        </b>
       </i>
       (literally in English: "Book of Deeds"), is the autobiographical chronicle of the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/James_I_of_Aragon" title="James I of Aragon">
        James I of Aragon
       </a>
       (1213 – 1276). It is written in the
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan language
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AbulafiaBates2014_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AbulafiaBates2014-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the first person and is the first chronologically of the four works classified as the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Grand_Catalan_Chronicles&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Grand Catalan Chronicles (page does not exist)">
        Grand Catalan Chronicles
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Quatre_Grandes_Chroniques" title="fr:Les Quatre Grandes Chroniques">
         fr
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Muntaner2006_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Muntaner2006-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       all belonging to the early medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
        Crown of Aragon
       </a>
       (in the northeastern part of what is now Spain), and its first royal dynasty, the
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Barcelona" title="House of Barcelona">
        House of Barcelona
       </a>
       . James I inherited as a child the titles of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Aragon" title="King of Aragon">
        King of Aragon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Count_of_Barcelona" title="Count of Barcelona">
        Count of Barcelona
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Lords_of_Montpellier" title="Lords of Montpellier">
        Lord of Montpellier
       </a>
       , but also became by conquest
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Majorca" title="List of monarchs of Majorca">
        King of Majorca
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Valencian_monarchs" title="List of Valencian monarchs">
        King of Valencia
       </a>
       . James emphasises in his chronicles his
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Majorca" title="Conquest of Majorca">
        conquest of Majorca
       </a>
       (1229) and of
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Valencia" title="Kingdom of Valencia">
        Valencia
       </a>
       (1238).
      </p>
      <p>
       James I of Aragon dedicates a couple of chapters to his mother
       <a href="/wiki/Maria_of_Montpellier" title="Maria of Montpellier">
        Maria of Montpellier
       </a>
       and his father
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_II_of_Aragon" title="Peter II of Aragon">
        Peter II of Aragon
       </a>
       (called "Peter the Catholic"), who had been given the title of "
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rex_Catholicissimus" title="Rex Catholicissimus">
         Rex Catholicissimus
        </a>
       </i>
       " by the Pope after the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Navas_de_Tolosa" title="Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa">
        Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa
       </a>
       in which he helped
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_VIII_of_Castile" title="Alfonso VIII of Castile">
        Alfonso VIII of Castile
       </a>
       fight against the
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       , one year before his death. Peter II of Aragon died defending his vassal lords of
       <a href="/wiki/Occitania" title="Occitania">
        Occitania
       </a>
       , who were accused of allowing the
       <a href="/wiki/Catharism" title="Catharism">
        Cathar heresy
       </a>
       to proliferate in their counties. He was killed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Muret" title="Battle of Muret">
        Battle of Muret
       </a>
       , fighting against the
       <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">
        Crusader
       </a>
       troops commanded by
       <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_5th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 5th Earl of Leicester">
        Simon de Montfort
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RosenbergSwitten2013_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RosenbergSwitten2013-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barber2004_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barber2004-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the text of the
       <i>
        Llibre dels fets
       </i>
       was dictated and edited by James I, the actual writing was done by scribes, not James himself; it is written is colloquial language, representing the native tongue as spoken, and its style is direct.
      </p>
      <p>
       The conquest by James I in 1229 of
       <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       , one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">
        Balearic Islands
       </a>
       held by the
       <a href="/wiki/Almohad_Caliphate" title="Almohad Caliphate">
        Muslim Almohads
       </a>
       , and his consequent founding of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Majorca" title="Kingdom of Majorca">
        Kingdom of Majorca
       </a>
       , probably inspired him to start the dictation of his chronicles, he having had an active part in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
         Reconquista
        </a>
       </i>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       (in the context of Europe's medieval Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">
        Crusades
       </a>
       ). The
       <i>
        Llibre dels fets
       </i>
       narrative ends with James' death in 1276. Though the original is lost, many ancient copies of the codex have survived.
      </p>
      <p>
       The oldest extant manuscript written in the original
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan language
       </a>
       , a copy dating to 1343, was commissioned by the abbot of the
       <a href="/wiki/Poblet_Monastery" title="Poblet Monastery">
        Poblet Monastery
       </a>
       . An older manuscript dating to 1313, the "
       <i>
        Cronice Illustrissimi Regis Aragonum"
       </i>
       , was the version translated into
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       from the Catalan original "
       <i>
        Llibre dels Feyts del Rei en Jacme"
       </i>
       . The Latin translation is signed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">
        Dominican
       </a>
       friar
       <a href="/wiki/Pere_Marsili" title="Pere Marsili">
        Pere Marsili
       </a>
       , who was ordered by
       <a href="/wiki/James_II_of_Aragon" title="James II of Aragon">
        James II of Aragon
       </a>
       (James I's grandson) to honour his grandfather's memory by promulgating his words in the internationally used Latin language.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Particular_features">
        Particular features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llibre_dels_fets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Particular features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As the title itself indicates, more than a bare chronicle, the
       <i>
        Llibre dels fets
       </i>
       is in fact a "Book of Deeds". Studies conducted in the 1980s concluded that this medieval manuscript is of an undefined literary style, since it was dictated entirely orally. James I of Aragon, a cultivated man, dictated the entire book to royal scribes, who at that point in history commonly performed the labour of actually writing the king's words with pen on paper. Its style is informal and colloquial.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here is an extract of the first lines:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <i>
         ... Raconta Mon Senyor San Jaume que la fe sense obres, morta es. Aquesta paraula va voler complr Senyor en els nostres fets...
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        (English: ... says My Lord Saint James that faith without actions, is dead. This word he wanted to accomplish, Lord, in our deeds...)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The principal characteristics of James' style are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Religious Feeling
       </li>
       <li>
        The love of the King for his realms
       </li>
       <li>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Structure">
        Structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llibre_dels_fets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1039" data-file-width="750" height="305" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg/220px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg/330px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg/440px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f1r.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1619 Frontispiece of Catalan manuscript made by Jaume Ferrera
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the
       <i>
        Llibre del Fets
       </i>
       , James I of Aragon describes his life and his most important actions, such as the conquest of the Muslim-held
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Valencian_monarchs" title="List of Valencian monarchs">
        Valencia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Majorca" title="Conquest of Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       . The narrative begins with his birth in 1203 and ends with his death in 1276. The prologue and epilogue are written in a different style, more erudite and perfectionist than the rest of the text, and presumably written after his death. James was generally very explicit in expressing himself, as shown in his recitation of his deeds:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <i>
         E per tal que los hòmens coneguessen, quan hauríem passada aquesta vida mortal, ço que nós hauríem fet [...] e per dar eximpli a tots los altres hòmes del món...
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        (English: So that men acknowledge, when we have past this mortal life, this that we have accomplished [,,,] and to give example to all the other men of the world...)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       In the Latin translation of 1313 by
       <a href="/wiki/Pere_Marsili" title="Pere Marsili">
        Pere Marsili
       </a>
       , the friar informs that he has translated chapters from the manuscripts then kept in the royal archives, indicating that the texts of the chronicles already existed and that they were written in the vulgar language, i.e., not in Latin, but in
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The oldest preserved copy of the manuscript in the Catalan language is the copy ordered in 1343, more than 60 years after James' death, by the Abbot of the
       <a href="/wiki/Poblet_Monastery" title="Poblet Monastery">
        Poblet Monastery
       </a>
       . The text of the Catalan manuscript is nearly identical to that of the Latin translation, but the Catalan version cannot be a copy of the Latin version, as the Catalan one is written in the first person, mainly using the majestic plural "we", and only a few times the singular "I", while the Latin version is written in the third person, using the first person in only a few quotes. This fact makes it improbable that the Catalan manuscript comes from the Latin version.
      </p>
      <p>
       The internal structure of both versions seems to indicate two moments in time: the first part may have been dictated around 1240, shortly after James' conquest of Valencia. The facts before 1228 are explained in a brief, imprecise way even with significant errors, while from then on, the narrative shows greater detail and precision.
      </p>
      <p>
       The supposed second part might have been dictated around 1274, and has a similar structure; the facts from 1242-1265 are condensed in a few pages, while the later years are again explicated in great detail. The prologue and the section that describes his illness and death were probably written or dictated by someone in James' trust. The Catalan copy of 1343 and the Latin translation of 1313 have left posterity the same content.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="General_content">
        General content
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llibre_dels_fets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: General content">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The content of the
       <i>
        Llibre dels fets
       </i>
       , the chronicles of James I, can be divided into four parts:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        1208–1228: Some chapters dedicated to his ancestors and parents, his mother (
        <a href="/wiki/Maria_of_Montpellier" title="Maria of Montpellier">
         Maria of Montpellier
        </a>
        ) by whom he was Lord of Montpellier, and to his father (
        <a href="/wiki/Peter_II_of_Aragon" title="Peter II of Aragon">
         Peter II of Aragon "The Catholic"
        </a>
        ), by whom he was
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Aragon" title="King of Aragon">
         King of Aragon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/County_of_Barcelona" title="County of Barcelona">
         Count of Barcelona
        </a>
        and Count of Urgell. James' quite unexpected birth. Chapters dedicated to his father's death, known as Peter “The Catholic” (title of “
        <a href="/wiki/Rex_Catholicissimus" title="Rex Catholicissimus">
         Rex Catholicissimus
        </a>
        ” given to him by the Pope, after the
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Las_Navas_de_Tolosa" title="Battle of Las Navas de Tolosa">
         battle of Las Navas de Tolosa
        </a>
        against the
        <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
         Moors
        </a>
        ), and his death at the
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Muret" title="Battle of Muret">
         battle of Muret
        </a>
        , while defending his
        <a href="/wiki/Vassal" title="Vassal">
         vassal Lords
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/Occitania" title="Occitania">
         Occitania
        </a>
        against the invading crusader troops, mostly
        <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_France" title="Kingdom of France">
         Frankish
        </a>
        , commanded by
        <a href="/wiki/Simon_de_Montfort,_5th_Earl_of_Leicester" title="Simon de Montfort, 5th Earl of Leicester">
         Simon de Montfort, 5th Earl of Leicester
        </a>
        in the context of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathar_Crusade" title="Cathar Crusade">
         Cathar Crusade
        </a>
        (or
        <a href="/wiki/Albigensian_Crusade" title="Albigensian Crusade">
         Albigensian Crusade
        </a>
        , as it started at the southern city of
        <a href="/wiki/Albi" title="Albi">
         Albi
        </a>
        , while casually the Regent Queen of the Franks at that time was
        <a href="/wiki/Blanche_of_Castile" title="Blanche of Castile">
         Blanche of Castile
        </a>
        ). James' captivity at the age of 5 after his father's death, and how Simon de Montfort wanted to marry him to his daughter (failed plan that would have brought the De Montfort family into the
        <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
         Crown of Aragon
        </a>
        ). Finally James was returned to Aragon, where the
        <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">
         Templar Knights
        </a>
        , a powerful
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Military_religious_orders" title="Military religious orders">
         military religious order
        </a>
        within the medieval Crown of Aragon), guarded and raised him. James' childhood at
        <a href="/wiki/Monz%C3%B3n_Castle" title="Monzón Castle">
         Monzon Castle
        </a>
        , (the Templars' main castle).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2010_8-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Smith2010-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        He also explains his further marriage to Eleanor of Castile.
       </li>
       <li>
        1229–1240: The most detailed part of the "Llibre dels fets”. The
        <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Majorca" title="Conquest of Majorca">
         conquest of Majorca
        </a>
        (1229). This would be the first step in the history of the
        <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
         Crown of Aragon
        </a>
        . Shortly after that would come the
        <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Valencia" title="Kingdom of Valencia">
         conquest of Valencia
        </a>
        in 1238. The book tries to prove how King James accomplishments were Divine Will.
       </li>
       <li>
        1240–1265: Describes the conflicts with the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Saracen" title="Saracen">
          Saracen
         </a>
        </i>
        rebels from Valencia.
       </li>
       <li>
        1265–1276: Again a narrative describing the battles against the
        <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
         Moors
        </a>
        . The conquest of Murcia. Also a lot of political episodes which claim to justify his actions. The last chapters, which explain the king's illness and death were written and included in the Chronicle presumably after James' death.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_and_style">
        Language and style
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llibre_dels_fets&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Language and style">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A didactic and justifying intention is largely reflected throughout the chronicle as a religious impulse, indicating that James I believed the execution of the work was guided by divine providence. The king, who normally desired to appear as an epic hero, not only recounts military and political history in the narrative, but also frequently mentions small details of his daily life, as well as some of his most intimate thoughts.
      </p>
      <p>
       A "popular and vivid language" full of proverbs and colloquial expressions is used in the chronicles, which also quote foreign personages speaking other languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">
        Galician-Portuguese
       </a>
       (used by the Crown of Castile),
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       </span>

         <i>
          Llibre dels Fets
         </i>
         , the
         <i>
          Cronice Illustrissimi Regis Aragonum
         </i>
         by Friar
         <a href="/wiki/Pere_Marsili" title="Pere Marsili">
          Pere Marsili
         </a>
         following the order of James II of Aragon (grandson of James I of Aragon).
        </div>

      <p>
       Five codices of the text from the
       <a href="/wiki/14th_century_in_literature" title="14th century in literature">
        14th century
       </a>
       and two from the
       <a href="/wiki/15th_century_in_literature" title="15th century in literature">
        15th
       </a>
       are preserved, all based on a translation of the original Catalan text into
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">
        Dominican friar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pere_Marsili" title="Pere Marsili">
        Pere Marsili
       </a>
       at the order of King James' grandson (his namesake James II of Aragon). He intended to adapt the original text to contemporary manners and style, as can be read:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        [...] So that the deeds of His glorious grandfather (James I), collected in a truthful but vulgar style, shall be put to date and once translated to Latin, form a single History volume, a full chronicle in which all the actions of the king his grandfather (James I the Conqueror) will be woven together.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-:0-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>

       Friar Pere Marsili finished this royal assignment on 2 April 1313, and then petitioned the king that a copy of the manuscript be made for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friars_Preachers" title="Friars Preachers">
        Friars Preachers
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       , his homeland, to be used on the day of the "Feast of the Banner":
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        [...] with the purpose of the last day of the year, the annual feast which commemorates the conquest of the city of Majorca, for God's glory and the eternally worshiping memory of his Luckiest Prince (James I), and so the friars who preach on this significative solemn date in front of the whole clergy and people, could rely to this book, and more firmly be informed of the truth of the facts.”
        <i>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_9-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-:0-9">
           [9]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The official delivery of the
       <i>
        Cronice Illustrissimi Regis Aragonum domini Jacobi victorissimi principis
       </i>
       was made on 2 June 1314
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Genis_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Genis-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at the Church of the Friars Preachers (
       <i>
        església dels frares predicadors
       </i>
       ) of Valencia.
      </p>
      <p>
       Six official copies of the Latin translation by Pere Marsili exist. Four dating from the 14th century
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (conserved respectively in the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Catalonia" title="National Library of Catalonia">
        National Library of Catalonia
       </a>
       , the Archives of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Majorca" title="Kingdom of Majorca">
        Kingdom of Majorca
       </a>
       , the Archives of the Cathedral of
       <a href="/wiki/Palma,_Majorca" title="Palma, Majorca">
        Palma de Majorca
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Barcelona" title="University of Barcelona">
        University of Barcelona's
       </a>
       Library). A copy from the 17th century is kept at the
       <a href="/wiki/General_Archive_of_the_Crown_of_Aragon" title="General Archive of the Crown of Aragon">
        Archives of the Crown of Aragon
       </a>
       and another from the 19th in the
       <a href="/wiki/Real_Academia_de_la_Historia" title="Real Academia de la Historia">
        Real Academia de la Historia
       </a>
       of Spain.

           </a>
           . The only illustration in the manuscript that has survived. It depicts a supper in
           <a href="/wiki/Tarragona" title="Tarragona">
            Tarragona
           </a>
           , where James with his lords planned the
           <a href="/wiki/Conquest_of_Majorca" title="Conquest of Majorca">
            Conquest of Majorca
           </a>
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="thumb tleft">
         <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Libre-feyts-fe.jpg">
           <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1154" data-file-width="750" height="339" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Libre-feyts-fe.jpg/220px-Libre-feyts-fe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Libre-feyts-fe.jpg/330px-Libre-feyts-fe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Libre-feyts-fe.jpg/440px-Libre-feyts-fe.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
          </a>
          <div class="thumbcaption">
           <div class="magnify">
            <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Libre-feyts-fe.jpg" title="Enlarge">
            </a>
           </div>
           1343 Catalan manuscript Prologue
          </div>
         </div>
        </div>
        Dated to 1343, the oldest surviving codex in the original Catalan language is the copy ordered by Ponç de Copons, the abbot of the
        <a href="/wiki/Poblet_Monastery" title="Poblet Monastery">
         Poblet Monastery
        </a>
        , as mentioned:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <i>
         Aquest libre féu escriure l’onrat en Ponç de Copons, per la gràcia de Déu, abbat de l’honrat monestir de Sancta Maria de Poblet, en lo qual monestir jau lo molt alt senyor En Jaume, aqueyl que aquest libre parla, dels feyts que féu ni li endevengueren en la sua vida
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        (English: This book was ordered by the honorable Ponç de Copons, by God's grace, abbot of the St Maria Poblet Monastery, where there lies the most high Lord Sir James, the one about who the book tells, about the deeds and acts that he encountered in his lifetime). Friar Celestí des Torrents, who finished this manuscript on 17 September 1343.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This copy from the Poblet Monastery was made from an original manuscript owned by the Royal Chancellery. On 11 November 1343, King
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_IV_of_Aragon" title="Peter IV of Aragon">
        Peter IV of Aragon
       </a>
       sent a letter to the abbot of the Poblet Monastery demanding the return of the original codex
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1585 King
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">
        Philip II of Spain
       </a>
       visited the Poblet Monastery and ordered a copy of the chronicle for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Biblioteca_de_San_Lorenzo_de_El_Escorial" title="Real Biblioteca de San Lorenzo de El Escorial">
        Royal Library of San Lorenzo de El Escorial, Madrid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="935" data-file-width="650" height="316" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg/220px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg/330px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg/440px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._41r.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1380 Catalan copy contains two illustrations, both of friars; this first one depicts Dominican Friar Miguel Fabra i Berenguer de Castellbisbal.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="903" data-file-width="650" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg/220px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg/330px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg/440px-Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Llibre-dels-Feyts-1380-f._1r.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1380 Catalan manuscript first page: "...says My Lord Saint James that faith without actions, is dead. This word he wanted to accomplish, Lord, in our deeds..."
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The second Catalan codex manuscript source, dating to 1380, comes directly from the Royal Chancellery of King Peter IV of Aragon, and must be a direct copy of the original, as the king himself commanded Johan de
       <a href="/wiki/Barbastro" title="Barbastro">
        Barbastro
       </a>
       to make it. The text in Latin:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <i>
         Mandato serenissimi domini petri dei gratir regis Aragonum valentiae, Majoricarum, cardinieae et Corsicae, Comitisque Barchinonae, Rossilionis et Ceritaniae [...] Ego Iohannes de Barbastro de scribania predicti domini Regis Aragonum, oriundus Cesaraugustae scripsi Ciuitate Barchinonae Anno a Nativitate Dmi. Mo. CCCo. octuagesimo sripsi
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        (English: By order of Peter, by God's Grace King of
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_Aragonese_monarchs" title="List of Aragonese monarchs">
         Aragon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Valencia" title="Kingdom of Valencia">
         Valencia
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Majorca" title="Kingdom of Majorca">
         Majorca
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Sardinia" title="List of monarchs of Sardinia">
         Sardinia
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
         Corsica
        </a>
        , Count of Barcelona,
        <a href="/wiki/County_of_Roussillon" title="County of Roussillon">
         Roussillon
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/County_of_Cerdanya" title="County of Cerdanya">
         Cerdanya
        </a>
        [...] I Johan de Barbastro, scribe chosen by the King of Aragon [...] in the City of Barcelona, year of The Lord's Nativity, 1380)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Its prologue, in Catalan, reads:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        <i>
         Aquest es lo començament del prolech sobre el libre que feu el rey en Jacme per la gracia de Deu rey de Arago e de Mallorches e de Valencia, comte de Barchinona e d'Urgell e senyor de Muntpesler de tots los fets e de les gracies que Nostre Senyor li féu en la sua vida
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        (English: This is the beginning of the prologue about the book that King James made, by Holy Grace,
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_Aragonese_monarchs" title="List of Aragonese monarchs">
         King of Aragon
        </a>
        , of
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_monarchs_of_Majorca" title="List of monarchs of Majorca">
         Majorca
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_Valencian_monarchs" title="List of Valencian monarchs">
         Valentia
        </a>
        , Count of Barcelona and Count of Urgell, and Lord of Montpellier, of all his deeds and gifts our Lord gave to him in his lifetime.)
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       A relevant fact about this copy is that Johan de
       <a href="/wiki/Barbastro" title="Barbastro">
        Barbastro
       </a>
       used an official codex from the Royal Chancellerie (now disappeared). King Pere IV ("the Ceremonious") ordered three copies: one for Majorca, one for Barcelona and another for Valencia. Only the Majorcan copy has survived, and is now preserved in the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_Catalonia" title="National Library of Catalonia">
        National Library of Catalonia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This first printed edition was ordered and paid for by the
       <a href="/wiki/Valencia" title="Valencia">
        Jury of the city of Valencia
       </a>
       in 1557. Made in a period of historical inquiries, once the print was finished, a copy was sent to Madrid to King Philip II of Spain (known by the Catalans as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Castile" title="Philip II of Castile">
        Philip II of Castile
       </a>
       ) from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Habsburg" title="Spanish Habsburg">
        House of Habsburg
       </a>
       , who had also been very interested in the manuscript codex kept in the Poblet Monastery.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1038" data-file-width="750" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg/220px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg/330px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg/440px-Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Historia_del_molt_alt_y_invictissim_senyor_lo_Rey_Don_Jayme_de_Aragon-1619-f62r.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         1619 Catalan copy from Jaume Ferrera of the Poblet Monastery's manuscript. This faithful depiction is the second and last of the book, and has disappeared in the other copies.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Other manuscripts have survived, all copies of the one made for the Poblet Monastery in 1343. There is one relevant codex between them conserved in the library of the University of Barcelona made by student Jaume Ferrera by order of his master, Prior Jaume Ramon Vila,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who added a prologue, which is its singular feature. The Prior explains the reason he ordered the present copy of the
       <i>
        Llibre dels fets,
       </i>
       was "to deny the forgery issues that
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Castilian
       </a>
       historians were throwing at
       <a href="/wiki/Catalans" title="Catalans">
        Catalans
       </a>
       ". He indicates as well that the illustrations are faithful copies of the manuscript dated 1343 from the Poblet Monastery. The other relevant feature of this codex is the second original illustration that did not survive from the Poblet manuscript.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this picture we can see the
       <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_the_Palace" title="Mayor of the Palace">
        Mayor of the Palace
       </a>
       , Hugh de Forcalquier, and Blasco de Alagón kneeling before James I.
      </p>
