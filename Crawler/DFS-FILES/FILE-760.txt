      <p>
       The
       <b>
        name of
        <i>
         México
        </i>
       </b>
       has several
       <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">
        hypotheses
       </a>
       that entail the
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        origin, history, and use of the name
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9xico" title="México">
         México
        </a>
       </i>
       , which dates back to 14th century
       <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">
        Mesoamerica
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuatl_language" title="Nahuatl language">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       means
       <i>
        place of the Mexica
       </i>
       but the
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnonym" title="Ethnonym">
        ethnonym
       </a>
       <i>
        Mexicatl
       </i>
       itself is of unknown etymology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Scholars have suggested "Hare of the Aloes" or a synonym of the war god Huitzilopochtli as possible roots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An alternate understanding is that the name may come from the word mexixin, a cress that grew in the swamplands of
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Texcoco" title="Lake Texcoco">
        Texcoco Lake
       </a>
       . It was an edible grass that the Aztecs or Mexica survived on as they settled where today lies México City.
      </p>
      <p>
       The country
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M%C3%A9xico" title="México">
        México
       </a>
       did not name its capital after itself, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       —the accepted name internationally—but the converse actually applies. Before Spanish times, the capital was formally named
       <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">
        Tenochtitlan
       </a>
       , but was the seat of the Mexica Empire which is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec_Empire" title="Aztec Empire">
        Aztec Empire
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As far back as 1590, the
       <a href="/wiki/Theatrum_Orbis_Terrarum" title="Theatrum Orbis Terrarum">
        Theatrum Orbis Terrarum
       </a>
       showed that the northern part of the
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       was known as "America Mexicana" (Mexican America), as México City was the seat for the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       viceroyalty.
       <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       is mistaken as the old name for México, rather than the name of a large of expanse of land which covered much of North America and included the Caribbean and the Philippines. Since
       <i>
        <b>
         New Spain
        </b>
       </i>
       wasn't actually a state or a contiguous part of land, in modern times we would thought of it as a
       <i>
        Jurisdiction
       </i>
       under the command of the authorities in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       . Under the Spaniards, Mexico was both the name of the capital and its sphere of influence, most of which exists as
       <a href="/wiki/Greater_Mexico_City" title="Greater Mexico City">
        Greater Mexico City
       </a>
       and the State of Mexico. Some parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Puebla" title="Puebla">
        Puebla
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morelos" title="Morelos">
        Morelos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hidalgo_(state)" title="Hidalgo (state)">
        Hidalgo
       </a>
       were also part of Spanish-era Mexico.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1821, the continental part of New Spain seceded from Spain during the
       <a href="/wiki/Trienio_Liberal" title="Trienio Liberal">
        Trienio Liberal
       </a>
       , in which
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agustin_de_Iturbide" title="Agustin de Iturbide">
        Agustin de Iturbide
       </a>
       marched triumphantly with his
       <a href="/wiki/Army_of_the_Three_Guarantees" title="Army of the Three Guarantees">
        Army of the Three Guarantees
       </a>
       (religion, independence, and unity). This was followed by the birth of the short-lived
       <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
        Mexican Empire
       </a>
       that used the "Mexico" name according to the convention used previously by
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Imperium Romanum
        </span>
       </i>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Roman_Empire" title="Holy Roman Empire">
        Holy Roman Empire
       </a>
       , whereby the capital gives rise to the name of the Empire. This was the first recorded use of "Mexico" as a country title.
      </p>
      <p>
       After the Empire fell and the Republic was established in 1824, a
       <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">
        Federation
       </a>
       name form was adopted; which was, at most times, more
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_jure" title="De jure">
         de jure
        </a>
       </i>
       than
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/De_facto" title="De facto">
         de facto
        </a>
       </i>
       . The Mexican name stuck, leading to the formation of the Mexican Republic which formally is known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_Mexican_States_(1824%E2%80%931864)" title="United Mexican States (1824–1864)">
        United Mexican States
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Complications arose with the capital's former colloquial and semi-official name "Ciudad de Mexico, Distrito Federal (Mexico, D.F.)", which appears on postal addresses and is frequently cited in the media, thus creating a duplication whereas the shortened name was "Mexico, D.F., Mexico". Legally, the name was simply Distrito Federal (Federal District or District of the Federation). This ended with the change in statute of Mexico City into a state in 2016. Today it's officially called only "Ciudad de México, México" abbreviated CDMX, Mexico.
      </p>
      <p>
       The official name of the country is the "United Mexican States" (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Estados Unidos Mexicanos
        </span>
       </i>
       ), since it is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_divisions_of_Mexico" title="Political divisions of Mexico">
        federation of thirty-two states
       </a>
       . The official name was first used in the
       <a href="/wiki/1824_Constitution_of_Mexico" title="1824 Constitution of Mexico">
        Constitution of 1824
       </a>
       , and was retained in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constitutions_of_Mexico" title="Constitutions of Mexico">
        constitutions of 1857 and 1917
       </a>
       . Informally, "Mexico" is used along with "Mexican Republic" (
       <i>
        República Mexicana
       </i>
       ). On 22 November 2012, outgoing Mexican President
       <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Felipe Calderón">
        Felipe Calderón
       </a>
       proposed changing the official name of the country to simply
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Anahuac_(Aztec)" title="Anahuac (Aztec)">
        Anahuac
       </a>
       (meaning land surrounded by water) was the name in
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       given to what is now Mexico during
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Hispanic" title="Pre-Hispanic">
        Pre-Hispanic
       </a>
       times. When the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Conquistador" title="Conquistador">
        conquistadors
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Tenochtitlan" title="Siege of Tenochtitlan">
        besieged México-Tenochtitlan
       </a>
       in 1521, it was almost completely destroyed. It was rebuilt during the following three years, after which it was designated as a municipality and capital of the vice-royalty of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Spain" title="New Spain">
        New Spain
       </a>
       . In 1524 the municipality of
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       was established, known as
       <i>
        México Tenustitlan
       </i>
       , and as of 1585 became officially known simply
       <i>
        Ciudad de México
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name
       <i>
        Mexico
       </i>
       was used only to refer to the city, and later to a province within New Spain. It was not until the independence of the vice-royalty of New Spain that "Mexico" became the traditional and conventional short-form name of the country.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 1810s, different insurgent groups advocated and fought for the independence of the vice-royalty of New Spain. This vast territory was composed of different
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intendencia" title="Intendencia">
         intendencias
        </a>
       </i>
       and provinces, successors of the kingdoms and captaincies general administered by the vice-regal capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       . In 1813, the deputies of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_Anahuac" title="Congress of Anahuac">
        Congress of Anahuac
       </a>
       signed the document
       <i>
        Acta Solemne de la Declaración de Independencia de la América Septentrional
       </i>
       , ("
       <a href="/wiki/Solemn_Act_of_the_Declaration_of_Independence_of_Northern_America" title="Solemn Act of the Declaration of Independence of Northern America">
        Solemn Act of the Declaration of Independence of Northern America
       </a>
       "). In 1814 the Supreme Congress of the revolutionary forces that met at
       <a href="/wiki/Apatzing%C3%A1n" title="Apatzingán">
        Apatzingán
       </a>
       (in today's state of
       <a href="/wiki/Michoac%C3%A1n" title="Michoacán">
        Michoacán
       </a>
       ) drafted
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1814_Constitution_of_Mexico" title="1814 Constitution of Mexico">
        the first constitution
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in 1814 whereby the name
       <i>
        América Mexicana
       </i>
       ("Mexican America") was chosen for the country. The head of the insurgent forces, however, was defeated by the royalist forces, and the constitution was never enacted.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Servando_Teresa_de_Mier" title="Servando Teresa de Mier">
        Servando Teresa de Mier
       </a>
       , in a treatise written in 1820 in which he discussed the reasons why New Spain was the only overseas territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       that had not yet secured its independence, chose the term
       <i>
        Anáhuac
       </i>
       to refer to the country.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This term, in Nahuatl, was used by the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexica" title="Mexica">
        Mexica
       </a>
       to refer to the territory they dominated. According to some linguists, it means "near or surrounded by waters", probably in reference to
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Texcoco" title="Lake Texcoco">
        Lake Texcoco
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       even though it was also the word used to refer to the
       <a href="/wiki/World" title="World">
        world
       </a>
       or the terrestrial universe (as when used in the phrase
       <i>
        Cem Anáhuac
       </i>
       , "the entire earth") and in which their capital,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexico-Tenochtitlan" title="Mexico-Tenochtitlan">
        Mexico-Tenochtitlan
       </a>
       , was at the centre and at the same time at the centre of the waters, being built on an island in a lake.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2150" data-file-width="1590" height="297" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg/220px-Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg/330px-Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg/440px-Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Acta_Independencia_Mexico_1821.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Declaration of Independence of the Mexican Empire, 1821
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In September 1821, the independence of Mexico was finally recognized by Spain, achieved through an alliance of royalist and revolutionary forces. The former tried to preserve the
       <i>
        status quo
       </i>
       of the vice-royalty, menaced by the liberal reforms taking place in Spain, through the establishment of an autonomous
       <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_monarchy" title="Constitutional monarchy">
        constitutional monarchy
       </a>
       under an independence hero. Agustín was crowned and given the titles of:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agustin_de_Iturbide" title="Agustin de Iturbide">
        Agustin de Iturbide
       </a>
       por la divina providencia y por el Congreso de la Nación, primer Emperador Constitucional de Mexico (Agustín de Iurbide First Constitutional Emperor of Mexico by Divine Providence and by the Congress of the Nation). The name chosen for the country was
       <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
        <i>
         Imperio Mexicano
        </i>
        , "Mexican Empire"
       </a>
       . The empire collapsed in 1823, and the republican forces drafted a
       <a href="/wiki/Constitution" title="Constitution">
        constitution
       </a>
       the following year whereby a
       <a href="/wiki/Federalism" title="Federalism">
        federal form of government
       </a>
       was instituted. In the
       <a href="/wiki/1824_Constitution_of_Mexico" title="1824 Constitution of Mexico">
        1824 constitution
       </a>
       , which gave rise to the Mexican
       <a href="/wiki/Federation" title="Federation">
        federation
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Estados Unidos Mexicanos
       </i>
       (also
       <i>
        Estados-unidos mexicanos
       </i>
       ) –
       <i>
        Mexican United States
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Mexican United-States
       </i>
       (official English translation:
       <i>
        United Mexican States
       </i>
       ) – was adopted as the country's official name.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1857_Constitution_of_Mexico" title="1857 Constitution of Mexico">
        constitution of 1857
       </a>
       used the term
       <i>
        República Mexicana
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Mexican Republic
       </i>
       ) interchangeably with
       <i>
        Estados Unidos Mexicanos
       </i>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1917_Constitution_of_Mexico" title="1917 Constitution of Mexico">
        the current constitution, promulgated in 1917
       </a>
       , only uses the latter
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        United Mexican States
       </i>
       is the normative English translation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Mexican_Empire" title="Second Mexican Empire">
        name "Mexican Empire" was briefly revived
       </a>
       from 1863 to 1867 by the conservative government that instituted a constitutional monarchy for a second time under
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maximilian_of_Habsburg" title="Maximilian of Habsburg">
        Maximilian of Habsburg
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       On 22 November 2012, incumbent President
       <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Felipe Calderón">
        Felipe Calderón
       </a>
       sent to the Mexican Congress a piece of legislation to change the country's name officially to simply Mexico. To go into effect, the bill would have to be passed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Congress_of_Mexico" title="Congress of Mexico">
        both houses of Congress
       </a>
       , as well as a majority of Mexico's 31 state legislatures. Coming within just a week before
       <a href="/wiki/Felipe_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Felipe Calderón">
        Calderón
       </a>
       turned power over to then
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/President_elect" title="President elect">
        president elect
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Enrique_Pe%C3%B1a_Nieto" title="Enrique Peña Nieto">
        Enrique Peña Nieto
       </a>
       , many of the president's critics saw the proposal as nothing more than a symbolic gesture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_of_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to one legend,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the war deity and patron of the Mexica
       <a href="/wiki/Huitzilopochtli" title="Huitzilopochtli">
        Huitzilopochtli
       </a>
       possessed
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mexitl" title="Mexitl">
         Mexitl
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Mexi
       </i>
       as a secret name. Mexico would then mean "Place of Mexi" or "Land of the War God."
      </p>
      <p>
       Another hypothesis
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gob_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gob-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggests that
       <i>
        Mēxihco
       </i>
       derives from a
       <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">
        portmanteau
       </a>
       of the Nahuatl words for "moon" (
       <i>
        mētztli
       </i>
       ) and navel (
       <i>
        xīctli
       </i>
       ). This meaning ("Place at the Center of the Moon") might then refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Tenochtitlan" title="Tenochtitlan">
        Tenochtitlan
       </a>
       's position in the middle of
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Texcoco" title="Lake Texcoco">
        Lake Texcoco
       </a>
       . The system of interconnected lakes, of which Texcoco formed the center, had the form of a rabbit, which the Mesoamericans
       <a href="/wiki/Pareidolia" title="Pareidolia">
        pareidolically
       </a>
       associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rabbit" title="Moon rabbit">
        moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Still another hypothesis
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gob_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-gob-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       offers that it is derived from
       <i>
        Mectli,
       </i>
       the goddess of
       <a href="/wiki/Agave_americana" title="Agave americana">
        maguey
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       These last two suggestions are deprecated by linguist Frances Karttunen,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       since the final form "Mēxihco" differs in
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">
        vowel length
       </a>
       from both proposed elements. Nahua
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponymy
       </a>
       is full of mysticism, however, as it was pointed out by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       missionary
       <a href="/wiki/Bernardino_de_Sahag%C3%BAn" title="Bernardino de Sahagún">
        Bernardino de Sahagún
       </a>
       . In his mystic interpretation, Mexico could mean "Center of the World," and, in fact, it was represented as such in various codices, as a place where all water currents that cross the
       <i>
        Anahuac
       </i>
       ("world" or "land surrounded by seas") converge (see image on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mendoza_codex" title="Mendoza codex">
        Mendoza codex
       </a>
       ). It is thus possible that the other meanings (or even the "secret name" Mexi) were then popular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pseudoetymologies" title="Pseudoetymologies">
        pseudoetymologies
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There has also been suggestion of Hebrew origin:
      </p>
      <p>
       According to a letter written by a Creole priest, Fray Servando Teresa de Mier, and translated by Jace Willard, many Nahuatl (Aztec) names had Hebrew roots:
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         According to Torquemada, "The first missionaries, in order to write the Aztec tongue . . . that we call Mexican, were in agreement with the wisest Indians created in the School of Santiago Tlatilolco (sic), and as their pronunciation has two Hebrew letters, Sade and Scin, they substitute them in their writing by approximating the first with tz and the second with a soft x. But . . . the majority of the conquistadors, being from Extremadura or Andalucia, or Arab in their pronunciation, strongly pronounced all of the x's written by the missionaries. . . . Because of this the Spaniards said "Mexico" (Mejico), even though the Indians invariably pronounced it "Mexico" (Mescico) with the Hebrew letter Scin. . ."
         <p>
          Mexico with a soft x like the Indians pronounced it means: "where Christ is worshiped" and [thus the term] "Mexicans" is the same as "Christians." . . . And Mexi, I ask, means what? As the Indians pronounced it, it is a Hebrew word that means, taking it from the Latin unctus, what we call "anointed," taking it from the Greek Chrestous, what we call "Christ," and taking it from the Hebrew Mesci, what we call "Messiah."
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
           <a href="#cite_note-18">
            [18]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </p>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetic_evolution">
        Phonetic evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_of_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Phonetic evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Nahuatl word
       <i>
        Mēxihco
       </i>
       , pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [meːˈʃiʔko]
        </a>
       </span>
       , was transliterated as "México" using Medieval Spanish orthography, in which the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       represented the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
        voiceless postalveolar fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       , the equivalent of English
       <i>
        sh
       </i>
       in "shop"), making "México" pronounced as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmeʃiko]
       </span>
       . At the time, Spanish
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       represented the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
        voiced postalveolar fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       , like the English
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       in "vision", or French
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       today). However, by the end of the fifteenth century
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       had evolved into a voiceless palato-alveolar sibilant as well, and thus both
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       represented the same sound (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       ). During the sixteenth century this sound evolved into a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
        voiceless velar fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , like the
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       in Scottish "loch"), and
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       began to be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈmexiko]
        </a>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2138" data-file-width="3000" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg/220px-Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg/330px-Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg/440px-Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Map_of_Mexico_1847.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Mapa de Méjico 1847
         </i>
         , showing the alternative disused spellings for Mexico, Texas and Oaxaca
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Given that both
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       represented the same new sound (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       ), and in lack of a spelling convention, many words that originally had the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       sound, began to be written with
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       (e.g. it wasn't uncommon to find both
       <i>
        exército
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ejército
       </i>
       used during the same time period, even though that due to historicity, the correct spelling would have been
       <i>
        exército
       </i>
       ). The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       , the institution in charge of regulating the Spanish language, was established in 1713, and its members agreed to simplify spelling, and set
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       to represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /x/
       </span>
       regardless of the original spelling of the word, and
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       to represent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ks/
       </span>
       . (The
       <i>
        ph
       </i>
       spelling underwent a similar removal, in that it was simplified as
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       in all words, e.g.
       <i>
        philosophía
       </i>
       became
       <i>
        filosofía
       </i>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       Nevertheless, there was ambivalence in the application of this rule in Mexican toponyms:
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       was used alongside
       <i>
        Méjico
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
         Texas
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Tejas
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oaxaca" title="Oaxaca">
         Oaxaca
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Oajaca
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Xalixco
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Jalisco" title="Jalisco">
         Jalisco
        </a>
       </i>
       , etc., as well as in proper and last names:
       <i>
        Xavier
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Javier
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Ximénez
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Jiménez
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Roxas
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Rojas
       </i>
       are spelling variants still used today. In any case, the spelling
       <i>
        Méjico
       </i>
       for the name of the country is little used in Mexico or the rest of the Spanish-speaking world today. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       itself recommends the spelling "México".
      </p>
      <p>
       In present-day Spanish,
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈmexiko]
        </a>
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmehiko]
       </span>
       , the latter pronunciation used mostly in dialects of southern Mexico, the Caribbean, much of Central America, some places in South America, and the Canary Islands and western Andalusia in Spain where
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       has become a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
        voiceless glottal fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈmeçiko]
       </span>
       in Chile and Peruvian coast where
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_fricative" title="Voiceless palatal fricative">
        voiceless palatal fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_fricative#Palatal" title="Voiceless palatal fricative">
         ç
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       is an allophone of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       before palatal vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [i], [e]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Normative_spelling_in_Spanish">
        Normative spelling in Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Name_of_Mexico&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Normative spelling in Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       is the predominant
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       spelling variant used throughout
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       , and universally used in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       , whereas
       <i>
        Méjico
       </i>
       is used infrequently in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       . During the 1990s, the Real Academia Española recommended that
       <i>
        México
       </i>
       be the normative spelling of the word and all its derivatives, even though this spelling does not match the pronunciation of the word.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since then, the majority of publications adhere to the new normative in all Spanish-speaking countries even though the disused variant can still be found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The same rule applies to all Spanish toponyms in
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        America
       </a>
       , and on some occasions in the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       , even though in most official or regional languages of Spain (
       <a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">
        Asturian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonese_language" title="Leonese language">
        Leonese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       ) and Portuguese, the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       is still pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
