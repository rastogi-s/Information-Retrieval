      <p>
       <b>
        Lombard
       </b>
       (native name
       <i>
        lumbàart
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lumbard
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lombard
       </i>
       , depending on the orthography;
       <small>
        pronounced
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [lumˈbɑːrt]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a language
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       belonging to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cisalpine_(linguistics)" title="Cisalpine (linguistics)">
        Cisalpine
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       group, within the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . It is a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect#Dialect_and_language_clusters" title="Dialect">
        cluster of homogeneous varieties
       </a>
       used by at least 3,500,000 native speakers in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       (most of
       <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">
        Lombardy
       </a>
       and some areas of neighbouring regions, notably the eastern side of
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       ) and Southern
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       (cantons of
       <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">
        Ticino
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">
        Graubünden
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . The languages closest to Lombard are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al" title="Franco-Provençal">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">
        Romansh
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_substratum" title="Linguistic substratum">
        linguistic substratum
       </a>
       having left its mark on this language is that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ligures" title="Ligures">
        ancient Ligures
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnoletto_p._120_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Agnoletto_p._120-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D'Ilario_p._28_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-D'Ilario_p._28-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Available information about this variety is extremely vague and limited
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnoletto_p._120_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Agnoletto_p._120-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D'Ilario_p._28_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-D'Ilario_p._28-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . This is in sharp contradistinction to the picture that can be drawn about the group which replaced the Ligures, the
       <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">
        Celts
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D'Ilario_p._29_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-D'Ilario_p._29-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Contributions from the Celts to local languages were self-evident, so that Lombard language is still classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance_languages" title="Gallo-Romance languages">
        Gallo-Romance language
       </a>
       (from ancient Romans name for Celts,
       <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">
        Gauls
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnoletto_p._120_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Agnoletto_p._120-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman domination
       </a>
       shaped dialects spoken in ancient Lombardy, such that
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       of this language find their origin in the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin language
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-D'Ilario_p._29_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-D'Ilario_p._29-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . This influence was not yet homogeneous
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnoletto_p._120_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Agnoletto_p._120-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ; idioms of different areas were influenced by previous linguistic substrata and each area was marked by a stronger or weaker characterisation in comparison to Ligure or ancient Celtic languages
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Agnoletto_p._120_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Agnoletto_p._120-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lombardic_language" title="Lombardic language">
        Lombardic language
       </a>
       left clear traces too, as it was the variety spoken by Longobards, a
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
        Germanic population
       </a>
       which dominated a large section of
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , including Lombardy, after the fall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">
        Western Roman Empire
       </a>
       . Lombardic had acted as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linguistic_superstratum" title="Linguistic superstratum">
        linguistic superstratum
       </a>
       over Lombard, since the Longobards did not impose their language on the population. Lombardic left traces without Germanicising the local language, such that Lombard preserved its Romance nature
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">
        Status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lombard is considered a
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">
        minority language
       </a>
       , structurally separate from
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue
       </a>
       and by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
         UNESCO
        </a>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.helsinki.fi/~tasalmin/europe_index.html" rel="nofollow">
         Red Book on Endangered Languages
        </a>
       </i>
       . However, Italy and Switzerland do not recognize Lombard speakers as a linguistic minority. In Italy this is the same as for most other minority languages
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , which are normally
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       considered
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_Italian" title="Regional Italian">
        Italian dialects
       </a>
       - despite the fact that they belong to different subgroups of the Romance language family, and their historical development is not derived from Italian
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Speakers">
        Speakers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Speakers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Historically, the vast majority of
       <a href="/wiki/Lombards" title="Lombards">
        Lombards
       </a>
       spoke only Lombard
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . With the rise of Standard Italian throughout Italy and Switzerland, one is not likely to find wholly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoglot" title="Monoglot">
        monolingual
       </a>
       Lombard speakers, but a small minority may still be uncomfortable speaking the dominant Italian. Surveys in Italy find that all Lombard speakers also speak
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , and their command of each of the two languages varies according to their
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geographical
       </a>
       position as well as their
       <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
        socio
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
        economic
       </a>
       situation. The most reliable predictor was found to be the speaker's age: studies have found that young people are much less likely to speak Lombard as proficiently as their grandparents did.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lombard is from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italian" title="Gallo-Italian">
        Gallo-Italian
       </a>
       languages, which share features with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Romance" title="Gallo-Romance">
        Gallo-Romance
       </a>
       languages and other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Romance" title="Western Romance">
        Western Romance
       </a>
       languages.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Varieties">
        Varieties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Varieties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The varieties of the Italian provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_City_of_Milan" title="Metropolitan City of Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Varese" title="Province of Varese">
        Varese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Como" title="Province of Como">
        Como
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Lecco" title="Province of Lecco">
        Lecco
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Lodi" title="Province of Lodi">
        Lodi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Monza_and_Brianza" title="Province of Monza and Brianza">
        Monza and Brianza
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pavia" title="Province of Pavia">
        Pavia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Mantua" title="Province of Mantua">
        Mantua
       </a>
       belong to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Lombard_dialects" title="Western Lombard dialects">
        Western Lombard
       </a>
       , and the ones of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Bergamo" title="Province of Bergamo">
        Bergamo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Brescia" title="Province of Brescia">
        Brescia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cremona" title="Province of Cremona">
        Cremona
       </a>
       are dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Lombard_dialect" title="Eastern Lombard dialect">
        Eastern Lombard
       </a>
       . All the varieties spoken in the Swiss areas (both in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_(subnational_entity)" title="Canton (subnational entity)">
        canton
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">
        Ticino
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canton_(subnational_entity)" title="Canton (subnational entity)">
        canton
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">
        Graubünden
       </a>
       ) are Western, and both Western and Eastern varieties are found in the Italian areas.
      </p>
      <p>
       The varieties of the Alpine valleys of
       <a href="/wiki/Valchiavenna" title="Valchiavenna">
        Valchiavenna
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Valtellina" title="Valtellina">
        Valtellina
       </a>
       (province of
       <a href="/wiki/Sondrio" title="Sondrio">
        Sondrio
       </a>
       ) and upper-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valcamonica" title="Valcamonica">
        Valcamonica
       </a>
       (province of Brescia) and the four Lombard valleys of the Swiss canton of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">
        Graubünden
       </a>
       , although they have some peculiarities of their own and some traits in common with Eastern Lombard, should be considered Western
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . Also, dialects from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piedmontese" title="Piedmontese">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       provinces of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbano-Cusio-Ossola" title="Verbano-Cusio-Ossola">
        Verbano-Cusio-Ossola
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Novara" title="Province of Novara">
        Novara
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Valsesia" title="Valsesia">
        Valsesia
       </a>
       valley (province of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Vercelli" title="Province of Vercelli">
        Vercelli
       </a>
       ), and the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Tortona" title="Tortona">
        Tortona
       </a>
       are closer to Western Lombard than to Piedmontese.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Lombard_literature" title="Western Lombard literature">
        Western Lombard literature
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Lombard variety with the oldest literary tradition (from the 13th century) is that of
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       , but now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milanese" title="Milanese">
        Milanese
       </a>
       , the native Lombard variety of the area, has almost completely been superseded by Italian from the heavy influx of immigrants from other parts of Italy (especially
       <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">
        Apulia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">
        Campania
       </a>
       ) during the fast industrialization after the
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        Second World War
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ticinese_dialect" title="Ticinese dialect">
        Ticinese
       </a>
       is a comprehensive denomination for the Lombard varieties spoken in Swiss
       <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">
        Canton Ticino (Tessin)
       </a>
       , and the
       <i>
        Ticinese koiné
       </i>
       is the Western Lombard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9" title="Koiné">
        koiné
       </a>
       used by speakers of local dialects (particularly those diverging from the
       <i>
        koiné
       </i>
       itself) when they communicate with speakers of other Lombard dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Ticino" title="Ticino">
        Ticino
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graub%C3%BCnden" title="Graubünden">
        Grigioni
       </a>
       , or Italian
       <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">
        Lombardy
       </a>
       . The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9" title="Koiné">
        koiné
       </a>
       is similar to Milanese and the varieties of the neighbouring provinces on the Italian side of the border.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is extant literature in other varieties of Lombard, for example
       <i>
        La masséra da bé
       </i>
       , a theatrical work in early Eastern Lombard, written by Galeazzo dagli Orzi (1492–?) presumably in 1554.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2014)">
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Standard Italian is widely used in Lombard-speaking areas. However, the status of Lombard is quite different in the Swiss and Italian areas, such that the Swiss areas have now become the real stronghold of Lombard.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Switzerland">
        In Switzerland
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: In Switzerland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LSI_CDE.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="389" data-file-width="518" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/LSI_CDE.jpg/220px-LSI_CDE.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/LSI_CDE.jpg/330px-LSI_CDE.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/LSI_CDE.jpg/440px-LSI_CDE.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LSI_CDE.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The LSI, published in 2004
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the Swiss areas, the local Lombard
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       are generally better preserved and more vital than in Italy. No negative feelings are associated with the use of Lombard in everyday life, even with complete strangers. Some radio and television programmes, particularly comedies, are occasionally broadcast by the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.rsi.ch" rel="nofollow">
        Swiss Italian-speaking broadcasting company
       </a>
       in Lombard. Moreover, it is common for people from the street to answer in Lombard in spontaneous interviews. Even some television ads in Lombard have been reported. The major research institution working on Lombard dialects is located in
       <a href="/wiki/Bellinzona" title="Bellinzona">
        Bellinzona
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        CDE - Centro di dialettologia e di etnografia
       </i>
       , a governmental (cantonal) institution); there is no comparable institution in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . In December 2004, the CDE released a dictionary in five volumes, covering all the Lombard varieties spoken in the Swiss areas.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Italy">
        In Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lombard_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: In Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Today, in most urban areas of Italian Lombardy, people under 40 years old speak almost exclusively Italian in their daily lives because of schooling and
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       broadcasts in Italian. However, in Periferic Lombardy (
       <a href="/wiki/Valtellina" title="Valtellina">
        Valtellina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Como" title="Lake Como">
        Lake Como
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bergamo" title="Bergamo">
        Bergamo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brescia" title="Brescia">
        Brescia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lodi,_Lombardy" title="Lodi, Lombardy">
        Lodi
       </a>
       ), Lombard is still vital.
      </p>
      <p>
       That is from a number of historical and social reasons: its usage has been historically discouraged by
       <a href="/wiki/Politics_of_Italy" title="Politics of Italy">
        Italian politicians
       </a>
       , probably as it was regarded as an obstacle to the attempt to create a 'national identity' because speaking a nonstandard variety is a sign of poor schooling or a low
       <a href="/wiki/Social_status" title="Social status">
        social status
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Now, the
       <a href="/wiki/Political_party" title="Political party">
        political party
       </a>
       most supportive of Lombard (and of the varieties of Northern Italy in general) is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_League_(Italy)" title="Northern League (Italy)">
        Northern League
       </a>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       (in the past, on the other hand, the leftist parties were the ones giving support to local varieties).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Thus, speaking a dialect of some minority languages might be politically controversial in Italy.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A certain revival of the use of Lombard has been observed in the last decade, when the use of Lombard has become a way to express one's local identity and to distance oneself from Roman-oriented mainstream Italian culture. The popularity of modern artists singing their lyrics in some Lombard dialect (in Italian "
       <i>
        rock dialettale
       </i>
       ", the most well-known of such artists being
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.davidevandesfroos.com/" rel="nofollow">
        Davide Van de Sfroos
       </a>
       ) is also a relatively new but growing phenomenon involving both the Swiss and Italian areas.
      </p>

It is spoken in the Italian region of Lombardy, in the Novara province of Piedmont, and in Switzerland. Mutual intelligibility between speakers of Lombard and monolingual Italian speakers has been reported as very low (Tamburelli 2014). Although some Lombard varieties, Milanese in particular, enjoy a rather long and prestigious literary tradition, Lombard is now mostly used in informal domains. According to Ethnologue, Piedmontese and Lombard are spoken by between 1,600,000 and 2,000,000 speakers and around 3,500,000 speakers respectively.
