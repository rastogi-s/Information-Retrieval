    Castilian Spanish
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       In English,
       <b>
        Castilian Spanish
       </b>
       sometimes refers to the variety of
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        Peninsular Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in northern and central
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       or as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_standard" title="Language standard">
        language standard
       </a>
       for radio and TV speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Spanish, the term
       <i>
        castellano
       </i>
       (Castilian) usually refers to the Spanish language as a whole, or to the medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish language
       </a>
       , a predecessor to modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Castilian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:452px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="788" height="286" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png/450px-Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png/675px-Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6b/Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png 2x" width="450"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castillian_dialects_in_spain.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of languages and dialects in Spain.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Names_given_to_the_Spanish_language" title="Names given to the Spanish language">
        Names given to the Spanish language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        Castilian Spanish
       </i>
       can be used in English for the specific varieties of Spanish spoken in north and central Spain. Sometimes it is more loosely used to denote the Spanish spoken in all of Spain as compared to Spanish spoken in Latin America. There are several different
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        varieties of Spanish
       </a>
       , which should not be confused with the other official and non official
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Spain" title="Languages of Spain">
        languages in Spain
       </a>
       , of which Castilian is only the most prominent because it is the only one official throughout the whole national territory.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term in Spanish for varieties spoken in Northern and Central Spanish would be
       <i>
        castellano septentrional
       </i>
       ("Northern Castilian").
       <i>
        Español castellano
       </i>
       , the literal translation of
       <i>
        Castilian Spanish
       </i>
       , while not being a common expression, would be understood literally and it would only refer to varieties found in
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
        Castile
       </a>
       itself, so the varieties found for instance in
       <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navarra" title="Navarra">
        Navarra
       </a>
       would be excluded even though they are a part of
       <i>
        castellano septentrional
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_variations_in_Spain">
        Regional variations in Spain
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Castilian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Regional variations in Spain">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Inside Spain, there are many regional variations of Spanish, which can be divided roughly into four major dialectal areas:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Northern Spanish (northern coast, Ebro and Duero valleys, upper Tajo and upper Júcar valleys). The dialects in this area are sometimes called
        <i>
         Castilian Spanish
        </i>
        (only in English), but in fact it excludes quite a large portion in the
        <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
         historical region of Castile
        </a>
        and includes areas not in it.
       </li>
       <li>
        Transitional area between North and South (
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
         Murcia
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrid_(autonomous_community)" title="Madrid (autonomous community)">
         Madrid
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/La_Mancha" title="La Mancha">
         La Mancha
        </a>
        ). The dialects in this area have traits which are often popularly associated with Andalusia, such as implosive s-aspiration (systematic or conditioned by context).
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
         Murcia
        </a>
        are often lumped into a Southern variety with Andalusian rather than being considered a part of the transitional area, since Southern traits are more pervasive there.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian Spanish
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
         Canarian Spanish
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Difference_with_Latin_American_Spanish">
        Difference with Latin American Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Castilian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Difference with Latin American Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects_and_varieties" title="Spanish dialects and varieties">
        Spanish dialects and varieties
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       (Royal Spanish Academy or RAE) defines Castilian Spanish as a
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard language
       </a>
       , and many speakers accept RAE as the governing body of the language.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, some traits of the Spanish spoken in Spain are exclusive to that country, and for this reason, courses of Spanish as a
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       often neglect them, preferring
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       whilst
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_Spanish" title="European Spanish">
        European Spanish
       </a>
       is taught in Europe.
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">
        Spanish grammar
       </a>
       and to a lesser extent pronunciation can vary sometimes between variants.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most striking difference between dialects in central and northern Spain and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_Spanish" title="Latin American Spanish">
        Latin American Spanish
       </a>
       is
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distinci%C3%B3n" title="Distinción">
         distinción
        </a>
       </i>
       (distinction), that is, the pronunciation of the letter
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       before all vowels, and of
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       only for
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       , as a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
        voiceless dental fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       , English
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pronunciation_of_English_th" title="Pronunciation of English th">
         th
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       ing. Thus, in most variations of Spanish from Spain,
       <i>
        cinco
       </i>
       (five) is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈθiŋko/
       </span>
       as opposed to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈsiŋko/
       </span>
       in Latin American Spanish, and similarly for
       <i>
        zapato
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cerdo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        zorro
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Zurbarán
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Distinción
       </i>
       also occurs in the area around
       <a href="/wiki/Cusco_Region" title="Cusco Region">
        Cusco
       </a>
       , Peru, where
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
         θ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       survives in a few words like the numbers
       <i>
        doce
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        trece
       </i>
       and, with some people, in the verb
       <i>
        decir
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Additionally, all Latin-American dialects drop the familiar (that is, informal)
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       verb form for the second person plural, using
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       in all contexts. In most of Spain,
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is used only in a formal context. Some other minor differences are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The widespread use of
        <i>
         le
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         lo
        </i>
        as the masculine direct object pronoun, especially referring to people. This morphological variation, known as
        <i>
         leísmo
        </i>
        is typical of a strip of land in central Spain which includes Madrid, and recently it has spread to other regions.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the past, the sounds for "y" and "ll" were phonologically different in most
        <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
         European Spanish
        </a>
        subvarieties, compared with only a few
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_Spanish" title="Latin American Spanish">
         dialects in Latin America
        </a>
        , but that difference is now disappearing in all
        <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
         Peninsular Spanish
        </a>
        dialects, including the standard (that is, Castilian Spanish based on the
        <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
         Madrid
        </a>
        dialect). A distinct phoneme for "ll" is still heard in the speech of older speakers in rural areas throughout Spain, but most Spanish-speaking adults and youngsters merge "ll" and "y". In Latin America, "ll" remains different from "y" in traditional dialects along the Andes range, especially in the Peruvian highlands, all of Bolivia and also in Paraguay. In the
        <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
         Philippines
        </a>
        , speakers of
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
         Spanish
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Filipino_language" title="Filipino language">
         Filipino
        </a>
        employ the
        <a href="/wiki/Distinctive_feature" title="Distinctive feature">
         distinction
        </a>
        between "ll"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        and "y"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In most of Latin America,
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        is used more often than in mainland Spain; however, in Latin America, this tendency is less common among young people, especially in Caribbean dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Castilian Spanish, the letter
        <i>
         j
        </i>
        as well as the letter
        <i>
         g
        </i>
        before the letters
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        are pronounced as a stronger velar fricative
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        and very often the friction is uvular
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_uvular_fricative" title="Voiceless uvular fricative">
          χ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , while in Latin America they are generally guttural as well, but not as strong and the uvular realizations of European Spanish are not reported. In the
        <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
         Caribbean
        </a>
        , Colombia, Venezuela, other parts of Latin America, the Canary Islands, Extremadura and most of western
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusia
        </a>
        , as well as in the Philippines, it is pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Characteristic of Spanish from Spain (except from Andalusia and the Canary Islands) is the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_retracted_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant">
         voiceless alveolar retracted sibilant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s̺]
        </span>
        , also called apico-alveolar or grave, which is often perceived as intermediate between a laminal/dental
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        . This sound is prevalent also in Colombian
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paisa_region" title="Paisa region">
         Paisa region
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Andean_Spanish" title="Andean Spanish">
         Andean Spanish
        </a>
        dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         Debuccalization
        </a>
        of syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
          x
         </a>
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        , or dropping it entirely, so that
        <i>
         está
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
          [esˈta]
         </a>
        </span>
        ("s/he is") sounds like
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ehˈta]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [eˈta]
        </span>
        , in different degrees and contexts, occurs in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castile%E2%80%93La_Mancha" title="Castile–La Mancha">
         Castile–La Mancha
        </a>
        (except North-East) and
        <a href="/wiki/Community_of_Madrid" title="Community of Madrid">
         Madrid
        </a>
        ; this is most common in southern Spain (
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
         Andalusia
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">
         Murcia
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
         Canary Islands
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
         Ceuta
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
         Melilla
        </a>
        ), and some parts of Latin America.
       </li>
       <li>
        Words containing these three letters together -atl- are pronounced in a different way in Castilian Spanish as compared to Mexican Spanish. In Spain, words like
        <i>
         Atlántico
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         atleta
        </i>
        are pronounced according to the syllabication
        <i>
         At-lán-ti-co
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         at-le-ta
        </i>
        . Instead, in Mexico, the pronunciation follows the syllabication
        <i>
         A-tlán-ti-co
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         a-tle-ta
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
           <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (September 2016)">
            dubious
           </span>
          </a>
          <span class="metadata">
           –
           <a href="/wiki/Talk:Castilian_Spanish#Dubious" title="Talk:Castilian Spanish">
            discuss
           </a>
          </span>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Castilian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The meaning of certain words may differ greatly between both dialects of the language:
       <i>
        carro
       </i>
       refers to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Automobile" title="Automobile">
        car
       </a>
       in some Latin American dialects but to
       <a href="/wiki/Cart" title="Cart">
        cart
       </a>
       in Spain and some Latin American dialects. Sometimes there also appear gender differences:
       <i>
        el PC
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        personal computer
       </i>
       ) in Castilian Spanish and some Latin American Spanish,
       <i>
        la PC
       </i>
       in some Latin American Spanish, due to the widespread use of the gallicism
       <i>
        ordenador
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        l'ordinateur
       </i>
       in French) for computer in Castilian Spanish, which is masculine, instead of the Latin-American-preferred
       <i>
        computadora
       </i>
       , which is feminine, from the English word
       <i>
        computer
       </i>
       (the exceptions being Colombia and Chile, where PC is known as computador, which is masculine).
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, speakers of the second dialect tend to use words and polite-set expressions that, even if recognized by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        RAE
       </a>
       , are not widely used nowadays (some of them are even deemed as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anachronisms" title="Anachronisms">
        anachronisms
       </a>
       ) by speakers of Castilian Spanish. For example,
       <i>
        enojarse
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        enfadarse
       </i>
       are verbs with the same meaning (to become angry),
       <i>
        enojarse
       </i>
       being used much more in the Americas than in Spain, and
       <i>
        enfadarse
       </i>
       more in Spain than in the Americas.
      </p>
