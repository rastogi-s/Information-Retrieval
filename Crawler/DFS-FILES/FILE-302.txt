      <p>
       <b>
        Old Spanish
       </b>
       , also known as
       <b>
        Old Castilian
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         castellano antiguo
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <a class="mw-selflink selflink">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="osp" xml:lang="osp">
         romance castellano
        </span>
       </i>
       <small>
        pronounced
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [roˈmanʦe kasteˈʎano]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) or
       <b>
        Medieval Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español medieval
        </span>
       </i>
       ), originally a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquial" title="Colloquial">
        colloquial
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       spoken in the provinces of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       that provided the root for the early form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       that was spoken on the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       from the 10th century until roughly the beginning of the 15th century, before a consonantal readjustment gave rise to the evolution of modern Spanish. The poem
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cantar_de_Mio_Cid" title="Cantar de Mio Cid">
         Cantar de Mio Cid
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Poem of the Cid
       </i>
       ), published around 1200, remains the best known and most extensive work of literature in Old Spanish.
      </p>

      <p>
       The phonological system of Old Spanish was quite similar to that of other medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sibilants">
        Sibilants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sibilants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Among the consonants, there were seven
       <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
        sibilants
       </a>
       , including three sets of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless" title="Voiceless">
        voiceless
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiced
       </a>
       pairs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
         Voiceless alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant affricate">
          t͡s̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%87" title="Ç">
         ç
        </a>
        ⟩ before ⟨a⟩, ⟨o⟩, ⟨u⟩, and by ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar affricate">
         Voiced alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar sibilant affricate">
          d͡z̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨z⟩
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apicoalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless apicoalveolar fricative">
         Voiceless apicoalveolar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
          s̺
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨s⟩ in word-initial and word-final positions and before and after a consonant, and by ⟨ss⟩ between vowels
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_apicoalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced apicoalveolar fricative">
         Voiced apicoalveolar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced apico-alveolar sibilant">
          z̺
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨s⟩ between vowels and before voiced consonants
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         Voiceless postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨x⟩ (pronounced like the English
        <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
         digraph
        </a>
        ⟨sh⟩)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
         Voiced postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiced postalveolar fricative">
          ʒ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨j⟩, and (often) by ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩ (pronounced like the
        <i>
         si
        </i>
        in English
        <i>
         vi
         <b>
          si
         </b>
         on
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless postalveolar affricate">
         Voiceless postalveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">
          t͡ʃ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        : represented by ⟨ch⟩
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This set of sounds is identical to that found in medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       , and almost the same as the system present in the modern
       <a href="/wiki/Mirandese_language" title="Mirandese language">
        Mirandese language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Modern Spanish system evolved from the Old Spanish one through the following changes:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        The affricates
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar sibilant affricate">
          t͡s̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_sibilant_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar sibilant affricate">
          d͡z̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        were simplified to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laminodental" title="Laminodental">
         laminodental
        </a>
        fricatives
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_fricative#Voiced_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced alveolar fricative">
          z̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        , which remained distinct from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apicoalveolar" title="Apicoalveolar">
         apicoalveolar
        </a>
        sounds
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
          s̺
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiced apico-alveolar sibilant">
          z̺
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        (a distinction also present in
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
         Basque
        </a>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The voiced sibilants then all lost their voicing, merging with the voiceless ones. (Voicing remains before voiced consonants, e.g.
        <i>
         mismo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         desde
        </i>
        , but only
        <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
         allophonically
        </a>
        .)
       </li>
       <li>
        The merged
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
          ʃ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        was retracted to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
          x
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The merged
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        was drawn forward to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_fricative" title="Voiceless dental fricative">
          θ
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        . In
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
         Andalusia
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
         Canary Islands
        </a>
        , however (and as a result, in
        <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
         Latin America
        </a>
        ), the merged
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_apico-alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless apico-alveolar sibilant">
          s̺
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        was instead drawn forward, merging into
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_sibilant" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
          s̻
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Changes 2–4 all occurred in a short period of time, around 1550–1600. The change from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         ʃ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         x
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       is comparable to the same shift occurring in
       <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
        Modern Swedish
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Sj-sound" title="Sj-sound">
        sj-sound
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Old Spanish spelling of the sibilants was identical to modern
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       spelling, which still reflects the medieval language; Spanish was respelled in 1815 to reflect the pronunciation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ort1815_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ort1815-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples:
      </p>

        <i>
         casar
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>
        "bear"
        <abbr title="versus">
         vs.
        </abbr>
        <i>
         oso
        </i>

        <i>

        </i>
        in both cases,
        <abbr title="confer">

        <i>

        </i>

       </li>

        <i>

        <abbr title="versus">

        <i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="b_and_v">
        b and v
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: b and v">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The letters ⟨b⟩ and ⟨v⟩ still had distinct pronunciations; ⟨b⟩ still represented a stop consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       in all positions, while ⟨v⟩ was likely pronounced as a voiced
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabial
       </a>
       fricative or approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
         β
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilabial_approximant" title="Bilabial approximant">
         β̞
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (although word-initially it may have been pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Though I believe this is correct, there are a variety of sources that dispute this. (September 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The use of ⟨b⟩ and ⟨v⟩ in Old Spanish largely corresponded to their use in Modern Portuguese, which still distinguishes the two sounds (with the exception of Galician and some northern Portuguese dialects); the use of two phonemes also occurs in
       <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
        standard Valencian
       </a>
       spoken in eastern Catalonia and some areas in southern Catalonia,
       <a href="/wiki/Balearic_dialect" title="Balearic dialect">
        Balearic dialect
       </a>
       , as well as in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alguerese" title="Alguerese">
        Alguerese
       </a>
       (except standard
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       in eastern Catalonia). When Spanish spelling was changed in 1815, words with ⟨b⟩ and ⟨v⟩ were respelled etymologically in order to match Latin spelling whenever possible.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="f_and_h">
        f and h
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: f and h">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many words now written with an ⟨h⟩ were written with ⟨f⟩ in Old Spanish, although it was likely pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in most positions (but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiceless bilabial fricative">
         ɸ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
         r
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_lateral_approximant" title="Alveolar lateral approximant">
         l
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labio-velar_approximant" title="Voiced labio-velar approximant">
         w
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       and possibly
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant#Palatal" title="Palatal approximant">
         j
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ). The cognates of these words in Portuguese and most other Romance languages have
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       . Other words now spelled with an etymological ⟨h⟩ were spelled without any such consonant in Old Spanish (e.g.
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       , written
       <i>
        aver
       </i>
       in Old Spanish); these words have cognates in other Romance languages without
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         f
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       (e.g. French
       <i>
        avoir
       </i>
       , Italian
       <i>
        avere
       </i>
       , Portuguese
       <i>
        haver
       </i>
       with silent etymological ⟨h⟩).
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples:
      </p>

        </i>

        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>
         falar
        </i>
        )

        </i>

        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

        </i>
        "sickles",
        <i>

        </i>
        "throats/ravines" (Modern Spanish
        <i>
         hoces
        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>

        </i>
        ,
        <i>

        </i>

       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>
        (Modern Spanish
        <i>

        </i>

       </li>

        <i>

        </i>
        (Modern Spanish
        <i>

        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>
         ferir
        </i>
        )

        <i>

        </i>
        (Modern Spanish
        <i>

        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>

        </i>
        )

       <li>
        <i>

        </i>
        (Modern Spanish
        <i>

        </i>
        , Portuguese
        <i>

        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>

       Modern words with ⟨f⟩ before a vowel mostly represent learned or semi-learned borrowings from Latin, e.g.
       <i>

       </i>
       "to smoke" (compare inherited
       <i>
        humo
       </i>
       "smoke"),
       <i>
        satisfacer
       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="ch">
        ch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: ch">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">
         t͡ʃ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       originated in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Palatal_nasal">
        Palatal nasal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Palatal nasal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The palatal nasal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_nasal#Palatal_or_alveolo-palatal" title="Palatal nasal">
         ɲ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       was written ⟨nn⟩ (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate" title="Geminate">
        geminate
       </a>
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         NN
        </span>
       </span>
       being one of the sound's Latin origins), but was often abbreviated to ⟨ñ⟩ following the common
       <a href="/wiki/Scribal_abbreviation" title="Scribal abbreviation">
        scribal shorthand
       </a>
       of replacing an ⟨m⟩ or ⟨n⟩ with a
       <a href="/wiki/Tilde" title="Tilde">
        tilde
       </a>
       above the previous letter. In later times ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%91" title="Ñ">
        ñ
       </a>
       ⟩ was used exclusively, and came to be considered a letter in its own right by Modern Spanish. Also as in modern times, the palatal lateral
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_lateral_approximant" title="Palatal lateral approximant">
         ʎ
        </a>
        /
       </span>
       was indicated with ⟨ll⟩ (again reflecting an origin from a Latin geminate).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spelling">
        Spelling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In common with other European languages before
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       1600, the letters ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/I" title="I">
        i
       </a>
       ⟩ and ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/J" title="J">
        j
       </a>
       ⟩ were not distinguished, nor were ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/U" title="U">
        u
       </a>
       ⟩ and ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/V" title="V">
        v
       </a>
       ⟩. Modern editions of Old Spanish texts usually normalize the spelling to distinguish them as the modern language does.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology_and_syntax">
        Morphology and syntax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Old_Spanish_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology and syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In Old Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">
        perfect
       </a>
       constructions of movement verbs, such as
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       ('(to) go') and
       <i>
        venir
       </i>
       ('(to) come'), were formed using the auxiliary verb
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       ('(to) be'), as in Italian and French. For example,
       <i>
        Las mugieres
        <b>
         son llegadas
        </b>
        a Castiella
       </i>
       vs. Modern Spanish
       <i>
        Las mujeres han llegado a Castilla
       </i>
       ('The women have arrived in Castilla').
      </p>
      <p>
       Possession was expressed with the verb
       <i>
        aver
       </i>
       (Modern Spanish
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       , '(to) have') rather than
       <i>
        tener
       </i>
       . For example:
       <i>
        Pedro ha dos fijas
       </i>
       vs. Modern Spanish
       <i>
        Pedro tiene dos hijas
       </i>
       ('Pedro has two daughters').
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect" title="Perfect">
        perfect
       </a>
       tenses, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       often agreed with the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)#Types_of_object" title="Object (grammar)">
        direct object
       </a>
       . For example,
       <i>
        María
        <b>
         ha cantadas
        </b>
        dos canciones
       </i>
       vs. Modern Spanish
       <i>
        María ha cantado dos canciones
       </i>
       ('María has sung two songs'), yet this was inconsistent even in the earliest texts.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        Personal pronouns
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substantive" title="Substantive">
        substantives
       </a>
       were placed after the verb in any tense or
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        mood
       </a>
       unless a word with stress was present before the verb.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text in the vicinity of this tag needs examples for clarification (January 2016)">
           examples needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The future and conditional tenses were not yet fully
       <a href="/wiki/Grammaticalization" title="Grammaticalization">
        grammaticalised
       </a>
       as inflexions; rather, they were still periphrastic formations of the verb
       <i>
        aver
       </i>
       in the present or imperfect indicative followed by the infinitive of a main verb.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pronouns, therefore, following general placement rules, could be inserted between the main verb and the auxiliary in these periphrastic tenses. Compare this phenomenon with European
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesoclisis" title="Mesoclisis">
        mesoclisis
       </a>
       ):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         E dixo: ― Tornar-
         <b>
          m
         </b>
         -é a Jherusalem.
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fazienda_de_Ultra_Mar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Fazienda de Ultra Mar (page does not exist)">
          Fazienda de Ultra Mar
         </a>
        </i>
        , 194)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Y dijo: ―
         <b>
          Me
         </b>
         tornaré a Jerusalén.
        </i>
        (literal translation into Modern Spanish)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         E disse: ― Tornar-
         <b>
          me
         </b>
         -ei a Jerusalém.
        </i>
        (literal translation into Portuguese)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        And he said: "I will return to Jerusalem." (English translation)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         En pennar
         <b>
          gelo
         </b>
         he por lo que fuere guisado
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cantar_de_mio_Cid" title="Cantar de mio Cid">
          Cantar de mio Cid
         </a>
        </i>
        , 92)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Se lo
         </b>
         empeñaré por lo que sea razonable
        </i>
        (Modern Spanish equivalent)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Penhorar-
         <b>
          lho
         </b>
         -ei por o que for razoável
        </i>
        (Portuguese equivalent)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        I will pawn
        <b>
         it to them
        </b>
        for whatever is reasonable (English translation)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       When there was a stressed word before the verb, the pronouns would go before the verb: e.g.,
       <i>
        non
        <b>
         gelo
        </b>
        empeñar he por lo que fuere guisado
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Generally, an unstressed pronoun and a verb in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sentence" title="Simple sentence">
        simple sentences
       </a>
       combined into one word.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (July 2011)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_sentence_(linguistics)" title="Compound sentence (linguistics)">
        compound sentence
       </a>
       , the pronoun was found in the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dependent_clause" title="Dependent clause">
        clause
       </a>
       .
       <b>
        Example:
       </b>
       <i>
        la manol va besar = la mano le va a besar
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In comparison with the modern language, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_subjunctive" title="Future subjunctive">
        future subjunctive
       </a>
       was in common use (
       <i>
        fuere
       </i>
       in the second example above) whereas today it is generally found only in legal or solemn discourse, and in the spoken language in some dialects, particularly in areas of Venezuela replacing the imperfect subjunctive.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was used similarly to its Modern Portuguese counterpart, in place of the modern present subjunctive in a subordinate clause after
       <i>
        si
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cuando
       </i>
       , etc., when an event in the future is referenced.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Si vos assi lo
         <b>
          fizieredes
         </b>
         e la ventura me
         <b>
          fuere
         </b>
         complida
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Mando al vuestro altar buenas donas e Ricas
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Cantar de mio Cid
        </i>
        , 223–224)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Si vosotros así
         <b>
          hacéis
         </b>
         y la suerte me
         <b>
          favorece
         </b>
         ,
        </i>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Mando a vuestro altar ofrendas buenas y ricas
        </i>
        (modern equivalent)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        If you do so and fortune is favourable toward me,
       </dd>
       <dd>
        I will send to your altar fine and rich offerings (English translation)
       </dd>
      </dl>
