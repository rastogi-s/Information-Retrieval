      <p>
       <b>
        Gascon
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        Occitan:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Occitan" title="Help:IPA/Occitan">
         [ɡasˈku]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        French:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/French" title="Help:IPA/French">
         [ɡaskɔ̃]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a dialect of
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       . It is mostly spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">
        Gascony
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9arn" title="Béarn">
        Béarn
       </a>
       in southwestern France (in parts of the following French
       <i>
        départements
       </i>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es-Atlantiques" title="Pyrénées-Atlantiques">
        Pyrénées-Atlantiques
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hautes-Pyr%C3%A9n%C3%A9es" title="Hautes-Pyrénées">
        Hautes-Pyrénées
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Landes_(department)" title="Landes (department)">
        Landes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gers" title="Gers">
        Gers
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gironde" title="Gironde">
        Gironde
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lot-et-Garonne" title="Lot-et-Garonne">
        Lot-et-Garonne
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haute-Garonne" title="Haute-Garonne">
        Haute-Garonne
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ari%C3%A8ge_(department)" title="Ariège (department)">
        Ariège
       </a>
       ) and in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aran_Valley" title="Aran Valley">
        Aran Valley
       </a>
       of Catalonia.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aranese_language" title="Aranese language">
        Aranese
       </a>
       , a southern Gascon variety, is spoken in Catalonia and has been greatly influenced recently by
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and Spanish. Both these influences tend to differentiate it more and more from the dialects of Gascon spoken in France. Since the 2006 adoption of the new statute of
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       , Aranese is co-official with
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and Spanish in Catalonia (before, this status was valid for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aran_Valley" title="Aran Valley">
        Aran Valley
       </a>
       only).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_classification">
        Linguistic classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Linguistic classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language#Debates_concerning_linguistic_classification_and_orthography" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan: Debates concerning linguistic classification
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_substrate">
        Basque substrate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Basque substrate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The language spoken in Gascony before Roman rule was part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       dialectal continuum (see
       <a href="/wiki/Aquitanian_language" title="Aquitanian language">
        Aquitanian language
       </a>
       ); the fact that the word 'Gascon' comes from the Latin root
       <i>
        vasco
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        vasconem
       </i>
       , which is the same root that gives us 'Basque', implies that the speakers identified themselves at some point as Basque. There is a proven Basque
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">
        substrate
       </a>
       in the development of Gascon.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       This explains some of the major differences that exist between Gascon and other Occitan dialects.
      </p>
      <p>
       A typically Gascon feature that may arise from this substrate is the change from "f" to "h". Where a word originally began with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       in Latin, such as
       <i>
        festa
       </i>
       'party/feast', this sound was weakened to aspirated
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       and then, in some areas, lost altogether; according to the substrate theory, this is due to the Basque dialects' lack of an equivalent
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       . Thus we have Gascon
       <i>
        hèsta
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈhɛsto]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈɛsto]
       </span>
       . A similar change took place in continental Spanish. Thus Latin
       <i>
        facere
       </i>
       gives Spanish
       <i>
        hacer
       </i>
       ([aˈθer]) (or, in some parts of southwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [haˈsɛɾ]
       </span>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although some linguists deny the plausibility of the Basque substrate theory, it is widely assumed that Basque, the "Circumpyrenean" language (as put by Basque linguist Alfonso Irigoyen and defended by
       <a href="/wiki/Koldo_Mitxelena" title="Koldo Mitxelena">
        Koldo Mitxelena
       </a>
       , 1982), is the underlying language spreading around the Pyrenees onto the banks of the Garonne River, maybe as far east as the Mediterranean in Roman times (
       <i>
        niska
       </i>
       cited by
       <a href="/wiki/Joan_Coromines" title="Joan Coromines">
        Joan Coromines
       </a>
       as the name of each nymph taking care of the Roman spa
       <i>
        Arles de Tech
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Roussillon" title="Roussillon">
        Roussillon
       </a>
       , etc.).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Basque gradually eroded across Gascony in the High Middle Ages (Basques from the Val d'Aran cited still circa 1000), with vulgar Latin and Basque interacting and mingling, but eventually with the former replacing the latter north of the east and middle Pyrenees and developing into Gascon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that modern Basque has had lexical influence from Gascon in words like
       <i>
        beira
       </i>
       ("glass"),
       <i>
        polit
       </i>
       ("pretty", Gascon
       <i>
        polit
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        polida
       </i>
       ) to mention but a few. One way for the introduction of Gascon influence into
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       came about through language contact in bordering areas of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Basque_Country" title="Northern Basque Country">
        Northern Basque Country
       </a>
       , acting as adstrate. The other one takes place since the 11th century over the coastal fringe of
       <a href="/wiki/Gipuzkoa" title="Gipuzkoa">
        Gipuzkoa
       </a>
       extending from
       <a href="/wiki/Hondarribia" title="Hondarribia">
        Hondarribia
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/San_Sebasti%C3%A1n" title="San Sebastián">
        San Sebastian
       </a>
       , where Gascon was spoken up to the early 18th century and often used in formal documents until the 16th, with evidence of its occurrence in
       <a href="/wiki/Pasaia" title="Pasaia">
        Pasaia
       </a>
       still in the 1870s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A minor focus of influence was the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Way_of_St_James" title="Way of St James">
        Way of St James
       </a>
       and the establishment of ethnic boroughs in several towns based on the privileges bestowed on the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Occitania" title="Occitania">
         Francs
        </a>
       </i>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
        Navarrese kings
       </a>
       from the 12th until the early 14th century, whereas the variant spoken and used in written records is mainly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       of Toulouse.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage_of_the_language">
        Usage of the language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Usage of the language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="1200" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG/220px-Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG/330px-Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG/440px-Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bayonne_sign_in_French_Basque_Gascon-Occitan.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Trilingual sign in
         <a href="/wiki/Bayonne" title="Bayonne">
          Bayonne
         </a>
         : French, Basque, and Gascon Occitan ("Mayretat", "Sindicat d'initiatibe")
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A poll conducted in Béarn in 1982 indicated that 51% of the population could speak Gascon, 70% understood it, and 85% expressed a favourable opinion regarding the protection of the language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, use of the language has declined dramatically over recent years as a result of the
       <a href="/wiki/Francization#Francization_within_France" title="Francization">
        Francization
       </a>
       taking place during the last centuries, as Gascon is
       <a href="/wiki/Language_shift" title="Language shift">
        rarely transmitted to young generations
       </a>
       any longer (outside of schools, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Calandreta" title="Calandreta">
        Calandretas
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The usual term for Gascon is "patois", a word designating in France a non-official and usually devaluated dialect (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo_language" title="Gallo language">
        Gallo
       </a>
       ) or language (such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       ), regardless of the concerned region.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It is mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9arn" title="Béarn">
        Béarn
       </a>
       that the population uses concurrently the term "Béarnais" to designate its Gascon forms. This is because of the political past of Béarn, which was independent and then part of a sovereign state (the shrinking
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
        Kingdom of Navarre
       </a>
       ) from 1347 to 1620.
      </p>
      <p>
       In fact, there is no unified Béarnais dialect, as the language differs considerably throughout the province. Many of the differences in pronunciation can be divided into east, west, and south (the mountainous regions). For example, an 'a' at the end of words is pronounced "ah" in the west, "o" in the east, and "œ" in the south. Because of Béarn's specific political past, Béarnais has been distinguished from Gascon since the 16th century, not for linguistic reasons.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdialects">
        Subdialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Subdialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Gascon comprises three main linguistic areas:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The 'Garonnais Gascon' used on and next to the river Garonne valley. These regions know the least specific Gascon forms.
       </li>
       <li>
        The 'Southern Gascon' used in the south and in the south-west of the linguistic Gascon zone. The Gascon of these regions is the one with the most distinctive characteristics of Gascon, coming mainly from a supposed Basque substratum.
       </li>
       <li>
        The 'Intermediary Gascon'
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        in an intermediary zone between the two just mentioned.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_other_languages">
        Influences on other languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Gascon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Influences on other languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Probably as a consequence of the linguistic continuum of occidental Romania and the French influence over the
       <a href="/wiki/Marca_Hispanica" title="Marca Hispanica">
        Hispanic Mark
       </a>
       on the medieval times, shared similar and singular features are noticeable between Gascon and other Latin languages on the other side of the frontier:
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       and ultraoccidental Catalan (Catalan of
       <a href="/wiki/La_Franja" title="La Franja">
        La Franja
       </a>
       ) Gascon is also (with Spanish,
       <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">
        Navarro-Aragonese
       </a>
       and French) one of the Romance influences in
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque language
       </a>
       .
      </p>
