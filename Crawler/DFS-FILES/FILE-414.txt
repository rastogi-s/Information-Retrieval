      <p>
       <b>
        Burgos
       </b>
       (

        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>

        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈbuɾɣos]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
          UK
         </a>
         :
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">

           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <small>
         <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
          US
         </a>
         :
        </small>
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/ʊər/: 'our' in 'tour'">
            ʊər
           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">
            ɡ
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">

           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is a city in northern
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and the historic capital of
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
        Castile
       </a>
       . It is situated on the confluence of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arlanz%C3%B3n" title="Arlanzón">
        Arlanzón river
       </a>
       tributaries, at the edge of the Iberian central plateau. It has about 180,000 inhabitants in the actual city and another 20,000 in the metropolitan area. It is the capital of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgos_(province)" title="Burgos (province)">
        province of Burgos
       </a>
       , in the autonomous community of
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
        Castile and León
       </a>
       . Burgos was once the capital of the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Crown of Castile
       </a>
       , and the Burgos Laws or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leyes_de_Burgos" title="Leyes de Burgos">
        Leyes de Burgos
       </a>
       which first governed the behaviour of
       <a href="/wiki/Spaniards" title="Spaniards">
        Spaniards
       </a>
       towards the natives of the Americas were promulgated here in 1512.
      </p>
      <p>
       It has many historic landmarks, of particular importance; the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_of_Burgos" title="Cathedral of Burgos">
        Cathedral of Burgos
       </a>
       (declared
       <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
        World Heritage Site
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       in 1984),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       seat of the Metropolitan
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Burgos" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Burgos">
        Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Burgos
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_la_Real_de_Las_Huelgas" title="Abbey of Santa María la Real de Las Huelgas">
        Las Huelgas Reales Monastery
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Miraflores_Charterhouse" title="Miraflores Charterhouse">
        Miraflores Charterhouse
       </a>
       . A large number of churches, palaces and other buildings from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Medieval_age" title="Medieval age">
        medieval age
       </a>
       remain. The city is surrounded by the Fuentes Blancas and the Paseo de la Isla parks.
      </p>
      <p>
       Castilian nobleman, military leader and diplomat
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Cid_Campeador" title="El Cid Campeador">
        El Cid Campeador
       </a>
       is a significant historical figure in the city, as he was born a couple of kilometres north of Burgos and was raised and educated here.
      </p>
      <p>
       The city forms the principal crossroad of northern Spain along the
       <a href="/wiki/Camino_de_Santiago" title="Camino de Santiago">
        Camino de Santiago
       </a>
       , which runs parallel to the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=River_Arlanz%C3%B3n&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="River Arlanzón (page does not exist)">
        River Arlanzón
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It has a well-developed transportation system, forming the main communication node in northern Spain. In 2008, the international
       <a href="/wiki/Burgos_Airport" title="Burgos Airport">
        Burgos Airport
       </a>
       started to offer commercial flights. Furthermore,
       <a href="/wiki/AVE" title="AVE">
        AVE
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High_speed_train" title="High speed train">
        high speed trains
       </a>
       are planned to start service in the near future, stopping at the newly-built Rosa de Lima train station.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Human_Evolution" title="Museum of Human Evolution">
        Museum of Human Evolution
       </a>
       was opened in 2010, unique in its kind across the world and projected to become one of the top 10 most-visited museums in Spain. The museum features the first Europeans, which lived in this area 800,000 years ago.
      </p>
      <p>
       Burgos was selected as the
       <i>
        Spanish Gastronomy Capital
       </i>
       of 2013.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Toponymy">
        Toponymy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Toponymy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are several possible origins for the toponymy. When the city was founded, the inhabitants of the surrounding country moved into the fortified village, whose
       <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">
        Visigothic
       </a>
       name of Burgos signified consolidated
       <a href="/wiki/Defensive_wall" title="Defensive wall">
        walled
       </a>
       villages
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       (Gothic
       <i>
        baurgs
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The city began to be called
       <i>
        Caput Castellae
       </i>
       ("Cabeza de Castilla" or "Head of Castile").
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Burgos" title="Timeline of Burgos">
        Timeline of Burgos
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Homo_antecessor" title="Homo antecessor">
        Early humans
       </a>
       occupied sites around Burgos as early as 800,000 years ago. When the Romans took possession of what is now the province of Burgos, the site had been a
       <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">
        Celtic
       </a>
       city. In Roman times, it belonged to
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Citerior" title="Hispania Citerior">
        Hispania Citerior
       </a>
       ("Hither Spain") and then to
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania_Tarraconensis" title="Hispania Tarraconensis">
        Hispania Tarraconensis
       </a>
       . In the 5th century, the
       <a href="/wiki/Visigoths" title="Visigoths">
        Visigoths
       </a>
       drove back the
       <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">
        Suebi
       </a>
       , then the Berbers occupied almost all of
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
        Castile
       </a>
       in the 8th century, though only for a very brief period, and left little if any trace of their occupation. King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_III_of_Leon" title="Alfonso III of Leon">
        Alfonso III the Great
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        León
       </a>
       reconquered it about the middle of the 9th century, and built several castles for the defence of Christendom, which was then extended through the reconquest of lost territory. The region came to be known as Castile (Latin
       <i>
        castella
       </i>
       ), i.e. "land of castles".
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1619" data-file-width="3007" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg/200px-Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg/300px-Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg/400px-Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Castillo_de_burgos_exteriores.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Ruins of the
         <a href="/wiki/Castle_of_Burgos" title="Castle of Burgos">
          Castle of Burgos
         </a>
         , of a possible Visigothic origin.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Burgos was founded in 884 as an outpost of this
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        expanding Christian frontier
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when Diego Rodríguez "Porcelos", count of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/County_of_Castile" title="County of Castile">
        Castile
       </a>
       , governed this territory with orders to promote the increase of the Christian population; with this end in view he gathered the inhabitants of the surrounding country into one fortified village. The city began to be called
       <i>
        Caput Castellae
       </i>
       ("Cabeza de Castilla" or "Head of Castile"). The
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=County_of_Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="County of Burgos (page does not exist)">
        county (
        <i>
         condado
        </i>
        ) of Burgos
       </a>
       , subject to the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Leonese_monarchs" title="List of Leonese monarchs">
        Kings of León
       </a>
       , continued to be governed by counts and was gradually extended; one of these counts, Fernán González, established his independence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2304" height="133" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG/200px-Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG/300px-Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG/400px-Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Burgos_monasterio_huelgas_lou.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Santa_Mar%C3%ADa_la_Real_de_Las_Huelgas" title="Abbey of Santa María la Real de Las Huelgas">
          Abbey of Santa María la Real de Las Huelgas
         </a>
         , founded in 1180.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the 11th century, the city became the see of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Diocese_of_Burgos" title="Roman Catholic Diocese of Burgos">
        Roman Catholic Diocese of Burgos
       </a>
       and the capital of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Castile" title="Kingdom of Castile">
        Kingdom of Castile
       </a>
       . Burgos was a major stop for pilgrims on the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Way" title="French Way">
        French Way
       </a>
       the most popular path to
       <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">
        Santiago de Compostela
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a centre of trade between the
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Biscay" title="Bay of Biscay">
        Bay of Biscay
       </a>
       and the south, which attracted an unusually large foreign merchant population, who became part of the city oligarchy and excluded other foreigners.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Throughout the 13th and 14th centuries, Burgos was a favourite seat of the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        kings of León and Castile
       </a>
       and a favoured burial site. The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Consejo" title="Consejo">
         consejo
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_commune" title="Medieval commune">
        urban commune
       </a>
       of Burgos was firmly in the hands of an oligarchic class of
       <i>
        caballeros villanos
       </i>
       , the "peasant knights" of Burgos, who provided the monarchs with a mounted contingent: in 1255 and 1266 royal charters granted relief from taxes to those citizens of Burgos who owned horses and could arm themselves, provided that they continue to live within the city walls.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The merchant oligarchy succeeded the cathedral chapter as the major purchasers of land after 1250; they carried on their mercantile business in common with municipal or royal functions and sent their sons to England and Flanders to gain experience in overseas trade. A few families within the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hermandad" title="Hermandad">
         hermandades
        </a>
       </i>
       or confraternities like the Sarracín and Bonifaz succeeded in monopolising the post of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alcalde" title="Alcalde">
         alcalde
        </a>
       </i>
       , or mayor; a special court, the
       <i>
        alcalde del rey
       </i>
       was first mentioned at Burgos in 1281.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By the reign of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_X" title="Alfonso X">
        Alfonso X
       </a>
       , the exemption of the non-noble knights and religious corporations, combined with exorbitant gifts and grants to monasteries and private individuals, placed great stress on the economic well-being of the realm.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the century following the conquest of
       <a href="/wiki/Seville" title="Seville">
        Seville
       </a>
       (1248) on the Moors, Burgos became a testing ground for royal policies of increasing power against the
       <i>
        consejo
       </i>
       , in part by encouraging the right to appeal from the
       <i>
        consejo
       </i>
       to the king. In 1285,
       <a href="/wiki/Sancho_IV_of_Castile" title="Sancho IV of Castile">
        Sancho IV
       </a>
       added a new body to the
       <i>
        consejo
       </i>
       which came to dominate it: the
       <i>
        jurado
       </i>
       in charge of collecting taxes and overseeing public works; the king reserved the right to select its members. The city perceived that danger to its autonomy came rather from an uncontrolled aristocracy during royal minorities: Burgos joined the
       <i>
        hermandades
       </i>
       of cities that leagued together for mutual protection in 1295 and 1315. In the 14th century, official royal intrusion in city affairs was perceived as a palliative against outbreaks of violence by the large excluded class of smaller merchants and artisans, on whom the tax burden fell. The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alguacil" title="Alguacil">
         alguacil
        </a>
       </i>
       was the royal official instituted to judge disagreements.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 9 June 1345, sweeping aside the city government,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_XI" title="Alfonso XI">
        Alfonso XI
       </a>
       established direct royal rule of Burgos through the
       <i>
        Regimiento
       </i>
       of sixteen appointed men.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1574,
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Gregory_XIII" title="Pope Gregory XIII">
        Pope Gregory XIII
       </a>
       made the bishopric a Metropolitan
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Archdiocese_of_Burgos" title="Roman Catholic Archdiocese of Burgos">
        archbishorpic
       </a>
       , at the request of king
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_II_of_Spain" title="Philip II of Spain">
        Felipe II
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Burgos has been the scene of many wars: with the
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       , the struggles between León and Navarre, and between Castile and
       <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
        Aragon
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_War" title="Peninsular War">
        Peninsular War
       </a>
       against Napoleonic France, the
       <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Burgos" title="Siege of Burgos">
        siege of Burgos
       </a>
       (between 19 September to 21 October) was a scene of a withdrawal for
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">
        Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington
       </a>
       . Again in the 19th-century
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlist" title="Carlist">
        Carlist
       </a>
       civil
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wars_of_the_Spanish_succession" title="Wars of the Spanish succession">
        wars of the Spanish succession
       </a>
       Burgos was the scene of a battle. During the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
        Spanish Civil War
       </a>
       , Burgos was the base of General
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Franco
       </a>
       's rebel Nationalist government.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography_and_climate">
        Geography and climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Geography and climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       At an elevation of 856 metres (2,808 ft), the city of Burgos lies in the transition zone between a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Warm-summer_mediterranean" title="Warm-summer mediterranean">
        Warm-summer Mediterranean climate
       </a>
       (Kŏppen
       <i>
        Csb
       </i>
       ) and an
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_climate" title="Oceanic climate">
        Oceanic climate
       </a>
       (Kŏppen
       <i>
        Cfb
       </i>
       ), with some
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_climate" title="Continental climate">
        continental
       </a>
       influence resulting from its distance from the sea and higher altitude. Burgos' climate features chilly and windy winters, due to altitude and an inland location, which always include snow and temperatures below freezing. Temperature ranges can be extreme and Burgos is drier than Spain's coastal regions, although there is year-round precipitation. Average annual precipitation is 546 mm (21.5 in) and the average annual relative humidity is 72%. In winter, temperatures very often (almost every day) drop below freezing, often reaching temperatures as low as −10 °C (14 °F), and snowfalls are common, while the summer months see average high temperatures of 27.5 °C (81.5 °F). The lowest recorded temperature in Burgos was −21 °C (−6 °F) on 20 January 1885. The highest recorded temperature was 39 °C (102 °F) on 13 August 1987.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sights">
        Main sights
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Main sights">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Burgos is rich in ancient churches and convents. The three most notable are the cathedral, with its chapel of the Condestables de Castilla (Lords Constable of Castile), the monastery of Las Huelgas and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthusian" title="Carthusian">
        Carthusian
       </a>
       monastery of Miraflores. Minor notable churches are San Esteban, San Gil (Sancti Aegidii), San Pedro, San Cosme y San Damián, Santiago (Sancti Jacobi), San Lorenzo and San Lesmes (Adelelmi). The Convento de la Merced, occupied by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">
        Jesuits
       </a>
       , and the Hospital del Rey are also of historic and architectural interest.
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the other interesting architectural structures, in the walls of the city are the famous gateway of Santa María, erected for the first entrance of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emperor_Charles_V" title="Emperor Charles V">
        Emperor Charles V
       </a>
       , and the arch of Fernán González.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gothic_Cathedral">
        Gothic Cathedral
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Gothic Cathedral">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Burgos_Cathedral" title="Burgos Cathedral">
        Burgos Cathedral
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Construction on Burgos'
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">
        Gothic
       </a>
       Cathedral began in 1221 and spanned mainly from the 13th to 15th centuries. It has been declared a
       <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
        UNESCO World Heritage Site
       </a>
       . The west front is flanked by towers terminating in octagonal spires covered with open stonework traceries. The middle section, which serves as an entrance, has three alabaster pilasters, the intercolumnar spaces bearing panel-pictures representing the martyrdom of saints. The façade possessed ornate and fantastic surface decoration.
      </p>
      <p>
       The octagonal chapel of the Condestable, in florid, thus highly sculpted,
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_architecture" title="Gothic architecture">
        Gothic
       </a>
       design, has a roof finished with balustraded turrets, needle-pointed pinnacles and statues. In the lower portion, coats of arms, shields and crouching lions have been worked into the ensemble. The exterior of the sacristy is decorated with carved traceries, figures of angels and armoured knights. The elaborate
       <a href="/wiki/Tabernacle" title="Tabernacle">
        tabernacle
       </a>
       is composed of two octagonal sections in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corinthian_style" title="Corinthian style">
        Corinthian style
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Monasterio_de_las_Huelgas">
        Monasterio de las Huelgas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Monasterio de las Huelgas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Huelgas" title="Las Huelgas">
        Las Huelgas
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Monasterio de las Huelgas Reales
       </i>
       (Monastery of the Royal Retreats) on the outskirts of the city, was founded in 1180 by king
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfonso_VIII" title="Alfonso VIII">
        Alfonso VIII
       </a>
       , and was begun in a pre-Gothic style, although almost every style has been introduced over many additions. The remarkable cloisters have been described as "unrivalled for beauty both of detail and design, and perhaps unsurpassed by anything in its age and style in any part of Europe" (1911 Encyclopædia Britannica). One cloister has semicircular arches with delicate and varied columns; the other has an ogival style of early Gothic. The interior of the church has enormous columns supporting its magnificent vault; the entrance is modern. This convent historically benefited from extraordinary privileges granted to its abbess by kings and popes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Miraflores_Charterhouse">
        Miraflores Charterhouse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Miraflores Charterhouse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Miraflores_Charterhouse" title="Miraflores Charterhouse">
        Miraflores Charterhouse
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carthusian" title="Carthusian">
        Carthusian
       </a>
       monastery, Miraflores Charterhouse (
       <i>
        Cartuja de Miraflores
       </i>
       ) is situated about four kilometres from the historic city center. Among the treasures of the Charterhouse are the wooden statue of
       <a href="/wiki/Bruno_of_Cologne" title="Bruno of Cologne">
        St. Bruno
       </a>
       , the wooden choir stalls in the church and the mausoleum of King
       <a href="/wiki/John_II_of_Castile" title="John II of Castile">
        Juan II
       </a>
       and of his queen,
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_of_Portugal,_Queen_of_Castile" title="Isabella of Portugal, Queen of Castile">
        Isabella of Portugal
       </a>
       , constructed of marble and with their
       <a href="/wiki/Alabaster" title="Alabaster">
        alabaster
       </a>
       recumbent effigies. Around the top frieze are statues of angels in miniature. The French soldiers in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_War_of_Independence" title="Spanish War of Independence">
        Spanish War of Independence
       </a>
       (1814) mutilated this beautiful work, cutting off some of the heads and carrying them away to France. King Juan II's daughters by his first wife, heiresses Princessses
       <a href="/wiki/Catherine,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Catherine, Princess of Asturias">
        Catherine
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Eleanor,_Princess_of_Asturias" title="Eleanor, Princess of Asturias">

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principality_of_Asturias" title="Principality of Asturias">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_of_Human_Evolution">
        Museum of Human Evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Museum of Human Evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Museum of Human Evolution was inaugurated on 13 July 2010. Its foundation is based on the archeological site of Atapuerca located 20 km (12 mi) east of Burgos. The Atapuerca site has been designated a UNESCO
       <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
        World Heritage Site
       </a>
       . It contains several caves, where fossils and stone tools of the earliest known
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hominid" title="Hominid">
        hominids
       </a>
       in West Europe have been found, near
       <a href="/wiki/Atapuerca_Mountains" title="Atapuerca Mountains">
        Atapuerca Mountains
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The museum also allows the visitor to travel to the archaeological site and biological park.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Museum_of_Burgos">
        Museum of Burgos
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Museum of Burgos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It summarizes the history of the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Burgos" title="Province of Burgos">
        province of Burgos
       </a>
       . It has important objects and documents from all the ages, starting from Atapuerca, passing to the Romans and Iberians, and finishing in the contemporary period.
      </p>
      <p>
       The museum is located in a renascence palace, the House of Íñigo Angulo, which has a main patio that structures the museum.
      </p>

        Universities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      </h3>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        University of Burgos
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Burgos" title="University of Burgos">
        University of Burgos
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Often abbreviated 'UBU', the University of Burgos is a public university with about 10,000 students studying over 30 different undergraduate degrees, over 20 PhD Programmes, as well as several Official Masters and other graduate courses. It was established in 1994 when it was divided from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Valladolid" title="University of Valladolid">
        University of Valladolid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       UBU cooperates with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Erasmus_programme" title="Erasmus programme">
        Erasmus Project
       </a>
       , a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/European_union" title="European union">
        European Union (EU)
       </a>
       student-exchange programme, and each semester hosts students from various countries across the 15-state European Union, as well as Iceland, Lichtenstein, Norway, Malta and associated countries in Eastern Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additionally, university students from various regions around the world including China, Latin and North America are able to participate in exchange programmes to study abroad at the University of Burgos. In total, the University of Burgos has education agreements with over 100 international academic institutions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Most of the parks in the city of Burgos can be found along the river Arlanzón. Indeed, the banks of the river itself constitute a green corridor along the city. In total, there are nearly 3 square miles (1,878 acres) of parks in Burgos and one tree for every 3 inhabitants.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Parque del Castillo
        </b>
        (or the Castle Park) is one of the lungs of the city. The city grew from the southern foot of the hill of the castle. Gradually, some of the upper neighborhoods began disappearing. After the destruction of the castle, military installations were established there. In the 1950s, the engineer Jaquotot Mariano, who later became mayor of the city, decided to leave the hill completely bare. Besides the ruins of the castle, this park contains the
        <i>
         Bird Conservation Center
        </i>
        . It also has play areas for children and exercise. Also, there is a terraced area with gardens and an ornamental fountain. The highlight of the park is the viewpoint from which you get a great view of the city, especially the cathedral. A metal plaque on the rail at the vista point helps to identify the most characteristic places of the city. The park contains the unique view of the city of Burgos from the viewpoint of the Castle.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language">
        Language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Language">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , also called Castilian, originated in
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_(historical_region)" title="Castile (historical region)">
        Castile
       </a>
       and spread throughout the Crown of Castile's possessions following the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        reconquista
       </a>
       . It was eventually dispersed throughout the Spanish empire following the conquest and colonization of the Americas, and Spain after the union of the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">
        Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       's kingdoms being co-spoken with other languages. Of the many different variations and dialects found throughout the world, it's argued that the purest Spanish can be found in the region of Burgos due to the fact that the language first developed here in the 10th-century.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish language can be traced back to the monastery of Valpuesta located 100 km (62 mi) north-east of the city of Burgos. The
       <a href="/wiki/Cartularies_of_Valpuesta" title="Cartularies of Valpuesta">
        Valpuesta cartularies
       </a>
       are significant in the history of the Spanish language, and their status as manuscripts containing the earliest words written in Spanish has been officially recognised. The first utterings of Spanish continued in the “Silos Glosses”, recorded in the Burgos monastery of Santo Domingo de Silos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Burgos province is famous in gastronomy for:
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Queso de Burgos
       </b>
       , a white cheese which is soft and unctuous (because it is made with whey). Although originally made with sheep's milk, now cow's milk or mixtures are more common. Each
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Comarca" title="Comarca">
         comarca
        </a>
       </i>
       (rural district) produces a minor variation, and the major dairies produce an industrial product that is acceptable for people with sensitive digestion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Burgos is blessed with a moderate climate and this fresh cheese was able to be conserved there without the need for curing of more than 10 days. With the improvement of aseptic industrial production processes this can be extended to about 30 days at a cool 6 °C. Its production reaches 35,000 tons annually.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Morcilla de Burgos
       </b>
       , a pig's-blood sausage (
       <a href="/wiki/Black_pudding" title="Black pudding">
        black pudding
       </a>
       ), is a staple country food famous across the Iberian peninsula. Spiced with onions and herbs its most noticeable content is rice (often mistaken for fat) which makes it one of the lightest and healthiest products of its kind. Oral tradition says that it must be "salty, smooth and piquant" (see Spanish pages
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos_(desambiguaci%C3%B3n)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Burgos (desambiguación) (page does not exist)">
        Burgos (desambiguación)
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgos_(desambiguaci%C3%B3n)" title="es:Burgos (desambiguación)">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       for details). As with the Queso de Burgos, several comarcas or towns in the province (
       <a href="/wiki/Carde%C3%B1adijo" title="Cardeñadijo">
        Cardeñadijo
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sotopalacios&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sotopalacios (page does not exist)">
        Sotopalacios
       </a>
       , Aranda de Duero,
       <a href="/wiki/Briviesca" title="Briviesca">
        Briviesca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chalco_de_D%C3%ADaz_Covarrubias" title="Chalco de Díaz Covarrubias">
        Covarrubias
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villarcayo" title="Villarcayo">
        Villarcayo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Trespaderne" title="Trespaderne">
        Trespaderne
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_de_Ebro" title="Miranda de Ebro">
        Miranda de Ebro
       </a>
       ...) made their own morcillas, with minor variations between them.
      </p>
      <p>
       Even though Burgos is not on a D.O. wine is a fundamental piece in local gastronomy thanks to nearby wine cellars from Ribera de Duero, Rioja and
       <a href="/wiki/Arlanza_(comarca)" title="Arlanza (comarca)">

       D.O.

       <span class="mw-editsection">

       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HighSpeedSpain.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1646" data-file-width="2130" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/HighSpeedSpain.svg/220px-HighSpeedSpain.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/HighSpeedSpain.svg/330px-HighSpeedSpain.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/HighSpeedSpain.svg/440px-HighSpeedSpain.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>

      <p>
       The city is considered a first-class rail route through which one rail lines circulate, operated by
       <a href="/wiki/Renfe_Operadora" title="Renfe Operadora">
        Renfe
       </a>
       : Madrid-Irun. The faster trains use the AVE line between Madrid and Valladolid.
      </p>
      <p>
       2010 marked 150 years since the arrival of the first railway to the city; the first train was present on October 25, 1860. Madrid was joined with the French border and this was considered the main route of railway communication between Spain and Europe. Located at the halfway point, Burgos soon become an important hub of communications between the centre and the north.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since December 2007, the city has been connected to the main provincial capitals by high-speed service
       <a href="/wiki/Alvia" title="Alvia">
        Alvia
       </a>
       . It also provides other services, middle and long distance, which connect with the main population centres of the country. 18 long distance trains and 12 middle distance trains circulate through the station each day, which results in around 330,000 yearly users.
      </p>
      <p>
       The new railway station was opened on 12 December 2008 under the name Burgos-Rosa de Lima, belonging to ADIF. It is located in the neighbourhood of Villímar, northeast of the city, and has been adapted for the future arrival of the
       <a href="/wiki/AVE" title="AVE">
        AVE
       </a>
       . Bus 25 will take you direct to the city centre.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2017,
       <a href="/wiki/AVE" title="AVE">
        AVE
       </a>
       high speed trains will serve the city, connecting it to cities such as
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       in only 84 minutes, or
       <a href="/wiki/Bilbao" title="Bilbao">
        Bilbao
       </a>
       in 70 minutes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trams">
        Trams
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Trams">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A project to implement a tram for the city has been planned, which would run the length of the Boulevard, officially called the Avenida de Valencia. It would consist of a line of about 12 km (7 mi) long and will originate from the Burgos-Rosa de Lima railway station and end at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Burgos" title="University of Burgos">
        University of Burgos
       </a>
       . Its cost is estimated between 80 and 120 million euros.
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to the economic crisis and real estate, the project has been postponed temporarily until further funding becomes available. In February 2010 work began on the development of the Boulevard, which reserves a lane for public transport: first buses, and eventually the tram.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bicycle">
        Bicycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Bicycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The city has its own public bicycle rental system, called
       <i>
        Bicibur
       </i>
       . It has been designed by a local company and has more than 20 points of distribution throughout the city. A network of over 100 km (62 mi) of bicycle lanes net the city making it the 6th largest bicycle lane network in Spain and the first in kilometres per resident.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sustainable_mobility">
        Sustainable mobility
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Burgos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Sustainable mobility">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Burgos has undertaken many efforts concerning sustainable mobility, and it won the CiViTAs City of the Year in 2007, the most important Award at the European level and the Energy Globe Award in 2008 (the sustainability Nobel awards) in the Air Category for the same reason. The city is the leader among Spanish cities in terms of sustainable mobility with big efforts for a city centre free of cars, a free bicycle loan system and 100% clean public transport. Burgos now leads the CiViNET Network of cities concerned with sustainable mobility in Spain and Portugal.
      </p>
