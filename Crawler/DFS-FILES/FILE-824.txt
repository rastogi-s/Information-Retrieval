   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Canarian Spanish
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Canarian Spanish
       </b>
       (Spanish:
       <i>
        español de Canarias
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        español canario
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        habla canaria
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        isleño
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dialecto canario
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        vernacular canario
       </i>
       ) is a variant of standard
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canarian_people" title="Canarian people">
        Canarian people
       </a>
       . The variant is similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       variety spoken in Western
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       and (especially) to
       <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Spanish" title="Caribbean Spanish">
        Caribbean Spanish
       </a>
       and other
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        Hispanic American Spanish vernaculars
       </a>
       because of Canarian emigration to the Caribbean and Hispanic America over the years.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Canarian Spanish, therefore, heavily influenced the development of Caribbean Spanish and other Latin American Spanish vernaculars. Hispanic America and the Spanish-speaking Caribbean islands were originally largely settled by colonists from the Canary Islands and Andalusia so the dialects of the region, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard language
       </a>
       , were already quite close to Canarian and Andalusian speech. In the Caribbean, Canarian speech patterns were never regarded as either foreign or very different from the local accent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The incorporation of the Canary Islands into the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Castile" title="Crown of Castile">
        Crown of Castile
       </a>
       began with
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_III_of_Castile" title="Henry III of Castile">
        Henry III
       </a>
       (1402) and was completed under the
       <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Monarchs" title="Catholic Monarchs">
        Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       . The expeditions for their conquest started off mainly from ports of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       , which is why the Andalusians predominated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canaries
       </a>
       . There was also an important colonising contingent from
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       in the early
       <a href="/wiki/Conquest_(military)" title="Conquest (military)">
        conquest
       </a>
       of the Canaries, along with the Andalusians and the Castilians from
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spain" title="Peninsular Spain">
        mainland Spain
       </a>
       . In earlier times, Portuguese settled alongside the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_people" title="Spanish people">
        Spanish
       </a>
       in the north of
       <a href="/wiki/Gran_Canaria" title="Gran Canaria">
        Gran Canaria
       </a>
       , but they died off or were absorbed by the Spanish. The population that inhabited the islands before the conquest, the
       <a href="/wiki/Guanches" title="Guanches">
        Guanches
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       spoke a variety of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">
        Berber
       </a>
       (also called Amazigh) dialects. After the conquest, the indigenous
       <a href="/wiki/Guanche_language" title="Guanche language">
        Guanche language
       </a>
       was rapidly and almost completely eradicated in the archipelago. Only some names of plants and animals, terms related to
       <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">
        cattle
       </a>
       ranching and numerous island placenames survive.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Their geographic situation made the Canary Islands receive much outside influence, causing drastic cultural changes, including linguistic ones. As a result of heavy Canarian emigration to the Caribbean, particularly during colonial times, Caribbean Spanish is strikingly similar to Canarian Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canarian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        As is the case with most varieties of Spanish outside Mainland Spain, the preterite is generally used instead of the perfect. For example,
        <i>
         hoy visité a Juan
        </i>
        ('today I visited John') instead of
        <i>
         hoy he visitado a Juan
        </i>
        ('today I have visited John').
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As is the case with most varieties of Spanish outside of central and northern Spain, Canarians use
        <i>
         ustedes
        </i>
        for all second person plurals. Thus, instead of saying
        <i>
         vosotros estáis
        </i>
        , they say
        <i>
         ustedes están
        </i>
        . Only in few areas of the islands of El Hierro, La Palma and La Gomera the pronoun
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        is decreasingly used, generally only by some older speakers. In La Gomera and some parts of La Palma,
        <i>
         ustedes vos vais
        </i>
        is used. Archaic forms like
        <i>
         vaivos
        </i>
        are still used in some parts.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        As is the case with most varieties of Spanish outside of mainland Spain, in some diminutives, syllables are suppressed. Example:
        <i>
         cochito
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         cochecito
        </i>
        for small car, or
        <i>
         florita
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         florecita
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Disappearance of
        <i>
         de
        </i>
        ("of"), in certain expressions, as is the case with many varieties of Spanish outside mainland Spain:
        <i>
         casa Marta
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         casa de Marta
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         gofio millo
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         gofio de millo
        </i>
        , etc.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">
        Pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canarian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The most distinctive non-mainland (and Andalusian) Spanish characteristic is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        : the merger that consists of pronouncing the letters &lt; s &gt; and &lt;z&gt; or "soft" &lt;c&gt; alike:
        <i>
         caza
        </i>
        ('hunt') is pronounced exactly like
        <i>
         casa
        </i>
        ('house').
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The feature is common to most parts of the Spanish-speaking world outside of the northern three quarters of Spain (Castile and the surrounding provinces that have adopted a similar way of speaking).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        debuccalization. The phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is debuccalized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        in coda position, as is common in Andalusia, Extremadura, and Murcia, as well as many parts of Spanish America.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        (spelled as &lt;j&gt;, or as &lt;g&gt; before &lt;e&gt; or &lt; i &gt;) is usually aspirated (pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        ), as is common in Andalusia (especially the western part), as well as in many parts of
        <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
         Hispanic America
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        is realized as
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
         velar
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Canarian_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Canarian vocabulary has a notable influence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Guanche_language" title="Guanche language">
        Guanche language
       </a>
       , especially in the
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponymy
       </a>
       . In addition, many Canarian names come from the Guanche language, such as
       <i>
        Gara
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Acerina
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Beneharo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Jonay
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Tanausú
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Chaxiraxi
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Ayoze
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Yaiza
       </i>
       .
      </p>
