      <p>
       A (
       <b>
        geographical
       </b>
       )
       <b>
        meridian
       </b>
       (or
       <b>
        line of longitude
       </b>
       ) is the half of an imaginary
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       on the Earth's surface, terminated by the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       , connecting points of equal
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . The position of a point along the meridian is given by its
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       indicating how many degrees north or south of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       the point is. Each meridian is perpendicular to all
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        circles of latitude
       </a>
       . Each is also the same length, being half of a great circle on the Earth's surface and therefore measuring 20,003.93 km (12,429.9 miles).
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meridian_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term "meridian" comes from the Latin
       <i>
        meridies
       </i>
       , meaning "midday"; the sun crosses a given meridian midway between the times of sunrise and sunset on that meridian. The same Latin stem gives rise to the terms
       <a href="/wiki/12-hour_clock" title="12-hour clock">
        a.m. (ante meridiem) and p.m. (post meridiem)
       </a>
       used to disambiguate hours of the day when utilizing the
       <a href="/wiki/12-hour_clock" title="12-hour clock">
        12-hour clock
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic">
        Geographic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meridian_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prime_meridian.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="450" height="293" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Prime_meridian.jpg/220px-Prime_meridian.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Prime_meridian.jpg/330px-Prime_meridian.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d5/Prime_meridian.jpg/440px-Prime_meridian.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prime_meridian.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The astronomic prime meridian at Greenwich, England. The geodetic prime meridian is actually 102.478 meters east of this point since the adoption of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
          WGS84
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The meridian through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Greenwich_Observatory" title="Royal Greenwich Observatory">
        Greenwich (inside Greenwich Park)
       </a>
       , England, called the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian_(Greenwich)" title="Prime meridian (Greenwich)">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       , was set at zero degrees of longitude, while other meridians were defined by the angle at the center of the earth between where it and the prime meridian cross the equator. As there are 360
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       in a circle, the meridian on the opposite side of the earth from Greenwich, the antimeridian, forms the other half of a circle with the one through Greenwich, and is at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/180%C2%B0_longitude" title="180° longitude">
        180° longitude
       </a>
       near the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Date_Line" title="International Date Line">
        International Date Line
       </a>
       (with land mass and island deviations for boundary reasons). The meridians from West of Greenwich (0°) to the antimeridian (180°) define the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Hemisphere" title="Western Hemisphere">
        Western Hemisphere
       </a>
       and the meridians from East of Greenwich (0°) to the antimeridian (180°) define the
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Hemisphere" title="Eastern Hemisphere">
        Eastern Hemisphere
       </a>
       . Most maps show the lines of longitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       The position of the prime meridian has changed a few times throughout history, mainly due to the transit observatory being built next door to the previous one (to maintain the service to shipping). Such changes had no significant practical effect. Historically, the average error in the determination of longitude was much larger than the change in position. The adoption of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       ("World Geodetic System 84") as the positioning system has moved the
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_geodesy" title="Satellite geodesy">
        geodetic
       </a>
       prime meridian 102.478 metres east of its last
       <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_astronomy" title="Geodetic astronomy">
        astronomic
       </a>
       position (measured at Greenwich). The position of the current geodetic prime meridian is not identified at all by any kind of sign or marking (as the older astronomic position was) in Greenwich, but can be located using a GPS receiver.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic">
        Magnetic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Meridian_(geography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Magnetic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        magnetic meridian
       </b>
       is an equivalent imaginary line connecting the magnetic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_magnetic_pole" title="South magnetic pole">
        south
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_magnetic_pole" title="North magnetic pole">
        north poles
       </a>
       and can be taken as the horizontal
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Component_(vector)" title="Component (vector)">
        component
       </a>
       of magnetic force lines along the surface of the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
          <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (March 2012)">
           dubious
          </span>
         </a>
         <span class="metadata">
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:Meridian_(geography)#Dubious" title="Talk:Meridian (geography)">
           discuss
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Therefore, a compass needle will be parallel to the magnetic meridian. The angle between the magnetic and the true meridian is the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">
        magnetic declination
       </a>
       , which is relevant for navigating with a compass.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
