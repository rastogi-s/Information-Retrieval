      <p>
       <b>
        Amerind
       </b>
       is a hypothetical
       <a href="/wiki/Macrofamily" title="Macrofamily">
        higher-level language family
       </a>
       proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Greenberg" title="Joseph Greenberg">
        Joseph Greenberg
       </a>
       in 1960 and elaborated by his student
       <a href="/wiki/Merritt_Ruhlen" title="Merritt Ruhlen">
        Merritt Ruhlen
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Greenberg proposed that all of the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       belong to one of three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Language_families" title="Language families">
        language families
       </a>
       , the previously established
       <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut_languages" title="Eskimo–Aleut languages">
        Eskimo–Aleut
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Na-Dene_languages" title="Na-Dene languages">
        Na–Dene
       </a>
       , and with everything else—otherwise classified by specialists as belonging to dozens of independent families—as Amerind. Due to a large number of methodological flaws in the 1987 book
       <i>
        Language in the Americas
       </i>
       , the relationships he proposed between these languages have been rejected by the majority of historical linguists as spurious.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1997_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1997-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adelaar_1989_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Adelaar_1989-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berman_1992_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Berman_1992-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chafe_1987_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chafe_1987-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matisoff_1990_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matisoff_1990-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">

      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        Amerind
       </i>
       is also occasionally used to refer broadly to the various
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        indigenous languages of the Americas
       </a>
       without necessarily implying that they are a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genetic_(linguistics)" title="Genetic (linguistics)">
        genealogical
       </a>
       group. To avoid ambiguity, the term
       <b>
        Amerindian
       </b>
       is often used for the latter meaning.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amerind_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The idea that all the languages of the Americas are related goes back to the 19th century when early linguists such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Peter_Stephen_DuPonceau" title="Peter Stephen DuPonceau">
        Peter Stephen DuPonceau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_von_Humboldt" title="Wilhelm von Humboldt">
        Wilhelm von Humboldt
       </a>
       noticed that the languages of the Americas seemed to be very different from the better known European languages, yet seemingly also quite similar to each other. When studies of American Indian languages began in earnest in the early 20th century linguists quickly realized that the indigenous languages were in fact not all that similar, but had a diversity much greater than among the languages of Europe. After a period of uncertainty about whether indigenous languages could be described and investigated by the methods applied to European languages, the first linguists began the daunting task of trying to classify the languages of the Americas by using the
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        comparative method
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the most prolific and gifted linguists of his times was
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">
        Edward Sapir
       </a>
       , who was among the first to apply the comparative method to Native American languages. However, contrary to current practice in historical linguistics, Sapir also often relied on "hunches" and "gut feeling" when proposing new language families. Some of these suggestions have been proven correct while others have not. Sapir entertained the idea that ultimately all languages of the Americas might turn out to be provably related and such a phenomenon as the apparent Pan-American tendency to have first person forms with a prefixed n- was suggestive for this line of thought.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since Sapir's death in 1939, linguists have spent their time researching his proposals; typically, there have been two opposing camps in this endeavor: the so-called "
       <a href="/wiki/Lumpers_and_splitters" title="Lumpers and splitters">
        lumpers
       </a>
       " who usually look towards notions of genetic relationships, and the "splitters" who are widely critical of such proposals and expect successful family relations to be proven by the most rigorous standards of scholarship. Joseph Greenberg worked in the tradition of "lumpers" and following Sapir, was mindful of evidence not generally acceptable to those who hold that only actual
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_reconstruction" title="Linguistic reconstruction">
        linguistic reconstruction
       </a>
       —through the comparative method—can yield reliable proof of genetic relationships between languages. In elaborating his classification of the Amerind languages, Greenberg relied heavily on Sapir's early work on the North American languages and the highly impressionist classification of South American languages by
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Rivet" title="Paul Rivet">
        Paul Rivet
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       The main argument for the genetic unity of most Native American languages is an observed pronominal pattern in many Native American languages that have first person forms with
       <i>
        n-
       </i>
       and second person forms with
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       -. This pattern was first noted by
       <a href="/wiki/Alfredo_Trombetti" title="Alfredo Trombetti">
        Alfredo Trombetti
       </a>
       in 1905. This pattern was also noted by Sapir which caused him to suggest that ultimately all Native American languages would turn out to be related. In a personal letter to A. L. Kroeber he wrote (Sapir 1918):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-notsapi18_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-notsapi18-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <table class="cquote pullquote" role="presentation" style="margin:auto; border-collapse: collapse; border: none; background-color: transparent; width: auto;">
       <tr>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">
         “
        </td>
        <td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">
         <i>
          Getting down to brass tacks, how in the Hell are you going to explain general American n- 'I' except genetically? It's disturbing, I know, but (more) non-committal conservatism is only dodging, after all, isn't it? Great simplifications are in store for us.
         </i>
        </td>
        <td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">
         ”
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The supposed "n/m – I/you" pattern among Native American languages has attracted attention even from those linguists who are normally critical of such proposals.
       <a href="/wiki/Johanna_Nichols" title="Johanna Nichols">
        Johanna Nichols
       </a>
       has investigated the distribution of the languages that have the n/m contrast and found that they are mostly confined to the western coast of the Americas, and that similarly they exist in East Asia and Oceania. This caused her to suggest that they had spread through diffusion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This notion was rejected by Lyle Campbell, who argued that in fact the n/m pattern was not statistically significant in either area compared to the rest of the world. Campbell also showed that several of the languages that have the contrast today did not have it historically and that largely the pattern was consistent with chance resemblances, especially when taking into consideration the statistic prevalence of nasal consonants in all the pronominal systems of the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1997_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1997-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At right is a selection of singular Amerind pronouns from various languages, each of which are from separate well-attested families.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gender">
        Gender
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amerind_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Gender">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ruhlen reconstructed a morphological (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ablaut" title="Ablaut">
        ablaut
       </a>
       ) gender system for proto-Amerind, with masculine kinship terms containing the vowel *i and feminine the vowel *u, that he claims proves Greenberg's reconstruction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is based on Greenberg's *t'a'na 'child', to which Ruhlen adds a masculine derivation *t'i'na 'son, boy' and a feminine *t'u'na 'daughter, girl'.
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike the n-/m- pattern in the pronouns, an intact i/u gender system is not attested across language families, and the consensus is that the pattern is a spurious one.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Amerind_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The consensus among historical linguists specializing in Native American languages is that the Amerind hypothesis is unsupported by valid evidence,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1997_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1997-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mithun_1999_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mithun_1999-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       particularly because the basis for the proposal is
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_comparison" title="Mass comparison">
        mass comparison
       </a>
       , but also because of many other methodological flaws made by Greenberg in the elaboration of the hypothesis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matisoff_1990_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matisoff_1990-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rankin_1992_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rankin_1992-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1988_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1988-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Critics regard this technique as fundamentally flawed, unable to distinguish chance resemblances from those due to a historical relationship among the languages and providing no means of distinguishing resemblances due to common descent from those due to
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        language contact
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In addition, critics have pointed out errors in the citation of data, including erroneous forms, erroneous glosses, unjustified morphological segmentation, attribution to the wrong language, and citation of entirely spurious forms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Adelaar_1989_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Adelaar_1989-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berman_1992_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Berman_1992-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chafe_1987_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chafe_1987-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matisoff_1990_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matisoff_1990-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kimball_1992_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kimball_1992-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poser_1992_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Poser_1992-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rankin_1992_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rankin_1992-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1988_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1988-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A further criticism is that, contrary to normal scholarly practice, no source references are given for the data, which in most cases come from languages for which there is no standard, authoritative source. In addition, Greenberg does not normalize the spelling of the data, so it is impossible without knowing the source of each form to know what the notation represents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poser_1992_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Poser_1992-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Campbell_1988_28-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Campbell_1988-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While sympathetic to the idea of an Amerind language family,
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Swadesh" title="Morris Swadesh">
        Morris Swadesh
       </a>
       was critical of many of Greenberg's subdivisions and believed it was due to an insufficient number of comparisons by Greenberg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
