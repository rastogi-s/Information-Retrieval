      <p>
       <b>
        Columbia University Press
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/University_press" title="University press">
        university press
       </a>
       based in
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York City
       </a>
       , and affiliated with
       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_University" title="Columbia University">
        Columbia University
       </a>
       . It is currently directed by Jennifer Crewe (2014–present) and publishes titles in the
       <a href="/wiki/Humanities" title="Humanities">
        humanities
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sciences" title="Sciences">
        sciences
       </a>
       , including the fields of literary and cultural studies,
       <a href="/wiki/History" title="History">
        history
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">
        social work
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
        sociology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Religion" title="Religion">
        religion
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        film
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/International_studies" title="International studies">
        international studies
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Founded in 1893,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camhi2013_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Camhi2013-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Columbia University Press is notable for publishing reference works, such as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Columbia_Encyclopedia" title="The Columbia Encyclopedia">
         The Columbia Encyclopedia
        </a>
       </i>
       (1935–present),
       <i>
        The Columbia Granger's Index to Poetry
       </i>
       (online as
       <i>
        The Columbia World of Poetry Online
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        The Columbia Gazetteer of the World
       </i>
       (also online) and for publishing music.
      </p>
      <p>
       First among American university presses to publish in electronic formats,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       in 1998 the Press founded an online-only site,
       <i>
        <a class="external text" href="http://ciaonet.org" rel="nofollow">
         Columbia International Affairs Online
        </a>
       </i>
       (CIAO)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        Columbia Earthscape
       </i>
       (in 2009).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2011, Columbia University Press bought UK publisher Wallflower Press.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
