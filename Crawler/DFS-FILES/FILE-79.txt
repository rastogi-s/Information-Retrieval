      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Southern Hemisphere
       </b>
       is the half
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       which is south of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . It contains all or parts of five
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continents
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">
        Australia
       </a>
       , about 90% of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       , the southern third of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , and several southern islands off the continental
       <a href="/wiki/Mainland" title="Mainland">
        mainland
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       ), four
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">
        oceans
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        South Atlantic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Ocean" title="Southern Ocean">
        Southern
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        South Pacific
       </a>
       ) and most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Islands" title="Pacific Islands">
        Pacific Islands
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">
        Oceania
       </a>
       . Its surface is 80.9% water, compared with 60.7% water in the case of the Northern Hemisphere, and it contains 32.7% of Earth's land.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Owing to the tilt of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_rotation" title="Earth's rotation">
        Earth's rotation
       </a>
       relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic plane
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Summer" title="Summer">
        summer
       </a>
       is from December to March and
       <a href="/wiki/Winter" title="Winter">
        winter
       </a>
       is from June to September. September 22 or 23 is the vernal
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       and March 20 or 21 is the autumnal equinox. The
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       is in the middle of the southern hemispherical region.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Hemisphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Southern Hemisphere
       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
        climates
       </a>
       tend to be slightly milder than those at similar latitudes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       , except in the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       which is colder than the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       . This is because the Southern Hemisphere has significantly more ocean and much less land; water heats up and cools down more slowly than land.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="5109" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg/300px-Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg/450px-Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg/600px-Aurora_australis_panorama.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>

      <p>
       In the Southern Hemisphere the sun passes from east to west through the north, although north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_solar_time" title="Mean solar time">
        mean sun
       </a>
       can be directly overhead or due south at midday. The Sun rotating through the north causes an apparent right-left trajectory through the sky unlike the left-right motion of the Sun when seen from the Northern Hemisphere as it passes through the southern sky. Sun-cast shadows turn anticlockwise throughout the day and
       <a href="/wiki/Sundial" title="Sundial">
        sundials
       </a>
       have the hours increasing in the anticlockwise direction. During
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       viewed from a point to the south of the Tropic of Capricorn, the Moon moves from left to right on the disc of the Sun (see, for example, photos with timings of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_November_13,_2012" title="Solar eclipse of November 13, 2012">
        solar eclipse of November 13, 2012
       </a>
       ), while viewed from a point to the north of the Tropic of Cancer (i.e., in the Northern Hemisphere), the Moon moves from right to left during solar eclipses.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cyclones and tropical storms spin clockwise in the Southern Hemisphere (as opposed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticlockwise" title="Anticlockwise">
        anticlockwise
       </a>
       in the Northern Hemisphere) due to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coriolis_effect" title="Coriolis effect">
        Coriolis effect
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">
        temperate zone
       </a>
       , a subsection of the Southern Hemisphere, is nearly all oceanic. This zone includes the southern tip of
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       ; the southern half of
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ; parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       , going south from
       <a href="/wiki/Adelaide" title="Adelaide">
        Adelaide
       </a>
       , and all of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sagittarius_(constellation)" title="Sagittarius (constellation)">
        Sagittarius
       </a>
       constellation that includes the
       <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
        galactic centre
       </a>
       is a southern constellation and this, combined with clearer skies, makes for excellent viewing of the night sky from the Southern Hemisphere with brighter and more numerous stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Forests in the Southern Hemisphere have special features which set them apart from those in the Northern Hemisphere. Both
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       share, for example, unique
       <a href="/wiki/Beech" title="Beech">
        beech
       </a>
       species or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Nothofagus" title="Nothofagus">
         Nothofagus
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       has members of the closely related genera
       <a href="/wiki/Lophozonia" title="Lophozonia">
        Lophozonia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fuscospora" title="Fuscospora">
        Fuscospora
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Eucalyptus" title="Eucalyptus">
        eucalyptus
       </a>
       is native to
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       but is now also planted in
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Africa" title="Southern Africa">
        Southern Africa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       for pulp production and, increasingly,
       <a href="/wiki/Biofuel" title="Biofuel">
        biofuel
       </a>
       uses.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics_and_human_geography">
        Demographics and human geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Southern_Hemisphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Demographics and human geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Approximately 800 million humans live in the Southern Hemisphere, representing only 10–12% of the total global human population of 7.3 billion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Of those 800 million people, 141 million live on the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">
        Java
       </a>
       , the most populous island in the world, and another 200 million live in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       , the largest country by land area in the Southern Hemisphere. The most populous nation in the Southern Hemisphere is
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       , with 261 million people (around 30 million Indonesians live north of the equator on the northern portions of the islands of
       <a href="/wiki/Sumatra" title="Sumatra">
        Sumatra
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">
        Borneo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">
        Sulawesi
       </a>
       while the rest of the population lives in the Southern Hemisphere).
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       is the most spoken language in the Southern Hemisphere,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Javanese_language" title="Javanese language">
        Javanese
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest metropolitan areas in the Southern Hemisphere are
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">
        São Paulo
       </a>
       (21 million people),
       <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">
        Jakarta
       </a>
       (18 million people),
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       (12 million people) and
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro" title="Rio de Janeiro">
        Rio de Janeiro
       </a>
       (11 million people) and
       <a href="/wiki/Kinshasa" title="Kinshasa">
        Kinshasa
       </a>
       (11 million people). The most important financial and commercial centers in the Southern Hemisphere are
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">
        São Paulo
       </a>
       , where the
       <a href="/wiki/B3_(stock_exchange)" title="B3 (stock exchange)">
        Bovespa Index
       </a>
       is headquartered, along with
       <a href="/wiki/Sydney" title="Sydney">
        Sydney
       </a>
       , home to the
       <a href="/wiki/Australian_Securities_Exchange" title="Australian Securities Exchange">
        Australian Securities Exchange
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Johannesburg" title="Johannesburg">
        Johannesburg
       </a>
       , home to the
       <a href="/wiki/JSE_Limited" title="JSE Limited">
        Johannesburg Stock Exchange
       </a>
       and Buenos Aires, headquarters of the
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires_Stock_Exchange" title="Buenos Aires Stock Exchange">
        Buenos Aires Stock Exchange
       </a>
       , the oldest stock market in the Southern Hemisphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of the richest, most advanced nations in the world are found in the Southern Hemisphere, along with some of the poorest and least developed. Among the most developed are
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       , with a nominal GDP per capita of US$51,850 and a
       <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">
        Human Development Index
       </a>
       of 0.939, the second highest in the world as of 2016.
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       is also quite developed, with a nominal GDP per capita of US$38,385 and a
       <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">
        Human Development Index
       </a>
       of 0.915, putting it at #13 in the world in 2016. The least developed nations in the Southern Hemisphere cluster in Africa and Oceania, with
       <a href="/wiki/Burundi" title="Burundi">
        Burundi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">
        Mozambique
       </a>
       at the lowest ends of the
       <a href="/wiki/Human_Development_Index" title="Human Development Index">
        Human Development Index
       </a>
       , at 0.404 (#184 in the world) and 0.418 (#181 in the world) respectively. The nominal GDP per capitas of these two countries don't go above US$550 per capita, a tiny fraction of the incomes enjoyed by Australians and New Zealanders.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most widespread religions in the Southern Hemisphere are
       <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
        Christianity
       </a>
       in South America, southern Africa and Australia/New Zealand, followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islam
       </a>
       in most of the islands of Indonesia and in parts of southeastern Africa, and
       <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </a>
       , which is mostly concentrated on the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Bali" title="Bali">
        Bali
       </a>
       and neighboring islands.
      </p>
      <p>
       The oldest continuously inhabited city in the Southern Hemisphere is
       <a href="/wiki/Bogor" title="Bogor">
        Bogor
       </a>
       , in western
       <a href="/wiki/Java" title="Java">
        Java
       </a>
       , which was founded in 669 CE. Ancient texts from the Hindu kingdoms prevalent in the area definitively record 669 CE as the year when Bogor was founded. However, there is some evidence that
       <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar_City" title="Zanzibar City">
        Zanzibar
       </a>
       , an ancient port with around 200,000 inhabitants on the coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">
        Tanzania
       </a>
       , may be older than Bogor. A Greco-Roman text written between 1 CE and 100 CE, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythrean_Sea" title="Periplus of the Erythrean Sea">
        <i>
         Periplus of the Erythraean Sea
        </i>
       </a>
       , mentioned the island of
       <i>
        Menuthias
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       : Μενουθιάς) as a trading port on the east African coast, which is probably the small island of
       <a href="/wiki/Unguja" title="Unguja">
        Unguja
       </a>
       on which Zanzibar is located. The oldest proven archaeological site in the Southern Hemisphere is
       <a href="/wiki/Sechin_Bajo" title="Sechin Bajo">
        Sechin Bajo
       </a>
       , located on the coast of northern Peru, and dates back to 3600 BCE. Sechin Bajo may also be the oldest site for monumental architecture in the Americas.
      </p>
