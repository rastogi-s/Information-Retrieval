      <p>
       <b>
        Walloon
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
         Walon
        </span>
       </i>
       in Walloon) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       that is spoken as a primary language in large portions (70%) of
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , in some villages of Northern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       (near
       <a href="/wiki/Givet" title="Givet">
        Givet
       </a>
       ) and in the northeast part of
       <a href="/wiki/Wisconsin" title="Wisconsin">
        Wisconsin
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wisconsin_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wisconsin-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       until the mid 20th century and in some parts of Canada
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . It belongs to the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
         langue d'oïl
        </a>
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        language family
       </a>
       , whose most prominent member is the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French language
       </a>
       . The historical background of its formation was the territorial extension since 980 of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principality_of_Li%C3%A8ge" title="Principality of Liège">
        Principality of Liège
       </a>
       to the south and west.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite its rich
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       , beginning anonymously in the 16th century and with well-known authors since 1756, the use of Walloon has decreased markedly since France's annexation of
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       in 1795. This period definitively established French as the language of social promotion, far more than it was before.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">
        World War I
       </a>
       , public schools provided French-speaking education to all children, inducing a denigration of Walloon, especially when accompanied by official orders in 1952 to punish its use in schools. Subsequently, since the middle of the 20th century, generational transmission of the language has decreased, resulting in Walloon almost becoming a
       <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">
        dead language
       </a>
       . Today it is scarcely spoken among younger people. In 1996, the number of people with knowledge of the language was estimated at between 1 and 1.3 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Non-governmental_organization" title="Non-governmental organization">
        associations
       </a>
       , especially
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theatre_company" title="Theatre company">
        theatre companies
       </a>
       , are working to keep the language alive. Formally recognized as a
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_r%C3%A9gionale_endog%C3%A8ne" title="Langue régionale endogène">
          langue régionale endogène
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (regional indigenous language) of Belgium since 1990,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Walloon has also benefited from a continued
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corpus_planning" title="Corpus planning">
        corpus planning
       </a>
       process. The "Feller system" (1900) regularized transcription of the different accents. Since the 1990s, a common
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       was established (the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rifondou_walon" title="wa:Rifondou walon">
        Rifondou walon
       </a>
       ), which allowed large-scale publications, such as the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mwaisse_p%C3%A5dje" title="wa:Mwaisse pådje">
        <i>
         Walloon Wikipedia
        </i>
       </a>
       officially in 2003. In 2004, a Walloon translation of a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Tintin" title="The Adventures of Tintin">
         Tintin
        </a>
       </i>
       comic was released under the name
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Castafiore_Emerald" title="The Castafiore Emerald">
         L'èmerôde d'al Castafiore
        </a>
       </i>
       ; in 2007 an album consisting of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaston_Lagaffe" title="Gaston Lagaffe">
        Gaston Lagaffe
       </a>
       comic strips was published in Walloon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Walloon is more distinct as a language than
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        Belgian French
       </a>
       , which differs from the French spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       only in some minor points of
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronunciation
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Disputed_nature_of_Walloon">
        Disputed nature of Walloon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Disputed nature of Walloon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Plake_Hesta.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="640" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Plake_Hesta.JPG/220px-Plake_Hesta.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Plake_Hesta.JPG/330px-Plake_Hesta.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Plake_Hesta.JPG/440px-Plake_Hesta.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Plake_Hesta.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
           Hèsta
          </span>
         </i>
         , the Walloon name of the city of
         <a href="/wiki/Herstal" title="Herstal">
          Herstal
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Linguists had long classified Walloon as a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       of French, which in turn is a
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
          langue d'oïl
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       . Like French, it descended from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       . Arguing that a French-speaking person could not understand Walloon easily, especially in its eastern forms,
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Feller" title="Jules Feller">
        Jules Feller
       </a>
       (1859–1940) insisted that Walloon had an original "superior unity", which made it a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The phonological divisions of regional languages of southern Belgium were studied by the contemporary linguist E.B. Atwood. He defined the precise geographical repartition of the four chief dialects of Walloon. In addition, he defined them against the dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
        Lorrain
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champenois" title="Champenois">
        Champenois
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Since then, most linguists (among them
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_Remacle" title="Louis Remacle">
        Louis Remacle
       </a>
       ), and gradually also Walloon politicians, regard Walloon as a regional language, the first in importance in
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       . It is the only one to have originated from that part of
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       . The eleventh edition of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">
         Encyclopædia Britannica
        </a>
       </i>
       identified Walloon as the "northern-most Romance language".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">
        Geographic distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Walloon">
        Walloon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Walloon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Walloon is spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Wallonia" title="Wallonia">
        Wallonia
       </a>
       Region in Belgium. In addition, it is spoken in:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        a small part of
        <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
         France
        </a>
        : the
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/botte_de_Givet" title="fr:botte de Givet">
          botte de Givet
         </a>
        </i>
        in northern
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ardennes_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Ardennes (département)">
         Ardennes
        </a>
        , and several villages in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nord_(d%C3%A9partement)" title="Nord (département)">
         Nord
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_France" title="Departments of France">
         <i>
          département
         </i>
        </a>
        , making it one of the regional
        <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
         languages of France
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        a small district of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Door_County" title="Door County">
         Door County, Wisconsin
        </a>
        , US, owing to fairly large-scale immigration there in the 19th century;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Brussels" title="Brussels">
         Brussels
        </a>
        , by some Walloon residents.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although Walloon was widely spoken until the mid-20th century, today only a small proportion of the inhabitants of the region are fluent in the language. Most younger people (those born since the 1970s) know little more than a few
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Idiomatic_expression" title="Idiomatic expression">
        idiomatic expressions
       </a>
       , often
       <a href="/wiki/Profanity" title="Profanity">
        profanities
       </a>
       . The Walloon language is still part of the Walloon heritage; it is one component of Walloon identity.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialects">
        Dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="358" data-file-width="663" height="119" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png/220px-Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png/330px-Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png/440px-Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_of_Wallonia.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Linguistic map of Wallonia
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="744" data-file-width="1052" height="354" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg/500px-Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg/750px-Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg/1000px-Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg.png 2x" width="500"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Wallonie-linguistique-wa.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Main subdivisions of Walloon dialects
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Four dialects of Walloon developed in four distinct zones of Wallonia:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Central, spoken in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Namur_(city)" title="Namur (city)">
         Namur
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Nameur
         </span>
        </i>
        ), the Wallon capital, and the cities of
        <a href="/wiki/Wavre" title="Wavre">
         Wavre
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Åve
         </span>
        </i>
        ) and
        <a href="/wiki/Dinant" title="Dinant">
         Dinant
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        Eastern – in many respects the most conservative and idiosyncratic of the dialects, spoken in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Li%C3%A8ge_(city)" title="Liège (city)">
         Liège
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Lidje
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Verviers" title="Verviers">
         Verviers
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Vervî
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Malmedy" title="Malmedy">
         Malmedy
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Måmdi
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Huy" title="Huy">
         Huy
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Hu
         </span>
        </i>
        ), and
        <a href="/wiki/Waremme" title="Waremme">
         Waremme
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Wareme
         </span>
        </i>
        );
       </li>
       <li>
        Western – the dialect closest to French proper and with a strong
        <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
         Picard
        </a>
        influence, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Charleroi" title="Charleroi">
         Charleroi
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Tchårlerwè
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <a href="/wiki/Nivelles" title="Nivelles">
         Nivelles
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Nivele
         </span>
        </i>
        ), and
        <a href="/wiki/Philippeville" title="Philippeville">
         Philippeville
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Flipvile
         </span>
        </i>
        ); and
       </li>
       <li>
        Southern – close to the
        <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
         Lorrain
        </a>
        and to a lesser extent
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champenois" title="Champenois">
         Champenois
        </a>
        languages, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Bastogne" title="Bastogne">
         Bastogne
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Marche-en-Famenne" title="Marche-en-Famenne">
         Marche-en-Famenne
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Måtche-el-Fåmene
         </span>
        </i>
        ), and
        <a href="/wiki/Neufch%C3%A2teau,_Luxembourg_Province" title="Neufchâteau, Luxembourg Province">
         Neufchâteau
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Li Tchestea
         </span>
        </i>
        ), all in the
        <a href="/wiki/Ardennes" title="Ardennes">
         Ardennes
        </a>
        region.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Despite local
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
        phonetic
       </a>
       differences, there is a regional movement towards the adoption of a common spelling, called the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rifondou_walon" title="wa:Rifondou walon">
        Rifondou walon
       </a>
       . This
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Diasystem" title="Diasystem">
        diasystemic
       </a>
       , reflecting different pronunciations for different readers, a concept inspired by the spelling of
       <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">
        Breton
       </a>
       . The written forms attempt to reconcile current phonetic uses with ancient traditions (notably the reintroduction of
       <i>
        xh
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oi
       </i>
       that were used for writing Walloon until the late 19th century) and the language's own
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       logic.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_regional_languages">
        Other regional languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>

      <p>
       Other regional languages spoken in Wallonia, outside the Walloon domain, are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
         Picard
        </a>
        , in
        <a href="/wiki/Mons" title="Mons">
         Mons
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ath" title="Ath">
         Ath
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Tournai" title="Tournai">
         Tournai
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
         Lorrain
        </a>
        (also called
        <i>
         Gaumais
        </i>
        locally), in
        <a href="/wiki/Virton" title="Virton">
         Virton
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champenois" title="Champenois">
         Champenois
        </a>
        , in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bohan" title="Bohan">
         Bohan
        </a>
        ; and
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luxembourgish_language" title="Luxembourgish language">
         Luxembourgish
        </a>
        , in
        <a href="/wiki/Arlon" title="Arlon">
         Arlon
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Martelange" title="Martelange">
         Martelange
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The Picard, Lorrain and Champenois dialects spoken in Wallonia are sometimes also referred to as "Walloon", which may lead to confusion.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_family">
        Language family
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Language family">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Walloon is distinguished from other languages in the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
         langue d'oïl
        </a>
       </i>
       family both by archaism coming from Latin and by its significant borrowing from Germanic languages, as expressed in its phonetics, its
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       , and its
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       . At the same time, Walloon phonetics are singularly conservative: the language has stayed fairly close to the form it took during the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <ul>
       <li>
        Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ka]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [g + e, i, a]
        </span>
        gave Walloon affricate phonemes spelled "tch" (as in cherry) and "dj" (as in joke):
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          vatche
         </span>
        </i>
        (vs. French
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          vache
         </span>
        </i>
        = cow),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          djambe
         </span>
        </i>
        (
        <abbr title="French">
         Fr.
        </abbr>
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          jambe
         </span>
        </i>
        = leg).
       </li>
       <li>
        Latin
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        persisted in clusters:
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          spene
         </span>
        </i>
        (Fr.
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          épine
         </span>
        </i>
        = thorn, spine),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          fistu
         </span>
        </i>
        (wisp of straw),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          mwaîsse
         </span>
        </i>
        (Fr.
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          maître
         </span>
        </i>
        = master),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          fiesse

        (Fr.
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          fête
         </span>
        </i>
        = party, feast),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          tchestea
         </span>
        </i>
        (Fr.
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          château
         </span>
        </i>
        = castle),…
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Final_obstruent_devoicing" title="Final obstruent devoicing">
         Final obstruent devoicing
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          rodje
         </span>
        </i>
        (red) is pronounced exactly as
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          rotche
         </span>
        </i>
        (rock).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nasal_vowel" title="Nasal vowel">
         Nasal vowels
        </a>
        may be followed by nasal consonants, as in
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          djonne
         </span>
        </i>
        (young),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          crinme
         </span>
        </i>
        (cream),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          mannet
         </span>
        </i>
        (dirty), etc.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Vowel_length" title="Vowel length">
         Vowel length
        </a>
        has a phonological value. It allows to distinguish
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          cu
         </span>
        </i>
        (arse) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          cû
         </span>
        </i>
        (cooked),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          i l' hosse
         </span>
        </i>
        (he cradles her) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          i l' hôsse
         </span>
        </i>
        (he increases it),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          messe
         </span>
        </i>
        (mass) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          mêsse
         </span>
        </i>
        (master), etc.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The plural feminine adjectives before the noun take an unstressed ending "
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          -ès
         </span>
        </i>
        " (except in the Ardenne dialect): compare
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          li djaene foye
         </span>
        </i>
        (the yellow leaf) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          les djaenès foyes
         </span>
        </i>
        (the yellow leaves).
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no gender difference in definite articles and possessives (except in the Ardenne dialect): compare Walloon
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          li vweteure
         </span>
        </i>
        (the car, feminine) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          li cir
         </span>
        </i>
        (the sky, masculine), with French
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          la voiture
         </span>
        </i>
        but
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          le ciel
         </span>
        </i>
        ; Walloon has
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          si coir
         </span>
        </i>
        (his/her body, masculine) and
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          si finiesse
         </span>
        </i>
        (his/her window, feminine) while French has
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          son corps
         </span>
        </i>
        but
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          sa fenêtre
         </span>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Walloon has a few Latin remnants that have disappeared from neighboring Romance languages: compare Walloon
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          dispierter
         </span>
        </i>
        to
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          despertar
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        <i>
         <span lang="ro" xml:lang="ro">
          deștepta
         </span>
        </i>
        (all with the same meaning: "to awaken").
       </li>
       <li>
        The most distinctive feature is its number of borrowings from Germanic languages (Dutch and German dialects): compare Walloon
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          flåwe
         </span>
        </i>
        to today's Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          flauw
         </span>
        </i>
        (weak). Other common borrowings, among hundreds of others, are
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          dringuele
         </span>
        </i>
        (tip; Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          drinkgeld
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          crole
         </span>
        </i>
        (curl; Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          krul
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          spiter
         </span>
        </i>
        (to spatter; same root as the English
        <i>
         to spit
        </i>
        , and
        <i>
         to spew
        </i>
        , or German
        <i>
         <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
          spützen
         </span>
        </i>
        ; Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          spuwen
         </span>
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          li sprewe
         </span>
        </i>
        (the
        <a href="/wiki/Starling" title="Starling">
         starling
        </a>
        ; Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          spreeuw
         </span>
        </i>
        , or German
        <i>
         <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
          Sperling
         </span>
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax">
        Syntax
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Syntax">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The adjective is often placed before the noun: compare Walloon
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          on foirt ome
         </span>
        </i>
        (a strong man) with French
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          un homme fort
         </span>
        </i>
        ;
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          ene blanke måjhon
         </span>
        </i>
        (a white house) and French
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          une maison blanche
         </span>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Borrowing from Germanic languages: the construction
        <i>
         <span lang="wa" xml:lang="wa">
          Cwè çki c'est di ça po ene fleur?
         </span>
        </i>
        (what kind of flower is this?) can be compared word to word to German
        <i>
         <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
          Was ist das für eine Blume?
         </span>
        </i>
        and Dutch
        <i>
         <span lang="nl" xml:lang="nl">
          Wat is dat voor een bloem?
         </span>
        </i>
        , as opposed to Standard French
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          Quelle sorte de fleur est-ce?
         </span>
        </i>
        or (colloquially)
        <i>
         <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
          Quelle sorte de fleur est-ce que c'est?
         </span>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crupet_JPGW.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Crupet_JPGW.jpg/220px-Crupet_JPGW.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Crupet_JPGW.jpg/330px-Crupet_JPGW.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Crupet_JPGW.jpg/440px-Crupet_JPGW.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crupet_JPGW.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An
         <a href="/wiki/Inn" title="Inn">
          auberge
         </a>
         's sign in
         <a href="/wiki/Crupet" title="Crupet">
          Crupet
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From a linguistic point of view,
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_Remacle" title="Louis Remacle">
        Louis Remacle
       </a>
       has shown that a good number of the developments that we now consider typical of Walloon appeared between the 8th and 12th centuries. Walloon "had a clearly defined identity from the beginning of the 13th century". In any case, linguistic
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       texts from the time do not mention the language, although they mention others in the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Langue_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langue d'oïl">
         langue d'oïl
        </a>
       </i>
       family, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
        Lorrain
       </a>
       . During the 15th century, scribes in the region called the language "Roman" when they needed to distinguish it. It is not until the beginning of the 16th century that first occurrence of the word "Walloon" appeared in the current linguistic sense. In 1510 or 1511,
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Lemaire_de_Belges" title="Jean Lemaire de Belges">
        Jean Lemaire de Belges
       </a>
       made the connection between
       <i>
        <span lang="frm" xml:lang="frm">
         Rommand
        </span>
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        <span lang="frm" xml:lang="frm">
         Vualon
        </span>
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        <i>
         <span lang="frm" xml:lang="frm">
          Et ceux cy [les habitants de Nivelles] parlent le vieil langage Gallique que nous appellons Vualon ou Rommand (…). Et de ladite ancienne langue Vualonne, ou Rommande, nous usons en nostre Gaule Belgique: Cestadire en Haynau, Cambresis, Artois, Namur, Liege, Lorraine, Ardenne et le Rommanbrabant, et est beaucoup differente du François, lequel est plus moderne, et plus gaillart.
         </span>
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        And those people [the inhabitants of Nivelles] speak the old Gallic language which we call Vualon or Rommand (…). And we use the said old Vualon or Rommand language in our Belgian Gaul: That is to say in Hainaut, Cambrai, Artois, Namur, Liège, Lorraine, Ardennes and Rommand Brabant, and it is very different from French, which is more fashionable and courtly.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The word "Walloon" thus came closer to its current meaning: the
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       of the Roman part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Countries" title="Low Countries">
        Low Countries
       </a>
       . One might say that the period which saw the establishment of the unifying supremacy of the
       <a href="/wiki/Burgundian_(party)" title="Burgundian (party)">
        Burgundians
       </a>
       in the Walloon country was a turning-point in their linguistic history. The crystallization of a Walloon identity, as opposed to that of the
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         thiois
        </span>
       </i>
       (i.e. Dutch-speaking) regions of the Low Countries, established "Walloon" as a word for designating its people. Somewhat later, the vernacular of these people became more clearly distinct from central French and other neighbouring
       <i>
        langues d'oïl
       </i>
       , prompting the abandonment of the vague term "Roman" as a linguistic, ethnic, and political designator for "Walloon".
      </p>
      <p>
       Also at this time, following the
       <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">
        Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts
       </a>
       in 1539, the French language replaced Latin for all administrative purposes in France. Established as the academic language, French became the object of a political effort at normalization;
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <a href="/wiki/La_Pl%C3%A9iade" title="La Pléiade">
          La Pléiade
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       posited the view that when two languages of the same
       <a href="/wiki/Language_family" title="Language family">
        language family
       </a>
       coexist, each can be defined only in opposition to the other. Around the year 1600, the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language#Writing_system" title="French language">
        French writing system
       </a>
       became dominant in the Wallonia. From this time, too, dates a tradition of texts written in a language marked by traces of spoken Walloon. The written language of the preceding centuries,
       <i>
        scripta
       </i>
       , was a composite language with some Walloon characteristics but it did not attempt to be a systematic reproduction of the spoken language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Walloon_society_and_culture">
        Walloon society and culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Walloon_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Walloon society and culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1582" data-file-width="2080" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg/220px-Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg/330px-Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg/440px-Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Fosses-la-Ville_JPG06W.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Bilingual_sign" title="Bilingual sign">
          Bilingual
         </a>
         French-Wallon street sign in
         <a href="/wiki/Fosses-la-Ville" title="Fosses-la-Ville">
          Fosses-la-Ville
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Walloon was the predominant language of the Walloon people until the beginning of the 20th century, although they had a passing knowledge of French. Since that time, the use of French has spread to the extent that now only 15% of the Walloon population speak their ancestral language. Breaking the statistics down by age, 70–80% of the population aged over 60 speak Walloon, while only about 10% of those under 30 do so. Passing knowledge of Walloon is much more widespread: claimed by some 36–58% of the younger age bracket. Laurent Hendschel estimates there are 1,300,000 bilingual people in Wallonia (Walloon-French, Picard-French...).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many French words that pertain to mining and to the textile trade derive from the Walloon-Picard complex.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Legally, Walloon has been recognized since 1990 by the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Community_of_Belgium" title="French Community of Belgium">
        French Community of Belgium
       </a>
       , the cultural authority of Wallonia, as an "indigenous regional language" which must be studied in schools and encouraged. The Walloon cultural movement includes the
       <i>
        Union Culturelle Wallonne
       </i>
       , an organization of over 200 amateur theatre circles, writers' groups, and school councils. About a dozen Walloon magazines publish regularly. The
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         Société de Langue et de Littérature Wallonne
        </span>
       </i>
       , founded in 1856, promotes Walloon literature and the study (
       <a href="/wiki/Dialectology" title="Dialectology">
        dialectology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymology
       </a>
       , etc.) of the regional Roman languages of Wallonia. There is a difference between the Walloon culture, according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Manifesto_for_Walloon_culture" title="Manifesto for Walloon culture">
        Manifesto for Walloon culture
       </a>
       , and the Walloon language (even if the latter is a part of the culture).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
          <span title="This information is too vague. (October 2013)">
           vague
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

         <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">

          <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Schoovaerts" title="wa:José Schoovaerts">
           wa
          </a>
          )
         </span>
         for a 2010 issue of Walloon-speaking magazine
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Li_Rantoele&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Li Rantoele (page does not exist)">
          Li Rantoele
         </a>
         <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
          (
          <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Li_Rantoele_(gazete)" title="wa:Li Rantoele (gazete)">
           wa
          </a>
          )
         </span>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Walloon-language literature has been printed since the 16th century, or at least since the beginning of the 17th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It had its "golden age" during the peak of the
       <a href="/wiki/Flemish_immigration_to_Wallonia" title="Flemish immigration to Wallonia">
        Flemish immigration to Wallonia
       </a>
       in the 19th century: "That period saw an efflorescence of Walloon literature, plays and poems primarily, and the founding of many theaters and periodicals."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kellman_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kellman-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">
        New York Public Library
       </a>
       holds a large collection of literary works in Walloon, quite possibly the largest outside Belgium, and its holdings are representative of the output. Out of nearly a thousand works, twenty-six were published before 1880. Thereafter the numbers rise gradually year by year, reaching a peak of sixty-nine in 1903. After that, publications in Walloon fell markedly, to eleven in 1913.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kellman_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kellman-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Yves Quairiaux counted 4800 plays for 1860–1914, published or not.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this period, plays were almost the only popular entertainment in Wallonia. The Walloon-language theatre remains popular in the region; theatre is flourishing with more than 200 non-professional companies playing in the cities and villages of Wallonia for an audience of over 200,000 each year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During the 19th-century renaissance of Walloon-language literature, several authors adapted versions of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aesop%27s_Fables" title="Aesop's Fables">
         Aesop's Fables
        </a>
       </i>
       to the racy speech (and subject matter) of Liège.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They included
       <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_du_Vivier_de_Streel" title="wa:Charles du Vivier de Streel">
        Charles Duvivier
       </a>
       (in 1842); Joseph Lamaye (1845); and the team of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://wa.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Joseph_Dehin" title="wa:Jean-Joseph Dehin">
        Jean-Joseph Dehin
       </a>
       (1847, 1851-2) and François Bailleux (1851–67), who covered books I-VI.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Adaptations into other dialects were made by Charles Letellier (Mons, 1842) and Charles Wérotte (Namur, 1844). Decades later, Léon Bernus published some hundred imitations of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Fontaine" title="La Fontaine">
        La Fontaine
       </a>
       in the dialect of
       <i>
        Charleroi
       </i>
       (1872);
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       he was followed during the 1880s by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Dufrane" title="fr:Joseph Dufrane">
        Joseph Dufrane
       </a>
       , writing in the
       <i>
        Borinage
       </i>
       dialect under the pen-name
       <i>
        Bosquètia
       </i>
       . In the 20th century, Joseph Houziaux (1946) published a selection of 50 fables in the
       <i>
        Condroz
       </i>
       dialect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The motive among Walloon speakers in both France and Belgium was to assert regional identity against the growing centralism and encroachment of the language of the capital, on what had until then been predominantly monoglot areas.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are links between French literature and Walloon literature. For instance, the writer
       <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Queneau" title="Raymond Queneau">
        Raymond Queneau
       </a>
       set the publication of a Walloon Poets' anthology for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Editions_Gallimard" title="Editions Gallimard">
        Editions Gallimard
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ubu_roi" title="Ubu roi">
         Ubu roi
        </a>
       </i>
       was translated into Walloon by
       <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Blavier" title="André Blavier">
        André Blavier
       </a>
       , an important
       <a href="/wiki/%27Pataphysics" title="'Pataphysics">
        'pataphysician
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Verviers" title="Verviers">
        Verviers
       </a>
       , and friend of Queneau, for the new and important
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puppet_theater" title="Puppet theater">
        Puppet theater
       </a>
       of Liège of Jacques Ancion. The
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Al_Botro%C3%BBle&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Al Botroûle (page does not exist)">
        Al Botroûle
       </a>
       theater operated "as the umbilical cord" in Walloon, indicating a desire to return to the source.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gross-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jacques Ancion also wanted to develop a regular adult audience. "From the 19th century he included the Walloon play
       <i>
        Tati l'Pèriquî
       </i>
       by E. Remouchamps and the avant-garde
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ubu_roi" title="Ubu roi">
         Ubu roi
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Jarry" title="Alfred Jarry">
        A. Jarry
       </a>
       ."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gross_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gross-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The scholar Jean-Marie Klinkenberg writes, "[T]he dialectal culture is no more a sign of attachment to the past but a way to participate to a new synthesis".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Walloon is also being used in popular song. The most well-known singer in Walloon in present-day Wallonia is
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Dunker&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Dunker (page does not exist)">
        William Dunker
       </a>
       (
       <abbr title="born">
        b.
       </abbr>
       15 March 1959).
      </p>
