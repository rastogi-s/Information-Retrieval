      <p>
       <b>
        Standard French
       </b>
       (in French:
       <i>
        le français standard
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        le français normé
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        le français neutre
       </i>
       [Neutral French] or
       <i>
        le français international
       </i>
       [International French], the last being a Quebec invention) is an unofficial term for a
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard variety
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French language
       </a>
       . It is a set of spoken and written formal
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       used by the educated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophones
       </a>
       of several nations around the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       As French is a
       <a href="/wiki/Pluricentric_language" title="Pluricentric language">
        pluricentric language
       </a>
       , Standard French encompasses various
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        linguistic norms
       </a>
       (consisting of
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        prescribed usage
       </a>
       ). The
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       of Standard French is explained in various works on
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       and style such as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bescherelle" title="Bescherelle">
         Bescherelle
        </a>
       </i>
       , a reference summary of
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugations
       </a>
       first compiled in the 19th century by
       <a href="/wiki/Louis-Nicolas_Bescherelle" title="Louis-Nicolas Bescherelle">
        Louis-Nicolas Bescherelle
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le_Bon_Usage" title="Le Bon Usage">
         Le Bon Usage
        </a>
       </i>
       written in the 20th century by
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammarian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Grevisse" title="Maurice Grevisse">
        Maurice Grevisse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In France, Standard French is based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Pronunciation" title="Pronunciation">
        pronunciation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lexis_(linguistics)" title="Lexis (linguistics)">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       used in the formal registers of French in
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">
        Metropolitan France
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       , it is more often called "International French" or "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_Canada" title="Radio Canada">
        Radio Canada
       </a>
       French" because of decades of a foreign, European pronunciation dominating both news and cultural broadcasts until the 1970s. In the rest of Francophone Canada, the spoken and written varieties of formal
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
        Quebec French
       </a>
       as well as language in
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Canada" title="Government of Canada">
        Government of Canada
       </a>
       documents and speeches are viewed as Standard French. Linguists have been debating what actually constitutes the norm for Standard French in Quebec and Canada on a lexical level since research, to date, has concentrated much more on the differences from informal varieties of Quebec French and
       <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">
        Acadian French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Since French-speaking Canadians usually use reference works written by the French, by Belgians and by reputed Canadian
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguists
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lexicographer" title="Lexicographer">
        lexicographers
       </a>
       alike, answers concerning an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Endogeny" title="Endogeny">
        endogenous
       </a>
       norm are not always apparent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although Standard French has in fact undergone centuries of human intervention and
       <a href="/wiki/Language_planning" title="Language planning">
        language planning
       </a>
       , popular opinion contends that Standard French should consist solely of the rulings by the
       <a href="/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Académie française">
        Académie française
       </a>
       in France or in standardization from
       <a href="/wiki/Terminology" title="Terminology">
        terminological
       </a>
       work by the
       <a href="/wiki/Office_qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_de_la_langue_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Office québécois de la langue française">
        Office québécois de la langue française
       </a>
       in Quebec. There is further perceived or actual linguistic
       <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">
        hegemony
       </a>
       in favour of France by virtue of tradition, former
       <a href="/wiki/Imperialism" title="Imperialism">
        imperialism
       </a>
       , and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">
        demographic
       </a>
       majority. Such notions hinge on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prestige_dialect" title="Prestige dialect">
        linguistic prestige
       </a>
       rather than on a linguistic norm.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, despite the existence of many regional varieties of French in the Francophone world, Standard French is normally chosen as a model for learners of French as a foreign or
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       . The standard pronunciation of Metropolitan French is, out of concerns for comprehension or
       <a href="/wiki/Social_stigma" title="Social stigma">
        social stigma
       </a>
       , sometimes favoured over other standard national pronunciations when teaching French to non-native speakers in Francophone nations other than France.
