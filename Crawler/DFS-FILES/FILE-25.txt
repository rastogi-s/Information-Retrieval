      <p>
       <b>
        Geography
       </b>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        γεωγραφία
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        geographia
       </i>
       , literally "earth description"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) is a field of
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       devoted to the study of the lands, the features, the inhabitants, and the phenomena of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first person to use the word "γεωγραφία" was
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       (276–194 BC).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geography is an all-encompassing discipline that seeks an understanding of the Earth and its human and natural complexities—not merely where objects are, but how they have changed and come to be.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geography is often defined in terms of the two branches of
       <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">
        human geography
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">
        physical geography
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Human geography deals with the study of people and their communities, cultures, economies and interactions with the environment by studying their relations with and across space and place.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HGDICT_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HGDICT-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Physical geography deals with the study of processes and patterns in the natural environment like the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">
        hydrosphere
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Biosphere" title="Biosphere">
        biosphere
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Geosphere" title="Geosphere">
        geosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The four historical traditions in geographical research are:
       <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">
        spatial analyses
       </a>
       of natural and the human phenomena,
       <a href="/wiki/Area_studies" title="Area studies">
        area studies
       </a>
       of places and regions, studies of human-land relationships, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">
        Earth sciences
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geography has been called "the world discipline" and "the bridge between the human and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_science" title="Physical science">
        physical sciences
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Introduction">
        Introduction
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Geography is a systematic study of the Earth and its features. Traditionally, geography has been associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       and place names. Although many geographers are trained in
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponymy
       </a>
       and cartology, this is not their main preoccupation. Geographers study the
       <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
        space
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Temporal_database" title="Temporal database">
        temporal database
       </a>
       distribution of phenomena, processes, and features as well as the
       <a href="/wiki/Interaction" title="Interaction">
        interaction
       </a>
       of humans and their
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
        environment
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayes-Bohanan_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hayes-Bohanan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because space and place affect a variety of topics, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
        economics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Health" title="Health">
        health
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
        climate
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">
        plants
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">
        animals
       </a>
       , geography is highly interdisciplinary. The interdisciplinary nature of the geographical approach depends on an attentiveness to the relationship between physical and human phenomena and its spatial patterns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Names of places...are not geography...know by heart a whole
        <a href="/wiki/Gazetteer" title="Gazetteer">
         gazetteer
        </a>
        full of them would not, in itself, constitute anyone a
        <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">
         geographer
        </a>
        . Geography has higher aims than this: it seeks to classify phenomena (alike of the natural and of the political world, in so far as it treats of the latter), to compare, to generalize, to ascend from effects to causes, and, in doing so, to trace out the laws of nature and to mark their influences upon man. This is 'a description of the world'—that is Geography. In a word Geography is a Science—a thing not of mere names but of argument and reason, of cause and effect.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <a href="/wiki/William_Hughes_(geographer)" title="William Hughes (geographer)">
          William Hughes
         </a>
         , 1863
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Just as all phenomena exist in time and thus have a history, they also exist in space and have a geography.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Research_Council" title="United States National Research Council">
          United States National Research Council
         </a>
         , 1997
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Geography as a discipline can be split broadly into two main subsidiary fields:
       <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">
        human geography
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">
        physical geography
       </a>
       . The former largely focuses on the
       <a href="/wiki/Built_environment" title="Built environment">
        built environment
       </a>
       and how humans create, view, manage, and influence space. The latter examines the natural environment, and how
       <a href="/wiki/Organism" title="Organism">
        organisms
       </a>
       , climate,
       <a href="/wiki/Soil" title="Soil">
        soil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Landform" title="Landform">
        landforms
       </a>
       produce and interact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The difference between these approaches led to a third field,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_geography" title="Environmental geography">
        environmental geography
       </a>
       , which combines physical and human geography and concerns the interactions between the environment and humans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hayes-Bohanan_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hayes-Bohanan-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Branches">
        Branches
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_geography">
        Physical geography
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_geography" title="Physical geography">
        Physical geography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Physical geography (or physiography) focuses on geography as an
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">
        Earth science
       </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Integrated_geography">
        Integrated geography
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_geography" title="Integrated geography">
        Integrated geography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Integrated_geography" title="Integrated geography">
        Integrated geography
       </a>
       is concerned with the description of the spatial interactions between humans and the
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_environment" title="Natural environment">
        natural world
       </a>
       . It requires an understanding of the traditional aspects of physical and human geography, as well as the ways that human societies conceptualize the environment. Integrated geography has emerged as a bridge between the human and the physical geography, as a result of the increasing specialisation of the two sub-fields. Furthermore, as human relationship with the environment has changed as a result of
       <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
        globalization
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Technological_change" title="Technological change">
        technological change
       </a>
       , a new approach was needed to understand the changing and dynamic relationship. Examples of areas of research in the environmental geography include:
       <a href="/wiki/Emergency_management" title="Emergency management">
        emergency management
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Environmental_management" title="Environmental management">
        environmental management
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sustainability" title="Sustainability">
        sustainability
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Political_ecology" title="Political ecology">
        political ecology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geomatics">
        Geomatics
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geomatics" title="Geomatics">
        Geomatics
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Geabios3d.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="242" data-file-width="280" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Geabios3d.jpg/220px-Geabios3d.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d2/Geabios3d.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Geabios3d.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Digital Elevation Model (DEM)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Geomatics" title="Geomatics">
        Geomatics
       </a>
       is concerned with the application of computers to the traditional spatial techniques used in
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
        topography
       </a>
       . Geomatics emerged from the
       <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_revolution" title="Quantitative revolution">
        quantitative revolution
       </a>
       in geography in the mid-1950s. Today, geomatics methods include
       <a href="/wiki/Spatial_analysis" title="Spatial analysis">
        spatial analysis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        geographic information systems (GIS)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        remote sensing
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_positioning_system" title="Global positioning system">
        global positioning systems (GPS)
       </a>
       . Geomatics has led to a revitalization of some geography departments, especially in Northern America where the subject had a declining status during the 1950s.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_geography">
        Regional geography
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_geography" title="Regional geography">
        Regional geography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Regional geography is concerned with the description of the unique characteristics of a particular region such as it natural or human elements. The main aim is to understand, or define the uniqueness, or character of a particular region that consists of natural as well as human elements. Attention is paid also to
       <a href="/wiki/Regionalisation" title="Regionalisation">
        regionalization
       </a>
       , which covers the proper techniques of space
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delimitation" title="Delimitation">
        delimitation
       </a>
       into regions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_fields">
        Related fields
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">
         Urban planning
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Regional_planning" title="Regional planning">
         regional planning
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Spatial_planning" title="Spatial planning">
         spatial planning
        </a>
        : Use the science of geography to assist in determining how to develop (or not develop) the land to meet particular criteria, such as safety, beauty, economic opportunities, the preservation of the built or natural heritage, and so on. The planning of towns, cities, and rural areas may be seen as
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Applied_geography&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Applied geography (page does not exist)">
         applied geography
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Regional_science" title="Regional science">
         Regional science
        </a>
        : In the 1950s, the regional science movement led by
        <a href="/wiki/Walter_Isard" title="Walter Isard">
         Walter Isard
        </a>
        arose to provide a more quantitative and analytical base to geographical questions, in contrast to the descriptive tendencies of traditional geography programs. Regional science comprises the body of knowledge in which the spatial dimension plays a fundamental role, such as
        <a href="/wiki/Regional_science" title="Regional science">
         regional economics
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Resource_management" title="Resource management">
         resource management
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Location_theory" title="Location theory">
         location theory
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">
         urban
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Regional_planning" title="Regional planning">
         regional planning
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Transport" title="Transport">
         transport
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Communication" title="Communication">
         communication
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">
         human geography
        </a>
        , population distribution,
        <a href="/wiki/Landscape_ecology" title="Landscape ecology">
         landscape ecology
        </a>
        , and environmental quality.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">
         Interplanetary Sciences
        </a>
        : While the discipline of geography is normally concerned with the
        <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
         Earth
        </a>
        , the term can also be informally used to describe the study of other worlds, such as the
        <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
         planets
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
         Solar System
        </a>
        and even beyond. The study of systems larger than the Earth itself usually forms part of
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
         Astronomy
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Cosmology" title="Cosmology">
         Cosmology
        </a>
        . The study of other planets is usually called
        <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">
         planetary science
        </a>
        . Alternative terms such as
        <a href="/wiki/Geology_of_Mars" title="Geology of Mars">
         areology
        </a>
        (the study of Mars) have been proposed but are not widely used.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Techniques">
        Techniques
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As spatial interrelationships are key to this synoptic science,
       <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">
        maps
       </a>
       are a key tool. Classical
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       has been joined by a more modern approach to geographical analysis, computer-based
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        geographic information systems
       </a>
       (GIS).
      </p>
      <p>
       In their study, geographers use four interrelated approaches:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Systematic — Groups geographical knowledge into categories that can be explored globally.
       </li>
       <li>
        Regional — Examines systematic relationships between categories for a specific region or location on the planet.
       </li>
       <li>
        Descriptive — Simply specifies the locations of features and populations.
       </li>
       <li>
        Analytical — Asks
        <i>
         why
        </i>
        we find features and populations in a specific geographic area.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cartography">
        Cartography
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1508" data-file-width="1093" height="304" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg/220px-Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg/330px-Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg/440px-Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cook_chart_of_New_Zealand.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">
          James Cook
         </a>
         's 1770 chart of
         <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
          New Zealand
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        Cartography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cartography studies the representation of the Earth's surface with abstract symbols (map making). Although other subdisciplines of geography rely on maps for presenting their analyses, the actual making of maps is abstract enough to be regarded separately. Cartography has grown from a collection of drafting techniques into an actual science.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cartographers must learn
       <a href="/wiki/Cognitive_psychology" title="Cognitive psychology">
        cognitive psychology
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ergonomics" title="Ergonomics">
        ergonomics
       </a>
       to understand which symbols convey information about the Earth most effectively, and
       <a href="/wiki/Behaviorism" title="Behaviorism">
        behavioural psychology
       </a>
       to induce the readers of their maps to act on the information. They must learn
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy
       </a>
       and fairly advanced
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematics
       </a>
       to understand how the
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        shape of the Earth
       </a>
       affects the distortion of map symbols projected onto a flat surface for viewing. It can be said, without much controversy, that cartography is the seed from which the larger field of geography grew. Most geographers will cite a childhood fascination with maps as an early sign they would end up in the field.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_information_systems">
        Geographic information systems
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        Geographic information system
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Geographic information systems (GIS) deal with the storage of information about the Earth for automatic retrieval by a computer, in an accurate manner appropriate to the information's purpose. In addition to all of the other subdisciplines of geography, GIS specialists must understand
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_science" title="Computer science">
        computer science
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Database" title="Database">
        database
       </a>
       systems. GIS has revolutionized the field of cartography: nearly all mapmaking is now done with the assistance of some form of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_GIS_software" title="List of GIS software">
        GIS software
       </a>
       . GIS also refers to the science of using GIS software and GIS techniques to represent, analyse, and predict the spatial relationships. In this context, GIS stands for
       <i>
        geographic information science
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Remote_sensing">
        Remote sensing
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Remote_sensing" title="Remote sensing">
        Remote sensing
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Remote sensing is the science of obtaining information about Earth features from measurements made at a distance. Remotely sensed data comes in many forms, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_imagery" title="Satellite imagery">
        satellite imagery
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aerial_photography" title="Aerial photography">
        aerial photography
       </a>
       , and data obtained from hand-held sensors. Geographers increasingly use remotely sensed data to obtain information about the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_surface" title="Land surface">
        land surface
       </a>
       , ocean, and atmosphere, because it: (a) supplies objective information at a variety of spatial scales (local to global), (b) provides a synoptic view of the area of interest, (c) allows access to distant and inaccessible sites, (d) provides spectral information outside the visible portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       , and (e) facilitates studies of how features/areas change over time. Remotely sensed data may be analysed either independently of, or in conjunction with other digital data layers (e.g., in a geographic information system).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quantitative_methods">
        Quantitative methods
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geostatistics" title="Geostatistics">
        Geostatistics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Geostatistics" title="Geostatistics">
        Geostatistics
       </a>
       deal with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Numerical_data" title="Numerical data">
        quantitative data
       </a>
       analysis, specifically the application of statistical methodology to the exploration of geographic phenomena. Geostatistics is used extensively in a variety of fields, including
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">
        hydrology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
        petroleum
       </a>
       exploration, weather analysis,
       <a href="/wiki/Urban_planning" title="Urban planning">
        urban planning
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Logistics" title="Logistics">
        logistics
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Epidemiology" title="Epidemiology">
        epidemiology
       </a>
       . The mathematical basis for geostatistics derives from
       <a href="/wiki/Cluster_analysis" title="Cluster analysis">
        cluster analysis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Linear_discriminant_analysis" title="Linear discriminant analysis">
        linear discriminant analysis
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-parametric_statistics" title="Non-parametric statistics">
        non-parametric statistical tests
       </a>
       , and a variety of other subjects. Applications of geostatistics rely heavily on
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        geographic information systems
       </a>
       , particularly for the
       <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">
        interpolation
       </a>
       (estimate) of unmeasured points. Geographers are making notable contributions to the method of quantitative techniques.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Qualitative_methods">
        Qualitative methods
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnography" title="Ethnography">
        Ethnography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Geographic qualitative methods, or ethnographical research techniques, are used by human geographers. In
       <a href="/wiki/Cultural_geography" title="Cultural geography">
        cultural geography
       </a>
       there is a tradition of employing
       <a href="/wiki/Qualitative_research" title="Qualitative research">
        qualitative research
       </a>
       techniques, also used in
       <a href="/wiki/Anthropology" title="Anthropology">
        anthropology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
        sociology
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Participant_observation" title="Participant observation">
        Participant observation
       </a>
       and in-depth interviews provide human geographers with qualitative data.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_geography" title="History of geography">
        History of geography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The oldest known
       <a href="/wiki/World_map" title="World map">
        world maps
       </a>
       date back to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Babylon" title="Ancient Babylon">
        ancient Babylon
       </a>
       from the 9th century BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurt_A._Raaflaub_&amp;_Richard_J._A._Talbert_2009_147_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kurt_A._Raaflaub_&amp;_Richard_J._A._Talbert_2009_147-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The best known
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonia" title="Babylonia">
        Babylonian
       </a>
       world map, however, is the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_Map_of_the_World" title="Babylonian Map of the World">
         Imago Mundi
        </a>
       </i>
       of 600 BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The map as reconstructed by
       <a href="/wiki/Eckhard_Unger" title="Eckhard Unger">
        Eckhard Unger
       </a>
       shows
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylon
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">
        Euphrates
       </a>
       , surrounded by a circular landmass showing
       <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">
        Assyria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Urartu" title="Urartu">
        Urartu
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and several cities, in turn surrounded by a "bitter river" (
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus" title="Oceanus">
        Oceanus
       </a>
       ), with seven islands arranged around it so as to form a seven-pointed star. The accompanying text mentions seven outer regions beyond the encircling ocean. The descriptions of five of them have survived.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In contrast to the
       <i>
        Imago Mundi
       </i>
       , an earlier Babylonian
       <a href="/wiki/World_map" title="World map">
        world map
       </a>
       dating back to the 9th century BC depicted Babylon as being further north from the center of the world, though it is not certain what that center was supposed to represent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kurt_A._Raaflaub_&amp;_Richard_J._A._Talbert_2009_147_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kurt_A._Raaflaub_&amp;_Richard_J._A._Talbert_2009_147-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The ideas of
       <a href="/wiki/Anaximander" title="Anaximander">
        Anaximander
       </a>
       (c. 610 BC-c. 545 BC): considered by later Greek writers to be the true founder of geography, come to us through fragments quoted by his successors. Anaximander is credited with the invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">
        gnomon
       </a>
       , the simple, yet efficient Greek instrument that allowed the early measurement of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       . Thales is also credited with the prediction of eclipses. The foundations of geography can be traced to the ancient cultures, such as the ancient, medieval, and early modern
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">
        Chinese
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greeks
       </a>
       , who were the first to explore geography as both
       <a href="/wiki/Art" title="Art">
        art
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       , achieved this through
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_cartography" title="History of cartography">
        Cartography
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_philosophy" title="Greek philosophy">
        Philosophy
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_literature" title="Ancient Greek literature">
        Literature
       </a>
       , or through
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_mathematics" title="History of mathematics">
        Mathematics
       </a>
       . There is some debate about who was the first person to assert that the
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">
        Earth is spherical
       </a>
       in shape, with the credit going either to
       <a href="/wiki/Parmenides" title="Parmenides">
        Parmenides
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">
        Pythagoras
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Anaxagoras" title="Anaxagoras">
        Anaxagoras
       </a>
       was able to demonstrate that the profile of the Earth was circular by explaining
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipses
       </a>
       . However, he still believed that the Earth was a flat disk, as did many of his contemporaries. One of the first estimates of the radius of the Earth was made by
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first rigorous system of
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">
        latitude and longitude
       </a>
       lines is credited to
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       . He employed a
       <a href="/wiki/Sexagesimal" title="Sexagesimal">
        sexagesimal
       </a>
       system that was derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics" title="Babylonian mathematics">
        Babylonian mathematics
       </a>
       . The meridians were sub-divided into 360°, with each degree further subdivided 60′ (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minute_of_arc" title="Minute of arc">
        minutes
       </a>
       ). To measure the longitude at different location on Earth, he suggested using eclipses to determine the relative difference in time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The extensive mapping by the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Romans
       </a>
       as they explored new lands would later provide a high level of information for
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       to construct detailed
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">
        atlases
       </a>
       . He extended the work of
       <a href="/wiki/Hipparchus" title="Hipparchus">
        Hipparchus
       </a>
       , using a grid system on his maps and adopting a length of 56.5 miles for a degree.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From the 3rd century onwards,
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">
        Chinese
       </a>
       methods of geographical study and writing of geographical literature became much more comprehensive than what was found in Europe at the time (until the 13th century).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_512_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_512-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chinese geographers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Liu_An" title="Liu An">
        Liu An
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pei_Xiu" title="Pei Xiu">
        Pei Xiu
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jia_Dan" title="Jia Dan">
        Jia Dan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Shen_Kuo" title="Shen Kuo">
        Shen Kuo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fan_Chengda" title="Fan Chengda">
        Fan Chengda
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Zhou_Daguan" title="Zhou Daguan">
        Zhou Daguan
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Xu_Xiake" title="Xu Xiake">
        Xu Xiake
       </a>
       wrote important treatises, yet by the 17th century advanced ideas and methods of Western-style geography were adopted in China.
      </p>

      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Decline_of_the_Roman_Empire" title="Decline of the Roman Empire">
        fall of the Roman empire
       </a>
       led to a shift in the evolution of geography from
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Muslim_world" title="Muslim world">
        Islamic world
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-needham_volume_3_512_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-needham_volume_3_512-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geography_in_medieval_Islam" title="Geography in medieval Islam">
        Muslim geographers
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_al-Idrisi" title="Muhammad al-Idrisi">
        Muhammad al-Idrisi
       </a>
       produced detailed world maps (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tabula_Rogeriana" title="Tabula Rogeriana">
        Tabula Rogeriana
       </a>
       ), while other geographers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Yaqut_al-Hamawi" title="Yaqut al-Hamawi">
        Yaqut al-Hamawi
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_Biruni" title="Abu Rayhan Biruni">
        Abu Rayhan Biruni
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Battuta" title="Ibn Battuta">
        Ibn Battuta
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Khaldun" title="Ibn Khaldun">
        Ibn Khaldun
       </a>
       provided detailed accounts of their journeys and the geography of the regions they visited. Turkish geographer,
       <a href="/wiki/Mahmud_al-Kashgari" title="Mahmud al-Kashgari">
        Mahmud al-Kashgari
       </a>
       drew a world map on a linguistic basis, and later so did
       <a href="/wiki/Piri_Reis" title="Piri Reis">
        Piri Reis
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Piri_Reis_map" title="Piri Reis map">
        Piri Reis map
       </a>
       ). Further, Islamic scholars translated and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interpreting" title="Interpreting">
        interpreted
       </a>
       the earlier works of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Romans
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Greeks
       </a>
       and established the
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Wisdom" title="House of Wisdom">
        House of Wisdom
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">
        Baghdad
       </a>
       for this purpose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ahmed_ibn_Sahl_al-Balkhi" title="Ahmed ibn Sahl al-Balkhi">
        Abū Zayd al-Balkhī
       </a>
       , originally from
       <a href="/wiki/Balkh" title="Balkh">
        Balkh
       </a>
       , founded the "Balkhī school" of terrestrial mapping in
       <a href="/wiki/Baghdad" title="Baghdad">
        Baghdad
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E61-3_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-E61-3-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Suhrāb, a late tenth century Muslim geographer accompanied a book of geographical coordinates, with instructions for making a rectangular world map with
       <a href="/wiki/Equirectangular_projection" title="Equirectangular projection">
        equirectangular projection
       </a>
       or cylindrical equidistant projection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-E61-3_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-E61-3-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag needs to be fact-checked with the cited source(s). (October 2011)">
           verification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_Biruni" title="Abu Rayhan Biruni">
        Abu Rayhan Biruni
       </a>
       (976-1048) first described a polar equi-
       <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_equidistant_projection" title="Azimuthal equidistant projection">
        azimuthal equidistant projection
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was regarded as the most skilled when it came to mapping cities and measuring the distances between them, which he did for many cities in the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       . He often combined astronomical readings and mathematical equations, in order to develop methods of pin-pointing locations by recording degrees of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . He also developed similar techniques when it came to measuring the heights of
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
        mountains
       </a>
       , depths of the
       <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">
        valleys
       </a>
       , and expanse of the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       . He also discussed
       <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">
        human geography
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">
        planetary habitability
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . He also calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       of Kath,
       <a href="/wiki/Khwarezm" title="Khwarezm">
        Khwarezm
       </a>
       , using the maximum altitude of the Sun, and solved a complex
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesic
       </a>
       equation in order to accurately compute the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">
        circumference
       </a>
       , which were close to modern values of the Earth's circumference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His estimate of 6,339.9 km for the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        Earth radius
       </a>
       was only 16.8 km less than the modern value of 6,356.7 km. In contrast to his predecessors, who measured the Earth's circumference by sighting the Sun simultaneously from two different locations,
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Biruni" title="Al-Biruni">
        al-Biruni
       </a>
       developed a new method of using
       <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">
        trigonometric
       </a>
       calculations, based on the angle between a
       <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">
        plain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
        mountain
       </a>
       top, which yielded more accurate measurements of the Earth's circumference, and made it possible for it to be measured by a single person from a single location.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The European
       <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Discovery" title="Age of Discovery">
        Age of Discovery
       </a>
       during the 16th and the 17th centuries, where many new lands were discovered and accounts by European explorers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">
        Marco Polo
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Cook" title="James Cook">
        James Cook
       </a>
       revived a desire for both accurate geographic detail, and more solid theoretical foundations in Europe. The problem facing both explorers and geographers was finding the latitude and longitude of a geographic location. The problem of latitude was solved long ago but that of longitude remained; agreeing on what zero meridian should be was only part of the problem. It was left to
       <a href="/wiki/John_Harrison" title="John Harrison">
        John Harrison
       </a>
       to solve it by inventing the chronometer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harrison_Number_Four" title="Harrison Number Four">
        H-4
       </a>
       in 1760, and later in 1884 for the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Meridian_Conference" title="International Meridian Conference">
        International Meridian Conference
       </a>
       to adopt by convention the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwich_meridian" title="Greenwich meridian">
        Greenwich meridian
       </a>
       as zero meridian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 18th and the 19th centuries were the times when geography became recognized as a discrete
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_academic_disciplines" title="List of academic disciplines">
        academic discipline
       </a>
       , and became part of a typical
       <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
        university
       </a>
       curriculum in
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       (especially
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Berlin" title="Berlin">
        Berlin
       </a>
       ). The development of many geographic societies also occurred during the 19th century, with the foundations of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soci%C3%A9t%C3%A9_de_G%C3%A9ographie" title="Société de Géographie">
        Société de Géographie
       </a>
       in 1821,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Geographical_Society" title="Royal Geographical Society">
        Royal Geographical Society
       </a>
       in 1830,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_Geographical_Society" title="Russian Geographical Society">
        Russian Geographical Society
       </a>
       in 1845,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/American_Geographical_Society" title="American Geographical Society">
        American Geographical Society
       </a>
       in 1851,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Geographic_Society" title="National Geographic Society">
        National Geographic Society
       </a>
       in 1888.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Immanuel_Kant" title="Immanuel Kant">
        Immanuel Kant
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">
        Alexander von Humboldt
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Ritter" title="Carl Ritter">
        Carl Ritter
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Vidal_de_la_Blache" title="Paul Vidal de la Blache">
        Paul Vidal de la Blache
       </a>
       can be seen as a major turning point in geography from a philosophy to an academic subject.
      </p>
      <p>
       Over the past two centuries, the advancements in technology with computers have led to the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Geomatics" title="Geomatics">
        geomatics
       </a>
       and new practices such as participant observation and geostatistics being incorporated into geography's portfolio of tools. In the West during the 20th century, the discipline of geography went through four major phases:
       <a href="/wiki/Environmental_determinism" title="Environmental determinism">
        environmental determinism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_geography" title="Regional geography">
        regional geography
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Quantitative_revolution" title="Quantitative revolution">
        quantitative revolution
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Critical_geography" title="Critical geography">
        critical geography
       </a>
       . The strong interdisciplinary links between geography and the sciences of
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Botany" title="Botany">
        botany
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Economics" title="Economics">
        economics
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sociology" title="Sociology">
        sociology
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demographics" title="Demographics">
        demographics
       </a>
       have also grown greatly, especially as a result of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_system_science" title="Earth system science">
        earth system science
       </a>
       that seeks to understand the world in a holistic view.
      </p>

        <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
         Strabo
        </a>
        (64/63 BC – ca. AD 24) – wrote
        <a href="/wiki/Geographica" title="Geographica">
         Geographica
        </a>
        , one of the first books outlining the study of geography.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
         Ptolemy
        </a>
        (c.90–c.168) – compiled Greek and Roman knowledge into the book
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographia" title="Geographia">
         Geographia
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al_Idrisi" title="Al Idrisi">
         Al Idrisi
        </a>
        (Arabic: أبو عبد الله محمد الإدريسي; Latin: Dreses) (1100–1165/66) – author of Nuzhatul Mushtaq.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">
         Gerardus Mercator
        </a>
        (1512–1594) – innovative
        <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
         cartographer
        </a>
        produced the
        <a href="/wiki/Mercator_projection" title="Mercator projection">
         mercator projection
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">
         Alexander von Humboldt
        </a>
        (1769–1859) – Considered Father of modern geography, published the Kosmos and founder of the sub-field biogeography.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Carl_Ritter" title="Carl Ritter">
         Carl Ritter
        </a>
        (1779–1859) – Considered Father of modern geography. Occupied the first chair of geography at Berlin University.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Henry_Guyot" title="Arnold Henry Guyot">
         Arnold Henry Guyot
        </a>
        (1807–1884) – noted the structure of glaciers and advanced understanding in
        <a href="/wiki/Glacial_motion" title="Glacial motion">
         glacier motion
        </a>
        , especially in fast ice flow.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/William_Morris_Davis" title="William Morris Davis">
         William Morris Davis
        </a>
        (1850–1934) – father of American geography and developer of the
        <a href="/wiki/Cycle_of_erosion" title="Cycle of erosion">
         cycle of erosion
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paul_Vidal_de_la_Blache" title="Paul Vidal de la Blache">
         Paul Vidal de la Blache
        </a>
        (1845–1918) – founder of the French school of geopolitics and wrote the principles of human geography.
       </li>
       <li>
        Sir
        <a href="/wiki/Halford_Mackinder" title="Halford Mackinder">
         Halford John Mackinder
        </a>
        (1861–1947) – Co-founder of the
        <a href="/wiki/London_School_of_Economics" title="London School of Economics">
         LSE
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Geographical_Association" title="Geographical Association">
         Geographical Association
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ellen_Churchill_Semple" title="Ellen Churchill Semple">
         Ellen Churchill Semple
        </a>
        (1863–1932) – She was America's first influential female geographer.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Carl_O._Sauer" title="Carl O. Sauer">
         Carl O. Sauer
        </a>
        (1889–1975) – Prominent cultural geographer
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Walter_Christaller" title="Walter Christaller">
         Walter Christaller
        </a>
        (1893–1969) – human geographer and inventor of
        <a href="/wiki/Central_place_theory" title="Central place theory">
         Central place theory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Yi-Fu_Tuan" title="Yi-Fu Tuan">
         Yi-Fu Tuan
        </a>
        (born 1930) – Chinese-American scholar credited with starting Humanistic Geography as a discipline.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Karl_Butzer" title="Karl Butzer">
         Karl W. Butzer
        </a>
        (1934–2016) – An influential German-American geographer, cultural ecologist and environmental archaeologist.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Harvey_(geographer)" title="David Harvey (geographer)">
         David Harvey
        </a>
        (born 1935) – Marxist geographer and author of theories on spatial and urban geography, winner of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laur%C3%A9at_Prix_International_de_G%C3%A9ographie_Vautrin_Lud" title="Lauréat Prix International de Géographie Vautrin Lud">
         Vautrin Lud Prize
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Edward_Soja" title="Edward Soja">
         Edward Soja
        </a>
        (1941–2015) – Noted for his work on regional development, planning and governance along with coining the terms
        <a href="/wiki/Synekism" title="Synekism">
         Synekism
        </a>
        and Postmetropolis, winner of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laur%C3%A9at_Prix_International_de_G%C3%A9ographie_Vautrin_Lud" title="Lauréat Prix International de Géographie Vautrin Lud">
         Vautrin Lud Prize
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Michael_Frank_Goodchild" title="Michael Frank Goodchild">
         Michael Frank Goodchild
        </a>
        (born 1944) – prominent GIS scholar and winner of the RGS founder's medal in 2003.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Doreen_Massey_(geographer)" title="Doreen Massey (geographer)">
         Doreen Massey
        </a>
        (1944–2016) – Key scholar in the space and places of
        <a href="/wiki/Globalization" title="Globalization">
         globalization
        </a>
        and its pluralities, winner of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laur%C3%A9at_Prix_International_de_G%C3%A9ographie_Vautrin_Lud" title="Lauréat Prix International de Géographie Vautrin Lud">
         Vautrin Lud Prize
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Thrift" title="Nigel Thrift">
         Nigel Thrift
        </a>
        (born 1949) – originator of
        <a href="/wiki/Non-representational_theory" title="Non-representational theory">
         non-representational theory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
