      <p>
       <b>
        Focus
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_glossing_abbreviations" title="List of glossing abbreviations">
        abbreviated
       </a>
       <b>
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          FOC
         </span>
        </span>
       </b>
       ) is a grammatical category that determines which part of the sentence contributes new, non-derivable, or contrastive information.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halliday_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Halliday-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Focus is related to
       <a href="/wiki/Information_structure" title="Information structure">
        information structure
       </a>
       .
       <b>
        Contrastive focus
       </b>
       specifically refers to the coding of information that is contrary to the presuppositions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Interlocutor_(linguistics)" title="Interlocutor (linguistics)">
        interlocutor
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camacho_2010_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Camacho_2010-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Related terms include
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topic%E2%80%93comment" title="Topic–comment">
        Comment
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topic%E2%80%93comment" title="Topic–comment">
        Rheme
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Camacho_2010_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Camacho_2010-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>

        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Functional approaches">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Information structure has been described at length by a number of linguists as a grammatical phenomenon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lexicogrammatical structures that code prominence, or focus, of some information over other information has a particularly significant history dating back to the 19th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recent attempts to explain focus phenomena in terms of discourse function, including those by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Knud_Lambrecht&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Knud Lambrecht (page does not exist)">
        Knud Lambrecht
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Talmy_Giv%C3%B3n" title="Talmy Givón">
        Talmy Givón
       </a>
       , often connect focus with the packaging of new, old, and contrasting information. Lambrecht in particular distinguishes three main types of focus constructions: predicate-focus structure, argument-focus structure, and sentence-focus structure. Focus has also been linked to other more general cognitive processes, including
       <a href="/wiki/Attention" title="Attention">
        attention
       </a>
       orientation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Generative_approaches">
        Generative approaches
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Generative approaches">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Standard generative approaches to grammar argue that
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Semantics" title="Semantics">
        semantics
       </a>
       cannot exchange information directly (
       <i>
        See Fig. 1
       </i>
       ). Therefore, syntactic mechanisms including features and
       <a href="/wiki/Transformational_grammar" title="Transformational grammar">
        transformations
       </a>
       include prosodic information regarding focus that is passed to the semantics and phonology.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SyntaxSplit.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="126" data-file-width="224" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/b/bb/SyntaxSplit.jpg/220px-SyntaxSplit.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/b/bb/SyntaxSplit.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SyntaxSplit.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Fig. 1 The Y-Model of Syntax, Semantics and Phonology
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Focus may be highlighted either prosodically or syntactically or both, depending on the language. In syntax this can be done assigning focus markers, as shown in (1), or by preposing as shown in (2):
      </p>
      <p>
       (1) I saw [JOHN]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       (2) [JOHN]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       , I saw.
      </p>
      <p>
       In (1), focus is marked syntactically with the subscripted ‘f’ which is realized phonologically by a nuclear
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_accent" title="Pitch accent">
        pitch accent
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Cleft_sentence" title="Cleft sentence">
        Clefting
       </a>
       induces an obligatory intonation break. Therefore, in (2), focus is marked via word order and a nuclear pitch accent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Focus also relates to phonology and has ramifications for how and where
       <a href="/wiki/Prosody_(linguistics)" title="Prosody (linguistics)">
        suprasegmental
       </a>
       information such as
       <a href="/wiki/Rhythm" title="Rhythm">
        rhythm
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       is encoded in the grammar, and in particular intonational tunes that mark focus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaver_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beaver-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Speakers can use pitch accents on syllables to indicate what word(s) are in focus. New words are often accented while given words are not. The accented word(s) forms the focus domain. However, not all of the words in a focus domain need be accented. (See
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk1_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk1-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk2_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild1_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       for rules on accent placement and focus-marking). The focus domain can be either
       <i>
        broad
       </i>
       , as shown in (3), or
       <i>
        narrow
       </i>
       , as shown in (4) and (5):
      </p>
      <p>
       (3) Did you see a grey dog or a cat? I saw [a grey
       <b>
        DOG
       </b>
       ]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       (4) Did you see a grey dog or a grey cat? I saw a grey [
       <b>
        DOG
       </b>
       ]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       (5) Did you see a grey dog or a black dog? I saw a [
       <b>
        GREY
       </b>
       ]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       dog.
      </p>
      <p>
       The question/answer paradigm shown in (3)–(5) has been utilized by a variety of theorists
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaver_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beaver-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roberts-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to illustrate the range of contexts a sentence containing focus can be used felicitously. Specifically, the question/answer paradigm has been used as a diagnostic for what counts as new information. For example, the focus pattern in (3) would be infelicitous if the question was ‘Did you see a grey dog or a black dog?’.
      </p>
      <p>
       In (3) and (4), the pitch accent is marked in bold. In (3), the pitch accent is placed on
       <i>
        dog
       </i>
       but the entire noun phrase
       <i>
        a grey dog
       </i>
       is under focus. In (4), the pitch accent is also placed on
       <i>
        dog
       </i>
       but only the noun
       <i>
        dog
       </i>
       is under focus. In (5), pitch accent is placed on
       <i>
        grey
       </i>
       and only the adjective
       <i>
        grey
       </i>
       is under focus.
      </p>
      <p>
       Historically, generative proposals made focus a feature bound to a single word within a sentence.
       <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
        Chomsky
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">
        Halle
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chomsky_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chomsky-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       formulated a Nuclear Stress Rule that proposed there to be a relation between the main stress of a sentence and a single constituent. Since this constituent is prominent sententially in a way that can contrast with lexical stress, this was originally referred to as "nuclear" stress. The purpose of this rule was to capture the intuition that within each sentence, there is one word in particular that is accented more prominently due to its importance - this is said to form the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sentence_nucleus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sentence nucleus (page does not exist)">
        nucleus
       </a>
       of that sentence.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:137px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LeftPeriphery_Rizzi_1997_eng.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="208" data-file-width="135" height="208" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/LeftPeriphery_Rizzi_1997_eng.png" width="135"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LeftPeriphery_Rizzi_1997_eng.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Left periphery structure, according to Rizzi (1997)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Focus was later suggested to be a structural position at the beginning of the sentence (or on the left periphery) in
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       such as Italian, as the lexical head of a Focus Phrase (or FP, following the
       <a href="/wiki/X-bar_theory" title="X-bar theory">
        X-bar theory
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Phrase_structure_grammar" title="Phrase structure grammar">
        phrase structure
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_Jackendoff" title="Ray Jackendoff">
        Jackendoff
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackendoff_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jackendoff-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Selkirk,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk1_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk1-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk2_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rooth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooth1_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rooth1-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooth2_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rooth2-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Krifka,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krifka1_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Krifka1-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Schwarzschild
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild1_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       argue that focus consists of a feature that is assigned to a node in the syntactic representation of a sentence. Because focus is now widely seen as corresponding between heavy stress, or nuclear pitch accent, this feature is often associated with the phonologically prominent element(s) of a sentence.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sound structure (
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonological
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetic
       </a>
       ) studies of focus are not as numerous, as relational language phenomena tend to be of greater interest to syntacticians and semanticists. But this may be changing: a recent study found that not only do focused words and phrases have a higher range of pitch compared to words in the same sentence but that words following the focus in both
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       were lower than normal in pitch and words before a focus are unaffected. The precise usages of focus in natural language are still uncertain. A continuum of possibilities could possibly be defined between precisely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enunciation" title="Enunciation">
        enunciated
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Staccato" title="Staccato">
        staccato
       </a>
       styles of speech based on variations in
       <a href="/wiki/Pragmatics" title="Pragmatics">
        pragmatics
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timing_(linguistics)" title="Timing (linguistics)">
        timing
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently, there are two central themes in research on focus in generative linguistics. First, given what words or expressions are prominent, what is the meaning of some sentence? Rooth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooth1_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rooth1-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jacobs,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobs_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobs-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Krifka,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krifka1_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Krifka1-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and von Stechow
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stechow_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stechow-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       claim that there are lexical items and construction specific-rules that refer directly to the notion of focus. Dryer,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dryer_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dryer-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Kadmon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kadmon_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kadmon-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Marti,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marti_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marti-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Roberts,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roberts_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roberts-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Schwarzschild,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild2_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild2-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vallduvi,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vallduvi_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vallduvi-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Williams
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Williams-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       argue for accounts in which general principles of discourse explain focus sensitivity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaver_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beaver-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Second, given the meaning and syntax of some sentence, what words or expressions are prominent?
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prominence_and_meaning">
        Prominence and meaning
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Prominence and meaning">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Focus directly affects the semantics, or meaning, of a sentence. Different ways of pronouncing the sentence affects the meaning, or, what the speaker intends to convey. Focus distinguishes one interpretation of a sentence from other interpretations of the same sentence that do not differ in word order, but may differ in the way in which the words are taken to relate to each other. To see the effects of focus on meaning, consider the following examples:
      </p>
      <p>
       (6) John only introduced Bill to SUE.
      </p>
      <p>
       In (6), accent is placed on Sue. There are two readings of (6) - broad focus shown in (7) and narrow focus shown in (8):
      </p>
      <p>
       (7) John only [introduced Bill to SUE]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       (8) John only introduced Bill to [SUE]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The meaning of (7) can be summarized as
       <i>
        the only thing John did was introduce Bill to Sue
       </i>
       . The meaning of (8) can be summarized as
       <i>
        the only person to whom John introduced Bill is Sue
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In both (7) and (8), focus is associated with the focus sensitive expression
       <i>
        only
       </i>
       . This is known as association with focus. The class of focus sensitive expressions in which focus can be associated with includes exclusives (
       <i>
        only
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        just
       </i>
       ) non-scalar additives (
       <i>
        merely
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        too
       </i>
       ) scalar additives (
       <i>
        also
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        even
       </i>
       ), particularlizers (
       <i>
        in particular
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        for example
       </i>
       ), intensifiers, quantificational adverbs, quantificational determiners, sentential connectives, emotives, counterfactuals, superlatives, negation and generics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beaver_14-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beaver-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is claimed that focus operators must
       <a href="/wiki/C-command" title="C-command">
        c-command
       </a>
       their focus.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alternative_semantics">
        Alternative semantics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Alternative semantics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Beginning with Rooth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooth1_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rooth1-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rooth2_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rooth2-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the effects of focus on semantics can be said to be the introduction of a
       <a href="/wiki/Set_(mathematics)" title="Set (mathematics)">
        set
       </a>
       of alternatives that contrasts with the ordinary semantic meaning of a sentence. Consider the following example:
      </p>
      <p>
       (9) Mary only likes [SUE]
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Focus_marking">
        Focus marking
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Focus marking">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It has been claimed that
       <i>
        new
       </i>
       information in the discourse is accented while
       <i>
        given
       </i>
       information is not. Generally, the properties of
       <i>
        new
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        given
       </i>
       are referred to as a word's discourse status. Definitions of
       <i>
        new
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        given
       </i>
       vary. Halliday
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Halliday_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Halliday-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       defines
       <i>
        given
       </i>
       as “
       <a href="/wiki/Anaphora_(linguistics)" title="Anaphora (linguistics)">
        anaphorically
       </a>
       ” recoverable, while
       <i>
        new
       </i>
       is defined to be “textually and situationally non-derivable information”. To illustrate this point, consider the following discourse in (12) and (13):
      </p>
      <p>
       (12) Why don’t you have some French TOAST?
      </p>
      <p>
       (13) I’ve forgotten how to MAKE French toast.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladd_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladd-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In (13) we note that the verb
       <i>
        make
       </i>
       is not given by the sentence in (12). It is discourse new. Therefore, it is available for accentuation. However,
       <i>
        toast
       </i>
       in (13) is given in (12). Therefore, it is not available for accentuation. As previously mentioned, pitch accenting can relate to focus. Accented words are often said to be in focus or F-marked often represented by F-markers. The relationship between accent placement is mediated through the discourse status of particular syntactic nodes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The percolation of F-markings in a syntactic tree is sensitive to argument structure and head-phrase relations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild1_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Selkirk_and_accent_placement">
        Selkirk and accent placement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Selkirk and accent placement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Selkirk
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk1_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk1-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk2_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       develops an explicit account of how F-marking propagates up
       <a href="/wiki/Parse_tree" title="Parse tree">
        syntactic trees
       </a>
       . Accenting indicates F-marking. F-marking projects up a given syntactic tree such that both lexical items, i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Terminal_and_nonterminal_symbols" title="Terminal and nonterminal symbols">
        terminal nodes
       </a>
       and phrasal levels, i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Terminal_and_nonterminal_symbols" title="Terminal and nonterminal symbols">
        nonterminal nodes
       </a>
       , can be F-marked. Specifically, a set of rules determines how and where F-marking occurs in the syntax. These rules are shown in (1) and (2):
      </p>
      <p>
       (14)
       <b>
        Basic Rule
       </b>
       : An accented word is f-marked.
      </p>
      <p>
       (15)
       <b>
        Focus Projection
       </b>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          a. F-marking the head of a phrase licenses F-marking of the phrase.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          b. F-marking of the internal argument of a head licenses the F-marking of the head.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          c. F-marking of the antecedent of a trace left by NP or wh-movement licenses F-marking of the trace.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       To see how (14) and (15) apply, consider the following example:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <b>
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judy_(personal_name)" title="Judy (personal name)">
            Judy
           </a>
          </b>
          <sub>
           f
          </sub>
          [adopted
          <sub>
           f
          </sub>
          a
          <b>
           parrot
          </b>
          <sub>
           f
          </sub>
          ]
          <sub>
           f
          </sub>
          ]
          <sub>
           foc
          </sub>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
            [33]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Because there is no rule in (14) or (15) that licenses F-marking to the direct object from any other node, the direct object
       <i>
        parrot
       </i>
       must be accented as indicated in bold. Rule (15b) allows F-marking to project from the direct object to the head verb
       <i>
        adopted
       </i>
       . Rule (15a) allows F-marking to project from the head verb to the VP
       <i>
        adopted a parrot
       </i>
       . Selkirk
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk1_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk1-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk2_16-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       assumes the subject
       <i>
        Judy
       </i>
       is accented if F-marked as indicated in bold.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Schwarzschild_and_accent_placement">
        Schwarzschild and accent placement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Schwarzschild and accent placement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Schwarzschild
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild1_17-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Responses">
        Responses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Focus_(linguistics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Responses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       Recent empirical work by German
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggests that both Selkirk’s
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk1_15-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk1-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Selkirk2_16-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Selkirk2-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Schwarzschild’s
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schwarzschild1_17-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schwarzschild1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       theory of accentuation and F-marking makes incorrect predictions. Consider the following context:
      </p>
      <p>
       (26) Are the children playing their game?
      </p>
      <p>
       (27) Paul took down their tent that they play their game in.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been noted that prepositions are intrinsically weak and do not readily take accent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ladd_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ladd-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, both Selkirk and Schwarzschild predict that in the narrow focus context, an accent will occur at most on the preposition in (27) as shown in (28):
      </p>
      <p>
       (28) Paul took down their tent that they [play their game [in
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       <a href="/wiki/Trace_(linguistics)" title="Trace (linguistics)">
        t
       </a>
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       ]
       <sub>
        foc
       </sub>
       ].
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, the production experiment reported in German
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_33-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-German-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       showed that subjects are more likely to accent verbs or nouns as opposed to prepositions in the narrow focused context, thus ruling out accent patterns shown in (28). German
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       argue for a stochastic constraint-based grammar similar to Anttila
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Antilla_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Antilla-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Boersma
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Boersma_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Boersma-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that more fluidly accounts for how speakers accent words in discourse.
      </p>
