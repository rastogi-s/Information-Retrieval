      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetics" title="Phonetics">
        phonetics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        semivowel
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        glide
       </b>
       , also known as a
       <b>
        non-syllabic vocoid
       </b>
       , is a sound that is phonetically similar to a
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowel
       </a>
       sound but functions as the
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllable
       </a>
       boundary, rather than as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syllable_nucleus" title="Syllable nucleus">
        nucleus
       </a>
       of a syllable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Examples of semivowels in English are the consonants
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        w
       </i>
       , in
       <i>
        yes
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        west
       </i>
       . Written
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/j/: 'y' in 'yes'">
            j
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'w' in 'wind'">
            w
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        IPA
       </a>
       , y and w are near to the vowels
       <i>
        ee
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oo
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        seen
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        moon,
       </i>
       written
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">
            uː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        IPA
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Semivowel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Semivowels form a subclass of
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximants
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crystal_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-crystal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MartinezCeldran_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MartinezCeldran-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although "semivowel" and "approximant" are sometimes treated as synonymous,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       most authors agree that not all approximants are semivowels although the exact details may vary from author to author. For example,
       <a href="#CITEREFLadefogedMaddieson1996">
        Ladefoged &amp; Maddieson (1996)
       </a>
       do not consider the
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_approximant" title="Labiodental approximant">
        labiodental approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʋ]
       </span>
       to be a semivowel
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lm323_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lm323-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while
       <a href="#CITEREFMartínez_Celdrán2004">
        Martínez Celdrán (2004)
       </a>
       proposes that it should be considered one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mc8_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mc8-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       , the diacritic attached to non-syllabic vowel letters is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">
         <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_symbols_in_Unicode" title="Phonetic symbols in Unicode">
          U+
         </a>
         032F
        </span>
       </span>
       <span style="font-size:125%">
        ̯
       </span>
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">
        COMBINING
        <a href="/wiki/Inverted_breve" title="Inverted breve">
         INVERTED BREVE
        </a>
        BELOW
       </span>
       . When there is no room for the tack under a letter, it may be written above, using
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">

       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_approximant" title="Pharyngeal approximant">
        pharyngeal approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʕ̞]
       </span>
       is also equivalent to the semivowel articulation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Open_back_unrounded_vowel" title="Open back unrounded vowel">
        open back unrounded vowel
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɑ̯]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lm323_5-1">

         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>

       </a>

       </a>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </a>

        </a>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɨ]
       </span>

       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrast_with_vowels">
        Contrast with vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Semivowel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contrast with vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Semivowels, by definition, contrast with vowels by being non-syllabic. In addition, they are usually shorter than vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-crystal_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-crystal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In languages as diverse as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amharic_language" title="Amharic language">
        Amharic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yoruba_language" title="Yoruba language">
        Yoruba
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Zuni_language" title="Zuni language">
        Zuni
       </a>
       , semivowels are produced with a narrower constriction in the vocal tract than their corresponding vowels.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lm323_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-lm323-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, semivowels may be phonemically equivalent with vowels. For example, the English word
       <i>
        fly
       </i>
       can be considered either as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_syllable" title="Open syllable">
        open syllable
       </a>
       ending in a
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthong
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [flaɪ̯]
       </span>
       or as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Closed_syllable" title="Closed syllable">
        closed syllable
       </a>
       ending in a consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [flaj]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is unusual for a language to contrast a semivowel and a diphthong containing an equivalent vowel,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       but
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       contrasts the diphthong
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e̯a/
       </span>
       with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ja/
       </span>
       , a perceptually similar approximant-vowel sequence. The diphthong is analyzed as a single segment, and the approximant-vowel sequence is analyzed as two separate segments.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to phonological justifications for the distinction (such as the diphthong alternating with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       in singular-plural pairs), there are phonetic differences between the pair:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ja/
        </span>
        has a greater duration than
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e̯a/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        The transition between the two elements is longer and faster for
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ja/
        </span>
        than
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e̯a/
        </span>
        with the former having a higher F2 onset (greater constriction of the articulators).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although a phonological parallel exists between
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o̯a/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /wa/
       </span>
       , the production and perception of phonetic contrasts between the two is much weaker, likely because of lower lexical load for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /wa/
       </span>
       , which is limited largely to loanwords from
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , and a difficulty in maintaining contrasts between two back rounded semivowels in comparison to front ones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Contrast_with_fricatives.2Fspirant_approximants">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrast_with_fricatives/spirant_approximants">
        Contrast with fricatives/spirant approximants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Semivowel&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contrast with fricatives/spirant approximants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the standard definitions, semivowels (such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       ) contrast with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricatives
       </a>
       (such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       ) in that fricatives produce turbulence, but semivowels do not. In discussing
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , Martínez Celdrán suggests setting up a third category of "spirant approximant", contrasting both with semivowel approximants and with fricatives.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the spirant approximant is more constricted (having a lower
       <a href="/wiki/Formant" title="Formant">
        F2
       </a>
       amplitude), longer, and unspecified for rounding (
       <i>
        viuda
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈb
        <b>
         ju
        </b>
        ða]
       </span>
       'widow' vs.
       <i>
        ayuda
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈ
        <b>
         ʝʷu
        </b>
        ða]
       </span>
       'help'),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the distributional overlap is limited. The spirant approximant can only appear in the syllable onset (including word-initially, where the semivowel never appears). The two overlap in distribution after
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       :
       <i>
        enyesar
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ẽɲ
        <b>
         ɟʝ
        </b>
        eˈsaɾ]
       </span>
       ('to plaster')
       <i>
        aniego
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ãˈn
        <b>
         j
        </b>
        eɣo]
       </span>
       ('flood')
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and although there is dialectal and ideolectal variation, speakers may also exhibit other near-minimal pairs like
       <i>
        ab
        <b>
         ye
        </b>
        cto
       </i>
       ('abject') vs
       <i>
        ab
        <b>
         ie
        </b>
        rto
       </i>
       ('opened').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One potential minimal pair (depending on dialect) is
       <i>
        ya visto
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <b>
         (ɟ)ʝa
        </b>
        ˈβisto]
       </span>
       ('already seen') vs
       <i>
        y ha visto
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <b>
         ja
        </b>
        ˈβisto]
       </span>
       ('and he has seen').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Again, it is not present in all dialects. Other dialects differ in either merging the two or in enhancing the contrast by moving the former to another
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
