      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Language" title="Spanish Language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       grammar,
       <i>
        <b>
         voseo
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <small>
        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [boˈse.o]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       as a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        second person
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        singular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronoun
       </a>
       , including its
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugational verb forms
       </a>
       in many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialects_of_Spanish" title="Dialects of Spanish">
        dialects
       </a>
       . In dialects that have it, it is used either instead of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction#Spanish" title="T-V distinction">
         tú
        </a>
       </i>
       , or alongside it. Voseo is seldom taught to students of Spanish as a second language, and its precise usage varies across different regions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, in recent years it has become more accepted across the Spanish-speaking world as a valid part of regional dialects. Use of
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       for the second person singular (the form that is considered
       <i>
        standard
       </i>
       ) is known as
       <i>
        tuteo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is used extensively as the second person singular in
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Media_Luna" title="Media Luna">
        Eastern Bolivia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguayan_Spanish" title="Paraguayan Spanish">
        Paraguayan Spanish
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Spanish" title="Central American Spanish">
        Central American Spanish
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">
        El Salvador
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guatemala" title="Guatemala">
        Guatemala
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       , southern parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">
        Chiapas
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       had not been traditionally used in formal writing except in Argentina, Paraguay and Uruguay. This gradually changed in
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       , where even the most prestigious media networks and press began to use the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Dates_and_numbers#Chronological_items" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers">
          <span title="The time period mentioned near this tag is ambiguous. (April 2017)">
           when?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       reflecting the informal address in Spanish as opposed to the formal address of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . Nowadays it is very common to see billboards and other advertising media using
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       . In the dialect of Argentina, Paraguay and Uruguay (known as 'Rioplatense Spanish'),
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is also the standard form for use in television media.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is present in other countries as a regionalism, for instance in the Maracucho Spanish of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zulia_State" title="Zulia State">
        Zulia State
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuelan_Spanish" title="Venezuelan Spanish">
        Venezuelan Spanish
       </a>
       ), in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azuero_peninsula" title="Azuero peninsula">
        Azuero peninsula
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       , in various
       <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Colombia" title="Departments of Colombia">
        departments
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
        Ecuador
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_of_Ecuador#La_Sierra_(the_highlands)" title="Geography of Ecuador">
        Sierra
       </a>
       down to
       <a href="/wiki/Esmeraldas,_Ecuador" title="Esmeraldas, Ecuador">
        Esmeraldas
       </a>
       ). In
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       is present in some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andean" title="Andean">
        Andean
       </a>
       regions and
       <a href="/wiki/Cajamarca" title="Cajamarca">
        Cajamarca
       </a>
       , but the younger generations have ceased to use it (except parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Quito" title="Quito">
        Quito
       </a>
       ). It is also present in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ladino_language" title="Ladino language">
        Ladino
       </a>
       (spoken by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic_Jew" title="Sephardic Jew">
        Sephardic Jews
       </a>
       throughout
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Balkans" title="The Balkans">
        the Balkans
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       ), where it replaces
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       . In the United States,
       <a href="/wiki/Salvadoran_Americans" title="Salvadoran Americans">
        Salvadoran Americans
       </a>
       are by far the largest
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       users followed by other Central Americans, Hondurans, Nicaraguans, and Costa Ricans.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Needs a source showing/explaining how much these countries use voseo in the United States. (June 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Voseo can also be found in the context of using verb conjugations for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       as the subject pronoun (
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo#Verbal_Voseo_&amp;_Pronominal_Voseo" title="Voseo">
        verbal voseo
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as in the case of
       <a href="/wiki/Chilean_Spanish" title="Chilean Spanish">
        Chilean Spanish
       </a>
       , where this form coexists with the ordinary form of voseo.
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been claimed that the countries that use
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       today have in common that they were geographically isolated during colonial times; regions with good communications with Spain at that time — today's
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       — do not use
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       , or its use is confined to remote areas — this is the case in
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
        Ecuador
       </a>
       . In colonial times, there was, by law, no regular boat communication between today's
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Originally a second-person plural,
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       came to be used as a more polite second-person singular pronoun to be used among one's familiar friends. The following extract from a late 18th-century textbook is illustrative of usage at the time:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        We seldom make use in Spanish of the second Person Singular or Plural, but when through a great familiarity among friends, or speaking to God, or a wife and husband to themselves, or a father and mother to their children, or to servants.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         Examples
        </span>
        .
       </p>
       <i>
        O Dios, sois vos mi Padre verdadéro
       </i>
       , O God, thou art my true Father;
       <i>
        Tú eres un buen amígo
       </i>
       , Thou art a good friend.
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Raymundo del Pueyo,
         <i>
          A New Spanish Grammar, or the Elements of the Spanish Language
         </i>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
          <a href="#cite_note-4">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The standard formal way to address a person one was not on familiar terms with was to address such a person as
       <i>
        vuestra merced
       </i>
       ("your grace" originally abbreviated as
       <i>
        v.m.
       </i>
       ) in the singular and
       <i>
        vuestras mercedes
       </i>
       in the plural. Because of the literal meaning of these forms, they were accompanied by the corresponding third-person verb forms. Other formal forms of address included
       <i>
        vuestra excelencia
       </i>
       ("your excellence" abbreviated as
       <i>
        ussencia
       </i>
       ) and
       <i>
        vuestra señoria
       </i>
       ("your lordship/ladyship" abbreviated as
       <i>
        ussia
       </i>
       ). Today, both
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       are considered to be informal pronouns, with
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       being somewhat synonymous with
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       in regions where both are used. This was the situation when the Spanish language was brought to the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">
        Río de la Plata
       </a>
       area (around
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Montevideo" title="Montevideo">
        Montevideo
       </a>
       ) and to Chile.
      </p>
      <p>
       In time,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       lost currency in Spain but survived in a number of countries in Spanish-speaking America: Argentina, Paraguay, Bolivia (east), Uruguay, Costa Rica, Guatemala, Nicaragua, and some smaller areas; it is not found, or only found in internally remote areas (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Chiapas" title="Chiapas">
        Chiapas
       </a>
       ) in the countries historically best connected with Spain: Mexico, Panama, Cuba, Dominican Republic, Venezuela, Colombia, Perú, and
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        Vuestra merced
       </i>
       evolved into
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        vuestra merced &gt; usarced &gt; usted
       </i>
       ; in fact, "
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       " is still abbreviated as either
       <i>
        Vd
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Ud
       </i>
       ). Note that the term
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is a combined form of
       <i>
        vos otros
       </i>
       (meaning literally "ye/you others"), while the term
       <i>
        nosotros
       </i>
       comes from
       <i>
        nos otros
       </i>
       ("we/us others").
      </p>
      <p>
       In the first half of the 19th century the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was as prevalent in Chile as it was in Argentina. The current limitation of the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       in Chile is attributed to a campaign to eradicate it by the
       <a href="/wiki/Education_in_Chile" title="Education in Chile">
        Chilean education system
       </a>
       . The campaign was initiated by
       <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Bello" title="Andrés Bello">
        Andrés Bello
       </a>
       who considered the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       a manifestation of lack of education.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liuzete_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Liuzete-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Vos_as_a_replacement_for_other_forms_of_t.C3.BA">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vos_as_a_replacement_for_other_forms_of_tú">
        <i>
         Vos
        </i>
        as a replacement for other forms of
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Vos as a replacement for other forms of tú">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The independent disjunctive pronoun
       <i>
        ti
       </i>
       is also replaced by
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       . That is,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is both
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative
       </a>
       and the form to use after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       . Therefore,
       <i>
        para ti
       </i>
       "for you" becomes
       <i>
        para vos
       </i>
       , etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       The preposition-pronoun compound
       <i>
        contigo
       </i>
       "with you" becomes
       <i>
        con vos
       </i>
       .
       <br/>
       The direct and indirect object form
       <i>
        te
       </i>
       remains the same, unlike in the case of
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       , where it becomes
       <i>
        os
       </i>
       .
      </p>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>
       &lt;
       <i>

       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tuyo(s)
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        tuya(s)
       </i>
       &gt; rather than with
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       &lt;
       <i>
        vuestro(s)
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vuestra(s)
       </i>
       &gt;.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation_with_vos">
        Conjugation with
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Conjugation with vos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       All modern
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       conjugations derive from Old Spanish second person plural
       <i>
        -ades
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -edes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ides
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -odes
       </i>
       (as in
       <i>
        sodes
       </i>
       , 'you are').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapesa_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lapesa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 14th and 15th centuries saw an evolution of these conjugations, with
       <i>
        -ades
       </i>
       originally giving
       <i>
        -áis
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -edes
       </i>
       giving
       <i>
        -és
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapesa_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lapesa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garcia_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-garcia-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        -ides
       </i>
       giving
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        -odes
       </i>
       giving
       <i>
        -óis
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapesa_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-lapesa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Soon analogous forms
       <i>
        -ás
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -éis
       </i>
       appeared.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapesa_6-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-lapesa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hence the variety of forms the contemporary American
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       adopts, some varieties featuring a generalized monophthong (most of them), some a generalized diphthong (e.g. Venezuela), and some combining monophthongs and diphthongs, depending on the conjugation (e.g. Chile). In the most general, monophthongized, conjugation paradigm, a difference between
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       forms and respective tuteo forms is visible exclusively in the present indicative, imperative and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       , and, most of the time, in the
       <i>
        preterite
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-garcia_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-garcia-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Below is a comparison table of the conjugation of several verbs for
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , and next to them the one for
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       , the informal second person plural currently used orally only in Spain; in oratory or legal language (highly formal forms of Spanish) it is used outside of Spain. Verb forms that agree with
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stressed
       </a>
       on the last syllable, causing the loss of the stem diphthong in those verbs, such as
       <i>
        poder
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        venir
       </i>
       , which are
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs#dipthongization" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
        stem-changing
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       General conjugation is the one that is most widely accepted and used in various countries such as Argentina, Uruguay, Paraguay, parts of Bolivia, Ecuador, and Colombia, as well as Central American countries.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some Uruguayan speakers combine the pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       with the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       conjugation (for example,
       <i>
        tú sabés
       </i>
       ). Conversely, speakers in some other places where both
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are used combine
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       with the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       conjugation (for example,
       <i>
        vos sabes
       </i>
       ). This is a frequent occurrence in the Argentine province of
       <a href="/wiki/Santiago_del_Estero_Province" title="Santiago del Estero Province">
        Santiago del Estero
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The verb forms employed with
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are also different in Chilean Spanish: Chileans with
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       delete the final
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       from the final diphthong
       <i>
        -áis
       </i>
       (and
       <i>
        -ois
       </i>
       ): (
       <i>
        vos/tú soi /erís
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        vos/tú estái
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In the case of the ending
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       (such as in
       <i>
        comís, podís, vivís, erís, venís
       </i>
       ), the final
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       is not totally dropped. Rather, in most cases, especially before a consonant, an aspiration similar to the 'h' sound in English is still audible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both Chilean Spanish and Venezuelan Maracucho Spanish are notable in that they preserve the diphthongized plural verb forms in all tenses, as still used with
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       in Spain. The same happens in the Azuero peninsula of Panama.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       , the
       <i>
        -áis
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -éis
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       , &amp;
       <i>
        -ois
       </i>
       endings are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /aʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eʃ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /iʃ/
       </span>
       , &amp;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /oʃ/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In Chile, it is much more usual to use
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       verb conjugation (
       <i>
        <b>
         tú
        </b>
        sabís
       </i>
       ). The use of
       <i>
        pronominal vos
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <b>
         vos
        </b>
        sabís
       </i>
       ) is reserved for very informal situations and may even be considered vulgar in some cases.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_Indicative">
        Present Indicative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Present Indicative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ol>
       <li>
        General conjugation: the final
        <b>
         -r
        </b>
        of the infinitive is replaced by
        <b>
         -s
        </b>
        , and an
        <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">
         acute accent
        </a>
        is added to the final vowel (i.e. the one preceding the final
        <i>
         <b>
          -s
         </b>
        </i>
        ) to keep the stress.
       </li>
       <li>
        Chilean:
        <ol>
         <li>
          the
          <b>
           -ar
          </b>
          ending of the infinitive is replaced by
          <b>
           -ái
          </b>
         </li>
         <li>
          both
          <b>
           -er
          </b>
          and
          <b>
           -ir
          </b>
          are replaced by
          <b>
           -ís
          </b>
          , which sounds more like
          <i>
           -íh
          </i>
          .
         </li>
        </ol>
       </li>
       <li>
        Venezuelan (Zulian): practically the same ending as modern Spanish
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        , yet with the final -S being aspirated so that:
        <i>
         -áis
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -éis
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -ís
        </i>
        sound like
        <i>
         -áih
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -éih
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         -íh
        </i>
        (phonetically resembling Chilean).
       </li>
      </ol>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Affirmative_imperative">
        Affirmative imperative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Affirmative imperative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       also differs in its affirmative
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperative
       </a>
       conjugation from both
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       . Specifically, the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       imperative is formed by dropping the final
       <i>
        -r
       </i>
       from the infinitive, but keeping the stress on the last syllable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lapesa_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-lapesa-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only verb that is irregular in this regard is
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       ; its
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       imperative is not used, with
       <i>
        andá
       </i>
       <b>
        *
       </b>
       (the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       imperative of
       <i>
        andar
       </i>
       ) being generally used instead; except for the Argentine province of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tucum%C3%A1n" title="Tucumán">
        Tucumán
       </a>
       , where the imperative
       <i>
        ite
       </i>
       is used, though considered to be non-standard Spanish.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subjunctive">
        Subjunctive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Subjunctive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In most places where voseo is used, it is applied also in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_la_Plata" title="Río de la Plata">
        Río de la Plata
       </a>
       region, both the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       -conjugation and the voseo conjugation are found, the
       <i>
        tú-
       </i>
       form being more common. In this variety, some studies have shown a pragmatic difference between the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       -form and the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       -form, such that the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       form carries information about the speaker's belief state, and can be stigmatized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, in Central America the subjunctive and negative command form is
       <i>
        no mintás
       </i>
       and in Chile it is
       <i>
        no mintái
       </i>
       ; however in Río de la Plata both
       <i>
        no mientas
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        no mintás
       </i>
       are found.
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
         Real Academia Española
        </a>
       </i>
       models its
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       conjugation tables with the most frequent, unstigmatized Río de la Plata usage and therefore omits the subjunctive voseo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DRAE-example_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DRAE-example-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_voseo_and_pronominal_voseo">
        Verbal voseo and pronominal voseo
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Verbal voseo and pronominal voseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        'Verbal voseo' refers to the use of the verb conjugation of
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        regardless of which pronoun is used.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Verbal voseo with a pronoun other than
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        is widespread in Chile, in which case one would use the pronoun
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        and the verb conjugation of
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        at the same time. E.g.: "tú venís", "tú escribís", "tú podís", "tú sabís", "tú vai", "tú estái".
       </dd>
       <dd>
        There are some partially rare cases of a similar sort of verbal voseo in Uruguay where one would say for example "
        <i>
         tú podés
        </i>
        ", "
        <i>
         tú sabés
        </i>
        ".
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        'Pronominal voseo' is the use of the pronoun
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        regardless of verb conjugation.
       </li>
      </ul>

      </p>

         Chile
        </a>

        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         vos
        </i>

      </p>

        <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">

        </a>

        </i>

        <i>
         tú
        </i>

        </i>

        <i>
         tú
        </i>

        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        remains the pronoun of maximum familiarity and solidarity, and also lack of respect.

        </i>
        .

         Usted
        </i>

        <i>

        </i>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synchronic_analysis_of_Chilean_and_River_Plate_verbal_voseos">
        Synchronic analysis of Chilean and River Plate verbal
        <i>
         voseos
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Synchronic analysis of Chilean and River Plate verbal voseos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The traditional assumption that Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms are derived from those corresponding to
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       has been challenged as synchronically inadequate in a Baquero and Westphal article,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on the grounds that it requires at least six different rules, including three monophthongization processes that completely lack phonological motivation. Alternatively, the article argues that the Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms are synchronically derived from underlying representations that coincide with those corresponding to the non-honorific second person singular
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       . The proposed theory requires the use of only one special rule in the case of Chilean
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       . This rule plus other rules that are independently justified in the language make it possible to synchronically derive all the Chilean and River Plate
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       verb forms in a straightforward manner. The article additionally solves the problem posed by the alternate verbal forms of Chilean
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       like the future indicative (e.g.
       <i>
        bailaríh
       </i>
       'you will dance'), the present indicative forms of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        habíh
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        hai
       </i>
       'you have'), and the present indicative of
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        soi
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        eríh
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        eréi
       </i>
       'you are'), without resorting to any ad hoc rules. The theoretical framework of the article is that of classic generative phonology.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Attitudes">
        Attitudes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voseo&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Attitudes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some countries, the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is used with family and friends (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        T-form
       </a>
       ), like
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       in other varieties of Spanish, and contrasts with the respectful
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       (V-form used with third person) which is used with strangers, elderly and people of higher
       <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">
        socioeconomic status
       </a>
       ; appropriate usage varies by dialect. In Central America,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       can be used among those considered equals, while
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       maintains its respectful usage. In Ladino, the pronoun
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       is completely absent, so the use of
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       with strangers and elders is the standard.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Voseo
       </i>
       was long considered a reprehensible practice by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">
        prescriptivist
       </a>
       grammarians (with the idea that only Castilian Spanish was good Spanish),
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       but it is now regarded simply as a local variant.
      </p>
