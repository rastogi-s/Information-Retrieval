      <p>
       <b>
        Neapolitan
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Exonym_and_endonym" title="Exonym and endonym">
        autonym
       </a>
       : (
       <i>
        ’o n
       </i>
       )
       <i>
        napulitano
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Neapolitan" title="Help:IPA/Neapolitan">
         [(o n)napuliˈtɑːnə]
        </a>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         napoletano
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a sect of
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        closely related dialects
       </a>
       spoken across much of
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">
        southern Italy
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GuardianDatasets_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GuardianDatasets-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not named specifically after the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
        Naples
       </a>
       , but rather the homonymous
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">
        Kingdom
       </a>
       that once covered most of the area; the city was in fact its capital. On October 14, 2008, a law by the Region of
       <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">
        Campania
       </a>
       stated that Neapolitan was to be protected.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-denaro_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-denaro-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While the term "Neapolitan language" is used in this article to refer to the language group of related dialects found in southern continental Italy, it may also refer more specifically to the dialect of the Neapolitan language spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Naples_metropolitan_area" title="Naples metropolitan area">
        Naples area
       </a>
       or in
       <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">
        Campania
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Neapolitan has had a significant influence on the intonation of
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       , of the
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       region of
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       , and the whole of Uruguay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">
        Distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Neapolitan dialects are distributed throughout most of continental
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Italy" title="Southern Italy">
        southern Italy
       </a>
       , historically united during the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Naples" title="Kingdom of Naples">
        Kingdom of Naples
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Two_Sicilies" title="Kingdom of the Two Sicilies">
        Kingdom of the Two Sicilies
       </a>
       , specifically southern
       <a href="/wiki/Lazio" title="Lazio">
        Lazio
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Gaeta" title="Gaeta">
        Gaeta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sora,_Lazio" title="Sora, Lazio">
        Sora
       </a>
       districts), southern
       <a href="/wiki/Marche" title="Marche">
        Marche
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Abruzzo" title="Abruzzo">
        Abruzzo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Molise" title="Molise">
        Molise
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Basilicata" title="Basilicata">
        Basilicata
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Campania" title="Campania">
        Campania
       </a>
       (Naples), northern and central
       <a href="/wiki/Apulia" title="Apulia">
        Apulia
       </a>
       , and northernmost
       <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">
        Calabria
       </a>
       . The dialects are part of a varied
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
        dialect continuum
       </a>
       , so the varieties in southern Lazio, Marche, Abruzzo, Molise, Apulia, Lucania and Calabria can typically be recognizable as regional groups of dialects. In western Abruzzo and Lazio the dialects give way to
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Italian" title="Central Italian">
        Central Italian
       </a>
       dialects such as
       <a href="/wiki/Romanesco_dialect" title="Romanesco dialect">
        Romanesco
       </a>
       . In central Calabria and southern Apulia, the dialects give way to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian language
       </a>
       . Largely due to massive southern Italian migration in the 20th century, there are also numbers of speakers in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_diaspora" title="Italian diaspora">
        Italian diaspora
       </a>
       communities in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       . However, in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       traditional Neapolitan has had considerable contact with English, and is significantly different from contemporary Neapolitan spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
        Naples
       </a>
       . English words are often used in place of Neapolitan words, especially among second-generation speakers.
      </p>

      <p>
       The southernmost regions of Italy—most of Calabria and southern Apulia, as well as Sicily—are home to
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian
       </a>
       rather than Neapolitan.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giambattista_Basile.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="319" data-file-width="250" height="281" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Giambattista_Basile.jpg/220px-Giambattista_Basile.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2e/Giambattista_Basile.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giambattista_Basile.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Basile" title="Giambattista Basile">
          Giambattista Basile
         </a>
         (1566–1632), author of a
         <a href="/wiki/Pentamerone" title="Pentamerone">
          collection of fairy tales
         </a>
         in Neapolitan that includes the earliest known versions of
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Rapunzel" title="Rapunzel">
           Rapunzel
          </a>
         </i>
         and
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Cinderella" title="Cinderella">
           Cinderella
          </a>
         </i>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Neapolitan is generally considered one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian languages
       </a>
       . There are notable differences among the various dialects, but they are all generally mutually intelligible.
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and Neapolitan are of variable mutual comprehensibility, depending on factors that are both affective and linguistic. There are notable grammatical differences, such as Neapolitan having nouns in the neuter form and a unique plural formation as well as historical phonological developments, which often obscure the cognacy of lexical items.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its evolution has been similar to that of Italian and other Romance languages from their roots in
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       . It may reflect a pre-Latin
       <a href="/wiki/Oscan_language" title="Oscan language">
        Oscan
       </a>
       influence, in the pronunciation of the
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       sound as an
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       sound (
       <a href="/wiki/Rhotacism_(sound_change)" title="Rhotacism (sound change)">
        rhotacism
       </a>
       ) at the beginning of a word or between two vowels ("doje" or "duje" (two, respectively feminine and masculine form), pronounced, and often spelled, as "roje"/"ruje",
       <i>
        vedé
       </i>
       (to see), pronounced as "veré", and often spelled so, also
       <i>
        cadé/caré
       </i>
       (to fall) and
       <i>
        Madonna/Maronna
       </i>
       ). Another purported Oscan influence is the historical assimilation of the consonant cluster /nd/ as /nn/, pronounced [nː] (it generally is reflected in spelling more consistently) ("
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/munno#Neapolitan" title="wikt:munno">
          munno
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       " ('world', Italian "
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/mondo#Italian" title="wikt:mondo">
          mondo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       "), "
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quanno#Neapolitan" title="wikt:quanno">
          quanno
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       " ('when', Italian "
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quando#Italian" title="wikt:quando">
          quando
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ")), along with the development of /mb/ as /mm/ (
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tammuro#Neapolitan" title="wikt:tammuro">
          tammuro
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       (drum), Italian
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/tamburo#Italian" title="wikt:tamburo">
          tamburo
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ), also consistently reflected in spelling. Other effects of the Oscan
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">
        substratum
       </a>
       are postulated, but substratum claims are highly controversial. In addition, the language was also affected by
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       . The language had never been standardised, and the word for
       <i>
        tree
       </i>
       has three different spellings:
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arbero#Neapolitan" title="wikt:arbero">
          arbero
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/arvero#Neapolitan" title="wikt:arvero">
          arvero
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%C3%A0varo#Neapolitan" title="wikt:àvaro">
          àvaro
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Neapolitan has enjoyed a rich literary, musical and theatrical history (notably
       <a href="/wiki/Giambattista_Basile" title="Giambattista Basile">
        Giambattista Basile
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eduardo_De_Filippo" title="Eduardo De Filippo">
        Eduardo De Filippo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Salvatore_Di_Giacomo" title="Salvatore Di Giacomo">
        Salvatore Di Giacomo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tot%C3%B2" title="Totò">
        Totò
       </a>
       ). Thanks to this heritage and the musical work of
       <a href="/wiki/Renato_Carosone" title="Renato Carosone">
        Renato Carosone
       </a>
       in the 1950s, Neapolitan is still in use in popular music, even gaining national popularity in the songs of
       <a href="/wiki/Pino_Daniele" title="Pino Daniele">
        Pino Daniele
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Nuova_Compagnia_di_Canto_Popolare" title="Nuova Compagnia di Canto Popolare">
        Nuova Compagnia di Canto Popolare
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The language has no official status within Italy and is not taught in schools. The
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Naples_Federico_II" title="University of Naples Federico II">
        University of Naples Federico II
       </a>
       offers (from 2003) courses in Campanian Dialectology at the faculty of Sociology, whose actual aim is not teaching students to speak the language, but studying its history, usage, literature and social role. There are also ongoing legislative attempts at the national level to have it recognized as an official
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">
        minority language
       </a>
       of Italy. It is however a recognized
       <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
        ISO
       </a>
       639 Joint Advisory Committee language with the language code of
       <i>
        nap
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alphabet_and_pronunciation">
        Alphabet and pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Alphabet and pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Neapolitan orthography consists of 22
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
        Latin
       </a>
       letters. Much like
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_orthography" title="Italian orthography">
        Italian orthography
       </a>
       , it does not contain
       <i>
        k,
       </i>
       <i>
        w,
       </i>
       <i>
        x,
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       even though these letters might be found in some foreign words. However, unlike Italian, it does contain the letter
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       . The English pronunciation guidelines that follow are based on
       <a href="/wiki/General_American" title="General American">
        General American
       </a>
       pronunciation and the values used may not be applicable to other dialects. (See also:
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet_chart_for_English_dialects" title="International Phonetic Alphabet chart for English dialects">
        International Phonetic Alphabet chart for English dialects
       </a>
       .)
      </p>
      <p>
       All Romance languages are closely related. Although Neapolitan shares a high degree of its vocabulary with Italian, the official language of Italy, differences in pronunciation often make the connection unrecognizable to those without knowledge of Neapolitan. The most striking phonological difference is the Neapolitan weakening of unstressed vowels into
       <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
        schwa
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        schwa
       </i>
       is pronounced like the
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        about
       </i>
       or the
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        upon
       </i>
       ). However it is also possible (and quite common for some Neapolitans) to speak standard Italian with a "Neapolitan accent"; that is, by pronouncing un-stressed vowels as schwa but by otherwise using only entirely standard words and grammatical forms. This is not Neapolitan proper, but a mere difference in Italian pronunciation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Therefore, while pronunciation presents the strongest barrier to comprehension, the grammar of Neapolitan is what sets it apart from Italian. In Neapolitan, for example, the gender and number of a word is expressed by a change in the accented vowel, whereas in Italian it is expressed by a change in the final vowel (e.g.
       <i>
        l
        <b>
         uo
        </b>
        ngo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        l
        <b>
         o
        </b>
        nga
       </i>
       ; Italian
       <i>
        lung
        <b>
         o
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lung
        <b>
         a
        </b>
       </i>
       ; masc. "long", fem. "long"). These and other morpho-syntactic differences distinguish the Neapolitan language from the Italian language and the Neapolitan accent.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vowels">
        Vowels
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vowels">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       While there are only five graphic vowels in Neapolitan, phonemically, there are seven. The vowels
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       can be either "
       <a href="/wiki/Close_vowel" title="Close vowel">
        closed
       </a>
       " or "
       <a href="/wiki/Open_vowel" title="Open vowel">
        open
       </a>
       " and the pronunciation is different for the two. The grave accent (à, è, ò) is used to denote open vowels, and the acute accent (é, í, ó, ú) is used to denote closed vowels. However, accent marks are not used in the actual spelling of words except when they occur on the final syllable of a word, such as
       <i>
        Totò, arrivà,
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pecché
       </i>
       and when they appear here in other positions it is only to demonstrate where the stress, or accent, falls in some words.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definite_articles">
        Definite articles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Definite articles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Neapolitan
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite articles
       </a>
       (corresponding to the English word "the") are
       <i>
        La
       </i>
       (feminine singular),
       <i>
        Lo
       </i>
       (masculine singular) and
       <i>
        Li
       </i>
       (plural for both), but in reality these forms will probably only be found in older literature (along with
       <i>
        Lu
       </i>
       and even
       <i>
        El
       </i>
       ), of which there is much to be found. Modern Neapolitan uses, almost entirely, shortened forms of these articles which are:
      </p>
      <p>
       Before a word beginning with a consonant:
      </p>
      <table border="1" class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
        </th>
        <th>
         Singular
        </th>
        <th>
         Plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Masculine
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’o
         </i>
        </td>
        <td rowspan="2">
         <i>
          ’e
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Feminine
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’a
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Neuter
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’o
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         –
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       These definite articles are always pronounced distinctly.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before a word beginning with a vowel:
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        l’
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ll’
       </i>
       for both masculine and feminine; for both singular and plural.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although both forms can be found, the
       <i>
        ll’
       </i>
       form is by far the most common.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is well to note that in Neapolitan the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       of a noun is not easily determined by the article, so other means must be used. In the case of
       <i>
        ’o
       </i>
       which can be either masculine singular or neuter singular (there is no neuter plural in Neapolitan), when it is neuter the initial consonant of the noun is doubled. As an example, the name of a language in Neapolitan is always neuter, so if we see
       <i>
        ’o nnapulitano
       </i>
       we know it refers to the Neapolitan language, whereas
       <i>
        ’o napulitano
       </i>
       would refer to a Neapolitan man.
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise, since
       <i>
        ’e
       </i>
       can be either masculine plural or feminine plural, when it is feminine plural, the initial consonant of the noun is doubled. As an example, let's consider
       <i>
        ’a lista
       </i>
       which in Neapolitan is feminine singular for "list." In the plural it becomes
       <i>
        ’e lliste
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There can also be problems with nouns whose singular form ends in
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       . Since plural nouns usually end in
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       whether masculine or feminine, the masculine plural is often formed by orthographically changing the spelling. As an example, let's consider the word
       <i>
        guaglione
       </i>
       (which means "boy", or "girl" in the feminine form):
      </p>
      <table border="1" class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
        </th>
        <th>
         Singular
        </th>
        <th>
         Plural
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Masculine
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’o guaglione
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’e guagliune
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Feminine
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’a guagliona
         </i>
        </td>
        <td>
         <i>
          ’e gguaglione
         </i>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       More will be said about these orthographically changing nouns in the section on Neapolitan nouns.
      </p>
      <p>
       A couple of notes about consonant doubling:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Doubling is a function of the article (and certain other words), and these same words may be seen in other contexts without the consonant doubled. More will be said about this in the section on consonant doubling.
       </li>
       <li>
        Doubling only occurs when the consonant is followed by a vowel. If it is followed by another consonant, such as in the word
        <i>
         spagnuolo (Spanish)
        </i>
        , no doubling occurs.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_conjugation">
        Verbal conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Verbal conjugation">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Neapolitan there are four finite modes: indicative, subjunctive, conditional and imperative, and three non-finite modes: infinitive, gerund and participle. Each mode has an active and a passive form. The only auxiliary verbs used in the active form is "(h)avé" (
       <i>
        en.
       </i>
       "to have",
       <i>
        it.
       </i>
       "avere"), which contrasts with Italian in which the intransitive verbs take "èssere" for their auxiliary. For example, we have:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <td>
         na.
        </td>
        <td>
         So stato a Nnapule ajere
        </td>
        <td>

        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         it.
        </td>
        <td>
         Sono stato a Napoli ieri
        </td>
        <td>
         AUX-BE-1st-SING-PRES "be"-PART-PAST "in"-PREP "Naples"-NOUN "yesterday"-ADVERB
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         en.
        </td>
        <td>
         I was in Naples yesterday
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Doubled_initial_consonants">
        Doubled initial consonants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Neapolitan_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Doubled initial consonants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Neapolitan, many times the initial consonant of a word is doubled. This is called Raddoppiamento sintattico in Italian as it also apply to the Italian phonology.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        All feminine plural nouns, when preceded by the feminine plural definite article,
        <i>
         ’e,
        </i>
        or by any feminine plural adjective, have their initial consonant doubled.
       </li>
       <li>
        All neuter singular nouns, when preceded by the neuter singular definite article,
        <i>
         ’o,
        </i>
        or by a neuter singular adjective, have their initial consonant doubled.
       </li>
       <li>
        In addition, other words also trigger this doubling. Below is a list of words that trigger the doubling of the initial consonant of the word that follows.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, when there is a pause after the "trigger" word, the phonological doubling does not occur (e.g.
       <i>
        Tu sî (g)guaglione, [You are a boy]
       </i>
       where
       <i>
        sî
       </i>
       is a "trigger" word causing doubling of the initial consonant in
       <i>
        guaglione
       </i>
       but in the phrase
       <i>
        ’e do sî, guagliò? [Where are you from, boy?]
       </i>
       no doubling occurs). Neither does doubling occur when the initial consonant is followed by another consonant (e.g.
       <i>
        ’o ttaliano [the Italian language], but ’o spagnuolo [the Spanish language], where
       </i>
       ’o is the neuter definite article). It should be noted that this is what happens phonologically and that an orthographic change is not required. The same thing happens in Italian, where multiple words trigger first-consonant doubling, e.g.
       <i>
        la casa
       </i>
       but
       <i>
        a (c)casa
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        io e (t)te
       </i>
       , etc.
      </p>

        <i>
         stà
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         <b>
          sto’
         </b>
        </i>
        (e.g.
        <i>
         sto’ (p)parlanno
        </i>
        )

       <li>
        From the verb
        <i>
         puté
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         <b>
          può
         </b>
        </i>
        ;
        <i>
         <b>
          pô
         </b>
        </i>
        (e.g. ;
        <i>
         isso pô (s)sapé
        </i>
        )

        <i>

          <i>
           <span lang="nap" xml:lang="nap">

           </span>
          </i>
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
