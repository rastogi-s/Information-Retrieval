      <p>
       <b>
        Shuadit
       </b>
       (also spelled
       <i>
        Chouhadite
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Chouhadit
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Chouadite
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Chouadit
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Shuhadit
       </i>
       ), also called
       <b>
        Judæo-Occitan
       </b>
       or less accurately
       <b>
        Judæo-Provençal
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Judæo-Comtadin
       </b>
       , is the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       dialect historically spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_France" title="History of the Jews in France">
        French Jews
       </a>
       . It was not a distinct language, and was indistinguishable from the Occitan spoken by non-Jews (Banitt 1963, Pansier 1925, Guttel &amp; Aslanov 2006:560).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Shuadit is known from documents dating to as early as the 11th century in France, and after suffering drastic declines beginning with the charter of the
       <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">
        Inquisition
       </a>
       in France, it finally
       <a href="/wiki/Language_death" title="Language death">
        died out
       </a>
       with its last known speaker in 1977.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shuadit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Shuadit writings came in two distinct varieties, religious texts and popular prose, and they were written by adapting the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_script" title="Hebrew script">
        Hebrew script
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Religious texts contained a significantly higher incidence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrews
       </a>
       and reflectef an overall more "educated" style, with many words alsofrom
       <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
        Old French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aramaic" title="Aramaic">
        Aramaic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       . The texts include a fragment of a 14th-century poem lauding Queen
       <a href="/wiki/Esther" title="Esther">
        Esther
       </a>
       . Also, a woman's
       <a href="/wiki/Siddur" title="Siddur">
        siddur
       </a>
       . This siddur contains an uncommon blessing, found in few other locations (including medieval
       <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_Jews" title="Lithuanian Jews">
        Lithuania
       </a>
       ), thanking
       <a href="/wiki/God_in_Judaism" title="God in Judaism">
        God
       </a>
       , in the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Jewish_prayers_and_blessings" title="List of Jewish prayers and blessings">
        morning blessings
       </a>
       , not for making her "according to His will" (she-asani kirtzono) but for making her as a woman.
      </p>
      <p>
       The extant texts comprising the collections of popular prose used far fewer borrowings and were essentially Occitan written with the Hebrew script, possibly indicating a Jewish preference, prevalent at the time for not using the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_alphabet" title="Latin alphabet">
        Latin alphabet
       </a>
       , regarded widely as synonymous with the oppressive Christian régimes. The texts demonstrate the extent to which the Jewish community of
       <a href="/wiki/Provence" title="Provence">
        Provence
       </a>
       was familiar with Hebrew as well as the extent to which the community was integrated into the larger surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Christian_culture" title="Christian culture">
        Christian culture
       </a>
       of the region.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonology">
        Phonology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shuadit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Shuadit had a number of phonological characteristics unlike all other
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_languages" title="Jewish languages">
        Jewish languages
       </a>
       . The name "Shuadit" literally means "Jewish" and is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       pronunciation of the Hebrew word "Yehudit" (initial *j became
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       , and *h was often elided between vowels).
      </p>
      <p>
       In words inherited from Hebrew and Aramaic, the letters
       <i>
        samekh
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        sin
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        thav
       </i>
       were all pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       , the same as
       <i>
        fe
       </i>
       . The conjecture is that the first two
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       phonemes merged with the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       phoneme, which then merged with the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       . That observation gives particular validity to the theory that Shuadit is an outgrowth of a much older
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judaeo-Latin" title="Judaeo-Latin">
        Judaeo-Latin
       </a>
       language, rather than an independent development within southern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       , as the second step also occurred during the development of Latin from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Italic" title="Proto-Italic">
        Proto-Italic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In words derived from Latin, there was a tendency to
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongise
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /l/
       </span>
       after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plosive" title="Plosive">
        plosives
       </a>
       and to delateralize
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʎ/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       . Also, both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       , as well as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʒ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʃ/
       </span>
       , merged to the single phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       . Thus, the Provençal words
       <i>
        plus
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        filho
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        juge
       </i>
       were respectively
       <i>
        pyus
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        feyo
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        šuše
       </i>
       in Shuadit.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence">
        Evidence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shuadit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evidence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A fundamental source for inferring information about the
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       of Shuadit is the comedy
       <i>
        Harcanot et Barcanot
       </i>
       . (See
       <i>
        Pansier
       </i>
       in the
       <a href="#References">
        References
       </a>
       section.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_II_of_Brazil" title="Pedro II of Brazil">
        Pedro II of Brazil
       </a>
       recorded a number of bilingual Hebrew-Shuadit religious
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poem" title="Poem">
        poems
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Decline">
        Decline
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Shuadit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Decline">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1498, the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_the_Jews_in_France" title="History of the Jews in France">
        French Jews
       </a>
       were formally expelled from
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       . Although the community was not finally compelled to depart until 1501, much of the community had by then become dispersed into other regions, notably
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       , and the underdeveloped regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       . However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Comtat_Venaissin" title="Comtat Venaissin">
        Comtat Venaissin
       </a>
       was then under the direct control of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pope" title="Pope">
        Pope
       </a>
       , and a small Jewish community continued to live there in relative isolation. From the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Revolution" title="French Revolution">
        French Revolution
       </a>
       , when French Jews were permitted to live legally anywhere in France as full citizens, the status of Shuadit began to decline rapidly. The last known native speaker,
       <a href="/wiki/Armand_Lunel" title="Armand Lunel">
        Armand Lunel
       </a>
       , died in 1977.
      </p>
