    Phoneme
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

         Look up
         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Special:Search/phoneme" title="wiktionary:Special:Search/phoneme">
            phoneme
           </a>
          </b>
         </i>
         in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        phoneme
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'f' in 'find'">
            f
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is one of the units of sound (or gesture in the case of sign languages, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chereme" title="Chereme">
        chereme
       </a>
       ) that distinguish one word from another in a particular language. For example, in most dialects of English, the sound patterns
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">
            ʌ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        thumb
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ʌ/: 'u' in 'cut'">
            ʌ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       (
       <i>
        dumb
       </i>
       ) are two separate words distinguished by the substitution of one phoneme,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       , for another phoneme,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       . (Two words like this that differ in meaning through a contrast of a single phoneme form what is called a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
         minimal pair
        </a>
       </i>
       ). In many other languages these would be interpreted as exactly the same set of phonemes (i.e.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       would be considered the same).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       , phonemes (
       <a href="#without-mps">
        usually
       </a>
       established by the use of minimal pairs, such as
       <i>
        kill
       </i>
       vs
       <i>
        kiss
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        pat
       </i>
       vs
       <i>
        bat
       </i>
       ) are written between slashes, e.g.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       . To show pronunciation more precisely linguists use square brackets, for example
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pʰ]
       </span>
       (indicating an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">
        aspirated
       </a>
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Within linguistics there are differing views as to exactly what phonemes are and how a given language should be analyzed in
       <i>
        phonemic
       </i>
       (or
       <i>
        phonematic
       </i>
       ) terms. However, a phoneme is generally regarded as an
       <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">
        abstraction
       </a>
       of a set (or
       <a href="/wiki/Equivalence_class" title="Equivalence class">
        equivalence class
       </a>
       ) of speech sounds (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
         phones
        </a>
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Bracket" title="Bracket">
        )
       </a>
       which are perceived as equivalent to each other in a given language. For example, in English, the
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       sounds in the words
       <i>
        kit
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        skill
       </i>
       are not identical (as described
       <a href="#Assignment_of_speech_sounds_to_phonemes">
        below
       </a>
       ), but they are distributional variants of a single phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       . Different speech sounds that are realizations of the same phoneme are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophones
       </a>
       . Allophonic variation may be conditioned, in which case a certain phoneme is realized as a certain allophone in particular phonological environments, or it may be free in which case it may vary randomly. In this way, phonemes are often considered to constitute an abstract
       <a href="/wiki/Underlying_representation" title="Underlying representation">
        underlying representation
       </a>
       for segments of words, while speech sounds make up the corresponding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonetic" title="Phonetic">
        phonetic
       </a>
       realization, or surface form.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notation">
        Notation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Notation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Phonemes are conventionally placed between
       <a href="/wiki/Slash_(punctuation)" title="Slash (punctuation)">
        slashes
       </a>
       in transcription, whereas speech sounds (phones) are placed between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_bracket" title="Square bracket">
        square brackets
       </a>
       . Thus
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /pʊʃ/
       </span>
       represents a sequence of three phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʊ/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       (the word
       <i>
        push
       </i>
       in standard English), while
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pʰʊʃ]
       </span>
       represents the phonetic sequence of sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pʰ]
       </span>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">
        aspirated
       </a>
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       ),
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʊ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       (the usual pronunciation of
       <i>
        push
       </i>
       ). This is not to be confused with the similar convention of the use of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angle_bracket" title="Angle bracket">
        angle brackets
       </a>
       to enclose the units of
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       , namely
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        graphemes
       </a>
       ; for example, ⟨f⟩ represents the written letter (grapheme)
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The symbols used for particular phonemes are often taken from the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       (IPA), the same set of symbols that are most commonly used for phones. (For computer typing purposes, systems such as
       <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">
        X-SAMPA
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kirshenbaum" title="Kirshenbaum">
        Kirshenbaum
       </a>
       exist to represent IPA symbols using only
       <a href="/wiki/ASCII" title="ASCII">
        ASCII
       </a>
       characters.) However, descriptions of particular languages may use different conventional symbols to represent the phonemes of those languages. For languages whose writing systems employ the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonemic_principle" title="Phonemic principle">
        phonemic principle
       </a>
       , ordinary letters may be used to denote phonemes, although this approach is often hampered by the complexity of the relationship between orthography and pronunciation (see
       <a href="#Correspondence_between_letters_and_phonemes">
        Correspondence between letters and phonemes
       </a>
       below).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Assignment_of_speech_sounds_to_phonemes">
        Assignment of speech sounds to phonemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assignment of speech sounds to phonemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="690" data-file-width="905" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg/250px-Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg/375px-Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg/500px-Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phoneme-allophone-determination-chart.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A simplified procedure for determining whether two sounds represent the same or different phonemes
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A phoneme is a sound or a group of different sounds perceived to have the same function by speakers of the language or dialect in question. An example is the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , which occurs in words such as
       <i>
        <b>
         c
        </b>
        at
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         k
        </b>
        it
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        s
        <b>
         c
        </b>
        at
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        s
        <b>
         k
        </b>
        it
       </i>
       . Although most native speakers do not notice this, in most English dialects the "c/k" sounds in these words are not identical: in
       <span class="unicode haudio">
        <span class="fn">
         <span style="white-space:nowrap">
          <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-kit.ogg" title="About this sound">
           <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/85/En-us-kit.ogg" title="En-us-kit.ogg">
          <i>
           kit
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
        <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">
         (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help">
          <span style="cursor:help;">
           help
          </span>
         </a>
         ·
         <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-kit.ogg" title="File:En-us-kit.ogg">
          <span style="cursor:help;">
           info
          </span>
         </a>
         )
        </small>
       </span>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [kʰɪt]
        </a>
       </span>
       the sound is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aspiration_(phonetics)" title="Aspiration (phonetics)">
        aspirated
       </a>
       , while in
       <span class="unicode haudio">
        <span class="fn">
         <span style="white-space:nowrap">
          <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-skill.ogg" title="About this sound">
           <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
          </a>
         </span>
         <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/En-us-skill.ogg" title="En-us-skill.ogg">
          <i>
           skill
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
        <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">
         (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help">
          <span style="cursor:help;">
           help
          </span>
         </a>
         ·
         <a href="/wiki/File:En-us-skill.ogg" title="File:En-us-skill.ogg">
          <span style="cursor:help;">
           info
          </span>
         </a>
         )
        </small>
       </span>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
         [skɪl]
        </a>
       </span>
       it is unaspirated. The words therefore contain different
       <i>
        speech sounds
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
         phones
        </a>
       </i>
       , transcribed
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       for the aspirated form,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       for the unaspirated one. These different sounds are nonetheless considered to belong to the same phoneme, because if a speaker used one instead of the other, the meaning of the word would not change: using the aspirated form
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        skill
       </i>
       might sound odd, but the word would still be recognized. By contrast, some other sounds would cause a change in meaning if substituted: for example, substitution of the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       would produce the different word
       <i>
        s
        <b>
         t
        </b>
        ill
       </i>
       , and that sound must therefore be considered to represent a different phoneme (the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The above shows that in English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allophones" title="Allophones">
        allophones
       </a>
       of a single phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       . In some languages, however,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       are perceived by native speakers as different sounds, and substituting one for the other can change the meaning of a word; this means that in those languages, the two sounds represent different phonemes. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       is the first sound of
       <i>
        kátur
       </i>
       meaning "cheerful", while
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       is the first sound of
       <i>
        gátur
       </i>
       meaning "riddles". Icelandic therefore has two separate phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kʰ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Minimal_pairs">
        Minimal pairs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Minimal pairs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A pair of words like
       <i>
        kátur
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gátur
       </i>
       (above) that differ only in one phone is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
        minimal pair
       </a>
       for the two alternative phones in question (in this case,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       ). The existence of minimal pairs is a common test to decide whether two phones represent different phonemes or are allophones of the same phoneme. To take another example, the minimal pair
       <i>
        <b>
         t
        </b>
        ip
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <b>
         d
        </b>
        ip
       </i>
       illustrates that in English,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       belong to separate phonemes,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       ; since these two words have different meanings, English speakers must be conscious of the distinction between the two sounds. In other languages, though, including
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_language" title="Korean language">
        Korean
       </a>
       , even though both sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       occur, no such minimal pair exists. The lack of minimal pairs distinguishing
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       in Korean provides evidence that in this language they are allophones of a single phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       . The word
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tata/
       </span>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [tada]
       </span>
       , for example. That is, when they hear this word, Korean speakers perceive the same sound in both the beginning and middle of the word, whereas an English speaker would perceive different sounds in these two locations. Signed languages, such as American Sign Language (ASL), also have minimal pairs, differing only in (exactly) one of the signs' parameters: handshape, movement, location, palm orientation, and non-manual signal or marker. A minimal pair may exist in the signed language if the basic sign stays the same but one of these parameters changes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="without-mps">
       </span>
       However, the absence of minimal pairs for a given pair of phones does not always mean that they belong to the same phoneme: they may be too dissimilar phonetically for it to be likely that speakers perceive them as the same sound. For example, English has no minimal pair for the sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        <b>
         h
        </b>
        at
       </i>
       ) and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ŋ]
       </span>
       (as in
       <i>
        ba
        <b>
         ng
        </b>
       </i>
       ), and the fact that they can be shown to be in
       <a href="/wiki/Complementary_distribution" title="Complementary distribution">
        complementary distribution
       </a>
       could be used to argue for their being allophones of the same phoneme. However, they are so dissimilar phonetically that they are considered separate phonemes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells198244_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells198244-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Phonologists have sometimes had recourse to "near minimal pairs" to show that speakers of the language perceive two sounds as significantly different even if no exact minimal pair exists in the lexicon. It is virtually impossible to find a minimal pair to distinguish English
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">
            ʃ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       from
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ʒ/: 's' in 'pleasure'">
            ʒ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       , yet it seems uncontroversial to claim that the two consonants are distinct phonemes. The two words 'pressure'
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">
            ʃ
           </span>
           <span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">
            ər
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       and 'pleasure'
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ɛ/: 'e' in 'dress'">
            ɛ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʒ/: 's' in 'pleasure'">
            ʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/ər/: 'er' in 'letter'">
            ər
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       can serve as a near minimal pair.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWells198248_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWells198248-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_features_with_phonemic_status">
        Other features with phonemic status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other features with phonemic status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While phonemes are normally conceived of as abstractions of discrete
       <a href="/wiki/Segment_(linguistics)" title="Segment (linguistics)">
        segmental
       </a>
       speech sounds (vowels and consonants), there are other features of pronunciation – principally
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tone
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       – which in some languages can change the meaning of words in the way that phoneme contrasts do, and are consequently called
       <i>
        phonemic
       </i>
       features of those languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Phonemic stress
       </i>
       is encountered in languages such as English. For example, the word
       <i>
        invite
       </i>
       stressed on the second syllable is a verb, but when stressed on the first syllable (without changing any of the individual sounds) it becomes a noun. The position of the stress in the word affects the meaning, and therefore a full phonemic specification (providing enough detail to enable the word to be pronounced unambiguously) would include indication of the position of the stress:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɪnˈvaɪ̯t/
       </span>
       for the verb,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈɪnvaɪt/
       </span>
       for the noun. In other languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , word stress cannot have this function (its position is generally predictable) and is therefore not phonemic (and is not usually indicated in dictionaries).
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Phonemic tones
       </i>
       are found in languages such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mandarin_Chinese" title="Mandarin Chinese">
        Mandarin Chinese
       </a>
       , in which a given syllable can have five different tonal pronunciations.
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable" style="text-align: center;">
       <tr>
        <td>
         妈
        </td>
        <td>
         麻
        </td>
        <td>
         马
        </td>
        <td>
         骂
        </td>
        <td>
         吗
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         mā
        </td>
        <td>
         má
        </td>
        <td>
         mǎ
        </td>
        <td>
         mà
        </td>
        <td>
         ma
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         mother
        </td>
        <td>
         hemp
        </td>
        <td>
         horse
        </td>
        <td>
         scold
        </td>
        <td>
         <a href="/wiki/Chinese_particles" title="Chinese particles">
          question particle
         </a>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Here, the character 妈 (pronounced
       <i>
        mā
       </i>
       , high level pitch) means "mother"; 麻 (
       <i>
        má
       </i>
       , rising pitch) means "hemp"; 马 (
       <i>
        mǎ
       </i>
       , falling then rising) means "horse"; 骂 (
       <i>
        mà
       </i>
       , falling) means "scold", and 吗 (
       <i>
        ma
       </i>
       , neutral tone) is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interrogative_particle" title="Interrogative particle">
        interrogative particle
       </a>
       . The tone "phonemes" in such languages are sometimes called
       <i>
        tonemes
       </i>
       . Languages such as English do not have phonemic tone, although they use
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       for functions such as emphasis and attitude.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution_of_allophones">
        Distribution of allophones
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Distribution of allophones">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When a phoneme has more than one
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophone
       </a>
       , the one actually heard at a given occurrence of that phoneme may be dependent on the phonetic environment (surrounding sounds) – allophones which normally cannot appear in the same environment are said to be in
       <a href="/wiki/Complementary_distribution" title="Complementary distribution">
        complementary distribution
       </a>
       . In other cases the choice of allophone may be dependent on the individual speaker or other unpredictable factors – such allophones are said to be in
       <a href="/wiki/Free_variation" title="Free variation">
        free variation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background_and_related_ideas">
        Background and related ideas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Background and related ideas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        phonème
       </i>
       (from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       φώνημα
       <i>
        phōnēma
       </i>
       , "sound made, utterance, thing spoken, speech, language"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Liddell_&amp;_Scott_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Liddell_&amp;_Scott-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) was reportedly first used by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A._Dufriche-Desgenettes" title="A. Dufriche-Desgenettes">
        A. Dufriche-Desgenettes
       </a>
       in 1873, but it referred only to a speech sound. The term
       <i>
        phoneme
       </i>
       as an
       <a href="/wiki/Abstraction" title="Abstraction">
        abstraction
       </a>
       was developed by the Polish linguist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jan_Niecis%C5%82aw_Baudouin_de_Courtenay" title="Jan Niecisław Baudouin de Courtenay">
        Jan Niecisław Baudouin de Courtenay
       </a>
       and his student
       <a href="/wiki/Miko%C5%82aj_Kruszewski" title="Mikołaj Kruszewski">
        Mikołaj Kruszewski
       </a>
       during 1875–1895.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJones1957_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJones1957-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term used by these two was
       <i>
        fonema
       </i>
       , the basic unit of what they called
       <i>
        psychophonetics
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Jones_(phonetician)" title="Daniel Jones (phonetician)">
        Daniel Jones
       </a>
       became the first linguist in the western world to use the term
       <i>
        phoneme
       </i>
       in its current sense, employing the word in his article "The phonetic structure of the Sechuana Language".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The concept of the phoneme was then elaborated in the works of
       <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Trubetzkoy" title="Nikolai Trubetzkoy">
        Nikolai Trubetzkoy
       </a>
       and others of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_School" title="Prague School">
        Prague School
       </a>
       (during the years 1926–1935), and in those of
       <a href="/wiki/Structuralism" title="Structuralism">
        structuralists
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_de_Saussure" title="Ferdinand de Saussure">
        Ferdinand de Saussure
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">
        Edward Sapir
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bloomfield" title="Leonard Bloomfield">
        Leonard Bloomfield
       </a>
       . Some structuralists (though not Sapir) rejected the idea of a cognitive or psycholinguistic function for the phoneme.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETwaddell1935_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETwaddell1935-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarris1951_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarris1951-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Later, it was used and redefined in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" title="Generative linguistics">
        generative linguistics
       </a>
       , most famously by
       <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
        Noam Chomsky
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">
        Morris Halle
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChomskyHalle1968_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChomskyHalle1968-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and remains central to many accounts of the development of modern
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       . As a theoretical concept or model, though, it has been supplemented and even replaced by others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClarkYallop1995chpt._11_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClarkYallop1995chpt._11-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some linguists (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Jakobson" title="Roman Jakobson">
        Roman Jakobson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">
        Morris Halle
       </a>
       ) proposed that phonemes may be further decomposable into
       <a href="/wiki/Distinctive_feature" title="Distinctive feature">
        features
       </a>
       , such features being the true minimal constituents of language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJakobsonHalle1968_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJakobsonHalle1968-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Features overlap each other in time, as do
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suprasegmental" title="Suprasegmental">
        suprasegmental
       </a>
       phonemes in oral language and many phonemes in sign languages. Features could be characterized in different ways: Jakobson and colleagues defined them in
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_phonetics" title="Acoustic phonetics">
        acoustic
       </a>
       terms,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJakobsonFantHalle1952_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJakobsonFantHalle1952-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Chomsky and Halle used a predominantly
       <a href="/wiki/Articulatory_phonetics" title="Articulatory phonetics">
        articulatory
       </a>
       basis, though retaining some acoustic features, while
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Ladefoged" title="Peter Ladefoged">
        Ladefoged
       </a>
       's system
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELadefoged2006268–276_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELadefoged2006268–276-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a purely articulatory system apart from the use of the acoustic term 'sibilant'.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the description of some languages, the term
       <a href="/wiki/Chroneme" title="Chroneme">
        chroneme
       </a>
       has been used to indicate contrastive length or
       <i>
        duration
       </i>
       of phonemes. In languages in which
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tones
       </a>
       are phonemic, the tone phonemes may be called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toneme" title="Toneme">
        tonemes
       </a>
       . Though not all scholars working on such languages use these terms, they are by no means obsolete.
      </p>
      <p>
       By analogy with the phoneme, linguists have proposed other sorts of underlying objects, giving them names with the suffix
       <i>
        -eme
       </i>
       , such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
         morpheme
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
         grapheme
        </a>
       </i>
       . These are sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Emic_unit" title="Emic unit">
        emic units
       </a>
       . The latter term was first used by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Pike" title="Kenneth Pike">
        Kenneth Pike
       </a>
       , who also generalized the concepts of
       <a href="/wiki/Emic_unit" title="Emic unit">
        emic and etic
       </a>
       description (from
       <i>
        phonemic
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        phonetic
       </i>
       respectively) to applications outside linguistics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPike1967_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPike1967-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Restrictions_on_occurrence">
        Restrictions on occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Restrictions on occurrence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">
        Phonotactics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Languages do not generally allow words or
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllables
       </a>
       to be built of any arbitrary sequences of phonemes; there are
       <a href="/wiki/Phonotactics" title="Phonotactics">
        phonotactic
       </a>
       restrictions on which sequences of phonemes are possible and in which environments certain phonemes can occur. Phonemes that are significantly limited by such restrictions may be called
       <i>
        restricted phonemes
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, examples of such restrictions include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋ/
        </span>
        , as in
        <i>
         si
         <b>
          ng
         </b>
        </i>
        , occurs only at the end of a syllable, never at the beginning (in many other languages, such as
        <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_language" title="Māori language">
         Māori
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">
         Swahili
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
         Tagalog
        </a>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Thai_language" title="Thai language">
         Thai
        </a>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋ/
        </span>
        can appear word-initially).
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /h/
        </span>
        occurs only before vowels and at the beginning of a syllable, never at the end (a few languages, such as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
         Arabic
        </a>
        , or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romanian_Language" title="Romanian Language">
         Romanian
        </a>
        allow
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /h/
        </span>
        syllable-finally).
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhotic_and_non-rhotic_accents" title="Rhotic and non-rhotic accents">
         non-rhotic dialects
        </a>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɹ/
        </span>
        can only occur immediately before a vowel, never before a consonant.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /w/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        occur only before a vowel, never at the end of a syllable (except in interpretations where a word like
        <i>
         boy
        </i>
        is analyzed as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /bɔj/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some phonotactic restrictions can alternatively be analyzed as cases of neutralization. See
       <a href="#Neutralization_and_archiphonemes">
        Neutralization and archiphonemes
       </a>
       below, particularly the example of the occurrence of the three English nasals before stops.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biuniqueness">
        Biuniqueness
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Biuniqueness">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        Biuniqueness
       </b>
       is a requirement of classic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Structuralism_(linguistics)" title="Structuralism (linguistics)">
        structuralist
       </a>
       phonemics. It means that a given
       <a href="/wiki/Phone_(phonetics)" title="Phone (phonetics)">
        phone
       </a>
       , wherever it occurs, must unambiguously be assigned to one and only one phoneme. In other words, the mapping between phones and phonemes is required to be many-to-one rather than
       <a href="/wiki/Many-to-many" title="Many-to-many">
        many-to-many
       </a>
       . The notion of biuniqueness was controversial among some pre-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" title="Generative linguistics">
        generative
       </a>
       linguists and was prominently challenged by
       <a href="/wiki/Morris_Halle" title="Morris Halle">
        Morris Halle
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Noam_Chomsky" title="Noam Chomsky">
        Noam Chomsky
       </a>
       in the late 1950s and early 1960s.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of the problems arising from the biuniqueness requirement is provided by the phenomenon of
       <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">
        flapping
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">
        North American English
       </a>
       . This may cause either
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       (in the appropriate environments) to be realized with the phone
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       (an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
        alveolar flap
       </a>
       ). For example, the same flap sound may be heard in the words
       <i>
        hi
        <b>
         tt
        </b>
        ing
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bi
        <b>
         dd
        </b>
        ing
       </i>
       , although it is clearly intended to realize the phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       in the first word and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       in the second. This appears to contradict biuniqueness.
      </p>
      <p>
       For further discussion of such cases, see the next section.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Neutralization_and_archiphonemes">
        <span id="Neutralization">
        </span>
        Neutralization and archiphonemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Neutralization and archiphonemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Phonemes that are contrastive in certain environments may not be contrastive in all environments. In the environments where they do not contrast, the contrast is said to be
       <b>
        neutralized
       </b>
       . In these positions it may become less clear which phoneme a given phone represents. Some
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonologists
       </a>
       prefer not to specify a unique phoneme in such cases, since to do so would mean providing redundant or even arbitrary information – instead they use the technique of
       <a href="/wiki/Underspecification" title="Underspecification">
        underspecification
       </a>
       . An
       <b>
        archiphoneme
       </b>
       is an object sometimes used to represent an underspecified phoneme.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of neutralization is provided by the Russian vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       . These phonemes are contrasting in
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stressed
       </a>
       syllables, but in unstressed syllables the contrast is lost, since both are
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
        reduced
       </a>
       to the same sound, usually
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə]
       </span>
       (for details, see
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction_in_Russian" title="Vowel reduction in Russian">
        vowel reduction in Russian
       </a>
       ). In order to assign such an instance of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə]
       </span>
       to one of the phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       , it is necessary to consider
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       factors (such as which of the vowels occurs in other forms of the words, or which
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflectional
       </a>
       pattern is followed). In some cases even this may not provide an unambiguous answer. A description using the approach of underspecification would not attempt to assign
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ə]
       </span>
       to a specific phoneme in some or all of these cases, although it might be assigned to an archiphoneme, written something like |A|, which reflects the two neutralized phonemes in this position.
      </p>
      <p>
       A somewhat different example is found in English, with the three
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal
       </a>
       phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m, n, ŋ/
       </span>
       . In word-final position these all contrast, as shown by the minimal triplet
       <i>
        sum
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sʌm/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        sun
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sʌn/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        sung
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /sʌŋ/
       </span>
       . However, before a
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       such as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p, t, k/
       </span>
       (provided there is no
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morpheme
       </a>
       boundary between them), only one of the nasals is possible in any given position:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m/
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋ/
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        limp, lint, link
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /lɪmp/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /lɪnt/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /lɪŋk/
       </span>
       ). The nasals are therefore not contrastive in these environments, and according to some theorists this makes it inappropriate to assign the nasal phones heard here to any one of the phonemes (even though, in this case, the phonetic evidence is unambiguous). Instead they may analyze these phones as belonging to a single archiphoneme, written something like |N|, and state the
       <a href="/wiki/Underlying_representation" title="Underlying representation">
        underlying representations
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        limp, lint, link
       </i>
       to be
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        |lɪNp|, |lɪNt|, |lɪNk|
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This latter type of analysis is often associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Trubetzkoy" title="Nikolai Trubetzkoy">
        Nikolai Trubetzkoy
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prague_school" title="Prague school">
        Prague school
       </a>
       . Archiphonemes are often notated with a capital letter within pipes, as with the examples |A| and |N| given above. Other ways the second of these might be notated include
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        |m-n-ŋ|
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        {m, n, ŋ
       </span>
       }, or |n*|.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example from English, but this time involving complete phonetic convergence as in the Russian example, is the flapping of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       in some American English (described above under
       <a href="#Biuniqueness">
        Biuniqueness
       </a>
       ). Here the words
       <i>
        betting
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bedding
       </i>
       might both be pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈbɛɾɪŋ]
       </span>
       , and if a speaker applies such flapping consistently, it would be necessary to look for morphological evidence (the pronunciation of the related forms
       <i>
        bet
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bed
       </i>
       , for example) in order to determine which phoneme the flap represents. As in the previous examples, some theorists would prefer not to make such a determination, and simply assign the flap in both cases to a single archiphoneme, written (for example) |D|.
      </p>
      <p>
       For a special kind of neutralization proposed in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_linguistics" title="Generative linguistics">
        generative
       </a>
       phonology, see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Absolute_neutralization" title="Absolute neutralization">
        absolute neutralization
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphophonemes">
        Morphophonemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Morphophonemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Morphophonology" title="Morphophonology">
        Morphophonology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        morphophoneme
       </b>
       is a theoretical unit at a deeper level of abstraction than traditional phonemes, and is taken to be a unit from which
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morphemes
       </a>
       are built up. A morphophoneme within a morpheme can be expressed in different ways in different
       <a href="/wiki/Allomorph" title="Allomorph">
        allomorphs
       </a>
       of that morpheme (according to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morphophonological" title="Morphophonological">
        morphophonological
       </a>
       rules). For example, the English plural morpheme
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       appearing in words such as
       <i>
        cats
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        dogs
       </i>
       can be considered to consist of a single morphophoneme, which might be written (for example) //z// or |z|, and which is pronounced as [s] after most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_consonant" title="Voiceless consonant">
        voiceless consonants
       </a>
       (as in
       <i>
        cat
        <b>
         s
        </b>
       </i>
       ) and [z] in most other cases (as in
       <i>
        dog
        <b>
         s
        </b>
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Numbers_of_phonemes_in_different_languages">
        Numbers of phonemes in different languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Numbers of phonemes in different languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A given language will use only a small subset of the many possible
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_sound" title="Speech sound">
        sounds
       </a>
       that the human
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Speech_organs" title="Speech organs">
        speech organs
       </a>
       can produce, and (because of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allophony" title="Allophony">
        allophony
       </a>
       ) the number of distinct phonemes will generally be smaller than the number of identifiably different sounds. Different languages vary considerably in the number of phonemes they have in their systems (although apparent variation may sometimes result from the different approaches taken by the linguists doing the analysis). The total phonemic inventory in languages varies from as few as 11 in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotokas" title="Rotokas">
        Rotokas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pirah%C3%A3_language" title="Pirahã language">
        Pirahã
       </a>
       to as many as 141 in
       <a href="/wiki/!Kung_language" title="!Kung language">
        !Xũ
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECrystal2010173_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECrystal2010173-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The number of phonemically distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       can be as low as two, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arrernte_language" title="Arrernte language">
        Arrernte
       </a>
       . At the other extreme, the
       <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">
        Bantu
       </a>
       language
       <a href="/wiki/Ngwe_language" title="Ngwe language">
        Ngwe
       </a>
       has 14 vowel qualities, 12 of which may occur long or short, making 26 oral vowels, plus 6 nasalized vowels, long and short, making a total of 38 vowels; while
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/!X%C3%B3%C3%B5_language" title="!Xóõ language">
        !Xóõ
       </a>
       achieves 31 pure vowels, not counting its additional variation by vowel length, by varying the
       <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
        phonation
       </a>
       . As regards
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonant
       </a>
       phonemes,
       <a href="/wiki/Puinave_language" title="Puinave language">
        Puinave
       </a>
       and the Papuan language
       <a href="/wiki/Tauade_language" title="Tauade language">
        Tauade
       </a>
       each have just seven, and
       <a href="/wiki/Rotokas_language" title="Rotokas language">
        Rotokas
       </a>
       has only six. !Xóõ, on the other hand, has somewhere around 77, and
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_phonology" title="Ubykh phonology">
        Ubykh
       </a>
       81. The
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English language
       </a>
       uses a rather large set of 13 to 21 vowel phonemes, including diphthongs, although its 22 to 26 consonants are close to average.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages, such as
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , have no phonemic
       <a href="/wiki/Tone_(linguistics)" title="Tone (linguistics)">
        tone
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stress
       </a>
       , while
       <a href="/wiki/Cantonese" title="Cantonese">
        Cantonese
       </a>
       and several of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kam%E2%80%93Sui_languages" title="Kam–Sui languages">
        Kam–Sui languages
       </a>
       have nine tones, and one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kru_languages" title="Kru languages">
        Kru languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wobe_language" title="Wobe language">
        Wobe
       </a>
       , has been claimed
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2017)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       to have 14, though this is disputed.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The most common vowel system consists of the five vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/, /e/, /a/, /o/, /u/
       </span>
       . The most common consonants are
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/, /t/, /k/, /m/, /n/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Relatively few languages lack any of these consonants, although it does happen: for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       lacks
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaiian_language" title="Hawaiian language">
        standard Hawaiian
       </a>
       lacks
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mohawk_language" title="Mohawk language">
        Mohawk
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">
        Tlingit
       </a>
       lack
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m/
       </span>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hupa_language" title="Hupa language">
        Hupa
       </a>
       lacks both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p/
       </span>
       and a simple
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , colloquial
       <a href="/wiki/Samoan_language" title="Samoan language">
        Samoan
       </a>
       lacks
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       , while
       <a href="/wiki/Rotokas_language" title="Rotokas language">
        Rotokas
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quileute_language" title="Quileute language">
        Quileute
       </a>
       lack
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /m/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /n/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_non-uniqueness_of_phonemic_solutions">
        The non-uniqueness of phonemic solutions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: The non-uniqueness of phonemic solutions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During the development of phoneme theory in the mid-20th century phonologists were concerned not only with the procedures and principles involved in producing a phonemic analysis of the sounds of a given language, but also with the reality or uniqueness of the phonemic solution. Some writers took the position expressed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kenneth_Pike" title="Kenneth Pike">
        Kenneth Pike
       </a>
       : "There is only one accurate phonemic analysis for a given set of data",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while others believed that different analyses, equally valid, could be made for the same data.
       <a href="/wiki/Yuen_Ren_Chao" title="Yuen Ren Chao">
        Yuen Ren Chao
       </a>
       (1934), in his article "The non-uniqueness of phonemic solutions of phonetic systems"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       stated "given the sounds of a language, there are usually more than one possible way of reducing them to a set of phonemes, and these different systems or solutions are not simply correct or incorrect, but may be regarded only as being good or bad for various purposes". The linguist F.W. Householder referred to this argument within linguistics as "God's Truth vs. hocus-pocus".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Different analyses of the English vowel system may be used to illustrate this. The article
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_Phonology" title="English Phonology">
        English Phonology
       </a>
       states that "English has a particularly large number of vowel phonemes" and that "there are 20 vowel phonemes in Received Pronunciation, 14–16 in General American and 20–21 in Australian English"; the present article (
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme#Numbers_of_phonemes_in_different_languages" title="Phoneme">
        Phoneme#Numbers of phonemes in different languages
       </a>
       ) says that "the English language uses a rather large set of 13 to 21 vowel phonemes". Although these figures are often quoted as a scientific fact, they actually reflect just one of many possible analyses, and later in the English Phonology article an alternative analysis is suggested in which some diphthongs and long vowels may be interpreted as comprising a short vowel linked to either /j/ or /w/. The transcription system for British English (RP) devised by the phonetician
       <a href="/wiki/Geoff_Lindsey" title="Geoff Lindsey">
        Geoff Lindsey
       </a>
       and used in the CUBE pronunciation dictionary also treats diphthongs as composed of a vowel plus /j/ or /w/.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The fullest exposition of this approach is found in Trager and Smith (1951), where all long vowels and diphthongs ("complex nuclei") are made up of a short vowel combined with either /j/, /w/ or /h/ (plus /r/ for rhotic accents), each thus comprising two phonemes: they wrote "The conclusion is inescapable that the complex nuclei consist each of two phonemes, one of the short vowels followed by one of three glides".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The transcription for the vowel normally transcribed /aɪ/ would instead be /aj/, /aʊ/ would be /aw/ and /ɑː/ would be /ah/. The consequence of this approach is that English could theoretically have only seven vowel phonemes, which might be symbolized /i/, /e/, /a/, /o/, /u/, /ʌ/ and /ə/, or even six if schwa were treated as an allophone of /ʌ/ or of other short vowels, a figure that would put English much closer to the average number of vowel phonemes in other languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the same period there was disagreement about the correct basis for a phonemic analysis. The
       <a href="/wiki/Structural_linguistics" title="Structural linguistics">
        Structuralist
       </a>
       position was that the analysis should be made purely on the basis of the sound elements and their distribution, with no reference to extraneous factors such as grammar, morphology or the intuitions of the native speaker; this position is strongly associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Bloomfield" title="Leonard Bloomfield">
        Leonard Bloomfield
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Zellig_Harris" title="Zellig Harris">
        Zellig Harris
       </a>
       claimed that it is possible to discover the phonemes of a language purely by examining the distribution of phonetic segments.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Mentalism_(psychology)" title="Mentalism (psychology)">
        mentalistic
       </a>
       definitions of the phoneme, Twaddell (1935) stated "Such a definition is invalid because (1) we have no right to guess about the linguistic workings of an inaccessible 'mind', and (2) we can secure no advantage from such guesses. The linguistic processes of the 'mind' as such are quite simply unobservable; and introspection about linguistic processes is notoriously a fire in a wooden stove."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This approach was opposed to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sapir" title="Edward Sapir">
        Edward Sapir
       </a>
       , who gave an important role to native speakers' intuitions about where a particular sound or groups of sounds fitted into a pattern. Using English [ŋ] as an example, Sapir argued that, despite the superficial appearance that this sound belongs to a group of nasal consonants, "no naive English-speaking person can be made to feel in his bones that it belongs to a single series with /m/ and /n/. ... It still
       <i>
        feels
       </i>
       like ŋg".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The theory of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generative_phonology" title="Generative phonology">
        generative phonology
       </a>
       which emerged in the 1960s explicitly rejected the Structuralist approach to phonology and favoured the mentalistic or cognitive view of Sapir.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Correspondence_between_letters_and_phonemes">
        Correspondence between letters and phonemes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Correspondence between letters and phonemes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Phonemic_orthography" title="Phonemic orthography">
        Phonemic orthography
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Phonemes are considered to be the basis for
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabetic
       </a>
       writing systems. In such systems the written symbols (
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        graphemes
       </a>
       ) represent, in principle, the phonemes of the language being written. This is most obviously the case when the alphabet was invented with a particular language in mind; for example, the Latin alphabet was devised for Classical Latin, and therefore the Latin of that period enjoyed a near one-to-one correspondence between phonemes and graphemes in most cases, though the devisers of the alphabet chose not to represent the phonemic effect of vowel length. However, because changes in the spoken language are often not accompanied by changes in the established
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       (as well as other reasons, including
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       differences, the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Morphophonology" title="Morphophonology">
        morphophonology
       </a>
       on orthography, and the use of foreign spellings for some
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       ), the correspondence between spelling and pronunciation in a given language may be highly distorted; this is the case with English, for example.
      </p>
      <p>
       The correspondence between symbols and phonemes in alphabetic writing systems is not necessarily a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/One-to-one_correspondence" title="One-to-one correspondence">
        one-to-one correspondence
       </a>
       . A phoneme might be represented by a combination of two or more letters (
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        digraph
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Digraph_(orthography)" title="Digraph (orthography)">
        trigraph
       </a>
       , etc.), like &lt;sh&gt; in English or &lt;sch&gt; in
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       (both representing phonemes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       ). Also a single letter may represent two phonemes, as in English &lt;x&gt; representing /gz/ or /ks/. There may also exist spelling/pronunciation rules (such as those for the pronunciation of &lt;c&gt; in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ) that further complicate the correspondence of letters to phonemes, although they need not affect the ability to predict the pronunciation from the spelling and vice versa, provided the rules are known.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonemes_in_sign_languages">
        Phonemes in sign languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoneme&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Phonemes in sign languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Sign_language" title="Sign language">
        sign languages
       </a>
       , the basic elements of gesture and location were formerly called
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chereme" title="Chereme">
         cheremes
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        cheiremes
       </i>
       but they are now generally referred to as phonemes, as with oral languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sign language phonemes are combinations of articulation bundles in ASL. These bundles may be classified as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Location_(sign_language)" title="Location (sign language)">
         tab
        </a>
       </i>
       (elements of location, from Latin
       <i>
        tabula
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Handshape" title="Handshape">
         dez
        </a>
       </i>
       (the hand shape, from
       <i>
        designator
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Movement_(sign_language)" title="Movement (sign language)">
         sig
        </a>
       </i>
       (the motion, from
       <i>
        signation
       </i>
       ), and with some researchers,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Orientation_(sign_language)" title="Orientation (sign language)">
         ori
        </a>
       </i>
       (orientation). Facial
       <a href="/wiki/Expression_(sign_language)" title="Expression (sign language)">
        expression
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mouthing" title="Mouthing">
        mouthing
       </a>
       are also considered articulation bundles. Just as with spoken languages, when these bundles are combined, they create phonemes.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Stokoe_notation" title="Stokoe notation">
        Stokoe notation
       </a>
       is no longer used by researchers to denote the phonemes of sign languages; William Stokoe's research, while still considered seminal, has been found
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (June 2017)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       to not describe American Sign Language sufficiently and cannot be used interchangeably with other signed languages. Originally developed for
       <a href="/wiki/American_Sign_Language" title="American Sign Language">
        American Sign Language
       </a>
       , it has also been applied to
       <a href="/wiki/British_Sign_Language" title="British Sign Language">
        British Sign Language
       </a>
       by Kyle and Woll, and to
       <a href="/wiki/Australian_Aboriginal_sign_languages" title="Australian Aboriginal sign languages">
        Australian Aboriginal sign languages
       </a>
       by Adam Kendon. Other sign notations, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hamburg_Notation_System" title="Hamburg Notation System">
        Hamburg Notation System
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/SignWriting" title="SignWriting">
        SignWriting
       </a>
       , are phonetic scripts capable of writing any sign language. Stokoe's work has been succeeded and improved upon by researcher Scott Liddell in his book
       <i>
        Grammar, Gesture, and Meaning in American Sign Language
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and both Stokoe and Liddell's work have been included in
       <i>
        Linguistics of American Sign Language
       </i>
       , 5th Edition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
