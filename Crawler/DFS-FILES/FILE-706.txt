         [ˈbaʝe ðel ˈkauka]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Departments_of_Colombia" title="Departments of Colombia">
        department
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       . It is in the western side of the country, facing the Pacific Ocean. Its capital is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Cali" title="Santiago de Cali">
        Santiago de Cali
       </a>
       . Given its privileged location, lately it has been considered as the Pacific Door of Colombia. Besides Cali such cities as
       <a href="/wiki/Buenaventura,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Buenaventura, Valle del Cauca">
        Buenaventura
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cartago,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Cartago, Valle del Cauca">
        Cartago
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tulu%C3%A1" title="Tuluá">
        Tuluá
       </a>
       have great economical, political, social and cultural influence on the department's life. Valle del Cauca has the largest number of independent towns (i.e. not in Metropolitan areas) with over 100,000 inhabitants in the country, counting six within its borders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated54_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated54-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Buenaventura,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Buenaventura, Valle del Cauca">
        Buenaventura
       </a>
       has the largest and busiest seaport in Colombia, moving about 8,500,000 tons of merchandise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The anthem of Valle del Cauca Department is "Salve Valle del Cauca, mi tierra" ("Hail Valley of the Cauca, my land").
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The department of Valle del Cauca is located in the western part of the country, between 3° 05’ and 5° 01’ latitude N, 75° 42’ and 77° 33’ longitude W. It borders the departments of
       <a href="/wiki/Risaralda_Department" title="Risaralda Department">
        Risaralda
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quind%C3%ADo_Department" title="Quindío Department">
        Quindío
       </a>
       to the north,
       <a href="/wiki/Cauca_Department" title="Cauca Department">
        Cauca
       </a>
       to the south,
       <a href="/wiki/Tolima_Department" title="Tolima Department">
        Tolima
       </a>
       to the east, and
       <a href="/wiki/Choc%C3%B3_Department" title="Chocó Department">
        Chocó
       </a>
       and the Pacific Ocean to the west. The valley is geographically bounded by the Central and Western mountain ranges and is watered by numerous rivers which empty into the
       <a href="/wiki/Cauca_River" title="Cauca River">
        Cauca River
       </a>
       . The department is divided into four zones: the Pacific Fringe, which is humid and mostly jungle; the western mountain range, also humid and full of jungle, heavily deforested due to the paper industry; the Andean valley of the Cauca river, whose surrounding lands are the most fertile of the country; and the western ridge of the central mountain range.
      </p>

      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hunter-gatherer_societies">
        Hunter-gatherer societies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hunter-gatherer societies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Palynology" title="Palynology">
        Palynological
       </a>
       analyses performed by experts have determined that during the Superior
       <a href="/wiki/Pleistocene" title="Pleistocene">
        Pleistocene
       </a>
       some (40,000 – 10,500 years ago), the valleys of El Dorado and Alto Calima had Andean forest and Sub-Andean vegetation. The discovery of projectiles indicated that there were communities of hunter-gatherers at the end of the Pleistocene and the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/Holocene" title="Holocene">
        Holocene
       </a>
       . The extinction of the Pleistocenic megafauna in the beginning of the Holocene forced humans to adapt to their new environment, becoming hunter-gatherers. In the lower basin of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calima_River" title="Calima River">
        Calima River
       </a>
       (Sauzalito River, El Recreo River and El Pital River) archaeologists found the oldest traces of hunter-gatherers that inhabited the Valley of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cauca_River" title="Cauca River">
        Cauca River
       </a>
       . According to these, in 5000 BC these societies already had some level of primitive agriculture and cultivated maize. There is little information about the years between 3000 and 1500 BC.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span id="Agricultural.E2.80.93pottery_societies_.281500_BC_.E2.80.93_600_AD.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural–pottery_societies_(1500_BC_–_600_AD)">
        Agricultural–pottery societies (1500 BC – 600 AD)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Agricultural–pottery societies (1500 BC – 600 AD)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In 1500 BC the first agricultural–pottery society appeared, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilama_culture" title="Ilama culture">
        Ilama culture
       </a>
       , extending along the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calima_River" title="Calima River">
        Calima River
       </a>
       (in what is nowadays the towns of
       <a href="/wiki/Restrepo,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Restrepo, Valle del Cauca">
        Restrepo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Calima,_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Calima, Valle del Cauca">
        Darien
       </a>
       . Its society had a social structure of
       <i>
        Cacicazgos
       </i>
       (chiefdoms) that prevailed until the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">
        arrival of the Spaniards
       </a>
       . The Ilama economy was based on migratory agriculture using maize, yuca, and beans; hunting, fishing, textile confectioning and metallurgy. The Chief or "
       <i>
        Cacique
       </i>
       " was the head of the settlement and also had "
       <i>
        chamanes
       </i>
       " (spiritual leaders), warriors, farmers, hunters, pottery men, and goldsmiths. By 100 AD the Ilamas had developed into the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Yotoco_Culture&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Yotoco Culture (page does not exist)">
        Yotoco Culture
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultura_Yotoco" title="es:Cultura Yotoco">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       , which expanded the region of the Ilamas further into the Cauca River to the Pacific Ocean, and to the south to the region of what is now the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Cali" title="Cali">
        Cali
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Yotocos were a highly stratified society, headed by caciques, which managed several settlements. They existed in the region until 1200 AD. The population had increased, forcing them to develop effective agricultural techniques to feed its population which also improved the techniques on pottery and metal works. The agriculture of the Yotocos was more varied than that of the Ilamas and was based on maize,
       <a href="/wiki/Cassava" title="Cassava">
        yuca
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beans" title="Beans">
        beans
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arracacha" title="Arracacha">
        arracacha
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Achiote" title="Achiote">
        achiote
       </a>
       among others. The Yotoco started declining in the 6th century AD.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Agricultural.E2.80.93pottery_in_Pre-Columbian_era_.28600.E2.80.931600.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Agricultural–pottery_in_Pre-Columbian_era_(600–1600)">
        Agricultural–pottery in Pre-Columbian era (600–1600)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Agricultural–pottery in Pre-Columbian era (600–1600)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       This archeological period is called the Late Period and is divided into Late Period I (6th to 13th centuries) and Late Period II (14th to 16th centuries). In Late period I the region of Valle del Cauca was inhabited by the Early
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sonso_culture" title="Sonso culture">
        Sonso culture
       </a>
       , Bolo, Sachamate and La Llanada. During Late Period II the region was inhabited by the Late Sonso Culture, Pichinde, Buga and Quebrada Seca. Their development is attributed to population growth. Almost all the settlers in the area became subject to the rule of one main
       <i>
        Cacique
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arrival_of_the_Spanish_and_Conquest">
        Arrival of the Spanish and Conquest
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Arrival of the Spanish and Conquest">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first 67 Spanish explorers arrived in the area after founding the village of
       <a href="/wiki/Popay%C3%A1n" title="Popayán">
        Popayán
       </a>
       , in an expedition from
       <a href="/wiki/Quito" title="Quito">
        Quito
       </a>
       headed by
       <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_de_Belalc%C3%A1zar" title="Sebastián de Belalcázar">
        Sebastián de Belalcázar
       </a>
       . In the Valle del Cauca the explorers founded the village of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Villa_de_Ampudia&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Villa de Ampudia (page does not exist)">
        Villa de Ampudia
       </a>
       , named after one of them,
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Ampudia" title="Juan de Ampudia">
        Juan de Ampudia
       </a>
       . By orders of Belalcázar the village was then moved to the Riviera of the Cauca River, within the Gorrones indigenous people's territory. In 1536 a Captain Muñoz ordered the city to be moved to the Valley, where the Village of
       <a href="/wiki/Cali" title="Cali">
        Cali
       </a>
       was founded on 25 July of that same year. Another Spanish explorer,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_de_Vadillo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan de Vadillo (page does not exist)">
        Juan de Vadillo
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_de_Vadillo" title="es:Juan de Vadillo">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       , coming from the village of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartagena_de_Indias" title="Cartagena de Indias">
        Cartagena de Indias
       </a>
       , entered Cali on 23 December 1538 with a second group of explorers, but he returned to Cartagena, leaving many of his men behind including
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Cieza_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Pedro Cieza de León">
        Pedro Cieza de León
       </a>
       . A third group of explorers, led by Admiral
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_Robledo" title="Jorge Robledo">
        Jorge Robledo
       </a>
       under orders of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lorenzo_de_Aldana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lorenzo de Aldana (page does not exist)">
        Lorenzo de Aldana
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorenzo_de_Aldana" title="es:Lorenzo de Aldana">
         es
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       , advanced to the North of the Valle del Cauca and founded the villages of
       <a href="/wiki/Anserma,_Caldas" title="Anserma, Caldas">
        Anserma
       </a>
       (now part of
       <a href="/wiki/Caldas_Department" title="Caldas Department">
        Caldas Department
       </a>
       ; 15 August 1539),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartago,_Colombia" title="Cartago, Colombia">
        Cartago
       </a>
       (9 August 1540), and
       <a href="/wiki/Medell%C3%ADn" title="Medellín">
        Antioquia

       (25 November 1541), and under command of
       <a href="/wiki/Pascual_de_Andagoya" title="Pascual de Andagoya">

       who came from

       to Cali with a fourth group of explorers.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Department_of_Valle_del_Cauca">
        Department of Valle del Cauca
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Department of Valle del Cauca">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/History_of_Santiago_de_Cali" title="History of Santiago de Cali">
        History of Santiago de Cali
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Department of Valle del Cauca was created by decree number 340 April 16, 1910 which also created 12 other departments for Colombia. The Valle del Cauca Department was a result of the union of four former departments;
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cartago_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cartago Department (page does not exist)">
        Cartago
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Buga_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Buga Department (page does not exist)">
        Buga
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Cali_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Cali Department (page does not exist)">
        Cali
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Government">
        Government
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Government">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Government_of_Valle_del_Cauca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Government of Valle del Cauca (page does not exist)">
        Government of Valle del Cauca
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The government of Valle del Cauca is similarly set up as the
       <a href="/wiki/Government_of_Colombia" title="Government of Colombia">
        Government of Colombia
       </a>
       in which there are three branches of power; judicial, executive and legislative with control institutions at government level. The executive branch in Valle del Department is represented by the
       <a href="/wiki/Governor_of_Valle_del_Cauca_Department" title="Governor of Valle del Cauca Department">
        Governor of Valle del Cauca Department
       </a>
       , the legislative branch is represented by the
       <a href="/wiki/Department_Assembly_of_Valle_del_Cauca" title="Department Assembly of Valle del Cauca">
        Department Assembly of Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       and its deputies and the judicial is represented by the four department level of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judicial_Branch_of_Colombia" title="Judicial Branch of Colombia">
        Judicial Branch of Colombia
       </a>
       ;
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Superior_Tribunal_of_Cali&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Superior Tribunal of Cali (page does not exist)">
        Superior Tribunal of Cali
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Penal_Court_of_the_Circuit_of_Cali&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Penal Court of the Circuit of Cali (page does not exist)">
        Penal Court of the Circuit of Cali
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Administrative_Tribunal_of_Valle_del_Cauca&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Administrative Tribunal of Valle del Cauca (page does not exist)">
        Administrative Tribunal of Valle del Cauca
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Superior_Military_Tribunal&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Superior Military Tribunal (page does not exist)">
        Superior Military Tribunal
       </a>
       for military cases. Valle del Cauca Department has 42 municipalities, each one having a mayor which is a popularly elected representative of the governor.
      </p>

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

          [4]
         </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
        Economy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Economy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The department's economy is mainly centered on agriculture. Its valley contains
       <a href="/wiki/Sugarcane" title="Sugarcane">
        sugar cane
       </a>
       , cotton,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soy" title="Soy">
        soy
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sorghum" title="Sorghum">
        sorghum
       </a>
       crops, and there are coffee crops in the mountains. The department is known for its sugar industry, which provides sugar to the markets of the rest of the country and nearby countries. The sugar is obtained from the large sugar cane plantations, which were introduced to the department by
       <a href="/wiki/Sebasti%C3%A1n_de_Belalc%C3%A1zar" title="Sebastián de Belalcázar">
        Sebastián de Belalcázar
       </a>
       . The production by the city of Yumbo also stands out, where several companies are found, most prominently the paper and cement businesses. The port at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buenaventura,_Colombia" title="Buenaventura, Colombia">
        Buenaventura
       </a>
       is Colombia's main port on the Pacific coast, allowing for the import and export of goods, and is of great importance for the economy of both the department and the country.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Demographics">
        Demographics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Demographics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       More than 80% of the population lives in cities or towns. The coverage of public services is among the highest in the country, with electrical power and education standing out the most. The food most closely associated with the department is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sancocho" title="Sancocho">
         sancocho
        </a>
        de gallina
       </i>
       , a stew made with an old hen, potatoes, yucca, corn and other ingredients; the characteristic flavor comes from a herb called
       <i>
        cimarron
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        recao
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Eryngium_foetidum" title="Eryngium foetidum">
        Eryngium foetidum
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Population">
        Population
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Valle_del_Cauca_Department&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Population">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The capital of the department is Santiago de Cali, with approximately 2,800,000 inhabitants, was founded by Sebastián de Belalcázar in 1536. It is made up of 42 municipalities, the most populous being, from north to south, Cartago (Famous for its craftsmanship, its embroidery and for the "Casa del Virrey", House of the Viceroy), Roldanillo (Venue of the museum on the artist Omar Rayo), Tuluá (Located in the middle of the department), Yumbo (Industrial capital of the department venue for more than 2000 Industries of various types), Ginebra, Palmira, Buga and Jamundí.
      </p>
