      <p>
       <b>
        British Latin
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        British Vulgar Latin
       </b>
       was the
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">
        Roman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">
        sub-Roman
       </a>
       periods. While Britain formed part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       , Latin became the principal language of the elite, especially in the more Romanized south and east of the island. However, it never substantially replaced the
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">
        Brittonic language
       </a>
       of the indigenous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" title="Britons (Celtic people)">
        Britons
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       especially in the less Romanized north and west. In recent years, scholars have debated the extent to which British Latin was distinguishable from its continental counterparts, which developed into the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       With the end of Roman rule, Latin was displaced as a spoken language by
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       in most of what became
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">
        Anglo-Saxon settlement
       </a>
       of the fifth and sixth centuries. It survived in the remaining Celtic regions of western Britain until about 700, when it was replaced by the local
       <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">
        Brittonic languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       At the inception of Roman rule in AD 43,
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       was inhabited by the indigenous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Britons_(Celtic_people)" title="Britons (Celtic people)">
        Britons
       </a>
       , who spoke the
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       language known as
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Brittonic" title="Common Brittonic">
        Brittonic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Britannia" title="Britannia">
         Britannia
        </a>
       </i>
       became a province of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       and remained part of the empire for nearly four hundred years until 409, spanning at its height in 160 the southern three-quarters of the island of Britain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBH_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CBH-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Historians often refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Britain" title="Roman Britain">
        Roman Britain
       </a>
       as comprising a "highland zone" to the north and west of the country and a "lowland zone" in the south and east,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with the latter being more thoroughly
       <a href="/wiki/Romanization_(cultural)" title="Romanization (cultural)">
        Romanized
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and having a
       <a href="/wiki/Romano-British_culture" title="Romano-British culture">
        Romano-British culture
       </a>
       . Particularly in the lowland zone,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       became the language of most of the townspeople, of administration and the ruling class, the army and, following the introduction of Christianity, the church. Brittonic remained the language of the peasantry, which was the bulk of the population; members of the rural élite were probably bilingual.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the highland zone, there were only limited attempts at Romanization, and Brittonic always remained the dominant language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millar142_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Millar142-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Throughout much of western Europe, from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">
        Late Antiquity
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       of everyday speech developed into locally distinctive varieties which ultimately became the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, after the
       <a href="/wiki/End_of_Roman_rule_in_Britain" title="End of Roman rule in Britain">
        end of Roman rule in Britain
       </a>
       during the early 5th century, Vulgar Latin died out as an everyday spoken language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Godden_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Godden-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The time that Vulgar Latin died out as a vernacular in Britain, its nature and and its characteristics been points of scholarly debate in recent years.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_evidence">
        Sources of evidence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources of evidence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An inherent difficulty in evidencing Vulgar Latin is that as an extinct spoken language form, no source provides a direct account of it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Reliance is on indirect sources of evidence such as "errors" in written texts and regional inscriptions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are held to be reflective of the everyday spoken language. Of particular linguistic value are private inscriptions made by ordinary people, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epitaphs" title="Epitaphs">
        epitaphs
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Votive_offerings" title="Votive offerings">
        votive offerings
       </a>
       , and "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Curse_tablets" title="Curse tablets">
        curse tablets
       </a>
       " (small metal sheets used in popular magic to curse people).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In relation to Vulgar Latin specifically as it was spoken in Britain,
       <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_H._Jackson" title="Kenneth H. Jackson">
        Kenneth H. Jackson
       </a>
       put forward in the 1950s what became the established view, which has only relatively recently been challenged.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jackson drew conclusions about the nature of British Latin from examining Latin loanwords that had passed into the British Celtic languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wollmann14_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wollmann14-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From the 1970s John Mann,
       <a href="/wiki/Eric_P._Hamp" title="Eric P. Hamp">
        Eric P. Hamp
       </a>
       and others used what Mann called "the sub-literary tradition" in inscriptions to identify spoken British Latin usage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1980s, Colin Smith used stone inscriptions in particular in this way, although much of what Smith has written has become out of date as a result of the large amount of Latin inscriptions found in Britain in recent years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The best known of these are the
       <a href="/wiki/Vindolanda_tablets" title="Vindolanda tablets">
        Vindolanda tablets
       </a>
       , the last two volumes of which were published in 1994 and 2003, but also include the
       <a href="/wiki/Bath_curse_tablets" title="Bath curse tablets">
        Bath curse tablets
       </a>
       , published in 1988, and other curse tablets found at a number of other sites throughout southern England from the 1990s onwards.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_a_distinctive_language_variety">
        Evidence of a distinctive language variety
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Evidence of a distinctive language variety">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Kenneth Jackson argued for a form of British Vulgar Latin, distinctive from continental Vulgar Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson82_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jackson82-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In fact, he identified two forms of British Latin: a lower-class variety of the language not significantly different from Continental Vulgar Latin and a distinctive upper-class Vulgar Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wollmann14_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wollmann14-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This latter variety, Jackson believed, could be distinguished from Continental Vulgar Latin by 12 distinct criteria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson82_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jackson82-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In particular, he characterised it as a conservative, hypercorrect "school" Latin with a "sound-system [which] was very archaic by ordinary Continental standards".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jackson107_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jackson107-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In recent years, research into British Latin has led to modification of Jackson's fundamental assumptions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wollmann14_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wollmann14-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In particular, his identification of 12 distinctive criteria for upper-class British Latin has been severely criticised.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, although British Vulgar Latin was probably not substantially different from the Vulgar Latin of
       <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">
        Gaul
       </a>
       , over a period of 400 years of Roman rule, British Latin would almost certainly have developed distinctive traits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wollmann17_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wollmann17-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That and the likely impact of the Brittonic substrate both mean that a specific British Vulgar Latin variety most probably developed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wollmann17_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wollmann17-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, if it did exist as a distinct dialect group, it has not survived extensively enough for diagnostic features to be detected, despite much new subliterary Latin being discovered in England in the 20th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extinction_as_a_vernacular">
        Extinction as a vernacular
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Extinction as a vernacular">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Sub-Roman_Britain" title="Sub-Roman Britain">
        Sub-Roman Britain
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:232px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg">
         <img alt="Map of Anglo-Saxon Britain" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1233" data-file-width="994" height="285" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg/230px-Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg/345px-Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg/460px-Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg.png 2x" width="230"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Britain_peoples_circa_600.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The approximate extent of Anglo-Saxon expansion into the former Roman province of
         <i>
          Britannia
         </i>
         , by c.600
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       It is not known when Vulgar Latin ceased to be spoken in Britain,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but it is likely that it continued to be widely spoken in various parts of Britain into the 5th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller27_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller27-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the lowland zone, Vulgar Latin was replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       during the course of the 5th and the 6th centuries, but in the highland zone, it gave way to
       <a href="/wiki/Brittonic_languages" title="Brittonic languages">
        Brittonic languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primitive_Welsh" title="Primitive Welsh">
        Primitive Welsh
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cornish_language" title="Cornish language">
        Cornish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Godden_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Godden-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, scholars have had a variety of views as to when exactly it died out as a vernacular. The question has been described as "one of the most vexing problems of the languages of early Britain."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller25_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller25-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lowland_zone">
        Lowland zone
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Lowland zone">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In most of what was to become
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Saxon_settlement_of_Britain" title="Anglo-Saxon settlement of Britain">
        Anglo-Saxon settlement
       </a>
       and the consequent introduction of Old English appear to have caused the extinction of Vulgar Latin as a vernacular.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Anglo-Saxons, a
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
        Germanic people
       </a>
       , spread westward across Britain in the 5th century to the 7th century, leaving only
       <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">
        Cornwall
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wales" title="Wales">
        Wales
       </a>
       in the southern part of the country
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hen_Ogledd" title="Hen Ogledd">
        Hen Ogledd
       </a>
       in the north under British rule.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The demise of Vulgar Latin in the face of Anglo-Saxon settlement is very different from the fate of the language in other areas of Western Europe that were subject to
       <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
        Germanic migration
       </a>
       , like France, Italy and Spain, where Latin and the Romance languages continued.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The likely reason is that in Britain there was a greater collapse in Roman institutions and infrastructure, leading to a much greater reduction in the status and prestige of the indigenous romanized culture and so the indigenous people was more likely to abandon their languages, in favour of the higher-status language of the Anglo-Saxons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are, however, sporadic indications of its survival in the Celtic population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller27_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Miller27-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pockets of spoken Latin may have survived as isolates in the Anglo-Saxons. As late as the 8th century, the Saxon inhabitants of St Albans, near the Roman city of
       <a href="/wiki/Verulamium" title="Verulamium">
        Verulamium
       </a>
       were aware of their ancient neighbour, which they knew alternatively as
       <i>
        Verulamacæstir
       </i>
       (or, under what
       <a href="/wiki/H._R._Loyn" title="H. R. Loyn">
        H. R. Loyn
       </a>
       terms "their own hybrid",
       <i>
        Vaeclingscæstir
       </i>
       , "the fortress of the followers of Wæcla") interpretable as a pocket of Romano-Britons that remained within the Anglo-Saxon countryside, probably speaking their own local neo-Latin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="947" data-file-width="1200" height="174" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg/220px-Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg/330px-Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg/440px-Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Memoria.Voteporigis.Protictoris.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Rubbing of a 6th-century stone inscription in Latin found in West Wales in 1895: "
         <a href="/wiki/Vortiporius" title="Vortiporius">
          Monument of Voteporigis the Protector
         </a>
         ".
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
          <a href="#cite_note-32">
           [32]
          </a>
         </sup>
         According to
         <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Charles-Edwards" title="Thomas Charles-Edwards">
          Thomas Charles-Edwards
         </a>
         , the inscription provides "decisive evidence" of how long Vulgar Latin was spoken in this part of Britain.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
          <a href="#cite_note-33">
           [33]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Highland_zone">
        Highland zone
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=British_Latin&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Highland zone">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Before Roman rule ended, Brittonic had remained the dominant language in the highland zone.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Millar142_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Millar142-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the speakers of Vulgar Latin were significantly but temporarily boosted in the 5th century by the influx of Romano-Britons from the lowland zone who were fleeing the Anglo-Saxons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham168_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Higham168-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These refugees are traditionally characterised as being "upper class" and "upper middle class".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certainly, Vulgar Latin maintained a higher social status than Brittonic in the highland zone in the 6th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although Latin continued to be spoken by many of the British elite in western Britain,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by about 700, it had died out.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C-E_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-C-E-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The incoming Latin-speakers from the lowland zone seem to have rapidly assimilated with the existing population, and adopted Brittonic.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Higham168_34-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Higham168-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The continued viability of British Latin may have been negatively affected by the loss to Old English of the areas where it had been strongest: the Anglo-Saxon conquest of the lowland zone may have indirectly ensured that Vulgar Latin would not survive in the highland zone either.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The assimilation to Brittonic appears to be the exact opposite to the situation in France, where the collapse of towns and the migration of large numbers of Latin-speakers into the countryside apparently caused the final extinction of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
