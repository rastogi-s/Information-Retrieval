      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        Geodesy
       </a>
       (/dʒiːˈɒdɨsi/), also named geodetics, is the scientific discipline that deals with the measurement and representation of the Earth. The
       <b>
        history of geodesy
       </b>
       began in antiquity and blossomed during the
       <a href="/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment" title="Age of Enlightenment">
        Age of Enlightenment
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Early ideas about the figure of the Earth held the Earth to be flat (see
       <a href="/wiki/Flat_Earth" title="Flat Earth">
        flat Earth
       </a>
       ), and the heavens a physical dome spanning over it. Two early arguments for a spherical Earth were that lunar eclipses were seen as circular shadows which could only be caused by a spherical Earth, and that
       <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">
        Polaris
       </a>
       is seen lower in the sky as one travels South.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenic_world">
        Hellenic world
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hellenic world">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        early Greeks
       </a>
       , in their speculation and theorizing, ranged from the flat disc advocated by
       <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
        Homer
       </a>
       to the spherical body postulated by
       <a href="/wiki/Pythagoras" title="Pythagoras">
        Pythagoras
       </a>
       . Pythagoras's idea was supported later by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pythagoras was a mathematician and to him the most perfect figure was a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       . He reasoned that the gods would create a perfect figure and therefore the Earth was created to be spherical in shape.
       <a href="/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus" title="Anaximenes of Miletus">
        Anaximenes
       </a>
       , an early Greek philosopher, believed strongly that the Earth was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rectangular" title="Rectangular">
        rectangular
       </a>
       in shape.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since the spherical shape was the most widely supported during the Greek Era, efforts to determine its size followed.
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       determined the circumference of the Earth (which is slightly over 40,000 km) to be 400,000 stadia (between 62,800 and 74,000 km or 46,250 and 39,250 mi) while
       <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">
        Archimedes
       </a>
       estimated 300,000 stadia (48,300 km or 30,000 mi), using the Hellenic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadia_(length)" title="Stadia (length)">
        stadion
       </a>
       which scholars generally take to be 185 meters or ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         10
        </sub>
       </span>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_mile" title="Geographical mile">
        geographical mile
       </a>
       . Plato's figure was a guess and Archimedes' a more conservative approximation.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hellenistic_world">
        Hellenistic world
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hellenistic world">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Egypt, a Greek scholar and philosopher,
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       (276 BCE – 195 BCE), devised a method for a more explicit measurement of the earth's size. Reports from the ancient city of Swenett, later known as Syene, stated that on the day of the
       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">
        summer solstice
       </a>
       , the midday sun shone to the bottom of a well. He observed in
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">
        Alexandria
       </a>
       , that the sun was not directly overhead. Instead, it cast a shadow with the vertical equal to 1/50th of a circle (7° 12'). To these observations, Hellenistic Astronomy and knowledge of the local geography had already established that; (1) on the day of the summer solstice, the midday sun was directly over the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
        Tropic of Cancer
       </a>
       ; (2) Syene was on this tropic; (3) Alexandria and Syene lay on a direct north-south line; (4) The sun was a relatively long way away (which would later be known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical unit
       </a>
       ). He determined that distance between Alexandria and Syene was 5000 stadia, (possibly by having hired someone to walk and measure the distance, but more likely using reports of travelers of the distance) or at the usual Hellenic 185 m per stadion, about 925 km.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="512" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/300px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/450px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/600px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Eratosthenes' method for determining the size of the Earth
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From these observations, measurements, and/or "known" facts, Eratosthenes concluded that since the angular deviation of the sun from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vertical_direction" title="Vertical direction">
        vertical direction
       </a>
       at Alexandria was also the angle of the subtended arc (see illustration), the linear distance between Alexandria and Syene was 1/50 of the circumference of the Earth which thus must be 50×5000 = 250,000 stadia or probably 25,000 geographical miles. The circumference of the Earth is 24,902 mi (40,075.16 km). Over the poles it is more precisely 40,008 km or 24,860 mi. The actual unit of measure used by Eratosthenes was the stadion. No one knows for sure what his stadion equals in modern units, possibly it was the Hellenic 185 m stadion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Had the experiment been carried out as described, it would not be remarkable if it agreed with actuality. What is remarkable is that the result was probably only about 0.4% too high. His measurements were subject to several inaccuracies: (1) though at the summer solstice the noon sun is overhead at the Tropic of Cancer, Syene was not exactly on the tropic (which was at 23° 43' latitude in that day) but about 22 geographical miles to the north; (2) the difference of latitude between Alexandria (31.2 degrees north latitude) and Syene (24.1 degrees) is really 7.1 degrees rather than the perhaps rounded (1/50 of a circle) value of 7° 12' that Eratosthenes used; (3) the actual solstice
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenith_distance" title="Zenith distance">
        zenith distance
       </a>
       of the noon sun at Alexandria was 31° 12' − 23° 43' = 7° 29' or about 1/48 of a circle not 1/50 = 7° 12', an error closely consistent with use of a vertical
       <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">
        gnomon
       </a>
       which fixes not the sun's center but the solar upper
       <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">
        limb
       </a>
       16' higher; (4) the most importantly flawed element, whether he measured or adopted it, was the latitudinal distance from Alexandria to Syene (or the true Tropic somewhat further south) which appears to have caused the overestimate that relates to most of the error in his resulting circumference of the earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       A parallel later ancient measurement of the size of the Earth was made by another Greek scholar,
       <a href="/wiki/Posidonius" title="Posidonius">
        Posidonius
       </a>
       . He noted that the star
       <a href="/wiki/Canopus" title="Canopus">
        Canopus
       </a>
       was hidden from view in most parts of Greece but that it just grazed the horizon at Rhodes. Posidonius is supposed to have measured the angular elevation of Canopus at Alexandria and determined that the angle was 1/48 of circle. He used a distance from Alexandria to Rhodes, 5000 stadia, and so he computed the Earth's circumference in stadia as 48 times 5000 = 240,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some scholars see these results as luckily semi-accurate due to cancellation of errors. But since the Canopus observations are both mistaken by over a degree, the "experiment" may be not much more than a recycling of Eratosthenes's numbers, while altering 1/50 to the correct 1/48 of a circle. Later, either he or a follower appears to have altered the base distance to agree with Eratosthenes's Alexandria-to-Rhodes figure of 3750 stadia since Posidonius's final circumference was 180,000 stadia, which equals 48×3750 stadia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 180,000 stadia circumference of Posidonius is suspiciously close to that which results from another method of measuring the earth, by timing ocean sunsets from different heights, a method which is inaccurate due to horizontal
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        atmospheric refraction
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The abovementioned larger and smaller sizes of the Earth were those used by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">
        Claudius Ptolemy
       </a>
       at different times, 252,000 stadia in his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       and 180,000 stadia in his later
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ptolemy%27s_Geography" title="Ptolemy's Geography">
         Geography
        </a>
       </i>
       . His midcareer conversion resulted in the latter work's systematic exaggeration of degree longitudes in the Mediterranean by a factor close to the ratio of the two seriously differing sizes discussed here, which indicates
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that the conventional size of the earth was what changed, not the stadion.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_India">
        Ancient India
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Ancient India">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Indian mathematician
       <a href="/wiki/Aryabhata" title="Aryabhata">
        Aryabhata
       </a>
       (AD 476–550) was a pioneer of
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_astronomy" title="Indian astronomy">
        mathematical astronomy
       </a>
       . He describes the earth as being spherical and that it rotates on its axis, among other things in his work
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%C4%80ryabha%E1%B9%AD%C4%ABya" title="Āryabhaṭīya">
        Āryabhaṭīya
       </a>
       . Aryabhatiya is divided into four sections. Gitika, Ganitha (mathematics), Kalakriya (reckoning of time) and Gola (
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       ). The discovery that the earth rotates on its own axis from west to east is described in Aryabhatiya ( Gitika 3,6; Kalakriya 5; Gola 9,10;).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated51_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated51-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, he explained the apparent motion of heavenly bodies is only an illusion (Gola 9), with the following simile;
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Just as a passenger in a boat moving downstream sees the stationary (trees on the river banks) as traversing upstream, so does an observer on earth see the fixed stars as moving towards the west at exactly the same speed (at which the earth moves from west to east.)
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Aryabhatiya also estimates the circumference of Earth, with an error of 1%.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Aryabhata gives the radii of the orbits of the planets in terms of the Earth-Sun distance as essentially their periods of rotation around the Sun. He also gave the correct explanation of lunar and solar eclipses and that the Moon shines by reflecting sunlight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated51_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated51-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_world">
        Islamic world
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Islamic world">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_and_cartography_in_medieval_Islam#Mathematical_geography_and_geodesy" title="Geography and cartography in medieval Islam">
        Geography and cartography in medieval Islam: Mathematical geography and geodesy
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Muslim scholars, who held to the
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">
        spherical Earth
       </a>
       theory, used it to calculate the distance and direction from any given point on the earth to
       <a href="/wiki/Mecca" title="Mecca">
        Mecca
       </a>
       . This determined the
       <a href="/wiki/Qibla" title="Qibla">
        Qibla
       </a>
       , or Muslim direction of prayer.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_mathematics" title="Arabic mathematics">
        Muslim mathematicians
       </a>
       developed
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry" title="Spherical trigonometry">
        spherical trigonometry
       </a>
       which was used in these calculations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Around AD 830 Caliph
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Ma%27mun" title="Al-Ma'mun">
        al-Ma'mun
       </a>
       commissioned a group of astronomers led by
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Musa_al-Khwarizmi" title="Muhammad ibn Musa al-Khwarizmi">
        Al-Khwarizmi
       </a>
       to measure the distance from Tadmur (
       <a href="/wiki/Palmyra" title="Palmyra">
        Palmyra
       </a>
       ) to
       <a href="/wiki/Raqqa" title="Raqqa">
        Raqqa
       </a>
       , in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">
        Syria
       </a>
       . They found the cities to be separated by one degree of latitude and the distance between them to be 66​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       miles
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="which mile? (January 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and thus calculated the Earth's circumference to be 24,000 miles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another estimate given was 56​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       Arabic miles per degree, which corresponds to 111.8 km per degree and a circumference of 40,248 km, very close to the currently modern values of 111.3 km per degree and 40,068 km circumference, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_astronomy" title="Islamic astronomy">
        Muslim astronomers
       </a>
       and geographers were aware of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_declination" title="Magnetic declination">
        magnetic declination
       </a>
       by the 15th century, when the Egyptian astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/%27Abd_al-%27Aziz_al-Wafa%27i" title="'Abd al-'Aziz al-Wafa'i">
        'Abd al-'Aziz al-Wafa'i
       </a>
       (d. 1469/1471) measured it as 7 degrees from
       <a href="/wiki/Cairo" title="Cairo">
        Cairo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Al-Biruni">
        Al-Biruni
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Al-Biruni">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Of the medieval
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Persia" title="Persia">
        Persian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abu_Rayhan_al-Biruni" title="Abu Rayhan al-Biruni">
        Abu Rayhan al-Biruni
       </a>
       (973–1048) it is said:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "Important contributions to geodesy and geography were also made by Biruni. He introduced techniques to measure the earth and distances on it using triangulation. He found the radius of the earth to be 6339.6 km
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
           <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (October 2017)">
            dubious
           </span>
          </a>
          <span class="metadata">
           –
           <a href="/wiki/Talk:History_of_geodesy#Islamic_world/al-Biruni_-_The_Unbearable_Lightness_of_Measuring" title="Talk:History of geodesy">
            discuss
           </a>
          </span>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        , a value not obtained in the West until the 16th century. His
        <i>
         Masudic canon
        </i>
        contains a table giving the coordinates of six hundred places, almost all of which he had direct knowledge."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       At the age of 17, Al-Biruni calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       of Kath,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Khwarazm" title="Khwarazm">
        Khwarazm
       </a>
       , using the maximum altitude of the Sun. Al-Biruni also solved a complex geodesic equation in order to accurately compute the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">
        circumference
       </a>
       , which were close to modern values of the Earth's circumference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khwarizm_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Khwarizm-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His estimate of 6,339.6 km for the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        Earth radius
       </a>
       was only 17.15 km less than the modern value of 6,356.7523142 km (WGS84 polar radius "b"). In contrast to his predecessors who measured the Earth's circumference by sighting the Sun simultaneously from two different locations, Al-Biruni developed a new method of using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trigonometric" title="Trigonometric">
        trigonometric
       </a>
       calculations based on the angle between a
       <a href="/wiki/Plain" title="Plain">
        plain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain" title="Mountain">
        mountain
       </a>
       top which yielded more accurate measurements of the Earth's circumference and made it possible for it to be measured by a single person from a single location.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savizi_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savizi-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lumpkin_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lumpkin-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Al-Biruni's method's motivation was to avoid "walking across hot, dusty deserts" and the idea came to him when he was on top of a tall mountain in India (present day
       <a href="/wiki/Pind_Dadan_Khan" title="Pind Dadan Khan">
        Pind Dadan Khan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
        Pakistan
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lumpkin_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lumpkin-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From the top of the mountain, he sighted the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dip" title="Magnetic dip">
        dip angle
       </a>
       which, along with the mountain's height (which he calculated beforehand), he applied to the
       <a href="/wiki/Law_of_sines" title="Law of sines">
        law of sines
       </a>
       formula. This was the earliest known use of dip angle and the earliest practical use of the law of sines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Savizi_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Savizi-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lumpkin_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lumpkin-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also made use of
       <a href="/wiki/Algebra" title="Algebra">
        algebra
       </a>
       to formulate trigonometric equations and used the
       <a href="/wiki/Astrolabe" title="Astrolabe">
        astrolabe
       </a>
       to measure angles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Khalili_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Khalili-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Identifying_reliable_sources" title="Wikipedia:Identifying reliable sources">
          <span title="The material near this tag relies on an unreliable source. Television documentaries are not RS; narrator relies on very dubious source himself. (October 2017)">
           unreliable source
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . His method can be summarized as follows:
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Abu_Reyhan_Biruni-Earth_Circumference.svg">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

         <a href="/wiki/Trigonometry" title="Trigonometry">
          trigonometric
         </a>
         equation relating the dip angle (between the true
         <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
          horizon
         </a>
         and astronomical horizon) observed from the top of a mountain to the height of that mountain.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>

       </i>

        h
       </i>

      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        map projections
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
         Cartography
        </a>
       </i>
       , which included a method for projecting a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        hemisphere
       </a>
       on a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_(mathematics)" title="Plane (mathematics)">
        plane
       </a>
       .

        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">

           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_equidistant_projection" title="Azimuthal equidistant projection">
        azimuthal equidistant projection
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was also regarded as the most skilled when it came to mapping cities and measuring the distances between them, which he did for many cities in the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       and western
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       . He often combined astronomical readings and mathematical equations, in order to develop methods of pin-pointing locations by recording degrees of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       . He also developed similar techniques when it came to measuring the heights of mountains, depths of
       <a href="/wiki/Valley" title="Valley">
        valleys
       </a>
       , and expanse of the horizon, in
       <i>
        The Chronology of the Ancient Nations
       </i>
       . He also discussed
       <a href="/wiki/Human_geography" title="Human geography">
        human geography
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">
        planetary habitability
       </a>
       of the Earth. He hypothesized that roughly a quarter of the Earth's surface is habitable by humans, and also argued that the shores of Asia and Europe were "separated by a vast sea, too dark and dense to navigate and too risky to try".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Medieval_Europe">
        Medieval Europe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Medieval Europe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Revising the figures attributed to Posidonius, another Greek philosopher determined 18,000 miles as the Earth's circumference. This last figure was promulgated by
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       through his world maps. The maps of Ptolemy strongly influenced the cartographers of the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       . It is probable that
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       , using such maps, was led to believe that Asia was only 3 or 4 thousand miles west of Europe.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's view was not universal, however, and chapter 20 of
       <i>
        Mandeville's Travels
       </i>
       (c. 1357) supports Eratosthenes' calculation.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was not until the 16th century that his concept of the Earth's size was revised. During that period the Flemish cartographer,
       <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Mercator" title="Gerardus Mercator">
        Mercator
       </a>
       , made successive reductions in the size of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       and all of Europe which had the effect of increasing the size of the earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_period">
        Early modern period
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Early modern period">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Theodolite" title="Theodolite">
        theodolite
       </a>
       and the development of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithm_table" title="Logarithm table">
        logarithm tables
       </a>
       allowed exact
       <a href="/wiki/Triangulation" title="Triangulation">
        triangulation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grade_measurement" title="Grade measurement">
        grade measurement
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
        Europe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Europe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1505 the cosmographer and explorer
       <a href="/wiki/Duarte_Pacheco_Pereira" title="Duarte Pacheco Pereira">
        Duarte Pacheco Pereira
       </a>
       calculated the value of the degree of the
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">
        meridian arc
       </a>
       with a margin of error of only 4%, when the current error at the time varied between 7 and 15%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Picard" title="Jean Picard">
        Jean Picard
       </a>
       performed the first modern meridian arc measurement in 1669–1670. He measured a
       <a href="/wiki/Baseline_(surveying)" title="Baseline (surveying)">
        baseline
       </a>
       using wooden rods, a telescope (for his
       <a href="/wiki/Angle#Angles_in_geography_and_astronomy" title="Angle">
        angular measurements
       </a>
       ), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Logarithms" title="Logarithms">
        logarithms
       </a>
       (for computation).
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cassini" title="Jacques Cassini">
        Jacques Cassini
       </a>
       later continued Picard's arc (
       <a href="/wiki/Paris_meridian" title="Paris meridian">
        Paris meridian
       </a>
       arc) northward to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dunkirk,_France" title="Dunkirk, France">
        Dunkirk
       </a>
       and southward to the
       <a href="/wiki/France%E2%80%93Spain_border" title="France–Spain border">
        Spanish border
       </a>
       . Cassini divided the measured arc into two parts, one northward from
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       , another southward. When he computed the length of a degree from both chains, he found that the length of one degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       in the northern part of the chain was shorter than that in the southern part (see illustration).
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID;_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="900" data-file-width="623" height="289" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF/200px-CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF/300px-CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF/400px-CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID%3B_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CASSINIS%27_ELLIPSOID;_HUYGEN%27S_THEORETICAL_ELLIPSOID.GIF" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cassini's ellipsoid; Huygens' theoretical ellipsoid
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       This result, if correct, meant that the earth was not a sphere, but a
       <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
        prolate spheroid
       </a>
       (taller than wide). However, this contradicted computations by
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens" title="Christiaan Huygens">
        Christiaan Huygens
       </a>
       . Newton's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Law of universal gravitation">
        theory of gravitation
       </a>
       predicted the Earth to be an
       <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
        oblate spheroid
       </a>
       (wider than tall), with a
       <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">
        flattening
       </a>
       of 1:230.
      </p>
      <p>
       The issue could be settled by measuring, for a number of points on earth, the relationship between their distance (in north-south direction) and the angles between their
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zeniths
       </a>
       . On an oblate Earth, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meridional" title="Meridional">
        meridional
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">
        distance corresponding to one degree of longitude
       </a>
       will grow toward the poles, as can be
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc#Meridian_distance_on_the_ellipsoid" title="Meridian arc">
        demonstrated mathematically
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">
        French Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       dispatched two expeditions. One expedition (1736–37) under
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Louis_Maupertuis" title="Pierre Louis Maupertuis">
        Pierre Louis Maupertuis
       </a>
       was sent to
       <a href="/wiki/Me%C3%A4nmaa#French_Geodesic_Mission" title="Meänmaa">
        Torne Valley
       </a>
       (near the Earth's northern pole). The
       <a href="/wiki/French_Geodesic_Mission" title="French Geodesic Mission">
        second mission
       </a>
       (1735–44) under
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bouguer" title="Pierre Bouguer">
        Pierre Bouguer
       </a>
       was sent to what is modern-day
       <a href="/wiki/Ecuador" title="Ecuador">
        Ecuador
       </a>
       , near the equator. Their measurements demonstrated an oblate Earth, with a flattening of 1:210. This approximation to the true shape of the Earth became the new
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Asia_and_Americas">
        Asia and Americas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Asia and Americas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       Bouguer noticed, as did
       <a href="/wiki/George_Everest" title="George Everest">
        George Everest
       </a>
       in the 19th century
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Trigonometric_Survey" title="Great Trigonometric Survey">
        Great Trigonometric Survey
       </a>
       of India, that the astronomical vertical tended to be pulled in the direction of large mountain ranges, due to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational
       </a>
       attraction of these huge piles of rock. As this vertical is everywhere perpendicular to the idealized surface of mean sea level, or the
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       , this means that the figure of the Earth is even more irregular than an ellipsoid of revolution. Thus the study of the "
       <a href="/wiki/Undulation_of_the_geoid" title="Undulation of the geoid">
        undulation of the geoid
       </a>
       " became the next great undertaking in the science of studying the figure of the Earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">
        19th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=History_of_geodesy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 19th century">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Puissant,_Louis_%E2%80%93_Trait%C3%A9_de_g%C3%A9od%C3%A9sie,_1842_%E2%80%93_BEIC_583789.jpg">

          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Louis_Puissant" title="Louis Puissant">

         </a>
         ,
         <i>
          Traité de géodésie
         </i>
         , 1842
        </div>

      <p>
       In the late 19th century the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Mitteleuropäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       (Central European Arc Measurement) was established by several central European countries and a Central Bureau was set up at the expense of
       <a href="/wiki/Prussia" title="Prussia">
        Prussia
       </a>
       , within the Geodetic Institute at Berlin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One of its most important goals was the derivation of an international
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       formula which should be optimal not only for
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       but also for the whole world. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Mitteleuropäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       was an early predecessor of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        International Association of Geodesy
       </a>
       (IAG) one of the constituent sections of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Union_of_Geodesy_and_Geophysics" title="International Union of Geodesy and Geophysics">
        International Union of Geodesy and Geophysics
       </a>
       (IUGG) which was founded in 1919.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1860, the Russian Government at the instance of Otto Sturves invited the Governments of Belgium, France, Prussia and England to connect their triangulations in order to measure the length of an arc of parallel in latitude 52° and to test the accuracy of the figure and dimensions of the Earth, as derived from the measurements of arc of meridian. In order to combine the measurements, it was necessary to compare the geodetic standards of length used in the different countries. The British Government invited those of France, Belgium, Prussia, Russia, India, Australia, Austria, Spain, United States and Cape of Good Hope to send their standards to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">
        Ordnance Survey
       </a>
       office in Southampton. Notably the standards of France, Spain and United States were based on the metric system, whereas those of Prussia, Belgium and Russia where calibrated against the
       <a href="/wiki/Toise" title="Toise">
        toise
       </a>
       , of which the oldest physical representative was the Toise of Peru. The Toise of Peru had been constructed in 1735 for
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Bouguer" title="Pierre Bouguer">
        Bouguer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Marie_de_La_Condamine" title="Charles Marie de La Condamine">
        De La Condamine
       </a>
       as their standard of reference in the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Geodesic_Mission" title="French Geodesic Mission">
        French Geodesic Mission
       </a>
       , conducted in actual Ecuador from 1735 to 1744 in collaboration with the Spanish officers
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Juan_y_Santacilia" title="Jorge Juan y Santacilia">
        Jorge Juan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Ulloa" title="Antonio de Ulloa">
        Antonio de Ulloa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ross_Clarke" title="Alexander Ross Clarke">
        Alexander Ross Clarke
       </a>
       and Henry James published the first results of the standards' comparisons in 1867. The same year Russia, Spain and Portugal joined the "
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Europäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       " and the General Conference of the association proposed the
       <a href="/wiki/Metre" title="Metre">
        metre
       </a>
       as a uniform length standard for the Arc Measurement and recommended the establishment of an
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Europäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       decided the creation of an international geodetic standard at the General Conference held in Paris in 1875. The
       <a href="/wiki/Metre_Convention" title="Metre Convention">
        Metre Convention
       </a>
       was signed in 1875 in Paris and the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       was created under the supervision of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">
        International Committee for Weights and Measures
       </a>
       . The first president of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Committee_for_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Committee for Weights and Measures">
        International Committee for Weights and Measures
       </a>
       was the Spanish geodesist
       <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_e_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_de_Ibero" title="Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero">
        Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero
       </a>
       . He also was the president of the Permanent Commission of the "
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Europäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       " from 1874 to 1886. In 1886 the association changed name for the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        International Geodetic Association
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carlos_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_e_Ib%C3%A1%C3%B1ez_de_Ibero" title="Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero">
        Carlos Ibáñez e Ibáñez de Ibero
       </a>
       was reelected as president. He remained in this position until his death in 1891. During this period the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        International Geodetic Association
       </a>
       gained world wide importance with the joining of United States, Mexico, Chile, Argentina and Japan. In 1883 the General Conference of the "
       <a href="/wiki/International_Association_of_Geodesy" title="International Association of Geodesy">
        Europäische Gradmessung
       </a>
       " proposed to select the
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian_(Greenwich)" title="Prime meridian (Greenwich)">
        Greenwich meridian
       </a>
       as the prime meridian in the hope that
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_of_Great_Britain_and_Ireland" title="United Kingdom of Great Britain and Ireland">
        Great Britain
       </a>
       would accede to the
       <a href="/wiki/Metre_Convention" title="Metre Convention">
        Metre Convention
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Most of the relevant theories were derived by the German geodesist
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Robert_Helmert" title="Friedrich Robert Helmert">
        Friedrich Robert Helmert
       </a>
       in his famous books
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=qt2CAAAAIAAJ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Die mathematischen und physikalischen Theorieen der höheren Geodäsie
        </i>
        , Einleitung und 1. Teil
       </a>
       (1880) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=p9-CAAAAIAAJ" rel="nofollow">
        2. Teil
       </a>
       (1884); English translation:
       <a class="external text" href="http://geographiclib.sourceforge.net/geodesic-papers/helmert80-en.html" rel="nofollow">
        Mathematical and Physical Theories of Higher Geodesy, Vol. 1
       </a>
       and
       <a class="external text" href="http://geographiclib.sourceforge.net/geodesic-papers/helmert84-en.html" rel="nofollow">
        Vol. 2
       </a>
       . Helmert also derived the first global ellipsoid in 1906 with an accuracy of 100 meters (0.002 percent of the Earth's radii). The
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        US
       </a>
       geodesist
       <a href="/wiki/John_Fillmore_Hayford" title="John Fillmore Hayford">
        Hayford
       </a>
       derived a global ellipsoid in ~1910, based on intercontinental
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostasy
       </a>
       and an accuracy of 200 m. It was adopted by the IUGG as "international ellipsoid 1924".
      </p>
