       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
        Tagalog language
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Filipino-Tagalog-Not-So-Simple_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Filipino-Tagalog-Not-So-Simple-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       an
       <a href="/wiki/Austronesian_languages" title="Austronesian languages">
        Austronesian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional_languages" title="Regional languages">
        regional
       </a>
       language that is widely spoken in the Philippines. As of 2007, Tagalog is the
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first language
       </a>
       of 28 million people,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NationalencyklopedinTagalog_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NationalencyklopedinTagalog-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or about one-third of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_population" title="Philippine population">
        Philippine population
       </a>
       , while 45 million speak Tagalog as their
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-e18_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-e18-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tagalog is among the 185
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Philippines" title="Languages of the Philippines">
        languages of the Philippines
       </a>
       identified in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
         Ethnologue
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Officially, Filipino is defined by the
       <a href="/wiki/Commission_on_the_Filipino_Language" title="Commission on the Filipino Language">
        Commission on the Filipino Language
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Komisyon sa Wikang Filipino
       </i>
       in Filipino or simply KWF) as "the native dialect, spoken and written, in
       <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">
        Metro Manila
       </a>
       , the National Capital Region, and in other urban centers of the archipelago."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Resolution-92-1_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Resolution-92-1-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Filipino is taken to be a
       <a href="/wiki/Pluricentric_language" title="Pluricentric language">
        pluricentric language
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Indeed, there have been observed "emerging varieties of Filipino which deviate from the grammatical properties of Tagalog" in
       <a href="/wiki/Cebu" title="Cebu">
        Cebu
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Constantino2000_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Constantino2000-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Davao_City" title="Davao City">
        Davao City
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iloilo" title="Iloilo">
        Iloilo
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which together with Metro Manila form the four largest metropolitan areas in the Philippines. In reality, however, Filipino has been variously described as "simply Tagalog in syntax and grammar, with no grammatical element or lexicon coming from ... other major Philippine dialects,"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-InquirerJulian_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-InquirerJulian-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and as "essentially a formalized version of Tagalog."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In most contexts, Filipino is understood to be an alternative name for Tagalog,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or the Metro Manila dialect of Tagalog.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There was no common language in the Philippine archipelago when the Spanish arrived in the 16th century. The four major
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        trade languages
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" title="Visayan languages">
        Visayan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kapampangan_language" title="Kapampangan language">
        Kapampangan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pangasinan_language" title="Pangasinan language">
        Pangasinan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ilocano_language" title="Ilocano language">
        Ilocano
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       As the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_languages" title="Philippine languages">
        Philippine languages
       </a>
       are all closely related and therefore easy for Filipinos to learn, most speakers of smaller languages spoke two or more of such regional languages.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first dictionary of Tagalog was written by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">
        Franciscan
       </a>
       Pedro de San Buenaventura,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vocabularioAmbeth_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-vocabularioAmbeth-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and published in 1613 by the "Father of Filipino Printing"
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tomas_Pinpin" title="Tomas Pinpin">
        Tomas Pinpin
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Pila,_Laguna" title="Pila, Laguna">
        Pila
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Laguna_(province)" title="Laguna (province)">
        Laguna
       </a>
       . A latter book of the same name was written by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_people" title="Czech people">
        Czech
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuit" title="Jesuit">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       missionary
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Clain" title="Pablo Clain">
        Paul Klein
       </a>
       at the beginning of the 18th century. Klein spoke Tagalog and used it actively in several of his books. He wrote the first dictionary, which he later passed over to Francisco Jansens and José Hernandez.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further compilation of his substantial work was prepared by Juan de Noceda and Pedro de Sanlucar and published as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vocabulario_de_la_Lengua_Tagala" title="Vocabulario de la Lengua Tagala">
         Vocabulario de la Lengua Tagala
        </a>
       </i>
       in Manila in 1754 and then repeatedly
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       re-edited with the last edition being in 2013 in Manila.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On November 13, 1936, Commonwealth act No. 184 created the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Language_Institute" title="National Language Institute">
        National Language Institute
       </a>
       and tasked it with making a study and survey of each existing native language, hoping to choose which was to be the base for a standardized national language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On November 12, 1937, the First National Assembly of the Philippine Commonwealth approved the law for the establishment of the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surian_ng_Wikang_Pambansa" title="Surian ng Wikang Pambansa">
         Surián ng Wikang Pambansâ
        </a>
       </i>
       (National Language Institute; NLI). This institute would be responsible for surveying and researching existing native languages in order to determine among them the basis for an artificial 'national language of the Philippines'. Then-president
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_L._Quezon" title="Manuel L. Quezon">
        Manuel L. Quezon
       </a>
       later appointed representatives for each major regional language to form NLI. Led by
       <a href="/wiki/Jaime_C._de_Veyra" title="Jaime C. de Veyra">
        Jaime C. De Veyra
       </a>
       , who sat as the chair of the Institute and as the representative of
       <a href="/wiki/Waray_language" title="Waray language">
        Samar-Leyte-Visayans
       </a>
       , the Institute's members were composed of Santiago A. Fonacier (representing the
       <a href="/wiki/Ilocano_language" title="Ilocano language">
        Ilokano-speaking regions
       </a>
       ), Filemon Sotto (the
       <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">
        Cebu-Visayans
       </a>
       ), Casimiro Perfecto (the
       <a href="/wiki/Bikol_languages" title="Bikol languages">
        Bikolanos
       </a>
       ), Felix S. Sales Rodriguez (the
       <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">
        Panay-Visayans
       </a>
       ), Hadji Butu (the languages of Filipino
       <a href="/wiki/Moro_people" title="Moro people">
        Muslims
       </a>
       ), and Cecilio Lopez (the
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_language" title="Tagalog language">
        Tagalogs
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspillera_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-aspillera-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On December 13, 1937, President of the new 'national language,' Quezon issued Executive order No. 134, s. 1937, approving the adoption of Tagalog as the language of the Philippines', and declared and proclaimed the national language so based on the Tagalog dialect as the national language of the Philippines.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On December 31 of the same year, Quezon proclaimed Tagalog as the basis of the
       <i>
        Wikang Pambansâ
       </i>
       (National Language) based on the following factors:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aspillera_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-aspillera-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Tagalog is widely spoken and is the most understood in all the
        <a href="/wiki/Regions_of_the_Philippines" title="Regions of the Philippines">
         Philippine Regions
        </a>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        It is not divided into smaller daughter languages, as
        <a href="/wiki/Visayan_languages" title="Visayan languages">
         Visayan
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Bikol_languages" title="Bikol languages">
         Bikol
        </a>
        are;
       </li>
       <li>
        Its literary tradition is the richest of all native Philippine languages, the most developed and extensive (mirroring that of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuscan_language" title="Tuscan language">
         Tuscan language
        </a>
        <i>
         vis-à-vis
        </i>
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        ). More books are written in Tagalog than in any other
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autochthonous_language" title="Autochthonous language">
         autochthonous
        </a>
        Philippine language but Spanish, but this is mainly by virtue of law and privilege;
       </li>
       <li>
        Tagalog has always been the language of
        <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">
         Manila
        </a>
        , the political and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Concentration_of_wealth" title="Concentration of wealth">
         economic center
        </a>
        of the Philippines during the Spanish and American eras.
       </li>
       <li>
        Spanish was the language of the 1896
        <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Revolution" title="Philippine Revolution">
         Revolution
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Katipunan" title="Katipunan">
         Katipunan
        </a>
        , but the revolution was led by people who also spoke Tagalog.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       In 1959, the language became known as
       <i>
        Pilipino
       </i>
       in an effort to dissociate it from the
       <a href="/wiki/Tagalog_people" title="Tagalog people">
        Tagalog ethnic group
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gonzalez98_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gonzalez98-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Constitution_of_the_Philippines#1973_constitution" title="Constitution of the Philippines">
        1973 Constitution
       </a>
       , in both its original form and as amended in 1976, designated English and Pilipino as official languages and provided for development and formal adoption of a common national language, termed
       <i>
        Filipino
       </i>
       , to replace Pilipino. However, neither the original nor the amended version specified either Tagalog or Pilipino as the basis for Filipino. Instead they tasked the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Assembly_of_the_Philippines" title="National Assembly of the Philippines">
        National Assembly
       </a>
       to:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        take steps toward the development and formal adoption of a common national language to be known as Filipino.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This move has drawn much criticism from the nation's other ethnic groups.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1987, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1987_Constitution_of_the_Philippines" title="1987 Constitution of the Philippines">
        new constitution
       </a>
       designated Filipino as the national language and, along with English, as an official language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That constitution included several provisions related to the Filipino language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Article XIV, Section 6, omits any mention of Tagalog as the basis for Filipino, and states that:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-1987_constitution_art_14_sec_6-7-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        as Filipino evolves, it shall be further developed and enriched on the basis of existing Philippine and other languages.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       And also states in the article:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Subject to provisions of law and as the Congress may deem appropriate, the Government shall take steps to initiate and sustain the use of Filipino as a medium of official communication and as language of instruction in the educational system.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       and:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The regional languages are the auxiliary official languages in the regions and shall serve as auxiliary media of instruction therein.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Republic Act No. 7104, approved on August 14, 1991, created the Commission on the Filipino Language, reporting directly to the President and tasked to undertake, coordinate and promote researches for the development, propagation and preservation of Filipino and other Philippine languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On May 13, 1992, the commission issued Resolution 92-1, specifying that Filipino is the
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        indigenous written and spoken language of
        <a href="/wiki/Metro_Manila" title="Metro Manila">
         Metro Manila
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_cities_in_the_Philippines" title="List of cities in the Philippines">
         other urban centers
        </a>
        in the Philippines used as the language of communication of
        <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_the_Philippines" title="Ethnic groups in the Philippines">
         ethnic groups
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Res921_32-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Res921-32">
          [32]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       However, as with the 1973 and 1987 Constitutions, 92-1 neither went so far as to categorically identify nor dis-identify this language as Tagalog. Definite, absolute, and unambiguous interpretation of 92-1 is the prerogative of the
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_Philippines" title="Supreme Court of the Philippines">
        Supreme Court
       </a>
       in the absence of directives from the KWF, otherwise the sole legal arbiter of the Filipino language.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:No_original_research" title="Wikipedia:No original research">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly contains original research. (February 2017)">
           original research?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Filipino was presented and registered with the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization" title="International Organization for Standardization">
        International Organization for Standardization
       </a>
       (ISO), by Ateneo de Manila University student Martin Gomez, and was added to the ISO registry of languages on September 21, 2004 with it receiving the ISO
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_ISO_639-2_codes" title="List of ISO 639-2 codes">
        639-2 code
       </a>
       <i>
        fil
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISO_639-2_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ISO_639-2-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In June 2007, Ricardo Maria Nolasco, Chair of the
       <i>
        Komisyon sa Wikang Filipino
       </i>
       (Commission on the Filipino Language), acknowledged that Filipino was simply Tagalog in syntax and grammar, with as yet no grammatical element or lexicon coming from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ilokano_language" title="Ilokano language">
        Ilokano
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cebuano_language" title="Cebuano language">
        Cebuano
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hiligaynon_language" title="Hiligaynon language">
        Hiligaynon
       </a>
       , or any of the other
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_languages" title="Philippine languages">
        Philippine languages
       </a>
       . He said further that this is contrary to the intention of Republic Act No. 7104 that requires that the
       <a href="/wiki/National_language" title="National language">
        national language
       </a>
       be developed and enriched by the lexicon of the country's other languages, something that the commission is working towards.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nolasco07_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nolasco07-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 24 August 2007, Nolasco elaborated further on the relationship between Tagalog and Filipino in a separate article, as follows:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Are "Tagalog," "Pilipino" and "Filipino" different languages? No, they are mutually intelligible varieties, and therefore belong to one language. According to the KWF, Filipino is that speech variety spoken in Metro Manila and other urban centers where different ethnic groups meet. It is the most prestigious variety of Tagalog and the language used by the national mass media.
       </p>
       <p>
        The other yardstick for distinguishing a language from a dialect is: different grammar, different language. "Filipino", "Pilipino" and "Tagalog" share identical grammar. They have the same determiners (ang, ng and sa); the same personal pronouns (siya, ako, niya, kanila, etc.); the same demonstrative pronouns (ito, iyan, doon, etc.); the same linkers (na, at and ay); the same particles (na and pa); and the same verbal affixes -in, -an, i- and -um-. In short, same grammar, same language.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-prestige_35-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-prestige-35">
          [35]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       On 22 August 2007, it was reported that three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malolos_City" title="Malolos City">
        Malolos City
       </a>
       regional trial courts in
       <a href="/wiki/Bulacan" title="Bulacan">
        Bulacan
       </a>
       decided to use Filipino, instead of
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_English" title="Philippine English">
        English
       </a>
       , in order to promote the national language. Twelve
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stenographers" title="Stenographers">
        stenographers
       </a>
       from Branches 6, 80 and 81, as model courts, had undergone training at Marcelo H. del Pilar College of Law of
       <a href="/wiki/Bulacan_State_University" title="Bulacan State University">
        Bulacan State University
       </a>
       following a directive from the
       <a href="/wiki/Supreme_Court_of_the_Philippines" title="Supreme Court of the Philippines">
        Supreme Court of the Philippines
       </a>
       . De la Rama said it was the dream of Chief Justice
       <a href="/wiki/Reynato_Puno" title="Reynato Puno">
        Reynato Puno
       </a>
       to implement the program in other areas such as
       <a href="/wiki/Laguna_(province)" title="Laguna (province)">

       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cavite" title="Cavite">
        Cavite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quezon" title="Quezon">

       </a>

       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">

      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Filipino_vs._Tagalog">
        Filipino vs. Tagalog
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Filipino_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Filipino vs. Tagalog">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In practical terms,
       <i>
        Filipino
       </i>
       is the official name of Tagalog, or even a synonym of it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ELL_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ELL-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Today's Filipino language is best described as "Tagalog-based".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-morrow_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-morrow-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Use of term
       <i>
        Filipino
       </i>
       in this context is a
       <a href="/wiki/Metalepsis" title="Metalepsis">
        metalepsis
       </a>
       which suggests
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">
        metaphorically
       </a>
       that the national language is based on an amalgam of Philippine languages rather than on Tagalog alone. The language is usually called
       <i>
        Tagalog
       </i>
       within the Philippines and among Filipinos to differentiate it from other Philippine languages, but it has also come to be known as
       <i>
        Filipino
       </i>
       to differentiate it from the languages of other countries; the former implies a regional origin, the latter a national. This is similar to the use of
       <a href="/wiki/Names_given_to_the_Spanish_language" title="Names given to the Spanish language">
        names given to the Spanish language
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        Castilian
       </i>
       tends to be used within Spain, and
       <i>
        Spanish
       </i>
       in international settings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In connection with the use of Filipino, or specifically the promotion of the national language, the related term
       <i>
        Tagalista
       </i>
       is frequently used. While the word
       <i>
        Tagalista
       </i>
       literally means "one who specializes in Tagalog language or culture" or a "Tagalog specialist", in the context of the debates on the national language and "
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_Manila" title="Imperial Manila">
        Imperial Manila
       </a>
       ", the word
       <i>
        Tagalista
       </i>
       is used as a reference to "people who promote or would promote the primacy of Tagalog at the expense of [the] other [Philippine] indigenous tongues".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tagalista_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tagalista-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In academic contexts, Tagalog may be written with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diacritics" title="Diacritics">
        diacritics
       </a>
       , but this is generally ignored with Filipino. Filipino is constitutionally designated as the national language of the Philippines and, along with English, is one of the two official languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
