      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Glottolog
        </b>
       </i>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bibliographic" title="Bibliographic">
        bibliographic
       </a>
       database of the world's lesser-known languages, developed and maintained first at the former
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_Evolutionary_Anthropology" title="Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology">
        Max Planck Institute for Evolutionary Anthropology
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Leipzig" title="Leipzig">
        Leipzig
       </a>
       , Germany, and since 2015 at the new
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Planck_Institute_for_the_Science_of_Human_History" title="Max Planck Institute for the Science of Human History">
        Max Planck Institute for the Science of Human History
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Jena" title="Jena">
        Jena
       </a>
       , Germany. In addition to the languoid catalogue (the catalog of the world's languages and language families), Glottolog provides a comprehensive bibliography on the world's smaller languages. It differs from the similar catalogue
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
         Ethnologue
        </a>
       </i>
       in several ways, in that it attempts only to accept languages which the editors have been able to confirm both exist and are distinct (varieties which have not been confirmed, but are inherited from another source, are tagged as "spurious" or "unattested");
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       it attempts only to classify languages into families which have been demonstrated to be valid; extensive bibliographic information is provided, especially for lesser-known languages; and, to a limited extent, alternative names are listed according to the sources which use them. The language names used in the bibliographic entries are identified by ISO 639-3 code or Glottolog's own code (Glottocode); this is an area of ongoing expansion.
       <i>
        Glottolog
       </i>
       differs in several negative ways as well: Notably, apart from a single point-location on a map at its geographic center, no ethnographic or demographic information is provided. External links are provided to ISO,
       <i>
        Ethnologue
       </i>
       , and other online language databases.
      </p>
      <p>
       Edition 2.2 was published online in 2013, and licensed under a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_Attribution-Sharealike_3.0_Unported_License" title="Creative Commons Attribution-Sharealike 3.0 Unported License">
        Creative Commons Attribution-Sharealike 3.0 Unported License
       </a>
       . Edition 2.4 was published in 2015, and edition 3.0 in 2017.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_families">
        Language families
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Glottolog
       </i>
       is more conservative in its classification than most online databases in establishing membership of languages and groups within families, but more liberal in considering unclassified languages to be isolates.         <a href="#cite_note-2">

       </sup>

      <p>
       Creoles are classified with the language that supplied their basic
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to languages classified into families,
       <i>
        Glottolog
       </i>
       recognizes 79
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgins
       </a>
       (including one sign pidgin), 24
       <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">
        mixed languages
       </a>
       , 8
       <a href="/wiki/Artificial_language" title="Artificial language">
        artificial languages
       </a>
       , 9
       <a href="/wiki/Register_(sociolinguistics)" title="Register (sociolinguistics)">
        speech registers
       </a>
       (including 3
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Auxiliary_sign_language" title="Auxiliary sign language">
        auxiliary sign systems
       </a>
       ), 118 attested but
       <a href="/wiki/Unclassified_language" title="Unclassified language">
        unclassifiable languages
       </a>
       (including 2 sign languages), 61 unattested languages, and 270
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spurious_language" title="Spurious language">
        spurious languages
       </a>
       (such as retired ISO entries, and including 3 sign languages) kept for bookkeeping purposes.
      </p>
