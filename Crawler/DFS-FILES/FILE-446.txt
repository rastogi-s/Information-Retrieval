      <p>
       <b>
        Fusional languages
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        inflected languages
       </b>
       are a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic languages
       </a>
       , distinguished from
       <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">
        agglutinative languages
       </a>
       by their tendency to use a single inflectional
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morpheme
       </a>
       to denote multiple grammatical, syntactic, or semantic features. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       verb
       <i>
        comer
       </i>
       ("to eat") has the first-person singular preterite tense form
       <i>
        comí
       </i>
       ('I ate'); the single suffix
       <i>
        -í
       </i>
       represents
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       the features of first-person singular agreement and preterite tense, instead of having a separate affix for each feature.
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples of fusional
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       are:
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pashto_language" title="Pashto language">
        Pashto
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Punjabi_language" title="Punjabi language">
        Punjabi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindustani_language" title="Hindustani language">
        Hindustani
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       (classical and modern),
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">
        Iberian Romance
       </a>
       dialect continuum,
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">
        Irish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">
        Lithuanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">
        Latvian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
        Faroese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polish_language" title="Polish language">
        Polish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">
        Bulgarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Serbo-Croatian" title="Serbo-Croatian">
        Serbo-Croatian
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovenian_language" title="Slovenian language">
        Slovenian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Caucasian_languages" title="Northeast Caucasian languages">
        Northeast Caucasian languages
       </a>
       are weakly fusional.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another notable group of fusional languages is the
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       group; however,
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Hebrew" title="Modern Hebrew">
        Modern Hebrew
       </a>
       is much more analytic than
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Hebrew" title="Classical Hebrew">
        Classical Hebrew
       </a>
       “both with nouns and with verbs”.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquial" title="Colloquial">
        Colloquial
       </a>
       varieties of Arabic are more analytic than the standard language, having lost all noun
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declensions
       </a>
       , and in many cases also featuring simplified
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A high degree of fusion is also found in many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Finno-Ugric" title="Finno-Ugric">
        Finno-Ugric
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Samoyedic_language" title="Samoyedic language">
        Samoyedic languages
       </a>
       , like
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">
        Sami languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Skolt_Sami_language" title="Skolt Sami language">
        Skolt Sami
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Unusually for a natively North American language,
       <a href="/wiki/Navajo_language" title="Navajo language">
        Navajo
       </a>
       is sometimes described as fusional due to its complex and inseparable verb morphology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An illustration of fusionality is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       word
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          bonus
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("good"). The ending
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          -us
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       denotes masculine
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nominative_case" title="Nominative case">
        nominative case
       </a>
       , and singular
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . Changing any one of these features requires replacing the suffix
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          -us
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       with a different one. In the form
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          bonum
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , the ending
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          -um
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       denotes masculine
       <a href="/wiki/Accusative_case" title="Accusative case">
        accusative
       </a>
       singular, neuter accusative singular, or neuter nominative singular.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fusional_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Fusional languages generally tend to lose their inflection over the centuries – some languages much more quickly than others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-unfolding_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-unfolding-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       was fusional, some of its descendants have shifted to a more
       <a href="/wiki/Analytic_language" title="Analytic language">
        analytic
       </a>
       structure, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">
        Modern English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
        Danish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Afrikaans" title="Afrikaans">
        Afrikaans
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Agglutinative_language" title="Agglutinative language">
        agglutinative
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_language" title="Persian language">
        Persian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian
       </a>
       . Other descendants are fusional, including
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Lithuanian_language" title="Lithuanian language">
        Lithuanian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latvian_language" title="Latvian language">
        Latvian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       and certain
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages shift over time from agglutinative to fusional. For example, while most
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uralic_language" title="Uralic language">
        Uralic languages
       </a>
       are predominantly agglutinative,
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_language" title="Estonian language">
        Estonian
       </a>
       is markedly evolving in the direction of a fusional language. On the other hand,
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       , its close relative, exhibits fewer fusional traits, thereby keeping closer to the mainstream Uralic type.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Declension">
        Declension
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fusional_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Declension">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox mbox-small-left ambox-content" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg">
          <img alt="[icon]" data-file-height="31" data-file-width="44" height="14" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/30px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/40px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png 2x" width="20"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text">
         <div class="mbox-text-span">
          <b>
           This section needs expansion
          </b>
          .
          <small>
           You can help by
           <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fusional_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=">
            adding to it
           </a>
           .
          </small>
          <small>
           <i>
            (May 2013)
           </i>
          </small>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       One feature of many fusional languages is their systems of
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declensions
       </a>
       . Here nouns and adjectives have a suffix attached to them to specify
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        grammatical case
       </a>
       (their uses in the clause),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_(grammar)" title="Number (grammar)">
        number
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        grammatical gender
       </a>
       ; pronouns may alter their forms entirely to encode this information. In most
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       and modern
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , encoding for case is merely vestigial; this is because it no longer encompasses nouns and adjectives, but only pronouns. Compare the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       <i>
        egli
       </i>
       (masculine singular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">
        nominative
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        gli
       </i>
       (masculine singular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dative" title="Dative">
        dative
       </a>
       , or indirect object),
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (masculine singular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accusative" title="Accusative">
        accusative
       </a>
       ) and
       <i>
        lui
       </i>
       (also masculine singular accusative, but emphatic and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indirect_object" title="Indirect object">
        indirect case
       </a>
       to be used with prepositions), corresponding to the single vestigial pair
       <i>
        he, him
       </i>
       in English.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">
        Conjugation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fusional_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Conjugation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        Conjugation
       </a>
       is the alteration of the form of a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       to encode information about some or all of
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        grammatical mood
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice" title="Grammatical voice">
        voice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
        tense
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        aspect
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        grammatical gender
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . In a fusional language, two or more of these pieces of information may be conveyed in a single morpheme, typically a suffix.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , the verbal suffix depends on the mood, tense, and aspect of the verb, as well as on the person and number (but not the gender) of its subject. This gives rise to typically
       <a href="/wiki/French_conjugation#First_group_verbs_(-er_verbs)" title="French conjugation">
        forty-five different single-word forms
       </a>
       of the verb, each conveying some or all of a mood (one of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicative_mood" title="Indicative mood">
        indicative
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Subjunctive_mood" title="Subjunctive mood">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">
        conditional
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
        imperative
       </a>
       ), a tense (
       <a href="/wiki/Past_tense" title="Past tense">
        past
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
        present
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">
        future
       </a>
       ), an aspect (
       <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">
        perfective
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">
        imperfective
       </a>
       ), and a person and a number (first, second, or third person singular or plural). To change any one of these pieces of information without changing the others requires using a different suffix, the key characteristic of fusionality.
      </p>
      <p>
       English has two examples of conjugational fusion. The verbal suffix
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       indicates a combination of present tense with both third person and singularity of the associated subject, and the verbal suffix
       <i>
        -ed
       </i>
       , used in a verb with no auxiliary verb, conveys both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Progressive_aspect" title="Progressive aspect">
        non-progressive aspect
       </a>
       and past tense.
      </p>
