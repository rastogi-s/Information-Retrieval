      <p>
       A
       <b>
        French creole
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        French-based creole language
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole language
       </a>
       (contact language with native speakers) for which French is the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lexifier" title="Lexifier">
         lexifier
        </a>
       </i>
       . Most often this lexifier is not modern French but rather a 17th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">
        koiné
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Paris" title="Paris">
        Paris
       </a>
       , the French Atlantic harbours, and the nascent French colonies. French-based creole languages are spoken natively by millions of people worldwide, primarily in the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       and on archipelagos throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian Ocean
       </a>
       . This article also contains information on French
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgin
       </a>
       languages, contact languages that lack native speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       These contact languages are not to be confused with contemporary (non-creole) French language varieties spoken overseas in, for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       (mostly in
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec" title="Quebec">
        Quebec
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritime_Provinces" title="Maritime Provinces">
        Maritime Provinces
       </a>
       ), the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prairies" title="Prairies">
        Canadian Prairie provinces
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">
        Louisiana
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/New_England" title="New England">
        New England
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">
        Maine
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">
        New Hampshire
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Vermont" title="Vermont">
        Vermont
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Guadeloupe" title="Guadeloupe">
           Guadeloupe
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Eles_des_Saintes" title="Îles des Saintes">
           Îles des Saintes
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Dominica" title="Dominica">
           Dominica
          </a>
          ,
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Lucia" title="St. Lucia">
           St. Lucia
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_and_Tobago" title="Trinidad and Tobago">
           Trinidad and Tobago
          </a>
          and many other smaller islands. Although all of the creoles spoken on these islands are considered to be the same language, there are noticeable differences between the dialects of each island. Notably, the Creole spoken in the Eastern (windward) part of the island
          <a href="/wiki/Saint_Barth%C3%A9lemy#Demographics" title="Saint Barthélemy">
           Saint-Barthélemy
          </a>
          is spoken exclusively by a white population of European descent, imported into the island from
          <a href="/wiki/Saint_Kitts" title="Saint Kitts">
           Saint Kitts
          </a>
          in 1648.
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Creole_French" title="Dominican Creole French">
             Dominican Creole French
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="/wiki/Grenadian_Creole#Grenadian_Creole_French" title="Grenadian Creole">
             Grenadian Creole French
            </a>
            ,
            <a href="/wiki/Saint_Lucian_Creole_French" title="Saint Lucian Creole French">
             Saint Lucian Creole French
            </a>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guianese_Creole" title="Guianese Creole">
           Guianese Creole
          </a>
          is a
          <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
           language
          </a>
          spoken in
          <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">
           French Guiana
          </a>
          , and to a lesser degree in
          <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">
           Suriname
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/Guyana" title="Guyana">
           Guyana
          </a>
          . It is closely related to
          <a href="/wiki/Antillean_Creole" title="Antillean Creole">
           Antillean Creole
          </a>
          , but there are some noteworthy differences between the two.
         </li>
         <li>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karip%C3%BAna_Creole_French" title="Karipúna Creole French">
           Karipúna
          </a>
          , spoken in
          <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
           Brazil
          </a>
          , mostly in the state of
          <a href="/wiki/Amap%C3%A1" title="Amapá">
           Amapá
          </a>
          . It was developed by Amerindians, with possible influences from immigrants from neighboring
          <a href="/wiki/French_Guiana" title="French Guiana">
           French Guiana
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
           French
          </a>
          territories of the
          <a href="/wiki/Caribbean_Sea" title="Caribbean Sea">
           Caribbean
          </a>
          and with a recent lexical
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adstratum" title="Adstratum">
           adstratum
          </a>
          from
          <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
           Portuguese
          </a>
          .
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="/wiki/Lanc-Patu%C3%A1_creole" title="Lanc-Patuá creole">
             Lanc-Patuá
            </a>
            , spoken more widely in the state of
            <a href="/wiki/Amap%C3%A1" title="Amapá">
             Amapá
            </a>
            , is a variety of the former, possibly the same language.
           </li>
          </ul>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
