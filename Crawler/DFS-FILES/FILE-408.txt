      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Visigoths
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
         UK
        </a>
        :
       </small>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">

          /

       <small>

         US
        </a>
        :
       </small>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">

           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">

           </span>

        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Visigothi, Wisigothi, Vesi, Visi, Wesi, Wisi
        </span>
       </i>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         Visigoti
        </span>
       </i>
       ) were the western branches of the nomadic tribes of
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_peoples" title="Germanic peoples">
        Germanic peoples
       </a>
       referred to collectively as the
       <a href="/wiki/Goths" title="Goths">
        Goths
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These tribes flourished and spread throughout the late
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Antiquity" title="Late Antiquity">
        Late Antiquity
       </a>
       , or what is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Migration_Period" title="Migration Period">
        Migration Period
       </a>
       . The Visigoths emerged from earlier Gothic groups (possibly the
       <a href="/wiki/Thervingi" title="Thervingi">
        Thervingi
       </a>
       ) who had invaded the Roman Empire beginning in 376 and had defeated the Romans at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople" title="Battle of Adrianople">
        Battle of Adrianople
       </a>
       in 378.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeather199852–57,_300–301_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeather199852–57,_300–301-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Relations between the Romans and the Visigoths were variable, alternately warring with one another and making treaties when convenient.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Visigoths invaded Italy under
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
        Alaric I
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">
        sacked Rome in 410
       </a>
       . After the Visigoths sacked Rome, they began settling down, first in southern
       <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">
        Gaul
       </a>
       and eventually in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       , where they founded the
       <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">
        Visigothic Kingdom
       </a>
       and maintained a presence from the 5th to the 8th centuries AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843–844_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006843–844-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Visigoths first settled in southern Gaul as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">
         foederati
        </a>
       </i>
       to the Romans – a relationship established in 418. However, they soon fell out with their Roman hosts (for reasons that are now obscure) and established their own kingdom with its
       <a href="/wiki/Capital_city" title="Capital city">
        capital
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">
        Toulouse
       </a>
       . They next extended their authority into
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">
        Hispania
       </a>
       at the expense of the
       <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">
        Suebi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       . In 507, however, their rule in Gaul was ended by the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       under
       <a href="/wiki/Clovis_I" title="Clovis I">
        Clovis I
       </a>
       , who defeated them in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vouill%C3%A9" title="Battle of Vouillé">
        Battle of Vouillé
       </a>
       . After that, the Visigoth kingdom was limited to
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">
        Hispania
       </a>
       , and they never again held territory north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       other than
       <a href="/wiki/Septimania" title="Septimania">
        Septimania
       </a>
       . A small, elite group of Visigoths came to dominate the governance of that region at the expense of those who had previously ruled there, particularly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       province of
       <a href="/wiki/Spania" title="Spania">
        Spania
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Suebi" title="Kingdom of the Suebi">
        Kingdom of the Suebi
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In or around 589, the Visigoths under
       <a href="/wiki/Reccared_I" title="Reccared I">
        Reccared I
       </a>
       converted from
       <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">
        Arianism
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Christianity" title="Nicene Christianity">
        Nicene Christianity
       </a>
       , gradually adopting the culture of their Hispano-Roman subjects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClaude1998119–120_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClaude1998119–120-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Their legal code, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Code" title="Visigothic Code">
         Visigothic Code
        </a>
       </i>
       (completed in 654) abolished the longstanding practice of applying different laws for Romans and Visigoths. Once legal distinctions were no longer being made between
       <i>
        Romani
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Gothi
       </i>
       , they became known collectively as
       <i>
        Hispani
       </i>
       . In the century that followed, the region was dominated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Councils_of_Toledo" title="Councils of Toledo">
        Councils of Toledo
       </a>
       and the episcopacy. (Little else is known about the Visigoths' history during the 7th century, since records are relatively sparse.) In 711 or 712, a force of
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        invading African Moors
       </a>
       defeated the Visigoths in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guadalete" title="Battle of Guadalete">
        Battle of Guadalete
       </a>
       . Their
       <a href="/wiki/Roderic" title="Roderic">
        king
       </a>
       and many members of their governing elite were killed, and their kingdom rapidly collapsed. Gothic identity survived, however, especially in
       <a href="/wiki/Marca_Hispanica" title="Marca Hispanica">
        Marca Hispanica
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">
        Kingdom of Asturias
       </a>
       , which had been founded by the Visigothic nobleman
       <a href="/wiki/Pelagius_of_Asturias" title="Pelagius of Asturias">
        Pelagius of Asturias
       </a>
       after his victory over the
       <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">
        Moors
       </a>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Covadonga" title="Battle of Covadonga">
        Battle of Covadonga
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       During their governance of the Kingdom of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">
        Hispania
       </a>
       , the Visigoths
       <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_art_and_architecture" title="Visigothic art and architecture">
        built several churches
       </a>
       that survive. They also left many artifacts, which have been discovered in increasing numbers by archaeologists in recent times. The
       <a href="/wiki/Treasure_of_Guarrazar" title="Treasure of Guarrazar">
        Treasure of Guarrazar
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Votive_crown" title="Votive crown">
        votive crowns
       </a>
       and crosses is the most spectacular. They founded the only new cities in western Europe from the fall of the Western half of the Roman Empire until the rise of the
       <a href="/wiki/Carolingian_dynasty" title="Carolingian dynasty">
        Carolingian dynasty
       </a>
       . Many
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Germanic_origin#Names" title="List of Spanish words of Germanic origin">
        Visigothic names are still in use in modern Spanish and Portuguese
       </a>
       . Their most notable legacy, however, was the
       <i>
        Visigothic Code
       </i>
       , which served, among other things, as the basis for court procedure in most of Christian
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberia
       </a>
       until the
       <a href="/wiki/Late_Middle_Ages" title="Late Middle Ages">
        Late Middle Ages
       </a>
       , centuries after the demise of the kingdom.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Nomenclature:_Vesi.2C_Ostrogothi.2C_Tervingi.2C_Greuthungi">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature:_Vesi,_Ostrogothi,_Tervingi,_Greuthungi">
        Nomenclature: Vesi, Ostrogothi, Tervingi, Greuthungi
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nomenclature: Vesi, Ostrogothi, Tervingi, Greuthungi">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Contemporaneous references to the Gothic tribes use the terms "Vesi" (Latin for Visigoths), "
       <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">
        Ostrogothi
       </a>
       ", "
       <a href="/wiki/Thervingi" title="Thervingi">
        Thervingi
       </a>
       ", and "
       <a href="/wiki/Greuthungi" title="Greuthungi">
        Greuthungi
       </a>
       ". Most scholars have concluded that the terms "Vesi" and "Tervingi" were both used to refer to one particular tribe, while the terms "Ostrogothi" and "Greuthungi" were used to refer to another.
       <a href="/wiki/Herwig_Wolfram" title="Herwig Wolfram">
        Herwig Wolfram
       </a>
       points out that while primary sources occasionally list all four names (as in, for example,
       <i>
        Gruthungi, Austrogothi, Tervingi, Visi
       </i>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whenever they mention two different tribes, they always refer either to "the Vesi and the Ostrogothi" or to "the Tervingi and the Greuthungi", and they never pair them up in any other combination.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This conclusion is supported by
       <a href="/wiki/Jordanes" title="Jordanes">
        Jordanes
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeather1998300–301_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeather1998300–301-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who identified the Visigoth (Vesi) kings from
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
        Alaric I
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_II" title="Alaric II">
        Alaric II
       </a>
       as the heirs of the 4th century Tervingian king
       <a href="/wiki/Athanaric" title="Athanaric">
        Athanaric
       </a>
       , and the Ostrogoth kings from
       <a href="/wiki/Theoderic_the_Great" title="Theoderic the Great">
        Theoderic the Great
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Theodahad" title="Theodahad">
        Theodahad
       </a>
       as the heirs of the Greuthungi king
       <a href="/wiki/Ermanaric" title="Ermanaric">
        Ermanaric
       </a>
       . In addition, the
       <i>
        Notitia Dignitatum
       </i>
       equates the Vesi with the Tervingi in a reference to the years 388–391.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest sources for each of the four names are roughly contemporaneous. The first recorded reference to "the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tervingi" title="Tervingi">
        Tervingi
       </a>
       " is in a
       <a href="/wiki/Eulogy" title="Eulogy">
        eulogy
       </a>
       of the emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Maximian" title="Maximian">
        Maximian
       </a>
       (285–305), delivered in or shortly after 291 (perhaps at
       <a href="/wiki/Trier" title="Trier">
        Trier
       </a>
       on 20 April 292)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and traditionally ascribed to
       <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Mamertinus" title="Claudius Mamertinus">
        Claudius Mamertinus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It says that the "Tervingi, another division of the Goths" (
       <i>
        Tervingi pars alia Gothorum
       </i>
       ), joined with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taifali" title="Taifali">
        Taifali
       </a>
       to attack the
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gepidae" title="Gepidae">
        Gepidae
       </a>
       . (The term "Vandals" may have been a mistaken reference to the "
       <a href="/wiki/Victohali" title="Victohali">
        Victohali
       </a>
       ", since around 360 the historian
       <a href="/wiki/Eutropius_(historian)" title="Eutropius (historian)">
        Eutropius
       </a>
       reports that
       <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">
        Dacia
       </a>
       was currently inhabited by Taifali, Victohali, and Tervingi.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [d]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first recorded reference to "the Greuthungi" is by
       <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">
        Ammianus Marcellinus
       </a>
       , writing no earlier than 392 and perhaps later than 395, recounting the words of a Tervingian chieftain who is attested as early as 376.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first known use of the term "Ostrogoths" is in a document dated September 392 from
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824_7-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Claudian" title="Claudian">
        Claudian
       </a>
       mentions that they, together with the
       <i>
        Gruthungi
       </i>
       , inhabit
       <a href="/wiki/Phrygia" title="Phrygia">
        Phrygia
       </a>
       .)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn52_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn52-13">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:327px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gutthiuda.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="800" height="222" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gutthiuda.jpg/325px-Gutthiuda.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gutthiuda.jpg/488px-Gutthiuda.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Gutthiuda.jpg/650px-Gutthiuda.jpg 2x" width="325"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gutthiuda.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Gutthiuda
         <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
          [
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
            <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
             citation needed
            </span>
           </a>
          </i>
          ]
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Wolfram notes that "Vesi" and "Ostrogothi" were terms each tribe used to boastfully describe itself and argues that "Tervingi" and "Greuthungi" were geographical identifiers each tribe used to describe the other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would explain why the latter terms dropped out of use shortly after 400, when the Goths were displaced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Huns#Before_Attila" title="Huns">
        Hunnic invasions
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824_7-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198824-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As an example of this geographical naming practice, Wolfram cites an account by
       <a href="/wiki/Zosimus" title="Zosimus">
        Zosimus
       </a>
       of a group of people living north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">
        Danube
       </a>
       who called themselves "the Scythians" but were called "the Greutungi" by members of a different tribe living north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Danube" title="Danube">
        Ister
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn57_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn57-14">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Wolfram believes that the people Zosimus describes were those Tervingi who had remained behind after the Hunnic conquest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn57_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn57-14">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the most part, all of the terms discriminating between different Gothic tribes gradually disappeared after they moved into the Roman Empire.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The last indication that the Goths whose king reigned at Toulouse thought of themselves as "Vesi" is found in a
       <a href="/wiki/Panegyric" title="Panegyric">
        panegyric
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Avitus" title="Avitus">
        Avitus
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Sidonius_Apollinaris" title="Sidonius Apollinaris">
        Sidonius Apollinaris
       </a>
       dated 1 January 456.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Most recent scholars (notably
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Heather" title="Peter Heather">
        Peter Heather
       </a>
       ) have concluded that Visigothic group identity emerged only within the Roman Empire.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeather199852–57,_130–178,_302–309_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeather199852–57,_130–178,_302–309-15">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Roger_Collins" title="Roger Collins">
        Roger Collins
       </a>
       believes that the Visigothic identity emerged from the
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(376%E2%80%93382)" title="Gothic War (376–382)">
        Gothic War of 376–382
       </a>
       when a collection of Tervingi, Greuthungi, and other "barbarian" contingents banded together in multiethnic
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">
         foederati
        </a>
       </i>
       (Wolfram's "federate armies") under Alaric I in the eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">
        Balkans
       </a>
       , since they had become a multi ethnic group and could no longer claim to be exclusively Tervingian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins200422–24_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins200422–24-16">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term "Visigoth" was an invention of the 6th century.
       <a href="/wiki/Cassiodorus" title="Cassiodorus">
        Cassiodorus
       </a>
       , a Roman in the service of Theodoric the Great, invented the term "Visigothic" to match that of "Ostrogothic", terms he thought of as signifying "western Goths" and "eastern Goths" respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The western–eastern division was a simplification (and a literary device) of 6th century historians; political realities were more complex.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826-17">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further, Cassiodorus used the term "Goths" to refer only to the Ostrogoths, whom he served, and reserved the geographical term "Visigoths" for the Gallo-Spanish Goths. This usage, however, was adopted by the Visigoths themselves in their communications with the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Empire
       </a>
       and was still in use in the 7th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826-17">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other names for other Gothic divisions abounded. A "Germanic" Byzantine or Italian author referred to one of the two peoples as the
       <i>
        Valagothi
       </i>
       , meaning "Roman Goths", and in 469 the Visigoths were called the "Alaric Goths".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198826-17">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Etymology_of_Tervingi_and_Vesi.2FVisigothi">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology_of_Tervingi_and_Vesi/Visigothi">
        Etymology of Tervingi and Vesi/Visigothi
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Etymology of Tervingi and Vesi/Visigothi">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The name
       <i>
        Tervingi
       </i>
       may mean "forest people".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is supported by evidence that geographic descriptors were commonly used to distinguish people living north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
        Black Sea
       </a>
       both before and after Gothic settlement there, by evidence of forest-related names among the Tervingi, and by the lack of evidence for an earlier date for the name pair Tervingi–Greuthungi than the late 3rd century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387–388,_fn58_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387–388,_fn58-18">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That the name
       <i>
        Tervingi
       </i>
       has pre-Pontic, possibly Scandinavian, origins still has support today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn58_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn58-19">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Visigoths are called
       <i>
        Wesi
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Wisi
       </i>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trebellius_Pollio" title="Trebellius Pollio">
        Trebellius Pollio
       </a>
       , Claudian, and Sidonius Apollinaris.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStevenson189936,_fn15_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStevenson189936,_fn15-20">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The word is
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_language" title="Gothic language">
        Gothic
       </a>
       for "good", implying the "good or worthy people",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825_8-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198825-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       related to Gothic
       <i>
        iusiza
       </i>
       "better" and a reflex of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto_Indo-European_Language" title="Proto Indo-European Language">
        Indo-European
       </a>
       *
       <i>
        wesu
       </i>
       "good", akin to
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       <i>
        gwiw
       </i>
       "excellent",
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       <i>
        eus
       </i>
       "good",
       <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
        Sanskrit
       </a>
       <i>
        vásu-ş
       </i>
       "id.". Jordanes relates the tribe's name to a river, though this is most likely a
       <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">
        folk etymology
       </a>
       or legend like his similar story about the Greuthung name.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn58_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988387,_fn58-19">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_origins">
        Early origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Early origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Visigoths emerged from the Gothic tribes, most likely a derivative name for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gutones" title="Gutones">
        Gutones
       </a>
       , a people believed to have their origins in
       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
        Scandinavia
       </a>
       and who migrated southeastwards into eastern Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram199739–40_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram199739–40-21">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such understanding of their origins is largely the result of Gothic traditions and their true genesis as a people is as obscure as that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alamanni" title="Alamanni">
        Alamanni
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000149_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000149-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Visigoths spoke an eastern Germanic language that was distinct by the 4th century. Eventually the Gothic language died as a result of contact with other European people during the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844-23">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Long struggles between the neighboring Vandili and Lugii people with the Goths may have contributed to their earlier exodus into mainland Europe. The vast majority of them settled between the Oder and Vistula rivers until overpopulation (according to Gothic legends or tribal sagas) forced them to move south and east, where they settled just north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
        Black Sea
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram199742–43_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram199742–43-24">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unfortunately this legend is not supported by archaeological evidence so its validity is disputable. Historian Malcolm Todd contends that while this large
       <i>
        en masse
       </i>
       migration is possible, the movement of Gothic peoples south-east was more likely the result of warrior bands moving closer to the wealth of
       <a href="/wiki/Ukraine" title="Ukraine">
        Ukraine
       </a>
       and the cities of the Black Sea coast. Perhaps what is most notable about the Gothic people in this regard was that by the middle of the 3rd century AD, they were "the most formidable military power beyond the lower Danube frontier".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000149–150_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000149–150-25">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram198842–55_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram198842–55-26">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contact_with_Rome">
        Contact with Rome
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Contact with Rome">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2788" data-file-width="1972" height="311" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG/220px-Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG/330px-Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG/440px-Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tezaurul_de_la_Pietroasele_Closca_MNIR_Tezaur.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <center>
          The
          <a href="/wiki/Pietroasele_Treasure" title="Pietroasele Treasure">
           Pietroasele Treasure
          </a>
          discovered in Romania, attributed to the Visigoths
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOdobescu18891-100_27-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOdobescu18891-100-27">
            [23]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </center>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Throughout the third and fourth centuries there were numerous conflicts and exchanges of varying types between the Goths and their neighbors. After the Romans withdrew from the territory of Dacia, the local population was subjected to constant invasions by the migratory tribes, among the first being the Goths.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGeorgescu199111_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGeorgescu199111-28">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 238, the Goths invaded across the Danube into the Roman province of
       <a href="/wiki/Moesia" title="Moesia">
        Moesia
       </a>
       , pillaging and exacting payment through hostage taking. During that same year consequent the war with the Persians in 238, Goths also appear in the Roman armies of
       <a href="/wiki/Gordian_III" title="Gordian III">
        Gordian III
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000150_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000150-29">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When subsidies to the Goths were stopped, the Goths organized and in 250 joined a major barbarian invasion led by the Germanic king, Kniva.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000150_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000150-29">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Success on the battlefield against the Romans inspired additional invasions into the northern
       <a href="/wiki/Balkans" title="Balkans">
        Balkans
       </a>
       and deeper into
       <a href="/wiki/Anatolia" title="Anatolia">
        Anatolia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000150–151_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000150–151-30">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Starting in approximately 255, the Goths added a new dimension to their attacks by taking to the sea and invading harbors which brought them into conflict with the Greeks as well. When the city of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pityus" title="Pityus">
        Pityus
       </a>
       fell to the Goths in 256, the Goths were further emboldened. Sometime between 266–267, the Goths raided Greece but when they attempted to move into the Bosporus straits to attack Byzantium, they were repulsed. Along with other Germanic tribes, they attacked further into Anatolia, assaulting Crete and Cyprus on the way; shortly thereafter, they pillaged Troy and the temple of Artemis at Ephesus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000151_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000151-31">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Throughout the reign of emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Constantine_the_Great" title="Constantine the Great">
        Constantine the Great
       </a>
       , the Visigoths continued to conduct raids on Roman territory south of the Danube River.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844-23">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 332, relations between the Goths and Romans were stabilized by a treaty but this was not to last.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000152_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000152-32">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="War_with_Rome_.28376.E2.80.93382.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="War_with_Rome_(376–382)">
        War with Rome (376–382)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: War with Rome (376–382)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Gothic_War_(376%E2%80%93382)" title="Gothic War (376–382)">
        Gothic War (376–382)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Goths remained in
       <a href="/wiki/Dacia" title="Dacia">
        Dacia
       </a>
       until 376, when one of their leaders,
       <a href="/wiki/Fritigern" title="Fritigern">
        Fritigern
       </a>
       , appealed to the Roman emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">
        Valens
       </a>
       to be allowed to settle with his people on the south bank of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Danube_River" title="Danube River">
        Danube
       </a>
       . Here, they hoped to find refuge from the
       <a href="/wiki/Huns" title="Huns">
        Huns
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844–845_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006844–845-33">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Valens permitted this, as he saw in them "a splendid recruiting ground for his army".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFuller199855_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFuller199855-34">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, a
       <a href="/wiki/Famine" title="Famine">
        famine
       </a>
       broke out and Rome was unwilling to supply them with either the food they were promised or the land. Generally, the Goths were abused by the Romans,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006845_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006845-35">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who began forcing the now starving Goths to trade away their children so as to stave off starvation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurant195024_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurant195024-36">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Open revolt ensued, leading to 6 years of plundering throughout the Balkans, the death of a Roman Emperor and a disastrous defeat of the Roman army.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDurant195024–25_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDurant195024–25-37">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_Adrianople_(378)" title="Battle of Adrianople (378)">
        Battle of Adrianople
       </a>
       in 378 was the decisive moment of the war. The Roman forces were slaughtered and the Emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Valens" title="Valens">
        Valens
       </a>
       was killed during the fighting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESarris200236_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESarris200236-38">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Precisely how Valens fell remains uncertain but Gothic legend tells of how the emperor was taken to a farmhouse, which was set on fire above his head, a tale made more popular by its symbolic representation of a heretical emperor receiving hell's torment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007178–179_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007178–179-39">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of Rome's leading officers and some of their most elite fighting men died during the battle which struck a major blow to Roman prestige and the Empire's military capabilities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007179_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007179-40">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Adrianople shocked the Roman world and eventually forced the Romans to negotiate with and settle the tribe within the empire's boundaries, a development with far reaching consequences for the eventual
       <a href="/wiki/Fall_of_the_Western_Roman_Empire" title="Fall of the Western Roman Empire">
        fall of Rome
       </a>
       . Fourth-century Roman soldier and historian,
       <a href="/wiki/Ammianus_Marcellinus" title="Ammianus Marcellinus">
        Ammianus Marcellinus
       </a>
       , ended his chronology of Roman history with this battle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKatz195588–89_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKatz195588–89-41">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite the severe consequences for Rome, Adrianople was not nearly as productive overall for the Visigoths and their gains were short-lived. Still confined to a small and relatively impoverished province of the Empire, another Roman army was being gathered against them, an army which also had amid its ranks, other disaffected Goths.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000154_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000154-42">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Intense campaigns against the Visigoths followed their victory at Adrianople for upwards of three years. Approach routes across the Danube provinces were effectively sealed off by concerted Roman efforts and while there was no decisive victory to claim, it was essentially a Roman triumph ending in a treaty in 382. The treaty struck with the Goths was to be the first
       <i>
        foedus
       </i>
       on imperial Roman soil. It required these semi-autonomous Germanic tribes to raise troops for the Roman army in exchange for arable land and freedom from Roman legal structures within the Empire.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007179–180_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007179–180-43">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [e]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reign_of_Alaric_I">
        Reign of Alaric I
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Reign of Alaric I">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1600" data-file-width="1171" height="273" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg/200px-Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg/300px-Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg/400px-Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alaric_entering_Athens.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An illustration of
         <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
          Alaric
         </a>
         entering
         <a href="/wiki/Athens" title="Athens">
          Athens
         </a>
         in 395
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
        Alaric I
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The new emperor,
       <a href="/wiki/Theodosius_I" title="Theodosius I">
        Theodosius I
       </a>
       , made peace with the rebels, and this peace held essentially unbroken until Theodosius died in 395.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns2003322,_374_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns2003322,_374-46">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In that year, the Visigoths' most famous king,
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_I" title="Alaric I">
        Alaric I
       </a>
       , took the throne. Theodosius was succeeded by his incapable sons:
       <a href="/wiki/Arcadius" title="Arcadius">
        Arcadius
       </a>
       in the east and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavius_Augustus_Honorius" title="Flavius Augustus Honorius">
        Honorius
       </a>
       in the west. In 397, Alaric was named military commander of the eastern Illyrian prefecture by Arcadius.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006845_35-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006845-35">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Over the next 15 years, an uneasy peace was broken by occasional conflicts between Alaric and the powerful Germanic generals who commanded the Roman armies in the east and west, wielding the real power of the empire. Finally, after the western general
       <a href="/wiki/Stilicho" title="Stilicho">
        Stilicho
       </a>
       was executed by Honorius in 408 and the Roman legions massacred the families of thousands of barbarian soldiers who were trying to assimilate into the Roman empire, Alaric decided to march on Rome.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFrassetto2003204–205_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFrassetto2003204–205-47">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After two defeats in Northern Italy and a siege of Rome ended by a negotiated pay-off, Alaric was cheated by another Roman faction. He resolved to cut the city off by capturing its port. On August 24, 410, however, Alaric's troops entered Rome through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salarian_Gate" title="Salarian Gate">
        Salarian Gate
       </a>
       , to plunder its riches in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sack_of_Rome_(410)" title="Sack of Rome (410)">
        sack of Rome
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007214–217_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalsall2007214–217-48">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, Rome, still the capital officially, was no longer the effective seat of the government of the Western Roman Empire. From the late 370s up to 402,
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       was the seat of government, but after the siege of Milan the Imperial Court moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Ravenna" title="Ravenna">
        Ravenna
       </a>
       in 402. Honorius visited Rome often, and after his death in 423 the emperors resided mostly there. Rome's fall severely shook the Empire's confidence, especially in the West. Loaded with booty, Alaric and the Visigoths extracted as much as they could with the intention of leaving Italy from Basilicata to northern
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       . Alaric died before the disembarkation and was buried supposedly near the ruins of Croton. He was succeeded by his wife’s brother.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins199963–65_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins199963–65-49">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visigothic_kingdom">
        Visigothic kingdom
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Visigothic kingdom">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom" title="Visigothic Kingdom">
        Visigothic Kingdom
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1967" data-file-width="2830" height="243" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png/350px-Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png/525px-Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png/700px-Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Europe_and_the_Near_East_at_476_AD.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Europe at the fall of the Western Roman Empire in 476 AD
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Visigothic Kingdom was a Western European power in the 5th to 7th centuries, created in Gaul when the Romans lost their control of the western half of their empire. For a brief period, the Visigoths controlled the strongest kingdom in Western Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams200451_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams200451-50">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In response to the invasion of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispania" title="Hispania">
        Roman Hispania
       </a>
       of 409 by the
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">
        Alans
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Suebi" title="Suebi">
        Suebi
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honorius_(emperor)" title="Honorius (emperor)">
        Honorius
       </a>
       , the emperor in the West, enlisted the aid of the Visigoths to regain control of the territory. Between 408–410 the Visigoths caused so much damage to Rome and the immediate periphery that nearly a decade later, the provinces in and around the city were only able to contribute one-seventh of their previous tax shares.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHeather2005434_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeather2005434-51">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 418, Honorius rewarded his Visigothic
       <a href="/wiki/Foederati" title="Foederati">
        federates
       </a>
       by giving them land in
       <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Aquitania" title="Gallia Aquitania">
        Gallia Aquitania
       </a>
       on which to settle after they had attacked the four tribes - Sueves, Asding and Siling Vandala and Alans - who had crossed the Rhine near Mainze the last day of 409 and eventually were invited into Spain by a Roman usurper in the Fall of 409 (the later two tribes were devastated). This was probably done under
       <i>
        hospitalitas
       </i>
       , the rules for billeting army soldiers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESivan1987759–772_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESivan1987759–772-52">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The settlement formed the nucleus of the future Visigothic kingdom that would eventually expand across the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       and onto the Iberian peninsula. That Visigothic settlement proved paramount to Europe's future as had it not been for the Visigothic warriors who fought side-by-side with the Roman troops under general
       <a href="/wiki/Flavius_Aetius" title="Flavius Aetius">
        Flavius Aetius
       </a>
       , it is perhaps possible that
       <a href="/wiki/Attila" title="Attila">
        Attila
       </a>
       would have seized control of Gaul, rather than the Romans being able to retain dominance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBurns2003382_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBurns2003382-53">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Visigoths' second great king,
       <a href="/wiki/Euric" title="Euric">
        Euric
       </a>
       , unified the various quarreling factions among the Visigoths and, in 475, forced the Roman government to grant them full independence. According to historian J. B. Bury, Euric was probably the "greatest of the Visigothic kings" for he managed to secure territorial gains denied to his predecessors and even acquired access to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBury2000211–212_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBury2000211–212-54">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At his death, the Visigoths were the most powerful of the successor states to the Western Roman Empire and were at the very height of their power.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006846_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006846-55">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Visigothic_Kingdom.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1222" data-file-width="1115" height="241" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Visigothic_Kingdom.png/220px-Visigothic_Kingdom.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Visigothic_Kingdom.png/330px-Visigothic_Kingdom.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d7/Visigothic_Kingdom.png/440px-Visigothic_Kingdom.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Visigothic_Kingdom.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Greatest extent of the Visigothic kingdom of Toulouse in orange dark and light, c. 500
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       At this point, the Visigoths were also the dominant power in the
       <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Peninsula" title="Iberian Peninsula">
        Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       , quickly crushing the
       <a href="/wiki/Alans" title="Alans">
        Alans
       </a>
       and forcing the
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       into
       <a href="/wiki/North_Africa" title="North Africa">
        north Africa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarr2004421_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarr2004421-56">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 500, the Visigothic Kingdom, centred at
       <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">
        Toulouse
       </a>
       , controlled Aquitania and
       <a href="/wiki/Gallia_Narbonensis" title="Gallia Narbonensis">
        Gallia Narbonensis
       </a>
       and most of Hispania with the exception of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_the_Suebi" title="Kingdom of the Suebi">
        Kingdom of the Suebi
       </a>
       in the northwest and small areas controlled by the
       <a href="/wiki/Basques" title="Basques">
        Basques
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Cantabri" title="Cantabri">
        Cantabrians
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETodd2000165_57-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETodd2000165-57">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Any survey of western Europe taken during this moment would have led one to conclude that the very future of Europe itself "depended on the Visigoths".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBury2000213_58-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBury2000213-58">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in 507, the Franks under Clovis I defeated the Visigoths in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Vouill%C3%A9" title="Battle of Vouillé">
        Battle of Vouillé
       </a>
       and wrested control of Aquitaine. King
       <a href="/wiki/Alaric_II" title="Alaric II">
        Alaric II
       </a>
       was killed in battle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006846_55-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006846-55">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After Alaric's death, Visigothic nobles spirited his heir, the child-king
       <a href="/wiki/Amalaric" title="Amalaric">
        Amalaric
       </a>
       , first to
       <a href="/wiki/Narbonne" title="Narbonne">
        Narbonne
       </a>
       , which was the last Gothic outpost in Gaul, and further across the Pyrenees into Hispania. The center of Visigothic rule shifted first to
       <a href="/wiki/Barcelona" title="Barcelona">
        Barcelona
       </a>
       , then inland and south to
       <a href="/wiki/Toledo,_Spain" title="Toledo, Spain">
        Toledo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988243–245_59-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988243–245-59">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 511 to 526, the Visigoths were ruled by
       <a href="/wiki/Theoderic_the_Great" title="Theoderic the Great">
        Theoderic the Great
       </a>
       of the Ostrogoths as
       <i>
        de jure
       </i>
       regent for the young Amalaric. Then in 526, Amalaric reigned independently for five years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988245_60-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1988245-60">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Following Amalaric's assassination in 531, another Ostrogothic ruler, Theudis took his place.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarr2004421_56-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarr2004421-56">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometime in 549, the Visigoth
       <a href="/wiki/Athanagild" title="Athanagild">
        Athanagild
       </a>
       sought military assistance from Justinian I and while this aide helped Athanagild win his wars, the Romans had much more in mind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECarr2004421_56-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECarr2004421-56">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Granada and southernmost
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Baetica" title="Baetica">
        Baetica
       </a>
       were lost to representatives of the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Empire
       </a>
       (to form the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Spania" title="Spania">
        Spania
       </a>
       ) who had been invited in to help settle this Visigothic dynastic struggle, but who stayed on, as a hoped-for spearhead to a "Reconquest" of the far west envisaged by emperor
       <a href="/wiki/Justinian_I" title="Justinian I">
        Justinian I
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts199782–85_61-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts199782–85-61">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Imperial Roman armies took advantage of Visigothic rivalries and established a government at Córdoba.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts199782_62-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts199782-62">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hispania_700_AD.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2500" data-file-width="2500" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Hispania_700_AD.PNG/220px-Hispania_700_AD.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Hispania_700_AD.PNG/330px-Hispania_700_AD.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Hispania_700_AD.PNG/440px-Hispania_700_AD.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hispania_700_AD.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Visigothic Hispania and its regional divisions in 700, before the Muslim conquest
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The last Arian Visigothic king,
       <a href="/wiki/Liuvigild" title="Liuvigild">
        Liuvigild
       </a>
       , conquered most of the northern regions (Cantabria) in 574, the Suevic kingdom in 584, and regained part of the southern areas lost to the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantines
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins200051–53_63-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins200051–53-63">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which King
       <a href="/wiki/Suintila" title="Suintila">
        Suintila
       </a>
       recovered in 624.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEArce19994_64-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEArce19994-64">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The kingdom survived until 711, when King
       <a href="/wiki/Roderic" title="Roderic">
        Roderic
       </a>
       (Rodrigo) was killed while opposing an invasion from the south by the
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_Caliphate" title="Umayyad Caliphate">
        Umayyad Caliphate
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Guadalete" title="Battle of Guadalete">
        Battle of Guadalete
       </a>
       . This marked the beginning of the
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        Umayyad conquest of Hispania
       </a>
       , when most of Spain came under
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islamic
       </a>
       rule in the early 8th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoberts199796–100_65-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts199796–100-65">
         [60]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A Visigothic nobleman,
       <a href="/wiki/Pelagius_of_Asturias" title="Pelagius of Asturias">
        Pelayo
       </a>
       , is credited with beginning the Christian
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
         Reconquista
        </a>
       </i>
       of Iberia in 718, when he defeated the Umayyad forces in the
       <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Covadonga" title="Battle of Covadonga">
        Battle of Covadonga
       </a>
       and established the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Asturias" title="Kingdom of Asturias">
        Kingdom of Asturias
       </a>
       in the northern part of the peninsula.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams200460_66-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams200460-66">
         [61]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other Visigoths who refused to adopt the Muslim faith or live under their rule, fled north to the kingdom of the
       <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">
        Franks
       </a>
       , and Visigoths played key roles in the empire of
       <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">
        Charlemagne
       </a>
       a few generations later. In the early years of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_C%C3%B3rdoba" title="Emirate of Córdoba">
        Emirate of Córdoba
       </a>
       , a group of Visigoths who remained under Muslim dominance constituted the personal bodyguard of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">
        Emir
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Haras" title="Al-Haras">
        al-Haras
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolf201414–15_67-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolf201414–15-67">
         [62]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       During their long reign in Spain, the Visigoths were responsible for the
       <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Kingdom#Founding_of_cities" title="Visigothic Kingdom">
        only new cities founded in Western Europe between the 5th and 8th centuries
       </a>
       . It is certain (through contemporary Spanish accounts) that they founded four:
       <a href="/wiki/Reccopolis" title="Reccopolis">
        Reccopolis
       </a>
       , Victoriacum (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Vitoria-Gasteiz" title="Vitoria-Gasteiz">
        Vitoria-Gasteiz
       </a>
       , though perhaps
       <a href="/wiki/Iru%C3%B1a-Veleia" title="Iruña-Veleia">
        Iruña-Veleia
       </a>
       ), Luceo, and
       <a href="/wiki/Olite" title="Olite">
        Olite
       </a>
       . There is also a possible fifth city ascribed to them by a later Arabic source:
       <i>
        Baiyara
       </i>
       (perhaps modern
       <a href="/wiki/Montoro" title="Montoro">
        Montoro
       </a>
       ). All of these cities were founded for military purposes and three of them in celebration of victory. Oddly enough, despite that the Visigoths reigned in Spain for upwards of 250 years, there are a only few remnants of the Gothic language borrowed into
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEOstler2006307_68-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEOstler2006307-68">
         [63]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69">
        <a href="#cite_note-69">
         [f]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-71">
        <a href="#cite_note-71">
         [g]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . The Visigoths as heirs of the Roman empire lost their language and got intermarriaged with the Hispano-Roman population. The Visigothic kingdom was the origin of the Spanish nation.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Law">
        Law
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Law">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Visigothic_Code" title="Visigothic Code">
        Visigothic Code of Law
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        forum judicum
       </i>
       ), which had been part of
       <a href="/wiki/Aristocracy" title="Aristocracy">
        aristocratic
       </a>
       oral tradition, was set in writing in the early 7th century— and survives in two separate
       <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">
        codices
       </a>
       preserved at
       <a href="/wiki/El_Escorial" title="El Escorial">
        el Escorial
       </a>
       . It goes into more detail than a modern constitution commonly does and reveals a great deal about Visigothic social structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins20046–8_72-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins20046–8-72">
         [65]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the greatest contributions of the Visigoths to
       <a href="/wiki/Family_law" title="Family law">
        family law
       </a>
       was their protection of the property rights of married women, which was continued by
       <a href="/wiki/Law_of_Spain" title="Law of Spain">
        Spanish law
       </a>
       and ultimately evolved into the
       <a href="/wiki/Community_property" title="Community property">
        community property
       </a>
       system now in force throughout the majority of western Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECoolidge201117–25_73-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoolidge201117–25-73">
         [66]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Religion">
        Religion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Visigoths&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Religion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Before the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       , the Visigoths, as well as other Germanic peoples, followed what is now referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_paganism" title="Germanic paganism">
        Germanic paganism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram199758,_66,_72–74_74-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram199758,_66,_72–74-74">
         [67]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While the Germanic peoples were slowly converted to
       <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
        Christianity
       </a>
       by varying means, many elements of the pre-Christian culture and indigenous beliefs remained firmly in place after the conversion process, particularly in the more rural and distant regions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJames2009215–225_75-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJames2009215–225-75">
         [68]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Visigoths,
       <a href="/wiki/Ostrogoths" title="Ostrogoths">
        Ostrogoths
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Vandals" title="Vandals">
        Vandals
       </a>
       were
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christianized" title="Christianized">
        Christianized
       </a>
       while they were still outside the bounds of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       ; however, they converted to
       <a href="/wiki/Arianism" title="Arianism">
        Arianism
       </a>
       rather than to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nicene_Christianity" title="Nicene Christianity">
        Nicean ("Catholic") version
       </a>
       followed by most Romans, who considered them
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christian_heresy" title="Christian heresy">
        heretics
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram199775–79_76-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram199775–79-76">
         [69]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Visigothic leadership maintained its Arianism up until at least the reign of King
       <a href="/wiki/Liuvigild" title="Liuvigild">
        Liuvigild
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWolfram1997265–269_77-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWolfram1997265–269-77">
         [70]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

          </a>
         </div>
         Capital from the Visigothic church of
         <a href="/wiki/San_Pedro_de_la_Nave" title="San Pedro de la Nave">
          San Pedro de la Nave
         </a>
         ,
         <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Zamora" title="Province of Zamora">
          province of Zamora
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       There was a religious gulf between the Visigoths, who had for a long time adhered to Arianism, and their Catholic subjects in Hispania. There were also deep sectarian splits among the Catholic population of the peninsula which contributed to the toleration of the Arian Visigoths on the peninsula. The Visigoths scorned to interfere among Catholics but were interested in decorum and public order.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78">
        <a href="#cite_note-78">
         [h]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sources indicate that the Iberian Visigoths maintained their Christian Arianism until 589 when Recarred I converted to Catholicism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMathisenSivan199940_79-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMathisenSivan199940-79">
         [71]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When the Visigoths took over Spain,
       <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       constituted a large and very ancient proportion of the population. Many were farmers, but they worked in a wide range of occupations, and were a major component of the urbanized population of the larger towns particularly of eastern Spain. During the period in which the Visigoths adhered to Arianism, the situation of the Jews seems to have remained relatively good. Previous Roman and Byzantine law determined their status, and it already sharply discriminated against them, but royal jurisdiction was in any case quite limited: local lords and populations related to Jews as they saw fit. We read of rabbis being asked by non-Jews to bless their fields, for example.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGraetz189444_80-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGraetz189444-80">
         [72]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Historian Jane Gerber relates that some of the Jews "held ranking posts in the government or the army; others were recruited and organized for garrison service; still others continued to hold senatorial rank".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGerber19929_81-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGerber19929-81">
         [73]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In general, then, they were well respected and well-treated by the Visigothic kings, that is, until their transition from Arianism to Catholicism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERoth199435–40_82-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoth199435–40-82">
         [74]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Catholic conversion across Visigothic society reduced much of the friction between their people and the native Spanish population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006847_83-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWaldmanMason2006847-83">
         [75]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One chief purpose of this conversion was to unify the realm under the Church, and one of the key complaints of the Church had long been that Jews had too much status, prosperity and influence. Local nobles relied on their Jewish and non-Jewish sectors of the population to enhance the local economy and the noble's independent power. Visigothic political structure had traditionally given extensive powers to local nobles (who even elected their kings), so the king was in many ways merely 'the first amongst equals,' and central authority was weak. The status of the Jews therefore impacted both symbolically and politically on local aristocrats. Almost immediately, therefore, King Reccared convened the first Council of Toledo to settle religious disputations related to the conversion from Arianism to Catholicism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins1999211–212_84-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins1999211–212-84">
         [76]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discriminatory laws passed at this Council seem not to have been well nor universally enforced, however, as indicated by several more Councils of Toledo that were held in subsequent years that repeated these laws, and extended their stringency. These entered canon law and became legal precedents in other parts of Europe as well. The culmination of this process occurred under King Sisibut, with a decree ordering the forced conversion of all Jews in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins200060_85-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins200060-85">
         [77]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, even this apparently achieved only partial success: similar decrees were repeated with increasing irritation and effect by later kings, as central power was consolidated. These laws either decreed the forcible baptism of the Jews or forbade circumcision, Jewish rites and observance of the Sabbath and festivals. Throughout the seventh century, Jews were flogged, executed, had their property confiscated, were subjected to ruinous taxes, forbidden to trade and, at times, dragged to the baptismal font. Many were obliged to accept Christianity but continued privately to observe the Jewish religion and practices.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGonzalez-Salinero1999140–147_86-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzalez-Salinero1999140–147-86">
         [78]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The decree of 613 set off a century of torment for Spanish Jewry, which was only ended by the Muslim conquest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-87">
        <a href="#cite_note-87">
         [i]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The political aspects of the imposition of Church power cannot be ignored in these matters. With the conversion of the Visigothic kings to
       <a href="/wiki/Chalcedonian_Christianity" title="Chalcedonian Christianity">
        Chalcedonian Christianity
       </a>
       , the bishops increased in power, until, at the
       <a href="/wiki/Fourth_Council_of_Toledo" title="Fourth Council of Toledo">
        Fourth Council of Toledo
       </a>
       in 633, they took upon themselves the right that the nobles had previously had to select a king from among the royal family. This was the same synod that declared that all Jews must be
       <a href="/wiki/Baptism" title="Baptism">
        baptised
       </a>
       . As far as the Visigoths were concerned, the time for religious pluralism "was past".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELim1999209–210_88-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELim1999209–210-88">
         [79]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By the end of the 7th century, Catholic conversion made the Visigoths less distinguishable from the indigenous Roman citizens of the Iberian peninsula; when the last Visigothic strongholds fell to the Muslim armies, whose subsequent invasions transformed Spain from the beginning of the 8th century, their Gothic identity faded.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECollins200060–62_89-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECollins200060–62-89">
         [80]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the eighth through 11th centuries, the
       <a href="/wiki/Muladi" title="Muladi">
        <i>
         muwallad
        </i>
       </a>
       clan of the
       <a href="/wiki/Banu_Qasi" title="Banu Qasi">
        Banu Qasi
       </a>
       claimed descent from the Visigothic
       <a href="/wiki/Count_Cassius" title="Count Cassius">
        Count Cassius
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFletcher200645_90-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFletcher200645-90">
         [81]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
