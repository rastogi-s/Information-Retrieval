      <p>
       A
       <b>
        geostationary orbit
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        geostationary Earth orbit
       </b>
       (often loosely referred to as
       <b>
        geosynchronous equatorial orbit
       </b>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (
       <b>
        GEO
       </b>
       ) is a circular
       <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit" title="Geosynchronous orbit">
        geosynchronous orbit
       </a>
       35,786 kilometres (22,236 mi) above the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       and following the direction of the Earth's rotation. An object in such an orbit appears motionless, at a fixed position in the sky, to ground observers.
       <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">
        Communications satellites
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Weather_satellite" title="Weather satellite">
        weather satellites
       </a>
       are often placed in geostationary orbits, so that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_antenna" title="Satellite antenna">
        satellite antennas
       </a>
       (located on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       ) that communicate with them do not have to rotate to track them, but can be pointed permanently at the position in the sky where the satellites are located. Using this characteristic, ocean color satellites with visible and near-infrared light sensors (e.g. the
       <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Ocean_Color_Imager" title="Geostationary Ocean Color Imager">
        Geostationary Ocean Color Imager
       </a>
       (GOCI)) can also be operated in geostationary orbit in order to monitor sensitive changes of ocean environments.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">
        geostationary
       </a>
       orbit is a particular type of geosynchronous orbit, which is an orbit which has an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       equal to the Earth's rotational period (one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       ) and thus the distinction being that while an object in geosynchronous orbit returns to the same point in the sky at the same time each day, an object in geostationary orbit never leaves that position. Geosynchronous orbits move around relative to a point on the earth's surface, because while geostationary orbits have an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 0 degrees relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , geosynchronous orbits have varying inclinations and eccentricities.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first appearance of a geostationary
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       in popular literature was in the first
       <a href="/wiki/Venus_Equilateral" title="Venus Equilateral">
        Venus Equilateral
       </a>
       story by
       <a href="/wiki/George_O._Smith" title="George O. Smith">
        George O. Smith
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VE_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-VE-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but Smith did not go into details. British
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_fiction_author" title="Science fiction author">
        science fiction author
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
        Arthur C. Clarke
       </a>
       disseminated the idea widely, with more details on how it would work, in a 1945 paper entitled "Extra-Terrestrial Relays — Can Rocket Stations Give Worldwide Radio Coverage?", published in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_World" title="Wireless World">
         Wireless World
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine. Clarke acknowledged the connection in his introduction to
       <i>
        The Complete Venus Equilateral
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VEintro_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-VEintro-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The orbit, which Clarke first described as useful for broadcast and relay communications satellites,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is sometimes called the
       <b>
        Clarke Orbit
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similarly, the
       <b>
        Clarke Belt
       </b>
       is the part of space about 35,786 km (22,236 mi) above
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       , in the plane of the equator, where near-geostationary orbits may be implemented. The Clarke Orbit is about 265,000 km (165,000 mi) in circumference.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Practical_uses">
        Practical uses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Practical uses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_satellite" title="Geosynchronous satellite">
        Geosynchronous satellite
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Most commercial
       <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">
        communications satellites
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Broadcast_satellite" title="Broadcast satellite">
        broadcast satellites
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SBAS" title="SBAS">
        SBAS
       </a>
       satellites operate in geostationary orbits. A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geosynchronous_transfer_orbit" title="Geosynchronous transfer orbit">
        geostationary transfer orbit
       </a>
       is used to move a satellite from
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       (LEO) into a geostationary orbit. The first satellite placed into a geostationary orbit was the
       <a href="/wiki/Syncom" title="Syncom">
        Syncom
       </a>
       -3, launched by a
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_(rocket_family)#Delta_D" title="Delta (rocket family)">
        Delta D rocket
       </a>
       in 1964.
      </p>
      <p>
       A worldwide network of operational geostationary meteorological satellites is used to provide visible and infrared images of Earth's surface and atmosphere. These satellite systems include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the United States
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">
         GOES
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Meteosat" title="Meteosat">
         Meteosat
        </a>
        , launched by the
        <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
         European Space Agency
        </a>
        and operated by the European Weather Satellite Organization,
        <a href="/wiki/EUMETSAT" title="EUMETSAT">
         EUMETSAT
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
         Japanese
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Himawari_(satellite)" title="Himawari (satellite)">
         Himawari
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Chinese
        <a href="/wiki/Fengyun" title="Fengyun">
         Fengyun
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
         India
        </a>
        's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/INSAT" title="INSAT">
         INSAT
        </a>
        series
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Statite" title="Statite">
        statite
       </a>
       , a hypothetical satellite that uses a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">
        solar sail
       </a>
       to modify its orbit, could theoretically hold itself in a geostationary "orbit" with different altitude and/or inclination from the "traditional" equatorial geostationary orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Communications">
        Communications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Communications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Satellites in geostationary orbits are far enough away from Earth that communication latency becomes significant — about a quarter of a second for a trip from one ground-based transmitter to the satellite and back to another ground-based transmitter; close to half a second for a round-trip communication from one Earth station to another and then back to the first.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, for ground stations at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">
        latitudes
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        φ
       </i>
       = ±45° on the same
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
        meridian
       </a>
       as the satellite, the time taken for a signal to pass from Earth to the satellite and back again can be computed using the

      <p>
       Geostationary satellites are directly overhead at the equator and become lower in the sky the further north or south one travels. As the observer's latitude increases, communication becomes more difficult due to factors such as atmospheric refraction, Earth's thermal emission, line-of-sight obstructions, and signal reflections from the ground or nearby structures.        </sup>

        Molniya
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tundra_orbit" title="Tundra orbit">
        Tundra
       </a>
       orbits, which have excellent visibility at high latitudes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_allocation">
        Orbit allocation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit allocation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Satellites in geostationary orbit must all occupy a single ring above the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . The requirement to space these satellites apart to avoid harmful radio-frequency interference during operations means that there are a limited number of orbital "slots" available, and thus only a limited number of satellites can be operated in geostationary orbit. This has led to conflict between different countries wishing access to the same orbital slots (countries near the same
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       but differing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latitudes" title="Latitudes">
        latitudes
       </a>
       ) and radio frequencies. These disputes are addressed through the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Telecommunication_Union" title="International Telecommunication Union">
        International Telecommunication Union
       </a>
       's allocation mechanism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 1976 Bogotá Declaration, eight countries located on the Earth's equator claimed sovereignty over the geostationary orbits above their territory, but the claims gained no international recognition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_stability">
        Orbital stability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Orbital stability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A geostationary orbit can be achieved only at an altitude very close to 35,786 km (22,236 mi) and directly above the equator. This equates to an orbital velocity of 3.07 km/s (1.91 mi/s) and an orbital period of 1,436 minutes, which equates to almost exactly one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_day" title="Sidereal day">
        sidereal day
       </a>
       (23.934461223 hours). This ensures that the satellite will match the Earth's rotational period and has a stationary
       <a href="/wiki/Footprint_(satellite)" title="Footprint (satellite)">
        footprint
       </a>
       on the ground. All geostationary satellites have to be located on this ring.
      </p>
      <p>
       A combination of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        lunar
       </a>
       gravity,
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       gravity, and the flattening of the Earth at its poles causes a
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       motion of the orbital plane of any geostationary object, with an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       of about 53 years and an initial inclination gradient of about 0.85° per year, achieving a maximal inclination of 15° after 26.5 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2015_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2015-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To correct for this
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        orbital perturbation
       </a>
       , regular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orbital_stationkeeping" title="Orbital stationkeeping">
        orbital stationkeeping
       </a>
       manoeuvres are necessary, amounting to a
       <a href="/wiki/Delta-v" title="Delta-v">
        delta-v
       </a>
       of approximately 50 m/s per year.
      </p>
      <p>
       A second effect to be taken into account is the longitudinal drift, caused by the asymmetry of the Earth – the equator is slightly elliptical. There are two stable (at 75.3°E and 252°E) and two unstable (at 165.3°E and 14.7°W) equilibrium points. Any geostationary object placed between the equilibrium points would (without any action) be slowly accelerated towards the stable equilibrium position, causing a periodic longitude variation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2015_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2015-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The correction of this effect requires
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_station-keeping#Station-keeping_in_geostationary_orbit" title="Orbital station-keeping">
        station-keeping maneuvers
       </a>
       with a maximal delta-v of about 2 m/s per year, depending on the desired longitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        Solar wind
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_pressure" title="Radiation pressure">
        radiation pressure
       </a>
       also exert small forces on satellites; over time, these cause them to slowly drift away from their prescribed orbits.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the absence of servicing missions from the Earth or a renewable propulsion method, the consumption of thruster propellant for station keeping places a limitation on the lifetime of the satellite.
       <a href="/wiki/Hall-effect_thruster" title="Hall-effect thruster">
        Hall-effect thrusters
       </a>
       , which are currently in use, have the potential to prolong the service life of a satellite by providing high-efficiency
       <a href="/wiki/Electrically_powered_spacecraft_propulsion" title="Electrically powered spacecraft propulsion">
        electric propulsion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Limitations_to_usable_life_of_geostationary_satellites">
        Limitations to usable life of geostationary satellites
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geostationary_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Limitations to usable life of geostationary satellites">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When they run out of thruster fuel, the satellites are at the end of their service life, as they are no longer able to stay in their allocated orbital position. The transponders and other onboard systems generally outlive the thruster fuel and, by stopping N–S station keeping, some satellites can continue to be used in
       <a href="/wiki/Inclined_orbit" title="Inclined orbit">
        inclined orbits
       </a>
       (where the orbital track appears to follow a
       <a href="/wiki/Analemma#Analemmas_of_geosynchronous_satellites" title="Analemma">
        figure-eight loop centred on the equator
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or else be elevated to a "graveyard"
       <a href="/wiki/Graveyard_orbit" title="Graveyard orbit">
        disposal orbit
       </a>
       .
      </p>
