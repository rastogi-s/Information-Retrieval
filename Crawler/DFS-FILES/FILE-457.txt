      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish verbs
       </b>
       form one of the more complex areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_grammar" title="Spanish grammar">
        Spanish grammar
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       is a relatively
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic language
       </a>
       with a moderate to high degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       , which shows up mostly in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_verb_conjugation" title="Spanish verb conjugation">
        Spanish verb conjugation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As is typical of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbs" title="Verbs">
        verbs
       </a>
       in virtually all languages, Spanish verbs express an action or a state of being of a given subject, and like verbs in most
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , Spanish verbs undergo
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       according to the following categories:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
         Tense
        </a>
        :
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_Tense" title="Past Tense">
         past
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
         present
        </a>
        , or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_Tense" title="Future Tense">
         future
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
         Number
        </a>
        : singular or
        <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
         plural
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
         Person
        </a>
        : first, second or third
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction#Spanish" title="T–V distinction">
         T–V distinction
        </a>
        : familiar or formal
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_Mood" title="Grammatical Mood">
         Mood
        </a>
        :
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicative_mood" title="Indicative mood">
         indicative
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Subjunctive_mood" title="Subjunctive mood">
         subjunctive
        </a>
        , or
        <a href="/wiki/Imperative_mood" title="Imperative mood">
         imperative
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
         Aspect
        </a>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">
         perfective
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">
         imperfective
        </a>
        (distinguished only in the past tense as
        <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
         preterite
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">
         imperfect
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_Voice" title="Grammatical Voice">
         Voice
        </a>
        :
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_Voice" title="Active Voice">
         active
        </a>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Passive_Voice" title="Passive Voice">
         passive
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The modern Spanish verb system has sixteen distinct complete
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       paradigms (i.e. sets of forms for each combination of tense and mood (tense refers to when the action takes place, and mood or mode refers to the mood of the subject – e.g., certainty vs. doubt), plus one incomplete
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       paradigm (the imperative), as well as three
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        non-temporal forms
       </a>
       (the infinitive, gerund, and past participle).
      </p>
      <p>
       The fourteen regular tenses are also subdivided into seven
       <i>
        simple tenses
       </i>
       and seven
       <i>
        compound tenses
       </i>
       (also known as the perfect). The seven
       <i>
        compound tenses
       </i>
       are formed with the
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verb
       </a>
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       followed by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Past_participle" title="Past participle">
        past participle
       </a>
       . Verbs can be used in other forms, such as the present progressive, but in grammar treatises they are not usually considered a special tense but rather
       <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">
        periphrastic
       </a>
       verbal constructions.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Accidents_of_a_verb">
        Accidents of a verb
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Accidents of a verb">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A verbal accident is defined as one of the changes of form that a verb can undergo. Spanish verbs have five accidents. Every verb changes according to the following:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Person_and_number">
        Person and number
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Person and number">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish verbs are conjugated in three persons, each having a singular and a plural form. In some varieties of Spanish, such as that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Río de la Plata Region
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        a special form of the second person
       </a>
       is used.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Spanish is a "
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        pro-drop language
       </a>
       ", subject pronouns are often omitted.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_person">
        First person
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: First person">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The grammatical first person refers to the speaker ("I"). The first person plural refers to the speaker together with at least one other person.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Yo)
         <b>
          soy
         </b>
        </i>
        : "I am"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Nosotros/Nosotras)
         <b>
          somos
         </b>
        </i>
        : "We are"; the feminine form
        <i>
         nosotras
        </i>
        is used only when referring to a group that is composed entirely of females; otherwise,
        <i>
         nosotros
        </i>
        is used.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Second_person">
        Second person
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Second person">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The grammatical second person refers to the addressee, the receiver of the communication ("you"). Spanish has different pronouns (and verb forms) for "you," depending on the relationship, familiar or formal, between speaker and addressee.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Singular forms
       </b>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Tú)
         <b>
          eres
         </b>
        </i>
        : "You are"; familiar singular; used when addressing someone who is of close affinity (a member of the family, a close friend, a child, a pet). Also the form used to address the
        <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">
         deity
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (
         <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
          Vos
         </a>
         )
         <b>
          sos
         </b>
        </i>
        : "You are"; familiar singular; generally used in the same way as
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        . Its use is restricted to some areas of
        <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
         Hispanic America
        </a>
        ; where
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        are both used,
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        is used to denote a closer affinity.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Usted)
         <b>
          es
         </b>
        </i>
        : "You are"; formal singular; used when addressing a person respectfully, someone older, someone not known to the speaker, or someone of some social distance. Although it is a second-person pronoun, it uses third-person verb forms (and object pronouns and possessives) because it developed as a contraction of
        <i>
         vuestra merced
        </i>
        (literally, "your mercy" or "your grace").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <b>
        Plural forms
       </b>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Vosotros/Vosotras)
         <b>
          sois
         </b>
        </i>
        : "You (all) are"; familiar plural; used when addressing people who are of close affinity (members of the family, friends, children, pets). The feminine form
        <i>
         vosotras
        </i>
        is used only when addressing a group composed entirely of females; otherwise,
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        is used. Used primarily in
        <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
         Spain
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Equatoguinean_Spanish" title="Equatoguinean Spanish">
         Equatorial Guinea
        </a>
        , though it may appear in old, formal texts from other countries, such as the
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
         Philippines
        </a>
        ,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        or in the initial line of the Argentine national anthem (“Oíd, mortales, el grito sagrado”).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ustedes)
         <b>
          son
         </b>
        </i>
        : "You (all) are"; formal plural where
        <i>
         vosotros
        </i>
        is used; both familiar and formal plural elsewhere. Where it is strictly formal, used when addressing people respectfully or addressing people of some social distance. Like
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        , it uses third-person verb forms, for the same reasons.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Third_person">
        Third person
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Third person">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The grammatical third person refers to a person or thing other than the speaker or the addressee.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Singular forms
       </b>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Él)
         <b>
          es
         </b>
        </i>
        : "He/it is"; used for a male person or a thing of
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         masculine
        </a>
        (grammatical) gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ella)
         <b>
          es
         </b>
        </i>
        : "She/it is"; used for a female person or a thing of
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         feminine
        </a>
        (grammatical) gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ello)
         <b>
          es
         </b>
        </i>
        : "It is"; used to refer to
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         neuter
        </a>
        nouns such as facts, ideas, situations, and sets of things; rarely used as an explicit subject.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <b>
        Plural forms
       </b>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ellos)
         <b>
          son
         </b>
        </i>
        : "They are"; used for a group of people or things that includes at least one person or thing of
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         masculine
        </a>
        (grammatical) gender.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (Ellas)
         <b>
          son
         </b>
        </i>
        : "They are"; used for a group of people or things that are all of
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         feminine
        </a>
        (grammatical) gender.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mood">
        Mood
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Mood">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Grammatical mood is one of a set of distinctive forms that are used to signal modality. In Spanish, every verb has forms in three moods.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Indicative mood: The indicative mood, or evidential mood, is used for factual statements and positive beliefs. The Spanish conditional, although semantically expressing the dependency of one action or proposition on another, is generally considered a tense of the indicative mood, because, syntactically, it can appear in an independent clause.
       </li>
       <li>
        Subjunctive mood: The subjunctive mood expresses an imagined or desired action in the past, present, or future.
       </li>
       <li>
        Imperative mood: The imperative mood expresses direct commands, requests, and prohibitions. In Spanish, using the imperative mood may sound blunt or even rude, so it is often used with care.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tense">
        Tense
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Tense">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The tense of a verb indicates the time when the action occurs. It may be in the past, present, or future.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impersonal_or_non-finite_forms_of_the_verb">
        Impersonal or non-finite forms of the verb
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Impersonal or non-finite forms of the verb">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        Non-finite
       </a>
       verb forms refer to an action or state without indicating the time or person. Spanish has three impersonal forms: the infinitive, the gerund, and the past participle.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Infinitive">
        Infinitive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Infinitive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The infinitive is generally the form found in dictionaries. It corresponds to the English "base-form" or "dictionary form" and is usually indicated in English by "to _____" ("to sing," "to write," etc.). The ending of the infinitive is the basis of the names given in English to the three classes of Spanish verbs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        " verbs (
        <i>
         primera conjugación
        </i>
        ["first conjugation"])
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         hablar
        </i>
        ("to speak");
        <i>
         cantar
        </i>
        ("to sing");
        <i>
         bailar
        </i>
        ("to dance")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "
        <i>
         -er
        </i>
        " verbs (
        <i>
         segunda conjugación
        </i>
        ["second conjugation"])
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         beber
        </i>
        ("to drink");
        <i>
         leer
        </i>
        ("to read");
        <i>
         comprender
        </i>
        ("to understand")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "
        <i>
         -ir
        </i>
        " verbs (
        <i>
         tercera conjugación
        </i>
        ["third conjugation"])
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         vivir
        </i>
        ("to live");
        <i>
         sentir
        </i>
        ("to feel");
        <i>
         escribir
        </i>
        ("to write")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gerund">
        Gerund
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Gerund">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Although in English grammar the
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerund
       </a>
       refers to the
       <i>
        -ing
       </i>
       form of the verb used as a noun, in Spanish the term refers to a verb form that behaves more like an adverb. It is created by adding the following endings to the stem of the verb (i.e. the infinitive without the last two letters):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        verbs:
        <i>
         <b>
          -ando
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         hablando
        </i>
        ("speaking");
        <i>
         cantando
        </i>
        ("singing");
        <i>
         bailando
        </i>
        ("dancing")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -er
        </i>
        verbs:
        <i>
         <b>
          -iendo
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         bebiendo
        </i>
        ("drinking");
        <i>
         leyendo
        </i>
        (with spelling change; "reading");
        <i>
         comprendiendo
        </i>
        ("understanding")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -ir
        </i>
        verbs:
        <i>
         <b>
          -iendo
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         viviendo
        </i>
        ("living");
        <i>
         sintiendo
        </i>
        (with stem-vowel change; "feeling");
        <i>
         escribiendo
        </i>
        ("writing")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Certain verbs have irregular gerund forms:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Most
        <i>
         -ir
        </i>
        verbs undergo a
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs#Vowel_raising" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
         predictable stem-vowel change
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         sentir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         sintiendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         medir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         midiendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         repetir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         repitiendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         dormir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         durmiendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         morir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         muriendo
        </i>
        . One
        <i>
         -er
        </i>
        verb also belongs to this group:
        <i>
         poder
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         pudiendo
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In verbs whose stem ends in a vowel, the spelling of the
        <i>
         -iendo
        </i>
        ending is changed to
        <i>
         -yendo
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         oír
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         oyendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         caer
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         cayendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         leer
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         leyendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         traer
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         trayendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         construir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         construyendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         huir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         huyendo
        </i>
        . The "stemless" verb
        <i>
         ir
        </i>
        belongs to this group, with
        <i>
         yendo
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        For
        <i>
         -er
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         -ir
        </i>
        verbs whose stem ends in &lt;ñ&gt; or &lt;ll&gt;, the
        <i>
         -iendo
        </i>
        ending is reduced to
        <i>
         -endo
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         tañer
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         tañendo
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         bullir
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         bullendo
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The gerund has a variety of uses and can mean (with
       <i>
        haciendo
       </i>
       , for example) "doing/while doing/by doing/because of one's doing/through doing" and so on. It is also used to form
       <a href="/wiki/Continuous_and_progressive_aspects" title="Continuous and progressive aspects">
        progressive
       </a>
       constructions, such as
       <i>
        estoy haciendo
       </i>
       ("I am doing"). The gerund cannot be used as an adjective and, unlike in most European languages, generally has no corresponding adjectival forms. The now-mostly archaic present participle, which ended in
       <i>
        -ante
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -iente
       </i>
       and formerly filled this function, in some cases survives as such an adjective (e.g.
       <i>
        durmiente
       </i>
       ("sleeping"),
       <i>
        interesante
       </i>
       ("interesting")), but such cases are limited, and in cases where it does not, other constructions must be used to express the same ideas: where in English one would say "the crying baby", one would say in Spanish
       <i>
        el bebé que llora
       </i>
       ("the baby who's crying";
       <i>
        llorante
       </i>
       is archaic).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Past_participle">
        Past participle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Past participle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_irregular_participles" title="List of Spanish irregular participles">
        List of Spanish irregular participles
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The past participle corresponds to the English
       <i>
        -en
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -ed
       </i>
       form of the verb. It is created by adding the following endings to the verb stem:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        verbs:
        <i>
         <b>
          -ado
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         hablado
        </i>
        ("spoken");
        <i>
         cantado
        </i>
        ("sung");
        <i>
         bailado
        </i>
        ("danced")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -er
        </i>
        verbs:
        <i>
         <b>
          -ido
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         bebido
        </i>
        ("drunk");
        <i>
         leído
        </i>
        (requires accent mark; "read");
        <i>
         comprendido
        </i>
        ("understood")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         -ir
        </i>
        verbs,:
        <i>
         <b>
          -ido
         </b>
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Examples:
        <i>
         vivido
        </i>
        ("lived");
        <i>
         sentido
        </i>
        ("felt");
        <i>
         hervido
        </i>
        ("boiled")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The past participle, ending invariably in
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       , is used following the
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verb
       </a>
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       to form the compound or perfect:
       <i>
        (Yo) he
        <b>
         hablado
        </b>
       </i>
       ("I have spoken");
       <i>
        (Ellos) habían
        <b>
         hablado
        </b>
       </i>
       ("They had spoken"); etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       When the past participle is used as an adjective, it inflects for both gender and number – for example,
       <i>
        una lengua
        <b>
         hablada
        </b>
        en España
       </i>
       ("a language spoken in Spain").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voice">
        Voice
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Voice">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In grammar, the voice of a verb describes the relationship between the action (or state) that the verb expresses and the participants identified by its arguments (
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        object
       </a>
       , etc.). When the subject is the
       <a href="/wiki/Agent_(grammar)" title="Agent (grammar)">
        agent
       </a>
       or doer of the action, the verb is in the active voice. When the subject is the
       <a href="/wiki/Patient_(grammar)" title="Patient (grammar)">
        patient
       </a>
       , target, or undergoer of the action, it is said to be in the passive voice.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_aspect">
        Verbal aspect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Verbal aspect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Verbal aspect marks whether an action is completed (perfect), a completed whole (perfective), or not yet completed (imperfective).
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Perfect: In Spanish, verbs that are conjugated with
        <i>
         <b>
          haber
         </b>
        </i>
        ("to have [done something]") are in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perfect_aspect" title="Perfect aspect">
         perfect aspect
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Perfective: In Spanish, verbs in the preterite are in the
        <a href="/wiki/Perfective_aspect" title="Perfective aspect">
         perfective aspect
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Imperfective: In Spanish, the present, imperfect, and future tenses are in the
        <a href="/wiki/Imperfective_aspect" title="Imperfective aspect">
         imperfective aspect
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugation">

      </h2>

      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_indicative">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

        </span>
       </span>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The indicative mood has five simple tenses, each of which has a corresponding perfect form. In older classifications, the conditional tenses were considered part of an independent
       <i>
        conditional mood
       </i>
       . Continuous forms (such as
       <i>
        estoy hablando
       </i>
       ) are usually not considered part of the verbal paradigm, though they often appear in books addressed to English speakers who are learning Spanish. Modern grammatical studies count only the simple forms as tenses, and the other forms as products of tenses and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        aspects
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <span id="Simple_tenses_.28tiempos_simples.29">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The simple tenses are the forms of the verb without the use of a modal or helping verb. The following are the simple tenses and their uses:
      </p>

       <b>
        Uses
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       The present is used to indicate the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Actual present
        </b>
        : This expresses an action that is being done at the very moment.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         María
         <b>
          habla
         </b>

        </i>
        = "María is speaking with Juan on the telephone"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Habitual present
        </b>
        : This expresses an action that is regularly and habitually being done.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         María
         <b>
          va
         </b>
         al campo todos los sábados
        </i>
        = "María goes to the countryside every Saturday"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Atemporal present
        </b>
        : This expresses general truths that are not bounded by time.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Dos más dos
         <b>
          son
         </b>
         cuatro
        </i>
        = "Two plus two equals four"
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Los planetas
         <b>
          giran
         </b>
         alrededor del sol
        </i>
        = "The planets revolve around the sun"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Historical present
        </b>
        : This expresses an action that happened in the past but is accepted as historical fact.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Fernando Magallanes
         <b>
          descubre
         </b>
         las Filipinas el 15 de marzo de 1521
        </i>
        = "Ferdinand Magellan discovered the Philippines on 15 March 1521"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         An immediate future
        </b>
        : This expresses an action that will be done in the very near future with a high degree of certainty.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Este junio,
         <b>
          viajo
         </b>
         a España
        </i>
        = "This June, I am travelling to Spain"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Imperative value
        </b>
        : In some areas of Spain and
        <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
         Hispanic America
        </a>
        , the present can be used (with an exclamatory tone) with an imperative value.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         ¡Ahora
         <b>
          te vas
         </b>
         y
         <b>
          pides
         </b>
         disculpas al señor Ruiz!
        </i>
        = "Now go and ask pardon from Mr. Ruiz!"
       </dd>
      </dl>

        </b>

       </li>

        <i>

         <b>
          hablaba
         </b>

        </i>

       </dd>
      </dl>

       <li>
        <b>
         An action interrupted by another action
        </b>
        : This expresses an action that was in progress when another action took place.
       </li>

       <dd>
        <i>

        </i>

         </b>

        </i>

       </dd>
      </dl>

          Será
         </b>
         Fabio.
        </i>
        = "Who (do you suppose) is knocking at the door? — It must be Fabio" or "Who will that be knocking at the door? — That'll be Fabio"; this use of the future tense also occurs in English; see
        <a href="/wiki/Future_tense#Relation_among_tense.2C_aspect.2C_and_modality_implications_of_.22will.22_and_.22going_to.22" title="Future tense">
         Future Tense, Relation among tense, aspect, and modality implications of "will" and "going to"
        </a>
        ).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Command, prohibition, or obligation
        </b>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         No
         <b>
          llevarás
         </b>
         a ese hombre a mi casa
        </i>
        = "Do not bring that man to my house" or, more accurately, "You will not bring that man to my house"; this form is also used to assert a command, prohibition, or obligation in English.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Courtesy
        </b>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         ¿Te
         <b>
          importará
         </b>
         encender la televisión?
        </i>
        = "Would you mind turning on the television?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Another common way to represent the future is with a present indicative conjugation of
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       followed by
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       plus an infinitive verb:
       <i>
        <b>
         Voy a viajar
        </b>
        a Bolivia en el verano
       </i>
       ("I'm going to travel to Bolivia in the summer").
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Compound_tenses_.28tiempos_compuestos.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_tenses_(tiempos_compuestos)">
        Compound tenses (
        <i>
         tiempos compuestos
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Compound tenses (tiempos compuestos)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       All the compound tenses are formed with
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       followed by the past participle of the main verb.
       <i>
        Haber
       </i>
       changes its form for person, number, and the like, while the past participle remains invariable, ending with
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       regardless of the number or gender of the subject.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span id="Present_perfect_.28pret.C3.A9rito_perfecto.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_perfect_(pretérito_perfecto)">
        Present perfect (
        <i>
         pretérito perfecto
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Present perfect (pretérito perfecto)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       In the present perfect, the present indicative of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       is used as an auxiliary, and it is followed by the past participle of the main verb. In most of Spanish America, this tense has virtually the same use as the English present perfect:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Te
         <b>
          he dicho
         </b>
         mi opinión
        </i>
        = "I have told you my opinion"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In most of Spain the tense has an additional use—to express a past action or event that is contained in an unfinished period of time or that has effects in the present:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este mes
         <b>
          ha llovido
         </b>
         mucho, pero hoy hace buen día
        </i>
        = "It rained a lot this month, but today is a fine day"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h5>
       <span id="Past_perfect_or_pluperfect_.28pret.C3.A9rito_pluscuamperfecto.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Past_perfect_or_pluperfect_(pretérito_pluscuamperfecto)">
        Past perfect or pluperfect (
        <i>
         pretérito pluscuamperfecto
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Past perfect or pluperfect (pretérito pluscuamperfecto)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       In this tense, the imperfect form of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       is used as a modal, and it is followed by the past participle of the main verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (yo) había
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (tú) habías
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (él/ella/ello/usted) había
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (nosotros/nosotras) habíamos
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (vosotros/vosotras) habíais
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (ellos/ellas/ustedes) habían
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <b>
        Uses
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       The past perfect is used to express the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         A past action that occurred prior to another past action
        </b>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Yo
         <b>
          había esperado
         </b>
         tres horas cuando él llegó
        </i>
        = "I had been waiting for three hours when he arrived"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h5>
       <span id="Past_anterior_.28pret.C3.A9rito_anterior.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Past_anterior_(pretérito_anterior)">
        Past anterior (
        <i>
         pretérito anterior
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Past anterior (pretérito anterior)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       The past anterior combines the preterite form of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       with the past participle of the main verb. It is very rare in spoken Spanish, but it is sometimes used in formal written language, where it is almost entirely limited to subordinate (temporal, adverbial) clauses. Thus, it is usually introduced by temporal conjunctions such as
       <i>
        cuando
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        apenas
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        en cuanto
       </i>
       . It is used to express an action that ended immediately before another past action:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (yo) hube
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (tú) hubiste
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (él/ella/ello/usted) hubo
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (nosotros/nosotras) hubimos
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (vosotros/vosotras) hubisteis
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (ellos/ellas/ustedes) hubieron
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando
         <b>
          hubieron llegado
         </b>
         todos, empezó la ceremonia
        </i>
        = "When everyone had arrived, the ceremony began"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Apenas María
         <b>
          hubo terminado
         </b>
         la canción, su padre entró
        </i>
        = "As soon as Maria had finished the song, her father came in"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It is often replaced by either the preterite or the pluperfect, with the same meaning:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Apenas María
         <b>
          terminó
         </b>
         la canción, su padre entró
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Apenas María
         <b>
          había terminado
         </b>
         la canción, su padre entró
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h5>
       <span id="Future_perfect_.28futuro_compuesto.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_perfect_(futuro_compuesto)">
        Future perfect (
        <i>
         futuro compuesto
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Future perfect (futuro compuesto)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       The future perfect is formed with the future indicative of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       followed by the past participle of the main verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         (yo) habré
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (tú) habrás
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (él/ella/ello/usted) habrá
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (nosotros/nosotras) habremos
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (vosotros/vosotras) habréis
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         (ellos/ellas/ustedes) habrán
        </i>
        + past participle
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Habré hablado
        </i>
        = "I shall/will have spoken"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It is used to indicate a future action that will be finished right before another action:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando yo llegue a la fiesta, ya se habrán marchado todos
        </i>
        = "When I get to the party, everyone will already have left"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_conditional">
        The conditional
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: The conditional">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span id="Simple_conditional_.28condicional_simple_or_pospret.C3.A9rito.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Simple_conditional_(condicional_simple_or_pospretérito)">
        Simple conditional (
        <i>
         condicional simple
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         pospretérito
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Simple conditional (condicional simple or pospretérito)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       As with the future, the conditional uses the entire infinitive as the stem. The following endings are attached to it:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Pronoun subject
        </th>
        <th>
         (All regular verbs)
         <i>
          Infinitive form
         </i>
         +
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         yo
        </td>
        <td>
         -ía
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         tú/vos
        </td>
        <td>
         -ías
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         él/ella/ello/usted
        </td>
        <td>
         -ía
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         nosotros/nosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         -íamos
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         vosotros/vosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         -íais
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         ellos/ellas/ustedes
        </td>
        <td>
         -ían
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <b>
        Uses
       </b>
      </p>
      <p>
       The conditional is used to express the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Courtesy
        </b>
        : Using this mood softens a request, making it more polite.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Señor, ¿
         <b>
          podría
         </b>
         darme una copa de vino?
        </i>
        = "Sir, could you give me a glass of wine?"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Polite expression of a desire (using
         <i>
          querer
         </i>
         )
        </b>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         <b>
          Querría
         </b>
         ver la película esta semana
        </i>
        = "I would like to see the film this week"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         In a
         <i>
          then
         </i>
         clause whose realization depends on a hypothetical
         <i>
          if
         </i>
         clause:
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Si yo fuera rico,
         <b>
          viajaría
         </b>
         a Sudamérica
        </i>
        = "If I were rich, I would travel to South America"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Speculation about past events
        </b>
        (the speaker's knowledge is indirect, unconfirmed, or approximating):
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         ¿Cuantas personas asistieron a la inauguración del Presidente? — No lo sé;
         <b>
          habría
         </b>
         unas 5.000
        </i>
        = "How many people attended the President's inauguration? — I do not know; there must have been about 5,000")
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         A future action in relation to the past
        </b>
        : This expresses future action that was imagined in the past.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Cuando era pequeño, pensaba que me
         <b>
          gustaría
         </b>
         ser médico
        </i>
        = "When I was young, I thought that I would like to be a doctor"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         A suggestion
        </b>
        :
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         Yo que tú, lo
         <b>
          olvidaría
         </b>
         completamente
        </i>
        = "If I were you, I would forget him completely"
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h4>
       <span id="Conditional_perfect_or_compound_conditional_.28condicional_compuesto_or_antepospret.C3.A9rito.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conditional_perfect_or_compound_conditional_(condicional_compuesto_or_antepospretérito)">
        Conditional perfect or compound conditional (
        <i>
         condicional compuesto
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         antepospretérito
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Conditional perfect or compound conditional (condicional compuesto or antepospretérito)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The conditional perfect refers to a hypothetical past action.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo habría hablado si me hubieran/hubiesen dado la oportunidad
        </i>
        = "I would have spoken if they had given me the opportunity to"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_imperative">
        The imperative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: The imperative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The imperative mood has three specific forms, corresponding to the pronouns
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       are used in different regional dialects;
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       only in Spain). These forms are used only in positive expressions, not negative ones. The subjunctive supplements the imperative in all other cases (negative expressions and the conjugations corresponding to the pronouns
       <i>
        nosotros
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        él/ella
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ellos/ellas
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The imperative can also be expressed in three other ways:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Using the present or future indicative to form an emphatic command:
        <i>
         Comerás la verdura
        </i>
        ("You will eat the vegetables")
       </li>
       <li>
        The first person plural imperative ("Let's...") can also be expressed by
        <i>
         Vamos a
        </i>
        + infinitive:
        <i>
         ¡Vamos a comer!
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        Indirect commands with
        <i>
         que
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         Que lo llame el secretario
        </i>
        ("Have the secretary call him")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span id="Affirmative_imperative_.28imperativo_positivo.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Affirmative_imperative_(imperativo_positivo)">
        Affirmative imperative (
        <i>
         imperativo positivo
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Affirmative imperative (imperativo positivo)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The positive form of the imperative mood in regular verbs is formed by adding the following to the stem:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Pronoun subject
        </th>
        <th>
         <i>
          -ar
         </i>
         verbs
        </th>
        <th>
         <i>
          -er
         </i>
         verbs
        </th>
        <th>
         <i>
          -ir
         </i>
         verbs
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         tú
        </td>
        <td>
         -a
        </td>
        <td colspan="2">
         -e
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         vos
        </td>
        <td>
         -á
        </td>
        <td>
         -é
        </td>
        <td>
         -í
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         usted
        </td>
        <td>
         -e
        </td>
        <td colspan="2">
         -a
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         nosotros/nosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         -emos
        </td>
        <td colspan="2">
         -amos
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         vosotros/vosotras
        </td>
        <td>
         -ad (-ar)
        </td>
        <td>
         -ed (-er)
        </td>
        <td>
         -id (-ir)
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         ustedes
        </td>
        <td>
         -en
        </td>
        <td colspan="2">
         -an
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       The singular imperative
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       coincides with the third-person singular of the indicative for all but a few irregular verbs. The plural
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       is always the same as the infinitive, but with a final
       <i>
        -d
       </i>
       instead of an
       <i>
        -r
       </i>
       in the formal, written form; the informal spoken form is the same as the infinitive. The singular
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       drops the
       <i>
        -r

      </p>
      <p>
       <small>
        Beginner's rule:
       </small>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <small>
         To conjugate something that is positive in the imperative mood for the
         <i>
          tú
         </i>
         form (which is used most often), conjugate for the
         <i>
          tú
         </i>
         form and drop the
         <i>
          s
         </i>
         .
        </small>
       </li>
       <li>
        <small>
         To conjugate something that is negative in the imperative mood for the
         <i>
          tú
         </i>
         form (which also is used most often), conjugate in the
         <i>
          yo
         </i>
         form, drop the
         <i>
          o
         </i>
         , add the opposite
         <i>
          tú
         </i>
         ending (if it is an
         <i>
          -ar
         </i>
         verb add
         <i>
          es
         </i>
         ; for an
         <i>
          -er
         </i>
         or
         <i>
          -ir
         </i>
         verb add
         <i>
          as
         </i>
         ), and then put the word
         <i>
          no
         </i>
         in front.
        </small>
       </li>
      </ol>

        Imperfect subjunctive (
        <i>
         imperfecto de subjuntivo
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       The imperfect subjunctive can be formed with either of two sets of endings: the "
       <i>
        -ra
       </i>
       endings" or the "
       <i>

       </i>

       <i>
        -ra
       </i>

       <i>
        -se
       </i>
       forms. In Spain, both sets of forms are used, but the
       <i>
        -ra
       </i>
       forms are predominant as well.

      <p>
       The future subjunctive is no longer used in modern language, except in legal language and some fixed expressions. The following endings are attached to the preterite stem:
      </p>

       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando hablaren...
        </i>
        = "Whenever they might speak..."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span id="Compound_tenses_.28tiempos_compuestos.29_2">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Compound_tenses_(tiempos_compuestos)_2">
        Compound tenses (
        <i>
         tiempos compuestos
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Compound tenses (tiempos compuestos)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the subjunctive mood, the subjunctive forms of the verb
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       are used with the past participle of the main verb.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span id="Present_perfect_subjunctive_.28pret.C3.A9rito_perfecto_de_subjuntivo.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_perfect_subjunctive_(pretérito_perfecto_de_subjuntivo)">
        Present perfect subjunctive (
        <i>
         pretérito perfecto de subjuntivo
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Present perfect subjunctive (pretérito perfecto de subjuntivo)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando yo haya hablado...
        </i>
        = "When I have spoken..."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h5>
       <span id="Pluperfect_subjunctive_.28pluscuamperfecto_de_subjuntivo.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pluperfect_subjunctive_(pluscuamperfecto_de_subjuntivo)">
        Pluperfect subjunctive (
        <i>
         pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Pluperfect subjunctive (pluscuamperfecto de subjuntivo)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Si yo hubiera hablado...
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Si yo hubiese hablado...
        </i>
        = "If I had spoken..."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h5>
       <span id="Future_perfect_subjunctive_.28futuro_compuesto_de_subjuntivo.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_perfect_subjunctive_(futuro_compuesto_de_subjuntivo)">
        Future perfect subjunctive (
        <i>
         futuro compuesto de subjuntivo
        </i>
        )
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: Future perfect subjunctive (futuro compuesto de subjuntivo)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       Like the simple future subjunctive, this tense is no longer used in modern Spanish.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cuando yo hubiere hablado...
        </i>
        = "When I shall have spoken..."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <small>
        Notes:
       </small>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <small>
         The present subjunctive is formed from the stem of the first person present indicative of a verb. Therefore, for an irregular verb like
         <i>
          salir
         </i>
         with the first person
         <i>
          salgo
         </i>
         , the present subjunctive would be
         <i>
          salga
         </i>
         , not
         <i>
          sala
         </i>
         .
        </small>
       </li>
       <li>
        <small>
         The choice between present subjunctive and imperfect subjunctive is determined by the tense of the main verb of the sentence.
        </small>
       </li>
       <li>
        <small>
         The future subjunctive is rarely used in modern Spanish and mostly appears in old texts, legal documents, and certain fixed expressions, such as
         <i>
          venga lo que viniere
         </i>
         ("come what may").
        </small>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Continuous_tenses">
        Continuous tenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: Continuous tenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Spanish grammar, continuous tenses are not formally recognized as in English. Although the imperfect expresses a continuity compared to the perfect (e.g.,
       <i>
        te esperaba
       </i>
       ["I was waiting for you"]), the continuity of an action is usually expressed by a verbal
       <a href="/wiki/Periphrasis" title="Periphrasis">
        periphrasis
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        perífrasis verbal
       </i>
       ), as in
       <i>
        estoy leyendo
       </i>
       ("I am reading"). However, one can also say
       <i>
        sigo leyendo
       </i>
       ("I am
       <i>
        still
       </i>
       reading"),
       <i>
        voy leyendo
       </i>
       ("I am
       <i>
        slowly but surely
       </i>
       reading"),
       <i>
        ando leyendo
       </i>
       ("I am
       <i>
        going around
       </i>
       reading"), and others.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_passive">
        The passive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: The passive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span id=".22True.22_passive">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"True"_passive'>
        "True" passive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title='Edit section: "True" passive'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The "true" passive is formed with
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       + the past participle, which in this case behaves like a normal adjective. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo soy amado
        </i>
        = "I [masc.] am loved"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tú eras amada
        </i>
        = "You [fem.] were being loved"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nosotros seremos amados
        </i>
        = "We [masc.] will be loved"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellas habrían sido amadas
        </i>
        = "They [fem.] would have been loved"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The "true" passive is used in a variety of situations, but its use is somewhat more limited than that of its English counterpart.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Se_passive">
        <i>
         Se
        </i>
        passive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: Se passive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the third person, reflexive constructions are often used to express ideas that could also be expressed in the passive. In such constructions, the recipient of the action is said to do the action to itself. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se habla español
        </i>
        = "Spanish is spoken" (lit. "Spanish speaks itself")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se me dio el libro
        </i>
        = "The book was given to me" (lit. "The book gave itself to me")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se perdieron los datos
        </i>
        = "The data were lost" (lit. The data lost themselves")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se puede hacer [ello]
        </i>
        = "It can be done" (lit. "It can do itself")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        se
       </i>
       passive is very common in the third person, but equivalent constructions cannot be used for the first and second persons:
       <i>
        Yo me amo
       </i>
       always translates to "I love myself" and never "I am loved".
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Irregular_verbs">
        Irregular verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Irregular verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
        Spanish irregular verbs
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A considerable number of verbs change the vowel
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       in the stem to the diphthong
       <i>
        ie
       </i>
       , and the vowel
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        ue
       </i>
       . This happens when the stem vowel receives the stress. These verbs are referred to as stem-changing verbs. Examples include
       <i>
        p
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        nsar
       </i>
       ("to think"; e.g.,
       <i>
        p
        <b>
         ie
        </b>
        nso
       </i>
       ["I think"]),
       <i>
        s
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        ntarse
       </i>
       ("to sit"; e.g.,
       <i>
        me s
        <b>
         ie
        </b>
        nto
       </i>
       ["I sit"]),
       <i>
        emp
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        zar
       </i>
       ("to begin"; e.g.,
       <i>
        emp
        <b>
         ie
        </b>
        zo
       </i>
       ["I begin"]),
       <i>
        v
        <b>
         o
        </b>
        lver
       </i>
       ("to return"; e.g.,
       <i>
        v
        <b>
         ue
        </b>
        lvo
       </i>
       ["I return"]), and
       <i>
        ac
        <b>
         o
        </b>
        starse
       </i>
       ("to go to bed"; e.g.,
       <i>
        me ac
        <b>
         ue
        </b>
        sto
       </i>
       ["I go to bed"]).
      </p>
      <p>
       Virtually all verbs of the third conjugation (
       <i>
        -ir
       </i>
       ), if they have
       <i>
        -e-
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -o-
       </i>
       in their stem, undergo a vowel-raising change whereby
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       changes to
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       changes to
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       , in some of their forms (for details, see
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
        Spanish irregular verbs
       </a>
       ). Examples include
       <i>
        p
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        dir
       </i>
       ("to ask for"; e.g.,
       <i>
        p
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        de
       </i>
       ["he/she asks for"]),
       <i>
        comp
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        tir
       </i>
       ("to compete"; e.g.,
       <i>
        comp
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        te
       </i>
       ["he/she competes"]), and
       <i>
        derr
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        tirse
       </i>
       ("to melt"; e.g.,
       <i>
        se derr
        <b>
         i
        </b>
        te
       </i>
       ["it melts"]).
      </p>
      <p>
       The so-called I-go verbs add a medial
       <i>
        -g-
       </i>
       in the first-person singular present tense (making the
       <i>
        Yo
       </i>
       ["I"] form end in
       <i>
        -go
       </i>
       ; e.g.,
       <i>
        tener
       </i>
       ["to have"] becomes
       <i>
        tengo
       </i>
       ["I have"];
       <i>
        venir
       </i>
       ["to come"] becomes
       <i>
        vengo
       </i>
       ["I come"]). These verbs are often irregular in other forms as well.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: Usage">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_simple_and_continuous_forms">
        Contrasting simple and continuous forms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=55" title="Edit section: Contrasting simple and continuous forms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is no strict distinction between simple and continuous forms in Spanish as there is in English. In English, "I do" is one thing (a habit) and "I am doing" is another (current activity). In Spanish,
       <i>
        hago
       </i>
       can be either of the two, and
       <i>
        estoy haciendo
       </i>
       stresses the latter. Although not as strict as English, Spanish is stricter than French or German, which have no systematic distinction between the two concepts at all. This optionally continuous meaning that can be underlined by using the continuous form is a feature of the present and imperfect. The preterite never has this meaning, even in the continuous form, and the future has it only when it is in the continuous form.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Present
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué haces?
        </i>
        could be either "What do you do?" or "What are you doing?"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué estás haciendo?
        </i>
        is only "What are you doing?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Imperfect
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué hacías?
        </i>
        could be either "What did you use to do?" or "What were you doing?"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué estabas haciendo?
        </i>
        is only "What were you doing?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Preterite
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué hiciste?
        </i>
        is "What did you do?"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué estuviste haciendo?
        </i>
        is "What were you doing (all of that time)?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that since the preterite by nature refers to an event seen as having a beginning and an end, and not as a context, the use of the continuous form of the verb only adds a feeling for the length of time spent on the action. The future has two main forms in Spanish, the imperfect (compound) future and the simple one. The difference between them is one of aspect. The compound future is done with the conjugated
       <i>
        ir
       </i>
       (which means "to go," but may also mean "will" in this case) plus the infinitive and, sometimes, with a present progressive verb added as well.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Future
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué vas a hacer?
        </i>
        is "What are you going to do?" (implies that it will be done again, as in a routine)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué vas a estar haciendo?
        </i>
        is "What are you going to be doing?" (does not necessarily imply that it will be done)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué harás?
        </i>
        is "What will you do?" (will be completed immediately, or done just once)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué estarás haciendo?
        </i>
        is "What will you be doing?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_present_and_the_future">
        Contrasting the present and the future
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=56" title="Edit section: Contrasting the present and the future">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Both the present and the future can express future actions, the latter more explicitly so. There are also expressions that convey the future.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi padre llega mañana
        </i>
        = "My father arrives tomorrow" (out of context,
        <i>
         llega
        </i>
        could mean both "he is arriving now" or "he usually arrives")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi padre estará llegando mañana
        </i>
        = "My father will be arriving tomorrow"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi padre va a llegar mañana
        </i>
        = "My father is going to arrive tomorrow" (future with
        <i>
         ir
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi padre llegará mañana
        </i>
        = "My father will arrive tomorrow" (future tense)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi padre está a punto de llegar
        </i>
        = "My father is about to arrive" (immediate future with
        <i>
         estar a punto
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The future tense can also simply express guesses about the present and immediate future:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué hora es?
        </i>
        <i>
         Serán las tres
        </i>
        = "What time is it?" "It is about three (but I have not checked)"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Quién llama a la puerta? Será José
        </i>
        = "Who is at the door? It must be José"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The same is applied to imperfect and conditional:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Qué hora era?
        </i>
        <i>
         Serían las tres
        </i>
        = "What time was it?" "It was about three (but I had not checked)"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Quién llamaba a la puerta? Sería José
        </i>
        = "Who was at the door? It must have been José"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Studies have shown that Spanish-speaking children learn this use of the future tense before they learn to use it to express future events
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       (the English future with "will" can also sometimes be used with this meaning). The other constructions detailed above are used instead. Indeed, in some areas, such as Argentina and Uruguay, speakers hardly use the future tense to refer to the future.
      </p>
      <p>
       The future tense of the subjunctive mood is also obsolete in practice. As of today, it is only found in legal documents and the like. In other contexts, the present subjunctive form always replaces it.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_preterite_and_the_imperfect">
        Contrasting the preterite and the imperfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: Contrasting the preterite and the imperfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fundamental_meanings_of_the_preterite_and_the_imperfect">
        Fundamental meanings of the preterite and the imperfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=58" title="Edit section: Fundamental meanings of the preterite and the imperfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Spanish has two fundamental past tenses, the preterite and the imperfect. Strictly speaking, the difference between them is one of not
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
        tense
       </a>
       but
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        aspect
       </a>
       , in a manner that is similar to that of the Slavic languages. However, within Spanish grammar, they are customarily called tenses.
      </p>
      <p>
       The difference between the preterite and the imperfect (and in certain cases, the perfect) is often hard to grasp for English speakers. English has just one past-tense form, which can have aspect added to it by auxiliary verbs, but not in ways that reliably correspond to what occurs in Spanish. The distinction between them does, however, correspond rather well to the distinctions in other Romance languages, such as between the French
       <i>
        imparfait
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        passé simple
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        passé composé
       </i>
       or between the Italian
       <i>
        imperfetto
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        passato remoto
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        passato prossimo
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The imperfect fundamentally presents an action or state as being a
       <i>
        context
       </i>
       and is thus essentially descriptive. It does not present actions or states as having ends and often does not present their beginnings either. Like the Slavic imperfective past, it tends to show actions that used to be done at some point, as in a routine. In this case, one would say
       <i>
        Yo jugaba
       </i>
       ("I used to play"),
       <i>
        Yo leía
       </i>
       ("I used to read"), or
       <i>
        Yo escribía
       </i>
       ("I used to write").
      </p>
      <p>
       The preterite (as well as the perfect, when applicable) fundamentally presents an action or state as being an
       <i>
        event
       </i>
       , and is thus essentially narrative. It presents actions or states as having beginnings and ends. This also bears resemblance to the Slavic perfective past, as these actions are usually viewed as done in one stroke. The corresponding preterite forms would be
       <i>
        Yo jugué
       </i>
       ("I played"),
       <i>
        Yo leí
       </i>
       ("I read") or
       <i>
        Yo escribí
       </i>
       ("I wrote").
      </p>
      <p>
       As stated above, deciding whether to use the preterite or the imperfect can present some difficulty for English speakers. But there are certain topics, words, and key phrases that can help one decide if the verb should be conjugated in the preterite or the imperfect. These expressions co-occur significantly more often with one or the other of the two tenses, corresponding to a completed action (preterite) or a repetitive action or a continuous action or state (imperfect) in the past.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparison_with_English_usage">
        Comparison with English usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=59" title="Edit section: Comparison with English usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The English simple past can express either of these concepts. However, there are devices that allow us to be more specific. Consider, for example, the phrase "the sun shone" in the following contexts:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        "The sun shone through his window; John knew that it was going to be a fine day."
       </li>
       <li>
        "The sun was shining through his window; John knew that it was going to be a fine day."
       </li>
       <li>
        "The sun shone through his window back in those days."
       </li>
       <li>
        "The sun used to shine through his window back in those days."
       </li>
       <li>
        "The sun shone through his window the moment that John pulled back the curtain."
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       In the first two, it is clear that the shining refers to the background to the events that are about to unfold in the story. It is talking about what
       <i>
        was happening
       </i>
       . One has a choice between making this explicit with the past continuous, as in (2), or using the simple past and allowing the context to make it clear what is intended, as in (1). In Spanish, these would be in the imperfect, optionally in the imperfect continuous.
      </p>
      <p>
       In (3) and (4), it is clear that the shining refers to a regular, general, habitual event. It is talking about what
       <i>
        used to happen
       </i>
       . One has a choice between making this explicit with the expression "used to," as in (4), or using the simple past and allowing the context to make it clear what we mean, as in (3). In Spanish, these would be in the imperfect, optionally with the auxiliary verb
       <i>
        soler
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In (5), only the simple past is possible. It is talking about a single event presented as occurring at a specific point in time (the moment John pulled back the curtain). The action starts and ends with this sentence. In Spanish, this would be in the preterite (or alternatively in the perfect, if the event has only just happened).
      </p>

         <b>

        </b>

      </p>

      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>

         <b>
          tenía
         </b>

         <b>
          cruzaba
         </b>

        <b>
         was
        </b>
        always/always
        <b>
         used to be
        </b>
        careful when I
        <b>
         crossed
        </b>
        /
        <b>
         used to cross
        </b>
        the road"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The imperfect is used for both verbs since they refer to habits in the past. Either verb could optionally use the expression "used to" in English.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me bañé
        </i>
        = "I took a bath"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The preterite is used if this refers to a single action or event—that is, the person took a bath last night.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me bañaba
        </i>
        = "I took baths"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The imperfect is used if this refers to any sort of habitual action—that is, the person took a bath every morning. Optionally,
       <i>
        solía bañarme
       </i>
       can specifically express "I used to take baths".
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tuvo una hija
        </i>
        = "She had a daughter"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The preterite is used if this refers to an event—here, a birth.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tenía una hija
        </i>
        = "She had a/one daughter"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The imperfect is used if this refers to the number of children by a certain point, as in "She had one daughter when I met her ten years ago; she may have more now". A description.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that when describing the life of someone who is now dead, the distinction between the two tenses blurs. One might describe the person's life saying
       <i>
        tenía una hija
       </i>
       , but
       <i>
        tuvo una hija
       </i>
       is very common because the person's whole life is viewed as a whole, with a beginning and an end. The same goes for
       <i>
        vivía/vivió en...
       </i>
       "he lived in...".
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps the verb that English speakers find most difficult to translate properly is "to be" in the past tense ("was"). Apart from the choice between the verbs
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       (see below), it is often very hard for English speakers to distinguish between contextual and narrative uses.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Alguien
         <b>
          cogió
         </b>
         mis CD. ¿Quién
         <b>
          fue
         </b>
         ?
        </i>
        = "Someone
        <b>
         took
        </b>
        my CDs. Who
        <b>
         was
        </b>
        it?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Here the preterite is used because it is an event. A good clue is the tense in which
       <i>
        cogió
       </i>
       is.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          Había
         </b>
         una persona que
         <b>
          miraba
         </b>
         los CD. ¿Quién
         <b>
          era
         </b>
         ?
        </i>
        = "There
        <b>
         was
        </b>
        a person who
        <b>
         was looking
        </b>
        at the CDs. Who
        <b>
         was
        </b>
        it?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Here the imperfect is used because it is a description (the start and end of the action is not presented; it is something that was in progress at a certain time). Again, a good clue is the tense of the other verbs.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_preterite_and_the_perfect">
        Contrasting the preterite and the perfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=61" title="Edit section: Contrasting the preterite and the perfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The preterite and the perfect are distinguished in a similar way as the equivalent English tenses. Generally, whenever the present perfect ("I have done") is used in English, the perfect is also used in Spanish. In addition, there are cases in which English uses a simple past ("I did") but Spanish requires a perfect. In the remaining cases, both languages use a simple past.
      </p>
      <p>
       As in English, the perfect expresses past actions that have some link to the present. The preterite expresses past actions as being past, complete and done with. In both languages, there are dialectal variations.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Frame_of_reference_includes_the_present:_perfect">
        Frame of reference includes the present: perfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=62" title="Edit section: Frame of reference includes the present: perfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       If it is implicitly or explicitly communicated that the frame of reference for the event includes the present and the event or events may therefore continue occurring, then both languages strongly prefer the perfect.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        With references including "this" including the present
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este año me he ido de vacaciones dos veces
        </i>
        = "This year I have gone on vacation twice"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Esta semana ha sido muy interesante
        </i>
        = "This week has been very interesting"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        With other references to recent periods including the present
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         No he hecho mucho hoy
        </i>
        = "I have not done much today"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No ha pasado nada hasta la fecha
        </i>
        = "Nothing has happened to date"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hasta ahora no se me ha ocurrido
        </i>
        = "Until now it has not occurred to me"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        With reference to someone's life experience (his/her life not being over)
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Alguna vez has estado en África?
        </i>
        = "Have you ever been in Africa?"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi vida no ha sido muy interesante
        </i>
        = "My life has not been very interesting"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Jamás he robado nada
        </i>
        = "Never have I stolen anything"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Frame_of_reference_superficially_includes_the_present:_perfect">
        Frame of reference superficially includes the present: perfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=63" title="Edit section: Frame of reference superficially includes the present: perfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Sometimes expressions like "today", "this year", and "this week" are used to express the idea these periods are over. This requires the simple past in English. For example, in December one might speak of the year in the simple past because we are assuming that all of that year's important events have occurred and one can talk as though it were over. Other expressions—such as "this weekend," if today is Monday—refer to a period which is definitely over; the word "this" just distinguishes it from other weekends. There is a tendency in Spanish to use the perfect even for this type of time reference, even though the preterite is possible and seems more logical.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este fin de semana hemos ido al zoo
        </i>
        = "This weekend we went to the zoo"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hoy he tenido una jornada muy aburrida
        </i>
        = "Today I had a very boring day at work"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consequences_continue_into_the_present:_perfect">
        Consequences continue into the present: perfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=64" title="Edit section: Consequences continue into the present: perfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       As in English, the perfect is used when the consequences of which an event are referred.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Alguien ha roto esta ventana
        </i>
        = "Someone has broken this window" (the window is currently in a broken state)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nadie me ha dicho qué pasó aquel día
        </i>
        = "Nobody has told me what happened that day" (therefore, I still do not know)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These same sentences in the preterite would purely refer to the past actions, without any implication that they have repercussions now.
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, this type of perfect is not possible if a precise time frame is added or even implied. One cannot say "I have been born in 1978," because the date requires "I was born," despite the fact there is arguably a present consequence in the fact that the person is still alive. Spanish sporadically uses the perfect in these cases.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         He nacido en 1978
        </i>
        (usually
        <i>
         Nací en 1978
        </i>
        ) = "I was born in 1978"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me he criado en Madrid
        </i>
        (usually
        <i>
         Me crié en Madrid
        </i>
        ) = "I grew up in Madrid"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_event_itself_continues_into_the_present:_perfect_or_present">
        The event itself continues into the present: perfect or present
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=65" title="Edit section: The event itself continues into the present: perfect or present">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       If the event itself has been happening recently and is also happening right now or expected to continue happening soon, then the preterite is impossible in both languages. English requires the perfect, or better yet the perfect continuous. Spanish requires the perfect, or better yet the present simple:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Últimamente ha llovido mucho
        </i>
        /
        <i>
         Últimamente llueve mucho
        </i>
        = "It has rained / It has been raining a lot recently"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This is the only use of the perfect that is common in colloquial speech across Latin America.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialectal_variation">
        Dialectal variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=66" title="Edit section: Dialectal variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the Canary Islands and across Latin America, there is a colloquial tendency to replace most uses of the perfect with the preterite. This use varies according to region, register, and education.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Y vos alguna vez estuviste allá?
        </i>
        =
        <i>
         ¿Y tú alguna vez has estado allí?
        </i>
        = "And have you ever been there?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The one use of the perfect that does seem to be normal in Latin America is the perfect for actions that continue into the present (not just the time frame, but the action itself). Therefore, "I have read a lot in my life" and "I read a lot this morning" would both be expressed with
       <i>
        leí
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        he leído
       </i>
       , but "I have been reading" is expressed by
       <i>
        he leído
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A less standard use of the perfect is found in Ecuador and Colombia. It is used with present or occasionally even future meaning. For example,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakira_Mebarak" title="Shakira Mebarak">
        Shakira Mebarak
       </a>
       in her song "Ciega, Sordomuda" sings,
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Bruta, ciega, sordomuda, / torpe, traste, testaruda; / es todo lo que he sido
        </i>
        = "Clumsy, blind, dumb, / blundering, useless, pig-headed; / that is all that I had been"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_subjunctive_and_the_imperative">
        Contrasting the subjunctive and the imperative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=67" title="Edit section: Contrasting the subjunctive and the imperative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The subjunctive mood expresses wishes and hypothetical events. It is often employed together with a conditional verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Desearía que estuvieses aquí.
        </i>
        = "I wish that you were here."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me alegraría mucho si volvieras mañana.
        </i>
        = "I would be very glad if you came back tomorrow."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The imperative mood shows commands given to the hearer (the second person). There is no imperative form in the third person, so the subjunctive is used. The expression takes the form of a command or wish directed at the hearer, but referring to the third person. The difference between a command and a wish is subtle, mostly conveyed by the absence of a wishing verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Que venga el gerente.
        </i>
        = "Let the manager come.", "Have the manager come."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Que se cierren las puertas.
        </i>
        = "Let the doors be closed.", "Have the doors closed."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       With a verb that expresses wishing, the above sentences become plain subjunctive instead of direct commands:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Deseo que venga el gerente.
        </i>
        = "I wish for the manager to come."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Quiero que se cierren las puertas.
        </i>
        = "I want the doors (to be) closed."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_present_and_the_future_subjunctive">
        Contrasting the present and the future subjunctive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=68" title="Edit section: Contrasting the present and the future subjunctive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The future tense of the subjunctive is found mostly in old literature or
       <a href="/wiki/Legal_English" title="Legal English">
        legal language
       </a>
       and is even misused in conversation by confusing it with the past tense (often due to the similarity of its characteristic suffix,
       <i>
        -ere
       </i>
       , as opposed to the suffixes of the past tense,
       <i>
        -era
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        -ese
       </i>
       ). It is very rare otherwise.
      </p>
      <p>
       It survives in the common expression
       <i>
        sea lo que fuere
       </i>
       and the proverb
       <i>
        allá donde fueres, haz lo que vieres
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        allá donde
       </i>
       can be replaced by
       <i>
        a la tierra donde
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        si a Roma
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The proverb illustrates how it used to be used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        With
        <i>
         si
        </i>
        referring to the future, as in
        <i>
         si a Roma fueres...
        </i>
        . This is now expressed with the present indicative:
        <i>
         si vas a Roma...
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         si fueras a Roma...
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        With
        <i>
         cuando
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         donde
        </i>
        , and the like, referring to the future, as in
        <i>
         allá donde fueres...
        </i>
        . This is now expressed with the present subjunctive:
        <i>
         vayas adonde vayas...
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_the_preterite_and_the_past_anterior">
        Contrasting the preterite and the past anterior
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=69" title="Edit section: Contrasting the preterite and the past anterior">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The past anterior is rare nowadays and restricted to formal use. It expresses a very fine nuance: the fact that an action occurs just after another (had) occurred, with words such as
       <i>
        cuando
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        nada más
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        en cuanto
       </i>
       ("when", "no sooner", "as soon as"). In English, one must use either the simple past or the past perfect; Spanish has something specific between the two.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         En cuanto el delincuente hubo salido del cuarto, la víctima se echó a llorar
        </i>
        = "As soon as the criminal (had) left the room, the victim burst into tears"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The use of
       <i>
        hubo salido
       </i>
       shows that the second action happened immediately after the first.
       <i>
        Salió
       </i>
       might imply that it happened at the same time, and
       <i>
        había salido
       </i>
       might imply it happened some time after.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, colloquial Spanish has lost this tense and the corresponding nuance, and the preterite must be used instead in all but the most formal of writing.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_ser_and_estar">
        Contrasting
        <i>
         ser
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         estar
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=70" title="Edit section: Contrasting ser and estar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_copula" title="Romance copula">
        Romance copula
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The differences between
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       are considered one of the most difficult concepts for non-native speakers. Both
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       translate into
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       as "to be", but they have different uses, depending on whether they are used with nouns, with adjectives, with past
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Participles" title="Participles">
        participles
       </a>
       (more precisely,
       <i>
        passive
       </i>
       participles), or to express location.
      </p>
      <p>
       Only
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       is used to equate one
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_phrase" title="Noun phrase">
        noun phrase
       </a>
       with another, and thus it is the verb for expressing a person's occupation ("Mi hermano es estudiante"/"My brother is a student"). For the same reason,
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       is used for telling the date or the time, regardless of whether the subject is explicit ("Hoy es miércoles"/"Today is Wednesday") or merely implied ("Son las ocho"/"It's eight o'clock").
      </p>
      <p>
       When these verbs are used with adjectives, the difference between them may be generalized by saying that
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       expresses nature and
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       expresses state. Frequently—although not always—adjectives used with
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       express a permanent quality, while their use with
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       expresses a temporary situation. There are exceptions to the generalization; for example, the sentence "Tu mamá está loca" ("Your mother is crazy") can express either a temporary or a permanent state of craziness.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Ser
       </i>
       generally focuses on the essence of the subject, and specifically on qualities that include:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Nationality
       </li>
       <li>
        Possession
       </li>
       <li>
        Physical and personality traits
       </li>
       <li>
        Material
       </li>
       <li>
        Origin
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       <i>
        Estar
       </i>
       generally focuses on the condition of the subject, and specifically on qualities that include:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Physical condition
       </li>
       <li>
        Feelings, emotions, and states of mind
       </li>
       <li>
        Appearance
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       In English, the sentence "The boy is boring" uses a different adjective than "The boy is bored". In Spanish, the difference is made by the choice of
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         El chico es aburrido
        </i>
        uses
        <i>
         ser
        </i>
        to express a permanent trait ("The boy is boring").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         El chico está aburrido
        </i>
        uses
        <i>
         estar
        </i>
        to express a temporary state of mind ("The boy is bored").
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The same strategy is used with many adjectives to express either an inherent trait (
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       ) or a transitory state or condition (
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       ). For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "María es guapa" uses
        <i>
         ser
        </i>
        to express an essential trait, meaning "María is a good-looking person."
       </li>
       <li>
        "María está guapa" uses
        <i>
         estar
        </i>
        to express a momentary impression: "María looks beautiful" (a comment on her present appearance, without any implication about her inherent characteristics).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       is used with the past participle of a verb, it forms the "true"
       <a href="/wiki/Passive_voice" title="Passive voice">
        passive voice
       </a>
       , expressing an event ("El libro
       <b>
        fue escrito
       </b>
       en 2005"/"The book
       <b>
        was written
       </b>
       in 2005"). When the past participle appears with
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       , it forms a "passive of result" or "
       <a href="/wiki/Stative_verb" title="Stative verb">
        stative
       </a>
       passive" ("El libro ya
       <b>
        está escrito
       </b>
       "/"The book
       <b>
        is
       </b>
       already
       <b>
        written
       </b>
       "—see
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conjugation" title="Spanish conjugation">
        Spanish conjugation
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Location of a person or thing is expressed with
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       —regardless of whether temporary or permanent ("El hotel
       <b>
        está
       </b>
       en la esquina"/"The hotel is on the corner"). Location of an event is expressed with
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       ("La reunión
       <b>
        es
       </b>
       en el hotel"/"The meeting is [takes place] in the hotel").
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contrasting_haber_and_tener">
        Contrasting
        <i>
         haber
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tener
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=71" title="Edit section: Contrasting haber and tener">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The verbs
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tener
       </i>
       are easily distinguished, but they may pose a problem for learners of Spanish who speak other Romance languages (where the cognates of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tener
       </i>
       are used differently), for English speakers (where "have" is used as a verb and as an auxiliary), and others.
       <i>
        Haber
       </i>
       derives from Latin
       <i>
        habeō
       </i>
       ,; with the basic meaning of "to have".
       <i>
        Tener
       </i>
       derives from Latin
       <i>
        teneō
       </i>
       , with the basic meaning of "to hold", "to keep". As
       <i>
        habeō
       </i>
       began to degrade and become reduced to just ambiguous monosyllables in the present tense, the Iberian Romance languages (Spanish, Gallician-Portuguese, and Catalan) restricted its use and started to use
       <i>
        teneō
       </i>
       as the ordinary verb expressing having and possession. French instead reinforced
       <i>
        habeō
       </i>
       with obligatory subject pronouns.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Haber:_expressing_existence">
        <i>
         Haber
        </i>
        : expressing existence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=72" title="Edit section: Haber: expressing existence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Haber
       </i>
       is used as an impersonal verb to show existence of an object or objects, which is generally expressed as an indefinite noun phrase. In English, this corresponds to the use of "there" + the corresponding inflected form of "to be". When used in this sense,
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       has a special present-tense form:
       <i>
        hay
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        ha
       </i>
       . The
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       is a fossilized form of the mediaeval Castilian pronoun
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        i
       </i>
       , meaning "there", which is cognate with French
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       and Catalan
       <i>
        hi
       </i>
       , and comes from Latin
       <i>
        ibi
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike in English, the thing that "is there" is not the subject of the sentence, and therefore there is no agreement between it and the verb. This echoes constructions seen in languages such as French (
       <i>
        il y a
       </i>
       = "it there has"), Catalan (
       <i>
        hi ha
       </i>
       = "[it] there has"), and even Chinese (有
       <i>
        yǒu
       </i>
       = "[it] has"):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hay un gato en el jardín.
        </i>
        = "There is a cat in the garden."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         En el baúl hay fotografías viejas.
        </i>
        = "In the trunk there are old photographs."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       To form perfect constructions, the past participle
       <i>
        habido
       </i>
       is used:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ha habido mucha confusión de esto.
        </i>
        = "There's been a lot of confusion about this."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ha habido pocos hasta ahora.
        </i>
        = "There've been a few so far."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It is possible, in certain types of emphasis, to put the verb after the object:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Revistas hay?
        </i>
        = "Are there any magazines?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There is a tendency to make
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       agree with what follows, as though it were the subject, particularly in tenses other than the present indicative. There is heavier stigma on inventing plural forms for
       <i>
        hay
       </i>
       , but
       <i>
        hain
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        han
       </i>
       , and suchlike are sometimes encountered in non-standard speech. The form
       <i>
        habemos
       </i>
       is common (meaning "there are, including me"); it very rarely replaces
       <i>
        hemos
       </i>
       to form the present perfect tense in modern language,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in certain contexts it is even acceptable in formal or literary language:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Había un hombre en la casa.
        </i>
        = "There was a man in the house."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Había unos hombres en la casa.
        </i>
        = "There were some men in the house." (standard)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Habían unos hombres en la casa.
        </i>
        = "There were some men in the house." (non-standard)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         En esta casa habemos cinco personas.
        </i>
        = "In this house there are five of us." (non-standard)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Habría habido muchos más si supiera.
        </i>
        = "There would have been a lot more if I knew." (standard)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nos las habemos con un gran jugador.
        </i>
        = "We are confronting a great player." (standard)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <i>
        Haber
       </i>
       as an existence verb is never used other than in the third person. To express existence of a first or second person, the verb
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       ("to be [located/present]") or
       <i>
        existir
       </i>
       ("to exist") is used, and there is subject–verb agreement.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Haber:_impersonal_obligation">
        <i>
         Haber
        </i>
        : impersonal obligation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=73" title="Edit section: Haber: impersonal obligation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The phrase
       <i>
        haber que
       </i>
       (in the third person singular and followed by a subordinated construction with the verb in the infinitive) carries the meaning of necessity or obligation without specifying an agent. It is translatable as "it is necessary", but a paraphrase is generally preferable in translation. Note that the present-tense form is
       <i>
        hay
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hay que abrir esa puerta.
        </i>
        = "That door needs opening", "We have to open that door".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Habrá que abrir esa puerta.
        </i>
        = "That door will need opening", "We are going to have to open that door".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Aunque haya que abrir esa puerta.
        </i>
        = "Even if that door needs to be opened".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This construction is comparable to French
       <i>
        il faut
       </i>
       and Catalan
       <i>
        cal
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Hay que
       </i>
       always goes with the infinitive.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Haber:_personal_obligation">
        <i>
         Haber
        </i>
        : personal obligation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=74" title="Edit section: Haber: personal obligation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A separate construction is
       <i>
        haber de
       </i>
       + infinitive. It is not impersonal. It tends to express a certain nuance of obligation and a certain nuance of future tense, much like the expression "to be to". It is also often used similarly to
       <i>
        tener que
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        deber
       </i>
       ("must", "ought to"). Note that the third personal singular of the present tense is
       <i>
        ha
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mañana he de dar una charla ante la Universidad
        </i>
        = "Tomorrow I am to give a speech before the University".
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ha de comer más verduras
        </i>
        = "She/he ought to eat more vegetables".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Haber:_forming_the_perfect">
        <i>
         Haber
        </i>
        : forming the perfect
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=75" title="Edit section: Haber: forming the perfect">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Haber
       </i>
       is also used as an auxiliary to form the perfect, as shown elsewhere. Spanish uses only
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       for this, unlike French and Italian, which use the corresponding cognates of
       <i>
        haber
       </i>
       for most verbs, but cognates of
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       ("to be") for certain others.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ella se ha ido al mercado
        </i>
        = "She has gone to the market"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellas se han ido de paseo
        </i>
        = "They have gone on a walk"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Habéis fregado los platos?
        </i>
        = "Have you (all) done the washing-up?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tener">
        <i>
         Tener
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=76" title="Edit section: Tener">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Tener
       </i>
       is a verb with the basic meaning of "to have", in its essential sense of "to possess", "to hold", "to own". As in English, it can also express obligation (
       <i>
        tener que
       </i>
       + infinitive). It also appears in a number of phrases that show emotion or physical states, expressed by nouns, which in English tend to be expressed by "to be" and an adjective.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi hijo tiene una casa nueva
        </i>
        = "My son has a new house"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tenemos que hablar
        </i>
        = "We have to talk"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tengo hambre
        </i>
        = "I am hungry" (lit. "I have hunger")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There are numerous phrases like
       <i>
        tener hambre
       </i>
       that are not literally translated in English, such as:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-abouttenernouns_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-abouttenernouns-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener hambre
        </i>
        = "to be hungry"; "to have hunger"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener sed
        </i>
        = "to be thirsty"; "to have thirst"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener cuidado
        </i>
        = "to be careful"; "to have caution"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener __ años
        </i>
        = "to be __ years old"; "to have __ years"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener celos
        </i>
        = "to be jealous"; "to have jealousy"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener éxito
        </i>
        = "to be successful"; "to have success"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tener vergüenza
        </i>
        = "to be ashamed"; "to have shame"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note:
       <i>
        Estar hambriento
       </i>
       is a literal translation of "To be hungry", but it is rarely used in Spanish nowadays.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">
        Negation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=77" title="Edit section: Negation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Verbs are negated by putting
       <i>
        no
       </i>
       before the verb. Other negative words can either replace this
       <i>
        no
       </i>
       or occur after the verb:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hablo español
        </i>
        = "I speak Spanish"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No hablo español
        </i>
        = "I do not speak Spanish"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Nunca hablo español
        </i>
        = "I never speak Spanish"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         No hablo nunca español
        </i>
        = "I do not ever speak Spanish"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Expressing_movement">
        Expressing movement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=78" title="Edit section: Expressing movement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish verbs describing motion tend to emphasize direction instead of manner of motion. According to the pertinent classification, this makes Spanish a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb_framing" title="Verb framing">
        verb-framed
       </a>
       language. This contrasts with English, where verbs tend to emphasize manner, and the direction of motion is left to helper particles,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverbs
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "We drove away" =
        <i>
         Nos fuimos en coche
        </i>
        (lit. "We went (away) by car")
       </li>
       <li>
        "He swam to Ibiza" =
        <i>
         Fue a Ibiza nadando
        </i>
        (lit. "He went to Ibiza swimming")
       </li>
       <li>
        "They ran off" =
        <i>
         Huyeron corriendo
        </i>
        (lit. "They fled running")
       </li>
       <li>
        "She crawled in" =
        <i>
         Entró a gatas
        </i>
        (lit. "She entered on all fours")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Quite often, the important thing is the direction, not the manner. Therefore, although "we drove away" translates into Spanish as
       <i>
        nos fuimos en coche
       </i>
       , it is often better to translate it as just
       <i>
        nos fuimos
       </i>
       . For example:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        <i>
         La llevé al aeropuerto en coche, pero se le había olvidado el tiquete, así que fuimos a casa [en coche] por él, luego volvimos [en coche] hacia el aeropuerto, pero luego tuvimos que volver [en coche] por el pasaporte, y ya era imposible que consiguiésemos facturar el equipaje...
        </i>
        = "I drove her to the airport, but she had forgotten her ticket, so we drove home to get it, then drove back towards the airport, but then had to drive back home for her passport, by which time there was zero chance of checking in..."
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_nouns">
        Verbal nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_verbs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=79" title="Edit section: Verbal nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish verbal nouns (e.g. "running", "coming", "thinking" in English) are identical in form to the infinitive of the verb from which they are derived, and their gender is masculine. They are generally used with the definite article, and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns#Clitic_pronouns_and_enclitization" title="Spanish pronouns">
        enclitic pronouns
       </a>
       attach to them as they would a normal infinitive. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         el comer
        </i>
        = "eating"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         el correr
        </i>
        = "running"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         el no esperar
        </i>
        = "not waiting"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         el darte un libro
        </i>
        = "giving you a book"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         el guardártelos
        </i>
        = "saving them for you"
       </li>
      </ul>
