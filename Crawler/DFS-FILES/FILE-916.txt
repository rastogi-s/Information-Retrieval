      <p>
       <b>
        Auvergnat
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Auvergnat language
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (endonym
       <i>
        auvernhat
       </i>
       ) is an idiom spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       in part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Massif_Central" title="Massif Central">
        Massif Central
       </a>
       and in particular, in most of
       <a href="/wiki/Auvergne" title="Auvergne">
        Auvergne
       </a>
       , province that gives it its name.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since there are no universally accepted criteria for distinguishing languages ​​from dialects, there is no real consensus on the structural nature of Auvergnat. It is generally classified as a dialect of oc but some linguists and academics consider it's a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       in its own right.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With around 80,000 speakers in the Auvergne region at the beginning of the 21st century, the language appears to be seriously threatened.
      </p>

      </div>
      <p>
       The effective borders of Auvergnat do not completely coincide with those of the current Region of Auvergne or the historical region of Auvergne but can be described as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The entire
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9partement" title="Département">
         département
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Puy-de-D%C3%B4me" title="Puy-de-Dôme">
         Puy-de-Dôme
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        a large section of the département of
        <a href="/wiki/Cantal" title="Cantal">
         Cantal
        </a>
        , largely centered on Saint-Flour and Mauriac. It is to be noted that Aurillac and the Pierrefort Canton speak a dialect called
        <i>
         carladézien
        </i>
        , a form of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languedocien" title="Languedocien">
         languedocien
        </a>
        , with Auvergnat conjugations and the rest largely similar to standard
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languedocien" title="Languedocien">
         languedocien
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The larger portion of the
        <a href="/wiki/Haute-Loire" title="Haute-Loire">
         Haute-Loire
        </a>
        aside Yssingeaux which speaks
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaro-Alpine" title="Vivaro-Alpine">
         Vivaro-Alpine
        </a>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        the southern half of
        <a href="/wiki/Allier" title="Allier">
         Allier
        </a>
        , or the Bourbonnais province, near
        <a href="/wiki/Montlu%C3%A7on" title="Montluçon">
         Montluçon
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Vichy" title="Vichy">
         Vichy
        </a>
        , with the northern half toward Moulins traditionally speaking French.
       </li>
       <li>
        A large fringe of eastern
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Limousin_(region)" title="Limousin (region)">
         Limousin
        </a>
        , in the département of
        <a href="/wiki/Corr%C3%A8ze" title="Corrèze">
         Corrèze
        </a>
        , near
        <a href="/wiki/Ussel,_Corr%C3%A8ze" title="Ussel, Corrèze">
         Ussel
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Bort-les-Orgues" title="Bort-les-Orgues">
         Bort-les-Orgues
        </a>
        . This is a transition zone between Auvergnat and Limousin
       </li>
       <li>
        The communities of Noirétable and La Chamba in the western side of the département of
        <a href="/wiki/Loire_(department)" title="Loire (department)">
         Loire
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="A_more_precise_view_on_Auvergnat_boundaries">
        A more precise view on Auvergnat boundaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: A more precise view on Auvergnat boundaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are strong oppositions between Pierre Bonnaud
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (for whom the Auvergnat is a language of its own, see the light orange line on the map – note it is including the easternmost part of the Marchois dialect) and for instance Roger Teulat.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Language_boundaries">
        Language boundaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Language boundaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        1: Latin pratus (meadow) &gt; pra (Occitan, Francoprovençal) vs. pré (French)
       </li>
       <li>
        2: Latin nepos (nephew) &gt; nebot (Occitan) vs. neveu… (French, Francoprovençal)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Light blue area labelled fr is for French-Langue d’Oïl. Light purple area labelled frp is for Francoprovençal.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialect_boundaries">
        Dialect boundaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dialect boundaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        0: No paroxyton in the north – defines the Marchois dialect, a name often given to the Occitan spoken in the Croissant area. The area labelled m is the one of the Marchois dialect.
       </li>
       <li>
        3: The ca/cha boundary separated Northern Occitan (Limousin, Auvergnat, Vivaro-Alpine, Marchois) and Southern Occitan (Gascon, Languedocien, Provençal). It is part of the Auvergnat boundary, along with 7, in Cantal. Area lm is for the Limousin dialect, va for Vivaro-Alpine, lg for Languedocien.
       </li>
       <li>
        4: Feminines in
        <i>
         –ada
        </i>
        have lost intervocalic
        <i>
         d
        </i>
        and became
        <i>
         –aa
        </i>
        : this separates Auvergnat and Vivaro-Alpine (almost the same boundary for first person indicative present in
        <i>
         –o
        </i>
        [u], another characteristic of Vivaro-Alpine).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_boundaries">
        Definition boundaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Definition boundaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       These are not characteristic of Auvergnat as a whole but allow for defining a boundary:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        5 and 6: These match the Core Auvergnat, according to Pierre Bonnaud. They may be used for the western boundary of Auvergnat. Area 5 has the pronunciation [jaR] of
        <i>
         èr
        </i>
        (around [ɛR]), area 6 has the palatalisation of 'cl
        <i>
         to [kʎ] or [çʎ].
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        7: Southeastern boundary has an opposition between
        <i>
         dreita
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         drecha
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        8: This is for the southern boundary: the plural of
        <i>
         braç
        </i>
        has the same pronunciation than the singular; only the article allows differentiation.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that most Occitanists use rather 7 than 8 to define the southern boundary.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_variation">
        Internal variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Internal variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Note some of the definition boundaries allow defining an internal variation. The most traditional one between Lower or Northern Auvergnat and Upper or Southern Auvergnat is the mutation of
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       before [k], [p], and [t] (line 9). Lower Auvergnat, defined by Teulat, is the light green area labelled 1 on the map. Upper Auvergnat, defined by Teulat, is the light brown-yellow area labelled 2 on the map. A broader area (light yellow) is generally defined. A Northwestern Auvergnat may be defined as well by 5 and 6. The Northeastern (East of 5 and 6, North of 9) has, according to Bonnaud, a stronger influence from French phonetics (a bit like Marchois).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subdialects">
        Subdialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Subdialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Auvergnat is most often categorized in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Northern_Occitan" title="Northern Occitan">
        Northern Occitan
       </a>
       dialect group, along with
       <a href="/wiki/Limousin_dialect" title="Limousin dialect">
        Limousin
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vivaro-Alpine" title="Vivaro-Alpine">
        Vivaro-Alpine
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       There are two primary distinctions in Auvergnat:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Northern Auvergnat (
        <i>
         nord-auvergnat
        </i>
        , also
        <i>
         bas-auvergnat
        </i>
        ) in Puy-de-Dôme and Allier (Bourbonnais) and Haute-Loire north of
        <a href="/wiki/Brioude" title="Brioude">
         Brioude
        </a>
        .
        <ul>
         <li>
          In the south of Allier (Bourbonnais), the local
          <i>
           nord-auvergnat
          </i>
          has been heavily influenced by French, but Auvergnat linguistic traits remain dominant. This transition zone to French, called locally
          <i>
           le Croissant
          </i>
          , includes the northern fringe of Limousin. See also the article on
          <a href="/wiki/Bourbonnais" title="Bourbonnais">
           Bourbonnais
          </a>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Southern Auvergnat (
        <i>
         sud-auvergnat
        </i>
        , also
        <i>
         haut-auvergnat
        </i>
        ) spoken in Cantal, Haute-Loire (with a part of Ardèche and most of Lozère).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The suggestion that Auvergnat is an independent language, distinct from Occitan, has found little resonance with linguists, especially Romance linguists. It is strongly defended by those who espouse the
       <i>
        norme bonnaudienne
       </i>
       a standardization of Auvergnat.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_Vitality_and_Usage">
        Linguistic Vitality and Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Auvergnat_(language)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Linguistic Vitality and Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An understanding of the vitality and overall usage of Auvergnat can be garnered from a survey carried out in 2006 in the Auvergne region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest group of the two languages spoken in the Auvergne region is referred to as
       <i>
        patois
       </i>
       (78% of the population) compared with other regional terms, with certain cultural identities emerging, such as
       <i>
        auvergnat
       </i>
       (10%),
       <i>
        occitan
       </i>
       (8%),
       <i>
        bourbonnais
       </i>
       (5%) or
       <i>
        langue d’oc
       </i>
       (4%).
      </p>
      <p>
       The regional language, whether Occitan (in the whole of the Auvergne region) or Oïl (the north of Allier), represents a strong presence in the region:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        61% claim to understand their regional language more-or-less, with 22% claiming to understand it easily or perfectly.
       </li>
       <li>
        42% claim to be able to speak it more-or-less, with 12% claiming it as easy.
       </li>
       <li>
        29% claim to read Auvergnat more-or-less, with 10% claiming it easy.
       </li>
       <li>
        17% claim to write Auvergnat more-or-less, with 4% declaring it easy.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A large part of the population that understands or speaks even a little or, moreover, fluently, neither know how to write nor read in that language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Language learning is found to be essential within the home, according to the survey, (grandparents noted as 61%, or other family members at 50%) with a very weak result from the schools (10%). Herein is found the problem of language-transmission when dependent upon State sponsorship. 40% of adults who did not teach their language to their children report regretting it at the time of the survey. This feeling is reported more strongly among the 35 or less demographic, at 58%. The desire to learn the local language is reported strongly, with increasing representation among the young, reported at 23%. According to the survey the desire to incorporate local language learning in schools is as follows: Haute-Loire (53%), Puy-de-Dôme (51%) et Cantal (74%). The desire to teach to their own children is strong (41%) and is stronger still with the 35 and under demographic (58%). 71% of the region's inhabitants are favorable to the idea of maintaining the regional language and culture, with a stronger result in the 35-and-unders (76%). To achieve this desire, different institutions are expected to play a role (in percentage of those surveyed):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        France 3 Auvergne, the local television channel should offer regional language programming (54%)
       </li>
       <li>
        The Region (54%), National Education (43%), the Culture Ministry (42%) and the communities are seen by most Auvergnats as the most correct venues to develop and pass on the Auvergnat language and culture.
       </li>
      </ul>
