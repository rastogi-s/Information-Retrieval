      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Llanero Spanish
       </b>
       is the set of linguistic modalities of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken in the region of Venezuelan-Colombian
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Llanos_(South_America)" title="Los Llanos (South America)">
        Los Llanos
       </a>
       . It is characterized by mixing elements of
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       more indigenous elements.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phonetics">
        Phonetics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Phonetics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Has
       <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
        yeísmo
       </a>
       and articulation of the "r" to its weakening (
       <i>
        vorqueta
       </i>
       by volqueta, a phenomenon seen in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arauca_(Colombia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arauca (Colombia) (page does not exist)">
        Arauca (Colombia)
       </a>
       ) or its disappearance in the infinitive (
       <i>
        ventiá, aserrá, ordeñá, cogé
       </i>
       ...)
      </p>
      <p>
       It also has the articulation of the "s" (implosive), the aspiration (
       <i>
        maíh
       </i>
       =
       <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
        maíz
       </a>
       ) or loss (
       <i>
        cataplama
       </i>
       =
       <a href="/wiki/Poultice" title="Poultice">
        cataplasma
       </a>
       ). Also appears the feature of the aspiration of the "s" prevocalic (
       <i>
        ji eñol, eso je li olvida
       </i>
       = sí señor, eso se le olvida).
      </p>
      <p>
       Its intervocalic fricatives (b-d-g) weaken or disappear in the llanero speech (
       <i>
        auacero
       </i>
       =
       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">
        aguacero
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       It preserves the sound of the old "h". It is a feature more seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arauca_(Colombia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arauca (Colombia) (page does not exist)">
        Arauca
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Casanare_Department" title="Casanare Department">
        Casanare
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        joyo, jumo, mojo, jallan, sajuma, ajoga, ajita
       </i>
       by hoyo, humo, moho, hallan, sahúma, ahoga, ahíta).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Suppresses or weakens the redundant -s of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plural" title="Plural">
        plural
       </a>
       , eg, ''
       <i>
        los antioqueño, loj
        <a href="/wiki/Dog" title="Dog">
         perro
        </a>
        , cuatronarice
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        cuatronarices
       </i>
       local species of
       <a href="/wiki/Snake" title="Snake">
        snake
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        loj
        <a href="/wiki/Godparent" title="Godparent">
         padrino
        </a>
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Has a similar nominal composition to costeño dialects, eg,
       <i>
        pativoltiao
       </i>
       (pata + volteado ie
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        noun
       </a>
       +
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Formation of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Past_composite&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Past composite (page does not exist)">
        past composite
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       with the verb “
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       ”, eg: “
       <i>
        Si no
        <b>
         fuera
        </b>
       </i>
       (hubiera)
       <i>
        sido por Guadalupe Salcedo
       </i>
       …”
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lexicon">
        Lexicon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Lexicon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some lexical forms of costeño origin registered in the region are: “
       <i>
        cautivar
       </i>
       ” (cultivate), “
       <i>
        concha
       </i>
       ” (shell or peel), “
       <a href="/wiki/Pollino" title="Pollino">
        pollino
       </a>
       ” (young donkey)," “
       <i>
        yerna
       </i>
       ” (daughter).
      </p>
      <p>
       It also has contributions from the Western
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombian
       </a>
       as “
       <i>
        hamero
       </i>
       ” (wrapper of
       <a href="/wiki/Corncob" title="Corncob">
        cob
       </a>
       ), “
       <i>
        choclo
       </i>
       ” (tender
       <a href="/wiki/Maize" title="Maize">
        maize
       </a>
       ) or “
       <i>
        rabipelao
       </i>
       ” (
       <a href="/wiki/Opossum" title="Opossum">
        opossum
       </a>
       ).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indigenous_Inheritance">
        Indigenous Inheritance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Indigenous Inheritance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Perhaps the most typical of Llanero Spanish it is in the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_language" title="Indigenous language">
        Indigenous
       </a>
       inheritance, in many indigenous terms are incorporated into this speech.
      </p>
      <p>
       Are sometimes the names of regionals
       <a href="/wiki/Plant" title="Plant">
        plants
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        cumare
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Astrocaryum_aculeatum" title="Astrocaryum aculeatum">
         Astrocaryum aculeatum
        </a>
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        moriche
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mauritia_flexuosa" title="Mauritia flexuosa">
         Mauritia flexuosa
        </a>
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        mapora
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Roystonea_oleracea" title="Roystonea oleracea">
         Roystonea oleracea
        </a>
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        suy, yaray,
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bototo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bototo (page does not exist)">
         bototo
        </a>
       </i>
       , etc.; among others, the names of objects of indigenous cultures adopted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        Creole
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        chiramo
       </i>
       (hanging utensil),
       <i>
        budare, mapire
       </i>
       (basket),
       <i>
        chirama
       </i>
       (basket) 'catumare
       <i>
        (palm vessel),
       </i>
       corota
       <i>
        (calabash vessel), etc .; or the indigenous foods adopted in the Creole cuisine as
       </i>
       majule
       <i>
        (porridge of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantain_(cooking)" title="Plantain (cooking)">
         plantain
        </a>
        ),
       </i>
       catibía
       <i>
        (dough of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manihot_esculenta" title="Manihot esculenta">
         cassava
        </a>
        striped), etc.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is superfluous to mention the abundant onomástica of indigenous origin: water names and place names like
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guatiqu%C3%ADa&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guatiquía (page does not exist)">
         Guatiquía
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Guayuriba, Guarca, Guaicaramo
       </i>
       any map or
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       text provides hundreds. Perhaps less known is the indigenous contribution to the
       <a href="/wiki/Anthroponymy" title="Anthroponymy">
        anthroponymy
       </a>
       that is seen in the not few last names of members of the Spanish-speaking communities,
       <i>
        Catimay, Cuburuco, Chaquea, Humejé, Tabaco, Tupanteve, Tumay, Achagua, Cuyaré, Chamarrabí, Chipiaje, Errenumá, Guacabare, Gaspaday, Guatumé, Itanare, Pirache, Renumá, Tarache, Yaguiduá, Yavimay, Yaya, Guanay,
       </i>
       etc.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Internal_development">
        Internal development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Llanero_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Internal development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Example of how the operation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       in the peculiar conditions of Los Llanos will produce more or less specific facts is the reorganization of certain lexical microsystems. Throughout the region of Casanare “
       <i>
        mirar
       </i>
       ” has advanced on the semantic field of “
       <i>
        ver
       </i>
       ” almost disappeared from ordinary speech to this
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       “
       <i>
        Entonces
        <b>
         miró
        </b>
        el presidente Rojas Pinilla que el Llano era una gran belleza
       </i>
       ”,
       <i>
        “¿Él no está por aquí? – No, no lo he
        <b>
         mirao
        </b>
        ”
       </i>
       ; “
       <i>
        Yo ya no
        <b>
         miro
        </b>
        pa’ trabajar esta cosa
       </i>
       ”; and the same phenomenon is starting to affect the couple “
       <i>
        oír – escuchar
       </i>
       ” in which the latter tends to absorb at first.
      </p>
      <p>
       Is also typical of Los Llanos the classification of the grocery grown
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Musacea&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Musacea (page does not exist)">
        musaceas
       </a>
       in three groups:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plantain_(cooking)" title="Plantain (cooking)">
        plantains
       </a>
       , bananas and topochos; the great importance in the life of the Llanero has this last variety makes form to it a special class.
      </p>
      <p>
       The indigenous influence also appears in an indirect and mediated way, not in aboriginal
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        languages
       </a>
       but because of characteristics specialties of coexistence of native and indigenous communities, characteristics that come to be very indicative of relationships among these communities, that is, between the silent struggle that continues to develop between them, phenomenas characterized by the concepts that the
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        Creole
       </a>
       has respect to indigenous: “
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/U%27wa_people" title="U'wa people">
         tunebo
        </a>
       </i>
       ” is "
       <i>
        ranger
       </i>
       ", “
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guajibo" title="Guajibo">
         guajibo
        </a>
       </i>
       ” to
       <i>
        shy or reclusive '"(
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Arauca_(Colombia)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Arauca (Colombia) (page does not exist)">
         Arauca
        </a>
        ); and in
        <a href="/wiki/Puerto_L%C3%B3pez" title="Puerto López">
         Puerto López
        </a>
        a saying that could well explain alone the struggles between the Llaneros and the Indians who caused commotion in the Colombian community was heard: "'Neither donkey is beast' 'or Indian is people'
       </i>
       , or cassava serves for provision
       <i>
        ”.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       And voices of traditional Hispanic roots only common in
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Llanos_(South_America)" title="Los Llanos (South America)">
        Los Llanos
       </a>
       or used it with a peculiar sense are, among others: “
       <i>
        el cerro
       </i>
       ” (the mountain range, the Andes), “
       <i>
        cachilapero
       </i>
       ” (the stealing cattle and disfigures its brands), "
       <i>
        cámara'
       </i>
       , “
       <i>
        camarita
       </i>
       ” (compañero, camarada), “
       <i>
        camazo
       </i>
       ” (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calabazo" title="Calabazo">
        calabazo
       </a>
       ), “
       <i>
        caramera
       </i>
       ” (cornamenta), 'guate' "(rural person), “
       <i>
        guafa
       </i>
       ” (guadua), “
       <i>
        magalla
       </i>
       ” (bag for the
       <a href="/wiki/Hammock" title="Hammock">
        hammock
       </a>
       ), “
       <i>
        pompo
       </i>
       ” (rough, clumsy), “
       <i>
        saquero
       </i>
       ” (cattle buyer), "
       <i>
        soropo
       </i>
       " "ensoropao" (palm leaf wall) etc, etc.
      </p>
