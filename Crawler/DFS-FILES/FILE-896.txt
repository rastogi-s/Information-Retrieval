      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portugal_in_the_period_of_discoveries" title="Portugal in the period of discoveries">
        Portuguese overseas exploration in the 15th and 16th centuries
       </a>
       led to the establishment of a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Empire" title="Portuguese Empire">
        Portuguese Empire
       </a>
       with trading posts, forts and colonies in Africa, Asia and the Americas. Contact between the Portuguese language and native languages gave rise to many Portuguese-based
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgins
       </a>
       , used as
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        linguas francas
       </a>
       throughout the Portuguese sphere of influence. In time, many of these pidgins were
       <a href="/wiki/Nativization" title="Nativization">
        nativized
       </a>
       , becoming new stable creole languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       As is the rule in most creoles, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       of these languages can be traced to the parent languages, usually with predominance of Portuguese; while the grammar is mostly original and unique to each creole with little resemblance to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_syntax" title="Portuguese syntax">
        syntax of Portuguese
       </a>
       or the substrate language.
      </p>
      <p>
       These creoles are (or were) spoken mostly by communities of descendants of Portuguese, natives, and sometimes other peoples from the Portuguese colonial empire.
      </p>
      <p>
       Until recently creoles were considered "degenerate" dialects of Portuguese unworthy of attention. As a consequence, there is little documentation on the details of their formation. Since the 20th century, increased study of creoles by linguists led to several theories being advanced. The
       <a href="/wiki/Monogenetic_theory_of_pidgins" title="Monogenetic theory of pidgins">
        monogenetic theory of pidgins
       </a>
       assumes that some type of pidgin language — dubbed West African Pidgin Portuguese — based on Portuguese was spoken from the 15th to 18th centuries in the forts established by the Portuguese on the West African coast. This variety was the starting point of all the pidgin and creole languages. This would explain to some extent why Portuguese lexical items can be found in many creoles, but more importantly, it would account for the numerous grammatical similarities shared by such languages, such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       <i>
        na
       </i>
       , meaning "in" and/or "on", which would come from the Portuguese contraction
       <i>
        na
       </i>
       meaning "in the" (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gender_(linguistics)" title="Gender (linguistics)">
        feminine
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Number_(linguistics)" title="Number (linguistics)">
        singular
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_name">
        Origin of the name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of the name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_peoples" title="Creole peoples">
        Creole peoples
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Portuguese word for "creole" is
       <i>
        crioulo
       </i>
       , which derives from the verb
       <i>
        criar
       </i>
       ("to raise", "to bring up") and a
       <a href="/wiki/Suffix" title="Suffix">
        suffix
       </a>
       <i>
        -oulo
       </i>
       of debated origin. Originally the word was used to distinguish the members of any ethnic group who were born and raised in the colonies from those who were born in their homeland. In Africa it was often applied to locally born people of (wholly or partly) Portuguese descent, as opposed to those born in Portugal; whereas in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       it was also used to distinguish locally born
       <a href="/wiki/Black_people" title="Black people">
        black people
       </a>
       of African descent from those who had been brought from Africa as slaves.
      </p>
      <p>
       In time, however, this generic sense was lost, and the word
       <i>
        crioulo
       </i>
       or its derivatives (like "Creole" and its equivalents in other languages) became the name of several specific Upper Guinean communities and their languages: the
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">
        Guinean
       </a>
       people and their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Kriol_language" title="Guinea-Bissau Kriol language">
        Kriol language
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">
        Cape Verdean
       </a>
       people and their
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
        Kriolu language
       </a>
       , all of which still today have very vigorous use, suppressing the importance of official standard Portuguese.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Upper_Guinea">
        Upper Guinea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Upper Guinea">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The oldest Portuguese-based creole are the so-called
       <i>
        Crioulos
       </i>
       of Upper Guinea, born around the Portuguese settlements along the northwest coast of Africa. Portuguese Creoles are the mother tongues of most people in
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">
        Cape Verde
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">
        Guinea-Bissau
       </a>
       , the creole is used as lingua franca among people speaking different languages, and is becoming the mother tongue of a growing population. They consist of two languages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau_Creole" title="Guinea-Bissau Creole">
         Guinea Creole (Kriol)
        </a>
        :
        <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
         lingua franca
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">
         Guinea-Bissau
        </a>
        , also spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Casamance" title="Casamance">
         Casamance
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Senegal" title="Senegal">
         Senegal
        </a>
        and in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gambia" title="Gambia">
         Gambia
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verdean_Creole" title="Cape Verdean Creole">
         Cape Verdean Creole (Kriolu, Kriol)
        </a>
        : a
        <a href="/wiki/Dialect_continuum" title="Dialect continuum">
         dialect continuum
        </a>
        on the islands of
        <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">
         Cape Verde
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>

        São Tomé and Príncipe

       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        </i>
        ]
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/Factory_(trading_post)" title="Factory (trading post)">

       </a>
       in the

        Gulf of Guinea
       </a>
       , but also in the Congo region.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angolar_language" title="Angolar language">

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Principense_language" title="Principense language">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="India">
        India
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: India">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-Portuguese_creoles" title="Indo-Portuguese creoles">
        Indo-Portuguese creoles
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The numerous Portuguese outposts in India and Sri Lanka gave rise to many Portuguese-based creole languages, of which only a few have survived to the present. The largest group were the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norteiro_Indo-Portuguese_languages" title="Norteiro Indo-Portuguese languages">
        Norteiro languages
       </a>
       , spoken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Norteiro_people" title="Norteiro people">
        Norteiro people
       </a>
       , the Christian Indo-Portuguese in the North
       <a href="/wiki/Konkan" title="Konkan">
        Konkan
       </a>
       . Those communities were centered on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba%C3%A7aim" title="Baçaim">
        Baçaim
       </a>
       , modern
       <a href="/wiki/Vasai" title="Vasai">
        Vasai
       </a>
       , which was then called the “Northern Court of
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_India" title="Portuguese India">
        Portuguese India
       </a>
       ” (in opposition to the "Southern Court" at
       <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">
        Goa
       </a>
       ). The creole languages spoken in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ba%C3%A7aim" title="Baçaim">
        Baçaim
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Salsete&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Salsete (page does not exist)">
        Salsete
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thane" title="Thane">
        Thana
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chevai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chevai (page does not exist)">
        Chevai
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mahim" title="Mahim">
        Mahim
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tecelaria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tecelaria (page does not exist)">
        Tecelaria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dadar" title="Dadar">
        Dadar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Parel" title="Parel">
        Parel
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cavel" title="Cavel">
        Cavel
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bandora_(town)" title="Bandora (town)">
        Bandora
       </a>
       (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Bandra" title="Bandra">
        Bandra
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Gorai" title="Gorai">
        Gorai
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Morol&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Morol (page does not exist)">
        Morol
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Andheri" title="Andheri">
        Andheri
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Versova_(Mumbai)" title="Versova (Mumbai)">
        Versova
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Malvan" title="Malvan">
        Malvan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Manori" title="Manori">
        Manori
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mazag%C3%A3o" title="Mazagão">
        Mazagão
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Chaul" title="Chaul">
        Chaul
       </a>
       are now extinct. The only surviving Norteiro creoles are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diu_Indo-Portuguese_language" title="Diu Indo-Portuguese language">
         Diu Indo-Portuguese
        </a>
        (almost extinct): in
        <a href="/wiki/Daman_and_Diu" title="Daman and Diu">
         Diu
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daman_Indo-Portuguese_language" title="Daman Indo-Portuguese language">
         Daman Indo-Portuguese (Língua da Casa)
        </a>
        : in
        <a href="/wiki/Daman_and_Diu" title="Daman and Diu">
         Daman
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kristi_language" title="Kristi language">
         Kristi
        </a>
        : in
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Korlai&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Korlai (page does not exist)">
         Korlai
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Maharashtra" title="Maharashtra">
         Maharashtra
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These surviving Norteiro creoles have suffered drastic changes in the last decades. Standard Portuguese re-influenced the creole of Daman in the mid-20th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Creoles of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_of_Coromandel" title="Coast of Coromandel">
        Coast of Coromandel
       </a>
       , such as of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Meliapor&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Meliapor (page does not exist)">
        Meliapor
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madras" title="Madras">
        Madras
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuticorin" title="Tuticorin">
        Tuticorin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cuddalore" title="Cuddalore">
        Cuddalore
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karikal" title="Karikal">
        Karikal
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pondicheri&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pondicheri (page does not exist)">
        Pondicheri
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tranquebar" title="Tranquebar">
        Tranquebar
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manapar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manapar (page does not exist)">
        Manapar
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negapatam" title="Negapatam">
        Negapatam
       </a>
       , were already extinct by the 19th century. Their speakers (mostly the people of mixed Portuguese-Indian ancestry, known locally as
       <a href="/wiki/Topasses" title="Topasses">
        Topasses
       </a>
       ) switched to
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       after the British takeover.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most of the creoles of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_of_Malabar" title="Coast of Malabar">
        Coast of Malabar
       </a>
       , namely those of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cananor" title="Cananor">
        Cananor
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tellicherry" title="Tellicherry">
        Tellicherry
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mah%C3%A9,_India" title="Mahé, India">
        Mahé
       </a>
       , Cochin (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">
        Kerala
       </a>
       ), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quilon" title="Quilon">
        Quilon
       </a>
       ) had become extinct by the 19th century. In Cananor and Tellicherry, some elderly people still spoke some creole in the 1980s. The only creole that is still spoken (by a few Christian families only) is:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vypin_Indo-Portuguese_language" title="Vypin Indo-Portuguese language">
         Vypin Indo-Portuguese
        </a>
        : in the
        <a href="/wiki/Vypin" title="Vypin">
         Vypin Island
        </a>
        , near Kerala.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Christians, even in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calcutta" title="Calcutta">
        Calcutta
       </a>
       , used Portuguese until 1811. A Portuguese Creole was still spoken in the early 20th century. Portuguese creoles were spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">
        Bengal
       </a>
       , such as at
       <a href="/wiki/Balasore" title="Balasore">
        Balasore
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pipli" title="Pipli">
        Pipli
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chandernagore" title="Chandernagore">
        Chandernagore
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chittagong" title="Chittagong">
        Chittagong
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Midnapore" title="Midnapore">
        Midnapore
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hooghly_(town)" title="Hooghly (town)">
        Hugli
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sri_Lanka">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <li>

        </a>
        : around
        <a href="/wiki/Batticaloa" title="Batticaloa">

        <a href="/wiki/Trincomalee" title="Trincomalee">
         Trincomalee
        </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Burghers" title="Portuguese Burghers">

         Kaffirs
        </a>

       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the past, Portuguese creoles were also spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">

       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Southeast_Asia">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="507" height="260" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png/220px-Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png/330px-Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png/440px-Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Southeast_asia_portuguese_creoles.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Southeast Asia Portuguese creoles: Papiá Kristang of Malaysia (1) and Macaista Chapado of Macao, SAR (2).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The earliest Portuguese creole in the region probably arose in the 16th century in
       <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">
        Malacca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
        Malaysia
       </a>
       , as well as in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moluccas" title="Moluccas">
        Moluccas
       </a>
       . After the takeover of those places by the Dutch in the 17th century, many creole-speaking slaves were taken to other places in Indonesia and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       , leading to several creoles that survived until recent times:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Kristang_language" title="Kristang language">
         Kristang (Cristão)
        </a>
        : in
        <a href="/wiki/Malacca" title="Malacca">
         Malacca
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
         Malaysia
        </a>
        ) and
        <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
         Singapore
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mardijker_language" title="Mardijker language">
         Mardijker
        </a>
        (extinct in the 19th century): by the
        <a href="/wiki/Mardijker_people" title="Mardijker people">
         Mardijker people
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/Batavia,_Dutch_East_Indies" title="Batavia, Dutch East Indies">
         Batavia
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Jakarta" title="Jakarta">
         Jakarta
        </a>
        ) =
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papia_Tugu_language" title="Papia Tugu language">
         Papiá Tugu
        </a>
        (extinct in 1978): in
        <a href="/wiki/Koja,_Jakarta" title="Koja, Jakarta">
         Kampung Tugu
        </a>
        , Jakarta, Indonesia.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Portugis_language" title="Portugis language">
         Portugis
        </a>
        (extinct around 1950): in the
        <a href="/wiki/Ambon_Island" title="Ambon Island">
         Ambon
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ternate" title="Ternate">
         Ternate
        </a>
        islands and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minahasa" title="Minahasa">
         Minahasa
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
         Indonesia
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bidau_Portuguese_language" title="Bidau Portuguese language">
         Bidau Portuguese
        </a>
        (extinct in the 1960s): in the
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bidau&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bidau (page does not exist)">
         Bidau
        </a>
        area of
        <a href="/wiki/Dili" title="Dili">
         Dili
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">
         East Timor
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The Malacca creole also had an influence on the creole of
       <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
        Macau
       </a>
       (see below).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Portuguese were present in the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Flores" title="Flores">
        Flores
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       since the 16th century, mainly in
       <a href="/wiki/Larantuka" title="Larantuka">
        Larantuka
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sikka_Regency" title="Sikka Regency">
        Sikka
       </a>
       ; but the local creole language, if any, has not survived.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other Portuguese-based creoles were once spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Thailand" title="Thailand">
        Thailand
       </a>
       (In KudeeJeen and Conception) and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bayingy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bayingy (page does not exist)">
        Bayingy
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">
        Burma
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Macau">
        Macau
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Macau">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Portuguese language was present in Portugal's colony
       <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
        Macau
       </a>
       since the mid-16th century. A Portuguese creole, Patua, developed there, first by interaction with the local
       <a href="/wiki/Cantonese_people" title="Cantonese people">
        Cantonese people
       </a>
       , and later modified by an influx of refugees from the Dutch takeover of Portuguese colonies in Indonesia.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Macanese_Patois" title="Macanese Patois">
         Macanese (Macaista, Patuá)
        </a>
        : in
        <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
         Macau
        </a>
        and, to a lesser extent, in
        <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
         Hong Kong
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Americas">
        Americas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Americas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ABCEilandenLocatie.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="115" data-file-width="250" height="101" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/ABCEilandenLocatie.PNG/220px-ABCEilandenLocatie.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/1e/ABCEilandenLocatie.PNG 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ABCEilandenLocatie.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Location map of Aruba, Bonnaire, and Curaçao, where Papiamento is spoken
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </a>
       (spoken in Aruba, Bonaire and Curaçao in the Caribbean) is a Portuguese-based creole that is undergoing significant Spanish influence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no consensus regarding the position of
       <a href="/wiki/Saramaccan_language" title="Saramaccan language">
        Saramaccan
       </a>
       , with some scholars classifying it as Portuguese Creole with an English relexification. Saramaccan may be an English Creole with Portuguese words, since structurally (morphology and syntax) it is related to the
       <b>
        Surinamese Creoles
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Sranan_Tongo" title="Sranan Tongo">
        Sranan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ndyuka_language" title="Ndyuka language">
        Ndyuka
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Maroon_spirit-possession_language" title="Jamaican Maroon spirit-possession language">
        Jamaican Maroon
       </a>
       ), despite the heavy percentage of Portuguese origin words. Other English creole languages of Suriname, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Paramaccan_people" title="Paramaccan people">
        Paramaccan
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Kwinti_language" title="Kwinti language">
        Kwinti
       </a>
       , have also Portuguese influences.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although sometimes classified as a creole, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cafund%C3%B3_language" title="Cafundó language">
        Cupópia
       </a>
       language from the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Quilombo_do_Cafund%C3%B3&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Quilombo do Cafundó (page does not exist)">
        Quilombo do Cafundó
       </a>
       , at
       <a href="/wiki/Salto_de_Pirapora" title="Salto de Pirapora">
        Salto de Pirapora
       </a>
       , São Paulo, discovered in 1978 and spoken by less than 40 people as a secret language,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is better classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       variety since it is structurally similar to Portuguese, in spite of having a large number of
       <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">
        Bantu
       </a>
       words in its lexicon. For languages with these characteristics, H. H. do Couto has forged the designation of
       <b>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Anticreole&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Anticreole (page does not exist)">
         Anticreole
        </a>
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which would be the inverse of a Creole language, as they are seen by the
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language#Theories_focusing_on_non-European_input" title="Creole language">
        non-European input theories
       </a>
       (i.e.:
       <b>
        Creoles
       </b>
       = African languages grammar + European languages lexicon;
       <b>
        Anticreoles
       </b>
       = European languages grammar + African languages lexicon).
      </p>
      <p>
       There is a Portuguese dialect in Helvécia, South of
       <a href="/wiki/Bahia" title="Bahia">
        Bahia
       </a>
       that is theorized as presenting signs of an earlier decreolization. Ancient Portuguese creoles originating from Africa are still preserved in the ritual songs of the Afro-Brazilian animist religions (
       <a href="/wiki/Candombl%C3%A9" title="Candomblé">
        Candomblé
       </a>
       )
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It has been conjectured that vernacular of Brazil (not the official and standard
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       ) resulted from decreolization of a creole based on Portuguese and native languages; but this is not a widely accepted view. Vernacular Brazilian Portuguese is continuous with European Portuguese, and in fact quite conservative in some aspects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origens_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Origens-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Academic specialists compiled by linguist Volker Noll
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       affirm that the Brazilian linguistic phenomena are the
       <i>
        "nativização"
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nativization" title="Nativization">
        nativization
       </a>
       /nativism of a most radically Romanic form. The phenomena in Brazilian Portuguese are Classical Latin and Old Portuguese heritage. This is not a creole form, but a radical Romanic form.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Origens_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Origens-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Regardless of borrowings and minor changes, it must be kept in mind that Brazilian Portuguese is not a Portuguese creole, since both grammar and vocabulary remain real Portuguese and its origins can be traced directly from 16th century European Portuguese.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some authors, like Swedish Parkvall,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       classify it as a
       <b>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Semicreole&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Semicreole (page does not exist)">
         Semicreole
        </a>
       </b>
       in the concept defined by Holm:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a Semicreole is a language that has undergone “partial restructuring, producing varieties which were never fully pidginized and which preserve a substantial part of their lexifier’s structure (...) while showing a noticeable degree of restructuring”. Nevertheless, scholars like Anthony Julius Naro and Maria Marta Pereira Scherre demonstrated how every single phenomenon found in Brazilian Portuguese can also be found in regional modern European Portuguese and 1500s and 1600s European Portuguese, such as the epic poetry of
       <a href="/wiki/Lu%C3%ADs_de_Cam%C3%B5es" title="Luís de Camões">
        Luís de Camões
       </a>
       , as well as other Romance languages such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aranese" title="Aranese">
        Aranese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       , classifying these phenomena as a natural Romance drift.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naro2007_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Naro2007-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vernacular Brazilian Portuguese is continuous with European Portuguese and its phonetics is more conservative in several aspects, characterizing the nativization of a
       <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">
        koiné
       </a>
       formed by several regional European Portuguese variations brought to Brazil and its natural drift.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naro2007_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Naro2007-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
