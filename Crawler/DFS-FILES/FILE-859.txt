      <p>
       <b>
        Nicaraguan Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Español nicaragüense
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is geographically defined as the form of Spanish spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       . Affectionately, Nicaraguan Spanish is often called
       <i>
        Nicañol.
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish dialect in Nicaragua shares many similarities with that of its neighbors in the region, but it also has some stark differences in pronunciation and usage.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nicaragua has the highest frequency, among Central American countries, of the use of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       —use of the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and its verb forms for the familiar second-person singular ("you"), in place of the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_Spanish" title="Standard Spanish">
        Standard Spanish
       </a>
       . In this regard it is similar to the usage of
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguayan_Spanish" title="Uruguayan Spanish">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Río de la Plata
       </a>
       region of South America.
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is used frequently in colloquial and familiar settings, but Nicaraguans also understand
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuteo" title="Tuteo">
        tuteo
       </a>
       . The use of "vos" can be heard in television programs and can be seen in written form in publications.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Caribbean_Coast_Autonomous_Region" title="North Caribbean Coast Autonomous Region">
        North Caribbean Coast Autonomous Region
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Caribbean_Coast_Autonomous_Region" title="South Caribbean Coast Autonomous Region">
        South Caribbean Coast Autonomous Region
       </a>
       , language and pronunciation is strongly influenced by native and creole dialects such as
       <a href="/wiki/Miskito_language" title="Miskito language">
        Miskito
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rama_language" title="Rama language">
        Rama
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumo_language" title="Sumo language">
        Sumo
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miskito_Coastal_Creole" title="Miskito Coastal Creole">
        Miskito Coastal Creole
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jamaican_Patois" title="Jamaican Patois">
        Jamaican Patois
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Garifuna_language" title="Garifuna language">
        Garifuna
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rama_Cay_Creole" title="Rama Cay Creole">
        Rama Cay Creole
       </a>
       but
       <a class="mw-selflink selflink">
        Spanish
       </a>
       continues to be the main language spoken.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Nicaraguan accent, like most New World Spanish, dates back to the 16th century in
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusia
       </a>
       . It shares later developments of Andalusian Spanish with that of
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       and the Caribbean/coastal regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Panama" title="Panama">
        Panama
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Honduras" title="Honduras">
        Honduras
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       . Nicaragua's relative isolation from
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       , however, and, to an extent, from other nations, fostered the development of the Nicaraguan accent, which did not change in the same ways that the Andalusian,
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian
       </a>
       , or other Spanish-American accents did.
      </p>
      <p>
       During its history, Nicaraguan Spanish has acquired many indigenous influences and several distinguishing characteristics. Until the 19th century, a hybrid form of Nahuat-Spanish was the common language of Nicaragua. Today,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nahuat" title="Nahuat">
        Nahuat
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mangue_language" title="Mangue language">
        Mangue
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">
        Mayan
       </a>
       words, along with their respective
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       , can be found in everyday speech. Also, as Nicaragua was part of
       <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
        First Mexican Empire
       </a>
       , there are a number of words widely used in Nicaragua which have
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">
        Mayan
       </a>
       or other native origins, in particular names for flora, fauna and
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponyms
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronunciation">
        Pronunciation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pronunciation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Notable characteristics of Nicaraguan phonology include the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The presence of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo#Seseo" title="Seseo">
         Seseo
        </a>
        wherein
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        are pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        . Seseo is common to
        <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
         Andalusian
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
         Canarian Spanish
        </a>
        varieties.
       </li>
       <li>
        Syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_4-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        (except in the southern departments of
        <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_San_Juan_Department" title="Río San Juan Department">
         Rio San Juan
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rivas_(department)" title="Rivas (department)">
         Rivas
        </a>
        ).
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /x/
        </span>
        is realized as glottal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
          h
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_4-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The pronunciation of intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        (orthographic
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         ll
        </i>
        ) is "weak", without friction.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_4-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Intervocalic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
        often disappears; the ending
        <i>
         -ado
        </i>
        is often
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ao]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_4-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no confusion between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , as in the Caribbean.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        is pronounced velar
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lipski_4-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Lipski-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Word-final
        <a href="/wiki/Voicelessness" title="Voicelessness">
         voiceless
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
         stops
        </a>
        (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /p/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t/
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        —rare in native Spanish words, but occurring in many words borrowed from English) are often merged in pronunciation as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [k]
        </span>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canfield_3-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-Canfield-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The Costa Rican ice cream shop
        <i>
         Pops,
        </i>
        with franchises in other Central American countries, is pronounced in certain regions of Nicaragua as
        <i>
         Pocs.
        </i>
        <i>
         Internet
        </i>
        is sometimes pronounced
        <i>
         Internec;
        </i>
        <i>
         cenit
        </i>
        is pronounced
        <i>
         cenic;
        </i>
        <i>
         laptop
        </i>
        is pronounced
        <i>
         lactoc;
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         robot
        </i>
        pronounced
        <i>
         roboc.
        </i>
        This is sometimes extended to native Spanish words where such stops are found at the end of a syllable. For example,
        <i>
         aceptar
        </i>
        is sometimes pronounced
        <i>
         acectar.
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        As Nicaragua was part of
        <a href="/wiki/First_Mexican_Empire" title="First Mexican Empire">
         First Mexican Empire
        </a>
        , Nicaraguan dialect adopted the
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless alveolar affricate">
         voiceless alveolar affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡s]
        </span>
        and the cluster
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tl]
        </span>
        (originally
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tɬ/
        </span>
        ) represented by the respective digraphs &lt;tz&gt; and &lt;tl&gt; in loanwords of Nahuatl origin, like
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Quetzal" title="Quetzal">
          quetzal
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         tlapalería
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t͡ɬapaleˈɾia]
        </span>
        ('hardware store'). Even words of Greek and Latin origin with &lt;tl&gt;, such as
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27Atl%C3%A1ntico%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''Atlántico''">
           <i>
            Atlántico
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
          <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%27%27atleta%27%27#Spanish" title="wikt:''atleta''">
           <i>
            atleta
           </i>
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        , are pronounced with the affricate:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aˈtle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        (compare
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈlãn̪t̪iko̞]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [aðˈle̞t̪a]
        </span>
        in Spain and other dialects in Hispanic America
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vos">
        Vos
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vos">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is the predominant second person singular pronoun used by most speakers in familiar or informal contexts to address people in the same age group.
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       is also used by adults in addressing children or juveniles, but children address adults with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjugations_with_the_vos_pronoun">
        Conjugations with the
        <i>
         vos
        </i>
        pronoun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Conjugations with the vos pronoun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Nicaraguan
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       is both pronominal and verbal; that is, speakers use the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       and the characteristic final-stressed verb forms. See
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo#Conjugation_with_vos" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Affirmative_Imperative">
        Affirmative Imperative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Affirmative Imperative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo#Affirmative_imperative" title="Voseo">
        Voseo Affirmative Imperative
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       The affirmative imperative in Nicaraguan
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       —like that of
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       —places stress on the last syllable. For example,
       <i>
        ¡Ven acá!
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ¡Ven aquí!
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        ¡Vení!
       </i>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usted">
        Usted
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usted">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Usted
       </i>
       is the formal second person singular pronoun in Nicaraguan Spanish, as in almost all modern varieties of Spanish.
       <i>
        Usted
       </i>
       is used in addressing elderly people, authorities, foreigners formally and in business settings. In contrast to neighboring
       <a href="/wiki/Costa_Rica" title="Costa Rica">
        Costa Rica
       </a>
       , Nicaraguans are more inclined to address a casual acquaintance as
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , rather than
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="T.C3.BA">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tú">
        Tú
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tú">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <i>
        Tú
       </i>
       is hardly used in Nicaraguan Spanish, except in addressing foreigners familiarly, in speech or in writing. Due in part to the influence of Mexican, Colombian, and Venezuelan television programming, Nicaraguans are familiar with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tuteo" title="Tuteo">
        tuteo
       </a>
       , and some television viewers, especially children, have begun to use it in limited contexts.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nicaraguan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A number of words widely used in Nicaragua which have
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mayan_languages" title="Mayan languages">
        Mayan
       </a>
       or other native origins, in particular names for flora, fauna and
       <a href="/wiki/Toponymy" title="Toponymy">
        toponyms
       </a>
       . Some of these words are used in most, or all, Spanish-speaking countries, like
       <i>
        chocolate
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        aguacate
       </i>
       ("avocado"), and some are only used in Mexico and Nicaragua. The latter include
       <i>
        guajolote
       </i>
       "turkey" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        huaxōlōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [waˈʃoːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       (although
       <i>
        pavo
       </i>
       is also used, as in other Spanish-speaking countries);
       <i>
        papalote
       </i>
       "kite" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        pāpālōtl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [paːˈpaːloːt͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       "butterfly"; and
       <i>
        jitomate
       </i>
       "tomato" &lt; Nahuatl
       <i>
        xītomatl
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Nahuatl" title="Help:IPA/Nahuatl">
         [ʃiːˈtomat͡ɬ]
        </a>
       </span>
       . For a more complete list see
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Spanish_words_of_Nahuatl_origin" title="List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin">
        List of Spanish words of Nahuatl origin
       </a>
       . Certain words that are present in Nicaraguan Spanish may not be immediately recognizable to non-Nicaraguans:
      </p>
