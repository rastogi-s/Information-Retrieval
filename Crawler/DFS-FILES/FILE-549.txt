      <p>
       A
       <b>
        school
       </b>
       is an institution designed to provide
       <a href="/wiki/Learning_space" title="Learning space">
        learning spaces
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Learning_environment" title="Learning environment">
        learning environments
       </a>
       for the teaching of
       <a href="/wiki/Student" title="Student">
        students
       </a>
       (or "pupils") under the direction of teachers. Most countries have systems of formal
       <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">
        education
       </a>
       , which is commonly
       <a href="/wiki/Compulsory_education" title="Compulsory education">
        compulsory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title='"How'>
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In these systems, students progress through a series of schools. The names for these schools vary by country (discussed in the
       <i>
        Regional
       </i>
       section below) but generally include
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">
        primary school
       </a>
       for young children and
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">
        secondary school
       </a>
       for teenagers who have completed primary education. An institution where
       <a href="/wiki/Higher_education" title="Higher education">
        higher education
       </a>
       is taught, is commonly called a
       <a href="/wiki/University_college" title="University college">
        university college
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
        university
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to these core schools, students in a given country may also attend schools before and after primary and secondary education.
       <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">
        Kindergarten
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Education" title="Education">
        pre-school
       </a>
       provide some schooling to very young children (typically ages 3–5).
       <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
        University
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">
        vocational school
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/College" title="College">
        college
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Seminary" title="Seminary">
        seminary
       </a>
       may be available after secondary school. A school may be dedicated to one particular field, such as a school of economics or a school of dance.
       <a href="/wiki/Alternative_school" title="Alternative school">
        Alternative schools
       </a>
       may provide nontraditional curriculum and methods.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also non-government schools, called private schools. Private schools may be required when the government does not supply adequate, or special education. Other private schools can also be religious, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Christian_school" title="Christian school">
        Christian schools
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Madrasa" title="Madrasa">
        madrasa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hawza" title="Hawza">
        hawzas
       </a>
       (Shi'a schools),
       <a href="/wiki/Yeshiva" title="Yeshiva">
        yeshivas
       </a>
       (Jewish schools), and others; or schools that have a higher standard of education or seek to foster other personal achievements. Schools for adults include institutions of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corporate_training" title="Corporate training">
        corporate training
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Military_education_and_training" title="Military education and training">
        military education and training
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Business_school" title="Business school">
        business schools
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Home_schooling" title="Home schooling">
        home schooling
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Educational_technology" title="Educational technology">
        online schools
       </a>
       , teaching and learning take place outside a traditional school building. Schools are commonly organized in several different
       <a href="/wiki/School_organizational_models" title="School organizational models">
        organizational models
       </a>
       , including departmental, small learning communities, academies, integrated, and schools-within-a-school.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       derives from Greek
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        σχολή
       </span>
       <i>
        (
       </i>
       <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        scholē
       </span>
       ), originally meaning "leisure" and also "that in which leisure is employed", but later "a group to whom lectures were given, school".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_development">
        History and development
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_education" title="History of education">
        History of education
       </a>
      </div>

      <p>
       The concept of grouping students together in a centralized location for learning has existed since
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_antiquity" title="Classical antiquity">
        Classical antiquity
       </a>
       . Formal schools have existed at least since
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        ancient Greece
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Academy" title="Academy">
        Academy
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        ancient Rome
       </a>
       (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Education_in_Ancient_Rome" title="Education in Ancient Rome">
        Education in Ancient Rome
       </a>
       )
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_India" title="History of India">
        ancient India
       </a>
       (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurukul" title="Gurukul">
        Gurukul
       </a>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_China" title="History of China">
        ancient China
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_education_in_China" title="History of education in China">
        History of education in China
       </a>
       ). The
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine Empire
       </a>
       had an established schooling system beginning at the primary level. According to
       <i>
        Traditions and Encounters
       </i>
       , the founding of the primary education system began in 425 AD and "...
       <a href="/wiki/Soldier" title="Soldier">
        military personnel
       </a>
       usually had at least a primary education ...". The sometimes efficient and often large government of the Empire meant that educated citizens were a must. Although Byzantium lost much of the grandeur of Roman culture and extravagance in the process of surviving, the Empire emphasized efficiency in its war manuals. The Byzantine education system continued until the empire's collapse in 1453 AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In Western Europe a considerable number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathedral_schools" title="Cathedral schools">
        cathedral schools
       </a>
       were founded during the
       <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">
        Early Middle Ages
       </a>
       in order to teach future clergy and administrators, with the oldest still existing, and continuously operated, cathedral schools being
       <a href="/wiki/The_King%27s_School,_Canterbury" title="The King's School, Canterbury">
        The King's School, Canterbury
       </a>
       (established 597 CE),
       <a href="/wiki/King%27s_School,_Rochester" title="King's School, Rochester">
        King's School, Rochester
       </a>
       (established 604 CE),
       <a href="/wiki/St_Peter%27s_School,_York" title="St Peter's School, York">
        St Peter's School, York
       </a>
       (established 627 CE) and
       <a href="/wiki/Thetford_Grammar_School" title="Thetford Grammar School">
        Thetford Grammar School
       </a>
       (established 631 CE). Beginning in the 5th century CE
       <a href="/wiki/Monastic_school" title="Monastic school">
        monastic schools
       </a>
       were also established throughout Western Europe, teaching both religious and secular subjects.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islam
       </a>
       was another culture that developed a school system in the modern sense of the word. Emphasis was put on knowledge, which required a systematic way of teaching and spreading knowledge, and purpose-built structures. At first,
       <a href="/wiki/Mosque" title="Mosque">
        mosques
       </a>
       combined both religious performance and learning activities, but by the 9th century, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrassa" title="Madrassa">
        madrassa
       </a>
       was introduced, a school that was built independently from the mosque, such as
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Al_Quaraouiyine" title="University of Al Quaraouiyine">
        al-Qarawiyyin
       </a>
       , founded in 859 CE. They were also the first to make the
       <i>
        Madrassa
       </i>
       system a public domain under the control of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caliph" title="Caliph">
        Caliph
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ottomans" title="Ottomans">
        Ottomans
       </a>
       , the towns of
       <a href="/wiki/Bursa" title="Bursa">
        Bursa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Edirne" title="Edirne">
        Edirne
       </a>
       became the main centers of learning. The Ottoman system of
       <a href="/wiki/K%C3%BClliye" title="Külliye">
        Külliye
       </a>
       , a building complex containing a mosque, a hospital, madrassa, and public kitchen and dining areas, revolutionized the education system, making learning accessible to a wider public through its free meals, health care and sometimes free accommodation.
      </p>

      <p>
       In Europe, universities emerged during the 12th century; here,
       <a href="/wiki/Scholasticism" title="Scholasticism">
        scholasticism
       </a>
       was an important tool, and the academicians were called
       <i>
        schoolmen
       </i>
       . During the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       and much of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Modern" title="Early Modern">
        Early Modern
       </a>
       period, the main purpose of schools (as opposed to universities) was to teach the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin language
       </a>
       . This led to the term
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">
        grammar school
       </a>
       , which in the United States informally refers to a primary school, but in the United Kingdom means a school that selects entrants based on ability or aptitude. Following this, the school curriculum has gradually broadened to include literacy in the vernacular language as well as technical, artistic, scientific and practical subjects.
      </p>

       Obligatory school attendance became common in parts of Europe during the 18th century. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denmark-Norway" title="Denmark-Norway">
        Denmark-Norway
       </a>
       , this was introduced as early as in 1739-1741, the primary end being to increase the literacy of the
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://no.wikipedia.org/wiki/almue" title="no:almue">
         almue
        </a>
       </i>
       , i.e. the "regular people".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many of the earlier public schools in the United States and elsewhere were
       <a href="/wiki/One-room_school" title="One-room school">
        one-room schools
       </a>
       where a single teacher taught seven grades of boys and girls in the same classroom. Beginning in the 1920s, one-room schools were consolidated into multiple classroom facilities with transportation increasingly provided by
       <a href="/wiki/Kid_hack" title="Kid hack">
        kid hacks
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/School_bus" title="School bus">
        school buses
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       The use of the term
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       varies by country, as do the names of the various levels of education within the country.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_Kingdom_and_Commonwealth_of_Nations">
        United Kingdom and Commonwealth of Nations
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the United Kingdom, the term
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       refers primarily to pre-
       <a href="/wiki/University" title="University">
        university
       </a>
       institutions, and these can, for the most part, be divided into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-school" title="Pre-school">
        pre-schools
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nursery_school" title="Nursery school">
        nursery schools
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_school" title="Primary school">
        primary schools
       </a>
       (sometimes further divided into
       <a href="/wiki/Infant_school" title="Infant school">
        infant school
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Junior_school" title="Junior school">
        junior school
       </a>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_school" title="Secondary school">
        secondary schools
       </a>
       . Various types of secondary schools in England and Wales include
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">
        grammar schools
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Comprehensive_school" title="Comprehensive school">
        comprehensives
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secondary_modern" title="Secondary modern">
        secondary moderns
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_(England)" title="Academy (England)">
        city academies
       </a>
       . In Scotland, while they may have different names, all Secondary schools are the same, except in that they may be funded by the state, or independently funded (see next paragraph). It is unclear if "Academies", which are a hybrid between state and independently funded/controlled schools and have been introduced to England in recent years, will ever be introduced to Scotland. School performance in Scotland is monitored by
       <a href="/wiki/Her_Majesty%27s_Inspectorate_of_Education" title="Her Majesty's Inspectorate of Education">
        Her Majesty's Inspectorate of Education
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Ofsted" title="Ofsted">
        Ofsted
       </a>
       reports on performance in England and
       <a href="/wiki/Estyn" title="Estyn">
        Estyn
       </a>
       reports on performance in Wales.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United Kingdom, most schools are publicly funded and known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/State_schools" title="State schools">
        state schools
       </a>
       or maintained schools in which tuition is provided free. There are also private schools or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Independent_school_(UK)" title="Independent school (UK)">
        independent schools
       </a>
       that charge fees. Some of the most selective and expensive private schools are known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(UK)" title="Public school (UK)">
        public schools
       </a>
       , a usage that can be confusing to speakers of
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_English" title="North American English">
        North American English
       </a>
       . In North American usage, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Public_school_(government_funded)" title="Public school (government funded)">
        public school
       </a>
       is one that is publicly funded or run.
      </p>
      <p>
       In much of the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth of Nations
       </a>
       , including Australia, New Zealand, India, Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, South Africa, Kenya, and Tanzania, the term
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       refers primarily to pre-university institutions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="India">
        India
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3000" data-file-width="4000" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/220px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/330px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG/440px-Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Perumacheri_AUP_School.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A school building in
         <a href="/wiki/Kannur" title="Kannur">
          Kannur
         </a>
         , India
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In ancient India, schools were in the form of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gurukul" title="Gurukul">
        Gurukuls
       </a>
       . Gurukuls were traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
        Hindu
       </a>
       residential schools of learning; typically the teacher's house or a monastery. During the Mughal rule,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Madrasah" title="Madrasah">
        Madrasahs
       </a>
       were introduced in India to educate the children of Muslim parents. British records show that indigenous education was widespread in the 18th century, with a school for every temple, mosque or village in most regions of the country. The subjects taught included Reading, Writing, Arithmetic, Theology, Law, Astronomy, Metaphysics, Ethics, Medical Science and Religion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the British rule in India, Christian missionaries from England, USA and other countries established missionary and boarding schools throughout the country. Later as these schools gained in popularity, more were started and some gained prestige. These schools marked the beginning of modern schooling in India and the syllabus and calendar they followed became the benchmark for schools in modern India. Today most of the schools follow the missionary school model in terms of tutoring, subject / syllabus, governance etc.with minor changes. Schools in India range from schools with large campuses with thousands of students and hefty fees to schools where children are taught under a tree with a small / no campus and are totally free of cost. There are various boards of schools in India, namely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Board_for_Secondary_Education" title="Central Board for Secondary Education">
        Central Board for Secondary Education
       </a>
       (CBSE), Council for the Indian School Certificate Examinations (CISCE), Madrasa Boards of various states, Matriculation Boards of various states, State Boards of various boards, Anglo Indian Board, and so on. The typical syllabus today includes Language(s), Mathematics, Science — Physics, Chemistry, Biology, Geography, History, General Knowledge, Information Technology / Computer Science etc.. Extra curricular activities include physical education / sports and cultural activities like music, choreography, painting, theater / drama etc.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Europe">
        Europe
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bundesarchiv_B_145_Bild-F079063-0034,_Bonn,_Gymnasium,_Chemieunterricht.jpg" title="Enlarge">

         <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">

         </a>

      </div>
      <p>
       In much of continental Europe, the term
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       usually applies to
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">
        primary education
       </a>
       , with primary schools that last between four and nine years, depending on the country. It also applies to
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_education" title="Secondary education">
        secondary education
       </a>
       , with secondary schools often divided between
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(school)" title="Gymnasium (school)">
         Gymnasiums
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Vocational_school" title="Vocational school">
        vocational schools
       </a>
       , which again depending on country and type of school educate students for between three and six years. In Germany students graduating from Grundschule are not allowed to directly progress into a vocational school, but are supposed to proceed to one of Germany's general education schools such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gesamtschule" title="Gesamtschule">
        Gesamtschule
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hauptschule" title="Hauptschule">
        Hauptschule
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Realschule" title="Realschule">
        Realschule
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(Germany)" title="Gymnasium (Germany)">
        Gymnasium
       </a>
       . When they leave that school, which usually happens at age 15-19 they are allowed to proceed to a vocational school. The term school is rarely used for
       <a href="/wiki/Tertiary_education" title="Tertiary education">
        tertiary education
       </a>
       , except for some
       <i>
        upper
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        high
       </i>
       schools (German: Hochschule), which describe colleges and universities.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       modern schools (after
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       ), of both primary and secondary educations, often are combined, while secondary education might be split into accomplished or not. The schools are classified as middle schools of general education and for the technical purposes include "degrees" of the education they provide out of three available: the first — primary, the second — unaccomplished secondary, and the third — accomplished secondary. Usually the first two degrees of education (eight years) are always included, while the last one (two years) gives option for the students to pursue
       <a href="/wiki/Vocational_education" title="Vocational education">
        vocational
       </a>
       or specialized educations.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="North_America_and_the_United_States">
        North America and the United States
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In North America, the term
       <i>
        school
       </i>
       can refer to any educational institution at any level, and covers all of the following:
       <a href="/wiki/Preschool" title="Preschool">
        preschool
       </a>
       (for
       <a href="/wiki/Toddler" title="Toddler">
        toddlers
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Kindergarten" title="Kindergarten">
        kindergarten
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">
        elementary school
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_school" title="Middle school">
        middle school
       </a>
       (also called intermediate school or junior high school, depending on specific age groups and geographic region), high school (or in some cases senior high school), college, university, and
       <a href="/wiki/Graduate_school" title="Graduate school">
        graduate school
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United States, school performance through high school is monitored by each state's
       <a href="/wiki/State_education_agency" title="State education agency">
        department of education
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Charter_school" title="Charter school">
        Charter schools
       </a>
       are publicly funded elementary or secondary schools that have been freed from some of the rules, regulations, and statutes that apply to other public schools. The terms
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar_school" title="Grammar school">
        grammar school
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        grade school
       </i>
       are sometimes used to refer to a primary school.
      </p>

          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mr._Shake_Amargosa.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Primary school students with their teacher, Colombia, 2014
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Many schools are owned or funded by
       <a href="/wiki/State_(polity)" title="State (polity)">
        states
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Private_school" title="Private school">
        Private schools
       </a>
       operate independently from the government. Private schools usually rely on fees from families whose children attend the school for funding; however, sometimes such schools also receive government support (for example, through
       <a href="/wiki/School_voucher" title="School voucher">
        School vouchers
       </a>
       ). Many private schools are affiliated with a particular religion; these are known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parochial_schools" title="Parochial schools">
        parochial schools
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Starting_a_school">
        Starting a school
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Toronto_District_School_Board" title="Toronto District School Board">
        Toronto District School Board
       </a>
       is an example of a school board that allows parents to design and propose new schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When designing a school, factors that need to be decided include:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-(Group)1972_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-(Group)1972-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Goals: What is the purpose of education, and what is the school's role?
       </li>
       <li>
        Governance: Who will make which decisions?
       </li>
       <li>
        Parent involvement: In which ways are parents welcome at the school?
       </li>
       <li>
        Student body: Will it be, for example, a neighbourhood school or a specialty school?
       </li>
       <li>
        Student conduct: What behaviour is acceptable, and what happens when behaviour is inappropriate?
       </li>
       <li>
        Curriculum: What will be the curriculum model, and who will decide on curricula?
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Components_of_most_schools">
        Components of most schools
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Learning_environment" title="Learning environment">
        Learning environment
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Learning_space" title="Learning space">
        Learning space
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rathaykentst.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="435" data-file-width="1074" height="89" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Rathaykentst.jpg/220px-Rathaykentst.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Rathaykentst.jpg/330px-Rathaykentst.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/61/Rathaykentst.jpg/440px-Rathaykentst.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rathaykentst.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A school entrance building in Australia
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Schools are organized spaces purposed for teaching and learning. The classrooms, where teachers teach and students learn, are of central importance. Classrooms may be specialized for certain subjects, such as laboratory classrooms for science education and workshops for
       <a href="/wiki/Industrial_arts" title="Industrial arts">
        industrial arts
       </a>
       education.
      </p>
      <p>
       Typical schools have many other rooms and areas, which may include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Cafeteria" title="Cafeteria">
         Cafeteria
        </a>
        (Commons), dining hall or canteen where students eat lunch and often breakfast and snacks.
       </li>
       <li>
        Athletic field, playground,
        <a href="/wiki/Gym" title="Gym">
         gym
        </a>
        , and/or track place where students participating in sports or
        <a href="/wiki/Physical_education" title="Physical education">
         physical education
        </a>
        practice
       </li>
       <li>
        School yards, that is, all-purpose playfields typically in
        <a href="/wiki/Elementary_school" title="Elementary school">
         elementary schools
        </a>
        , often made of concrete, although some are being transformed into environmentally friendly teaching gardens by landscape artists such as
        <a href="/wiki/Sharon_Gamson_Danks" title="Sharon Gamson Danks">
         Sharon Gamson Danks
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChicagoTribune_8-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-ChicagoTribune-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchoolGardenWeekly_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-SchoolGardenWeekly-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Auditorium or hall where student theatrical and musical productions can be staged and where all-school events such as assemblies are held
       </li>
       <li>
        Office where the administrative work of the school is done
       </li>
       <li>
        Library where students ask librarians reference questions, check out books and magazines, and often use computers
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Computer_labs" title="Computer labs">
         Computer labs
        </a>
        where computer-based work is done and the internet accessed
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Security">
        Security
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1600" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg/220px-Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg/330px-Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg/440px-Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Warning_signs_at_CHS.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         To curtail violence, some schools have added
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCTV" title="CCTV">
          CCTV
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Surveillance" title="Surveillance">
          surveillance
         </a>
         cameras. This is especially common in schools with excessive gang activity or violence.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The safety of staff and students is increasingly becoming an issue for school communities, an issue most schools are addressing through improved security. Some have also taken measures such as installing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metal_detectors#Security_screening" title="Metal detectors">
        metal detectors
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Closed-circuit_television" title="Closed-circuit television">
        video surveillance
       </a>
       . Others have even taken measures such as having the children swipe identification cards as they board the school bus. For some schools, these plans have included the use of door numbering to aid public safety response.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (December 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other security concerns faced by schools include bomb threats, gangs, vandalism,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bullying" title="Bullying">
        bullying
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Health_services">
        Health services
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_health_services" title="School health services">
        School health services
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       School health services are services from medical, teaching and other professionals applied in or out of school to improve the health and well-being of children and in some cases whole families. These services have been developed in different ways around the globe but the fundamentals are constant: the early detection, correction, prevention or amelioration of disease, disability and abuse from which school-aged children can suffer.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Online_schools_and_classes">
        Online schools and classes
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Virtual_school" title="Virtual school">
        Virtual school
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some schools offer remote access to their classes over the Internet. Online schools also can provide support to traditional schools, as in the case of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/School_Net_Namibia" title="School Net Namibia">
        School Net Namibia
       </a>
       . Some online classes also provide experience in a class, so that when people take them, they have already been introduced to the subject and know what to expect, and even more classes provide High School/College credit allowing people to take the classes at their own pace. Many online classes cost money to take but some are offered free.
      </p>
      <p>
       Internet-based distance learning programs are offered widely through many universities. Instructors teach through online activities and assignments. Online classes are taught the same as physically being in class with the same curriculum. The instructor offers the syllabus with their fixed requirements like any other class. Students can virtually turn their assignments in to their instructors according to deadlines. This being through via email or in the course webpage. This allowing students to work at their own pace, yet meeting the correct deadline. Students taking an online class have more flexibility in their schedules to take their classes at a time that works best for them. Conflicts with taking an online class may include not being face to face with the instructor when learning or being in an environment with other students. Online classes can also make understanding the content difficult, especially when not able to get in quick contact with the instructor. Online students do have the advantage of using other online sources with assignments or exams for that specific class. Online classes also have the advantage of students not needing to leave their house for a morning class or worrying about their attendance for that class. Students can work at their own pace to learn and achieve within that curriculum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The convenience of learning at home has been a major attractive point for enrolling online. Students can attend class anywhere a computer can go—at home, a library or while traveling internationally. Online school classes are designed to fit your needs, while allowing you to continue working and tending to your other obligations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Online school education is divided into three subcategories: Online Elementary School, Online Middle School,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Forest_Trail_Academy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Forest Trail Academy (page does not exist)">
        Online High school
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">
        Stress
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As a profession, teaching has levels of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Work-related_stress" title="Work-related stress">
        work-related stress
       </a>
       (WRS)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WRS_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WRS-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that are among the highest of any profession in some countries, such as the United Kingdom and the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The degree of this problem is becoming increasingly recognized and support systems are being put into place.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSN_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TSN-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TSNs_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TSNs-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Teacher_education" title="Teacher education">
        Teacher education
       </a>
       increasingly recognizes the need to train those new to the profession to be aware of and overcome mental health challenges they may face.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Stress sometimes affects students more severely than teachers, up to the point where the students are prescribed stress medication. This stress is claimed to be related to standardized testing, and the pressure on students to score above average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        See
        <a href="/wiki/Cram_school" title="Cram school">
         Cram school
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       According to a 2008 mental health study by the Associated Press and mtvU,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       eight in 10 college students
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Naming_conventions_(geographic_names)" title="Wikipedia:Naming conventions (geographic names)">
          <span title="The geographic scope near this tag is ambiguous. (August 2016)">
           where?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       said they had sometimes or frequently experienced stress in their daily lives. This was an increase of 20% from a survey five years previously. 34 percent had felt depressed at some point in the past three months, 13 percent had been diagnosed with a mental health condition such as an anxiety disorder or depression, and 9 percent had seriously considered suicide.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discipline_towards_students">
        Discipline towards students
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/School_discipline" title="School discipline">
        School discipline
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="512" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg/220px-Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg/330px-Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg/440px-Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Grundschueler_Mouloud_Djibouti.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/Djiboutian" title="Djiboutian">
          Djiboutian
         </a>
         primary schoolgirl
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Schools and their teachers have always been under pressure — for instance, pressure to cover the curriculum, to perform well in comparison to other schools, and to avoid the stigma of being "soft" or "spoiling" toward students. Forms of discipline, such as control over when students may speak, and normalized behaviour, such as raising a hand to speak, are imposed in the name of greater efficiency. Practitioners of
       <a href="/wiki/Critical_pedagogy" title="Critical pedagogy">
        critical pedagogy
       </a>
       maintain that such disciplinary measures have no positive effect on student learning. Indeed, some argue that disciplinary practices detract from learning, saying that they undermine students' individual dignity and sense of
       <a href="/wiki/Self-esteem" title="Self-esteem">
        self-worth
       </a>
       —the latter occupying a more primary role in students'
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hierarchy_of_needs" title="Hierarchy of needs">
        hierarchy of needs
       </a>
       .
      </p>
