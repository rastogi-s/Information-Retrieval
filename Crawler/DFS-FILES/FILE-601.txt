      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish Sahara
       </b>
       (Spanish:
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Sahara Español
        </span>
       </i>
       ; Arabic:
       <span dir="rtl" lang="ar" xml:lang="ar">
        <big>
         الصحراء الإسبانية
        </big>
       </span>
       ‎
       <i>
        As-Sahrā'a Al-Isbānīyah
       </i>
       ) officially the
       <b>
        Overseas Province of the Spanish Sahara
       </b>
       , was the name used for the modern territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Sahara" title="Western Sahara">
        Western Sahara
       </a>
       when it was occupied and ruled as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Territory_(country_subdivision)" title="Territory (country subdivision)">
        territory
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       between 1884 and 1975. It had been one of the most recent acquisitions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       , as well as one of its last remaining holdings, which had once extended from the Americas to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Philippines" title="The Philippines">
        the Philippines
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/East_Asia" title="East Asia">
        East Asia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spain gave up its Saharan possession following Moroccan demands and international pressure, mainly from
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       resolutions regarding
       <a href="/wiki/Decolonisation_of_Africa" title="Decolonisation of Africa">
        decolonisation
       </a>
       . There was internal pressure from the native
       <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_people" title="Sahrawi people">
        Sahrawi
       </a>
       population and the claims of
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">
        Mauritania
       </a>
       also claimed the territory for a number of years based on its history, and then dropped all claims.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1975 Morocco occupied much of the territory, but the
       <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">
        Polisario Front
       </a>
       , promoting sovereignty of an independent
       <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">
        Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic
       </a>
       (SADR), fought a guerrilla resistance for 16 years against Morocco. In 1991 the UN negotiated a ceasefire, and has tried to arrange negotiations and a referendum to let the population vote on its future. Morocco controls the entire Atlantic coast and most of the landmass, population, and natural resources of Western Sahara.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanish_occupation">
        Spanish occupation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Sahara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spanish occupation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Before and during the Spanish occupation (1884–1975), the territory was inhabited by Saharan
       <a href="/wiki/Arabs" title="Arabs">
        Arabs
       </a>
       who lived in many oases and coastal villages. They worked mainly in fishing and camel herding. They speak the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hassaniya" title="Hassaniya">
        Hassaniya
       </a>
       language, a Bedouin
       <a href="/wiki/Arabic" title="Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       dialect. There is some dispute and ambiguity about whether the territory was under Moroccan royal sovereignty at the time when the Spanish claimed it in 1884.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Berlin_Conference" title="Berlin Conference">
        Berlin Conference
       </a>
       , where the European powers were establishing the rules for setting up zones of influence or protection in Africa, Spain declared 'a
       <a href="/wiki/Protectorate" title="Protectorate">
        protectorate
       </a>
       of the African coast' from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Blanc" title="Cape Blanc">
        Cape Blanc
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Bojador" title="Cape Bojador">
        Cape Bojador
       </a>
       on 26 December 1884. It officially informed the other powers in writing on 14 January 1885.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rezette_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rezette-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It began establishing trading posts and a military presence. In July 1885,
       <a href="/wiki/Alfonso_XII_of_Spain" title="Alfonso XII of Spain">
        King Alfonso XII
       </a>
       appointed
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Emilio_Bonelli&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Emilio Bonelli (page does not exist)">
        Emilio Bonelli
       </a>
       as commissioner of the
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_Oro" title="Río de Oro">
        Río de Oro
       </a>
       with civil and military authority. On 6 April 1887, the area was incorporated into the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Captaincy_General_of_the_Canary_Islands&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Captaincy General of the Canary Islands (page does not exist)">
        Captaincy General of the Canary Islands
       </a>
       for military purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rezette_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rezette-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the summer of 1886, under the sponsorship of the Spanish Society of Commercial Geography (
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         Sociedad Española de Geografía Comercial
        </span>
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Julio_Cervera_Baviera" title="Julio Cervera Baviera">
        Julio Cervera Baviera
       </a>
       , Felipe Rizzo (1823–1908) and Francisco Quiroga (1853–1894) traversed the colony, which was called
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_Oro" title="Río de Oro">
        Río de Oro
       </a>
       , and made
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topographical" title="Topographical">
        topographical
       </a>
       and astronomical observations. At the time, geographers had not mapped the territory and its features were not widely known. Their trek is considered the first scientific expedition in that part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">
        Sahara
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1886, Spain signed the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Treaty_of_Idjil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Treaty of Idjil (page does not exist)">
        Treaty of Idjil
       </a>
       , by which the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Emirate_of_Adrar&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Emirate of Adrar (page does not exist)">
        Emirate of Adrar
       </a>
       ceded the land of the colony to Spain. This treaty was of no legal value, since the
       <a href="/wiki/Emir" title="Emir">
        Emir
       </a>
       of Adrar had no claim to the territory, but since Morocco had no claim either (at least since the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hispano-Moroccan_Treaty_of_1767&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hispano-Moroccan Treaty of 1767 (page does not exist)">
        Hispano-Moroccan Treaty of 1767
       </a>
       ), the Spanish 'invented' a claim which Adrar could, with no harm to itself, immediately cede.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rezette_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rezette-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The borders of the territory were not clearly defined until treaties between
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       in the early 20th century. Spanish Sahara was created from the Spanish territories of Río de Oro and
       <a href="/wiki/Saguia_el-Hamra" title="Saguia el-Hamra">
        Saguia el-Hamra
       </a>
       in 1924. It was not part of the areas known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Morocco
       </a>
       and was administered separately from them.
      </p>
      <p>
       On entering the territory in 1884, Spanish forces were immediately challenged by stiff resistance from the indigenous
       <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_people" title="Sahrawi people">
        Sahrawi
       </a>
       tribes. A 1904 rebellion led by the powerful
       <a href="/wiki/Smara" title="Smara">
        Smara
       </a>
       -based
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Marabout" title="Marabout">
         marabout
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shaykh" title="Shaykh">
        Shaykh
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ma_al-%27Aynayn" title="Ma al-'Aynayn">
        Ma al-'Aynayn
       </a>
       , was put down by
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       in 1910, which ruled neighbouring
       <a href="/wiki/French_Algeria" title="French Algeria">
        Algeria
       </a>
       . This was followed by a wave of uprisings under Ma al-Aynayn's sons, grandsons and other political leaders.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_history">
        Modern history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Sahara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modern history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Morocco_Protectorate.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="388" data-file-width="406" height="210" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Morocco_Protectorate.svg/220px-Morocco_Protectorate.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Morocco_Protectorate.svg/330px-Morocco_Protectorate.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Morocco_Protectorate.svg/440px-Morocco_Protectorate.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Morocco_Protectorate.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Spanish and French protectorates in Morocco and Spanish Sahara, 1912.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Given such tribal uprisings, Spain found it difficult to control parts of the territory's large hinterland until 1934. After gaining independence in 1956, Morocco laid claim to Spanish Sahara as part of its historic pre-colonial territory. In 1957, the
       <a href="/wiki/Moroccan_Army_of_Liberation" title="Moroccan Army of Liberation">
        Moroccan Army of Liberation
       </a>
       nearly occupied the small territory of
       <a href="/wiki/Ifni" title="Ifni">
        Ifni
       </a>
       , north of Spanish Sahara, during the
       <a href="/wiki/Ifni_War" title="Ifni War">
        Ifni War
       </a>
       . The Spanish sent a regiment of paratroopers from the nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       and repelled the attacks. With the assistance of the French, Spain soon re-established control in the area through
       <i>
        Operaciones Teide-Ecoubillon
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It tried to suppress resistance politically. It forced some of the previously
       <a href="/wiki/Nomad" title="Nomad">
        nomadic
       </a>
       inhabitants of Spanish Sahara to settle in certain areas, and the rate of urbanisation was increased. In 1958, Spain united the territories of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saguia_el_Hamra" title="Saguia el Hamra">
        Saguia el Hamra
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%ADo_de_Oro" title="Río de Oro">
        Río de Oro
       </a>
       to form the
       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_province" title="Overseas province">
        overseas province
       </a>
       of Spanish Sahara, while ceding the province of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Juby" title="Cape Juby">
        Cape Juby
       </a>
       Strip (which included
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Villa_Bens" title="Villa Bens">
        Villa Bens
       </a>
       ) in the same year to Morocco.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1960s, Morocco continued to claim Spanish Sahara. It gained agreement by the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       to add the territory to the list of territories to be decolonised. In 1969, Spain returned
       <a href="/wiki/Ifni" title="Ifni">
        Ifni
       </a>
       to Morocco, but continued to retain Spanish Sahara.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1967, Spanish rule was challenged by the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harakat_Tahrir" title="Harakat Tahrir">
         Harakat Tahrir
        </a>
       </i>
       , a protest movement secretly organised by the Royal Moroccan Government. Spain suppressed the 1970
       <a href="/wiki/Zemla_Intifada" title="Zemla Intifada">
        Zemla Intifada
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1973, the
       <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">
        Polisario Front
       </a>
       was formed in a revival of militant Sahrawi nationalism. The Front's
       <a href="/wiki/Guerrilla_warfare" title="Guerrilla warfare">
        guerrilla
       </a>
       army grew rapidly, and Spain lost effective control over most of the territory by early 1975. Its effort to found a political rival, the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partido_de_Uni%C3%B3n_Nacional_Saharaui" title="Partido de Unión Nacional Saharaui">
         Partido de Unión Nacional Saharaui
        </a>
       </i>
       (PUNS), met with little success. Spain proceeded to co-opt tribal leaders by setting up the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Djema%27a" title="Djema'a">
         Djema'a
        </a>
       </i>
       , a political institution loosely based on traditional Sahrawi tribal leaders. The
       <i>
        Djema'a
       </i>
       members were hand-picked by the authorities, but given privileges in return for rubber-stamping Madrid's decisions.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the winter of 1975, just before the death of its long-time leader
       <a href="/wiki/Generalissimo" title="Generalissimo">
        Generalissimo
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_Franco" title="Francisco Franco">
        Francisco Franco
       </a>
       , Spain was confronted with an intensive campaign of territorial demands from Morocco and, to a lesser extent, from
       <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">
        Mauritania
       </a>
       . These culminated in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Green_March" title="Green March">
         Marcha Verde
        </a>
       </i>
       ('Green March'). After negotiating the
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid_Accords" title="Madrid Accords">
        Madrid Accords
       </a>
       with Morocco and Mauritania, Spain withdrew its forces and settlers from the territory.
      </p>
      <p>
       Morocco and Mauritania took control of the region. Mauritania later surrendered its claim after fighting an unsuccessful war against the Polisario Front. Morocco began fighting against the Polisario Front; after sixteen years, the UN negotiated a cease-fire in 1991. Today, the sovereignty of the territory remains in dispute. Morocco has resisted allowing a referendum to be held, and has continued to offer its people jobs in controlled territory.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_status">
        Present status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_Sahara&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Present status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="582" data-file-width="500" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg/220px-Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg/330px-Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg/440px-Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Stamp_Spanish_Sahara_1924_40c.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Postage stamp issued in 1924.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       considers the former Spanish Sahara a
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_list_of_Non-Self-Governing_Territories" title="United Nations list of Non-Self-Governing Territories">
        non-self-governing territory
       </a>
       , with Spain as the former administrative power and, since the 1970s,
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       as the current administrative power.
      </p>
      <p>
       UN peace efforts have been directed at holding a
       <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">
        referendum
       </a>
       on independence among the Sahrawi population, but this has not yet taken place. The
       <a href="/wiki/African_Union" title="African Union">
        African Union
       </a>
       (AU) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreign_relations_of_Western_Sahara" title="Foreign relations of Western Sahara">
        more than 80 governments
       </a>
       consider the territory to be the sovereign (albeit occupied) state of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sahrawi_Arab_Democratic_Republic" title="Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic">
        Sahrawi Arab Democratic Republic
       </a>
       (SADR), with a government-in-exile backed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Polisario_Front" title="Polisario Front">
        Polisario Front
       </a>
       .
      </p>
