      <p>
       <b>
        Eratosthenes of
        <a href="/wiki/Cyrene,_Libya" title="Cyrene, Libya">
         Cyrene
        </a>
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ɛr/: 'err' in 'merry'">
            ɛr
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc">
        Ἐρατοσθένης ὁ Κυρηναῖος
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        IPA:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Greek" title="Help:IPA/Greek">
         [eratostʰénɛːs]
        </a>
       </span>
       ;
       <span class="nowrap">
        <abbr title="circa">
         c.
        </abbr>
        276 BC
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       –
       <span class="nowrap">
        c.
        <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
         195/194 BC
        </span>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mathematics" title="Greek mathematics">
        Greek mathematician
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Geographer" title="Geographer">
        geographer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Poet" title="Poet">
        poet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomer
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Music_theory" title="Music theory">
        music theorist
       </a>
       . He was a man of learning, becoming the chief librarian at the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria" title="Library of Alexandria">
        Library of Alexandria
       </a>
       . He invented the discipline of
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       , including the terminology used today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roller,_Duane_W_2010_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roller,_Duane_W_2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He is best known for being the first person to calculate the
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_geodesy#Hellenic_world" title="History of geodesy">
        circumference of the Earth
       </a>
       , which he did by comparing altitudes of the mid-day sun at two places a known North-South distance apart. His calculation was remarkably accurate. He was also the first to calculate the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt#Obliquity_of_the_ecliptic_(Earth's_axial_tilt)" title="Axial tilt">
        tilt of the Earth's axis
       </a>
       (again with remarkable accuracy). Additionally, he may have accurately calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        distance from the Earth to the Sun
       </a>
       and invented the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leap_day" title="Leap day">
        leap day
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He created the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_world_maps#Eratosthenes_.28276.E2.80.93194_BCE.29" title="Ancient world maps">
        map of the world
       </a>
       , incorporating
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        parallels
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        meridians
       </a>
       based on the available geographic knowledge of his era.
      </p>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes was the founder of scientific
       <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">
        chronology
       </a>
       ; he endeavored to revise the dates of the chief literary and political events from the conquest of
       <a href="/wiki/Troy" title="Troy">
        Troy
       </a>
       . Eratosthenes dated
       <a href="/wiki/Iliupersis" title="Iliupersis">
        The Sack of Troy
       </a>
       to 1183 BC. In
       <a href="/wiki/Number_theory" title="Number theory">
        number theory
       </a>
       , he introduced the
       <a href="/wiki/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes" title="Sieve of Eratosthenes">
        sieve of Eratosthenes
       </a>
       , an efficient method of identifying
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_number" title="Prime number">
        prime numbers
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He was a figure of influence in many fields. According to an entry
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Suda" title="Suda">
        Suda
       </a>
       (a 10th-century reference), his critics scorned him, calling him
       <i>
        Beta
       </i>
       (the second letter of the Greek alphabet) because he always came in second in all his endeavors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nonetheless, his devotees nicknamed him
       <i>
        Pentathlos
       </i>
       after the Olympians who were well rounded competitors, for he had proven himself to be knowledgeable in every area of learning. Eratosthenes yearned to understand the complexities of the entire world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_James_T_1998_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_James_T_1998-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Life">
        Life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The son of Aglaos, Eratosthenes was born in 276 BC in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cyrene_(city)" title="Cyrene (city)">
        Cyrene
       </a>
       . Now part of modern-day
       <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">
        Libya
       </a>
       , Cyrene had been founded by the Greeks centuries earlier and became the capital of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentapolis_(North_Africa)" title="Pentapolis (North Africa)">
        Pentapolis (North Africa)
       </a>
       , a country of five cities: Cyrene, Arsinoe,
       <a href="/wiki/Berenice" title="Berenice">
        Berenice
       </a>
       , Ptolemias, and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollonia,_Cyrenaica" title="Apollonia, Cyrenaica">
        Apollonia, Cyrenaica
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_the_Great" title="Alexander the Great">
        Alexander the Great
       </a>
       conquered Cyrene in 332 BC, and following his death in 323 BC, its rule was given to one of his generals,
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_I_Soter" title="Ptolemy I Soter">
        Ptolemy I Soter
       </a>
       , the founder of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemaic_Kingdom" title="Ptolemaic Kingdom">
        Ptolemaic Kingdom
       </a>
       . Under Ptolemaic rule the economy prospered, based largely on the export of horses and
       <a href="/wiki/Silphium" title="Silphium">
        silphium
       </a>
       , a plant used for rich seasoning and medicine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roller,_Duane_W_2010_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roller,_Duane_W_2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cyrene became a place of cultivation, where knowledge blossomed. Like any young Greek, Eratosthenes would have studied in the local
       <a href="/wiki/Gymnasium_(ancient_Greece)" title="Gymnasium (ancient Greece)">
        gymnasium
       </a>
       , where he would have learned physical skills and social discourse as well as reading, writing, arithmetic, poetry, and music.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes went to Athens to further his studies. There he was taught
       <a href="/wiki/Stoicism" title="Stoicism">
        Stoicism
       </a>
       by its founder,
       <a href="/wiki/Zeno_of_Citium" title="Zeno of Citium">
        Zeno of Citium
       </a>
       , in philosophical lectures on living a virtuous life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He then studied under
       <a href="/wiki/Aristo_of_Chios" title="Aristo of Chios">
        Aristo of Chios
       </a>
       , who led a more
       <a href="/wiki/Cynicism_(philosophy)" title="Cynicism (philosophy)">
        cynical
       </a>
       school of philosophy. He also studied under the head of the
       <a href="/wiki/Platonic_Academy" title="Platonic Academy">
        Platonic Academy
       </a>
       , who was
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcesilaus_of_Pitane" title="Arcesilaus of Pitane">
        Arcesilaus of Pitane
       </a>
       . His interest in Plato led him to write his very first work at a scholarly level,
       <i>
        Platonikos
       </i>
       , inquiring into the mathematical foundation of Plato's philosophies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eratosthenes was a man of many perspectives and investigated the art of poetry under
       <a href="/wiki/Callimachus" title="Callimachus">
        Callimachus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was a talented and imaginative poet. He wrote poems: one in
       <a href="/wiki/Hexameter" title="Hexameter">
        hexameters
       </a>
       called
       <i>
        Hermes
       </i>
       , illustrating the god's life history; and another, in
       <a href="/wiki/Elegiac" title="Elegiac">
        elegiacs
       </a>
       called
       <i>
        Erigone
       </i>
       , describing the suicide of the Athenian maiden
       <a href="/wiki/Erigone_(daughter_of_Icarius)" title="Erigone (daughter of Icarius)">
        Erigone (daughter of Icarius)
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He wrote
       <i>
        Chronographies
       </i>
       , a text that scientifically depicted dates of importance, beginning with the
       <a href="/wiki/Trojan_War" title="Trojan War">
        Trojan War
       </a>
       . This work was highly esteemed for its accuracy.
       <a href="/wiki/George_Syncellus" title="George Syncellus">
        George Syncellus
       </a>
       was later able to preserve from
       <i>
        Chronographies
       </i>
       a list of 38 kings of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Egyptian_Thebes" title="Egyptian Thebes">
        Egyptian Thebes
       </a>
       . Eratosthenes also wrote
       <i>
        Olympic Victors
       </i>
       , a chronology of the winners of the Olympic Games. It is not known when he wrote his works, but they highlighted his abilities.
      </p>
      <p>
       These works and his great poetic abilities led the pharaoh
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_III_Euergetes" title="Ptolemy III Euergetes">
        Ptolemy III Euergetes
       </a>
       to seek to place him as a librarian at the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Alexandria" title="Library of Alexandria">
        Library of Alexandria
       </a>
       in the year 245 BC. Eratosthenes, then thirty years old, accepted Ptolemy's invitation and traveled to Alexandria, where he lived for the rest of his life. Within about five years he became Chief Librarian, a position that the poet
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollonius_Rhodius" title="Apollonius Rhodius">
        Apollonius Rhodius
       </a>
       had previously held. As head of the library Eratosthenes tutored the children of Ptolemy, including
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy_IV_Philopator" title="Ptolemy IV Philopator">
        Ptolemy IV Philopator
       </a>
       who became the fourth Ptolemaic pharaoh. He expanded the library's holdings: in Alexandria all books had to be surrendered for duplication. It was said that these were copied so accurately that it was impossible to tell if the library had returned the original or the copy. He sought to maintain the reputation of the Library of Alexandria against competition from the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Pergamum" title="Library of Pergamum">
        Library of Pergamum
       </a>
       . Eratosthenes created a whole section devoted to the examination of Homer, and acquired original works of great tragic dramas of
       <a href="/wiki/Aeschylus" title="Aeschylus">
        Aeschylus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sophocles" title="Sophocles">
        Sophocles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Euripides" title="Euripides">
        Euripides
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes made several important contributions to
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematics" title="Mathematics">
        mathematics
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       , and was a friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Archimedes" title="Archimedes">
        Archimedes
       </a>
       . Around 255 BC, he invented the
       <a href="/wiki/Armillary_sphere" title="Armillary sphere">
        armillary sphere
       </a>
       . In
       <i>
        On the Circular Motions of the Celestial Bodies
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cleomedes" title="Cleomedes">
        Cleomedes
       </a>
       credited him with having calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator#Exact_length_of_the_Equator" title="Equator">
        Earth's circumference
       </a>
       around 240 BC, using knowledge of the angle of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_elevation_angle" title="Solar elevation angle">
        elevation
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       at noon on the
       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">
        summer solstice
       </a>
       in Alexandria and on
       <a href="/wiki/Elephantine" title="Elephantine">
        Elephantine
       </a>
       Island near
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syene" title="Syene">
        Syene
       </a>
       (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">
        Aswan
       </a>
       , Egypt).
      </p>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes believed there was good and bad in every nation and criticized
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       for arguing that humanity was divided into Greeks and
       <a href="/wiki/Barbarian" title="Barbarian">
        barbarians
       </a>
       , and that the Greeks should keep themselves racially pure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As he aged he contracted
       <a href="/wiki/Ophthalmia" title="Ophthalmia">
        ophthalmia
       </a>
       , becoming blind around 195 BC. Losing the ability to read and to observe nature plagued and depressed him, leading him to voluntarily starve himself to death. He died in 194 BC at 82 in Alexandria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Measurement_of_the_Earth.27s_circumference">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_of_the_Earth's_circumference">
        Measurement of the Earth's circumference
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Measurement of the Earth's circumference">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="341" data-file-width="512" height="200" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/300px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/450px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg/600px-Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eratosthenes_measure_of_Earth_circumference.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration showing a portion of the globe showing a part of the African continent. The sunbeams shown as two rays hitting the ground at Syene and Alexandria. Angle of sunbeam and the gnomons (vertical pole) is shown at Alexandria, which allowed Eratosthenes' estimates of radius and circumference of Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes calculated the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        circumference of the Earth
       </a>
       without leaving Egypt. He knew that at
       <a href="/wiki/Noon#Solar_noon" title="Noon">
        local noon
       </a>
       on the summer solstice in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syene" title="Syene">
        Syene
       </a>
       (modern
       <a href="/wiki/Aswan" title="Aswan">
        Aswan
       </a>
       , Egypt), the Sun was directly overhead. (Syene is at latitude 24°05′ North, near to the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
        Tropic of Cancer
       </a>
       , which was 23°42′ North in 100 BC
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) He knew this because the shadow of someone looking down a deep well at that time in Syene blocked the reflection of the Sun on the water. He measured the Sun's angle of elevation at noon on in Alexandria by using a vertical rod, known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Gnomon" title="Gnomon">
        gnomon
       </a>
       , and measuring the length of its shadow on the ground.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Using the length of the rod, and the length of the shadow, as the legs of a triangle, he calculated the angle of the sun's rays. This turned out to be about 7°, or 1/50th the circumference of a circle. Taking the Earth as spherical, and knowing both the distance and direction of Syene, he concluded that the Earth's circumference was fifty times that distance.
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li class="mw-empty-elt">
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       His knowledge of the size of Egypt was founded on the work of many generations of
       <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
        surveying
       </a>
       trips. Pharaonic bookkeepers gave a distance between Syene and Alexandria of 5,000 stadia (a figure that was checked yearly).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2017)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       say that the distance was corroborated by inquiring about the time that it took to travel from Syene to Alexandria by camel.
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Sagan" title="Carl Sagan">
        Carl Sagan
       </a>
       said that Eratosthenes
       <a class="external text" href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mUxmCXSmtVo&amp;t=3m15s" rel="nofollow">
        paid a man to walk and measure the distance
       </a>
       . Some claim Eratosthenes used the Olympic stade of 176.4 m, which would imply a circumference of 44,100 km, an error of 10%,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the 184.8 m Italian stade became (300 years later) the most commonly accepted value for the length of the stade,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which implies a circumference of 46,100 km, an error of 15%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was unlikely, even accounting for his extremely primitive measuring tools, that Eratosthenes could have calculated an accurate measurement for the circumference of the Earth. He made five important assumptions (none of which is perfectly accurate):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        That the distance between Alexandria and Syene was 5000 stadia,
       </li>
       <li>
        That Alexandria is due north of Syene
       </li>
       <li>
        That Syene lies on the
        <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Cancer" title="Tropic of Cancer">
         Tropic of Cancer
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        That the Earth is a perfect sphere.
       </li>
       <li>
        That light rays emanating from the Sun are parallel.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes later rounded the result to a final value of 700 stadia per degree, which implies a circumference of 252,000 stadia, likely for reasons of calculation simplicity as the larger number is evenly divisible by 60.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-stade_18-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-stade-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2012, Anthony Abreu Mora repeated Eratosthenes's calculation with more accurate data; the result was 40,074 km, which is 66 km different (0.16%) from the currently accepted polar
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_radius" title="Earth radius">
        circumference of the Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:6-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Seventeen hundred years after Eratosthenes's death, while
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       studied what Eratosthenes had written about the size of the Earth, he chose to believe, based on a map by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toscanelli" title="Toscanelli">
        Toscanelli
       </a>
       , that the Earth's circumference was one-third smaller. Had Columbus set sail knowing that Eratosthenes's larger circumference value was more accurate, he would have known that the place that he made landfall was not Asia, but rather the
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="Geography">
       </span>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id=".22Father_of_geography.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"Father_of_geography"'>
        "Father of geography"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: "Father of geography"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg">
         <img alt="Eratosthenes' map of the world (194 B.C.)" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="611" data-file-width="1040" height="129" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg/220px-Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg/330px-Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg/440px-Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mappa_di_Eratostene.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         19th-century reconstruction of Eratosthenes' map of the (for the Greeks) known world,
         <span class="nowrap">
          <a href="/wiki/Circa" title="Circa">
           c.
          </a>
          194 BC
         </span>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_geodesy" title="History of geodesy">
        History of geodesy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_longitude" title="History of longitude">
        history of longitude
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes now continued from his knowledge about the Earth. Using his discoveries and knowledge of its size and shape, he began to sketch it. In the Library of Alexandria he had access to various travel books, which contained various items of information and representations of the world that needed to be pieced together in some organized format.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In his three-volume work
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="grc">
         <i>
          Geographika
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ), he described and mapped his entire known world, even dividing the Earth into five climate zones:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       two freezing zones around the poles, two temperate zones, and a zone encompassing the equator and the tropics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had invented geography. He created terminology that is still used today. He placed grids of overlapping lines over the surface of the Earth. He used parallels and meridians to link together every place in the world. It was now possible to estimate one's distance from remote locations with this network over the surface of the Earth. In the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       the names of over 400 cities and their locations were shown: this had never been achieved before.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Roller,_Duane_W_2010_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Roller,_Duane_W_2010-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unfortunately, his
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       has been lost to history,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="Please clarify the following statement or statements with a good explanation from a reliable source. (September 2017)">
           how?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       but fragments of the work can be pieced together from other great historians like
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
        Pliny
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polybius" title="Polybius">
        Polybius
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
        Strabo
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marcianus" title="Marcianus">
        Marcianus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The first book was something of an introduction and gave a review of his predecessors, recognizing their contributions that he compiled in the library. In this book Eratosthenes denounced
        <a href="/wiki/Homer" title="Homer">
         Homer
        </a>
        as not providing any insight into what he now described as geography. His disapproval of Homer's topography angered many who believed the world depicted in the Odyssey to be legitimate.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers,_James_T_1998_7-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Chambers,_James_T_1998-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
        He also commented on the ideas of the nature and origin of the Earth: he had thought of Earth as an immovable globe; while on its surface was a place that was changing. He had hypothesized that at one time the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mediterranean" title="Mediterranean">
         Mediterranean
        </a>
        was a vast lake that covered the countries that surrounded it; and had only become connected to the ocean to the west when a passage had opened up sometime in its history.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the second book is his discovery about the circumference of the Earth. This is where, according to Pliny, "The world was grasped." Eratosthenes described his famous story of the well in Syene, described above. This book would now be considered a text on
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mathematical_geography" title="Mathematical geography">
         mathematical geography
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        His third book of the
        <i>
         Geography
        </i>
        contained
        <a href="/wiki/Political_geography" title="Political geography">
         political geography
        </a>
        . He cited countries and used parallel lines to divide the map into sections, to give accurate descriptions of the realms. This was a breakthrough, and can be considered the beginning of geography.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2005_25-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Smith2005-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Achievements">
        Achievements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Achievements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes was described by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suda_Lexicon" title="Suda Lexicon">
        Suda Lexicon
       </a>
       as a Πένταθλος (Pentathlos) which can be translated as "All-Rounder", for he was skilled in a variety of things: He was a true polymath. He was nicknamed Beta because he was great at many things and tried to get his hands on every bit of information but never achieved the highest rank in anything;
       <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
        Strabo
       </a>
       accounts Eratosthenes as a mathematician among geographers and a geographer among mathematicians.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eusebius_of_Caesarea" title="Eusebius of Caesarea">
         Eusebius of Caesarea
        </a>
        in his
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preparation_for_the_Gospel" title="Preparation for the Gospel">
          Preparatio Evangelica
         </a>
        </i>
        includes a brief chapter of three sentences on celestial distances (
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.tertullian.org/fathers/eusebius_pe_15_book15.htm" rel="nofollow">
         Book XV
        </a>
        , Chapter 53). He states simply that Eratosthenes found the distance to the Sun to be "
        <span lang="grc">
         σταδίων μυριάδας τετρακοσίας καὶ ὀκτωκισμυρίας
        </span>
        " (literally "of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stadia_(length)" title="Stadia (length)">
         stadia
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Myriad" title="Myriad">
         myriads
        </a>
        400 and 80,000") and the distance to the Moon to be 780,000 stadia. The expression for the distance to the Sun has been translated either as 4,080,000 stadia (1903 translation by E. H. Gifford), or as 804,000,000 stadia (edition of Edouard des Places, dated 1974–1991). The meaning depends on whether Eusebius meant 400 myriad plus 80,000 or "400 and 80,000" myriad. With a stade of 185 m, 804,000,000 stadia is 149,000,000 km, approximately the distance from the Earth to the Sun.
       </li>
       <li>
        Eratosthenes also calculated the Sun's diameter. According to Macrobious, Eratosthenes made the diameter of the Sun to be about 27 times that of the Earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith2005_25-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Smith2005-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The actual figure is approximately 109 times.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
         <a href="#cite_note-27">
          [27]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        During his time at the Library of Alexandria, Eratosthenes devised a calendar using his predictions about the
        <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
         ecliptic
        </a>
        of the Earth. He calculated that there are 365 days in a year and that every fourth year there would be 366 days.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
         <a href="#cite_note-28">
          [28]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        He was also very proud of his solution for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Doubling_the_Cube" title="Doubling the Cube">
         Doubling the Cube
        </a>
        . His motivation was that he wanted to produce catapults. Eratosthenes constructed a mechanical line drawing device to calculate the cube, called the mesolabio. He dedicated his solution to King Ptolemy, presenting a model in bronze with it a letter and an epigram.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
         <a href="#cite_note-29">
          [29]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Archimedes was Eratosthenes' friend and he, too, worked on the war instrument with mathematics. Archimedes dedicated his book
        <i>
         The Method
        </i>
        to Eratosthenes, knowing his love for learning and mathematics.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
         <a href="#cite_note-30">
          [30]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_numbers">
        Prime numbers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Prime numbers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:447px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sieve_of_Eratosthenes_animation.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="369" data-file-width="445" height="369" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes_animation.gif" width="445"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         Sieve of Eratosthenes: algorithm steps for primes below 121 (including optimization of starting from prime's square).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes" title="Sieve of Eratosthenes">
        Sieve of Eratosthenes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Primality_test" title="Primality test">
        primality test
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes proposed a simple
       <a href="/wiki/Algorithm" title="Algorithm">
        algorithm
       </a>
       for finding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_numbers" title="Prime numbers">
        prime numbers
       </a>
       . This algorithm is known in mathematics as the
       <a href="/wiki/Sieve_of_Eratosthenes" title="Sieve of Eratosthenes">
        Sieve of Eratosthenes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In mathematics, the sieve of Eratosthenes (Greek: κόσκινον Ἐρατοσθένους), one of a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Generating_primes#Prime_sieves" title="Generating primes">
        prime number sieves
       </a>
       , is a simple, ancient algorithm for finding all prime numbers up to any given limit. It does so by iteratively marking as composite,
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       , not prime, the multiples of each prime, starting with the multiples of 2. The multiples of a given prime are generated starting from that prime, as a sequence of numbers with the same difference, equal to that prime, between consecutive numbers. This is the sieve's key distinction from using trial division to sequentially test each candidate number for divisibility by each prime.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Works">
        Works
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Eratosthenes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Works">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Eratosthenes was one of the most pre-eminent scholarly figures of his time, and produced works covering a vast area of knowledge before and during his time at the Library. He wrote on many topics — geography, mathematics, philosophy, chronology, literary criticism, grammar, poetry, and even old comedies. Unfortunately, there are only fragments left of his works after the
       <a href="/wiki/Destruction_of_the_Library_of_Alexandria" title="Destruction of the Library of Alexandria">
        Destruction of the Library of Alexandria
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dicks1971_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Dicks1971-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
