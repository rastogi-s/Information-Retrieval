      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Phoenix Metropolitan Area
       </b>
       – often referred to as the
       <b>
        Valley of the Sun
       </b>
       , the
       <b>
        Salt River Valley
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Metro Phoenix
       </b>
       – is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_metropolitan_area" title="United States metropolitan area">
        metropolitan area
       </a>
       , centered on the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenix,_Arizona" title="Phoenix, Arizona">
        Phoenix
       </a>
       , that includes much of the central part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._State" title="U.S. State">
        U.S. State
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
        Arizona
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Census_Bureau" title="United States Census Bureau">
        United States Census Bureau
       </a>
       designates the area as the
       <b>
        Phoenix-Mesa-Scottsdale
       </b>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_Statistical_Area" title="Metropolitan Statistical Area">
        Metropolitan Statistical Area (MSA)
       </a>
       , defining it as
       <a href="/wiki/Maricopa_County,_Arizona" title="Maricopa County, Arizona">
        Maricopa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pinal_County,_Arizona" title="Pinal County, Arizona">
        Pinal
       </a>
       counties. As of the Census Bureau's 2015 population estimates, Metro Phoenix had 4,574,351 residents, making it the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_Metropolitan_Statistical_Areas#United_States" title="List of Metropolitan Statistical Areas">
        12th largest
       </a>
       Metropolitan Area in the nation by population. The
       <a href="/wiki/Gross_domestic_product" title="Gross domestic product">
        gross domestic product
       </a>
       of the Phoenix Metropolitan Area was $215 billion in 2014,
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_U.S._metropolitan_areas_by_GDP" title="List of U.S. metropolitan areas by GDP">
        15th
       </a>
       largest amongst metro areas in the United States.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is also one of the fastest growing major metropolitan areas, gaining nearly 400,000 residents from 2010 to 2015, and more than 1.3 million since 2000. The population of the Phoenix Metropolitan Area increased by 45.3% from 1990 through 2000, compared to the average United States rate of 13.2%, helping to make
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
        Arizona
       </a>
       the second fastest growing state in the nation in the 1990s (the fastest was
       <a href="/wiki/Nevada" title="Nevada">
        Nevada
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 2000 Census reported the population of the metropolitan area to be 3,251,876.
      </p>
      <p>
       As for the 2010 Census, the two-county metropolitan area was reported to have a population of 4,192,887. Metro Phoenix grew by 941,011 people from April 2000 to April 2010, making it one of the fastest growing metro areas in the country. This also contributed to the entire state's exceptional growth, as the area is home to just over two-thirds of Arizona's population.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As of 2010, the Phoenix Metropolitan area consists of Maricopa and Pinal counties, comprising a total area of about 14,600 square miles. Because of the size of counties in Arizona, even though Maricopa and Pinal counties together contain nearly 4.5 million people, most of the area is uninhabited, which gives the MSA an extremely low density compared to other major MSAs in the nation.
      </p>
      <p>
       The average elevation is about 2,000 feet (610 m), with the highest point being 2,704 feet (824 m).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Phoenix Metropolitan area is notable for its warm, desert climate. On average, the area receives about 9 inches of rain annually, with less than 1 inch of snow every decade. In total, the region will see about 32 days of measurable precipitation each year. The MSA is one of the sunniest major metropolitan areas, receiving 295 days of sunshine, compared to the national average of 205. The average July high is about 104 °F (40 °C), with the average January low being about 37 °F (3 °C), still above freezing. Bestplaces gives the Phoenix Metropolitan Area a comfort index
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of 44/100, which is also the national average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Economy">
        Economy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Economy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Phoenix Metropolitan area has historically been the center of the state's economy. As with the state of Arizona, the area relied on the 5 C's (copper, cattle, climate, citrus, and cotton) for its economic growth and expansion. However, after World War II, the area entered the manufacturing industry, which spurred the growth of what would eventually be one of the largest urban areas in the nation. Currently, the two largest industries are manufacturing and tourism. About 10 million people visit from other States and Canada each year, due to the area's mild winters and long, sunny days. The technology and service industries currently account for almost 77% of total employment in the region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As well as a strong tourism industry, the Phoenix area has a significant business sector. Several Fortune 500 and Fortune 1000 companies have their international headquarters in the area, including
       <a href="/wiki/Avnet" title="Avnet">
        Avnet
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PetSmart_Inc." title="PetSmart Inc.">
        PetSmart
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Insight_Technologies&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Insight Technologies (page does not exist)">
        Insight Technologies
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Education_Group" title="Apollo Education Group">
        Apollo Education Group
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sprouts_Farmers_Market" title="Sprouts Farmers Market">
        Sprouts Farmers Market
       </a>
       . Other Fortune 500 companies with significant presence include
       <a href="/wiki/Banner_Health" title="Banner Health">
        Banner Health
       </a>
       , the state's largest private employer,
       <a href="/wiki/American_Airlines" title="American Airlines">
        American Airlines
       </a>
       , which merged with Tempe-based
       <a href="/wiki/US_Airways" title="US Airways">
        US Airways
       </a>
       , American Express, Wells Fargo, Boeing, and
       <a href="/wiki/Intel" title="Intel">
        Intel
       </a>
       , which has a large regional campus in Chandler.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Metropolitan Area ranks 5th in the nation in economic growth, which is a major comeback from the recession.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The unemployment rate of the area is 5.3%, lower than the national rate of 6.3%. It also has slightly higher recent job growth (1.99% compared to 1.18%) and higher projected job growth (38.7% compared to 36.1%). Although the area has significantly higher sales tax rates compared with the nation as a whole (8.3% to 6%), income tax rates are lower than the national average (3.36% to 4.72%). The largest occupation by population is in the office/administrative sector, comprising more than a quarter of all jobs in the region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arterial_roads">
        Arterial roads
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Arterial roads">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:287px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="905" data-file-width="1616" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png/285px-Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png/428px-Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png/570px-Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png 2x" width="285"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Road_Map_of_Central_Phoenix.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Arterial streets in Central Phoenix
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on arterial roads in the metro area, see
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_metropolitan_area_arterial_roads" title="Phoenix metropolitan area arterial roads">
        Phoenix metropolitan area arterial roads
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       Most of the arterial roads in the Phoenix metropolitan area are laid out on a regular
       <a href="/wiki/Grid_plan" title="Grid plan">
        grid
       </a>
       , following the
       <a href="/wiki/Section_line" title="Section line">
        section lines
       </a>
       established in the
       <a href="/wiki/Public_Land_Survey_System" title="Public Land Survey System">
        Public Land Survey System
       </a>
       . As a result, arterial roads in cities that had once been geographically separate may have been given different names while occupying the same section line. When these roads were extended to accommodate the growth in the area they eventually merged into a single road while the previous segments retained their existing names. This results in several cases of a road abruptly changing names; for example, Dunlap Avenue in Phoenix becomes Olive Avenue west of 43rd Avenue, in Glendale.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another quirk of a grid system based upon the Public Land Survey System is due to the occasional corrections in the grid caused by the curvature of the earth. This results in arterial roadways deviating slightly from a straight line, as can be seen in many locations where roads abruptly curve either just north or just south of Baseline Road to follow a new section line.
      </p>
      <p>
       The majority of the cities in the metropolitan area, as well as unincorporated areas in Maricopa County, observe the
       <a href="/wiki/House_numbering" title="House numbering">
        addressing system
       </a>
       employed by the City of Phoenix. A number of cities, however, retain their own addressing systems with differing reference points, creating the potential for multiple instances of a house number being found on the same named road.
      </p>
      <p>
       In terms of numbering systems, some roads that continue through multiple cities will switch numbering conventions several times. A drive eastward along Broadway Road, for example, will pass through Goodyear, Avondale, Phoenix, Tempe, Mesa, and Apache Junction, each with their own reference point for address numbering. Though the street does not curve, the direction changes from west to east in each city and back again when moving from one city to the next, causing considerable overlap in numbers.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Street_numbering_systems">
        Street numbering systems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most communities in Maricopa County use the Phoenix-County numbering system, with the point of origin at Central Avenue and Washington Street. In the Phoenix-County system, north-south numbered roads labeled "avenue", "drive", and "lane" are located West of Central Avenue, while those labeled "street", "place" and "way" are located east of Central Avenue. Starting with 579th Avenue
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the west near Tonopah the avenues count down with approximately 8 numbers per mile to 19th Avenue and count up again to from 16th Street to 228th Street
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       near Queen Creek in the east. They go, in order from west to east (although not all necessarily exist):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        2nd Avenue – 1st Dale – 1st Glen – 1st Lane – 1st Drive – 1st Avenue
       </li>
       <li>
        Central Avenue
       </li>
       <li>
        1st Street – 1st Place – 1st Way – 1st Terrace – 1st Run – 2nd Street
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This has been a source of confusion for some newcomers, who might end up, for example, at 91st
       <i>
        Avenue
       </i>
       and Thunderbird Road, when in fact they intended to go to 91st
       <i>
        Street
       </i>
       and Thunderbird Road, between 30 minutes and an hour away from one another depending on traffic.
      </p>
      <p>
       One beneficial quality of this arrangement for unfamiliar travelers is that the major north-south
       <a href="/wiki/Arterial_road" title="Arterial road">
        arterial roads
       </a>
       are rarely similarly named; the "avenue" arterials in the West Valley are all odd-numbered and the "street" arterials in the East Valley are even-numbered, with the exception of 7th Ave. &amp; 7th St., both being major roadways running parallel and each one-half mile from Central Ave.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rail">
        Rail
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Rail">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">
        Amtrak
       </a>
       's Los Angeles-New Orleans
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sunset_Limited" title="Sunset Limited">
         Sunset Limited
        </a>
       </i>
       served Phoenix from 1971 until it was rerouted on June 2, 1996, to a more southerly route between
       <a href="/wiki/Tucson,_Arizona" title="Tucson, Arizona">
        Tucson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Yuma,_Arizona" title="Yuma, Arizona">
        Yuma, Arizona
       </a>
       , in order to accommodate the
       <a href="/wiki/Union_Pacific_Railroad" title="Union Pacific Railroad">
        Union Pacific Railroad
       </a>
       's desire to abandon a portion of its Phoenix-to-Yuma "West Line." This made Phoenix one of the largest cities in the nation without direct passenger service. The designated
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maricopa_(Amtrak_station)" title="Maricopa (Amtrak station)">
        Phoenix-area stop
       </a>
       is in
       <a href="/wiki/Maricopa,_Arizona" title="Maricopa, Arizona">
        Maricopa
       </a>
       , about 40 miles south of downtown Phoenix.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Valley_Metro_Rail" title="Valley Metro Rail">
        light rail system
       </a>
       (dubbed the "METRO Light Rail," or "Worm") runs more than 20 miles from suburban Mesa, through Tempe and into Phoenix, traveling through the downtown area, offering access to Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport and linking two of the four metro area campuses of
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona_State_University" title="Arizona State University">
        Arizona State University
       </a>
       . The light rail began public operation on December 27, 2008, and it was projected to initially accommodate 26,000 boardings a day, or more than 8 million boardings in its first year. The Valley Metro Rail boardings has experienced constant growth since the beginning. In the year 2012, the light rail boarded just over 14 million people.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many expansions to the METRO system are currently in the early planning stages, and two are under construction. The Central Mesa extension project, which extends the Main Street line 2½ miles from its current terminus at Sycamore to Mesa Drive in Downtown Mesa, has finished construction and opened on August 22, 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Northwest rail project opened March 2016. The project extended the 19th Avenue track from its former terminus at Montebello Ave to Dunlap Avenue, 3 miles north.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many more extensions are funded, with further projects being studied for feasibility.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aviation">
        Aviation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoenix_metropolitan_area&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Aviation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In 2010,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Sky_Harbor_International_Airport" title="Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport">
        Phoenix Sky Harbor International Airport
       </a>
       was the 24th busiest passenger facility in the world and the 10th busiest in the United States, with more than 38 million passengers using the facility. With three terminal buildings encompassing 120 gates, more than 20 airlines offer daily non-stop flights to destinations throughout the world.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenix-Mesa_Gateway_Airport" title="Phoenix-Mesa Gateway Airport">
        Phoenix-Mesa Gateway Airport
       </a>
       started commercial passenger flights in 2004. The airport currently provides service to 39 destinations.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several municipal and regional airports in the metropolitan area; however, none of them are currently used by commercial airlines for passenger flights. They include, but are not limited to,
       <a href="/wiki/Glendale_Municipal_Airport" title="Glendale Municipal Airport">
        Glendale Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Deer_Valley_Airport" title="Phoenix Deer Valley Airport">
        Phoenix Deer Valley Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Phoenix_Goodyear_Airport" title="Phoenix Goodyear Airport">
        Phoenix Goodyear Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Scottsdale_Airport" title="Scottsdale Airport">
        Scottsdale Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Falcon_Field_(Arizona)" title="Falcon Field (Arizona)">
        Falcon Field
       </a>
       , Chandler Airport,
       <a href="/wiki/Buckeye_Municipal_Airport" title="Buckeye Municipal Airport">
        Buckeye Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phoenix_Regional_Airport" title="Phoenix Regional Airport">
        Phoenix Regional Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pleasant_Valley_Airport" title="Pleasant Valley Airport">
        Pleasant Valley Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Estrella_Sailport" title="Estrella Sailport">
        Estrella Sailport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_Airpark" title="Stellar Airpark">
        Stellar Airpark
       </a>
       , Skyranch at Carefree,
       <a href="/wiki/Gila_River_Memorial_Airport" title="Gila River Memorial Airport">
        Gila River Memorial Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pegasus_Airpark" title="Pegasus Airpark">
        Pegasus Airpark
       </a>
