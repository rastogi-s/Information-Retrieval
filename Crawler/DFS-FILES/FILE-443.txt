      <p>
       <b>
        Spanish grammar
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        español, castellano
       </i>
       ), which is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       that originated in north central Spain and is spoken today throughout Spain, some twenty countries in the Americas, and
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish is an
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflected
       </a>
       language. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verbs" title="Verbs">
        verbs
       </a>
       are potentially marked for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
        tense
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        aspect
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        mood
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       (resulting in some fifty
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_conjugation" title="Grammatical conjugation">
        conjugated
       </a>
       forms per verb). The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nouns" title="Nouns">
        nouns
       </a>
       form a two-
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       system and are marked for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . Pronouns can be inflected for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       (including a residual neuter), and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       , although the Spanish pronominal system represents a simplification of the ancestral Latin system.
      </p>

        </a>

        </div>

      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish was the first of the European vernaculars to have a grammar treatise,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Gram%C3%A1tica_de_la_lengua_castellana" title="Gramática de la lengua castellana">
         Gramática de la lengua castellana
        </a>
       </i>
       , written in 1492 by the
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusia" title="Andalusia">
        Andalusian
       </a>
       philologist
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">
        Antonio de Nebrija
       </a>
       and presented to Queen
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella of Castile
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Salamanca" title="Salamanca">
        Salamanca
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       (RAE, Royal Spanish Academy) traditionally dictates the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        normative
       </a>
       rules of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       , as well as its
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_orthography" title="Spanish orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Formal differences between
       <a href="/wiki/Peninsular_Spanish" title="Peninsular Spanish">
        Peninsular
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Americas" title="Spanish language in the Americas">
        American Spanish
       </a>
       are remarkably few, and someone who has learned the dialect of one area will generally have no difficulties using formal speech in the other; however, pronunciation does vary, as well as grammar and vocabulary.
      </p>
      <p>
       Recently published comprehensive Spanish reference grammars in English include
       <a href="#CITEREFDeBruyne1996">
        DeBruyne (1996)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="#CITEREFButtBenjamin2011">
        Butt &amp; Benjamin (2011)
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="#CITEREFBatchelorSan_José2010">
        Batchelor &amp; San José (2010)
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbs">
        Verbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Verbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_verbs" title="Spanish verbs">
        Spanish verbs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_conjugation" title="Spanish conjugation">
        Spanish conjugation
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_irregular_verbs" title="Spanish irregular verbs">
        Spanish irregular verbs
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Every Spanish verb belongs to one of three form classes, characterized by the
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       ending:
       <i>
        -ar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        -ir
       </i>
       —sometimes called the first, second, and third conjugations, respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       A Spanish verb has nine
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicative_mood" title="Indicative mood">
        indicative
       </a>
       tenses with more-or-less direct English equivalents: the
       <a href="/wiki/Present_tense" title="Present tense">
        present tense
       </a>
       ('I walk'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
        preterite
       </a>
       ('I walked'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">
        imperfect
       </a>
       ('I was walking' or 'I used to walk'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       ('I have walked'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Perfect_(grammar)" title="Perfect (grammar)">
        past perfect
       </a>
       — also called the
       <a href="/wiki/Pluperfect" title="Pluperfect">
        pluperfect
       </a>
       ('I had walked'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Future_tense" title="Future tense">
        future
       </a>
       ('I will walk'), the future perfect ('I will have walked'), the
       <a href="/wiki/Conditional_mood" title="Conditional mood">
        conditional simple
       </a>
       ('I would walk') and the conditional perfect ('I would have walked').
      </p>
      <p>
       In most dialects, each tense has six potential forms, varying for first, second, or third
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       and for singular or plural
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       . In the second person, Spanish maintains the so-called "
       <a href="/wiki/T%E2%80%93V_distinction" title="T–V distinction">
        T–V distinction
       </a>
       " between familiar and formal modes of address. The formal second-person pronouns (
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       ) take third-person verb forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second-person familiar plural is expressed in most of Spain with the pronoun
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       and its characteristic verb forms (e.g.
       <i>
        coméis
       </i>
       'you [pl.] eat'), while in Latin American Spanish it merges with the formal second-person plural (e.g.
       <i>
        ustedes comen
       </i>
       ). Thus
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is used as both the formal and familiar second-person pronoun in Latin America.
      </p>
      <p>
       In many areas of Latin America (especially Central America and southern South America), the second-person familiar singular pronoun
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       is replaced by
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       , which frequently requires its own characteristic verb forms, especially in the present indicative, where the endings are
       <i>
        -ás
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -és
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        -ís
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        -ar
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -ir
       </i>
       verbs, respectively. See "
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       In the tables of paradigms below, the (optional) subject pronouns appear in parentheses.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_indicative">
        Present indicative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Present indicative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The present indicative is used to express actions or states of being in a present time frame. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Yo
        <i>
         soy
        </i>
        alto (I
        <i>
         am
        </i>
        tall).
       </li>
       <li>
        Ella
        <i>
         canta
        </i>
        en el club (She
        <i>
         sings
        </i>
        in the club).
       </li>
       <li>
        Todos nosotros
        <i>
         vivimos
        </i>
        en un submarino amarillo (We all
        <i>
         live
        </i>
        in a yellow submarine).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Son
        </i>
        las diez y media ([It]
        <i>
         is
        </i>
        ten thirty).
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Past_tenses">
        Past tenses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Past tenses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish has a number of verb tenses used to express actions or states of being in a past time frame. The two that are "simple" in form (formed with a single word, rather than being
       <a href="/wiki/Compound_verb" title="Compound verb">
        compound verbs
       </a>
       ) are the
       <a href="/wiki/Preterite" title="Preterite">
        preterite
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">
        imperfect
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Preterite">
        Preterite
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Preterite">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The preterite is used to express actions or events that took place in the past, and which were instantaneous or are viewed as completed. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Ella se murió ayer (She died yesterday)
       </li>
       <li>
        Pablo apagó las luces (Pablo turned the lights off)
       </li>
       <li>
        Yo me comí el arroz (I ate the rice)
       </li>
       <li>
        Te cortaste el pelo (You had your hair cut)
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h4>
       <span id="Imperfect_or_.22copret.C3.A9rito.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Imperfect_or_"copretérito"'>
        Imperfect or "copretérito"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: Imperfect or "copretérito"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The imperfect expresses actions or states that are viewed as ongoing in the past. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Yo era cómico en el pasado (I was comical in the past)
       </li>
       <li>
        Usted comía mucho (You ate a lot – literally, this sentence is saying "You used to eat a lot", saying that in the past, the person being referred had a characteristic of "eating a lot")
       </li>
       <li>
        Ellos escuchaban la radio (They were listening to the radio)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       All three of the sentences above describe "non-instantaneous" actions that are viewed as continuing in the past. The characteristic in the first sentence and the action in the second were continuous, not instantaneous occurrences. In the third sentence, the speaker focuses on the action in progress, not on its beginning or end.

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Using_preterite_and_imperfect_together">
        Using preterite and imperfect together
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Using preterite and imperfect together">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The preterite and the imperfect can be combined in the same sentence to express the occurrence of an event in one clause during an action or state expressed in another clause. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ellos escuchaban la radio cuando oyeron un ruido afuera.
        </i>
        (They were listening to the radio when they heard a noise outside.)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo estaba en mi cuarto cuando usted entró.
        </i>
        (I was in my room when you came in.)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Era un día muy tranquilo cuando eso pasó.
        </i>
        (It was a very peaceful day when that happened.)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In all three cases, an event or completed action interrupts an ongoing state or action. For example, in the second sentence, the speaker states that he was in his room (expressed through the imperfect to reflect the ongoing or unfinished state of being there) when the other person "interrupted" that state by entering (expressed through the preterite to suggest a completed action).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_progressive_and_imperfect_progressive">
        Present progressive and imperfect progressive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Present progressive and imperfect progressive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The present and imperfect progressive both are used to express ongoing, progressive action in the present and past, respectively. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estoy haciendo mi tarea
        </i>
        . (I am doing my homework)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estamos estudiando
        </i>
        . (We are studying)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Estaba escuchando la radio
        </i>
        . (I was listening to the radio)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Él estaba limpiando su cuarto
        </i>
        . (He was cleaning his room)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The present progressive is formed by first conjugating the verb
       <i>
        estar
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        seguir
       </i>
       , depending on context, to the subject, and then attaching a gerund of the verb that follows. The past (imperfect) progressive simply requires the estar or seguir to be conjugated, depending on context, in imperfect, with respect to the subject.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Forming_gerunds">
        Forming gerunds
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </h4>

       </i>

       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hablando
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        caminando
       </i>
       . For
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -ir verbs
       </i>
       , replace the
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       or
       <i>

       </i>

       <i>
        -iendo
       </i>
       ; e.g.
       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>
       ,
       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>
        -iendo
       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subjunctive">
        Subjunctive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Subjunctive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The subjunctive of a verb is used to express certain connotations in sentences such as a wish or desire, a demand, an emotion, uncertainty, or doubt.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_subjunctive">
        Present subjunctive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Present subjunctive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Normally, a verb would be conjugated in the present indicative to indicate an event in the present frame of time.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Yo
        <b>
         soy
        </b>
        muy ambicioso (I
        <b>
         am
        </b>
        very ambitious)
       </li>
       <li>
        Marta
        <b>
         trae
        </b>
        la comida (Marta
        <b>
         brings
        </b>
        the food)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If the sentence expresses a desire, demand, or emotion, or something similar, in the present tense, the subjunctive is used.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Quiero que
        <b>
         seas
        </b>
        muy ambicioso (I want you to
        <b>
         be
        </b>
        very ambitious—literally, I want
        <i>
         that you be
        </i>
        very ambitious)
       </li>
       <li>
        Me alegro de que Marta
        <b>
         traiga
        </b>
        la comida (I am happy that Marta
        <b>
         brings
        </b>
        the food)
       </li>
       <li>
        Es una lástima que
        <b>
         llegues
        </b>
        tarde (It is a shame that you
        <b>
         arrive
        </b>
        late)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The subjunctive is also used to convey doubt, denial, or uncertainty.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Busco un amigo que
        <i>
         sea
        </i>
        simpático (I search for a friend who will
        <i>
         be
        </i>
        likable or I search for a likable friend)
       </li>
       <li>
        No hay ningún autor que lo
        <i>
         escriba
        </i>
        . (There are no authors who
        <i>
         write
        </i>
        that.)
       </li>
       <li>
        Es posible que ella
        <i>
         sepa
        </i>
        mucho (It is possible that she
        <i>
         knows
        </i>
        a lot.)
       </li>
       <li>
        No parece que
        <i>
         tengan
        </i>
        mucho dinero (It does not seem that they
        <i>
         have
        </i>
        much money.)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the first two examples, the ideally likable friend has not yet been found and remains an uncertainty, and authors "who write that" are not known to exist. In the third, possibility is not certainty, but rather a conjecture, and the last expresses clear doubt. Thus, subjunctive is used. Some of the phrases and verbs that require sentences to have subjunctive formation include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Dudar, negar, esperar, alegrarse de, temer, sentir, pedir, aconsejar, exigir, desear, querer, mandar
       </li>
       <li>
        Es necesario que, conviene que, no parece que, es dudoso que, es probable que, no creo que, importa que, parece mentira que
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some phrases that require the indicative instead, because they express certainty, include:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Es verdad que, es obvio que, es seguro que, parece que, es evidente que, creo que
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       To form the first-person singular subjunctive, first take the present indicative first-person singular ('yo') form of a verb. For example, the verbs hablar, comer, and vivir (To talk, to eat, to live) → Yo hablo, yo como, yo vivo. Then, replace the ending 'o' with the "opposite ending". This is done in the following way: if the verb is an -er or -ir verb such as comer, poder, vivir, or compartir, replace the ending 'o' with an 'a' i.e. : Yo como; yo puedo; yo vivo → Yo coma; yo pueda; yo viva. If the verb is an -ar verb such as hablar or caminar replace the ending 'o' with an 'e' i.e. : Yo hablo; yo camino → Yo hable, yo camine. This forms the first-person conjugation. The other conjugations work similarly, as follows:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Yo
        </b>
        → Yo hable; yo coma; yo viva
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Tú
        </b>
        → Tú hables; tú comas; tú vivas
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Él/Ella/Usted
        </b>
        → Él hable; él coma; él viva
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Nosotros
        </b>
        → Nosotros hablemos; nosotros comamos; nosotros vivamos
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vosotros
        </b>
        → Vosotros habléis; vosotros comáis; vosotros viváis
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Ellos/Ellas/Ustedes
        </b>
        → ellos hablen; ellos coman; ellos vivan
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Since the
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       forms are derived from
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       the following would be expected (and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_American_Spanish" title="Central American Spanish">
        used in Central America
       </a>
       )
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vos
        </b>
        → Vos hablés; vos comás; vos escribás
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Spanish_Academy" title="Royal Spanish Academy">
        Royal Spanish Academy
       </a>
       , following
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Argentinian usage
       </a>
       , recommends using the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       forms:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Vos
        </b>
        → Vos hables;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        vos comas;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        vos escribas
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Imperfect_subjunctive">
        Imperfect subjunctive
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Imperfect subjunctive">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Today, the two forms of the imperfect subjunctive are largely interchangeable.* The "-se" form derives (as in most Romance languages) from the Latin pluperfect subjunctive, while the "-ra" form derives from the Latin pluperfect indicative. The use of one or the other is largely a matter of personal taste and dialect. Many only use the
       <i>
        -ra
       </i>
       forms in speech, but vary between the two in writing. Many may spontaneously use either, or even prefer the rarer
       <i>
        -se
       </i>
       forms. The imperfect subjunctive is formed for basically the same reasons as the present subjunctive, but is used for other tenses and time frames.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">
        Nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_nouns" title="Spanish nouns">
        Spanish nouns
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender_in_Spanish" title="Grammatical gender in Spanish">
        Grammatical gender in Spanish
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish has nouns of two genders, masculine and feminine. It has been argued that this exemplifies the most difficult type of problem for second language learners (specifically, speakers of those languages in which this phenomenon does not exist, like English).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">
        Adjectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Adjectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_adjectives" title="Spanish adjectives">
        Spanish adjectives
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish generally uses adjectives in a similar way to English and most other Indo-European languages. However, there are three key differences between English and Spanish adjectives.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In Spanish, adjectives usually go
        <i>
         after
        </i>
        the noun they modify. The exception is when the writer/speaker is being slightly emphatic, or even poetic, about a particular quality of an object (rather than the mundane use of using the quality to specify which particular object they are referring to).
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Mi casa roja
          </i>
          could either mean that there are many red houses in the world but I wish to talk about the one that I happen to own, or that I have many houses but am referring to the red one.
          <i>
           Mi casa roja
          </i>
          = My house, the red one.
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           Mi roja casa
          </i>
          means that I am stressing how red my particular house is (probably the only house I have).
          <i>
           Mi roja casa
          </i>
          = My house, which is obviously red.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        In Spanish, adjectives
        <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
         agree
        </a>
        with what they refer to in terms of both number (singular/plural) and gender (masculine/feminine). For example,
        <i>
         taza
        </i>
        (cup) is feminine, so "the red cup" is
        <i>
         la taza roj
         <u>
          a
         </u>
        </i>
        , but
        <i>
         vaso
        </i>
        (glass) is masculine, so "the red glass" is
        <i>
         el vaso roj
         <u>
          o
         </u>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In Spanish, it is perfectly normal to let an adjective stand in for a noun or pronoun—with (where people are involved) no implication of condescension or rudeness. For example,
        <i>
         los altos
        </i>
        means "the tall ones" or "the tall men".
        <i>
         El grande
        </i>
        means "the big one" or "the big man".
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Determiners">
        Determiners
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Determiners">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_determiners" title="Spanish determiners">
        Spanish determiners
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish uses
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Determiner_(class)" title="Determiner (class)">
        determiners
       </a>
       in a similar way to English. The main difference is that they inflect for both number (singular/plural) and gender (masculine/feminine). Common determiners include
       <i>
        el
       </i>
       ("the"),
       <i>
        un
       </i>
       ("a"),
       <i>
        este
       </i>
       ("this"),
       <i>
        mucho
       </i>
       ("much, a lot"),
       <i>
        alguno
       </i>
       ("some"), and so on.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pronouns">
        Pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_pronouns" title="Spanish pronouns">
        Spanish pronouns
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish has a range of pronouns that in some ways work quite differently from English ones. They include:
       <i>
        yo, tú, vos, usted, él, ella, ello, nosotros, vosotros, ustedes, ellos, ellas, esto, eso, aquello
       </i>
       etc. Personal pronouns are usually omitted due to context, but it is not rare to see one in written text or in the spoken language, whether it be for emphasis or in cases where there may be some confusion between conjugations.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adverbs">
        Adverbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Adverbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish adverbs work much like their English counterparts, e.g.
       <i>
        muy
       </i>
       ("very"),
       <i>
        poco
       </i>
       ("a little"),
       <i>
        lejos
       </i>
       ("far"),
       <i>
        mucho
       </i>
       ("much, a lot"),
       <i>
        casi
       </i>
       ("almost"), etc. To form adverbs from adjectives, the adverbial suffix
       <i>
        -mente
       </i>
       is generally added to the feminine singular of the adjective, whether or not it differs from the masculine singular. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         claro
        </i>
        ("clear", m. sg.) →
        <i>
         clara
        </i>
        (f. sg.) →
        <i>
         claramente
        </i>
        ("clearly")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         rápido
        </i>
        ("fast, rapid", m. sg.) →
        <i>
         rápida
        </i>
        (f. sg.) →
        <i>
         rápidamente
        </i>
        ("fast, quickly, rapidly")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         natural
        </i>
        ("natural", m. &amp;. f. sg.) →
        <i>
         naturalmente
        </i>
        ("naturally")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         triste
        </i>
        ("sad", m. &amp; f. sg.) →
        <i>
         tristemente
        </i>
        ("sadly")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         audaz
        </i>
        ("bold", m. &amp; f. sg.) →
        <i>
         audazmente
        </i>
        ("boldly")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The adjectives
       <i>
        bueno
       </i>
       ("good") and
       <i>
        malo
       </i>
       ("bad") have irregular adverbial forms:
       <i>
        bien
       </i>
       ("well") and
       <i>
        mal
       </i>
       ("badly"), respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       As in English, some adverbs are identical to their adjectival counterparts. Thus words such as
       <i>
        temprano
       </i>
       ("early"),
       <i>
        lento
       </i>
       ("slow"), and
       <i>
        hondo
       </i>
       ("deep") can also mean "early" (as in English, as in "He arrived early") "slowly", and "deeply", respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       In series of consecutive adverbs that would each end in
       <i>
        -mente
       </i>
       on their own, the
       <i>
        -mente
       </i>
       is dropped from all but the last one, and the others are left as if they were adjectives in the feminine singular. Thus:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         rápida y fácilmente
        </i>
        = "quickly and easily"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lenta, cuidosa, y duchamente
        </i>
        = "slowly, carefully, and skillfully"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         parcial o completamente
        </i>
        = "partially or completely"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There are also a wide variety of adverbial phrases in Spanish, such as
       <i>
        a menudo
       </i>
       ("often"),
       <i>
        en todas partes
       </i>
       ("everywhere"),
       <i>
        de repente
       </i>
       ("suddenly"),
       <i>
        por fin
       </i>
       ("finally"), and
       <i>
        sin embargo
       </i>
       ("however, nevertheless").
      </p>
      <p>
       As with adjectives, the
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative" title="Comparative">
        comparative
       </a>
       of adverbs is generally formed by placing
       <i>
        más
       </i>
       ("more") or
       <i>
        menos
       </i>
       ("less") before the adverb. Thus
       <i>
        más temprano
       </i>
       ("earlier"),
       <i>
        más rápidamente
       </i>
       ("faster, quicker, more quickly"),
       <i>
        menos interesantemente
       </i>
       ("less interestingly"), etc. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Superlative" title="Superlative">
        superlative
       </a>
       is generally formed by placing the neuter article
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       before the comparative, although it is usually used with an additional qualifier phrase such as
       <i>
        que puedas
       </i>
       ("that you can") or
       <i>
        de todos
       </i>
       ("of all"):
       <i>
        lo más rápidamente que puedas
       </i>
       ("as quickly as you can", lit. "the most quickly that you can"),
       <i>
        lo más interesantemente de todos
       </i>
       ("most interestingly of all"),
       <i>
        lo menos claramente de ellos
       </i>
       ("the least clearly of them"), etc. As with their corresponding adjectival forms,
       <i>
        bien
       </i>
       ("well") and
       <i>
        mal
       </i>
       ("badly") have irregular comparative forms (
       <i>
        mejor
       </i>
       ("better") and
       <i>
        peor
       </i>
       ("worse"), respectively), and
       <i>
        más
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        menos
       </i>
       are the comparatives of
       <i>
        mucho
       </i>
       ("much, a lot") and
       <i>
        poco
       </i>
       ("a little"), respectively.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prepositions">
        Prepositions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Prepositions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_prepositions" title="Spanish prepositions">
        Spanish prepositions
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Spanish has a relatively large number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       , and does not use
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postposition" title="Postposition">
        postpositions
       </a>
       . The following list is traditionally cited:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        <i>
         A, ante, bajo, cabe, con, contra, de, desde, en, entre, hacia, hasta, para, por, según, sin, so, sobre, tras.
        </i>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Recently, two new prepositions have been added: "durante" and "mediante", usually placed at the end to preserve the list (which is usually learnt by heart by Spanish students).
      </p>
      <p>
       This list includes two archaic prepositions (
       <i>
        so
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        cabe
       </i>
       ), but leaves out two new Latinisms (
       <i>
        vía
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        pro
       </i>
       ) as well as a large number of very important compound prepositions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Prepositions in Spanish do not change a verb's meaning as they do in English. For example, to translate "run out of water", "run up a bill", "run down a pedestrian", and "run in a thief" into Spanish requires completely different verbs, and not simply the use of "correr" ("run") plus the corresponding Spanish preposition. This is more due to the nature of English
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phrasal_verbs" title="Phrasal verbs">
        phrasal verbs
       </a>
       rather than an inherent function of Spanish verbs or prepositions.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conjunctions">
        Conjunctions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Conjunctions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spanish conjunctions
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       ('and') and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       ('or') alter their form in both spoken and written language to
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       respectively when followed by an identical vowel sound. Thus,
       <i>
        padre
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        hijo
       </i>
       ('father and son'),
       <i>
        Fernando
        <b>
         e
        </b>
        Isabel
       </i>
       ('Ferdinand and Isabella'),
       <i>
        sujeto
        <b>
         u
        </b>
        objeto
       </i>
       ('subject or object'),
       <i>
        vertical
        <b>
         u
        </b>
        horizontal
       </i>
       ('vertical or horizontal').
      </p>
      <p>
       The change does not take place before the
       <i>
        (h)i
       </i>
       of a diphthong, as in
       <i>
        acero
        <b>
         y
        </b>
        hierro
       </i>
       ('steel and iron'). Nor does the conjunction
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       change when initial in a question (where it serves to introduce or reintroduce a name as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topic%E2%80%93comment" title="Topic–comment">
        topic
       </a>
       , rather than to link one element with another), as in
       <i>
        ¿
        <b>
         Y
        </b>
        Inés?
       </i>
       ('What about Inés?').
      </p>
      <p>
       When the conjunction
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       appears between numerals, it is usually spelled with an accent mark (
       <i>
        ó
       </i>
       ), in order to distinguish it from zero (0); thus,
       <i>
        2
        <b>
         ó
        </b>
        3
       </i>
       ('2 or 3') in contrast to
       <i>
        203
       </i>
       ('two-hundred three').
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Syntax_and_syntactic_variation">
        Syntax and syntactic variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Syntax and syntactic variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Order_of_constituents">
        Order of constituents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Order of constituents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanish unmarked word order for affirmative declarative sentences is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subject-verb-object" title="Subject-verb-object">
        subject-verb-object
       </a>
       (SVO); however, as in other Romance languages, in practice, word order is more variable, with
       <a href="/wiki/Topicalization" title="Topicalization">
        topicalization
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Focus_(linguistics)" title="Focus (linguistics)">
        focus
       </a>
       being the primary factors in the selection of a particular order.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verb-subject-object" title="Verb-subject-object">
        Verb-subject-object
       </a>
       (VSO),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Verb-object-subject" title="Verb-object-subject">
        verb-object-subject
       </a>
       (VOS), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Object-verb-subject" title="Object-verb-subject">
        object-verb-subject
       </a>
       (OVS) are also relatively common, while other orders are very uncommon outside of poetry.
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus, to simply say, "My friend wrote the book", one would say (SVO):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Mi amigo escribió el libro
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Although bare VSO and VOS are somewhat rare in declarative independent clauses, they are quite common in sentences in which something other than the subject or direct object functions as the focus. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Hace pocos años escribió mi amigo un libro
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Hace pocos años escribió un libro mi amigo
        </i>
        = "A few years ago, my friend wrote a book"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ayer vio mi madre a mi amigo y le preguntó por su libro
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         Ayer vio a mi amigo mi madre y le preguntó por su libro
        </i>
        = "Yesterday, my mother saw my friend and asked him about his book"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dependent_clauses" title="Dependent clauses">
        dependent clauses
       </a>
       , the verb is placed before the subject (and thus often VSO or VOS) to avoid placing the verb in final position:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Este es el libro que
         <b>
          escribió
         </b>
         mi amigo
        </i>
        , but rarely
        <i>
         Este es el libro que mi amigo escribió
        </i>
        = "This is the book that my friend wrote"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A sentence in which the direct object is the
       <a href="/wiki/Topic_and_comment" title="Topic and comment">
        topic
       </a>
       or "theme" (old information), while the subject is part of the comment, or "rheme" (new information), often assumes OVS order. In this case the direct object noun phrase is supplemented with the appropriate direct object pronoun; for example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         El libro lo escribió mi amigo
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Because subject pronouns are
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-drop_language" title="Pro-drop language">
        often dropped
       </a>
       , one will often see sentences with a verb and direct object but no explicitly stated subject.
      </p>
      <p>
       In questions, VSO is usual (though not obligatory):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¿Escribió mi amigo el libro?
        </i>
        = "Did my friend write the book?"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Yes/no questions, regardless of constituent order, are generally distinguished from declarative sentences by context and intonation.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cleft_sentences">
        Cleft sentences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Cleft sentences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clefting" title="Clefting">
        cleft sentence
       </a>
       is one formed with the copular verb (generally with a
       <a href="/wiki/Dummy_pronoun" title="Dummy pronoun">
        dummy pronoun
       </a>
       like "it" as its subject), plus a word that "cleaves" the sentence, plus a subordinate clause. They are often used to put emphasis on a part of the sentence. Here are some examples of English sentences and their cleft versions:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "I did it." → "It was I who did it" or colloquially "It was me that did it."
       </li>
       <li>
        "You will stop smoking through willpower." → "It is through willpower that you will stop smoking."
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Spanish does not usually employ such a structure in simple sentences. The translations of sentences like these can be readily analyzed as being normal sentences containing relative pronouns. Spanish is capable of expressing such concepts without a special cleft structure thanks to its flexible word order.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, if we translate a cleft sentence such as "It was Juan who lost the keys", we get
       <i>
        Fue Juan el que perdió las llaves.
       </i>
       Whereas the English sentence uses a special structure, the Spanish one does not. The verb
       <i>
        fue
       </i>
       has no dummy subject, and the pronoun
       <i>
        el que
       </i>
       is not a cleaver but a nominalising relative pronoun meaning "the [male] one that". Provided we respect the parings of "el que" and "las llaves", we can play with the word order of the Spanish sentence without affecting its structure – although each permutation would, to a native speaker, give a subtly different shading of emphasis.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, we can say
       <i>
        Juan fue el que perdió las llaves
       </i>
       ("Juan was the one who lost the keys") or
       <i>
        El que perdió las llaves fue Juan
       </i>
       ("The one who lost the keys was Juan"). As can be seen from the translations, if this word order is chosen, English stops using the cleft structure (there is no more dummy "it" and a nominalising relative is used instead of the cleaving word) whilst in Spanish no words have changed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Here are some examples of such sentences:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Fue Juan
         <u>
          el que
         </u>
         perdió las llaves
        </i>
        = "It was John who lost the keys"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Son sólo tres días
         <u>
          los que
         </u>
         te quedan
        </i>
        = "It is only three days that you have left"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Seré yo
         <u>
          quien
         </u>
         se lo diga
        </i>
        = "It will be I who tells him"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Son pocos
         <u>
          los que
         </u>
         vienen y se quedan
        </i>
        = lit. "There are few who come and stay"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Note that it is ungrammatical to try to use just
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       to cleave such sentences as in English, but using
       <i>
        quien
       </i>
       in singular or
       <i>
        quienes
       </i>
       in plural is grammatical.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         *Fue Juan
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         perdió las llaves
        </i>
        (incorrect)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Fue Juan
         <u>
          quien
         </u>
         perdió las llaves
        </i>
        (correct)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       When prepositions come into play, things become complicated. Structures unambiguously identifiable as cleft sentences are used. The verb
       <i>
        ser
       </i>
       introduces the stressed element and then there is a nominaliser. Both of these are preceded by the relevant preposition. For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Fue
         <u>
          a mí a quien
         </u>
         le dio permiso
        </i>
        = "It was me to whom he gave permission", lit. "It was to me to whom he gave permission"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es
         <u>
          para nosotros para quienes
         </u>
         se hizo esto
        </i>
        = "It is us for whom this was made", lit. "It is for us for whom this was made"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es
         <u>
          por eso por lo que
         </u>
         lo hice
        </i>
        = "That is why I did it", more literally: "It is because of that that I did it", or completely literally: "It is because of that because of which I did it"
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es así
         <u>
          como
         </u>
         se debe hacer
        </i>
        = "It is this way that it must be done", lit. "It is this way how it must be done" (
        <i>
         como
        </i>
        replaces longer expressions such as
        <i>
         la forma en que
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This structure is quite wordy, and is therefore often avoided by not using a cleft sentence at all. Emphasis is conveyed just by word order and stressing with the voice (indicated here within bolding):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Me dio permiso
         <u>
          a
          <b>
           mí
          </b>
         </u>
        </i>
        = "He gave permission to
        <b>
         me
        </b>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se hizo esto
         <u>
          para
          <b>
           nosotros
          </b>
         </u>
        </i>
        = "This was done for
        <b>
         us
        </b>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <u>
          Por
          <b>
           eso
          </b>
         </u>
         lo hice
        </i>
        = "I did it because of
        <b>
         that
        </b>
        "
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Se debe hacer
         <u>
          <b>
           así
          </b>
         </u>
        </i>
        = "It must be done
        <b>
         this
        </b>
        way"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In casual speech, the complex cleaving pronoun is often reduced to
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , just as it is reduced to "that" in English. Foreign learners are advised to avoid this.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es para nosotros
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         se hizo esto
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es por eso
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         lo hice
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Fue a mí
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         le dio permiso
        </i>
        (preferred:
        <i>
         <b>
          a quien
         </b>
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Es así
         <u>
          que
         </u>
         se debe hacer
        </i>
        (preferred:
        <i>
         <b>
          como
         </b>
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the singular, the subordinate clause can agree either with the relative pronoun or with the subject of the main sentence, though the latter is seldom used. However, in the plural, only agreement with the subject of the main sentence is acceptable. Therefore:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Singular
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo fui el que
         <u>
          me lo bebí
         </u>
        </i>
        = "I was the one who drank it" (agreement with subject of main sentence)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Yo fui el que
         <u>
          se lo bebió
         </u>
        </i>
        (preferred form with same meaning, agreement with
        <i>
         el que
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La que lo
         <u>
          sé
         </u>
         soy yo
        </i>
        = "I am the one who knows" (agreement with subject of main sentence)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         La que lo
         <u>
          sabe
         </u>
         soy yo
        </i>
        = (preferred form with same meaning, agreement with
        <i>
         la que
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        Plural
       </dt>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Somos los únicos que no
         <u>
          tenemos
         </u>
         ni un centavo para apostar
        </i>
        = "We are the only ones who do not have even a cent to bet" (agreement with subject of main sentence) (from dialogue of the
        <a href="/wiki/Gabriel_Garc%C3%ADa_M%C3%A1rquez" title="Gabriel García Márquez">
         Gabriel García Márquez
        </a>
        novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/No_One_Writes_to_the_Colonel" title="No One Writes to the Colonel">
          El coronel no tiene quien le escriba
         </a>
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vosotras sois las que lo
         <u>
          sabéis
         </u>
        </i>
        = "You girls are the ones who know" (agreement with subject of main sentence)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dialectal_variations">
        Dialectal variations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Dialectal variations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_address">
        Forms of address
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Forms of address">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The use of
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       as a polite form of address is universal. However, there are variations in informal address.
       <i>
        Ustedes
       </i>
       replaces
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       in much of Andalusia, the Canary Islands and Latin America, except in the liturgical or poetic of styles. In some parts of Andalusia, the pronoun
       <i>
        ustedes
       </i>
       is used with the standard
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       endings.
      </p>
      <p>
       Depending on the region, Latin Americans may also replace the singular
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       with
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       . The choice of pronoun is a tricky issue and can even vary from village to village. Travellers are often advised to play it safe and call everyone
       <i>
        usted
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A feature of the speech of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Republic" title="Dominican Republic">
        Dominican Republic
       </a>
       and other areas where syllable-final /s/ is completely silent is that there is no audible difference between the second and third person singular form of the verb. This leads to redundant pronoun use, for example, the tagging on of
       <i>
        ¿tú ves?
       </i>
       (pronounced
       <i>
        tuvé
       </i>
       ) to the ends of sentences, where other speakers would say
       <i>
        ¿ves?
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voseo">
        <i>
         Voseo
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Voseo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        Voseo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Vos
       </i>
       was used in medieval Castilian as a polite form, like the French
       <i>
        vous
       </i>
       and the Italian
       <i>
        voi
       </i>
       , and it used the same forms as
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       . This gave three levels of formality:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Tú quieres
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vos queréis
        </i>
        (originally
        <i>
         queredes
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Vuestra merced quiere
        </i>
        (today
        <i>
         usted
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Whereas
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       was lost in standard Spanish, some dialects lost
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       , and began using
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       as the informal pronoun. The exact connotations of this practice, called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       , depend on the dialect. In certain countries there may be socioeconomic implications. El
       <i>
        voseo
       </i>
       uses the pronoun
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       , but maintains
       <i>
        te
       </i>
       as an object pronoun and
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        tuyo
       </i>
       as possessives.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
         voseo
        </a>
       </i>
       , verbs corresponding to
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       in the present indicative (roughly equivalent to the English simple present), are formed from the second person plural (the form for
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       ). If the second person plural ends in
       <b>
        áis
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        éis
       </b>
       , the form for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       drops the
       <b>
        i
       </b>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Vosotros habláis – vos hablás
       </li>
       <li>
        Vosotros tenéis – vos tenés
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Similarly the verb
       <b>
        ser
       </b>
       (to be) has:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Vosotros sois – vos sos
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       If the second person plural ends in
       <b>
        -ís
       </b>
       (with an accent on the
       <b>
        í
       </b>
       ), then the form for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is identical:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Vosotros vivís – vos vivís
       </li>
       <li>
        Vosotros oís – vos oís
       </li>
       <li>
        Vosotros huís – vos huís
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the imperative, the form for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       is also derived from the second person plural. The latter ends always in
       <b>
        -d
       </b>
       . So for the form for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       this
       <b>
        d
       </b>
       is removed, and if the verb has more than one syllable, an accent is added to the last vowel:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Tened (vosotros) – tené (vos)
       </li>
       <li>
        Dad (vosotros) – da (vos)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The only exception to these rules is in the verb
       <b>
        ir
       </b>
       (to go), which does not have an imperative form for
       <b>
        vos
       </b>
       and uses the analogous form of the verb
       <b>
        andar
       </b>
       , which has a similar meaning, and is regular:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Andad – andá
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the present subjunctive, the same rules as for the present indicative apply, though these forms coexist in Argentina with those for the pronoun
       <b>
        tú
       </b>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Que vosotros digáis – que vos digás
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Or:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Que tú digas – que vos digas
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Other tenses always have the same form for
       <b>
        vos
       </b>
       as for
       <b>
        tú
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Outside Argentina, other combinations are possible. For instance, people in
       <a href="/wiki/Maracaibo" title="Maracaibo">
        Maracaibo
       </a>
       may use standard
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       endings for
       <i>
        vos
       </i>
       <i>
        (vos habláis, que vos habléis)
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vosotros_imperative:_-ar_for_-ad">
        <i>
         Vosotros
        </i>
        imperative:
        <i>
         -ar
        </i>
        for
        <i>
         -ad
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Vosotros imperative: -ar for -ad">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In Spain, colloquially, the infinitive is used instead of the normative imperative for
       <i>
        vosotros
       </i>
       . This is not accepted in the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        normative
       </a>
       language.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡Venir!
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ¡Venid!
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡Callaros!
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ¡Callaos!
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         ¡Callarse!
        </i>
        in some dialects)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ¡Iros!
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         ¡Marcharos!
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ¡Idos!
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span id="Non-normative_-s_on_t.C3.BA_form">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-normative_-s_on_tú_form">
        Non-normative
        <i>
         -s
        </i>
        on
        <i>
         tú
        </i>
        form
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Non-normative -s on tú form">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A form used for centuries but never accepted normatively has an
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       ending in the second person singular of the preterite or simple past. For example,
       <i>
        lo hicistes
       </i>
       instead of the normative
       <i>
        lo hiciste
       </i>
       ;
       <i>
        hablastes tú
       </i>
       for
       <i>
        hablaste tú
       </i>
       . That is the only instance in which the
       <i>
        tú
       </i>
       form does not end in an
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistic_prescription" title="Linguistic prescription">
        normative
       </a>
       language.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Ladino
       </a>
       has gone further with
       <i>
        hablates
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Third-person_object_pronoun_variation">
        Third-person object pronoun variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Third-person object pronoun variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The third-person direct-object and indirect-object pronouns exhibit variation from region to region, from one individual to another, and even within the language of single individuals. The Real Academia Española prefers an "etymological" usage, one in which the indirect object function is carried by
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       (regardless of gender), and the direct object function is carried by
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       (according to the gender of the antecedent, and regardless of its animacy).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Academy also condones the use of
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       as a direct object form for masculine, animate antecedents (i.e. male humans). Deviations from these approved usages are named
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Le%C3%ADsmo" title="Leísmo">
         leísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       (for the use of
       <i>
        le
       </i>
       as a direct object), and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La%C3%ADsmo" title="Laísmo">
         laísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lo%C3%ADsmo" title="Loísmo">
         loísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       (for the use of
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        lo
       </i>
       as indirect objects). The object pronoun variation is studied in detail by
       <a href="#CITEREFGarcíaOtheguy1977">
        García &amp; Otheguy (1977)
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Here are some examples for this:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Leísmo
        </b>
        :
        <i>
         Le miraron
        </i>
        (They saw him/her/it).
        <u>
         Normative
        </u>
        :
        <i>
         lo miraron
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         la miraron
        </i>
        depending on the gender of the object.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Laísmo
        </b>
        :
        <i>
         La dijeron que se callara
        </i>
        (They told her to shut up).
        <u>
         Normative
        </u>
        :
        <i>
         Le dijeron que se callara
        </i>
        . The person who is told something is an indirect object in Spanish, and the substituting pronoun is the same for both genders.
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Loísmo
        </b>
        :
        <i>
         Lo dijeron que se callara
        </i>
        (They told him to shut up).
        <u>
         Normative
        </u>
        :
        <i>
         Le dijeron que se callara
        </i>
        . See above.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Que.C3.ADsmo_and_deque.C3.ADsmo">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Queísmo_and_dequeísmo">
        <i>
         Queísmo
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         dequeísmo
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish_grammar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Queísmo and dequeísmo">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Dependent_clause" title="Dependent clause">
        Noun clauses
       </a>
       in Spanish are typically introduced by the
       <a href="/wiki/Complementizer" title="Complementizer">
        complementizer
       </a>
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , and such a noun clause may serve as the object of the preposition
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       , resulting in the sequence
       <i>
        de que
       </i>
       in the standard language. This sequence, in turn, is often reduced colloquially to just
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       , and this reduction is called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Que%C3%ADsmo" title="Queísmo">
         queísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some speakers, by way of
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        hypercorrection
       </a>
       (i.e. in an apparent effort to avoid the "error" of
       <i>
        queísmo
       </i>
       ), insert
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       before
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       in contexts where it is not prescribed in standard grammar. This insertion of "extraneous"
       <i>
        de
       </i>
       before
       <i>
        que
       </i>
       — called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Deque%C3%ADsmo" title="Dequeísmo">
         dequeísmo
        </a>
       </i>
       — is generally associated with less-educated speakers.
      </p>
