      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        antipode
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) of any spot on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodal_point" title="Antipodal point">
        point
       </a>
       on Earth's surface
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diametrically
       </a>
       opposite to it; the
       <b>
        antipodes
       </b>
       of a region similarly represent the area opposite it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A pair of points
       <i>
        antipodal
       </i>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/əl/: 'le' in 'bottle'">
            əl
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) to each other are situated such that a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straight_line" title="Straight line">
        straight line
       </a>
       connecting the two would pass through Earth's center. Such points are as far away from each other as possible, a
       <a href="/wiki/Great-circle_distance" title="Great-circle distance">
        great-circle distance
       </a>
       of 10,800 nautical miles (20,000 km).
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       , "the Antipodes" may be used to refer to
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       , and "Antipodeans" to their inhabitants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geographically, the antipodes of Britain and Ireland are in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific Ocean
       </a>
       , south of New Zealand. This gave rise to the name of the
       <a href="/wiki/Antipodes_Islands" title="Antipodes Islands">
        Antipodes Islands
       </a>
       of New Zealand, which are close to the antipode of London at about
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Antipodes&amp;params=50_S_179_E_">
         <span class="geo-default">
          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           <span class="latitude">
            50°S
           </span>
           <span class="longitude">
            179°E
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          ﻿ / ﻿
         </span>
         <span class="geo-nondefault">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           50°S 179°E
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
           ﻿ /
           <span class="geo">
            -50; 179
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       . The antipodes of Australia are in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">
        North Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       , while parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       are antipodal to New Zealand.
      </p>
      <p>
       Approximately 15%
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="non-RS give lower values (May 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       of land territory is antipodal to other land, representing approximately 4.4% of the Earth's surface. The largest antipodal land masses are the
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_Archipelago" title="Malay Archipelago">
        Malay Archipelago
       </a>
       , antipodal to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon_Basin" title="Amazon Basin">
        Amazon Basin
       </a>
       and adjoining
       <a href="/wiki/Andes" title="Andes">
        Andean
       </a>
       ranges; east
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
        Mongolia
       </a>
       , antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Arctic_Archipelago" title="Canadian Arctic Archipelago">
        Canadian Arctic Archipelago
       </a>
       , antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/East_Antarctica" title="East Antarctica">
        East Antarctica
       </a>
       . There is a general paucity of antipodal land because the Southern hemisphere has fairly little land, and of that, the antipodes of Australia are in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic_Ocean" title="North Atlantic Ocean">
        North Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       , while the antipodes of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       are in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific Ocean
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The antipode of any place on the Earth is the place that is diametrically opposite it, so a line drawn from the one to the other passes through the centre of the Earth and forms a true
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EB1911-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the antipodes of New Zealand's lower North Island lie in Spain. Most of the Earth's land surfaces have ocean at their antipodes, this being a consequence of most land being in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Land_hemisphere" title="Land hemisphere">
        land hemisphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The singular
       <i>
        antipode
       </i>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Back-formation" title="Back-formation">
        back-formation
       </a>
       (c. 1540-50 CE) from the plural
       <i>
        antipodes
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       is the plural of the singular
       <i>
        antipous
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The antipode of any place on Earth is distant from it by 180° of
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       and as many degrees to the north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       as the original is to the south (or vice versa); in other words, the
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitudes
       </a>
       are numerically equal, but one is north and the other south.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EB1911_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-EB1911-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The maps shown here are based on this relationship; they show a
       <a href="/wiki/Lambert_azimuthal_equal-area_projection" title="Lambert azimuthal equal-area projection">
        Lambert azimuthal equal-area projection
       </a>
       of the Earth, in yellow, overlaid on which is another map, in blue, shifted horizontally by 180° of longitude and inverted about the Equator with respect to latitude.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        Noon
       </a>
       at one place is
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">
        midnight
       </a>
       at the other (ignoring
       <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">
        daylight saving time
       </a>
       and irregularly shaped
       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">
        time zones
       </a>
       ) and, with the exception of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        tropics
       </a>
       , the longest day at one point corresponds to the shortest day at the other, and
       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">
        midwinter
       </a>
       at one point coincides with
       <a href="/wiki/Midsummer" title="Midsummer">
        midsummer
       </a>
       at the other. Sunrise and sunset do not quite oppose each other at antipodes due to refraction of sunlight.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mathematical_description">
        Mathematical description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mathematical description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       If the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate" title="Geographic coordinate">
        geographic coordinates
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       ) of a point on the Earth's surface are (
       <i>
        φ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        θ
       </i>
       ), then the coordinates of the antipodal point are (−
       <i>
        φ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        θ
       </i>
       ± 180°). This relation holds true whether the Earth is approximated as a perfect
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       or as a
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In terms of the usual way these geographic coordinates are given, this transformation can be expressed symbolically as
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ° N/S
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        ° E/W
        <big>
         ↦
        </big>
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ° S/N (180 −
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        )° W/E,
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       that is, for the latitude (the North/South coordinate) the magnitude of the angle remains the same but N is changed to S and vice versa, and for the longitude (the East/West coordinate) the angle is replaced by its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supplementary_angle" title="Supplementary angle">
        supplementary angle
       </a>
       while E is exchanged for W. For example, the antipode of the point in China at
       <span class="nowrap">
        37° N 119° E
       </span>
       (a few hundred kilometres from
       <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
        Beijing
       </a>
       ) is the point in Argentina at
       <span class="nowrap">
        37° S 61° W
       </span>
       (a few hundred kilometres from
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        antipodes
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek:
       </a>
       ἀντίποδες (antípodes),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       plural of ἀντίπους (antipous), "with feet opposite (ours)",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from ἀντί (antí, “opposite”) + πούς (
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%BF%CF%8D%CF%82" title="wikt:πούς">
        poús
       </a>
       , “foot”). The Greek word is attested in
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       's dialogue
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue)" title="Timaeus (dialogue)">
         Timaeus
        </a>
       </i>
       , already referring to a spherical Earth, explaining the relativity of the terms "above" and "below":
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        For if there were any solid body in equipoise at the centre of the universe, there would be nothing to draw it to this extreme rather than to that, for they are all perfectly similar; and if a person were to go round the world in a circle, he would often, when standing at the antipodes of his former position, speak of the same point as above and below; for, as I was saying just now, to speak of the whole which is in the form of a globe as having one part above and another below is not like a sensible man.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Plato
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
          <a href="#cite_note-8">
           [7]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The term is taken up by
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        De caelo
       </i>
       308a.20),
       <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
        Strabo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
        Plutarch
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diogenes_Laertius" title="Diogenes Laertius">
        Diogenes Laertius
       </a>
       , and was adopted into
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        antipodes
       </i>
       . The Latin word changed its sense from the original "under the feet, opposite side" to "those with the feet opposite", i.e. a
       <a href="/wiki/Bahuvrihi" title="Bahuvrihi">
        bahuvrihi
       </a>
       referring to hypothetical people living on the opposite side of the Earth. Medieval illustrations imagine them in some way "inverted", with their feet growing out of their heads, pointing upward.
      </p>
      <p>
       In this sense,
       <i>
        Antipodes
       </i>
       first entered
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       in 1398 in a translation of the 13th century
       <i>
        De Proprietatibus Rerum
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Bartholomeus_Anglicus" title="Bartholomeus Anglicus">
        Bartholomeus Anglicus
       </a>
       , translated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_of_Trevisa" title="John of Trevisa">
        John of Trevisa
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Yonde in Ethiopia ben the Antipodes, men that haue theyr fete ayenst our fete.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       (
       <i>
        In Modern English: Yonder in Ethiopia are the Antipodes, men that have their feet against our feet.
       </i>
       )
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_significance">
        Historical significance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Historical significance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pomponius_Mela" title="Pomponius Mela">
        Pomponius Mela
       </a>
       , the first Roman geographer, asserted that the earth had two habitable zones, a North and South one, but that it would be impossible to get into contact with each other because of the unbearable heat at the Equator.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:182px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1224" data-file-width="1224" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png/180px-Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png/270px-Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png/360px-Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png 2x" width="180"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Crates_Terrestrial_Sphere.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Terrestrial Sphere of
         <a href="/wiki/Crates_of_Mallus" title="Crates of Mallus">
          Crates of Mallus
         </a>
         (c. 150 B.C.). Note the region of the antipodes in the southern half of the western hemisphere.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the time of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St_Augustine" title="St Augustine">
        St Augustine
       </a>
       , the Christian church was skeptical of the notion. Augustine asserted that "it is too absurd to say that some men might have set sail from this side and, traversing the immense expanse of ocean, have propagated there a race of human beings descended from that one first man."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Early_Middle_Ages" title="Early Middle Ages">
        Early Middle Ages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Isidore_of_Seville" title="Isidore of Seville">
        Isidore of Seville
       </a>
       's widely read
       <a href="/wiki/Etymologiae" title="Etymologiae">
        encyclopedia
       </a>
       presented the term "antipodes" as referring to
       <a href="/wiki/Antichthones" title="Antichthones">
        antichthones
       </a>
       (people who lived on the opposite side of the Earth), as well as to a geographical place; these people came to play a role in medieval discussions about the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flat_earth#Early_Middle_Ages" title="Flat earth">
        shape of the Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 748, in reply to a letter from
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Boniface" title="Saint Boniface">
        Saint Boniface
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pope_Zachary" title="Pope Zachary">
        Pope Zachary
       </a>
       declared the belief "that beneath the earth there was another world and other men, another sun and moon" to be heretical. In his letter, Boniface had apparently maintained that
       <a href="/wiki/Vergilius_of_Salzburg" title="Vergilius of Salzburg">
        Vergilius of Salzburg
       </a>
       held such a belief.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The antipodes being an attribute of a
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">
        spherical Earth
       </a>
       , some ancient authors used their perceived absurdity as an argument for a
       <a href="/wiki/Flat_Earth" title="Flat Earth">
        flat Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, knowledge of the spherical Earth was widespread during the Middle Ages, only occasionally disputed — the medieval dispute surrounding the antipodes mainly concerned the question whether people could live on the opposite side of the earth: since the torrid
       <a href="/wiki/Clime" title="Clime">
        clime
       </a>
       was considered impassable, it would have been impossible to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Evangelize" title="Evangelize">
        evangelize
       </a>
       them. This posed the problem that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Christ" title="Christ">
        Christ
       </a>
       told the apostles to evangelize all mankind; with regard to the unreachable antipodes, this would have been impossible. Christ would either have appeared a second time, in the antipodes, or left the damned irredeemable. Such an argument was forwarded by the Spanish theologian
       <a href="/wiki/Alonso_Tostado" title="Alonso Tostado">
        Alonso Tostado
       </a>
       as late as the 15th century and "St. Augustine doubts" was a response to Columbus's proposal to sail westwards to the Indies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The author of the Norwegian book
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Konungs_Skuggsj%C3%A1" title="Konungs Skuggsjá">
         Konungs Skuggsjá
        </a>
       </i>
       , from around 1250, discusses the existence of antipodes. He notes that (if they exist) they will see the sun in the north in the middle of the day and that they will have seasons opposite those of the Northern Hemisphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest surviving account by a European who had visited the Southern Hemisphere is that of
       <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">
        Marco Polo
       </a>
       (who, on his way home in 1292, sailed south of the
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">
        Malay Peninsula
       </a>
       ). He noted that it was impossible to see the star
       <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">
        Polaris
       </a>
       from there.
      </p>
      <p>
       The idea of dry land in the southern climes, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Terra_Australis" title="Terra Australis">
         Terra Australis
        </a>
       </i>
       , was introduced by
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       and appears on European maps as an imaginary continent from the 15th century. In spite of having been discovered relatively late by European explorers,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       was inhabited very early in human history; the ancestors of the
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australians" title="Indigenous Australians">
        Indigenous Australians
       </a>
       reached it at least 50,000 years ago.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="True_trip_.22around_the_world.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='True_trip_"around_the_world"'>
        True trip "around the world"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title='Edit section: True trip "around the world"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       To make the longest distance trip around the planet a traveler would have to pass through a set of antipodal points. All meridians can be crossed in one hemisphere--indeed, by walking around one of the poles--but such trips are shorter than a maximum circumnavigation. On the other hand, the greatest straight line distance that could in theory be covered is a trip exactly on the Equator. The Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">
        equatorial bulge
       </a>
       makes this slightly longer than one north-south around the world along a set of meridian lines, or on any other closed
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circle
       </a>
       route traveling at an angle. In any of these cases after half the world is passed every subsequent point will be antipodal to one already visited.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Air_travel_between_antipodes">
        Air travel between antipodes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Air travel between antipodes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are no non-stop scheduled flights between any two antipodal locations by commercial airline service—or anything even close. There is currently no commercial aircraft capable of travelling between antipodes at full load non-stop. (The current record holder
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_777-200LR" title="Boeing 777-200LR">
        Boeing 777-200LR
       </a>
       's maximum range is rated at 17,395 km.) There are also regulations regarding pilot's work time, so
       <a href="/wiki/Aircrew#Relief_crew" title="Aircrew">
        relief crewmembers
       </a>
       would be needed.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The longest non-stop scheduled flight was the
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore_Airlines_Flight_21" title="Singapore Airlines Flight 21">
        Singapore Airlines Flight 21
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Newark,_New_Jersey" title="Newark, New Jersey">
        Newark, New Jersey
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       (discontinued as of November 2013), covering 15,343 km (9,534 mi) in about 18.5 hours flight time, and this was far from a journey between nearly antipodal locations. Among flights with fuel stop and crew-change stop but still same flight number,
       <a href="/wiki/Air_New_Zealand" title="Air New Zealand">
        Air New Zealand
       </a>
       has one of the longest,
       <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">
        Auckland
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       , at 19,240 km (11,960 mi) over Los Angeles (18,360 km (11,410 mi) directly).
      </p>
      <p>
       A hypothetically perfect antipode flight would be
       <a href="/wiki/Tangier_Ibn_Battouta_Airport" title="Tangier Ibn Battouta Airport">
        Tangier Ibn Battouta Airport
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IATA" title="IATA">
        IATA
       </a>
       : TNG) to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Whangarei_Aerodrome" title="Whangarei Aerodrome">
        Whangarei Aerodrome
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       (IATA: WRE), whose designated locators are 10,800 nautical miles (20,001 km) apart,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the maximum possible distance, but their actual runways cross in projection. However, with only a length of 3599 ft, Whangarei's runway is too short to accommodate any current (as of 2015
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) commercial jet airliner, especially one with the required range. Traveling between them would presently need at least three plane changes, e.g. Paris–Los Angeles–Auckland. Taipei and Asuncion are 19,912 km apart; Santiago and Xian are 19,897 km apart; Madrid and Auckland are 19,590 km apart; Buenos Aires and Beijing are 19,260 km apart; Johannesburg and Honolulu are 19,188 km apart; New York City and Perth are 18,700 km apart.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="List_of_antipodes">
        List of antipodes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

      <p>
       Around 71% of the Earth's surface is covered by
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">
        oceans
       </a>
       , and seven-eighths of the Earth's land is confined to the
       <a href="/wiki/Land_and_water_hemispheres" title="Land and water hemispheres">
        land hemisphere
       </a>
       , so the majority of locations on land do not have land-based antipodes.
      </p>
      <p>
       The two largest human inhabited antipodal areas are located in East Asia (mainly eastern China) and South America (mainly northern Argentina and Chile). The Australian mainland is the largest landmass with its antipodes entirely in ocean, although some locations of mainland Australia and Tasmania are close to being antipodes of islands (Bermuda, Azores, Puerto Rico) in the North Atlantic Ocean. The largest landmass with antipodes entirely on land is the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Borneo" title="Borneo">
        Borneo
       </a>
       , whose antipodes are in the Amazon rainforest.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cities_and_geographic_features">
        Cities and geographic features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cities and geographic features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       is approximately antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Te_Arai" title="Te Arai">
        Te Arai Beach
       </a>
       about 85 km north of
       <a href="/wiki/Auckland" title="Auckland">
        Auckland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       . This illustrates the old yet correct saying that the
       <a href="/wiki/The_empire_on_which_the_sun_never_sets" title="The empire on which the sun never sets">
        sun never sets
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
        British Empire
       </a>
       ; the sun still does not set on the
       <a href="/wiki/Commonwealth_of_Nations" title="Commonwealth of Nations">
        Commonwealth of Nations
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The northern part of
       <a href="/wiki/New_Caledonia" title="New Caledonia">
        New Caledonia
       </a>
       , an overseas territory of France, is antipodal to some thinly populated desert in
       <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">
        Mauritania
       </a>
       , a part of the former
       <a href="/wiki/French_West_Africa" title="French West Africa">
        French West Africa
       </a>
       . Portions of
       <a href="/wiki/Suriname" title="Suriname">
        Suriname
       </a>
       , a former Dutch colony, are antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Sulawesi" title="Sulawesi">
        Sulawesi
       </a>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesian
       </a>
       island spelled
       <i>
        Celebes
       </i>
       when it was part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Netherlands_East_Indies" title="Netherlands East Indies">
        Netherlands East Indies
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Luzon" title="Luzon">
        Luzon
       </a>
       , the largest island of the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       , is antipodal to eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Bolivia" title="Bolivia">
        Bolivia
       </a>
       . As with the British Empire, the sun set neither on the French Empire, the Dutch Empire, nor the Spanish Empire at their peaks.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Vit%C3%B3ria_do_Palmar" title="Santa Vitória do Palmar">
        Santa Vitória do Palmar
       </a>
       , the most southerly town of more than 10,000 people in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       , is antipodal to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeju-do" title="Jeju-do">
        Jeju Island
       </a>
       , the southernmost territory of
       <a href="/wiki/South_Korea" title="South Korea">
        South Korea
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii_(island)" title="Hawaii (island)">
        Big Island
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       is antipodal to the
       <a href="/wiki/Okavango_Delta" title="Okavango Delta">
        Okavango Delta
       </a>
       in Botswana, with the island's largest city,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hilo" title="Hilo">
        Hilo
       </a>
       , antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Nxai_Pan_National_Park" title="Nxai Pan National Park">
        Nxai Pan National Park
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Easter_Island" title="Easter Island">
        Easter Island
       </a>
       is antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Desert_National_Park" title="Desert National Park">
        Desert National Park
       </a>
       , 35 km from
       <a href="/wiki/Jaisalmer" title="Jaisalmer">
        Jaisalmer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Desolate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kerguelen_Island" title="Kerguelen Island">
        Kerguelen Island
       </a>
       is antipodal to an area of thinly inhabited plains on the border between the Canadian provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Alberta" title="Alberta">
        Alberta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">
        Saskatchewan
       </a>
       and the US state of
       <a href="/wiki/Montana" title="Montana">
        Montana
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Heard_Island_and_McDonald_Islands" title="Heard Island and McDonald Islands">
        Heard Island and McDonald Islands
       </a>
       , an uninhabited Australian territory, is antipodal to an area in central
       <a href="/wiki/Saskatchewan" title="Saskatchewan">
        Saskatchewan
       </a>
       , including the towns of
       <a href="/wiki/Leask,_Saskatchewan" title="Leask, Saskatchewan">
        Leask
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Shellbrook,_Saskatchewan" title="Shellbrook, Saskatchewan">
        Shellbrook
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%8Ele_Saint-Paul" title="Île Saint-Paul">
        St. Paul Island
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amsterdam_Island" title="Amsterdam Island">
        Amsterdam Island
       </a>
       are antipodal to thinly populated parts of the eastern part of the US state of
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/South_Georgia_Island" title="South Georgia Island">
        South Georgia Island
       </a>
       is antipodal to the northernmost part of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sakhalin_Island" title="Sakhalin Island">
        Sakhalin Island
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Baikal" title="Lake Baikal">
        Lake Baikal
       </a>
       is partially antipodal to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Straits_of_Magellan" title="Straits of Magellan">
        Straits of Magellan
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Russian Antarctic research base
       <a href="/wiki/Bellingshausen_Station" title="Bellingshausen Station">
        Bellingshausen Station
       </a>
       is antipodal to a land location in
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russian
       </a>
       Siberia.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Rottnest_Island" title="Rottnest Island">
        Rottnest Island
       </a>
       , off the coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">
        Western Australia
       </a>
       , is approximately antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Bermuda" title="Bermuda">
        Bermuda
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Flores_Island_(Azores)" title="Flores Island (Azores)">
        Flores Island
       </a>
       , the westernmost island of the
       <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">
        Azores
       </a>
       , is nearly antipodal to
       <a href="/wiki/Flinders_Island" title="Flinders Island">
        Flinders Island
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Tasmania" title="Tasmania">
        Tasmania
       </a>
       and the Australian mainland.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Point_Nemo" title="Point Nemo">
        Point Nemo
       </a>
       , the point in the South Pacific Ocean most distant from any other land, is precisely opposite a desolate piece of desert in western Kazakhstan.
      </p>
      <p>
       By definition, the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North Pole
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       are antipodes.
      </p>
      <p>
       As can be seen on the purple/blue map, the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific Ocean
       </a>
       is so large that it stretches halfway around the world; parts of the Pacific off the coast of
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       are antipodal to parts of the same ocean off the coast of Southeast Asia.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extraterrestrial_examples">
        Extraterrestrial examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Antipodes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Extraterrestrial examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In a number of cases on extraterrestrial bodies in the Solar System, unusual geologic features (e.g., jumbled terrain or unique volcanic constructs) are located antipodal to major impact basins. It has been hypothesized that this results from focusing of some of the seismic waves (
       <a href="/wiki/P-wave" title="P-wave">
        p-waves
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_wave#Mechanical_waves" title="Surface wave">
        surface waves
       </a>
       ) produced by an impact at its antipode.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schultz1975_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schultz1975-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caloris_Basin" title="Caloris Basin">
         Caloris Basin
        </a>
        - "Weird Terrain" (
        <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
         Mercury
