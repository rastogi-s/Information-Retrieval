         The
         <i>
          Geography
         </i>
         of Ptolemy in a c.
         <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
          1411
         </span>
         Latin translation by
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobus_Angelus" title="Jacobus Angelus">
          Jacobus Angelus
         </a>
         with 27 maps by
         <a href="/wiki/Claudius_Clavus" title="Claudius Clavus">
          Claus Swart
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <b>
         Geography
        </b>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Γεωγραφικὴ Ὑφήγησις
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Geōgraphikḕ Hyphḗgēsis
       </i>
       ,
       <abbr title="Literally">
        lit.
       </abbr>
       "Geographical Guidance"), also known by its
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       names as the
       <i>
        <b>
         <i>
          <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
           Geographia
          </span>
         </i>
        </b>
       </i>
       and the
       <i>
        <b>
         <i>
          <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
           Cosmographia
          </span>
         </i>
        </b>
       </i>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Gazetteer" title="Gazetteer">
        gazetteer
       </a>
       , an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(book)" title="Atlas (book)">
        atlas
       </a>
       , and a treatise on
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       , compiling the geographical knowledge of the 2nd-century
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       . Originally written by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemy" title="Claudius Ptolemy">
        Claudius Ptolemy
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">
        Alexandria
       </a>
       around AD 150, the work was a revision of a now-lost atlas by
       <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">
        Marinus of Tyre
       </a>
       using additional Roman and
       <a href="/wiki/Parthian_Empire" title="Parthian Empire">
        Persian
       </a>
       gazetteers and new principles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBerggren2001_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerggren2001-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its translation into
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Arabic" title="Classical Arabic">
        Arabic
       </a>
       in the 9th century and
       <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Latin" title="Medieval Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       in 1406 was highly influential on the geographical knowledge and cartographic traditions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_and_cartography_in_medieval_Islam" title="Geography and cartography in medieval Islam">
        medieval Caliphate
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       Europe.
      </p>

      <p>
       Versions of Ptolemy's work in antiquity were probably proper
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(book)" title="Atlas (book)">
        atlases
       </a>
       with attached maps, although some scholars believe that the references to maps in the text were later additions.
      </p>
      <p>
       No
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       manuscript of the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       survives from earlier than the 13th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b267–268_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b267–268-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A letter written by the
       <a href="/wiki/Byzantine_Empire" title="Byzantine Empire">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
        monk
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Maximus_Planudes" title="Maximus Planudes">
        Maximus Planudes
       </a>
       records that he searched for one for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chora_Monastery" title="Chora Monastery">
        Chora Monastery
       </a>
       in the summer of 1295;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       one of the earliest surviving texts may have been one of those he then assembled.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Europe, maps were sometimes made redrawn using the coordinates provided by the text,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMilanesi1996_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMilanesi1996-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as Planudes was forced to do.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later scribes and publishers could then copy these new maps, as
       <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_I_of_Constantinople" title="Athanasius I of Constantinople">
        Athanasius
       </a>
       did for the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Byzantine_emperors" title="List of Byzantine emperors">
        emperor
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andronicus_II_Palaeologus" title="Andronicus II Palaeologus">
        Andronicus II Palaeologus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987b268-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The three earliest surviving texts with maps are those from
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">
        Istanbul
       </a>
       ) based on Planudes's work.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       translation of these texts was made in 1406 or 1407 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobus_Angelus" title="Jacobus Angelus">
        Jacobus Angelus
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Florence" title="Republic of Florence">
        Florence
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , under the name
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         Geographia Claudii Ptolemaei
        </span>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAngelusc._1406_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAngelusc._1406-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not thought that his edition had maps,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEClemens2008244_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEClemens2008244-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Chrysoloras" title="Manuel Chrysoloras">
        Manuel Chrysoloras
       </a>
       had given
       <a href="/wiki/Palla_Strozzi" title="Palla Strozzi">
        Palla Strozzi
       </a>
       a Greek copy of Planudes's maps in Florence in 1397.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contents">
        Contents
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contents">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       consists of three sections, divided among 8 books. Book I is a treatise on
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       , describing the methods used to assemble and arrange Ptolemy's data. From Book II through the beginning of Book VII, a gazetteer provides longitude and latitude values for the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">
        world known
       </a>
       to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Romans" title="Ancient Romans">
        ancient Romans
       </a>
       (the "
       <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">
        ecumene
       </a>
       "). The rest of Book VII provides details on three projections to be used for the construction of a map of the world, varying in complexity and fidelity. Book VIII constitutes an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlas_(book)" title="Atlas (book)">
        atlas
       </a>
       of regional maps. The maps include a recapitulation of some of the values given earlier in the work, which were intended to be used as captions to clarify the map's contents and maintain their accuracy during copying.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cartographical_treatise">
        Cartographical treatise
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cartographical treatise">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Maps based on
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        scientific principles
       </a>
       had been made in Europe since the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
        Eratosthenes
       </a>
       in the 3rd century BC. Ptolemy improved the treatment of
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        map projections
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (March 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       He provided instructions on how to create his maps in the first section of the work.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gazetteer">
        Gazetteer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gazetteer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The gazetteer section of Ptolemy's work provided
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate" title="Coordinate">
        coordinates
       </a>
       for all the places and geographical features in the work. Latitude was expressed in
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees of arc
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , the same system that is used now, though Ptolemy used fractions of a degree rather than minutes of arc.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       ran through the
       <a href="/wiki/Fortunate_Isles" title="Fortunate Isles">
        Fortunate Isles
       </a>
       , the westernmost land recorded,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWright1923_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWright1923-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at around the position of
       <a href="/wiki/El_Hierro" title="El Hierro">
        El Hierro
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The maps spanned 180 degrees of longitude from the Fortunate Isles in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Ptolemy was aware that Europe knew only about a quarter of the globe.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atlas">
        Atlas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Atlas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's work included a single large and less detailed world map and then separate and more detailed regional maps. The first Greek manuscripts compiled after
       <a href="/wiki/Maximus_Planudes" title="Maximus Planudes">
        Maximus Planudes
       </a>
       's rediscovery of the text had as many as 64 regional maps.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The standard set in Western Europe came to be 26: 10 European maps, 4 African maps, and 12 Asian maps. As early as the 1420s, these canonical maps were complemented by extra-Ptolemaic regional maps depicting, e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">
        Scandinavia
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Antiquity">
        Antiquity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Antiquity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The original treatise by
       <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">
        Marinus of Tyre
       </a>
       that formed the basis of Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       has been completely lost. A world map based on Ptolemy was displayed in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Augustodunum" title="Augustodunum">
        Augustodunum
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Autun" title="Autun">
        Autun
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ) in late Roman times.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria" title="Pappus of Alexandria">
        Pappus
       </a>
       , writing at
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandria" title="Alexandria">
        Alexandria
       </a>
       in the 4th century, produced a
       <a href="/wiki/Scholia" title="Scholia">
        commentary
       </a>
       on Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       and used it as the basis of his (now lost)
       <i>
        Chorography of the Ecumene
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987a234_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987a234-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later imperial writers and mathematicians, however, seem to have restricted themselves to commenting on Ptolemy's text, rather than improving upon it; surviving records actually show decreasing fidelity to real position.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDilke1987a234_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDilke1987a234-19">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Whereas previous Greco-Roman geographers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Strabo" title="Strabo">
        Strabo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder" title="Pliny the Elder">
        Pliny the Elder
       </a>
       demonstrated a reluctance to rely on the contemporary accounts of sailors and merchants who plied distant areas of the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian Ocean
       </a>
       , Marinus and Ptolemy betray a much greater receptiveness to incorporating information received from them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParker2008118_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParker2008118-20">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For instance, Grant Parker argues that it would be highly implausible for them to have constructed the
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">
        Bay of Bengal
       </a>
       as precisely as they did without the accounts of sailors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParker2008118_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParker2008118-20">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When it comes to the account of the
       <a href="/wiki/Golden_Chersonese" title="Golden Chersonese">
        Golden Chersonese
       </a>
       (i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Malay_Peninsula" title="Malay Peninsula">
        Malay Peninsula
       </a>
       ) and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Sinus" title="Magnus Sinus">
         Magnus Sinus
        </a>
       </i>
       (i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Thailand" title="Gulf of Thailand">
        Gulf of Thailand
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_China_Sea" title="South China Sea">
        South China Sea
       </a>
       ), Marinus and Ptolemy relied on the testimony of a Greek sailor named Alexandros, who claimed to have visited a far eastern site called "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cattigara" title="Cattigara">
        Cattigara
       </a>
       " (most likely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oc_Eo" title="Oc Eo">
        Oc Eo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
        Vietnam
       </a>
       , the site of unearthed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonine" title="Antonine">
        Antonine
       </a>
       -era Roman goods and not far from the region of
       <a href="/wiki/Jiaozhi" title="Jiaozhi">
        Jiaozhi
       </a>
       in northern Vietnam where
       <a href="/wiki/Twenty-Four_Histories" title="Twenty-Four Histories">
        ancient Chinese sources
       </a>
       claim several
       <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">
        Roman embassies
       </a>
       first landed in the 2nd and 3rd centuries).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYoung200129_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYoung200129-21">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMawer201338_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMawer201338-22">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESuárez199990-92_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESuárez199990-92-23">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYule191552_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYule191552-24">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Caliphate">
        Caliphate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Caliphate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_and_cartography_in_medieval_Islam" title="Geography and cartography in medieval Islam">
        Geography and cartography in medieval Islam
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amirbliulaie.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1055" data-file-width="611" height="380" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Amirbliulaie.png/220px-Amirbliulaie.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Amirbliulaie.png/330px-Amirbliulaie.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Amirbliulaie.png/440px-Amirbliulaie.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amirbliulaie.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Amir of Bani Bu Ali tribe, the likely
         <i>
          Bliulaie
         </i>
         of Ptolemy's map.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Geography_and_cartography_in_medieval_Islam" title="Geography and cartography in medieval Islam">
        Muslim cartographers
       </a>
       were using copies of Ptolemy's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Almagest" title="Almagest">
         Almagest
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       by the 9th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdson200461–62_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdson200461–62-25">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At that time, in the court of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_caliphs" title="List of caliphs">
        caliph
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Ma%CA%BEm%C5%ABm" title="Al-Maʾmūm">
        al-Maʾmūm
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Khw%C4%81razm%C4%AB" title="Al-Khwārazmī">
        al-Khwārazmī
       </a>
       compiled his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_the_Depiction_of_the_Earth" title="Book of the Depiction of the Earth">
         Book of the Depiction of the Earth
        </a>
       </i>
       which mimicked the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in providing the coordinates for 545 cities and regional maps of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nile_River" title="Nile River">
        Nile
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Island_of_the_Jewel" title="Island of the Jewel">
        Island of the Jewel
       </a>
       , the Sea of Darkness, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_of_Azov" title="Sea of Azov">
        Sea of Azov
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128_26-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A 1037 copy of these are the earliest extant maps from Islamic lands.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008127_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008127-27">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The text clearly states that al-Khwārazmī was working from an earlier map, although this could not have been an exact copy of Ptolemy's work: his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       was 10° east of Ptolemy's, he adds some places, and his latitudes differ.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128_26-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C.A._Nallino" title="C.A. Nallino">
        C.A. Nallino
       </a>
       suggests that the work was not based on Ptolemy but on a derivative world map,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENallino1939_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENallino1939-28">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       presumably in
       <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">
        Syriac
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128_26-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008128-26">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The colored
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Map_of_al-Ma%CA%BEm%C5%ABm&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Map of al-Maʾmūm (page does not exist)">
        map of al-Maʾmūm
       </a>
       constructed by a team including al-Khwārazmī was described by the
       <a href="/wiki/Persian_people" title="Persian people">
        Persian
       </a>
       encyclopædist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Al-Mas%CA%BF%C5%ABd%C4%AB" title="Al-Masʿūdī">
        al-Masʿūdī
       </a>
       around 956 as superior to the maps of
       <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">
        Marinus
       </a>
       and Ptolemy,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEal-Masʿūdī33_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEal-Masʿūdī33-29">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       probably indicating that it was built along similar mathematical principles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008130_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008130-30">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It included 4530 cities and over 200 mountains.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite beginning to compile numerous gazetteers of places and coördinates indebted to Ptolemy,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008129_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008129-31">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Muslim scholars made almost no direct use of Ptolemy's principles in the maps which have survived.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEdson200461–62_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEdson200461–62-25">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Instead, they followed al-Khwārazmī's modifications and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthogonal_projection" title="Orthogonal projection">
        orthogonal projection
       </a>
       advocated by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Suhr%C4%81b&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Suhrāb (page does not exist)">
        Suhrāb
       </a>
       's early 10th-century treatise on the
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Marvels_of_the_Seven_Climes_to_the_End_of_Habitation&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Marvels of the Seven Climes to the End of Habitation (page does not exist)">
         Marvels of the Seven Climes to the End of Habitation
        </a>
       </i>
       . Surviving maps from the medieval period were not done according to mathematical principles. The world map from the 11th-century
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Book_of_Curiosities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Book of Curiosities (page does not exist)">
         Book of Curiosities
        </a>
       </i>
       is the earliest surviving map of the
       <a href="/wiki/Caliphate" title="Caliphate">
        Muslim
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Christendom" title="Christendom">
        Christian
       </a>
       worlds to include a
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system" title="Geographic coordinate system">
        graticule
       </a>
       but the cartographer seems to have not understood its purpose, starting it from the left using twice the intended scale and then (apparently realizing his mistake) giving up halfway through.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008126–127_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008126–127-32">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its presence does strongly suggest the existence of earlier, now-lost maps which had been mathematically derived in the manner of Ptolemy,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008127_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008127-27">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       al-Khwārazmi, or Suhrāb. There are surviving reports of such maps.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERapoport2008129_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERapoport2008129-31">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Renaissance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Waldseem%C3%BCller_map" title="Waldseemüller map">
        Waldseemüller map
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sino-Roman_relations" title="Sino-Roman relations">
        Sino-Roman relations
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indo-Roman_relations" title="Indo-Roman relations">
        Indo-Roman relations
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Europeans_in_Medieval_China" title="Europeans in Medieval China">
        Europeans in Medieval China
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Chronology_of_European_exploration_of_Asia" title="Chronology of European exploration of Asia">
        Chronology of European exploration of Asia
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy's text reached Italy from
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       in about 1400 and was translated into Latin by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacobus_Angelus" title="Jacobus Angelus">
        Jacobus Angelus
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scarperia" title="Scarperia">
        Scarperia
       </a>
       around 1406.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAngelusc._1406_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAngelusc._1406-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first printed edition with maps, published in 1477 in
       <a href="/wiki/Bologna" title="Bologna">
        Bologna
       </a>
       , was also be the first printed book with engraved illustrations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landau1996_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Landau1996-33">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many editions followed (more often using
       <a href="/wiki/Woodcut" title="Woodcut">
        woodcut
       </a>
       in the early days), some following traditional versions of the maps, and others updating them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Landau1996_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Landau1996-33">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An edition printed at
       <a href="/wiki/Ulm" title="Ulm">
        Ulm
       </a>
       in 1482 was the first one printed north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
        Alps
       </a>
       . Also in 1482,
       <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Berlinghieri" title="Francesco Berlinghieri">
        Francesco Berlinghieri
       </a>
       printed the first edition in vernacular
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/w/index.php?title=File:Cosmographia,_1482.djvu&amp;page=7">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4015" data-file-width="2309" height="336" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu/page7-220px-Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu/page7-330px-Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu/page7-440px-Cosmographia%2C_1482.djvu.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cosmographia,_1482.djvu?page=7" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Edition printed in Ulm in 1482
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ptolemy had mapped the whole world from the
       <i>
        Fortunatae Insulae
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">
        Cape Verde
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       ) eastward to the eastern shore of the
       <i>
        Magnus Sinus
       </i>
       . This known portion of the world was comprised within 180 degrees. In his extreme east Ptolemy placed
       <i>
        Serica
       </i>
       (the Land of Silk), the
       <i>
        Sinarum Situs
       </i>
       (the Port of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinae" title="Sinae">
        Sinae
       </a>
       ), and the emporium of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kattigara" title="Kattigara">
         Cattigara
        </a>
       </i>
       . On the 1489 map of the world by Henricus Martellus, which was based on Ptolemy’s work, Asia terminated in its southeastern point in a cape, the Cape of Cattigara. Cattigara was understood by Ptolemy to be a port on the
       <i>
        Sinus Magnus,
       </i>
       or Great Gulf, the actual Gulf of Thailand, at eight and a half degrees north of the Equator, on the coast of Cambodia, which is where he located it in his
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Canon_of_Famous_Cities&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Canon of Famous Cities (page does not exist)">
        Canon of Famous Cities
       </a>
       . It was the easternmost port reached by shipping trading from the Graeco-Roman world to the lands of the Far East.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Ptolemy’s later and more well-known Geography, a scribal error was made and Cattigara was located at eight and a half degrees South of the Equator. On Ptolemaic maps, such as that of Martellus,
       <i>
        Catigara
       </i>
       was located on the easternmost shore of the
       <i>
        Mare Indicum,
       </i>
       180 degrees East of the Cape St Vincent at, due to the scribal error, eight and a half degrees South of the Equator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Catigara
       </i>
       is also shown at this location on Martin Waldseemüller’s 1507 world map, which avowedly followed the tradition of Ptolemy. Ptolemy’s information was thereby misinterpreted so that the coast of China, which should have been represented as part of the coast of eastern Asia, was falsely made to represent an eastern shore of the Indian Ocean. As a result, Ptolemy implied more land east of the 180th meridian and an ocean beyond.
       <a href="/wiki/Marco_Polo" title="Marco Polo">
        Marco Polo
       </a>
       ’s account of his travels in eastern Asia described lands and seaports on an eastern ocean apparently unknown to Ptolemy. Marco Polo’s narrative authorized the extensive additions to the Ptolemaic map shown on the 1492 globe of
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Behaim" title="Martin Behaim">
        Martin Behaim
       </a>
       . The fact that Ptolemy did not represent an eastern coast of Asia made it admissible for Behaim to extend that continent far to the east. Behaim’s globe placed Marco Polo’s Mangi and
       <a href="/wiki/Cathay" title="Cathay">
        Cathay
       </a>
       east of Ptolemy’s 180th meridian, and
       <a href="/wiki/Kublai_Khan" title="Kublai Khan">
        the Great Khan’s
       </a>
       capital,
       <a href="/wiki/Khanbaliq" title="Khanbaliq">
        <i>
         Cambaluc
        </i>
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Beijing" title="Beijing">
        Beijing
       </a>
       ), on the 41st parallel of latitude at approximately 233 degrees East. Behaim allowed 60 degrees beyond Ptolemy’s 180 degrees for the mainland of Asia and 30 degrees more to the east coast of
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cipangu" title="Cipangu">
         Cipangu
        </a>
       </i>
       (Japan). Cipangu and the mainland of Asia were thus placed only 90 and 120 degrees, respectively, west of the Canary Islands.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Codex Seragliensis was used as the base of a new edition of the work in 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStückelberger2006_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStückelberger2006-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This new edition was used to "decode" Ptolemy's coordinates of Books 2 and 3 by an interdisciplinary team of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/TU_Berlin" title="TU Berlin">
        TU Berlin
       </a>
       , presented in publications in 2010
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Relevant research on Ptolemy's
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       manuscripts and printed editions, concerning the
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       versions coordinates, has been carried out since 1998 by members of the cartography group, school of surveying engineering, at the Aristotle University of Thessaloniki. See, e.g. selective papers in the web journal "
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.e-perimetron.org" rel="nofollow">
        e-Perimetron
       </a>
       "
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Christopher_Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Christopher Columbus">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Columbus" title="Christopher Columbus">
        Christopher Columbus
       </a>
       modified this geography further by using 53⅔ Italian nautical miles as the length of a degree instead of the longer degree of Ptolemy, and by adopting
       <a href="/wiki/Marinus_of_Tyre" title="Marinus of Tyre">
        Marinus of Tyre
       </a>
       ’s longitude of 225 degrees for the east coast of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Magnus_Sinus" title="Magnus Sinus">
         Magnus Sinus
        </a>
       </i>
       . This resulted in a considerable eastward advancement of the longitudes given by
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Behaim" title="Martin Behaim">
        Martin Behaim
       </a>
       and other contemporaries of Columbus. By some process Columbus reasoned that the longitudes of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Asia" title="Eastern Asia">
        eastern Asia
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cipangu" title="Cipangu">
        Cipangu
       </a>
       respectively were about 270 and 300 degrees east, or 90 and 60 degrees west of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . He said that he had sailed 1100 leagues from the Canaries when he found
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       in 1492. This was approximately where he thought the coast of eastern Asia would be found. On this basis of calculation he identified
       <a href="/wiki/Hispaniola" title="Hispaniola">
        Hispaniola
       </a>
       with Cipangu, which he had expected to find on the outward voyage at a distance of about 700 leagues from the Canaries. His later voyages resulted in further exploration of Cuba and in the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       . At first South America, the
       <i>
        Mundus Novus
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">
        New World
       </a>
       ) was considered to be a great island of continental proportions; but as a result of his
       <a href="/wiki/Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus#Fourth_voyage" title="Voyages of Christopher Columbus">
        fourth voyage
       </a>
       , it was apparently considered to be identical with the great Upper India peninsula (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/India_Superior" title="India Superior">
        India Superior
       </a>
       ) represented by Behaim—the Cape of Cattigara. This seems to be the best interpretation of the sketch map made by Alessandro Zorzi on the advice of
       <a href="/wiki/Bartholomew_Columbus" title="Bartholomew Columbus">
        Bartholomew Columbus
       </a>
       (Christopher’s brother) around 1506, which bears an inscription saying that according to the ancient geographer Marinus of Tyre and Christopher Columbus the distance from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_St_Vincent" title="Cape St Vincent">
        Cape St Vincent
       </a>
       on the coast of Portugal to Cattigara on the peninsula of India Superior was 225 degrees, while according to Ptolemy the same distance was 180 degrees.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_modern_Ottoman_Empire">
        Early modern Ottoman Empire
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Early modern Ottoman Empire">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Prior to the 16th century knowledge of geography in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       was limited in scope, with almost no access to the works of earlier Islamic scholars that superseded Ptolemy. His Geography would again be translated and updated with commentary into Arabic under
       <a href="/wiki/Mehmed_the_Conqueror" title="Mehmed the Conqueror">
        Mehmed II
       </a>
       , who commissioned works from Byzantine scholar
       <a href="/wiki/George_Amiroutzes" title="George Amiroutzes">
        George Amiroutzes
       </a>
       in 1465 and the Florentine humanist
       <a href="/wiki/Francesco_Berlinghieri" title="Francesco Berlinghieri">
        Francesco Berlinghieri
       </a>
       in 1481.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Longitudes_error_and_Earth_size">
        Longitudes error and Earth size
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Geography_(Ptolemy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Longitudes error and Earth size">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Italian scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Lucio_Russo" title="Lucio Russo">
        Lucio Russo
       </a>
       points out two apparently distinct errors:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        considering a sample of 80 cities amongst the 6345 listed by Ptolemy, those that are both identifiable and for which we can expect a better distance measurement since they were well known, there is a systematic overestimation of the longitude by a factor 1.428 with a high confidence (coefficient of determination r² = 0.9935). This error produces an evident deformations in Ptolemy's world map most apparent for exampled in the profile of
        <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
         Italy
        </a>
        , which is markedly stretched horizontally.
       </li>
       <li>
        Ptolemy accepted that the known
        <a href="/wiki/Ecumene" title="Ecumene">
         Ecumene
        </a>
        spanned 180° of longitude, but instead of accepting
        <a href="/wiki/Eratosthenes" title="Eratosthenes">
         Eratosthenes
        </a>
        estimate for the circumference of the
        <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
         Earth
        </a>
        of 252,000 stadia, he shrinks it to 180,000 stadia, with a factor of 1.4 between the two figures.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Ptolemy took as location for the longitude 0° the
       <a href="/wiki/Fortunate_Isles" title="Fortunate Isles">
        Fortunate Isles
       </a>
       which at his times were identified with the
       <a href="/wiki/Canary_Islands" title="Canary Islands">
        Canary Islands
       </a>
       . The strange coincidence of the two aforementioned errors may be accounted for if one assumes that this identification was wrong and that at the time of Ptolemy's sources the Fortunate Isles where actually the eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Antilles" title="Antilles">
        Antilles
       </a>
       . Since Ptolemy could estimate the actual distance to the Canaries, Russo proposes that he purposely shrank the circumference of Earth to accommodate his data and his wrong identification of the Fortunate Isles. This suggests or even proves that the American continent was known in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_Antiquity" title="Classical Antiquity">
        Classical Antiquity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
