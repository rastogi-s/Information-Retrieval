      <p>
       The
       <b>
        languages of Spain
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         lenguas de España
        </span>
       </i>
       ), or
       <b>
        Spanish languages
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es">
         lenguas españolas
        </span>
       </i>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are the languages spoken or once spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       are the most widely spoken in Spain; of which
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language#Names_of_the_language" title="Spanish language">
        or Castilian
       </a>
       , is the only language which has
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official status
       </a>
       for the whole country.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Various other languages have co-official or recognised status in specific territories,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a number of unofficial languages and dialects are spoken in certain localities.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present-day_languages">
        Present-day languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Present-day languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In terms of the number of speakers and dominance, the most prominent of the languages of Spain is
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       (Castilian), spoken by about 99% of Spaniards as a first or second language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Catalan (or Valencian) is spoken by 19%, Galician by 5%, and Basque by 2% of the population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Distribution of the regional co-official languages in Spain:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aranese_language" title="Aranese language">
         Aranese
        </a>
        , co-official in
        <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
         Catalonia
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        It is spoken mainly in the
        <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
         Pyrenean
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Comarques_of_Catalonia" title="Comarques of Catalonia">
         comarca
        </a>
        of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aran_Valley" title="Aran Valley">
         Aran Valley
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         Val d'Aran
        </i>
        ), in north-western
        <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
         Catalonia
        </a>
        . It is a variety of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gascon_dialect" title="Gascon dialect">
         Gascon
        </a>
        , which in turn is a variety of the
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         Occitan language
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
         Basque
        </a>
        , co-official in the
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_Country_(autonomous_community)" title="Basque Country (autonomous community)">
         Basque Country
        </a>
        and northern
        <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
         Navarre
        </a>
        (see
        <i>
         <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ley_Foral_del_Vascuence#Zonificación" title="es:Ley Foral del Vascuence">
          Basque-speaking zone
         </a>
        </i>
        ). Basque is the only non-
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
         Romance language
        </a>
        (as well as non-
        <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
         Indo-European
        </a>
        ) with an official status in mainland Spain.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        , co-official in Catalonia, the
        <a href="/wiki/Balearic_Islands" title="Balearic Islands">
         Balearic Islands
        </a>
        and, as a distinct variant (
        <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
         Valencian
        </a>
        ), in the
        <a href="/wiki/Valencian_Community" title="Valencian Community">
         Valencian Community
        </a>
        . It is recognised—but not official—in
        <a href="/wiki/Aragon" title="Aragon">
         Aragon
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/La_Franja" title="La Franja">
          La Franja
         </a>
        </i>
        ). Furthermore, it is also spoken without official recognition in the municipality of
        <a href="/wiki/Carche" title="Carche">
         Carche
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Region_of_Murcia" title="Region of Murcia">
         Murcia
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
         Galician
        </a>
        , co-official in
        <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
         Galicia
        </a>
        . It is also spoken without official recognition in the adjacent western parts of the
        <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">
         Principality of Asturias
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
         Castile and León
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Spanish is official throughout the country; the rest of these languages have legal and co-official status in their respective communities, and (except Aranese) are widespread enough to have daily newspapers and significant book publishing and media presence in those communities. In the cases of Catalan and Galician, they are the main languages used by the Catalan and Galician regional governments and local administrations. A number of citizens in these areas consider their regional language as their primary language and Spanish as secondary.

        <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
         Castile and León
        </a>
        . Spoken in the provinces of
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Province of León">

        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Zamora" title="Province of Zamora">

       </li>

      <p>
       Spanish itself also has distinct dialects around the country; for example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Canarian_Spanish" title="Canarian Spanish">
        Canarian
       </a>
       dialects, each of these with their own subvarieties, some of them being partially closer to the Spanish of the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       , which they heavily influenced at different degrees, depending on the regions or periods, and according to different and non-homogeneous migrating or colonisation processes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Five very localised dialects are of difficult filiation:
       <a href="/wiki/Fala_language" title="Fala language">
        Fala
       </a>
       , a nearly extinct variety of its own mostly adscribed to the Galician-Portuguese group;
       <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_dialect" title="Cantabrian dialect">
        Cantabrian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Extremaduran_language" title="Extremaduran language">
        Extremaduran
       </a>
       , two Astur-Leonese dialects also regarded as Spanish dialects;
       <a href="/wiki/Galician-Asturian" title="Galician-Asturian">
        Eonavian
       </a>
       , a dialect between Asturian and Galician, closer to the latter according to several linguists; and
       <a href="/wiki/Benasquese_dialect" title="Benasquese dialect">
        Benasquese
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Ribagor%C3%A7an_dialect" title="Ribagorçan dialect">
        Ribagorçan
       </a>
       dialect that was formerly classified as Catalan, later as Aragonese, and which is now often regarded as a transitional language of its own. Asturian and Leonese are closely related to the local
       <a href="/wiki/Mirandese_language" title="Mirandese language">
        Mirandese
       </a>
       which is spoken on an adjacent territory but over the border into
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       . Mirandese is recognised and has some local official status.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the exception of Basque, which appears to be a
       <a href="/wiki/Language_isolate" title="Language isolate">
        language isolate
       </a>
       , all of the languages present in mainland Spain are
       <a href="/wiki/Indo-European_languages" title="Indo-European languages">
        Indo-European languages
       </a>
       , specifically
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro-Asiatic_languages" title="Afro-Asiatic languages">
        Afro-Asiatic languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">
        Arabic
       </a>
       (including Ceuta
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Darija" title="Darija">
        Darija
       </a>
       ) or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Berber_language" title="Berber language">
        Berber
       </a>
       (mainly
       <a href="/wiki/Riffian_language" title="Riffian language">
        Riffian
       </a>
       ), are spoken by the Muslim population of
       <a href="/wiki/Ceuta" title="Ceuta">
        Ceuta
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Melilla" title="Melilla">
        Melilla
       </a>
       and by recent immigrants (mainly from Morocco and Algeria) elsewhere.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Portuguese_and_Galician">
        Portuguese and Galician
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Languages_of_Spain&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Portuguese and Galician">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
        Galicia
       </a>
       , the mutual relationship between
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       has caused some controversy, since some linguists, such as Lindley Cintra,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       consider that they are still dialects of a common language, in spite of the differences in phonology and vocabulary (see
       <a href="/wiki/Reintegrationism" title="Reintegrationism">
        reintegrationism
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Others, such as Pilar Vázquez Cuesta,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       argue that they have become separate languages due to major differences in phonetics and vocabulary usage, and, to a lesser extent, morphology and syntax.
      </p>
      <p>
       In any case, the respective written standards are noticeably different one from another, partly because of the divergent phonological features and partly due to the usage of Spanish orthographic conventions over the Portuguese ones at the time of Galician
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standardisation
       </a>
       by the early 20th century.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The official (delegated onto both the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://gl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instituto_da_Lingua_Galega" title="gl:Instituto da Lingua Galega">
        Galician Language Institute
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Galician_Academy" title="Royal Galician Academy">
        Royal Galician Academy
       </a>
       standard) and widespread position is that Galician and Portuguese should be considered independent languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Galician-Portuguese" title="Galician-Portuguese">
        Galician-Portuguese
       </a>
       -based dialect known as
       <a href="/wiki/Fala_language" title="Fala language">
        Fala
       </a>
       is locally spoken in an area sometimes called
       <i>
        Valley of Jálama/Xálima
       </i>
       , which includes the towns of
       <a href="/wiki/San_Mart%C3%ADn_de_Trevejo" title="San Martín de Trevejo">
        San Martín de Trevejo
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Sa Martin de Trevellu
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Eljas" title="Eljas">
        Eljas
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        As Elhas
       </i>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Valverde_del_Fresno" title="Valverde del Fresno">
        Valverde del Fresno
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Valverdi du Fresnu
       </i>
       ), in the northwestern corner of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A1ceres_(province)" title="Cáceres (province)">
        Cáceres province
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Portuguese proper is still spoken by local people in three border areas:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The town of
        <a href="/wiki/La_Alamedilla" title="La Alamedilla">
         La Alamedilla
        </a>
        , in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salamanca_province" title="Salamanca province">
         Salamanca province
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The so-called Cedillo-horn, including
        <a href="/wiki/Cedillo" title="Cedillo">
         Cedillo
        </a>
        (in Portuguese
        <i>
         Cedilho
        </i>
        ) and
        <a href="/wiki/Herrera_de_Alc%C3%A1ntara" title="Herrera de Alcántara">
         Herrera de Alcántara
        </a>
        (in Portuguese
        <i>
         Ferreira de Alcântara
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        The town of
        <a href="/wiki/Olivenza" title="Olivenza">
         Olivenza
        </a>
        (in Portuguese
        <i>
         Olivença
        </i>
        ), in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Badajoz_(province)" title="Badajoz (province)">
         Badajoz province
        </a>
        , and its surrounding territory, which used to be Portuguese until the 19th century and is still claimed by Portugal.
       </li>
      </ul>
