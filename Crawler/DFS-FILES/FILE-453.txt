      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Linguistics" title="Linguistics">
        linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        conjugation
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʊ/: 'oo' in 'foot'">
            ʊ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɡ/: 'g' in 'guy'">
            ɡ
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ʃ/: 'sh' in 'shy'">
            ʃ
           </span>
           <span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">
            ən
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) is the creation of derived forms of a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       from its
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_parts" title="Principal parts">
        principal parts
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Inflection" title="Inflection">
        inflection
       </a>
       (alteration of form according to rules of
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       ). Conjugation may be affected by
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        gender
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
        tense
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_aspect" title="Grammatical aspect">
        aspect
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        mood
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammatical_voice" title="Grammatical voice">
        voice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       , and other
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_category" title="Grammatical category">
        grammatical categories
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Possession_(linguistics)" title="Possession (linguistics)">
        possession
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Definiteness" title="Definiteness">
        definiteness
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Politeness" title="Politeness">
        politeness
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Causative" title="Causative">
        causativity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Clusivity" title="Clusivity">
        clusivity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Interrogative" title="Interrogative">
        interrogativity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Transitivity_(grammar)" title="Transitivity (grammar)">
        transitivity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Valency_(linguistics)" title="Valency (linguistics)">
        valency
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Affirmative_and_negative" title="Affirmative and negative">
        polarity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Telicity" title="Telicity">
        telicity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Volition_(linguistics)" title="Volition (linguistics)">
        volition
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mirativity" title="Mirativity">
        mirativity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Evidentiality" title="Evidentiality">
        evidentiality
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Animacy" title="Animacy">
        animacy
       </a>
       , associativity,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Pluractionality" title="Pluractionality">
        pluractionality
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Reciprocal_(grammar)" title="Reciprocal (grammar)">
        reciprocity
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agreement
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polypersonal_agreement" title="Polypersonal agreement">
        polypersonal agreement
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Incorporation_(linguistics)" title="Incorporation (linguistics)">
        incorporation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Noun_class" title="Noun class">
        noun class
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Noun_classifier" title="Noun classifier">
        noun classifiers
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Verb_classifier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Verb classifier (page does not exist)">
        verb classifiers
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in some languages.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Agglutinative" title="Agglutinative">
        Agglutinative
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polysynthetic_language" title="Polysynthetic language">
        polysynthetic languages
       </a>
       tend to have the most complex conjugations albeit some
       <a href="/wiki/Fusional_language" title="Fusional language">
        fusional languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Archi_language" title="Archi language">
        Archi
       </a>
       can also have extremely complex conjugation. Typically the principal parts are the
       <a href="/wiki/Root_(linguistics)" title="Root (linguistics)">
        root
       </a>
       and/or several modifications of it (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stem_(linguistics)" title="Stem (linguistics)">
        stems
       </a>
       ). All the different forms of the same verb constitute a
       <a href="/wiki/Lexeme" title="Lexeme">
        lexeme
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/Canon_(basic_principle)" title="Canon (basic principle)">
        canonical
       </a>
       form of the verb that is conventionally used to represent that lexeme (as seen in dictionary entries) is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Lemma_(morphology)" title="Lemma (morphology)">
        lemma
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The term conjugation is applied only to the inflection of verbs, and not of other parts of speech (inflection of
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        nouns
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       ). Also it is often restricted to denoting the formation of
       <a href="/wiki/Finite_verb" title="Finite verb">
        finite forms
       </a>
       of a verb – these may be referred to as
       <i>
        conjugated forms
       </i>
       , as opposed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        non-finite forms
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Infinitive" title="Infinitive">
        infinitive
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Gerund" title="Gerund">
        gerund
       </a>
       , which tend not to be marked for most of the grammatical categories.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Conjugation
       </b>
       is also the traditional name for a group of verbs that share a similar conjugation pattern in a particular language (a
       <i>
        verb class
       </i>
       ). For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       is said to have four conjugations of verbs. This means that any
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regular_verb" title="Regular verb">
        regular
       </a>
       Latin verb can be conjugated in any person, number, tense, mood, and voice by knowing which of the four conjugation groups it belongs to, and its principal parts. A verb that does not follow all of the standard conjugation patterns of the language is said to be an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_verb" title="Irregular verb">
        irregular verb
       </a>
       . The system of all conjugated variants of a particular verb or class of verbs is called a
       <b>
        verb paradigm
       </b>
       ; this may be presented in the form of a
       <b>
        conjugation table
       </b>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Verbal_agreement">
        Verbal agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Verbal agreement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        Verbal agreement
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        concord
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morpho
       </a>
       -
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntactic
       </a>
       construct in which properties of the
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Object_(grammar)" title="Object (grammar)">
        objects
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       are indicated by the verb form. Verbs are then said to
       <a href="/wiki/Agreement_(linguistics)" title="Agreement (linguistics)">
        agree
       </a>
       with their subjects (resp. objects).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       verbs exhibit subject agreement of the following sort: whereas
       <i>
        I go
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you go
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        we go
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        they go
       </i>
       are all grammatical in standard English,
       <i>
        she go
       </i>
       is not (except in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subjunctive" title="Subjunctive">
        subjunctive
       </a>
       , as "They requested that
       <i>
        she go
       </i>
       with them"). Instead, a special form of the verb
       <i>
        to go
       </i>
       has to be used to produce
       <i>
        she goes
       </i>
       . On the other hand
       <i>
        I goes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you goes
       </i>
       etc. are not grammatical in standard English. (Things are different in some English dialects that lack agreement.) A few English verbs have no special forms that indicate subject agreement (
       <i>
        I may
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you may
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she may
       </i>
       ), and the verb
       <i>
        to be
       </i>
       has an additional form
       <i>
        am
       </i>
       that can only be used with the pronoun
       <i>
        I
       </i>
       as the subject.
      </p>
      <p>
       Verbs in written
       <a href="/wiki/French_verbs" title="French verbs">
        French
       </a>
       exhibit more intensive agreement
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       than English verbs:
       <i>
        je suis
       </i>
       (I am),
       <i>
        tu es
       </i>
       ("you are", singular
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T-V_distinction" title="T-V distinction">
        informal
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        elle est
       </i>
       (she is),
       <i>
        nous sommes
       </i>
       (we are),
       <i>
        vous êtes
       </i>
       ("you are", plural),
       <i>
        ils sont
       </i>
       (they are). Historically, English used to have a similar verbal paradigm. Some historic verb forms are used by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       as slightly archaic or more formal variants (
       <i>
        I do
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        thou dost
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she doth
       </i>
       , typically used by nobility) of the modern forms.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages with verbal agreement can leave certain subjects implicit when the subject is fully determined by the verb form. In
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , for instance, subject pronouns do not need to be explicitly present, even though in French, its close relative, they are obligatory. The Spanish equivalent to the French
       <i>
        je suis
       </i>
       (I am) can be simply
       <i>
        soy
       </i>
       (lit. "am"). The pronoun
       <i>
        yo
       </i>
       (I) in the explicit form
       <i>
        yo soy
       </i>
       is only required for emphasis or to clear ambiguity in complex texts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some languages have a richer agreement system in which verbs also agree with some or all of their objects.
       <a href="/wiki/Ubykh_language" title="Ubykh language">
        Ubykh
       </a>
       exhibits verbal agreement for the subject, direct object, indirect object, benefaction and ablative objects (
       <i>
        a.w3.s.xe.n.t'u.n
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        you gave it to him for me
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       can show agreement not only for subject, direct object and indirect object, but it also on occasion exhibits agreement for the listener as the implicit benefactor:
       <i>
        autoa ekarri digute
       </i>
       means "they brought us the car" (neuter agreement for listener), but
       <i>
        autoa ekarri ziguten
       </i>
       means "they brought us the car" (agreement for feminine singular listener).
      </p>
      <p>
       Languages with a rich agreement morphology facilitate relatively free word order without leading to increased ambiguity. The canonical word order in Basque is subject–object–verb. However, all permutations of subject, verb and object are permitted.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nonverbal_person_agreement">
        Nonverbal person agreement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Grammatical_conjugation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nonverbal person agreement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some languages,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       predicative
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
        copular
       </a>
       complements receive a form of person agreement that is distinct from that used on ordinary
       <a href="/wiki/Predicative_verb" title="Predicative verb">
        predicative verbs
       </a>
       . Although this is a form of conjugation in that it refers back to the person of the subject, it is not “verbal” because it always derives from
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       that have become
       <a href="/wiki/Clitic" title="Clitic">
        cliticised
       </a>
       to the nouns to which they refer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An example of nonverbal person agreement, along with contrasting verbal conjugation, can be found from
       <a href="/wiki/Beja_language" title="Beja language">
        Beja
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stassen40_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stassen40-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (person agreement affixes in bold):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         wun.tu.
         <b>
          wi
         </b>
        </i>
        , “you (fem.) are big”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         hadá.b.
         <b>
          wa
         </b>
        </i>
        , “you (masc.) are a sheik”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          e
         </b>
         .n.fór
        </i>
        , “he flees”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Another example can be found from
       <a href="/wiki/Ket_language" title="Ket language">
        Ket
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stassen40_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stassen40-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         fèmba.
         <b>
          di
         </b>
        </i>
        , “I am a
        <a href="/wiki/Tungusic_peoples" title="Tungusic peoples">
         Tungus
        </a>
        ”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <b>
          dɨ
         </b>
         .fen
        </i>
        , “I am standing”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic
       </a>
       , and a few
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indigenous_Australian_languages" title="Indigenous Australian languages">
        Australian Aboriginal languages
       </a>
       , predicative adjectives and copular complements take affixes that are identical to those used on predicative verbs, but their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negation_(linguistics)" title="Negation (linguistics)">
        negation
       </a>
       is different. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Turkish_language" title="Turkish language">
        Turkish
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         koş.u.yor.
         <b>
          sun
         </b>
        </i>
        “you are running”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         çavuş.
         <b>
          sun
         </b>
        </i>
        “you are a sergeant”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Under negation this becomes (negative affixes in bold):
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         koş.
         <b>
          mu
         </b>
         .yor.sun
        </i>
        “you are not running”
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         çavuş
         <b>
          değil
         </b>
         .sin
        </i>
        “you are not a sergeant”
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       For this reason, the person agreement affixes used with predicative adjectives and nominals in Turkic languages are considered to be nonverbal in character. In some analyses, they are viewed as a form of verbal takeover by a copular strategy.
      </p>
