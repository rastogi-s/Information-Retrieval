      <p>
       <b>
        Labial consonants
       </b>
       are consonants in which one or both lips are the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Active_articulator" title="Active articulator">
        active articulator
       </a>
       . The two common labial articulations are
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabials
       </a>
       , articulated using both lips, and
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodentals
       </a>
       , articulated with the lower lip against the upper teeth, both of which are present in
       <a href="/wiki/English_phonology" title="English phonology">
        English
       </a>
       . A third labial articulation is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dentolabial_consonant" title="Dentolabial consonant">
        dentolabials
       </a>
       , articulated with the upper lip against the lower teeth (the reverse of labiodental), normally only found in pathological speech. Generally precluded are
       <a href="/wiki/Linguolabial_consonant" title="Linguolabial consonant">
        linguolabials
       </a>
       , in which the tip of the
       <a href="/wiki/Tongue" title="Tongue">
        tongue
       </a>
       contacts the posterior side of the upper lip, making them
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronals
       </a>
       , though sometimes, they behave as labial consonants.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (January 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The most common distribution between bilabials and labiodentals is the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       one, in which the
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stops
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [m]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       , are bilabial and the
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricatives
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       , are labiodental.
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_fricative" title="Bilabial fricative">
        Bilabial fricatives
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilabial_approximant" title="Bilabial approximant">
        bilabial approximant
       </a>
       do not exist in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       , but they occur in many languages. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       consonant written
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       is pronounced, between vowels, as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_approximant" title="Voiced bilabial approximant">
        voiced bilabial approximant
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lip rounding, or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labialisation" title="Labialisation">
        labialization
       </a>
       , is a common
       <a href="/wiki/Approximant_consonant" title="Approximant consonant">
        approximant
       </a>
       -like
       <a href="/wiki/Secondary_articulation" title="Secondary articulation">
        co-articulatory
       </a>
       feature. English
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labio-velar_approximant" title="Voiced labio-velar approximant">
        voiced labialized velar approximant
       </a>
       , which is far more common than the purely
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bilabial_approximant" title="Bilabial approximant">
        labial approximant
       </a>
       [β̞]. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_the_Caucasus" title="Languages of the Caucasus">
        languages of the Caucasus
       </a>
       , labialized
       <a href="/wiki/Dorsal_consonant" title="Dorsal consonant">
        dorsals
       </a>
       like /kʷ/ and /qʷ/ are very common.
      </p>
      <p>
       Very few languages, however, make a distinction purely between
       <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
        bilabials
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
        labiodentals
       </a>
       , making "labial" usually a sufficient specification of a language's
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phonemes
       </a>
       . One exception is
       <a href="/wiki/Ewe_language" title="Ewe language">
        Ewe
       </a>
       , which has both kinds of fricatives, but the labiodentals are produced with greater articulatory force.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lack_of_labials">
        Lack of labials
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Labial_consonant&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lack of labials">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While most languages make use of purely labial phonemes, a few generally lack them. Examples are
       <a href="/wiki/Tlingit_language" title="Tlingit language">
        Tlingit
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eyak_language" title="Eyak language">
        Eyak
       </a>
       (both
       <a href="/wiki/Na-Dene_languages" title="Na-Dene languages">
        Na-Dené
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Wichita_language" title="Wichita language">
        Wichita
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Caddoan_languages" title="Caddoan languages">
        Caddoan
       </a>
       ), and the
       <a href="/wiki/Iroquoian_languages" title="Iroquoian languages">
        Iroquoian languages
       </a>
       except
       <a href="/wiki/Cherokee_language" title="Cherokee language">
        Cherokee
       </a>
       . All of these languages have seen labials introduced under the influence of English.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many of these languages are transcribed with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       and with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labialized_consonant" title="Labialized consonant">
        labialized consonants
       </a>
       . However, it is not always clear to what extent the lips are involved in such sounds. In the Iroquoian languages, for example,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /w/
       </span>
       involved little apparent rounding of the lips. See the
       <a href="/wiki/Tillamook_language" title="Tillamook language">
        Tillamook language
       </a>
       for an example of a language with "rounded" consonants and vowels that do not have any actual labialization.
      </p>
