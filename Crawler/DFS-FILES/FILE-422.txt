       In
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical linguistics
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        vowel breaking
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        vowel fracture
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <b>
        diphthongization
       </b>
       is the change of a
       <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">
        monophthong
       </a>
       into a
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthong
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Triphthong" title="Triphthong">
        triphthong

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Vowel breaking may be unconditioned or conditioned. It may be triggered by the presence of another sound or by stress, or it may be triggered in no particular way.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Assimilation">
        Assimilation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Assimilation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sometimes vowel breaking is defined as a subtype of diphthongization; then, it refers to harmonic (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilatory
       </a>
       ) process that involves diphthongization triggered by a following vowel or consonant.
      </p>
      <p>
       The original pure vowel typically breaks into two segments, and the first segment matches the original vowel and the second segment is harmonic with the nature of the triggering vowel or consonant. For example, the second segment may be /u/ (a back vowel) if the following vowel or consonant is back (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Pharyngeal_consonant" title="Pharyngeal consonant">
        pharyngeal
       </a>
       ), and the second segment may be /i/ (a front vowel) if the following vowel or consonant is front (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus, vowel breaking, in this restricted sense, can be viewed as an example of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       of a vowel to a following vowel or consonant.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Unconditioned">
        Unconditioned
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Unconditioned">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Vowel breaking is sometimes not assimilatory, not triggered by a neighboring sound. This is the case with the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">
        Great Vowel Shift
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       : all cases of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /iː uː/
       </span>
       changed to diphthongs.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stress">
        Stress
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Stress">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sometimes vowel breaking occurs only in stressed syllables. For instance, Vulgar Latin open-mid
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɛ ɔ/
       </span>
       changed to diphthongs only when stressed.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Vowel breaking is a very common sound change in the history of the English language, occurring at least three times (with some varieties adding a fourth) listed here in reverse chronological order:
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Southern_American_English">
        Southern American English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Southern American English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">
        Southern American English
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Vowel breaking is characteristic of the "Southern drawl" of
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_American_English" title="Southern American English">
        Southern American English
       </a>
       , where the short
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowels
       </a>
       have developed a glide up to [j], and then in some areas back down to schwa:
       <i>
        pat
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pæjət]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        pet
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pɛjət]
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        pit
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [pɪjət]
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Great_Vowel_Shift">
        Great Vowel Shift
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Great Vowel Shift">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The Great Vowel Shift changed the long vowels
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /iː uː/
       </span>
       to diphthongs. They became
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_English" title="Modern English">
        Modern English
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /aɪ aʊ/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Old English
        <i>
         <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">
          īs
         </span>
        </i>
        &gt; Modern English
        <i>
         ice
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /aɪs/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Old English
        <i>
         <span lang="ang" xml:lang="ang">
          hūs
         </span>
        </i>
        &gt; Modern English
        <i>
         house
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /haʊs/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Middle_English">
        Middle English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Middle English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English_phonology#Breaking" title="Middle English phonology">
        Middle English phonology § Breaking
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In early
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       , a vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       was inserted between a front vowel and a following
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ç]
       </span>
       in this context), and a vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       was inserted between a back vowel and a following
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /h/
       </span>
       (pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       in this context). This is a prototypical example of the narrow sense of "vowel breaking" as described above: The original vowel breaks into a diphthong that assimilates to the following consonant, gaining a front
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       before a
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal consonant
       </a>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       before a
       <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
        velar consonant
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Old_English">
        Old English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Old English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English_phonology#Breaking_and_retraction" title="Old English phonology">
        Old English phonology § Breaking and retraction
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English_phonology#Back_mutation" title="Old English phonology">
        Old English phonology § Back mutation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Old_English" title="Old English">
        Old English
       </a>
       , two forms of harmonic vowel breaking occurred: breaking and retraction, and back mutation.
      </p>
      <p>
       In prehistoric Old English, breaking and retraction changed stressed short and long front vowels
       <i>
        i, e, æ
       </i>
       to short and long diphthongs spelled
       <i>
        io, eo, ea
       </i>
       when followed by
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       or by
       <i>
        r, l
       </i>
       + another consonant (short vowels only), and sometimes
       <i>
        w
       </i>
       (only for certain short vowels).
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Proto-Germanic
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *fallan
        </span>
        &gt; Anglo-Frisian
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *fællan
        </span>
        &gt; Old English
        <i>
         feallan
        </i>
        "fall"
       </li>
       <li>
        PG
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *erþō
        </span>
        &gt; OE
        <i>
         eorþe
        </i>
        "earth"
       </li>
       <li>
        PG
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         *lirnoːjan
        </span>
        &gt; OE
        <i>
         liornan
        </i>
        "learn"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In late prehistoric Old English, back mutation changed short front
       <i>
        i, e, æ
       </i>
       to short diphthongs spelled
       <i>
        io, eo, ea
       </i>
       before a back vowel in the next syllable, if the intervening consonant is of a certain nature. The specific nature of which consonants trigger back umlaut and which block them varies from dialect to dialect.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Old_Norse">
        Old Norse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Old Norse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Norse#Breaking" title="Old Norse">
        Old Norse phonological processes
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Germanic_language" title="Proto-Germanic language">
        Proto-Germanic
       </a>
       stressed short
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        ja
       </i>
       or (before
       <i>
        u
       </i>
       )
       <i>
        jǫ
       </i>
       regularly in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Norse_language" title="Old Norse language">
        Old Norse
       </a>
       except after
       <i>
        w, r, l
       </i>
       . Examples are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        PG *
        <i>
         ek(a)
        </i>
        "I" → (east) ON
        <i>
         jak
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        <i>
         jag
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
         Danish
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Bokm%C3%A5l" title="Bokmål">
         Norwegian Bokmål
        </a>
        <i>
         jeg
        </i>
        , and
        <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
         Icelandic
        </a>
        <i>
         ek
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         ég
        </i>
        (but
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jutlandic" title="Jutlandic">
         Jutlandic
        </a>
        <i>
         æ, a
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">
         Nynorsk
        </a>
        <i>
         eg
        </i>
        ).
        <ul>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
           Faroese
          </a>
          has both. The standard form is
          <i>
           eg
          </i>
          , while the dialects of
          <a href="/wiki/Su%C3%B0uroy" title="Suðuroy">
           Suðuroy
          </a>
          have
          <i>
           jeg
          </i>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        PG *
        <i>
         hertōn
        </i>
        "heart" → ON
        <i>
         hjarta
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        hjärta,
        <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
         Faroese
        </a>
        <i>
         hjarta
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Nynorsk" title="Nynorsk">
         Norwegian Nynorsk
        </a>
        <i>
         hjarta
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
         Danish
        </a>
        <i>
         hjerte
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        PG *
        <i>
         erþō
        </i>
        "earth" → Proto-Norse *
        <i>
         erþū
        </i>
        → ON
        <i>
         jǫrð
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Swedish_language" title="Swedish language">
         Swedish
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
         Danish
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Norwegian_language" title="Norwegian language">
         Norwegian
        </a>
        <i>
         jord
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Faroese_language" title="Faroese language">
         Faroese
        </a>
        <i>
         jørð
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       According to some scholars,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the diphthongisation of
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       is an unconditioned sound change, whereas other scholars speak about
       <a href="/wiki/Epenthesis" title="Epenthesis">
        epenthesis
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_umlaut" title="Germanic umlaut">
        umlaut
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scottish_Gaelic">
        Scottish Gaelic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Scottish Gaelic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Vowel breaking is present in Scottish Gaelic with the following changes occurring often but variably between dialects: Archaic Irish
       <i>
        eː
       </i>
       → Scottish Gaelic
       <i>
        iə
       </i>
       and Archaic Irish
       <i>
        oː
       </i>
       → Scottish Gaelic
       <i>
        uə
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Specifically, central dialects have more vowel breaking than others.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_languages">
        Romance languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Romance languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       underwent vowel breaking. The
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       open vowels
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɛ/
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɔ/
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stressed
       </a>
       position underwent breaking only in open syllables in
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , but in both open and closed syllables in
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . Vowel breaking was completely absent in
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       . The result of breaking varies between languages:
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       became
       <i>
        ie
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ue
       </i>
       in Spanish,
       <i>
        ie
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        uo
       </i>
       in Italian, and
       <i>
        ie
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        eu
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ø/
       </span>
       in French.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romanian">
        Romanian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Romanian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Romanian#Breaking_of_stressed_open_e" title="History of Romanian">
        History of Romanian § Breaking of stressed open e
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Romanian#Breaking_of_e_and_o" title="History of Romanian">
        History of Romanian § Breaking of e and o
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Romanian_language" title="Romanian language">
        Romanian
       </a>
       underwent the general Romance breaking only with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɛ/
       </span>
       , as it did not have
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɔ/
       </span>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         pellis
        </i>
        &gt; Romanian
        <i>
         piele
        </i>
        "skin"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It underwent a later breaking of stressed
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       to
       <i>
        ea
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        oa
       </i>
       before a mid or open vowel:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         porta
        </i>
        &gt; Romanian
        <i>
         poartă
        </i>
        "gate"
       </li>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         flōs
        </i>
        (stem
        <i>
         flōr-
        </i>
        ) &gt; Romanian
        <i>
         floare
        </i>
        "flower"
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Sometimes a word underwent both forms of breaking in succession:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Latin
        <i>
         petra
        </i>
        &gt; Early Romanian
        <i>
         pietră
        </i>
        &gt; Romanian
        <i>
         piatră
        </i>
        "stone" (where
        <i>
         ia
        </i>
        results from hypothetical *
        <i>
         iea
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The diphthongs that resulted from the Romance and the Romanian breakings were modified when they occurred after palatalized consonants.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quebec_French">
        Quebec French
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Quebec French">
         edit

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French_phonology" title="Quebec French phonology">
        Quebec French phonology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
        Quebec French

      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tɑɔ̯ʁ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         père
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [pɛːʁ]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [paɛ̯ʁ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fleur
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [flœːʁ]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [flaœ̯ʁ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fort
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [fɔːʁ]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [fɑɔ̯ʁ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         autre
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [oːtʁ̥]
        </span>
        →
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ou̯tʁ̥]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>

        </span>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [õʊ̯̃z]
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proto-Indo-European">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Vowel_breaking&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Proto-Indo-European">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some scholars
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       believe that
       <a href="/wiki/Proto-Indo-European_language" title="Proto-Indo-European language">
        Proto-Indo-European
       </a>
       (PIE)
       <i>
        i, u
       </i>
       has a kind of breaking before an original
       <a href="/wiki/Laryngeal_theory" title="Laryngeal theory">
        laryngeal
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
        Armenian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">
        Tocharian
       </a>
       , whereas the other Indo-European languages have monophthongs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto_Indo-European_language" title="Proto Indo-European language">
         PIE
        </a>
        *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          gʷih
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          wos
         </span>
        </i>
        → *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          gʷioHwos
         </span>
        </i>
        "alive" →
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
         Gk.
        </a>
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         ζωός
        </span>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">
         Toch. B
        </a>
        <i>
         śāw-, śāy-
        </i>
        (but
        <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
         Skt.
        </a>
        <i>
         jīvá-
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Lat.
        </a>
        <i>
         vīvus
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto_Indo-European_language" title="Proto Indo-European language">
         PIE
        </a>
        *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          protih
          <sub>
           3
          </sub>
          kʷom
         </span>
        </i>
        → *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          protioHkʷom
         </span>
        </i>
        "front side" →
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
         Gk.
        </a>
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         πρόσωπον
        </span>
        "face",
        <a href="/wiki/Tocharian_languages" title="Tocharian languages">
         Toch. B
        </a>
        <i>
         pratsāko
        </i>
        "breast" (but
        <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
         Skt.
        </a>
        <i>
         prátīka-
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto_Indo-European_language" title="Proto Indo-European language">
         PIE
        </a>
        *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          duh
          <sub>
           2
          </sub>
          ros
         </span>
        </i>
        → *
        <i>
         <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
          duaHros
         </span>
        </i>
        "long" →
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
         Gk.
        </a>
        <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
         δηρός
        </span>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Armenian_language" title="Armenian language">
         Arm.
        </a>
        *
        <i>
         twār
        </i>
        →
        <i>
         erkar
        </i>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">
         Skt.
        </a>
        <i>
         dūrá-
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Lat.
        </a>
        <i>
         dūrus
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       However, the hypothesis has not been widely adopted.
