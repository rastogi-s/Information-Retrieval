      <p>
       <b>
        Juan Luna y Novicio
       </b>
       (October 23, 1857 – December 7, 1899), better known as
       <b>
        Juan Luna
       </b>
       was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Filipino_people" title="Filipino people">
        Filipino
       </a>
       painter, sculptor and a political activist of the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Revolution" title="Philippine Revolution">
        Philippine Revolution
       </a>
       during the late 19th century. He became one of the first recognized Philippine artists.
      </p>
      <p>
       His winning the gold medal in the 1884 Madrid Exposition of Fine Arts, along with the silver win of fellow Filipino painter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Resurrecci%C3%B3n_Hidalgo" title="Félix Resurrección Hidalgo">
        Félix Resurrección Hidalgo
       </a>
       , prompted a celebration which was a major highlight in the memoirs of members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_Movement" title="Propaganda Movement">
        Propaganda Movement
       </a>
       , with the fellow
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ilustrado" title="Ilustrado">
         Ilustrados
        </a>
       </i>
       toasting to the two painters' good health and to the brotherhood between Spain and the Philippines.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regarded for work done in the manner of the Spanish, Italian and French academies of his time, Luna painted literary and historical scenes, some with an underscore of political commentary. His allegorical works were inspired with classical balance, and often showed figures in theatrical poses.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">
        Early life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Born in the town of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Badoc,_Ilocos_Norte" title="Badoc, Ilocos Norte">
        Badoc, Ilocos Norte
       </a>
       in the northern Philippines, Juan N. Luna was the third among the seven children of Joaquín Posadas Luna and Laureana Ancheta Novicio-Luna. In 1861, the Luna family moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">
        Manila
       </a>
       and he went to
       <a href="/wiki/Ateneo_de_Manila_University" title="Ateneo de Manila University">
        Ateneo Municipal de Manila
       </a>
       where he obtained his
       <a href="/wiki/Bachelor_of_Arts" title="Bachelor of Arts">
        Bachelor of Arts
       </a>
       degree. He excelled in painting and drawing, and was influenced by his brother,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manuel_Luna_(painter)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manuel Luna (painter) (page does not exist)">
        Manuel N. Luna
       </a>
       , who, according to Filipino patriot
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Rizal" title="José Rizal">
        José Rizal
       </a>
       , was a better painter than Juan himself.
      </p>
      <p>
       Luna enrolled at Escuela Nautica de Manila (now
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Merchant_Marine_Academy" title="Philippine Merchant Marine Academy">
        Philippine Merchant Marine Academy
       </a>
       ) and became a sailor. He took drawing lessons under the illustrious painting teacher
       <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Guerrero" title="Lorenzo Guerrero">
        Lorenzo Guerrero
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ermita,_Manila" title="Ermita, Manila">
        Ermita, Manila
       </a>
       . He also enrolled in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_of_Fine_Arts" title="Academy of Fine Arts">
        Academy of Fine Arts
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Academia_de_Dibujo_y_Pintura" title="Academia de Dibujo y Pintura">
        Academia de Dibujo y Pintura
       </a>
       ) in Manila where he was influenced and taught how to draw by the Spanish artist
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Agustin_Saez&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Agustin Saez (page does not exist)">
        Agustin Saez
       </a>
       . Unfortunately, Luna's vigorous brush strokes displeased his teacher and Luna was discharged from the academy. However, Guerrero was impressed by his skill and urged Luna to travel to Cubao to further pursue his work.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Travel_abroad">
        Travel abroad
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Travel abroad">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1877 Manuel and Juan Luna traveled to Europe, where Manuel studied music and Juan painting. Juan entered the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Escuela_de_Bellas_Artes_de_San_Fernando" title="Escuela de Bellas Artes de San Fernando">
        Escuela de Bellas Artes de San Fernando
       </a>
       , where he befriended the painter Don
       <a href="/wiki/Alejo_Vera" title="Alejo Vera">
        Alejo Vera
       </a>
       . Luna was discontented with the style of teaching in school and decided that it would be much better to work with Vera.
      </p>
      <p>
       Vera brought him to Rome for some of his commissions, and Luna was exposed to the art of the
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       painters. It was in 1878 when his artistic talents was established with the opening of the first art exposition in
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       which was called the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Exposici%C3%B3n_Nacional_de_Bellas_Artes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes (page does not exist)">
        Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes
       </a>
       (National Demonstration of Fine Arts). From then on, Luna became engrossed in painting and produced a collection of paintings that he exhibited in the 1881 Exposition.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1881, his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Muerte_de_Cleopatra" title="La Muerte de Cleopatra">
         La Muerte de Cleopatra
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Death_of_Cleopatra" title="The Death of Cleopatra">
         The Death of Cleopatra
        </a>
       </i>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-KD_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-KD-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Christies_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Christies-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       won him a silver medal and came in second place. Luna's growing reputation as an artist led to a
       <i>
        pensionado
       </i>
       (pension) scholarship at 600 pesos annually through the Ayuntamiento of Manila. The condition was that he was obliged to develop a painting which captured the essence of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_history" title="Philippine history">
        Philippine history
       </a>
       which would then become the Ayuntamiento's property.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="As_an_artist">
        As an artist
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: As an artist">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Juan_Luna.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1340" data-file-width="1740" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Juan_Luna.jpg/220px-Juan_Luna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Juan_Luna.jpg/330px-Juan_Luna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Juan_Luna.jpg/440px-Juan_Luna.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Juan_Luna.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Juan Luna in his Paris studio.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 1883 Luna started the painting demanded of him by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ayuntamiento" title="Ayuntamiento">
        Ayuntamiento
       </a>
       . In May 1884, he shipped the large
       <a href="/wiki/Canvas" title="Canvas">
        canvas
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spoliarium" title="Spoliarium">
        Spoliarium
       </a>
       to Madrid for the year's Exposición Nacional de Bellas Artes. He was the first recipient of the three gold medals awarded in the exhibition and Luna gained recognition among the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Connoisseurs" title="Connoisseurs">
        connoisseurs
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Art_critics" title="Art critics">
        art critics
       </a>
       present. On June 25, 1884, Filipino and Spanish nobles organized an event celebrating Luna's win in the exhibition. That evening, Rizal prepared a speech for his friend, addressing the two significant things of his art work, which included the glorification of genius and the grandeur of his artistic skills.
      </p>
      <p>
       Luna developed a friendly relationship with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Spain" title="King of Spain">
        King of Spain
       </a>
       and was later commissioned by the Spanish Senate to paint a large canvas which was called the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Batalla_de_Lepanto" title="La Batalla de Lepanto">
         La Batalla de Lepanto
        </a>
        (The Battle of Lepanto)
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He moved to Paris in 1885 where he opened his own studio and befriended Hidalgo. A year after, he finished the piece
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El_Pacto_de_Sangre" title="El Pacto de Sangre">
         El Pacto de Sangre
        </a>
        (The Blood Compact)
       </i>
       in accordance with the agreement he had with the Ayuntamiento of Manila. Depicted in this piece was the
       <a href="/wiki/Blood_compact" title="Blood compact">
        blood compact
       </a>
       ceremony between the
       <a href="/wiki/Datu_Sikatuna" title="Datu Sikatuna">
        Datu Sikatuna
       </a>
       , one of the lords in Bohol island, and the Spanish conquistador
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_L%C3%B3pez_de_Legazpi" title="Miguel López de Legazpi">
        Miguel López de Legazpi
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Inquirer_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Inquirer-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is now displayed in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Malaca%C3%B1an_Palace" title="Malacañan Palace">
        Malacañan Palace
       </a>
       . He also sent two other paintings in addition to the one required; the second canvas sent to Manila was a portrait of López de Legazpi reconstructed by Luna from his recollection of a similar portrait he saw in the hall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cabildo_(council)" title="Cabildo (council)">
        Cabildo
       </a>
       , and the third was of Governor-general
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Blanco_y_Erenas" title="Ramón Blanco y Erenas">
        Ramón Blanco y Erenas
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1887, Luna once again traveled back to Spain to enter in that year's Exposition two of his pieces, the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Batalla_de_Lepanto" title="La Batalla de Lepanto">
         La Batalla de Lepanto
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rendici%C3%B3n_de_Granada&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rendición de Granada (page does not exist)">
         Rendición de Granada
        </a>
        (Surrender of Granada)
       </i>
       , which both won in the exhibition. He celebrated his triumph with his friends in Madrid with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Graciano_L%C3%B3pez-Jaena" title="Graciano López-Jaena">
        Graciano López-Jaena
       </a>
       delivered Luna a congratulatory speech. Luna's paintings are generally described as being vigorous and dramatic. With its elements of
       <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
        Romanticism
       </a>
       , his style shows the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">
        Delacroix
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rembrandt" title="Rembrandt">
        Rembrandt
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daumier" title="Daumier">
        Daumier
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Juan Luna masterpiece “¿A Do...Va la Nave?” sold for P46.8M at Makati auction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Juan_Luna_masterpiece_sold_for_P46.8M_at_Makati_auction_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Juan_Luna_masterpiece_sold_for_P46.8M_at_Makati_auction-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Marriage">
        Marriage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Marriage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On December 8, 1886, Luna married
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Maria_de_la_Paz_Pardo_de_Tavera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Maria de la Paz Pardo de Tavera (page does not exist)">
        Maria de la Paz Pardo de Tavera
       </a>
       , a sister of his friend Felix and
       <a href="/wiki/Trinidad_Pardo_de_Tavera" title="Trinidad Pardo de Tavera">
        Trinidad Pardo de Tavera
       </a>
       . The couple traveled to
       <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
        Venice
       </a>
       and Rome and settled in Paris. They had one son, whom they named
       <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9s_Luna_de_San_Pedro" title="Andrés Luna de San Pedro">
        Andrés
       </a>
       , and a daughter, Maria de la Paz, nicknamed Bibi, who died in infancy. Luna was fond of his wife. However, the jealous Luna frequently accused Paz of having an affair with a certain Monsieur Dussaq. Finally in a fit of jealousy, he killed his wife and mother-in-law and wounded his brother-in-law, Felix, on September 22, 1892. He was arrested and murder charges were filed against him.
      </p>
      <p>
       Luna was acquitted of charges on February 8, 1893, on the grounds of a crime of passion. Temporary insanity; the "unwritten law" at the time forgave men for killing unfaithful wives.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Case_of_Juan_Luna_Is_an_Argument_for_Legalizing_Divorce_in_RP_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Case_of_Juan_Luna_Is_an_Argument_for_Legalizing_Divorce_in_RP-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was ordered to pay the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pardo_de_Taveras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pardo de Taveras (page does not exist)">
        Pardo de Taveras
       </a>
       a sum of one thousand six hundred fifty one francs and eighty three cents, and an additional twenty five francs for postage, in addition to the 1 Franc of claims for damages ("dommages-intérêts"). Five days later, Luna went to Madrid with his brother,
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Luna" title="Antonio Luna">
        Antonio Luna
       </a>
       , and his son, Andrés.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Murcia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A few years later, in October 1889, the artist travelled to southeast Spain, Murcia, as contained in the news release at the time, was provisionally installed in Moratalla where he made a portrait of the daughter of Juan Tamayo, an eminent man in this town. Passing through Murcia gave two portraits of the Queen Regent had made to the Provincial and City Council, respectively, and one of these was acquired by the provincial body for five hundred seventy-five pesetas installed on top of the Hall of Sessions of the Provincial Palace and then going to the presidential office in April 1932, one year after the proclamation of the Second Spanish Republic, enters as a deposit Museum of Murcia along with several official portraits of royal iconography, being included in the different inventories and catalogs without sufficient data or references, just a terse "DJ.LUNA" as artistic attribution.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Final_years">
        Final years
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Luna&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Final years">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1894 Luna moved back to the Philippines and traveled to Japan in 1896, returning during the
       <b>
        Philippine Revolution of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cry_of_Balintawak" title="Cry of Balintawak">
         Cry of Balintawak
        </a>
        .
       </b>
       Unfortunately, on September 16, 1896, he and his brother
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Luna" title="Antonio Luna">
        Antonio Luna
       </a>
       were arrested by Spanish authorities for being involved with the
       <a href="/wiki/Katipunan" title="Katipunan">
        Katipunan
       </a>
       rebel army.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PS_2008_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PS_2008-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite his imprisonment, Luna was still able to produce a work of art which he gave to a visiting priest. He was pardoned by the Spanish courts on May 27, 1897 and was released from prison and he traveled back to Spain in July.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foreman_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Foreman-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         394
        </span>
       </sup>
       He returned to Manila in November 1898.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Foreman_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Foreman-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         394
        </span>
       </sup>
       In 1898, he was appointed by the executive board of the Philippine revolutionary government as a member of the Paris delegation which was working for the diplomatic recognition of the
       <i>
        República Filipina (
        <a href="/wiki/First_Philippine_Republic" title="First Philippine Republic">
         Philippine Republic
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       . In 1899, upon the signing of the
       <a href="/wiki/Treaty_of_Paris_(1898)" title="Treaty of Paris (1898)">
        Treaty of Paris (1898)
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Yale_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Yale-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Luna was named a member of the delegation to Washington, D.C. to press for the recognition of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philippine_government" title="Philippine government">
        Philippine government
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       He traveled back to the Philippines in December 1899 upon hearing of the murder of his brother Antonio by the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Kawit_Battalion&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Kawit Battalion (page does not exist)">
        Kawit Battalion
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cabanatuan" title="Cabanatuan">
        Cabanatuan
       </a>
       . He traveled to Hong Kong and died there on December 7, 1899 from a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heart_attack" title="Heart attack">
        heart attack
       </a>
       . His remains were buried in Hong Kong and in 1920 were exhumed and kept in Andrés Luna's house, to be later transferred to a niche at the Crypt of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Agustin_Church,_Manila" title="San Agustin Church, Manila">
        San Agustin Church
       </a>
       in the Philippines. Five years later, Juan would be reinstated as a world-renowned artist and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Peuple_et_Rois" title="Peuple et Rois">
         Peuple et Rois
        </a>
       </i>
       , his last major work, was acclaimed the best entry to the
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase_Exposition" title="Louisiana Purchase Exposition">
        Saint Louis World's Fair
       </a>
       in the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lopez_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lopez-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unfortunately some of his paintings were destroyed by fire in World War II.
      </p>
