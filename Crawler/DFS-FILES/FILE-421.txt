        consonants
       </a>
       , making them more
       <a href="/wiki/Sonority_hierarchy" title="Sonority hierarchy">
        sonorous
       </a>
       . The word
       <i>
        lenition
       </i>
       itself means "softening" or "weakening" (from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        lenis
       </i>
       = weak). Lenition can happen both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronic_analysis" title="Synchronic analysis">
        synchronically
       </a>
       (i.e. within a language at a particular point in time) and
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        diachronically
       </a>
       (i.e. as a
       <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_linguistics" title="Evolutionary linguistics">
        language changes over time
       </a>
       ). Lenition can involve such changes as making a consonant more sonorous, causing a consonant to lose its
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       (a phenomenon called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         debuccalization
        </a>
       </i>
       , which turns a consonant into a
       <a href="/wiki/Glottal_consonant" title="Glottal consonant">
        glottal consonant
       </a>
       like
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Glottal_stop" title="Glottal stop">
         ʔ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       ), or even causing a consonant to disappear entirely.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of synchronic lenition in
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       is found in
       <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">
        flapping
       </a>
       in some dialects: the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       of a word like
       <i>
        wait
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [weɪt]
       </span>
       becomes the more sonorous
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
         ɾ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in the related form
       <i>
        waiting
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈweɪɾɪŋ]
       </span>
       . Some dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       show
       <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
        debuccalization
       </a>
       of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_glottal_fricative" title="Voiceless glottal fricative">
         h
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       at the end of a
       <a href="/wiki/Syllable" title="Syllable">
        syllable
       </a>
       , so that a word like
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <i>
          estamos
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "we are" is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ehˈtamoh]
       </span>
       . An example of diachronic lenition can be found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       , where the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       of Latin
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         <i>
          patrem
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("father",
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accusative" title="Accusative">
        accusative
       </a>
       ) becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_and_alveolar_stops#Dental_or_denti-alveolar" title="Voiced dental and alveolar stops">
         d
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         <i>
          padre
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dental_approximant" title="Dental approximant">
         ð̞
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       in Spanish
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         <i>
          padre
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , while in French
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         <i>
          père
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       and Portuguese
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          pai
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       it has disappeared completely. Along with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       , lenition is one of the primary sources of
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_change" title="Phonological change">
        phonological change
       </a>
       of languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       In some languages, lenition has been grammaticalized into a
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_mutation" title="Consonant mutation">
        consonant mutation
       </a>
       , which means it is no longer triggered by its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological" title="Phonological">
        phonological
       </a>
       environment but is now governed by its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Syntactic" title="Syntactic">
        syntactic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphological
       </a>
       environment. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
        Welsh
       </a>
       , the word
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          cath
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       "cat" begins with the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       , but after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
        definite article
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          y
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , the
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       changes to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_stop" title="Voiced velar stop">
         ɡ
        </a>
        ]
       </span>
       : "the cat" in Welsh is
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          y gath
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       . This was historically due to intervocalic lenition, but in the plural, lenition does not happen, so "the cats" is
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          y cathod
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       , not *
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          y gathod
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       . The change of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɡ]
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        <span lang="cy" xml:lang="cy">
         <i>
          y gath
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       is thus caused by the syntax of the phrase, not by the phonological position of the consonant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The opposite of lenition,
       <a href="/wiki/Fortition" title="Fortition">
        fortition
       </a>
       , a sound change that makes a consonant "stronger", is less common.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types">
        Types
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lenition involves changes in
       <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
        manner of articulation
       </a>
       , sometimes accompanied by small changes in
       <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
        place of articulation
       </a>
       . There are two main lenition pathways: opening and sonorization. In both cases, a stronger sound becomes a weaker one. Lenition can be seen as a movement on the
       <a href="/wiki/Sonority_hierarchy" title="Sonority hierarchy">
        sonority hierarchy
       </a>
       from less sonorous to more sonorous, or on a strength hierarchy from stronger to weaker.
      </p>
      <p>
       In examples below, a
       <a href="/wiki/Greater-than_sign" title="Greater-than sign">
        greater-than sign
       </a>
       indicates that one sound changes to another. The notation
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ts]
       </span>
       means that
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       changes to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ts]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(sound_change)" title="Palatalization (sound change)">
        sound change of palatalization
       </a>
       sometimes involves lenition.
      </p>
      <p>
       Lenition includes the loss of a feature, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Glottalic_consonant" title="Glottalic consonant">
        deglottalization
       </a>
       , in which
       <a href="/wiki/Glottalization" title="Glottalization">
        glottalization
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Ejective_consonant" title="Ejective consonant">
        ejective articulation
       </a>
       is lost:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʼ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kˀ]
       </span>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [k]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The tables below show common sound changes involved in lenition. In some cases, lenition may skip one of the sound changes. The change voiceless stop &gt; fricative is more common than the series of changes voiceless stop &gt; affricate &gt; fricative.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Opening">
        Opening
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Opening">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the opening type of lenition, the articulation becomes more open with each step. Opening lenition involves several sound changes:
       <b>
        shortening
       </b>
       of double consonants,
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Affrication" title="Affrication">
         affrication
        </a>
       </b>
       of stops,
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Lenition#Spirantization" title="Lenition">
         spirantization
        </a>
       </b>
       of stops or affricates,
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Debuccalization" title="Debuccalization">
         debuccalization
        </a>
       </b>
       , and finally
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Elision" title="Elision">
         elision
        </a>
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tt]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tː]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        (shortening)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ts]
        </span>
        (affrication)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ts]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        (spirantization)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʔ]
        </span>
        ;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        (debuccalization)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ts]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʔ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [h]
        </span>
        &gt; ∅ (elision)
       </li>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sonorization">
        Sonorization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sonorization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The sonorization type involves voicing. Sonorizing lenition involves several sound changes: voicing, approximation, and vocalization.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="More sound changes are involved, and if there are specific names for the different changes, these should be listed. (February 2015)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [t]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [d]
        </span>
        (voicing)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [d]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ð]
        </span>
        (approximation)
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [d]
        </span>
        &gt;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [i]
        </span>
        (vocalization)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Sonorizing lenition occurs especially often intervocalically (between vowels). In this position, lenition can be seen as a type of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assimilation_(linguistics)" title="Assimilation (linguistics)">
        assimilation
       </a>
       of the consonant to the surrounding vowels, in which features of the consonant that are not present in the surrounding vowels (e.g. obstruction, voicelessness) are gradually eliminated.

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocalization">
        Vocalization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Vocalization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/L-vocalization" title="L-vocalization">
        <i>
         L
        </i>
        -vocalization
       </a>
       is a subtype of the sonorization type of lenition. It has two possible results: a velar approximant or back vowel, or a palatal approximant or front vowel. In
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       -vocalization of the sequence
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /al/
       </span>
       resulted in the
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthong
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /au/
       </span>
       , which was
       <a href="/wiki/Monophthongization" title="Monophthongization">
        monophthongized
       </a>
       , yielding the
       <a href="/wiki/Monophthong" title="Monophthong">
        monophthong
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       in Modern French.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed">
        Mixed
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mixed">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Sometimes a particular example of lenition mixes the opening and sonorization pathways. For example,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [kʰ]
       </span>
       may spirantize or open to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [x]
       </span>
       , then voice or sonorize to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Lenition can be seen in
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">
        Canadian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       , where
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       soften to a tap
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Flapping" title="Flapping">
        flapping
       </a>
       ) when not in initial position and followed by an unstressed vowel. For example, both
       <i>
        rate
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        raid
       </i>
       plus the suffix
       <i>
        -er
       </i>
       are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈɹeɪ̯ɾɚ]
       </span>
       . In many
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       dialects, a different lenition that affects only [t] takes place:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʔ]
       </span>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/T-glottalization" title="T-glottalization">
        T-glottalization
       </a>
       ). The Italian of Central and Southern Italy has a number of lenitions, the most widespread of which is the deaffrication of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͡ʃ/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       between vowels: post-pausal
       <i>
        cena
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈt͡ʃeːna]
       </span>
       'dinner' but post-vocalic
       <i>
        la cena
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [laˈʃeːna]
       </span>
       'the dinner'; the name
       <i>
        Luciano
       </i>
       , although structurally
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /luˈt͡ʃano/
       </span>
       , is normally pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [luˈʃaːno]
       </span>
       . In Tuscany,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d͡ʒ/
       </span>
       likewise is realized
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       between vowels, and in typical speech of Central Tuscany, the voiceless stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p t k/
       </span>
       in the same position are pronounced respectively
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɸ θ x/h]
       </span>
       , as in /la kasa/ → [laˈhaːsa] 'the house', /buko/ → [ˈbuːho] 'hole'.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diachronic">
        Diachronic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Diachronic">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diachronic_linguistics" title="Diachronic linguistics">
        Diachronic
       </a>
       lenition is found, for example, in the change from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       into
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , in which the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/intervocalic" title="wikt:intervocalic">
        intervocalic
       </a>
       voiceless stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [p t k]
       </span>
       first changed into their voiced counterparts
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b d ɡ]
       </span>
       , and later into the approximants or fricatives
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β̞ ð̞ ɣ̞]
       </span>
       :
       <i>
        vita
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        vida
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        lupa
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        loba
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        caeca
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        ciega
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        apotheca
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        bodega
       </i>
       . One stage in these changes goes beyond phonetic to have become a
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_change" title="Phonological change">
        phonological restructuring
       </a>
       , e.g. /lupa/ &gt; /loba/ (compare /lupa/ in Italian, with no change in the phonological status of /p/). The subsequent further weakening of the series to phonetic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β̞ ð̞ ɣ̞]
       </span>
       , as in [loβ̞a] is diachronic in the sense that the developments took place over time and displaced [b, d, g] as the normal pronunciations between vowels. It is also synchronic in an analysis of [β̞ ð̞ ɣ̞] as
       <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
        allophonic
       </a>
       realizations of /b, d, g/: illustrating with /b/, /bino/ 'wine' is pronounced [bino] after pause, but with [β̞] intervocalically, as in [de β̞ino] 'of wine'; likewise, /loba/ → [loβ̞a].
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar development occurred in the Celtic languages, where non-geminate intervocalic consonants were converted into their corresponding weaker counterparts through lenition (usually stops into fricatives but also laterals and trills into weaker laterals and taps), and voiceless stops became voiced. For example, Indo-European intervocalic -
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       - in
       <i>
        *
        <span class="Unicode" lang="ine" style="white-space: nowrap" title="Proto-Indo-European language" xml:lang="ine">
         teu̯teh₂
        </span>
       </i>
       "people" resulted in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Celtic" title="Proto-Celtic">
        Proto-Celtic
       </a>
       <i>
        *
        <span lang="cel" xml:lang="cel">
         tou̯tā
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Primitive_Irish" title="Primitive Irish">
        Primitive Irish
       </a>
       <i>
        *
        <span lang="pgl" xml:lang="pgl">
         tōθā
        </span>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Irish" title="Old Irish">
        Old Irish
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="sga" xml:lang="sga">
         túath
        </span>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tuaθ/
       </span>
       and ultimately complete deletion in modern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scots_Gaelic" title="Scots Gaelic">
        Scots Gaelic
       </a>
       <i>
        <span lang="gd" xml:lang="gd">
         tuath
        </span>
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t̪ʰuə/
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of historical lenition in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Germanic_language" title="Germanic language">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       is evidenced by Latin-English cognates such as
       <i>
        pater, tenuis
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        father, thin
       </i>
       . The Latin words preserved the original stops, which became fricatives in old Germanic by
       <a href="/wiki/Grimm%27s_law" title="Grimm's law">
        Grimm's law
       </a>
       . A few centuries later, the
       <a href="/wiki/High_German_consonant_shift" title="High German consonant shift">
        High German consonant shift
       </a>
       led to a second series of lenitions in
       <a href="/wiki/Old_High_German" title="Old High German">
        Old High German
       </a>
       , chiefly of post-vocalic stops, as evidenced in the English-German cognates
       <i>
        ripe, water
       </i>
       vs.
       <i>
        reif, Wasser
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Although actually a much more profound change encompassing syllable restructuring, simplification of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate_consonant" title="Geminate consonant">
        geminate consonants
       </a>
       as in the passage from Latin to Spanish such as CUPPA &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkopa/
       </span>
       'cup' is often viewed as a type of lenition (compare geminate-preserving Italian
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkɔppa/
       </span>
       ).
      </p>

      <p>

       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grammaticalisation" title="Grammaticalisation">

       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Blocked_lenition">
        Blocked lenition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Blocked lenition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some languages which have lenition have in addition complex rules affecting situations where lenition might be expected to occur but does not, often those involving
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Homorganic_consonants" title="Homorganic consonants">
        homorganic consonants
       </a>
       . In Scottish Gaelic, for example, there are three homorganic groups:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blas_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blas-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        d n t l s (usually called the
        <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
         dental
        </a>
        group in spite of the non-dental nature of the palatals)
       </li>
       <li>
        c g (usually called the
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_consonant" title="Velar consonant">
         velar
        </a>
        group)
       </li>
       <li>
        b f m p (usually called the
        <a href="/wiki/Labial_consonant" title="Labial consonant">
         labial
        </a>
        group)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In a position where lenition is expected due to the grammatical environment, lenition tends to be blocked if there are two adjacent homorganic consonants across the word boundary. For example:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blas_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blas-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         aon
        </i>
        'one' (which causes lenition) →
        <i>
         aon chas
        </i>
        'one leg' vs
        <i>
         ao
         <b>
          n t
         </b>
         aigh
        </i>
        'one house' (not
        <i>
         aon *thaigh
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         air an
        </i>
        'on the' (which causes lenition) →
        <i>
         air a' chas mhòr
        </i>
        'on the big leg' vs
        <i>
         air a
         <b>
          n t
         </b>
         aigh
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         onn
        </i>
        "on the brown house" (not
        <i>
         air an *thaigh *dhonn)
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In modern Scottish Gaelic this rule is only
       <a href="/wiki/Productivity_(linguistics)" title="Productivity (linguistics)">
        productive
       </a>
       in the case of dentals but not the other two groups for the vast majority of speakers. It also does not affect all environments any more. For example, while
       <i>
        aon
       </i>
       still invokes the rules of blocked lenition, a noun followed by an adjective generally no longer does so. Hence:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blas_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blas-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         ad
        </i>
        "hat" (a feminine noun causing lenition) →
        <i>
         ad dhonn
        </i>
        "a brown hat" (although some highly conservative speakers retain
        <i>
         ad donn
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         caileag
        </i>
        "girl" (a feminine noun causing lenition) →
        <i>
         caileag ghlic
        </i>
        "a smart girl" (not
        <i>
         caileag *glic
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       There is a significant number of frozen forms involving the other two groups (labials and velars) and environments as well, especially in surnames and place names:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blas_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blas-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Ma
         <b>
          cG
         </b>
         umaraid
        </i>
        'Montgomery' (
        <i>
         mac
        </i>
        +
        <i>
         Gumaraid
        </i>
        ) vs
        <i>
         Ma
         <b>
          cDh
         </b>
         òmhnaill
        </i>
        'MacDonald (
        <i>
         mac
        </i>
        +
        <i>
         Dòmhnall
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cai
         <b>
          mb
         </b>
         eul
        </i>
        'Campbell' (
        <i>
         cam
        </i>
        'crooked' +
        <i>
         beul
        </i>
        'mouth') vs
        <i>
         Ca
         <b>
          msh
         </b>
         ron
        </i>
        'Cameron' (
        <i>
         cam
        </i>
        +
        <i>
         sròn
        </i>
        'nose')
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         sgia
         <b>
          n-d
         </b>
         ubh
        </i>
        '
        <a href="/wiki/Sgian-dubh" title="Sgian-dubh">
         Sgian-dubh
        </a>
        ' (
        <i>
         sgian
        </i>
        'knife' +
        <i>
         dubh
        </i>
        '1 black 2 hidden';
        <i>
         sgian
        </i>
        as a feminine noun today would normally cause lenition on a following adjective) vs
        <i>
         sgian dhubh
        </i>
        "a black knife" (i.e., a common knife which just happens to be black)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Though rare, in some instances the rules of blocked lenition can be invoked by lost historical consonants, for example, in the case of the past-tense
       <a href="/wiki/Copula_(linguistics)" title="Copula (linguistics)">
        copula
       </a>
       <i>
        bu
       </i>
       , which in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Common_Celtic" title="Common Celtic">
        Common Celtic
       </a>
       had a final -t. In terms of blocked lenition, it continues to behave as a dental-final particle invoking blocked lenition rules:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blas_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blas-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         bu
         <b>
          d
         </b>
         ona am biadh
        </i>
        "bad was the food" versus
        <i>
         bu
         <b>
          mh
         </b>
         òr am beud
        </i>
        'great was the pity
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Blocked lenition phenomena are also known to occur in Irish and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       (orthographic b d g retained as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b, d, ɡ]
       </span>
       following nasals rather than their normal lenited forms
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β, ð, ɣ]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
        Orthography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Orthography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_morphology" title="Welsh morphology">
        Welsh morphology
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_initial_mutations" title="Irish initial mutations">
        Irish initial mutations
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the modern Celtic languages, lenition of the "fricating" type is usually denoted by adding an
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       to the lenited letter. In Welsh, for example,
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       change into
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ph
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        th
       </i>
       as a result of the so-called "aspirate mutation" (
       <i>
        carreg
       </i>
       , "stone" →
       <i>
        ei charreg
       </i>
       "her stone"). An exception is
       <a href="/wiki/Manx_language" title="Manx language">
        Manx
       </a>
       orthography, which tends to be more phonetic, although in some cases etymological principles are applied. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Gaelic_type" title="Gaelic type">
        Gaelic script
       </a>
       , fricating lenition (called simply
       <i>
        lenition
       </i>
       in Irish grammar contexts) is indicated by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dot_above#Overdot" title="Dot above">
        dot above
       </a>
       the affected consonant, while in the Roman script, the convention is to suffix the letter
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       to the consonant, to signify that it is lenited. Thus,
       <i>
        a ṁáṫair
       </i>
       is equivalent to
       <i>
        a mháthair
       </i>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Irish" title="Middle Irish">
        Middle Irish
       </a>
       manuscripts, lenition of
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       was indicated by the dot above, while lenition of
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       was indicated by the postposed
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       ; lenition of other letters was not indicated consistently in the orthography.
      </p>
      <p>
       Voicing lenition is represented by a simple letter switch in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brythonic_languages" title="Brythonic languages">
        Brythonic languages
       </a>
       , for instance
       <i>
        carreg
       </i>
       , "stone" →
       <i>
        y garreg
       </i>
       , "the stone" in Welsh. In
       <a href="/wiki/Irish_orthography" title="Irish orthography">
        Irish orthography
       </a>
       , it is shown by writing the "weak" consonant alongside the (silent) "strong" one:
       <i>
        peann
       </i>
       , "pen" →
       <i>
        ár bpeann
       </i>
       "our pen",
       <i>
        ceann
       </i>
       , "head" →
       <i>
        ár gceann
       </i>
       "our head" (sonorization is traditionally called "eclipsis" in Irish grammar).
      </p>
      <p>
       Although nasalization as a feature also occurs in most Scottish Gaelic dialects, it is not shown in the orthography on the whole as it is synchronic (i.e., the result of certain types of nasals affecting a following sound), rather than the diachronic Irish type sonorization (i.e., following historic nasals). For example
       <i>
        taigh
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t̪ʰɤj]
       </span>
       "house" →
       <i>
        an taigh
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ən̪ˠˈd̪ʱɤj]
       </span>
       "the house".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Oftedal_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Oftedal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Consonant_gradation">
        Consonant gradation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Consonant gradation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant_gradation" title="Consonant gradation">
        Consonant gradation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The phenomenon of consonant gradation in
       <a href="/wiki/Sami_languages" title="Sami languages">
        Samic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Finnic_languages" title="Finnic languages">
        Finnic languages
       </a>
       is also a form of lenition.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geminate" title="Geminate">
        geminate
       </a>
       consonants comes from Finnish, where geminates become simple consonants while retaining voicing or voicelessness (e.g.
       <i>
        katto
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        katon
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dubbaan
       </i>
       →
       <i>
        dubata
       </i>
       ). It is also possible for entire consonant clusters to undergo lenition, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Votic_language" title="Votic language">
        Votic
       </a>
       , where voiceless clusters become voiced, e.g.
       <i>
        itke-
       </i>
       "to cry" →
       <i>
        idgön
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       If a language has no obstruents other than voiceless stops, other sounds are encountered, as in Finnish, where the lenited grade is represented by
       <a href="/wiki/Chroneme" title="Chroneme">
        chronemes
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approximant" title="Approximant">
        approximants
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Flap_consonant" title="Flap consonant">
        taps
       </a>
       or even
       <a href="/wiki/Trill_consonant" title="Trill consonant">
        trills
       </a>
       . For example, Finnish used to have a complete set of spirantization reflexes for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p t k/
       </span>
       , though these have been lost in favour of similar-sounding phonemes. In Pohjanmaa
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_language" title="Finnish language">
        Finnish
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ð/
       </span>
       was changed into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       , thus the dialect has a synchronic lenition of an alveolar stop into an alveolar trill
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/ → /r/
       </span>
       . Furthermore, the same phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       undergoes
       <a href="/wiki/Assibilation" title="Assibilation">
        assibilation
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       before the vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       , e.g. root
       <i>
        vete-
       </i>
       "water" →
       <i>
        vesi
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        vere-
       </i>
       . Here,
       <i>
        vete-
       </i>
       is the stem,
       <i>
        vesi
       </i>
       is its nominative, and
       <i>
        vere-
       </i>
       is the same stem under consonant gradation.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fortition">
        Fortition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lenition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Fortition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Fortition" title="Fortition">
        Fortition
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Fortition is the opposite of lenition: a consonant mutation in which a consonant changes from one considered weak to one considered strong. Fortition is less frequent than lenition in the languages of the world, but word-initial and word-final fortition is fairly frequent.
      </p>
      <p>
       Italian, for example, presents numerous regular examples of word-initial fortition both historically (Lat.
       <i>
        Januarius
       </i>
       with initial
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /j/
       </span>
       &gt;
       <i>
        gennaio
       </i>
       , with
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       ) and synchronically (e.g.,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈkaza/
       </span>
       "house, home" →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkaːza]
       </span>
       but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /a ˈkaza/
       </span>
       "at home" →
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [aˈkːaːza]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Catalan is among numerous Romance languages with diachronic
       <a href="/wiki/Final-obstruent_devoicing" title="Final-obstruent devoicing">
        word-final devoicing
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        frigidus
       </i>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */ˈfɾɛd/
       </span>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈfɾɛt]
       </span>
       . Fortition also occurs in Catalan for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /b d ɡ/
       </span>
       in consonant clusters with a lateral consonant (Lat.
       <i>
        populus
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        poble
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpɔbːɫə]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈpɔpːɫə]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Word-medially,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /lː/
       </span>
       is subject to fortition in numerous Romance languages, ranging from
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dː]
       </span>
       in many speech types on Italian soil to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       in some varieties of Spanish.
      </p>
