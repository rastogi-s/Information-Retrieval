      <p>
       <b>
        Aranese
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="oc" xml:lang="oc">
         Aranés
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a standardized form of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenean
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
        Gascon
       </a>
       variety of the
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Val_d%27Aran" title="Val d'Aran">
        Val d'Aran
       </a>
       , in northwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Catalonia" title="Catalonia">
        Catalonia
       </a>
       close to the Spanish border with France, where it is one of the three
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official languages
       </a>
       beside
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . In 2010, it was named the third official language of the whole of Catalonia by the
       <a href="/wiki/Parliament_of_Catalonia" title="Parliament of Catalonia">
        Parliament of Catalonia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The official spellings of towns in Val d'Aran are Aranese; for example, the Aranese spelling
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vielha" title="Vielha">
         Vielha
        </a>
       </i>
       is used on maps and road signs instead of the Catalan and Spanish
       <i>
        Viella
       </i>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aranese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to a 2001 linguistic census by the Aranese government, about 90% of the inhabitants of Val d'Aran can understand the language, with those between 25 and 34 years old having the lowest rate, at around 80% (excluding those under the age of 4). Between 60 and 65% of the population can speak it; however, only 26% reported being able to write in Aranese.

      <p>
       In 2008, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Generalitat_of_Catalonia" title="Generalitat of Catalonia">
        Generalitat of Catalonia
       </a>
       surveyed the population (15 years old or older) in the Val d'Aran. The survey reported that 78.2% of the population could understand Aranese, 56.8% could speak it, 59.4% could read it, and 34.8% could write the language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Once considered to be an endangered language
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       spoken mainly by older people, it is now experiencing a renaissance; it enjoys co-official status with Catalan and Spanish within Val d'Aran, and since 1984 has been taught bilingually alongside Castilian in schools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Students in the Val d'Aran are required to have 2 hours of each Spanish, Catalan, and Aranese each week. At some levels of education, a foreign language is added to the three official languages—usually French due to proximity—and sometimes even 2 additional hours of English.
      </p>

          </span>
          is pronounced oy in the towns of Bausen and Canejan. Foreign words that have not been adopted into Aranese also retain
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /h/
          </span>
          :
          <i>
           hardware
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           maharajah
          </i>
          .
         </li>
         <li>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           /ɾ/
          </span>
          is pronounced
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [ɾ]
          </span>
          , except at the end of a word, where it is generally silent, regardless of what follows.
         </li>
        </ol>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loanwords">
        Loanwords
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aranese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Loanwords">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Since the Val d'Aran is located within Spanish and Catalan territory, Aranese is subject to certain influences from Castilian Spanish and Catalan. As such, Aranese has adopted several neologisms from them:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         actuar
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         agir
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         empresa
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         entrepresa
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         increment
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         aumentacion
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         laborau
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         professionau
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         matrícula
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         inscripcion
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         oficina
        </i>
        (vs.
        <i>
         burèu
        </i>
        , which is a
        <a href="/wiki/Gallicism" title="Gallicism">
         Gallicism
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Spanish and Catalan have also created deformations of words such as
       <i>
        abans
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        abantes
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        dempús
       </i>
       &gt;
       <i>
        despuès
       </i>
       . Some Hispanicisms are directly adopted into Aranese:
       <i>
        hasta
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regulation">
        Regulation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aranese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Regulation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Aranese is regulated under classic unifying standards of Occitan, defined initially by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lo%C3%ADs_Alib%C3%A8rt" title="Loís Alibèrt">
        Loís Alibèrt
       </a>
       . These standards of the
       <a href="/wiki/Conselh_de_la_Lenga_Occitana" title="Conselh de la Lenga Occitana">
        Conselh de la Lenga Occitana
       </a>
       (Occitan Language Council) have officially been recognized by the
       <a href="/wiki/Conselh_Generau_d%27Aran" title="Conselh Generau d'Aran">
        Conselh Generau d'Aran
       </a>
       (General Council of Aran) since 1999.
      </p>
      <p>
       In practice, several details standards diverge due to the popular or preferred usage of Aranese, in relation to other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gascon_dialect" title="Gascon dialect">
        Gascon
       </a>
       varieties. For instance:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the form of the feminine plural -AS in general Gascon is replaced with -ES in Aranese. Ex:
        <i>
         hemnes araneses
        </i>
        (Aranese women) in place of general Gascon
        <i>
         hemnas aranesas
        </i>
       </li>
       <li>
        the use of U in place of V. Ex:
        <i>
         auer
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         aver
        </i>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aranese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A reference on usage and conjugation of Aranese verbs entitled
       <i>
        Es Vèrbs conjugadi : morfologia verbau aranesa
       </i>
       was written by Verònica Barés Moga and published in 2003. A descriptive and normative reference grammar book, written in Aranese by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Aitor_Carrera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Aitor Carrera (page does not exist)">
        Aitor Carrera
       </a>
       , was published in March 2007. It includes a detailed breakdown of phonological and grammatical differences between varieties of Aranese in different villages in the valley.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dictionaries">
        Dictionaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aranese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dictionaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A dictionary of Aranese was written by the Catalan linguist
       <a href="/wiki/Joan_Coromines" title="Joan Coromines">
        Joan Coromines
       </a>
       as his doctoral thesis.
      </p>
      <p>
       A simple four-language Spanish–Aranese–Catalan–French dictionary exists, written by Frederic Vergés Bartau (see Bibliography).
      </p>
      <p>
       An Aranese-English and English–Aranese dictionary was published in 2006. It was written by Ryan Furness, a young man from
       <a href="/wiki/Minnesota" title="Minnesota">
        Minnesota
       </a>
       , after he became curious about the language when he traveled to Val d'Aran.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A detailed one-volume Catalan–Occitan and Occitan–Catalan dictionary was published under the auspices of the governments of Catalonia (
       <a href="/wiki/Generalitat_de_Catalunya" title="Generalitat de Catalunya">
        Generalitat de Catalunya
       </a>
       ) and Val d'Aran (
       <a href="/wiki/Conselh_Generau_d%27Aran" title="Conselh Generau d'Aran">
        Conselh Generau d'Aran
       </a>
       ). Although it calls the language "Occitan", it uses Aranese spelling and its preface says that special attention is given to the Aranese variety.
      </p>
