      <p>
       A
       <b>
        first language
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        native language
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        mother/father tongue
       </b>
       (also known as
       <b>
        arterial language
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        L1
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Language" title="Language">
        language
       </a>
       that a person has been exposed to from birth
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or within the
       <a href="/wiki/Critical_period_hypothesis" title="Critical period hypothesis">
        critical period
       </a>
       . In some countries, the term
       <i>
        native language
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        mother tongue
       </i>
       refers to the language of one's ethnic group rather than one's first language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Children brought up speaking more than one language can have more than one native language, and be
       <a href="/wiki/Simultaneous_bilingualism" title="Simultaneous bilingualism">
        bilingual
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Multilingual" title="Multilingual">
        multilingual
       </a>
       . By contrast, a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
         second language
        </a>
       </i>
       is any language that one speaks other than one's first language.
      </p>

      <p>
       One of the more widely accepted definitions of native speakers is that they were born in a particular country raised to speak the language of that country during the critical period of their development,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The person qualifies as a "native speaker" of a language by being born and immersed in the language during youth, in a family in which the adults shared a similar language experience as the child.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Native speakers are considered to be an authority on their given language because of their natural acquisition process regarding the language, as opposed to having learned the language later in life. That is achieved by personal interaction with the language and speakers of the language. Native speakers will not necessarily be knowledgeable about every grammatical rule of the language, but they will have good "intuition" of the rules through their experience with the language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes, the term "
       <b>
        mother tongue
       </b>
       " or "
       <b>
        mother language
       </b>
       " is used for the language that a person learned as a child at home (usually from their parents). Children growing up in bilingual homes can, according to this definition, have more than one mother tongue or native language.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the context of population censuses conducted on the Canadian population,
       <a href="/wiki/Statistics_Canada" title="Statistics Canada">
        Statistics Canada
       </a>
       defines
       <i>
        mother tongue
       </i>
       as "the first language learned at home in childhood and still understood by the individual at the time of the census."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is quite possible that the first language learned is no longer a speaker's dominant language. That includes young immigrant children whose families have moved to a new linguistic environment as well as people who learned their mother tongue as a young child at home (rather than the language of the majority of the community), who may have lost, in part or in totality, the language they first
       <a href="/wiki/Language_acquisition" title="Language acquisition">
        acquired
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Language_attrition" title="Language attrition">
        language attrition
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mother_tongue">
        Mother tongue
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mother tongue">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="500" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg/220px-Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg/330px-Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg/440px-Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Int-mother-lang-day-monument.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         International Mother Language Day Monument in Sydney, Australia, unveiling ceremony, 19 February 2006
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       According to
       <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Illich" title="Ivan Illich">
        Ivan Illich
       </a>
       , the term "mother tongue" was first used by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Catholic" title="Catholic">
        Catholic
       </a>
       monks to designate a particular language they used, instead of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       , when they are "speaking from the pulpit". That is, the "holy mother the Church" introduced this term and colonies inherited it from Christianity as a part of colonialism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In some countries, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">
        Kenya
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       , and various East Asian countries, "mother language" or "native language" is used to indicate the language of one's
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">
        ethnic group
       </a>
       in both common and journalistic parlance ("I have no apologies for not learning my mother tongue"), rather than one's first language. Also, in
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       , "mother tongue" refers to the language of one's
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnic_group" title="Ethnic group">
        ethnic group
       </a>
       regardless of actual proficiency, and the "first language" refers to English, which was established on the island under the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Empire" title="British Empire">
        British Empire
       </a>
       , which is the
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       for most post-independence Singaporeans because of its use as the language of instruction in government schools and as a working language.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">
        J. R. R. Tolkien
       </a>
       , in his 1955 lecture "
       <a href="/wiki/English_and_Welsh" title="English and Welsh">
        English and Welsh
       </a>
       ," distinguishes the "native tongue" from the "cradle tongue." The latter is the language one happens to learn during early childhood, and one's true "native tongue" may be different, possibly determined by an
       <a href="/wiki/Genetic_memory_(biology)" title="Genetic memory (biology)">
        inherited linguistic taste
       </a>
       and may later in life be discovered by a strong emotional affinity to a specific dialect (Tolkien personally confessed to such an affinity to the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Midlands_(region)" title="West Midlands (region)">
        West Midlands
       </a>
       in particular).
      </p>
      <p>
       On 17 November 1999, UNESCO designated 21 February as
       <a href="/wiki/International_Mother_Language_Day" title="International Mother Language Day">
        International Mother Language Day
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Significance">
        Significance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Significance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first language of a child is part of the personal, social and cultural identity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another impact of the first language is that it brings about the reflection and learning of successful social patterns of acting and speaking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is basically responsible for differentiating the linguistic competence of acting. While some argue that there is no such thing as "native speaker" or a "mother tongue," it is important to understand the key terms as well as understand what it means to be a "non-native" speaker and the implications that can have on one's life. Research suggest that while a non-native speaker may develop fluency in a targeted language after about two years of immersion, it can actually take between five and seven years for that child to be on the same working level as their native speaking counterparts. That has implications on the education of non-native speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The topic of native speaker also gives way to discussion about what exactly bilingualism is. One definition is that a person is bilingual by being equally proficient in both languages. A person who grows up speaking English and begins learning Spanish for four years is not necessarily bilingual unless he speaks the two languages with equal fluency. Pearl and Lambert were the first to test only “balanced” bilinguals—that is, children who are completely fluent in two languages and feel that neither is their “native” language because they grasp the two so perfectly. This study found the following: balanced bilinguals perform significantly better in tasks that require flexibility (they constantly shift between the two known languages depending on the situation/requires constant juggling), more aware of arbitrary nature of language and also that balanced bilinguals choose word associations based on logical rather than phonetic preferences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Multilingualism">
        Multilingualism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Multilingualism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One can have two or more native languages, thus being a native
       <i>
        bilingual
       </i>
       or indeed
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Multilingualism" title="Multilingualism">
         multilingual
        </a>
       </i>
       . The order in which these languages are learned is not necessarily the order of proficiency. For instance, if a French-speaking couple have a child who learned French first but then the child grew up in an English-speaking country, the child would likely be most proficient in English. Other examples are in
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Indonesia" title="Languages of Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kenya" title="Kenya">
        Kenya
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
        Malaysia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Singapore" title="Singapore">
        Singapore
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_South_Africa" title="Languages of South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       , where most people speak more than one language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The designation "native language," in its general usage, is thought to be imprecise and subject to various interpretations that are biased linguistically, especially with respect to bilingual children from ethnic minority groups. Many scholars
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       have given definitions of 'native language' based on common usage, the emotional relation of the speaker towards the language, and even its dominance in relation to the environment. However, all of three criteria lack precision. For many children whose home language differs from the language of the environment (the 'official' language), it is debatable which language is one's 'native language'.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Defining_native_language">
        Defining native language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Defining native language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Based on origin: the language(s) one learned first (the language(s) in which one has established the first long-lasting verbal contacts).
       </li>
       <li>
        Based on internal identification: the language(s) one identifies with/as a speaker of;
       </li>
       <li>
        Based on external identification: the language(s) one is identified with/as a speaker of, by others.
       </li>
       <li>
        Based on competence: the language(s) one knows best.
       </li>
       <li>
        Based on function: the language(s) one uses most.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Defining_.22native_speaker.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Defining_"native_speaker"'>
        Defining "native speaker"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title='Edit section: Defining "native speaker"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The article titled “The Native Speaker: An Achievable Model?” published by the Asian EFL Journal
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       states that there are six general principles that relate to the definition of "native speaker". The principles, according to the study, are typically accepted by language experts across the scientific field. A native speaker is defined according to the guidelines as this:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        The individual acquired the language in early childhood.
       </li>
       <li>
        The individual has intuitive knowledge of the language.
       </li>
       <li>
        The individual is able to produce fluent, spontaneous discourse.
       </li>
       <li>
        The individual is competent in communication.
       </li>
       <li>
        The individual identifies with or is identified by a language community.
       </li>
       <li>
        The individual has a dialect accent (including the official dialect).
       </li>
      </ol>
