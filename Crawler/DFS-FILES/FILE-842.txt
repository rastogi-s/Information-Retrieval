      <p>
       <b>
        Princeton University Press
       </b>
       is an independent
       <a href="/wiki/Academic_publishing" title="Academic publishing">
        publisher
       </a>
       with close connections to
       <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
        Princeton University
       </a>
       . Its mission is to disseminate scholarship within
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia" title="Academia">
        academia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Society" title="Society">
        society
       </a>
       at large.
      </p>
      <p>
       The press was founded by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Whitney_Darrow&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Whitney Darrow (page does not exist)">
        Whitney Darrow
       </a>
       , with the financial support of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Scribner_II" title="Charles Scribner II">
        Charles Scribner
       </a>
       , as a
       <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">
        printing press
       </a>
       to serve the Princeton community in 1905.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ErectedNYT_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ErectedNYT-2">
         [2]
        </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Princeton_University_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Princeton University Press was founded in 1905 by a recent Princeton graduate, Whitney Darrow, with financial support from another Princetonian,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Scribner_II" title="Charles Scribner II">
        Charles Scribner II
       </a>
       . Darrow and Scribner purchased the equipment and assumed the operations of two already existing local publishers, that of the
       <i>
        Princeton Alumni Weekly
       </i>
       and the Princeton Press. The new press printed both local newspapers, university documents,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Princetonian" title="The Daily Princetonian">
         The Daily Princetonian
        </a>
       </i>
       , and later added book publishing to its activities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Axtell_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Axtell-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Beginning as a small, for-profit printer, Princeton University Press was reincorporated as a nonprofit in 1910.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PW-79-22_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PW-79-22-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since 1911, the press has been headquartered in a purpose-built gothic-style building designed by
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Flagg" title="Ernest Flagg">
        Ernest Flagg
       </a>
       . The design of press’s building, which was named the Scribner Building in 1965, was inspired by the Plantin-Moretus Museum, a printing museum in Antwerp, Belgium. Princeton University Press established a European office, in Woodstock, England, north of Oxford, in 1999, and opened an additional office, in Beijing, in early 2017.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bollingen_Series">
        Bollingen Series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Princeton_University_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bollingen Series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Princeton University Press's Bollingen Series had its beginnings in the
       <a href="/wiki/Bollingen_Foundation" title="Bollingen Foundation">
        Bollingen Foundation
       </a>
       , a 1943 project of
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Mellon" title="Paul Mellon">
        Paul Mellon
       </a>
       's Old Dominion Foundation. From 1945, the foundation had independent status, publishing and providing fellowships and grants in several areas of study, including
       <a href="/wiki/Archaeology" title="Archaeology">
        archaeology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Poetry" title="Poetry">
        poetry
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Psychology" title="Psychology">
        psychology
       </a>
       . The Bollingen Series was given to the university in 1969.
      </p>
