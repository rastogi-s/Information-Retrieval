      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Glosas Emilianenses
       </b>
       (Spanish for "glosses of [the monastery of Saint] Millán/Emilianus") are
       <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">
        glosses
       </a>
       written in a
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Codex" title="Codex">
        codex
       </a>
       . These
       <a href="/wiki/Marginalia" title="Marginalia">
        marginalia
       </a>
       are important as early examples of writing in a form of Romance similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , and in
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       . The anonymous author is assumed to have been a monk at the monastery now known as Suso ("the upper one"), one of the twin monasteries of
       <a href="/wiki/San_Mill%C3%A1n_de_la_Cogolla" title="San Millán de la Cogolla">
        San Millán de la Cogolla
       </a>
       (now in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(autonomous_community)" title="La Rioja (autonomous community)">
        La Rioja
       </a>
       , then in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
        Kingdom of Navarre
       </a>
       ). He wrote about a thousand years ago in three languages:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        A simplified version of
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
          Latin
         </a>
        </b>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         The medieval form of a
         <a href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_languages" title="Iberian Romance languages">
          Hispanic Romance
         </a>
         language
        </b>
        (traditionally regarded as Castilian or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
         Old Spanish
        </a>
        , but now most often classified as
        <a href="/wiki/Navarro-Aragonese" title="Navarro-Aragonese">
         Navarro-Aragonese
        </a>
        or a related dialect);
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Medieval Basque
        </b>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The latter two would have been the
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernacular
       </a>
       languages in the region surrounding the monastery, although there is a possibility that the author of the glosses was an incomer to the area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">

      <p>
       The Glosses were formerly considered to include the first instances of early Spanish. However, in November 2010, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Real Academia Española
       </a>
       declared that the first appearances of written Spanish can be found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cartularies_of_Valpuesta" title="Cartularies of Valpuesta">
        Cartularies of Valpuesta
       </a>
       , medieval documents in Latin from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burgos_(province)" title="Burgos (province)">
        province of Burgos
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location_of_the_glosses">
        Location of the glosses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glosas_Emilianenses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location of the glosses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The codex is known as
       <i>
        Aemilianensis 60
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Aemilianus
       </i>
       is Latin for
       <a href="/wiki/Emilian_of_Cogolla" title="Emilian of Cogolla">
        Emilian
       </a>
       , "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mill%C3%A1n" title="Millán">
        Millán
       </a>
       " or "
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia_(region_of_Italy)" title="Emilia (region of Italy)">
        Emiliano
       </a>
       " in modern
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ) and was preserved in the monastery library at Yuso (the lower re-foundation of the monastery). Its significance was recognised in the early twentieth century when it was brought to the attention of the philologist
       <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Men%C3%A9ndez_Pidal" title="Ramón Menéndez Pidal">
        Ramón Menéndez Pidal
       </a>
       . The manuscript's current location is the
       <a href="/wiki/Real_Academia_de_la_Historia" title="Real Academia de la Historia">
        Real Academia de la Historia
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Romance_glosses">
        Romance glosses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glosas_Emilianenses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Romance glosses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Codiceemil.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1647" data-file-width="1263" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Codiceemil.jpg/200px-Codiceemil.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Codiceemil.jpg/300px-Codiceemil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Codiceemil.jpg/400px-Codiceemil.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Codiceemil.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Page 72 of the Aemilianensis 60 codex. The
         <a href="/wiki/Gloss_(annotation)" title="Gloss (annotation)">
          gloss
         </a>
         in the bottom right-hand margin of the page is the most extensive one in the codex.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       There is still some debate as to whether the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_Romance_language" title="Iberian Romance language">
        Iberian Romance language
       </a>
       of the glosses should be classed as an early form of Castilian or of Aragonese, although some recent studies show that most features belong indeed to the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not the only text to be difficult to classify: other texts traditionally assumed to be in Old Spanish, like the
       <a href="/wiki/Kharja" title="Kharja">
        Kharjas
       </a>
       , are proved to be in a different medieval Romance,
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       , which happens to be classified along with Aragonese in a Pyrenean-Mozarabic group. Some scholars have proposed that it is anachronistic to classify such varieties of Ibero-Romance according to dialectal labels based on geographical particularism before the thirteenth century, leaving the Glosas to be understood as "in an unspecialized informal register of Ibero-Romance".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, should the Romance language of the glosses be classified, San Millán de la Cogolla's former reputation as the "birthplace of the Spanish language" was important in its designation as a
       <a href="/wiki/World_Heritage_Site" title="World Heritage Site">
        World Heritage Site
       </a>
       ("cultural" type) in 1997.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Text_and_translation">
        Text and translation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glosas_Emilianenses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Text and translation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The longest gloss appears on page 72 of the manuscripts. The Spanish
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">
        philologist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A1maso_Alonso" title="Dámaso Alonso">
        Dámaso Alonso
       </a>
       called this little prayer the "first cry of the Spanish language" (in Spanish:

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Basque_glosses">
        Basque glosses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Glosas_Emilianenses&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Basque glosses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1758" data-file-width="2598" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg/220px-Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg/330px-Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/86/Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg/440px-Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Euskara-san-millan-yuso.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Plaque at Yuso monastery commemorating "the first phrases in the Basque language"
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Aemilianensis 60
       </i>
       has been publicized as the earliest known codex with inscriptions in Basque, though other codices are posited.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Only two of the glosses in
       <i>
        Aemilianensis 60
       </i>
       (of a total of about one thousand) are actually in Basque.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These short texts (only 6 words in total) can be seen on the 1974 plaque. However, it has been suggested that some of the Romance glosses reflect the influence of the Basque language, the implication being that their author was a fluent Basque-speaker.
      </p>
