      <p>
       An
       <b>
        orogeny
       </b>
       is an event that leads to a large structural deformation of the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
        lithosphere
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       and uppermost
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       ) due to the interaction between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">
        tectonic plates
       </a>
       . An
       <b>
        orogen
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        orogenic belt
       </b>
       develops when a continental plate crumples and is pushed upwards to form one or more
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_range" title="Mountain range">
        mountain ranges
       </a>
       ; this involves many geological processes collectively called
       <b>
        orogenesis
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Waltham_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Waltham-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vine_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vine-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Orogeny is the primary mechanism by which
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_formation" title="Mountain formation">
        mountains are built
       </a>
       on continents.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The word "orogeny" comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       (
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        ὄρος
       </span>
       <i>
        oros
       </i>
       , "mountain" +
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γένεσις
       </span>
       <i>
        genesis
       </i>
       for "creation, origin").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chambers_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chambers-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though it was used before him, the term was employed by the American geologist
       <a href="/wiki/Grove_Karl_Gilbert" title="Grove Karl Gilbert">
        G.K. Gilbert
       </a>
       in 1890 to describe the process of mountain building as distinguished from
       <a href="/wiki/Epeirogenic_movement" title="Epeirogenic movement">
        epeirogeny
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Friedman_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Friedman-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Continental-continental_convergence_Fig21contcont.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="173" data-file-width="301" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Continental-continental_convergence_Fig21contcont.gif/220px-Continental-continental_convergence_Fig21contcont.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Continental-continental_convergence_Fig21contcont.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Continental-continental_convergence_Fig21contcont.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Continental_collision" title="Continental collision">
          Continental collision
         </a>
         of two continental plates to form a collisional orogen. However, usually no continental crust is subducted, only uplifted. (example: the
         <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
          Alps
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Formation of an orogen is accomplished in part by the tectonic processes of
       <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
        subduction
       </a>
       (where a
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continent
       </a>
       rides forcefully over an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oceanic_plate" title="Oceanic plate">
        oceanic plate
       </a>
       (noncollisional orogens)) or
       <a href="/wiki/Convergent_boundary" title="Convergent boundary">
        convergence
       </a>
       of two or more continents (collisional orogens).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Press_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Press-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Orogeny usually produces long arcuate (from the Latin
       <i>
        <span lang="la" xml:lang="la">
         arcuare
        </span>
       </i>
       , "to bend like a bow") structures, known as
       <i>
        orogenic belts
       </i>
       . Generally, orogenic belts consist of long parallel strips of
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       exhibiting similar characteristics along the length of the belt. Orogenic belts are associated with subduction zones, which consume
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       , produce volcanoes, and build
       <a href="/wiki/Island_arc" title="Island arc">
        island arcs
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-YuanEtAl2009_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-YuanEtAl2009-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geologists attribute the arcuate structure to the rigidity of the descending plate, and island arc cusps relate to tears in the descending lithosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Turcotte_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Turcotte-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These island arcs may be added to a continent during an orogenic event.
      </p>
      <p>
       The processes of orogeny can take tens of millions of years and build mountains from plains or from the
       <a href="/wiki/Seabed" title="Seabed">
        seabed
       </a>
       . The topographic height of orogenic mountains is related to the principle of
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostasy
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Allen_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Allen-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that is, a balance of the downward
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Newton's law of universal gravitation">
        gravitational force
       </a>
       upon an upthrust mountain range (composed of light,
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">
        continental crust
       </a>
       material) and the buoyant upward forces exerted by the dense underlying
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilcock_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilcock-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Frequently,
       <a href="/wiki/Geological_formation" title="Geological formation">
        rock formations
       </a>
       that undergo orogeny are severely deformed and undergo
       <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
        metamorphism
       </a>
       . Orogenic processes may push deeply buried rocks to the surface. Sea-bottom and near-shore material may cover some or all of the orogenic area. If the orogeny is due to two continents colliding, very high mountains can result (see
       <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">
        Himalayas
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       An orogenic event may be studied: (a) as a tectonic structural event, (b) as a geographical event, and (c) as a chronological event.
      </p>
      <p>
       Orogenic events:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        cause distinctive structural phenomena related to tectonic activity
       </li>
       <li>
        affect rocks and crust in particular regions, and
       </li>
       <li>
        happen within a specific period
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span id="Orogen_.28or_.22orogenic_system.22.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Orogen_(or_"orogenic_system")'>
        Orogen (or "orogenic system")
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: Orogen (or "orogenic system")'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ForelandBasinSystem.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="348" data-file-width="994" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/81/ForelandBasinSystem.png/350px-ForelandBasinSystem.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/81/ForelandBasinSystem.png/525px-ForelandBasinSystem.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/81/ForelandBasinSystem.png/700px-ForelandBasinSystem.png 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ForelandBasinSystem.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Foreland Basin System
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An orogeny produces an
       <i>
        orogen
       </i>
       , or (mountain) range-
       <i>
        foreland basin
       </i>
       system.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Foreland_basin" title="Foreland basin">
         foreland basin
        </a>
       </i>
       forms ahead of the orogen due mainly to loading and resulting
       <a href="/wiki/Lithospheric_flexure" title="Lithospheric flexure">
        flexure of the lithosphere
       </a>
       by the developing mountain belt. A typical foreland basin is subdivided into a wedge-top basin above the active orogenic wedge, the foredeep immediately beyond the active front, a forebulge high of flexural origin and a back-bulge area beyond, although not all of these are present in all foreland-basin systems. The basin migrates with the orogenic front and early deposited foreland basin sediments become progressively involved in folding and thrusting.
       <a href="/wiki/Sediment" title="Sediment">
        Sediments
       </a>
       deposited in the foreland basin are mainly derived from the
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
        erosion
       </a>
       of the actively uplifting rocks of the mountain range, although some sediments derive from the foreland. The fill of many such basins shows a change in time from deepwater marine (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">
         flysch
        </a>
       </i>
       -style) through shallow water to continental (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Molasse" title="Molasse">
         molasse
        </a>
       </i>
       -style) sediments.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DeCelles&amp;Giles_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DeCelles&amp;Giles-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orogenic_cycle">
        Orogenic cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orogenic cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Wilson_cycle" title="Wilson cycle">
        Wilson cycle
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Although orogeny involves
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       , the tectonic forces result in a variety of associated phenomena, including
       <a href="/wiki/Magmatism" title="Magmatism">
        magmatism
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
        metamorphism
       </a>
       , crustal melting, and crustal thickening. What exactly happens in a specific orogen depends upon the strength and
       <a href="/wiki/Rheology" title="Rheology">
        rheology
       </a>
       of the continental lithosphere, and how these properties change during orogenesis.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to orogeny, the orogen (once formed) is subject to other processes, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Sedimentation" title="Sedimentation">
        sedimentation
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
        erosion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vine_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vine-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The sequence of repeated cycles of sedimentation,
       <a href="/wiki/Deposition_(geology)" title="Deposition (geology)">
        deposition
       </a>
       and erosion, followed by burial and metamorphism, and then by formation of granitic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Batholiths" title="Batholiths">
        batholiths
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tectonic_uplift" title="Tectonic uplift">
        tectonic uplift
       </a>
       to form
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_chain" title="Mountain chain">
        mountain chains
       </a>
       , is called the
       <i>
        orogenic cycle
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Johnson-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Twiss_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Twiss-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Caledonian_orogeny" title="Caledonian orogeny">
         Caledonian Orogeny
        </a>
       </i>
       refers to the
       <a href="/wiki/Silurian" title="Silurian">
        Silurian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Devonian" title="Devonian">
        Devonian
       </a>
       events that resulted from the collision of
       <a href="/wiki/Laurentia" title="Laurentia">
        Laurentia
       </a>
       with Eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Avalonia" title="Avalonia">
        Avalonia
       </a>
       and other former fragments of
       <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">
        Gondwana
       </a>
       . The
       <i>
        Caledonian Orogen
       </i>
       resulted from these events and various others that are part of its peculiar orogenic cycle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strachan_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Strachan-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In summary, an orogeny is a long-lived deformational episode during which many geological phenomena play a role. The orogeny of an orogen is only part of the orogen's orogenic cycle.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Erosion">
        Erosion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Erosion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion" title="Erosion">
        Erosion
       </a>
       represents a subsequent phase of the orogenic cycle. Erosion inevitably removes much of the mountains, exposing the core or
       <i>
        mountain roots
       </i>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphic_rocks" title="Metamorphic rocks">
        metamorphic rocks
       </a>
       brought to the surface from a depth of several kilometres).
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        Isostatic
       </a>
       movements may help such exhumation by balancing out the buoyancy of the evolving orogen. Scholars debate about the extent to which erosion modifies the patterns of tectonic deformation (see
       <a href="/wiki/Erosion_and_tectonics" title="Erosion and tectonics">
        erosion and tectonics
       </a>
       ). Thus, the final form of the majority of old orogenic belts is a long arcuate strip of crystalline metamorphic rocks sequentially below younger sediments which are thrust atop them and which dip away from the orogenic core.
      </p>
      <p>
       An orogen may be almost completely eroded away, and only recognizable by studying (old) rocks that bear traces of orogenesis. Orogens are usually long, thin, arcuate tracts of rock that have a pronounced linear structure resulting in
       <a href="/wiki/Terrane" title="Terrane">
        terranes
       </a>
       or blocks of deformed rocks, separated generally by
       <a href="/wiki/Suture_(geology)" title="Suture (geology)">
        suture zones
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Strike_and_dip" title="Strike and dip">
        dipping
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">
        thrust faults
       </a>
       . These thrust faults carry relatively thin slices of rock (which are called
       <a href="/wiki/Nappe" title="Nappe">
        nappes
       </a>
       or thrust sheets, and differ from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic_plate" title="Tectonic plate">
        tectonic plates
       </a>
       ) from the core of the shortening orogen out toward the margins, and are intimately associated with
       <a href="/wiki/Fold_(geology)" title="Fold (geology)">
        folds
       </a>
       and the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
        metamorphism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merle_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Merle-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biology">
        Biology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Biology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the 1950s and 1960s the study of orogeny, coupled with
       <a href="/wiki/Biogeography" title="Biogeography">
        biogeography
       </a>
       (the study of the distribution and evolution of flora and fauna),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flora_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-flora-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mid_ocean_ridge" title="Mid ocean ridge">
        mid ocean ridges
       </a>
       , contributed greatly to the theory of plate tectonics. Even at a very early stage, life played a significant role in the continued existence of oceans, by affecting the composition of the atmosphere. The existence of oceans is critical to sea-floor spreading and subduction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lowman_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lowman-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        <i>

          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sharma_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sharma-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">

         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relationship_to_mountain_building">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Relationship to mountain building">

        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SunRiver.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1536" data-file-width="2048" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/SunRiver.JPG/250px-SunRiver.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/SunRiver.JPG/375px-SunRiver.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/SunRiver.JPG/500px-SunRiver.JPG 2x" width="250"/>

         </div>
         An example of
         <a href="/wiki/Thin-skinned_deformation" title="Thin-skinned deformation">
          thin-skinned deformation
         </a>
         (
         <a href="/wiki/Thrust_fault" title="Thrust fault">

         </a>
         ) of the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sevier_Orogeny" title="Sevier Orogeny">

         in

        </div>

        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sierra_Nevada_Mountains.JPG">

        </a>

         <div class="magnify">

         <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">
          Sierra Nevada Mountains
         </a>
         (a result of delamination) as seen from the
         <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
          International Space Station
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mountain_formation" title="Mountain formation">
        Mountain formation
       </a>
       occurs through a number of mechanisms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Huggett_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Huggett-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Einsele_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Einsele-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Douglas_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Douglas-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Mountain complexes result from irregular successions of tectonic responses due to sea-floor spreading, shifting lithosphere plates, transform faults, and colliding, coupled and uncoupled continental margins.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Peter J Coney
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Coney_22-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Coney-22">
           [22]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Large modern orogenies often lie on the margins of present-day continents; the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alleghenian_orogeny" title="Alleghenian orogeny">
        Alleghenian
       </a>
       (Appalachian),
       <a href="/wiki/Laramide_orogeny" title="Laramide orogeny">
        Laramide
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Andean_orogeny" title="Andean orogeny">
        Andean orogenies
       </a>
       exemplify this in the Americas. Older inactive orogenies, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Algoman_orogeny" title="Algoman orogeny">
        Algoman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Penokean_orogeny" title="Penokean orogeny">
        Penokean
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antler_Orogeny" title="Antler Orogeny">
        Antler
       </a>
       , are represented by deformed rocks and sedimentary basins further inland.
      </p>
      <p>
       Areas that are rifting apart, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mid-ocean_ridge" title="Mid-ocean ridge">
        mid-ocean ridges
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/East_African_Rift" title="East African Rift">
        East African Rift
       </a>
       , have mountains due to thermal buoyancy related to the hot mantle underneath them; this thermal buoyancy is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Dynamic_topography" title="Dynamic topography">
        dynamic topography
       </a>
       . In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strike-slip" title="Strike-slip">
        strike-slip
       </a>
       orogens, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/San_Andreas_Fault" title="San Andreas Fault">
        San Andreas Fault
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thrust_tectonics#Restraining_bends_on_strike-slip_faults" title="Thrust tectonics">
        restraining bends
       </a>
       result in regions of localized crustal shortening and mountain building without a plate-margin-wide orogeny.
       <a href="/wiki/Hotspot_(geology)" title="Hotspot (geology)">
        Hotspot
       </a>
       volcanism results in the formation of isolated mountains and mountain chains that are not necessarily on tectonic-plate boundaries.
      </p>
      <p>
       Regions can also experience uplift as a result of
       <a href="/wiki/Delamination_(geology)" title="Delamination (geology)">
        delamination of the lithosphere
       </a>
       , in which an unstable portion of cold
       <a href="/wiki/Lithosphere" title="Lithosphere">
        lithospheric
       </a>
       root drips down into the mantle, decreasing the density of the lithosphere and causing buoyant uplift.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delamination_lee_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-delamination_lee-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An example is the
       <a href="/wiki/Sierra_Nevada_(U.S.)" title="Sierra Nevada (U.S.)">
        Sierra Nevada
       </a>
       in California. This range of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fault-block_mountain" title="Fault-block mountain">
        fault-block mountains
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gerrard_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gerrard-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       experienced renewed uplift after a delamination of the lithosphere beneath them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-delamination_lee_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-delamination_lee-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, uplift and erosion related to
       <a href="/wiki/Epeirogenic_movement" title="Epeirogenic movement">
        epeirogenesis
       </a>
       (large-scale vertical motions of portions of continents without much associated folding, metamorphism, or deformation)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holmes_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Holmes-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       can create local topographic highs.
      </p>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Rundle" title="Mount Rundle">
        Mount Rundle
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Canada_Highway" title="Trans-Canada Highway">
        Trans-Canada Highway
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Banff,_Alberta" title="Banff, Alberta">
        Banff
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Canmore,_Alberta" title="Canmore, Alberta">
        Canmore
       </a>
       provides a classic example of a mountain cut in dipping-layered rocks. Millions of years ago a collision caused an orogeny forcing horizontal layers of an ancient ocean crust to be thrust up at an angle of 50–60°. That left Rundle with one sweeping, tree-lined smooth face, and one sharp, steep face where the edge of the uplifted layers are exposed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_concept">
        History of the concept
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orogeny&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History of the concept">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Before the development of geologic concepts during the 19th century, the presence of marine
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
        fossils
       </a>
       in mountains was explained in
       <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
        Christian
       </a>
       contexts as a result of the Biblical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deluge_(mythology)" title="Deluge (mythology)">
        Deluge
       </a>
       . This was an extension of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoplatonic" title="Neoplatonic">
        Neoplatonic
       </a>
       thought, which influenced
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_early_Christian_writers" title="List of early Christian writers">
        early Christian writers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 13th-century
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dominican_order" title="Dominican order">
        Dominican
       </a>
       scholar
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Albert_the_Great" title="Albert the Great">
        Albert the Great
       </a>
       posited that, as erosion was known to occur, there must be some process whereby new mountains and other land-forms were thrust up, or else there would eventually be no land; he suggested that marine fossils in mountainsides must once have been at the sea-floor. Orogeny was used by
       <a href="/wiki/Amanz_Gressly" title="Amanz Gressly">
        Amanz Gressly
       </a>
       (1840) and
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Thurmann" title="Jules Thurmann">
        Jules Thurmann
       </a>
       (1854) as
       <i>
        orogenic
       </i>
       in terms of the creation of mountain elevations, as the term
       <i>
        mountain building
       </i>
       was still used to describe the processes.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elie_de_Beaumont" title="Elie de Beaumont">
        Elie de Beaumont
       </a>
       (1852) used the evocative "Jaws of a Vise" theory to explain orogeny, but was more concerned with the height rather than the implicit structures created by and contained in orogenic belts. His theory essentially held that mountains were created by the squeezing of certain rocks.
       <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">
        Eduard Suess
       </a>
       (1875) recognised the importance of horizontal movement of rocks. The concept of a
       <i>
        precursor
        <a href="/wiki/Geosyncline" title="Geosyncline">
         geosyncline
        </a>
       </i>
       or initial downward warping of the solid earth (Hall, 1859) prompted
       <a href="/wiki/James_Dwight_Dana" title="James Dwight Dana">
        James Dwight Dana
       </a>
       (1873) to include the concept of
       <i>
        compression
       </i>
       in the theories surrounding mountain-building. With hindsight, we can discount Dana's conjecture that this contraction was due to the cooling of the Earth (aka the
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysical_global_cooling" title="Geophysical global cooling">
        cooling Earth
       </a>
       theory). The cooling Earth theory was the chief paradigm for most geologists until the 1960s. It was, in the context of orogeny, fiercely contested by proponents of vertical movements in the crust (similar to
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Tephrotectonics&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Tephrotectonics (page does not exist)">
        tephrotectonics
       </a>
       ), or convection within the
       <a href="/wiki/Asthenosphere" title="Asthenosphere">
        asthenosphere
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Steinmann" title="Gustav Steinmann">
        Gustav Steinmann
       </a>
       (1906) recognised different classes of orogenic belts, including the
       <i>
        Alpine type orogenic belt
       </i>
       , typified by a
       <a href="/wiki/Flysch" title="Flysch">
        flysch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Molasse" title="Molasse">
        molasse
       </a>
       geometry to the sediments;
       <a href="/wiki/Ophiolite" title="Ophiolite">
        ophiolite
       </a>
       sequences,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholeiitic" title="Tholeiitic">
        tholeiitic
       </a>
       basalts, and a
       <a href="/wiki/Nappe" title="Nappe">
        nappe
       </a>
       style fold structure.
      </p>
      <p>
       In terms of recognising orogeny as an
       <i>
        event
       </i>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leopold_von_Buch" title="Leopold von Buch">
        Leopold von Buch
       </a>
       (1855) recognised that orogenies could be placed in time by bracketing between the youngest deformed rock and the oldest undeformed rock, a principle which is still in use today, though commonly investigated by
       <a href="/wiki/Geochronology" title="Geochronology">
        geochronology
       </a>
       using radiometric dating.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hendrik_Jan_Zwart&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hendrik Jan Zwart (page does not exist)">
        H.J. Zwart
       </a>
       (1967)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       drew attention to the metamorphic differences in orogenic belts, proposing three types, modified by
       <a href="/wiki/Wallace_Spencer_Pitcher" title="Wallace Spencer Pitcher">
        W. S. Pitcher
       </a>
       in 1979
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and further modified as:
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Dennis, 1982 ? not as presented in Pitcher ref (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Hercynotype (
        <a href="/wiki/Back-arc_basin" title="Back-arc basin">
         back-arc basin
        </a>
        type);
        <ul>
         <li>
          Shallow, low-pressure
          <a href="/wiki/Metamorphism" title="Metamorphism">
           metamorphism
          </a>
          ; thin metamorphic zones
         </li>
         <li>
          Metamorphism dependent on increase in temperature
         </li>
         <li>
          Abundant
          <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">
           granite
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/Migmatite" title="Migmatite">
           migmatite
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          Few ophiolites,
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultramafic" title="Ultramafic">
           ultramafic
          </a>
          rocks virtually absent
         </li>
         <li>
          very wide orogen with small and slow uplift
         </li>
         <li>
          nappe structures rare
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Alpinotype (ocean trench style);
        <ul>
         <li>
          deep, high pressure, thick metamorphic zones
         </li>
         <li>
          metamorphism of many facies, dependent on decrease in pressure
         </li>
         <li>
          few granites or migmatites
         </li>
         <li>
          abundant ophiolites with ultramafic rocks
         </li>
         <li>
          Relatively narrow orogen with large and rapid uplift
         </li>
         <li>
          Nappe structures predominant
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        Cordilleran (arc) type;
        <ul>
         <li>
          dominated by calc-alkaline
          <a href="/wiki/Igneous_rock" title="Igneous rock">
           igneous rocks
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Andesite" title="Andesite">
           andesites
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Granite" title="Granite">
           granite
          </a>
          <a href="/wiki/Batholith" title="Batholith">
           batholiths
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          general lack of
          <a href="/wiki/Migmatite" title="Migmatite">
           migmatites
          </a>
          , low
          <a href="/wiki/Geothermal_gradient" title="Geothermal gradient">
           geothermal gradient
          </a>
         </li>
         <li>
          lack of ophiolite and abyssal
          <a href="/wiki/Sedimentary_rock" title="Sedimentary rock">
           sedimentary rocks
          </a>
          (black
          <a href="/wiki/Shale" title="Shale">
           shale
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="/wiki/Chert" title="Chert">
           chert
          </a>
          , etcetera)
         </li>
         <li>
          low-pressure metamorphism, moderate uplift
         </li>
         <li>
          lack of nappes
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The advent of plate tectonics has explained the vast majority of orogenic belts and their features. The cooling earth theory (principally advanced by
       <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes" title="René Descartes">
        Descartes
       </a>
       ) is dispensed with, and tephrotectonic style vertical movements have been explained primarily by the process of
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostasy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some oddities exist, where simple collisional tectonics are modified in a transform plate boundary, such as in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       , or where island arc orogenies, for instance in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Guinea" title="New Guinea">
        New Guinea
       </a>
       occur away from a continental backstop. Further complications such as Proterozoic continent-continent collisional orogens, explicitly the
       <a href="/wiki/Musgrave_Block" title="Musgrave Block">
        Musgrave Block
       </a>
       in Australia, previously inexplicable
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are being brought to light with the advent of seismic imaging techniques which can resolve the deep crust structure of orogenic belts.
      </p>
