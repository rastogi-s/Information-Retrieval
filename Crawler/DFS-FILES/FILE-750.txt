      <p>
       <b>
        Spanglish
       </b>
       (a
       <a href="/wiki/Portmanteau" title="Portmanteau">
        portmanteau
       </a>
       of the words "Spanish" and "English") is a name sometimes given to various contact dialects or
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole languages
       </a>
       that result from interaction between Spanish and English used by people who speak both languages or parts of both languages. It is a blend of Spanish and English
       <a href="/wiki/Lexical_item" title="Lexical item">
        lexical items
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       . Spanglish is not a
       <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">
        pidgin
       </a>
       , because unlike pidgin languages, Spanglish can be the primary speech form for some individuals.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Spanglish can be considered a variety of Spanish with heavy use of English or a variety of English with heavy use of Spanish.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It can be more related either to Spanish or to English, depending on the circumstances. Since Spanglish arises independently in each region, it reflects the locally spoken varieties of English and Spanish. In general different varieties of Spanglish are not necessarily mutually intelligible.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term was introduced by the Puerto Rican poet
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Ti%C3%B3" title="Salvador Tió">
        Salvador Tió
       </a>
       in the late 1940s, when he called it
       <b>
        Espanglish
       </b>
       or
       <i>
        Inglañol
       </i>
       (from
       <i>
        Español
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        English
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Inglés
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        Español
       </i>
       , respectively).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of these creoles have become recognized languages in their own right, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Llanito" title="Llanito">
        Llanito
       </a>
       language of
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/San_Andr%C3%A9s%E2%80%93Providencia_Creole" title="San Andrés–Providencia Creole">
        San Andrés–Providencia Creole
       </a>
       of Colombia.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_distribution">
        History and distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanglish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the late 1940s, the Puerto Rican journalist, poet, and essayist
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Ti%C3%B3" title="Salvador Tió">
        Salvador Tió
       </a>
       coined the terms
       <i>
        Espanglish
       </i>
       (later shortened to
       <i>
        Spanglish
       </i>
       ) for Spanish spoken with some English terms, and the less commonly used
       <i>
        Inglañol
       </i>
       (for English spoken with some Spanish terms).
      </p>
      <p>
       After Puerto Rico became a United States territory in 1898, Spanglish became progressively more common there as the United States Army and the early colonial administration tried to impose the English language on island residents. Between 1902 and 1948, the main language of instruction in public schools (used for all subjects except Spanish language courses) was English. Currently Puerto Rico is nearly unique in having both English and Spanish as its official languages
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (but see also
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico#Official_language" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       ). Consequently, many American English words are now found in the vocabulary of Puerto Rican Spanish.
       <i>
        Spanglish
       </i>
       may also be known by different regional names.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanglish does not have one unified dialect—specifically, the varieties of Spanglish spoken in New York, Florida, Texas, and California differ. Spanglish is so popular in many Spanish-speaking communities in the United States, especially in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miami_Hispanic" title="Miami Hispanic">
        Miami Hispanic
       </a>
       community, that monolingual speakers of standard Spanish may have difficulty in understanding it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is common in Panama, where the 96-year (1903–1999) U.S. control of the
       <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal" title="Panama Canal">
        Panama Canal
       </a>
       influenced much of local society, especially among the former residents of the
       <a href="/wiki/Panama_Canal_Zone" title="Panama Canal Zone">
        Panama Canal Zone
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Zonian" title="Zonian">
        Zonians
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Puerto Ricans living on the island of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Croix" title="St. Croix">
        St. Croix
       </a>
       speak in informal situations a unique Spanglish-like combination of
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rican_Spanish" title="Puerto Rican Spanish">
        Puerto Rican Spanish
       </a>
       and the local
       <a href="/wiki/Virgin_Islands_Creole" title="Virgin Islands Creole">
        Crucian dialect
       </a>
       , which is very different from the Spanglish spoken elsewhere. The same applies to the large Puerto Rican-descended populations of New York City and Boston.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanglish is spoken commonly in the modern United States, reflecting the growth of the Hispanic-American population due to immigration. According to the U.S. Census Bureau, the population of Hispanics grew from 35.3 million to 53 million between 2000 and 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman,_B._2000_&amp;_US_Census_2012_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guzman,_B._2000_&amp;_US_Census_2012-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hispanics have become the largest minority ethnic group in the US. More than 80% are of Mexican descent. Mexican Americans form one of the fastest-growing groups, increasing from 20.6 million to 34.5 million between 2000 and 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guzman,_B._2000_&amp;_US_Census_2012_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Guzman,_B._2000_&amp;_US_Census_2012-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Around 58% of this community chose California, especially Southern California, as their new home. Spanglish is widely used throughout the heavily Mexican-American and other Hispanic communities of Southern California.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothman,_Jason_pg._1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rothman,_Jason_pg._1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of Spanglish has become important to Hispanic communities throughout the United States in areas such as Miami, New York City, Texas, and California. In Miami a Spanglish familiarly known as "Cubonics" is spoken.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rothman,_Jason_pg._1_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rothman,_Jason_pg._1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanglish is known as bilingualism/
       <i>
        semi-lingualism.
       </i>
       The acquisition of the first language is interrupted or unstructured language input follows from the second language. This can also happen in reverse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many Mexican-Americans (Chicanos), immigrants and bilinguals express themselves in various forms of Spanglish. For many, Spanglish serves as a basis for self-identity, but others believe that it should not exist.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanglish is difficult, because if the speaker learned the two languages in separate contexts, they use the
       <i>
        conditioned system
       </i>
       , in which the referential meanings in the two languages differ considerably. Those who were literate in their first language before learning the other, and who have support to maintain that literacy, are sometimes those least able to master their second language. Spanglish is part of
       <i>
        receptive bilingualism.
       </i>
       Receptive bilinguals are those who understand a second language but don't speak it. That is when they use Spanglish. Receptive bilinguals are also known as
       <i>
        productively bilingual
       </i>
       , since—to give an answer, the speaker exerts much more mental effort to answer in English, Spanish, or Spanglish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Without first understanding the culture and history of the region where Spanglish evolved as a practical matter an in depth familiarizing with multiple cultures. This knowledge, indeed the mere fact of one's having that knowledge, often forms an important part of both what one considers one's personal identity and what others consider one's identity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spanglish_patterns">
        Spanglish patterns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanglish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Spanglish patterns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spanglish is informal and lacks documented structure and rules, although speakers can consistently judge the grammaticality of a phrase or sentence. From a linguistic point of view, Spanglish often is mistakenly labeled many things. Spanglish is not a
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       of Spanish because, though people claim they are native Spanglish speakers, Spanglish itself is not a language on its own, but speakers speak English or Spanish with a heavy influence from the other language. The definition of Spanglish has been unclearly explained by scholars and linguists despite being noted so often. Spanglish is the fluid exchange of language between English and Spanish, present in the heavy influence in the words and phrases used by the speaker.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanglish is currently considered a hybrid language by linguists—many actually refer to Spanglish as "Spanish-English
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code-switching
       </a>
       ", though there is some influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicalization" title="Lexicalization">
        borrowing, and lexical and grammatical shifts
       </a>
       as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The inception of Spanglish is due to the influx of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American" title="Latin American">
        Latin American
       </a>
       people into
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       , specifically the United States of America.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As mentioned previously, the phenomenon of Spanglish can be separated into two different categories:
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code switching
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicalization" title="Lexicalization">
        borrowing, and lexical and grammatical shifts
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Codeswitching has sparked controversy because it is seen "as a corruption of Spanish and English, a 'linguistic pollution' or 'the language of a "raced," underclass people'."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, a fluent bilingual speaker addressing another bilingual speaker might engage in code switching with the sentence, "I'm sorry I cannot attend next week's meeting porque tengo una obligación de negocios en Boston, pero espero que I'll be back for the meeting the week after"—which means, "I'm sorry I cannot attend next week's meeting because I have a business obligation in Boston, but I hope to be back for the meeting the week after."
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calques">
        Calques
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanglish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Calques">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Calques" title="Calques">
        Calques
       </a>
       are translations of entire words or phrases from one language into another. They represent the simplest forms of Spanglish, as they undergo no lexical or grammatical structural change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stavans_2000_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stavans_2000-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The use of calques is common throughout most languages, evident in the calques of Arabic exclamations used in Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes-Alcala,_pg._107_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Montes-Alcala,_pg._107-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "to call back" →
        <i>
         llamar p'atrás
        </i>
        (volver a llamar)
       </li>
       <li>
        "It's up to you." →
        <i>
         Está p'arriba de ti.
        </i>
        (Depende de ti.)
       </li>
       <li>
        "to run for governor" →
        <i>
         correr para gobernador
        </i>
        (presentarse para gobernador)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montes-Alcala,_pg._107_17-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Montes-Alcala,_pg._107-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Loan_words">
        Loan words
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanglish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Loan words">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        Loan words
       </a>
       occur in any language due to the presence of items or ideas not present in the culture before, such as modern technology. The increasing rate of technological growth requires the use of loan words from the donor language due to the lack of its definition in the lexicon of the main language. This partially deals with the "prestige" of the donor language, which either forms a dissimilar or more similar word from the loan word. The growth of modern technology can be seen in the expressions: "hacer click" (to click), "mandar un e-mail" (to send an e-mail), "faxear" (to fax), "textear" (to text message), or "hacker" (hacker). Some words borrowed from the donor languages are adapted to the language, while others remain unassimilated (e. g. "sandwich"). The items most associated with Spanglish refer to words assimilated into the main morphology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Borrowing words from English and "Spanishizing" them has typically occurred through immigrants.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This method makes new words by pronouncing an English word "Spanish style", thus dropping final consonants, softening others, and replacing certain consonants (i.e., M's, N's, V's) with B's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Examples:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "Aseguranza" (insurance)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Biles" (bills)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Chorcha" (church)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Ganga" (gang)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Líder" (leader) – considered an established Anglicism
       </li>
       <li>
        "Lonchear/Lonchar" (to have lunch)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Marqueta" (market)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Taipear" (to type)
       </li>
       <li>
        "Troca" (truck) – Widely used in most of northern Mexico as well
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fromlostiano" title="Fromlostiano">

       </a>
       is a type of artificial and humorous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wordplay" title="Wordplay">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Idiom" title="Idiom">

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>
       , which is a word-for-word translation of
       <i>
        de perdidos al río
       </i>
       ; an idiom that means that one is prone to choose a particularly risky action in a desperate situation (this is somewhat comparable to the English idiom
       <i>
        in for a penny, in for a pound
       </i>
       ). The humor comes from the fact that while the expression is completely grammatical in English, it makes no sense to a native English speaker. Hence it is necessary to understand both languages to appreciate the humor.
      </p>
      <p>
       This phenomenon was first noted in the book
       <i>
        From Lost to the River
       </i>
       in 1995.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The book describes six types of
       <i>
        fromlostiano
       </i>
       :
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Translations of Spanish idioms into English:
        <i>
         With you bread and onion
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Contigo pan y cebolla
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         Nobody gave you a candle in this burial
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Nadie te ha dado vela en este entierro
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         To good hours, green sleeves
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         A buenas horas mangas verdes
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Translations of American and British celebrities' names into Spanish:
        <i>
         Vanesa Tumbarroja
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Vanessa Redgrave
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Translations of American and British street names into Spanish:
        <i>
         Calle del Panadero
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Baker Street
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Translations of Spanish street names into English:
        <i>
         Shell Thorn Street
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Calle de Concha Espina
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Translations of multinational corporations' names into Spanish:
        <i>
         Ordenadores Manzana
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Apple Computers
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        Translations of Spanish
        <a href="/wiki/Minced_oath" title="Minced oath">
         minced oaths
        </a>
        into English:
        <i>
         Tu-tut that I saw you
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         Tararí que te vi
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       The use of Spanglish has evolved over time. It has emerged as a way of conceptualizing one's thoughts whether it be in speech or on paper.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Identity">
        Identity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanglish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Identity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The use of Spanglish is often associated with an individual's association with identity (in terms of language learning) and reflects how many minority-American cultures feel toward their heritage. Commonly in ethnic communities within the United States, the knowledge of one's
       <a href="/wiki/Heritage_language" title="Heritage language">
        heritage language
       </a>
       tends to assumably signify if one is truly of a member of their culture. Just as Spanish helps individuals identify with their Spanish identity, Spanglish is slowly becoming the poignant realization of the Hispanic-American's, especially Mexican-American's, identity within the United States. Individuals of Hispanic descent living in America face living in two very different worlds and need a new sense of bi-cultural and bilingual identity of their own experience. "This synergy of cultures and struggle with identity is reflected in language use and results in the mixing of Spanish and English." Spanglish is used to facilitate communication with others in both worlds; "...code-switching is not merely a random phenomena but rather a complex system composed of a variety". While some individuals believe that Spanglish should not be considered a language, it is a language that has evolved and is continuing to grow and affect the way new generations are educated, culture change, and the production of media.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Living within the United States creates a synergy of culture and struggles for many Mexican-Americans. The hope to retain their cultural heritage/language and their dual-identity in American society is one of the major factors that lead to the creation of Spanglish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Speakers of standard forms of Spanish may at times denounce Spanglish as a corrupted dialect. In fact, Spanglish is not about necessarily assimilating to English—it is about acculturating and accommodating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_New_York_Times_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_New_York_Times-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Still, Spanglish has variously been accused of corrupting and endangering the real Spanish language, and holding kids back, though linguistically speaking, there is no such thing as a pure or real language.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Presently, "Spanglish" is still viewed by most as a rather derogatory and patronizing word to its community because it seems like a "bastardized language". In reality, Spanglish has its own culture and has a reputation of its own.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is commonly assumed that Spanglish is a jargon: part Spanish and part English, with neither "gravitas nor a clear identity", says the author of
       <i>
        Spanglish
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and proponent of Spanglish, Ilan Stavans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Use of the word Spanglish reflects the wide range of views towards the mixed language in the United States. In Latino communities, the term Spanglish is used in a positive and proud connotation by political leaders.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also used by linguists and scholars promoted for use in literary writing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite the promotion of positive use of the term by activists and scholars alike, the term is often used with a negative connotation. People often refer to themselves as 'Spanglish speakers' if they do not speak Spanish well. The term Spanglish is also often used as a disparaging way to describe individuals that do not speak English fluently and are in the process of learning, assuming the inclusion of Spanglish as a lack of English fluency.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
