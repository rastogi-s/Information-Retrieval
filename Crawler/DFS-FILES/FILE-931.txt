      <p>
       <b>
        Dialects of the
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French language
        </a>
       </b>
       are spoken in France and around the world. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophones
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       generally use
       <a href="/wiki/Metropolitan_France" title="Metropolitan France">
        Metropolitan
       </a>
       French (spoken in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris,_France" title="Paris, France">
        Paris
       </a>
       and considered
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_language" title="Standard language">
        standard
       </a>
       ) although some also use regional
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dialects" title="Dialects">
        dialects
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        varieties
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Meridional_French" title="Meridional French">
        Meridional French
       </a>
       . In Europe outside France there are
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        Belgian French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Swiss_French" title="Swiss French">
        Swiss French
       </a>
       , and in Italy
       <a href="/wiki/Aostan_French" title="Aostan French">
        Aostan French
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       , French is an
       <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
        official language
       </a>
       along with English; the two main dialects of French in Canada are
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
        Quebec French
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">
        Acadian French
       </a>
       , but also another dialect commonly grouped as Canadian French, used by Anglophones speaking French as a second language or by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophones" title="Francophones">
        Francophones
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       using a different dialect. In
       <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">
        Lebanon
       </a>
       , French was an official language until 1941 and the main dialect spoken there is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lebanese_French" title="Lebanese French">
        Lebanese French
       </a>
       or Levantine French. Note that the discussion here refers to varieties of the French language, not to the Romance sister languages (sometimes considered dialects) of French spoken in France (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Limousin_dialect" title="Limousin dialect">
        Limousin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gascon_language" title="Gascon language">
        Gascon
       </a>
       , etc.; for these languages see:
       <a href="/wiki/Langues_d%27o%C3%AFl" title="Langues d'oïl">
        Langues d'oïl
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francoproven%C3%A7al" title="Francoprovençal">
        Francoprovençal
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_France" title="Languages of France">
        languages of France
       </a>
       ). See also
       <a href="/wiki/French-based_creole_languages" title="French-based creole languages">
        French-based creole languages
       </a>
       , which are also considered separate languages.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Africa">
        Africa
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Africa">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/African_French" title="African French">
        African French
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maghreb_French" title="Maghreb French">
        Maghreb French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       French is an administrative language and is commonly but unofficially used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       states,
       <a href="/wiki/Mauritania" title="Mauritania">
        Mauritania
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Algeria" title="Algeria">
        Algeria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">
        Morocco
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tunisia" title="Tunisia">
        Tunisia
       </a>
       . As of 2006, an estimated 115 million African people spread across 31 African countries can speak French either as a
       <a href="/wiki/First_language" title="First language">
        first
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Second_language" title="Second language">
        second language
       </a>
       , making
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       the continent with the most French speakers in the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2007_report_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2007_report-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While there are many varieties of African French, common features include the use of an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
        alveolar trill
       </a>
       and use of borrowed words from local languages.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Acadian">
        Acadian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Acadian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Acadian_French" title="Acadian French">
        Acadian French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Acadian French is a variant of French spoken by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophone
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Acadian" title="Acadian">
        Acadians
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canadian
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maritimes" title="Maritimes">
        Maritime provinces
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saint_John_River_(New_Brunswick)" title="Saint John River (New Brunswick)">
        Saint John River
       </a>
       Valley in the northern part of the U.S. state of
       <a href="/wiki/Maine" title="Maine">
        Maine
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Magdalen_Islands" title="Magdalen Islands">
        Magdalen Islands
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Havre-Saint-Pierre" title="Havre-Saint-Pierre">
        Havre-Saint-Pierre
       </a>
       , along the St. Lawrence's north shore. Speakers of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_French" title="Metropolitan French">
        Metropolitan French
       </a>
       and even of other Canadian dialects have some difficulty understanding Acadian French.
      </p>
      <p>
       Notable features include
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tj/
       </span>
       becoming
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡ʃ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɡ/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dj/
       </span>
       becoming
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d͡ʒ]
       </span>
       before
       <a href="/wiki/Front_vowel" title="Front vowel">
        front vowels
       </a>
       and the use of some archaic words.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chiac">
        Chiac
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Chiac">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Chiac" title="Chiac">
        Chiac
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Chiac is a dialect of combined Acadian French and English and is spoken mainly around
       <a href="/wiki/Moncton" title="Moncton">
        Moncton
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Brunswick" title="New Brunswick">
        New Brunswick
       </a>
       . The pronunciation of French words by is very different from other dialects and resembles English pronunciation. Chiac cannot be identified solely on its frequent use of English words since many other French dialects use many English words as well, but Chiac has an unusual amount of English. Chiac French has developed through proximity to English-speakers who settled nearby during the colonial period. Sounds that are characteristic of Chiac are the different use of the letters "d", "t", "r", and "c" (such as the word "bec", normally spoken in French with a softer "c" sound but spoken as "beck" with a hard "c" as in the English pronunciation). Other differences include the use of vowel sounds such as "ea", "eo", "on", "an", and "oi". Such English-stylized pronunciations are different from other dialects of North American French such as Québécois and Brayon. Some forms of Chiac deviate from the original language to the extent that it is nearly incomprehensible to the larger francophone community. Chiac is perhaps best categorized as a
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole language
       </a>
       alongside
       <a href="/wiki/Haitian_Creole" title="Haitian Creole">
        Haitian Creole
       </a>
       (of Haiti) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_French" title="Louisiana Creole French">
        Louisiana Creole
       </a>
       (of Louisiana), French dialects that incorporate Indigenous, African, and other European languages, as opposed to dialects such as Québécois and Brayon that deviate slightly from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metropolitan_French" title="Metropolitan French">
        Metropolitan French
       </a>
       but are nonetheless derived primarily from earlier dialects of French with little contribution from other source languages.
      </p>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Quebec_French" title="Quebec French">
        Quebec French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Quebec French is the dominant and most prevalent regional
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variety
       </a>
       of French found in
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       . Although Quebec French constitutes a coherent and standard system, it has no objective norm since the very organization mandated to establish it, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Office_qu%C3%A9b%C3%A9cois_de_la_langue_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Office québécois de la langue française">
         Office québécois de la langue française
        </a>
       </i>
       , believes that objectively standardizing Quebec French would lead to reduced interintelligibility with other French communities around the world.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ontario">
        Ontario
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ontario">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ontario_French" title="Ontario French">
        Ontario French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ontario French is often divided into two categories: North and South. The further north the more French is spoken and the closer the dialect and culture is to Quebec French. The further south, the French becomes a more pronounced French with a more English cultural influence as well as a Parisian grammar and dialect structure, as both Parisian and Canadian French are taught in the French immersion schools.
      </p>
      <p>
       Notable features include
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɪ]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʏ]
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʊ]
       </span>
       as allophones of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       , and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       in closed syllables and affrication of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡s]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d͡z]
       </span>
       before
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       (the word
       <i>
        tu
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t͡sy]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Long vowels are generally diphthongized in closed syllables (the word
       <i>
        fête
       </i>
       is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [faɛ̯t]
       </span>
       ).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Louisiana">
        Louisiana
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Louisiana">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_French" title="Louisiana French">
        Louisiana French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Louisiana French, the largest of the groupings, is spoken mostly in
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana" title="Louisiana">
        Louisiana
       </a>
       and derives from the forms of the language spoken by the colonists of lower
       <a href="/wiki/Louisiana_(New_France)" title="Louisiana (New France)">
        French Louisiana
       </a>
       . Louisiana French is traditionally into three dialects:
       <a href="/wiki/Colonial_French" title="Colonial French">
        Colonial French
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cajun_French" title="Cajun French">
        Modern Louisiana French
       </a>
       or "Acadian" French, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louisiana_Creole_French" title="Louisiana Creole French">
        Louisiana Creole French
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ammon307_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ammon307-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lsu_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lsu-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Colonial French was originally the dialect spoken by the land-holding educated classes. Acadian, the dialect of the
       <a href="/wiki/Acadians" title="Acadians">
        Acadians
       </a>
       who came to French Louisiana in droves following their
       <a href="/wiki/Expulsion_of_the_Acadians" title="Expulsion of the Acadians">
        expulsion
       </a>
       from Acadia during the
       <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">
        French and Indian War
       </a>
       , was spoken largely by the white lower classes. Louisiana Creole, a
       <a href="/wiki/French-based_creole_languages" title="French-based creole languages">
        creole
       </a>
       that developed long before Haitian immigrants arrived in Louisiana, largely developed as the tongue of the Louisiana Creole community and a significant portion of self-identified Cajuns. However, linguists now believe that the Colonial and Acadian dialects have largely merged into modern Louisiana French but remain distinct from Louisiana Creole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lsu_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lsu-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       French has gained co-official status with English in Louisiana, and there is both a thriving multi-generational base of speakers as well as a growing network of French immersion schools across the state in order to preserve the language. Louisiana also has a French-language society called CODOFIL (Conseil pour le développement du français en Louisiane).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Missouri">
        Missouri
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Missouri">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri_French" title="Missouri French">
        Missouri French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri_French" title="Missouri French">
        Missouri French
       </a>
       is now spoken by a handful of people in the
       <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">
        Midwestern United States
       </a>
       , primarily in
       <a href="/wiki/Missouri" title="Missouri">
        Missouri
       </a>
       . It is the last remnant of the form of French once spoken widely in the region known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Illinois_Country" title="Illinois Country">
        Illinois Country
       </a>
       , which was colonized as part of French Louisiana. It is considered very moribund, with only a few elderly speakers still fluent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ammon307_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ammon307-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="New_England">
        New England
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <a href="/wiki/Saint_John_River_(Bay_of_Fundy)" title="Saint John River (Bay of Fundy)">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Aroostook_County,_Maine" title="Aroostook County, Maine">

         ]

      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Haitian_French" title="Haitian French">
        Haitian French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Haitian French is the variety of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
        Haiti
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcult_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hcult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main difference between Haitian French and the Metropolitan French is in the Haitian speaker's intonation, a rather subtle
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haitian_creole" title="Haitian creole">
        creole
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcult_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hcult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Importantly, differences are not enough cause problems between both speakers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hcult_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-hcult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cambodian">
        Cambodian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Cambodian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_Cambodia" title="French language in Cambodia">
        French language in Cambodia
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Cambodian French is the French of
       <a href="/wiki/Cambodia" title="Cambodia">
        Cambodia
       </a>
       . It dates back to the
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        French
       </a>
       colonization of
       <a href="/wiki/French_Indochina" title="French Indochina">
        Indochina
       </a>
       in 1863. Colonists taught French to the local inhabitants, especially the
       <a href="/wiki/Khmer_people" title="Khmer people">
        Khmer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Overseas_Chinese" title="Overseas Chinese">
        Chinese
       </a>
       . The locals also taught the colonists
       <a href="/wiki/Khmer_language" title="Khmer language">
        Khmer
       </a>
       and some
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chinese_spoken_languages" title="Chinese spoken languages">
        spoken variants
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Teochew_dialect" title="Teochew dialect">
        Teochew
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Yue_Chinese" title="Yue Chinese">
        Cantonese
       </a>
       . Cambodian French was influenced by Khmer and Chinese spoken variants, and it was spoken by children of French men married to Khmer or ethnic Chinese women.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cambodian French is still used as a second language in some schools, universities and government offices, but most of the younger generations and members of the business world choose to learn
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       . Mostly, only older natives still speak French. Since the 1990s, there has been a small revival of French in Cambodia with French-language schools and centres opening. Many Cambodian students travel to France to receive studies as well as French-language media.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, Cambodia still has the smallest Francophone population of the three French-speaking Asian countries, the others being Vietnam and Laos.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indian_French">
        Indian French
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Indian French">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_French" title="Indian French">
        Indian French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Indian French is the French spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        Indians
       </a>
       in the former colonies of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pondicherry_district" title="Pondicherry district">
        Pondichéry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chandannagar" title="Chandannagar">
        Chandannagar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Karaikal_district" title="Karaikal district">
        Karaikal
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mah%C3%A9,_India" title="Mahé, India">
        Mahé
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yanam_language" title="Yanam language">
        Yanam
       </a>
       . There is a considerable influence from
       <a href="/wiki/Dravidian_languages" title="Dravidian languages">
        Dravidian languages
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_language" title="Tamil language">
        Tamil
       </a>
       (Puducherry Tamil dialect),
       <a href="/wiki/Telugu_language" title="Telugu language">
        Telugu
       </a>
       (Yanam Telugu dialect) and
       <a href="/wiki/Malayalam" title="Malayalam">
        Malayalam
       </a>
       (Mahé Malayalam dialect).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lao">
        Lao
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Lao">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_Laos" title="French language in Laos">
        French language in Laos
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lao French is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Laos" title="Laos">
        Laos
       </a>
       . It goes back to the French colonization of
       <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">
        Indochina
       </a>
       despite a decline in the language after the country's independence and the communist takeover. A revival has now raised the number of students learning French to 35%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, the Laotian élite and the elderly population speak French, which is the diplomatic language of Laos.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vietnam">
        Vietnam
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Vietnam">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/French_language_in_Vietnam" title="French language in Vietnam">
        French language in Vietnam
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Vietnamese French is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam" title="Vietnam">
        Vietnam
       </a>
       , which has the largest Francophone population in Asia. Over 5% of the population learn the language or speak it well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-La_Francophonie_in_Asia_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-La_Francophonie_in_Asia-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       French is also spoken among the elderly in Vietnam as a legacy of the colonial French era and also by the country's élite. A French
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pidgin_language" title="Pidgin language">
        pidgin
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/T%C3%A2y_B%E1%BB%93i" title="Tây Bồi">
        Tây Bồi
       </a>
       , was spoken by Vietnamese servants in French households during the colonial era. Since the end of the
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
        Vietnam War
       </a>
       in 1975, the number of French-speakers in Vietnam and the number of students taking the language have declined, but French remains taught as an optional foreign language in higher education.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aostan">
        Aostan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Aostan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Aostan_French" title="Aostan French">
        Aostan French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Aostan_French" title="Aostan French">
        Aostan French
       </a>
       (French:
       <i>
        français valdôtain
       </i>
       ) is the variety of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Aosta_Valley" title="Aosta Valley">
        Aosta Valley
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , where there is a significant trilingual
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francophone" title="Francophone">
        francophone
       </a>
       population. Some expressions, words and phrases are different from
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_French" title="Standard French">
        Standard French
       </a>
       , some of them are similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Swiss_French" title="Swiss French">
        Swiss French
       </a>
       and some reflecting the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese language
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       . Both French and Italian overlay the indigenous local language continuum of Aosta Valley, called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vald%C3%B4tain_dialect" title="Valdôtain dialect">
         Valdôtain
        </a>
       </i>
       (locally,
       <i>
        patois
       </i>
       ), which is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al" title="Franco-Provençal">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       in type.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Belgian">
        Belgian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Varieties_of_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Belgian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Belgian_French" title="Belgian French">
        Belgian French
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Belgian French (French:
       <i>
        français de Belgique
       </i>
       ) is the variety of
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       spoken mainly in the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Community_of_Belgium" title="French Community of Belgium">
        French Community
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , alongside related minority
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional languages
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Walloon_language" title="Walloon language">
        Walloon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Picard_language" title="Picard language">
        Picard
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Champenois" title="Champenois">
        Champenois
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lorrain_language" title="Lorrain language">
        Gaumais
       </a>
       . Belgian French and the French of northern France are almost identical.
      </p>
      <p>
       Notable features include a strong distinction between long and short vowels, the lack of the approximant
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɥ/
       </span>
       , and the use of certain
       <a href="/wiki/Belgicism" title="Belgicism">
        Belgicisms
       </a>
       .
      </p>
