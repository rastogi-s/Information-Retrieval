    Maximus Planudes
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Maximus Planudes
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        Μάξιμος Πλανούδης
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        Máximos Planoúdēs
       </i>
       ; c.
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        1260
       </span>
       – c.
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        1305
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       Greek monk, scholar, anthologist, translator,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Philologist" title="Philologist">
        grammarian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Theology" title="Theology">
        theologian
       </a>
       at Constantinople. Through his translations from Latin into Greek and from Greek into Latin he brought the Greek East and the Latin West into closer contact with one another. He is now best known as a compiler of the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_Anthology" title="Greek Anthology">
        Greek Anthology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maximus_Planudes&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Maximus Planudes lived during the reigns of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Byzantine" title="Byzantine">
        Byzantine
       </a>
       emperors
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_VIII_Palaiologos" title="Michael VIII Palaiologos">
        Michael VIII
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Andronikos_II_Palaiologos" title="Andronikos II Palaiologos">
        Andronikos II
       </a>
       . He was born at
       <a href="/wiki/Nicomedia" title="Nicomedia">
        Nicomedia
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Bithynia" title="Bithynia">
        Bithynia
       </a>
       in 1260, but the greater part of his life was spent in
       <a href="/wiki/Constantinople" title="Constantinople">
        Constantinople
       </a>
       , where as a
       <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
        monk
       </a>
       he devoted himself to study and teaching. On entering the monastery he changed his original name Manuel to Maximus.
      </p>
      <p>
       Planudes possessed a knowledge of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       remarkable at a time when
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       were regarded with some hostility by the Greeks of the Byzantine Empire. To this accomplishment he probably owed his selection as one of the ambassadors sent by emperor Andronikos II in 1327 to remonstrate with the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Venice" title="Republic of Venice">
        Venetians
       </a>
       for their attack upon the
       <a href="/wiki/Republic_of_Genoa" title="Republic of Genoa">
        Genoese
       </a>
       settlement in
       <a href="/wiki/Galata" title="Galata">
        Galata
       </a>
       near Constantinople. A more important result was that Planudes, especially by his translations, paved the way for the revival of the study of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek language
       </a>
       and literature in western Europe.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1828" data-file-width="2512" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/220px-Add_19391_19-20.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/330px-Add_19391_19-20.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/Add_19391_19-20.png/440px-Add_19391_19-20.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Add_19391_19-20.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The early-14th century map of the
         <a href="/wiki/British_Isles" title="British Isles">
          British Isles
         </a>
         from the
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Codex_Vatopedinus_655&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Codex Vatopedinus 655 (page does not exist)">
          Codex Vatopedinus 655
         </a>
         ,
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
          <a href="#cite_note-3">
           [3]
          </a>
         </sup>
         sometimes associated with Planudes.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       He was the author of numerous works, including: a Greek
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       in the form of question and answer, like the
       <i>
        Erotemata
       </i>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manuel_Moschopulus" title="Manuel Moschopulus">
        Manuel Moschopulus
       </a>
       , with an appendix on the so-called "
       <a href="/wiki/Political_verse" title="Political verse">
        Political verse
       </a>
       "; a treatise on
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       ; a biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Aesop" title="Aesop">
        Aesop
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Prose" title="Prose">
        prose
       </a>
       version of the
       <a href="/wiki/Fable" title="Fable">
        fables
       </a>
       ;
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Scholia" title="Scholia">
         scholia
        </a>
       </i>
       on certain Greek authors; two
       <a href="/wiki/Hexameter" title="Hexameter">
        hexameter
       </a>
       poems, one a eulogy of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Claudius_Ptolemaeus" title="Claudius Ptolemaeus">
        Claudius Ptolemaeus
       </a>
       — whose
       <i>
        Geography
       </i>
       was rediscovered by Planudes, who translated it into Latin— the other an account of the sudden change of an
       <a href="/wiki/Ox" title="Ox">
        ox
       </a>
       into a
       <a href="/wiki/Mouse" title="Mouse">
        mouse
       </a>
       ; a treatise on the method of calculating in use amongst the Indians (ed.
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=C._J._Gerhardt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="C. J. Gerhardt (page does not exist)">
        C. J. Gerhardt
       </a>
       , Halle, 1865); and
       <i>
        scholia
       </i>
       to the first two books of the
       <i>
        Arithmetic
       </i>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Diophantus" title="Diophantus">
        Diophantus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       His numerous translations from the Latin included
       <a href="/wiki/Cicero" title="Cicero">
        Cicero
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dream_of_Scipio" title="Dream of Scipio">
         Somnium Scipionis
        </a>
       </i>
       with the commentary of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Macrobius_Ambrosius_Theodosius" title="Macrobius Ambrosius Theodosius">
        Macrobius
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Ovid" title="Ovid">
        Ovid
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Heroides" title="Heroides">
         Heroides
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metamorphoses_(poem)" title="Metamorphoses (poem)">
         Metamorphoses
        </a>
       </i>
       ;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anicius_Manlius_Severinus_Boethius" title="Anicius Manlius Severinus Boethius">
        Boethius'
       </a>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/De_consolatione_philosophiae" title="De consolatione philosophiae">
         De consolatione philosophiae
        </a>
       </i>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/Augustine_of_Hippo" title="Augustine of Hippo">
        Augustine's
       </a>
       <i>
        De trinitate
       </i>
       . Traditionally, a translation of Julius Caesar's
       <i>
        De Bello Gallico
       </i>
       has been attributed to Planudes, but this is a much repeated mistake.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Daly_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Daly-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Heller_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Heller-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These translations were not only useful to Greek speakers but were also widely used in western Europe as textbooks for the study of Greek.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is, however, for his edition of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Greek_Anthology" title="Greek Anthology">
         Greek Anthology
        </a>
       </i>
       that he is best known. This edition, the
       <a href="/wiki/Anthology_of_Planudes" title="Anthology of Planudes">
        Anthology of Planudes
       </a>
       or Planudean Anthology, is shorter than the Heidelberg text (the
       <a href="/wiki/Palatine_Anthology" title="Palatine Anthology">
        Palatine Anthology
       </a>
       ), and largely overlaps it, but contains 380 epigrams not present in it, normally published with the others, either as a sixteenth book or as an appendix.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       J. W. Mackail in his book
       <i>
        Select Epigrams from the Greek Anthology
       </i>
       , has this to add of him:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Among his works were translations into Greek of Augustine's
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_of_God_(book)" title="City of God (book)">
          City of God
         </a>
        </i>
        and Caesar's
        <i>
         Gallic War
        </i>
        . The restored Greek Empire of the Palaeologi was then fast dropping to pieces. The Genoese colony of Pera usurped the trade of Constantinople and acted as an independent state; and it brings us very near the modern world to remember that Planudes was the contemporary of
        <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
         Petrarch
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
