      <p>
       <b>
        Fortition
       </b>
       is a consonantal change from a 'weak' sound to a 'strong' one, the opposite of the more common
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       . For example, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricative
       </a>
       or an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approximant" title="Approximant">
        approximant
       </a>
       may become a
       <a href="/wiki/Stop_consonant" title="Stop consonant">
        stop
       </a>
       (i.e.
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [v]
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [r]
       </span>
       becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       ). Although not as typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_change" title="Sound change">
        sound change
       </a>
       as lenition, fortition may occur in prominent positions, such as at the beginning of a word or stressed syllable; as an effect of reducing
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        markedness
       </a>
       ; or due to
       <a href="/wiki/Morphological_leveling" title="Morphological leveling">
        morphological leveling
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The extremely common approximant sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       is sometimes subject to fortition; since it is a semivowel, almost any change to the sound other than simple deletion would constitute fortition. It has changed into the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_palatal_fricative" title="Voiced palatal fricative">
        voiced fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       in a number of indigenous languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       , such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Eskimo%E2%80%93Aleut_languages" title="Eskimo–Aleut languages">
        Eskimo–Aleut languages
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ket_language" title="Ket language">
        Ket
       </a>
       , and also in some varieties of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . Via a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_palatal_approximant" title="Voiceless palatal approximant">
        voiceless palatal approximant
       </a>
       , it has turned in some
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       into
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ç]
       </span>
       , the voiceless equivalent of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       and also cross-linguistically rare though less so than
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʝ]
       </span>
       . Another change turned
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [j]
       </span>
       to an affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       during the development of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Fortition of the cross-linguistically rare
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interdental_fricatives" title="Interdental fricatives">
        interdental fricatives
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð]
       </span>
       to the almost universal corresponding stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [t]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [d]
       </span>
       is relatively common. This has occurred in most continental
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic languages
       </a>
       and several
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_dialects" title="English dialects">
        English dialects
       </a>
       , several
       <a href="/wiki/Uralic_languages" title="Uralic languages">
        Uralic languages
       </a>
       , and a few
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       , among others. This has the result of reducing the
       <a href="/wiki/Markedness" title="Markedness">
        markedness
       </a>
       of the sounds
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ð]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Fortition also frequently occurs with voiceless versions of the common
       <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
        lateral
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Approximant" title="Approximant">
        approximant
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       , usually sourced from combinations of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [l]
       </span>
       with a voiceless
       <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">
        obstruent
       </a>
       . The product is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral fricative">
        voiceless alveolar lateral fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɬ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Cushitic language
       <a href="/wiki/Iraqw_language" title="Iraqw language">
        Iraqw
       </a>
       , *d has lenited to /r/ between vowels, but *r has undergone fortition to /d/ word initially.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to language-internal development, fortition can also occur when a language acquires loanwords.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goidelic" title="Goidelic">
        Goidelic
       </a>
       languages frequently display fortition in loanwords as most initial
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative" title="Fricative">
        fricatives
       </a>
       (except for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̪]
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [f]
       </span>
       ) are disallowed in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dictionary_form" title="Dictionary form">
        citation form
       </a>
       of Goidelic words. Thus initial fricatives of loanwords are strengthened to the corresponding
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        unlenited
       </a>
       variant or the nearest equivalent if the fricative is not part of the phoneme inventory.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Post-nasal_fortition">
        Post-nasal fortition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fortition&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Post-nasal fortition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Spanish voiced stops/fricatives
       <i>
        b d y g
       </i>
       are strengthened to stops
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [b d ɟʝ ɡ]
       </span>
       initially, but also after nasals. Such post-nasal fortition is very common in
       <a href="/wiki/Bantu_languages" title="Bantu languages">
        Bantu languages
       </a>
       . For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Swahili_language" title="Swahili language">
        Swahili
       </a>
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       become
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       after a nasal prefix, and
       <i>
        w
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       ; voiceless stops become aspirated. In
       <a href="/wiki/Shambala_language" title="Shambala language">
        Shambala
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        l
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        r
       </i>
       become
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        gh
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɣ]
       </span>
       become
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       as well. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bukusu_language" title="Bukusu language">
        Bukusu
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [β]
       </span>
       and
       <i>
        w
       </i>
       become
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        j
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [dʒ]
       </span>
       , and
       <i>
        l, r
       </i>
       become
       <i>
        d
       </i>
       . In other languages, voiceless fricatives
       <i>
        f, s, hl
       </i>
       become affricates
       <i>
        pf, ts, tl
       </i>
       ; see for example
       <a href="/wiki/Xhosa_language" title="Xhosa language">
        Xhosa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is similar to the epenthetic stop in words like
       <i>
        dance
       </i>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈdæns ~ ˈdænts]
       </span>
       ) in many dialects of English, which effectively is fortition of fricative
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s]
       </span>
       to affricate
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ts]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
