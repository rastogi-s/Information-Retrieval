      <p>
       <b>
        Regional Italian
       </b>
       , sometimes also called
       <b>
        dialects of Italian
       </b>
       , is any regional
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Variety_(linguistics)" title="Variety (linguistics)">
        variety
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       . The various forms of Regional Italian have phonological,
       <a href="/wiki/Metre_(poetry)" title="Metre (poetry)">
        prosodic
       </a>
       and lexical features which originate from the underlying
       <a href="/wiki/Stratum_(linguistics)#Substratum" title="Stratum (linguistics)">
        substrate
       </a>
       , the actual
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        languages of Italy
       </a>
       . Such languages, with special reference to those being not politically recognized, are usually but imprecisely called "dialects" (
       <i>
        <span lang="it">
         <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dialetti#Italian" title="wikt:dialetti">
          <i>
           dialetti
          </i>
         </a>
        </span>
       </i>
       ), even though they are not
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       and are notably distinct from it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [note 2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The various
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_language" title="Corsican language">
        Corsican
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Italian" title="Central Italian">
        Central Italian
       </a>
       dialects are, to some extent, close to Standard Italian in terms of linguistic features, because the latter was based on a somewhat polished form of
       <a href="/wiki/Florentine_dialect" title="Florentine dialect">
        Florentine
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Regional_Italian_and_the_languages_of_Italy">
        Regional Italian and the languages of Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Regional Italian and the languages of Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The difference between Regional Italian and the actual
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        languages of Italy
       </a>
       , often imprecisely referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       , is exemplified by the following: in
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       , the language spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">
        Veneto
       </a>
       , "we are arriving" would be translated into
       <i>
        sémo drio rivàr
       </i>
       , which is quite distinct from the Standard Italian "stiamo arrivando". In the regional Italian of Veneto, the same expression would be
       <i>
        stémo rivando
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        siamo dietro ad arrivare
       </i>
       . The same relationship holds throughout the rest of Italy: the local dialect of standard Italian is usually influenced by the underlying regional language, which can be very different from Italian with regard to
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(linguistics)" title="Morphology (linguistics)">
        morphology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Vocabulary" title="Vocabulary">
        vocabulary
       </a>
       . Anyone who knows Standard Italian well can usually understand Regional Italian, while not managing to grasp the regional languages.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many contemporary Italian regions already had different
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Substratum" title="Substratum">
        substrata
       </a>
       before the conquest of Italy and the islands by the ancient Romans: Northern Italy had a
       <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
        Celtic
       </a>
       (that part of Italy was known as
       <i>
        Gallia Cisalpina
       </i>
       , "Gallia on this side of the Alps"), a
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(ancient)" title="Ligurian language (ancient)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Venetic" title="Venetic">
        Venetic
       </a>
       substratum. Central Italy had an
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_language" title="Etruscan language">
        Etruscan
       </a>
       substratum, Southern Italy had an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italic_language" title="Italic language">
        Italic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       substratum, and finally
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       had a
       <a href="/wiki/Paleo-Sardinian_language" title="Paleo-Sardinian language">
        Nuragic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Punic_language" title="Punic language">
        Punic
       </a>
       substratum. These languages in their respective territories contributed in creolising Latin, the official language of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">
        Roman Empire
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Even though the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_School" title="Sicilian School">
        Sicilian School
       </a>
       , using the
       <a href="/wiki/Sicilian_language" title="Sicilian language">
        Sicilian language
       </a>
       , had been prominent earlier, by the 14th century the
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan dialect
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Florence" title="Florence">
        Florence
       </a>
       had gained prestige once
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante Alighieri
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
        Francesco Petrarca (Petrarch)
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">
        Giovanni Boccaccio
       </a>
       all wrote major works in it: the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Divina_Commedia" title="Divina Commedia">
        Divina Commedia
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Il_Canzoniere" title="Il Canzoniere">
        Canzoniere
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/The_Decameron" title="The Decameron">
        Decameron
       </a>
       . It was up to
       <a href="/wiki/Pietro_Bembo" title="Pietro Bembo">
        Pietro Bembo
       </a>
       , a Venetian, to identify Florentine as the language for the peninsula in the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Prose_della_volgar_lingua&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Prose della volgar lingua (page does not exist)">
        Prose della volgar lingua
       </a>
       in which he set up Petrarch as the perfect model. Italian, however, was a literary language and so was a written rather than spoken language, except in Tuscany and Corsica.
      </p>
      <p>
       The creation of a unified Italian language was the main goal of
       <a href="/wiki/Alessandro_Manzoni" title="Alessandro Manzoni">
        Alessandro Manzoni
       </a>
       , who advocated building a national language mainly derived from Florence's vernacular with some inputs from Lombard and Venetian. Italian was then an unwieldy means for expressing thought. Having lived in Paris for a long time, Manzoni had noticed that French, on the contrary, was a very lively language, spoken by ordinary people in the city's streets. The only Italian city where common people spoke something similar to literary Italian was Florence, so he thought that Italians should choose Florentine as the basis for the national language.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Italian_history" title="Italian history">
        Italian Peninsula's history
       </a>
       of fragmentation and colonization by foreign powers (especially
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Austria-Hungary" title="Austria-Hungary">
        Austria-Hungary
       </a>
       ) between the fall of the
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Roman_Empire" title="Western Roman Empire">
        Western Roman Empire
       </a>
       and its unification in 1861 played a considerable role in further jeopardizing the linguistic situation. When the unification process took place, the newly founded country used Italian mainly as a literary language. Many
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       and non-Romance
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional languages
       </a>
       were spoken throughout the Italian Peninsula and the islands, each with their own local dialects. Following
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_unification" title="Italian unification">
        Italian unification
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Massimo_Taparelli,_marquis_d%27Azeglio" title="Massimo Taparelli, marquis d'Azeglio">
        Massimo Taparelli, marquis d'Azeglio
       </a>
       , one of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Camillo_Benso,_conte_di_Cavour" title="Camillo Benso, conte di Cavour">
        Cavour's
       </a>
       ministers, is said to have stated that while Italy had been created, there still was to create Italians (that is, a common
       <a href="/wiki/National_identity" title="National identity">
        national identity
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Italian as a spoken language was born in two "linguistic labs"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       consisting of the metropolitan areas in
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       , which functioned as magnets for internal migration. Immigrants were only left with the national language as a
       <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">
        lingua franca
       </a>
       to communicate with both the locals and other immigrants. After unification, Italian started to be taught at primary schools and its use by ordinary people increased considerably, along with mass
       <a href="/wiki/Literacy" title="Literacy">
        literacy
       </a>
       . The regional dialects of Italian, as a product of standard Italian clashing with the regional languages, were also born.
      </p>
      <p>
       The various
       <a href="/wiki/Languages_of_Italy" title="Languages of Italy">
        regional languages
       </a>
       would be kept to be used by the population as their normal means of expression until the 1950s, when breakthroughs in literacy and the advent of TV broadcasting made Italian become more and more widespread, usually in its regional varieties.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">
        Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The solution to the so-called language question, which concerned Manzoni, came to the nation as a whole in the second half of the 20th century by
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       , as its widespread adoption as a popular household appliance in Italy was the main factor in helping all Italians learn the common national language regardless of class or education level.
      </p>
      <p>
       At roughly the same time, many southerners moved to the north to find jobs. The powerful
       <a href="/wiki/Trade_union" title="Trade union">
        trade unions
       </a>
       successfully campaigned against the use of dialects to maintain unity among the workers. The use of Standard Italian helped the southerners, whose "dialects" were not
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible" title="Mutually intelligible">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       with those of northerners, assimilate. The large number of mixed marriages, especially in large industrial cities such as
       <a href="/wiki/Milan" title="Milan">
        Milan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turin" title="Turin">
        Turin
       </a>
       , resulted in a generation that could speak only Standard Italian and usually only partly understand their parents' "dialects".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diaspora">
        Diaspora
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Diaspora">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Primarily within North American Italian diaspora communities, Italian dialects that have nearly died out in Italy have been preserved in several major cities across
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . That is due, in large part, to older-generation immigrants, often with low levels of education, having left Italy during or before
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       and maintaining little contact with either Italy or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       . A significant number of endangered dialects have survived, passed on from one generation to another to varying degrees. They have kept innumerable archaisms as well as adopted linguistic features and lexical borrowings from
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_English" title="Canadian English">
        Canadian English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_French" title="Canadian French">
        Canadian French
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_American_Spanish" title="Latin American Spanish">
        Latin American Spanish
       </a>
       , respective to the milieu of the individual community in question.
      </p>
      <p>
       To a much smaller degree, a similar situation occurred in Middle Eastern-Italian communities, namely those of
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lebanon" title="Lebanon">
        Lebanon
       </a>
       , as well as South American-Italian diasporas in
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       . Italian diasporas within Europe tend to maintain much stronger ties with Italy and also have easier access to Italian television, which almost exclusively broadcasts in the standard language.
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">

        </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics_of_regional_Italian">
        Characteristics of regional Italian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Characteristics of regional Italian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Establishing precise boundaries is very difficult in linguistics, and this operation at the limit can be accomplished for individual phenomena (such as the realization of a sound), but not for all of them: it is necessary to proceed in part by abstractions. In general, an isogloss is an imaginary line that marks the boundary of a linguistic phenomenon. The line traditionally referred to as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Spezia-Rimini_line" title="La Spezia-Rimini line">
        La Spezia-Rimini
       </a>
       (though it is currently moving to the Massa-Senigallia line) is an important isogloss for Southern Europe, which delimits a continuum of languages and dialects characterized by similar phenomena that differ from others for these same phenomena.
      </p>
      <p>
       This imaginary line is used here to define not only a boundary between dialect groups, but also between Northern regional Italian on the one hand and Central and Southern regional Italian on the other. Other well-defined areas are the Tuscan, the extreme Southern Italian (comprising the peninsular part of
       <a href="/wiki/Calabria" title="Calabria">
        Calabria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Salento" title="Salento">
        Salento
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sicily" title="Sicily">
        Sicily
       </a>
       ), and finally the Sardinian ones.
      </p>
      <p>
       Based on borders like La Spezia-Rimini, here are the most well-identified groups of regional Italian.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Northern_Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Northern Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Northern regional Italian is characterized by a different distribution of the open and closed "e" and "o" ([e, ɛ, o, ɔ]) compared to the Florentine model, particularly evident in Milan, where the open "e" is pronounced at the end of the word (perché [per.kɛ]) or in the word body in closed syllable (i.e. followed by consonant: stesso [stɛs.so]) and the closed "e" in word body in open syllable (i.e. not followed by consonant: bene [ˈbeː.ne] ]). Except for the extreme Ligurian Levante, in Liguria and especially in the capital there is the opposite phenomenon: there is a tendency to close all the "e" even where the Italian standard does not envisage it. In Genoa for example the names Mattèo, Irène, Emanuèle and the name of the city are pronounced with the closed "e"; Moreover, there is no difference in the pronunciation of the word "pesca" either to mean the fruit and the act of fishing.
      </p>
      <p>
       A characteristic of the north in opposition to the south is the always voiced ([z]) consonant in intervocalic position, whereas in the south it is always voiceless: [kɔː.za] vs. [kɔː.sa]. Also in opposition to the south, the north is characterized by the reduction of phonosyntactic doubling at the beginning of the word (after vowels) and the almost total abandonment of the preterite tense in verb forms as it is not present in the majority of Gallo-italic languages (they are replaced by the present perfect).
      </p>
      <p>
       Widespread use of
       <a href="/wiki/Determiner" title="Determiner">
        determiners
       </a>
       before feminine names ("la Giulia") is also noted in almost all the north while the determiner coupled with male names ("il Carlo") is typical of the Po Valley.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the vocabulary are used words like "anguria", which means watermelon, (also common in Sardinia and Sicily) instead of "cocomero", "bologna" for "mortadella" (but not everywhere), "piuttosto che" (rather than) in the sense of "or" not "instead", etc. The latter, in particular, is a costume that has begun to spread also in other areas of Italy, stirring up the linguistic concern [9], as it is used with a semantic sense in contrast to that of the standard Italian.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Tuscany">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Tuscany and especially in Florence, the
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_gorgia" title="Tuscan gorgia">
        Tuscan gorgia
       </a>
       is very well known. That is, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lenition" title="Lenition">
        lenition
       </a>
       of the occlusive consonants in the post-vocal position, even at the beginning of the word if the previous word ends up by vowel: la casa "the house" [lahaː.sa], even to its total disappearance. Always in phonetics there is a decrease in the diphthong uo (ova, scola, bona instead of uova, scuola, buona), while in the syntax a tripartite system of demonstrative adjectives is in use: - codesto - instead of "questo" to indicate the subject close to the speaker (first person), the contact person (second person), or none of the two (third person). We also note the use of the impersonal formula to the first plural person: "noi si va" instead of "noi andiamo" and the replacement of the pronoun pronounced second person singular with the form of the pronoun object: "Te che fai stasera?" instead of "Tu che fai stasera?". There are several cases of this phenomenon even in the written language. Also typical of several areas including Tuscany is the use of the article before its own female name (la Elena, la Giulia); Such use has passed from Tuscany even in other regions, when used in front of the surname of known characters, particularly of the past (il Manzoni). In the vocabulary there is the use of spenge instead of "spegne" (extinguish) or words like "balocco", "busse" instead of "percosse" (beatings), "rena" instead of "sabbia" (sand), "cencio" instead of "panno" (cloth), "cocomero" instead of "anguria " (watermelon), etc.
      </p>
      <p>
       It should be pointed out that the Tuscan historical dialects (including
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_language" title="Corsican language">
        Corsican
       </a>
       ) belong to the same linguistic system of Italian, with minimal morphological and lexical differences compared to the standard Italian, so from this point of view there is no substantial difference Between local and regional Italian dialect; Obvious differences are in the pronunciation of consonants, thanks to phenomena such as the Tuscan gorgia, which have never entered the standard Italian.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Central_and_Southern_Italy">
        Central and Southern Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Central and Southern Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Central and Southern regional Italian, is characterized by the usage of the
       <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
        affricate consonant
       </a>
       s in front of the
       <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
        nasal consonants
       </a>
       (insomma [in.tsom.ma] instead of [in.som.ma]), and by the doubling of the g's and b's (abile [ab.bi.le] instead of [a.bi.le] , regina [reddʒiː.na] instead of [redʒiː.na]). A popular trait in the everyday southern speech is the usage of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apocope" title="Apocope">
        apocope
       </a>
       of the final syllable of the words, (ma' for mamma "mom", professo' for professore "professor", compa' for compare "buddy, homie" etc.).
      </p>
      <p>
       In continental Southern Italy there is a different distribution of closed and open vowels (The pronounce "giòrno" with an open o is very widespread in Campania for example), while in Calabria, Salento and Sicily closed vowels are completely missing and speakers just pronounce open vowels ([ɛ, ɔ]), while in the other regions the discrepancies with the pronunciation Standards are minor (albeit relevant) and non-homogeneous; On the Adriatic side is more evident, as in certain areas of central-east Abruzzo (Chieti-Sulmona), largely in central-northern Apulia (Foggia-Bari-Taranto), and in eastern Basilicata (Matera) where it is present The so-called "syllabic isocronism": free syllable vowels are all pronounced closed and those in complicated syllable all open (see the well-known example "a póco di pòllo"); Even in the
       <a href="/wiki/Teramo" title="Teramo">
        Teramo
       </a>
       area (northern Abruzzo), and up to Pescara, the vowels are pronounced with a single open sound (for example "dove volète andare stasèra?" [ˈdɔvɛ vɔˈlɛtɛ aɳˈdarɛ staˈsɛra], Thus showing an inexplicable coincidence with the phonetic outcomes of Sicily and Calabria, although there is no direct link with them. As already mentioned here, the intervocalic s is always voiced, and the use of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preterite_Tense" title="Preterite Tense">
        preterite
       </a>
       is also frequent instead of the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Present_perfect" title="Present perfect">
        present perfect
       </a>
       . In continental southern Italy, from Rome down to Calabria, possessive pronouns often are placed after the noun: for example "il libro mio" instead of "il mio libro" (my book).
      </p>
      <p>
       Another characteristic of regional Italian varieties in central and southern Italy is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Deaffrication" title="Deaffrication">
        deaffrication
       </a>
       of the letter c between vowels or at the beginning of a word followed by a vowell: in almost all peninsular Italy from Tuscany to Sicily "luce" is pronounced ([ˈluʃe]) instead of ([ˈlut͡ʃe]), "cena" is pronounced ([ˈʃena]) instead of ([ˈt͡ʃena]) as it is pronounced in northern Italy and in standard Italian.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Regional_Italian&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Sardinia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Based on the significant linguistic distance between the
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian language
       </a>
       (and
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia#Languages" title="Sardinia">
        any other
       </a>
       traditionally spoken in Sardinia) and Italian, the Sardinianised Italian emerging from the contact between such languages is to be considered a group of its own and its features can not be directly ascribed to either the Northern or the Southern Italian varieties.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is to be pointed out that, while the introduction of Sardinian words in a full Italian conversation is generally accepted, especially if they are Italianised in the process (e.g.
       <i>
        tzurpu
       </i>
       "blind",
       <i>
        scimpru
       </i>
       "dumb" and
       <i>
        babbu
       </i>
       "father" becoming
       <i>
        ciurpo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        scimpro
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        babbo
       </i>
       respectively), the regional Sardinian variety of Italian embracing almost any of the following syntactic and morphological changes is placed on the low end of the diastratic spectrum, and its usage (while relatively common among the less educated) is not positively valued by both the native Sardinian speakers, who regard it as neither Sardinian nor Italian and nickname it
       <i>
        italianu porcheddìnu
       </i>
       ("piggy Italian" standing for broken Italian), and the Italian monolingual ones.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Sardinianised Italian, the verb is usually sent back to the end of the sentence, especially in any exclamatory and interrogative sentences used in the direct speech (e.g.
       <i>
        Legna vi serve?
       </i>
       "Are you in need of some wood?" from the Sardinian
       <i>
        Linna bos serbit?
       </i>
       , compared with the standard Italian
       <i>
        Avete bisogno di un po' di legna?
       </i>
       ). It is also common for the interrogative sentences to use a pleonastic
       <i>
        tutto
       </i>
       , from the Sardinian
       <i>
        totu
       </i>
       , like in
       <i>
        Cosa tutto hai visto?
       </i>
       "What have you seen?" from
       <i>
        Ite totu as bidu?
       </i>
       compared with the standard Italian
       <i>
        Cosa hai visto?
       </i>
       . The present continuous makes use of the verb
       <i>
        essere
       </i>
       "to be" like in English rather than
       <i>
        stare
       </i>
       (e.g.
       <i>
        Sempre andando e venendo è!
       </i>
       "He/She is always walking up and down" from
       <i>
        Semper/Sempri andande e beninde est!
       </i>
       compared with the standard Italian
       <i>
        Sta sempre andando e venendo!
       </i>
       ): that is because the present continuous built with verb
       <i>
        stare
       </i>
       does not, in such regional variety, express the idea of an action ongoing at a certain point, but rather something that will take place in the very near future, almost on the point of happening (e.g.
       <i>
        Sto andando a scuola
       </i>
       with the meaning of "I'm about to go to school" rather than "Right now as we speak, I'm going to school"). It is also common to use
       <a href="/wiki/Antiphrasis" title="Antiphrasis">
        antiphrastic
       </a>
       formulas which are alien to Italian,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by means of the particle
       <i>
        già
       </i>
       (Sard.
       <i>
        jai
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        giai
       </i>
       ) which is similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       use of
       <i>
        ja... schon
       </i>
       especially for ironic purposes, in order to convey sardonic remarks (e.g.
       <i>
        Già sei tutto studiato, tu!
       </i>
       "You're so well educated!" from
       <i>
        Jai ses totu istudiatu, tue!
       </i>
       which roughly stands for "You are so ignorant and full of yourself!", or
       <i>
        Già è poco bello!
       </i>
       "He/It is not so beautiful!" from
       <i>
        Jai est pacu bellu!
       </i>
       meaning actually "He/It is so beautiful!"). One also needs to take into consideration the presence of a number of other Sardinian-specific idiomatic phrases being literally translated into Italian (like
       <i>
        Cosa sembra?
       </i>
       "What does it look like?" from
       <i>
        Ite paret?
       </i>
       meaning "How do you do?" compared to the standard Italian
       <i>
        Come stai?
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Mi dice sempre cosa!
       </i>
       "She/He's always scolding me!" from the Sardinian
       <i>
        Semper cosa mi narat!
       </i>
       compared to the standard Italian
       <i>
        Mi rimprovera sempre!
       </i>
       , or again
       <i>
        Non fa!
       </i>
       "No chance!" from
       <i>
        Non fachet!
       </i>
       /
       <i>
        Non fait!
       </i>
       compared to standard Italian
       <i>
        Non si può!
       </i>
       ), that would make little sense to an Italian speaker from another region.
      </p>
      <p>
       As mentioned earlier, a number of Sardinian and other local loanwords (be they Italianised or not) are also present in such regional dialect of Italian.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, for what regards phonetics, the regional Italian spoken in Sardinia follows the same five-vowel system of the Sardinian language without length differentiation, rather than the seven-vowel system.
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphony" title="Metaphony">
        Metaphony
       </a>
       has also been observed: any tonic
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        o
       </i>
       ([e, o]) have a closed sound whenever they are followed by a closed vowel (i, u), and they have it open if they are followed by an open one (a, e, o).
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercorrection" title="Hypercorrection">
        Hypercorrection
       </a>
       is also common when applying the Italian rule of
       <a href="/wiki/Syntactic_gemination" title="Syntactic gemination">
        syntactic gemination
       </a>
       ; intervocalic
       <i>
        t
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        p
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        v
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        c
       </i>
       are usually elongated. Intervocalic /s/ voicing is the same of Northern Italy, that is /z/.
      </p>
