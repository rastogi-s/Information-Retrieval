   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Selenographic coordinates
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

         <img alt="Moon-map.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="936" data-file-width="936" height="400" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Moon-map.png/400px-Moon-map.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Moon-map.png/600px-Moon-map.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Moon-map.png/800px-Moon-map.png 2x" width="400"/>

         <div class="magnify">

          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Selenographic coordinates
       </b>
       are used to refer to locations on the surface of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       . Any position on the lunar surface can be referenced by specifying two numerical values, which are comparable to the
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       of Earth. The longitude gives the position east or west of the Moon's prime meridian, which is the line passing from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_north_pole" title="Lunar north pole">
        lunar north pole
       </a>
       through the point on the lunar surface directly facing Earth to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_south_pole" title="Lunar south pole">
        lunar south pole
       </a>
       . (See also
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        Earth's prime meridian
       </a>
       .) This can be thought of as the midpoint of the visible Moon as seen from the Earth. The latitude gives the position north or south of the lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       . Both of these coordinates are given in
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Astronomers defined the fundamental location in the selenographic coordinate system by the small, bowl-shaped satellite crater '
       <a href="/wiki/M%C3%B6sting_(crater)" title="Mösting (crater)">
        Mösting A
       </a>
       '
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="Probably wrong. See: https://the-moon.wikispaces.com/Selenographic+Coordinates (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . The coordinates of this crater are defined as:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <table border="0">
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            Latitude:
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           3° 12' 43.2" South
          </td>
         </tr>
         <tr>
          <td>
           <i>
            Longitude:
           </i>
          </td>
          <td>
           5° 12' 39.6" West
          </td>
         </tr>
        </table>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The coordinate system has become precisely defined due to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_Experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment">
        Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Anything past 90°E or 90°W would not be seen from Earth, except for
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       , which makes
       <a href="/wiki/Moon#Two_sides_of_the_Moon" title="Moon">
        59% of the Moon visible
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Selenographic_colongitude">
        Selenographic colongitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenographic_coordinates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Selenographic colongitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        selenographic colongitude
       </b>
       is the longitude of the morning
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_terminator" title="Lunar terminator">
        terminator on the Moon
       </a>
       , as measured in degrees westward from the prime meridian. The morning terminator forms a half-circle across the Moon where the Sun is just starting to rise. As the Moon continues in its orbit, this line advances in longitude. The value of the selenographic colongitude increases from 0° to 359° in the direction of the advancing terminator.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunrise occurs at the prime meridian when the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        Lunar phase
       </a>
       reaches First Quarter, after one fourth of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_day" title="Lunar day">
        lunar day
       </a>
       . At this location the selenographic colongitude at sunrise is defined as 0°. Thus, by the time of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_Moon" title="Full Moon">
        Full Moon
       </a>
       the colongitude increases to 90°; at Last Quarter it is 180°, and at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Moon" title="New Moon">
        New Moon
       </a>
       the colongitude reaches 270°. Note that the Moon is nearly invisible from the Earth at New Moon phase except during a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The low
       <a href="/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics)" title="Angle of incidence (optics)">
        angle of incidence
       </a>
       of arriving sunlight tends to pick out features by the sharp shadows they cast, thus the area near the terminator is usually the most favorable for viewing or
       <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
        photographing
       </a>
       lunar features through a
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       . The observer will need to know the location of the terminator to plan observations of selected features. The selenographic colongitude is useful for this purpose.
      </p>
      <p>
       The selenographic longitude of the evening terminator is equal to the colongitude plus 180°.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ruklcolpres_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ruklcolpres-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Longitude">
        Longitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Selenographic_coordinates&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Longitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Longitude on the Moon is measured both east and west from its
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_meridian" title="Prime meridian">
        prime meridian
       </a>
       . When no direction is specified, east is positive and west is negative.
      </p>
      <p>
       Roughly speaking, the Moon's prime meridian lies near the center of the Moon's disc as seen from Earth. For precise applications, many coordinate systems have been defined for the Moon, each with a slightly different prime meridian. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       recommends the "mean Earth/polar axis" system,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in which the prime meridian is the average direction (from the Moon's center) of the Earth's center.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
