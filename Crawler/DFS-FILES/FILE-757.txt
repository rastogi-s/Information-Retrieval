   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Digraph (orthography)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

         <a href="/wiki/Welsh_orthography" title="Welsh orthography">
          Welsh
         </a>
         , the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ll_(digraph)" title="Ll (digraph)">
          digraph ⟨Ll⟩, ⟨ll⟩
         </a>
         fused for a time into a
         <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
          ligature
         </a>
         .

       <tr>

          <img alt="[]" data-file-height="100" data-file-width="75" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Parentesi_Quadre.svg/30px-Parentesi_Quadre.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Parentesi_Quadre.svg/45px-Parentesi_Quadre.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Parentesi_Quadre.svg/60px-Parentesi_Quadre.svg.png 2x" width="30"/>
         </a>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text plainlist" style="vertical-align:middle; font-size:95%;">
         <b>
          This article contains
          <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
           IPA
          </a>
          phonetic symbols.
         </b>
         Without proper
         <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Fonts" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
          rendering support
         </a>
         , you may see
         <a href="/wiki/Specials_(Unicode_block)#Replacement_character" title="Specials (Unicode block)">
          question marks, boxes, or other symbols
         </a>
         instead of
         <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
          Unicode
         </a>
         characters. For an introductory guide on IPA symbols, see
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA" title="Help:IPA">
          Help:IPA
         </a>
         .
        </td>
       </tr>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        digraph
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        digram
       </b>
       (from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        δίς
       </span>
       <i>
        <span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">
         dís
        </span>
       </i>
       , "double" and
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        γράφω
       </span>
       <i>
        <span lang="el-Latn" xml:lang="el-Latn">
         gráphō
        </span>
       </i>
       , "to write") is a pair of
       <a href="/wiki/Character_(symbol)" title="Character (symbol)">
        characters
       </a>
       used in the
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       of a language to write either a single
       <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
        phoneme
       </a>
       (distinct sound), or a sequence of phonemes that does not correspond to the normal values of the two characters combined.
      </p>
      <p>
       Digraphs are often used for phonemes that cannot be represented using a single character, like the English
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sh_(digraph)" title="Sh (digraph)">
         sh
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <i>
        ship
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        fish
       </i>
       . In other cases they may be relics from an earlier period of the language when they had a different pronunciation, or represent a distinction which is made only in certain dialects, like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English_wh" title="English wh">
        English
        <i>
         wh
        </i>
       </a>
       . They may also be used for purely
       <a href="/wiki/Etymology" title="Etymology">
        etymological
       </a>
       reasons, like
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh_(digraph)" title="Rh (digraph)">
         rh
        </a>
       </i>
       in English. Digraphs are used in some
       <a href="/wiki/Romanization" title="Romanization">
        Romanization
       </a>
       schemes, like the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zh_(digraph)" title="Zh (digraph)">
         zh
        </a>
       </i>
       often used to represent the
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_language" title="Russian language">
        Russian
       </a>
       letter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%D0%96" title="Ж">
        ж
       </a>
       . As an alternative to digraphs, orthographies and Romanization schemes sometimes use letters with
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        diacritics
       </a>
       , like the Czech
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/%C5%A0" title="Š">
         š
        </a>
       </i>
       , which has the same function as the English digraph
       <i>
        sh
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In some languages' orthographies, digraphs (and occasionally
       <a href="/wiki/Trigraph_(orthography)" title="Trigraph (orthography)">
        trigraphs
       </a>
       ) are considered individual
       <a href="/wiki/Letter_(alphabet)" title="Letter (alphabet)">
        letters
       </a>
       , meaning that they have their own place in the
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabet
       </a>
       , and cannot be separated into their constituent
       <a href="/wiki/Grapheme" title="Grapheme">
        graphemes
       </a>
       , e.g. when
       <a href="/wiki/Collation" title="Collation">
        sorting
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Abbreviation" title="Abbreviation">
        abbreviating
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">
        hyphenating
       </a>
       . Examples are found in
       <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_language" title="Hungarian language">
        Hungarian
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        cs, dz, dzs, gy, ly, ny, sz, ty, zs
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Slovak_language" title="Slovak language">
        Slovak
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        ch
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        dž
       </i>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Albanian_language" title="Albanian language">
        Albanian
       </a>
       (dh, gj, ll, nj, rr, sh, th, xh, zh) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaj%27s_Latin_Alphabet" title="Gaj's Latin Alphabet">
        Gaj's Latin Alphabet
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        lj, nj, dž
       </i>
       ). In
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       , when the
       <a href="/wiki/IJ_(digraph)" title="IJ (digraph)">
        digraph
        <i>
         ij
        </i>
       </a>
       is capitalized, both letters are capitalized (
       <i>
        IJ
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Digraphs may develop into
       <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
        ligatures
       </a>
       , but these are distinct concepts – a ligature involves a graphical combination of two characters, as when
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       are fused into
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%86" title="Æ">
         æ
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_letters">
        Double letters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Double letters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Digraphs may consist of two different characters (heterogeneous digraphs) or two instances of the same character (homogeneous digraphs). In the latter case, they are generally called
       <b>
        double
       </b>
       (or
       <b>
        doubled
       </b>
       )
       <b>
        letters
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Doubled
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowel
       </a>
       letters are commonly used to indicate a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_vowel" title="Long vowel">
        long vowel
       </a>
       sound. This is the case in
       <a href="/wiki/Finnish_orthography" title="Finnish orthography">
        Finnish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Estonian_orthography" title="Estonian orthography">
        Estonian
       </a>
       , for instance, where ⟨uu⟩ represents a longer version of the vowel denoted by ⟨u⟩, ⟨ää⟩ represents a longer version of the vowel denoted by ⟨ä⟩, and so on. In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_English_orthography" title="Middle English orthography">
        Middle English
       </a>
       , the sequences ⟨ee⟩ and ⟨oo⟩ were used in a similar way, to represent lengthened "e" and "o" sounds respectively; these spellings have been retained in modern
       <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">
        English orthography
       </a>
       , but the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Vowel_Shift" title="Great Vowel Shift">
        Great Vowel Shift
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_English_vowels" title="Phonological history of English vowels">
        other historical sound changes
       </a>
       mean that the modern pronunciations are quite different from the original ones.
      </p>
      <p>
       Doubled
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonant
       </a>
       letters can also be used to indicate a long or
       <a href="/wiki/Gemination" title="Gemination">
        geminated
       </a>
       consonant sound. In
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_orthography" title="Italian orthography">
        Italian
       </a>
       , for example, consonants written double are pronounced longer than single ones. This was the original meaning of doubled consonants in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_orthography" title="Old English orthography">
        Old English
       </a>
       , but during the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_English" title="Middle English">
        Middle English
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Early_Modern_English" title="Early Modern English">
        Early Modern English
       </a>
       period, phonemic consonant length was lost and a spelling convention developed in which a doubled consonant serves to indicated that a preceding vowel is to be pronounced short. In modern English, for example, the ⟨pp⟩ of
       <i>
        tapping
       </i>
       differentiates the first vowel sound from that of
       <i>
        taping
       </i>
       . In rare cases, doubled consonant letters represent a true geminate consonant in modern English; this may occur when two instances of the same consonant come from different
       <a href="/wiki/Morpheme" title="Morpheme">
        morphemes
       </a>
       , for example ⟨nn⟩ in
       <i>
        unnatural
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        un
       </i>
       +
       <i>
        natural
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In some cases, the sound represented by a doubled consonant letter is distinguished in some other way than length from the sound of the corresponding single consonant letter:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">
         Welsh
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ll" title="Ll">
         ⟨ll⟩
        </a>
        stands for a voiceless
        <a href="/wiki/Lateral_consonant" title="Lateral consonant">
         lateral consonant
        </a>
        , while in
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
         Catalan
        </a>
        it stands for a
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal consonant
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In several languages of western Europe, including English,
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        and Catalan, the digraph ⟨ss⟩ is used between vowels to represent the voiceless sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        , since an ⟨s⟩ alone between vowels normally represents the voiced sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In Spanish and Catalan,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rr_(digraph)" title="Rr (digraph)">
         ⟨rr⟩
        </a>
        is used between vowels for the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
         alveolar trill
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        , since an ⟨r⟩ alone between vowels represents an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
         alveolar flap
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɾ/
        </span>
        (the two are different phonemes in these languages).
       </li>
       <li>
        In Spanish the digraph ⟨nn⟩ formerly indicated
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɲ/
        </span>
        (a
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_nasal" title="Palatal nasal">
         palatal nasal
        </a>
        ); it developed into the
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%91" title="Ñ">
         letter ñ
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
         Basque
        </a>
        , double consonant letters generally mark
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         palatalized
        </a>
        versions of the single consonant letter, as in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dd_(digraph)" title="Dd (digraph)">
         ⟨dd⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ll" title="Ll">
         ⟨ll⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tt_(digraph)" title="Tt (digraph)">
         ⟨tt⟩
        </a>
        . However, ⟨rr⟩ is a trill, contrasting with the single-letter flap, as in Spanish, and the palatal version of ⟨n⟩ is written ⟨ñ⟩.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In several European writing systems, including the English one, the doubling of the letter ⟨c⟩ or ⟨k⟩ is represented as the heterogeneous digraph ⟨ck⟩ instead of ⟨cc⟩ or ⟨kk⟩ respectively. In native German words, the doubling of ⟨z⟩, which corresponds to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ts/
       </span>
       , is replaced by the digraph ⟨tz⟩.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pan-dialectical_digraphs">
        Pan-dialectical digraphs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Pan-dialectical digraphs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some languages have a unified orthography with digraphs that represent distinct pronunciations in different dialects (
       <a href="/wiki/Diaphoneme" title="Diaphoneme">
        diaphonemes
       </a>
       ). For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Breton_language" title="Breton language">
        Breton
       </a>
       there is a digraph ⟨zh⟩ that is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [z]
       </span>
       in most dialects, but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       in
       <i>
        Vannetais.
       </i>
       Similarly, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saintongeais" title="Saintongeais">
        Saintongeais
       </a>
       dialect of French has a digraph ⟨jh⟩ that is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [h]
       </span>
       in words that correspond to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʒ]
       </span>
       in standard French. Similarly, Catalan has a digraph ⟨ix⟩ that is pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʃ]
       </span>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Catalan" title="Eastern Catalan">
        Eastern Catalan
       </a>
       , but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [jʃ]
       </span>
       or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [js]
       </span>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Catalan" title="Western Catalan">
        Western Catalan
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Valencian" title="Valencian">
        Valencian
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discontinuous_digraphs">
        Discontinuous digraphs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Discontinuous digraphs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The pair of letters making up a phoneme are not always adjacent. This is the case with English
       <a href="/wiki/Silent_e" title="Silent e">
        silent e
       </a>
       . For example, the sequence
       <i>
        a...e
       </i>
       has the sound
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eɪ/
       </span>
       in English
       <i>
        cake.
       </i>
       This is the result of three historical sound changes:
       <i>
        cake
       </i>
       was originally
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kakə/
       </span>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_syllable" title="Open syllable">
        open syllable
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ka/
       </span>
       came to be pronounced with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_vowel" title="Long vowel">
        long vowel
       </a>
       , and later the final
       <a href="/wiki/Schwa" title="Schwa">
        schwa
       </a>
       dropped off, leaving
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kaːk/
       </span>
       . Later still, the vowel
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /aː/
       </span>
       became
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /eɪ/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       However, alphabets may also be designed with discontinuous digraphs. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Tatar_language" title="Tatar language">
        Tatar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_alphabets" title="Cyrillic alphabets">
        Cyrillic alphabet
       </a>
       , for example, the letter
       <i>
        ю
       </i>
       is used to write both
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ju/
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jy/
       </span>
       . Usually the difference is evident from the rest of the word, but when it is not, the sequence
       <i>
        ю...ь
       </i>
       is used for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jy/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        юнь
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jyn/
       </span>
       'cheap'.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Brahmic_scripts" title="Brahmic scripts">
        Indic alphabets
       </a>
       are distinctive for their discontinuous vowels, such as Thai เ...อ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɤː/
       </span>
       in เกอ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kɤː/
       </span>
       . Technically, however, these are
       <a href="/wiki/Diacritic" title="Diacritic">
        diacritics
       </a>
       , not full letters; whether they are digraphs is thus a matter of definition.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ambiguous_letter_sequences">
        Ambiguous letter sequences
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ambiguous letter sequences">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="mbox-small plainlinks sistersitebox" role="presentation" style="background-color:#f9f9f9;border:1px solid #aaa;color:#000">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <img alt="" class="noviewer" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="391" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/60px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/80px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png 2x" width="40"/>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text plainlist">
         Look up
         <i>
          <b>
           <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Category:English_words_with_pseudo-digraphs" title="wiktionary:Category:English words with pseudo-digraphs">
            Category:English words with pseudo-digraphs
           </a>
          </b>
         </i>
         in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Some letter pairs should not be interpreted as digraphs, but appear due to
       <a href="/wiki/Compound_(linguistics)" title="Compound (linguistics)">
        compounding
       </a>
       , like in
       <i>
        hogshead
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        cooperate
       </i>
       . This is often not marked in any way, so must be memorized as an exception. Some authors, however, indicate it either by breaking up the digraph with a
       <a href="/wiki/Hyphen" title="Hyphen">
        hyphen
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        hogs-head
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        co-operate
       </i>
       , or with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umlaut_(diacritic)" title="Umlaut (diacritic)">
        trema mark
       </a>
       , as in
       <i>
        coöperate
       </i>
       , though usage of this diaeresis has declined
       <a href="/wiki/Diaeresis_(diacritic)#In_English" title="Diaeresis (diacritic)">
        in English
       </a>
       within the last century. When it occurs in names such as
       <a href="/wiki/Clapham" title="Clapham">
        Clapham
       </a>
       , Townshend and Hartshorne, it is never marked in any way. Positional alternative glyphs may help to disambiguate in certain cases, e.g. when round ⟨s⟩ is used as a final variant of long ⟨ſ⟩ the English digraph resembling
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       would always be ⟨ſh⟩.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Romanization_of_Japanese" title="Romanization of Japanese">
        romanization of Japanese
       </a>
       , the constituent sounds (
       <a href="/wiki/Mora_(linguistics)" title="Mora (linguistics)">
        morae
       </a>
       ) are usually indicated by digraphs, but some are indicated by a single letter, and some with a trigraph. The case of ambiguity is the syllabic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%E3%82%93" title="ん">
        ん
       </a>
       , which is written as
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       (or sometimes
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       ), except before vowels or
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       where it is followed by an apostrophe as
       <i>
        n’
       </i>
       . For example, the given name じゅんいちろう is romanized as Jun’ichirō, so that it is parsed as /jun.i.chi.rou/, rather than as /ju.ni.chi.rou/.
      </p>
      <p>
       In several
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       , e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech
       </a>
       , double letters may appear in compound words, but they are not considered digraphs. Examples:
       <i>
        bezzubý
       </i>
       ‘toothless’,
       <i>
        cenný
       </i>
       ‘valuable’,
       <i>
        černooký
       </i>
       ‘black-eyed’.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_alphabetization">
        In alphabetization
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In alphabetization">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some languages, certain digraphs and
       <a href="/wiki/Trigraph_(orthography)" title="Trigraph (orthography)">
        trigraphs
       </a>
       are counted as distinct letters in themselves, and assigned to a specific place in the
       <a href="/wiki/Alphabet" title="Alphabet">
        alphabet
       </a>
       , separate from that of the sequence of characters which composes them, for purposes of
       <a href="/wiki/Orthography" title="Orthography">
        orthography
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Collation" title="Collation">
        collation
       </a>
       . For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaj%E2%80%99s_Latin_alphabet" title="Gaj’s Latin alphabet">
         Gaj’s Latin alphabet
        </a>
        used to write
        <a href="/wiki/Bosnian_language" title="Bosnian language">
         Bosnian
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Croatian_language" title="Croatian language">
         Croatian
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serbian_(language)" title="Serbian (language)">
         Serbian
        </a>
        , the digraphs
        <a href="/wiki/D%C5%BE" title="Dž">
         ⟨dž⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lj_(digraph)" title="Lj (digraph)">
         ⟨lj⟩
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nj_(digraph)" title="Nj (digraph)">
         ⟨nj⟩
        </a>
        (which correspond to the single
        <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Serbian Cyrillic alphabet">
         Cyrillic letters
        </a>
        ⟨џ⟩, ⟨љ⟩, ⟨њ⟩) are treated as distinct letters.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Czech_alphabet" title="Czech alphabet">
         Czech
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovak_alphabet" title="Slovak alphabet">
         Slovak
        </a>
        alphabet,
        <a href="/wiki/Ch_(digraph)" title="Ch (digraph)">
         ⟨ch⟩
        </a>
        is treated as a distinct letter, coming after
        <a href="/wiki/H" title="H">
         ⟨h⟩
        </a>
        in the alphabet. Also, in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slovak_alphabet" title="Slovak alphabet">
         Slovak
        </a>
        alphabet the relatively rare digraphs
        <a href="/wiki/Dz_(digraph)" title="Dz (digraph)">
         ⟨dz⟩
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/D%C5%BE" title="Dž">
         ⟨dž⟩
        </a>
        are treated as distinct letters.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Danish_and_Norwegian_alphabet" title="Danish and Norwegian alphabet">
         Danish and Norwegian alphabet
        </a>
        , the former digraph
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aa_(digraph)" title="Aa (digraph)">
         ⟨aa⟩
        </a>
        , where it appears in older names, is sorted as if it were the letter
        <a href="/wiki/%C3%85" title="Å">
         ⟨å⟩
        </a>
        , which replaced it.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_alphabet" title="Dutch alphabet">
         Dutch alphabet
        </a>
        , the digraph
        <a href="/wiki/IJ_(digraph)" title="IJ (digraph)">
         ⟨ij⟩
        </a>
        is sometimes written as a
        <a href="/wiki/Typographic_ligature" title="Typographic ligature">
         ligature
        </a>
        and may be sorted with
        <a href="/wiki/Y" title="Y">
         ⟨y⟩
        </a>
        (in the
        <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
         Netherlands
        </a>
        , though not usually in
        <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
         Belgium
        </a>
        ); however, regardless of where it is used, when a Dutch word starting with 'ij' is capitalized, the entire digraph is capitalized (
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/IJmeer" title="IJmeer">
          IJmeer
         </a>
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/IJmuiden" title="IJmuiden">
          IJmuiden
         </a>
        </i>
        ). Other
        <a href="#Dutch">
         Dutch digraphs
        </a>
        are never treated as single letters.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_alphabet" title="Hungarian alphabet">
         Hungarian
        </a>
        , the digraphs
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_cs" title="Hungarian cs">
         ⟨cs⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_dz" title="Hungarian dz">
         ⟨dz⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_gy" title="Hungarian gy">
         ⟨gy⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_ly" title="Hungarian ly">
         ⟨ly⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_ny" title="Hungarian ny">
         ⟨ny⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_sz" title="Hungarian sz">
         ⟨sz⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_ty" title="Hungarian ty">
         ⟨ty⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_zs" title="Hungarian zs">
         ⟨zs⟩
        </a>
        , and the trigraph
        <a href="/wiki/Hungarian_dzs" title="Hungarian dzs">
         ⟨dzs⟩
        </a>
        , have their own places in the alphabet (where ⟨cs⟩ follows ⟨c⟩, ⟨dz⟩ and ⟨dzs⟩ follow ⟨d⟩, etc.)
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_alphabet" title="Spanish alphabet">
         Spanish
        </a>
        , the digraphs
        <a href="/wiki/Ch_(digraph)" title="Ch (digraph)">
         ⟨ch⟩
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Ll" title="Ll">
         ⟨ll⟩
        </a>
        were formerly treated as distinct letters, but are now split into their constituent letters.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_alphabet" title="Welsh alphabet">
         Welsh
        </a>
        , the alphabet includes the digraphs
        <a href="/wiki/Ch_(digraph)" title="Ch (digraph)">
         ⟨ch⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dd_(digraph)" title="Dd (digraph)">
         ⟨dd⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ff_(digraph)" title="Ff (digraph)">
         ⟨ff⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Ll" title="Ll">
         ⟨ll⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ng_(digraph)" title="Ng (digraph)">
         ⟨ng⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ph_(digraph)" title="Ph (digraph)">
         ⟨ph⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rh_(digraph)" title="Rh (digraph)">
         ⟨rh⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Th_(digraph)" title="Th (digraph)">
         ⟨th⟩
        </a>
        . However,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mh_(digraph)" title="Mh (digraph)">
         ⟨mh⟩
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Nh_(digraph)" title="Nh (digraph)">
         ⟨nh⟩
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ngh_(letter)" title="Ngh (letter)">
         ⟨ngh⟩
        </a>
        , which represent
        <a href="/wiki/Consonant_mutation" title="Consonant mutation">
         mutated
        </a>
        voiceless consonants, are not treated as distinct letters.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Wymysorys_language" title="Wymysorys language">
         Wymysorys
        </a>
        micro-language,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ao_(digraph)" title="Ao (digraph)">
         ⟨ao⟩
        </a>
        is treated as a distinct letter.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Most other languages, including English, French, German, Polish, etc., treat digraphs as combinations of separate letters for alphabetization purposes.
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="English">
        English
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: English">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       English has both homogeneous digraphs (doubled letters) and heterogeneous digraphs (digraphs consisting of two different letters). Those of the latter type include the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        ⟨sc⟩ normally represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
         voiceless alveolar fricative
        </a>
        -
        <i>
         scene
        </i>
        ) or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         voiceless postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        -
        <i>
         conscious
        </i>
        ) before ⟨e⟩ or ⟨i⟩.
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨ng⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋ/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
         velar nasal
        </a>
        ) as in
        <i>
         thing
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Ch_(digraph)" title="Ch (digraph)">
         ch
        </a>
        ⟩ usually corresponds to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless postalveolar affricate">
         voiceless postalveolar affricate
        </a>
        -church), to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_plosive" title="Voiceless velar plosive">
         voiceless velar plosive
        </a>
        ) when used as an etymological digraph in words of Greek origin (
        <i>
         christ
        </i>
        ), less commonly to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         voiceless postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        ) in words of French origin (
        <i>
         champagne
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨ck⟩ corresponds to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        as in
        <i>
         check
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Gh_(digraph)" title="Gh (digraph)">
         gh
        </a>
        ⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_velar_plosive" title="Voiced velar plosive">
         voiced velar plosive
        </a>
        ) at the beginning of words (
        <i>
         ghost
        </i>
        ), represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         voiceless labiodental fricative
        </a>
        in
        <i>
         enough
        </i>
        ) or is
        <a href="/wiki/Silent_letter" title="Silent letter">
         silent
        </a>
        at the end of words (
        <i>
         sigh
        </i>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨ph⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         voiceless labiodental fricative
        </a>
        ), as in
        <i>
         siphon
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨rh⟩ represents English
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        in words of Greek origin, such as
        <i>
         rhythm
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Sh_(digraph)" title="Sh (digraph)">
         sh
        </a>
        ⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_postalveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless postalveolar fricative">
         voiceless postalveolar fricative
        </a>
        ), as in
        <i>
         sheep
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨ti⟩ usually represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        word-medially before a vowel, as in
        <i>
         education
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Th_(digraph)" title="Th (digraph)">
         th
        </a>
        ⟩ usually corresponds to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_interdental_fricative" title="Voiceless interdental fricative">
         voiceless interdental fricative
        </a>
        ) in
        <i>
         thin
        </i>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ð/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_interdental_fricative" title="Voiced interdental fricative">
         voiced interdental fricative
        </a>
        ) in
        <i>
         then
        </i>
        . See also
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pronunciation_of_English_th" title="Pronunciation of English th">
         Pronunciation of English ⟨th⟩
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨wh⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /hw/
        </span>
        in some conservative dialects;
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /w/
        </span>
        in other dialects (
        <i>
         while
        </i>
        ); and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /h/
        </span>
        in a few words where it is followed by ⟨o⟩, such as
        <i>
         who
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         whole
        </i>
        . See also
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phonological_history_of_wh" title="Phonological history of wh">
         Phonological history of ⟨wh⟩
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨zh⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʒ/
        </span>
        in words transliterated from Slavic languages, and in American dictionary pronunciation spelling.
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨ci⟩ usually appears as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        before vowels, like in
        <i>
         facial
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         artificial
        </i>
        . Otherwise it is
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /si/
        </span>
        as in
        <i>
         fancier
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         icier
        </i>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /sɪ/
        </span>
        as in
        <i>
         acid
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         rancid
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨wr⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        . Originally, it stood for a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labialisation" title="Labialisation">
         labialized
        </a>
        sound, while ⟨r⟩ without ⟨w⟩ was non-labialized, but this distinction was lost in most dialects, the two sounds merging into a single
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         alveolar approximant
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Allophone" title="Allophone">
         allophonically
        </a>
        labialized at the start of syllables, as in
        <i>
         red
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɹʷɛd]
        </span>
        . See also
        <a href="/wiki/Rhotic_consonant" title="Rhotic consonant">
         Rhotic consonant
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨qu⟩ usually represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kw/
        </span>
        ; ⟨q⟩ is conventionally followed by ⟨u⟩ and a vowel letter as in
        <i>
         quick
        </i>
        , with
        <a href="/wiki/List_of_English_words_containing_Q_not_followed_by_U" title="List of English words containing Q not followed by U">
         some exceptions.
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Digraphs may also be composed of vowels. Some letters ⟨a, e, o⟩ are preferred for the first position, others for the second ⟨i, u⟩. The latter have
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allograph" title="Allograph">
        allographs
       </a>
       ⟨y, w⟩ in
       <a href="/wiki/English_orthography" title="English orthography">
        English orthography
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       <li>
        ⟨
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lj_(digraph)" title="Lj (digraph)">

        </a>

        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       <li>
        ⟨
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nj_(digraph)" title="Nj (digraph)">

        ⟩ corresponds to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_postalveolar_affricate" title="Voiced postalveolar affricate">

      <p>
       Note that in the
       <a href="/wiki/Serbian_Cyrillic_alphabet" title="Serbian Cyrillic alphabet">
        Cyrillic orthography

       , these sounds are represented by single letters (љ, њ, џ).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Czech_language" title="Czech language">
        Czech

      </p>
      <ul>

        </span>

        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_velar_fricative" title="Voiceless velar fricative">
         voiceless velar fricative
        </a>
        ), counted as a distinct letter
       </li>
       <li>

        <a href="/wiki/Dz_(digraph)" title="Dz (digraph)">
         dz

         /d͡z/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_alveolar_affricate" title="Voiced alveolar affricate">
         voiced alveolar affricate
        </a>
        ), counted as a distinct letter in Slovak, relatively rare digraph
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/D%C5%BE" title="Dž">
         dž
        </a>
        ⟩ corresponds to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d͡ʒ/

         voiceless alveolar trill
        </a>
        ), pronounced roughly like the combination
        <i>
         hr
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨th⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_interdental_fricative" title="Voiceless interdental fricative">
         voiceless interdental fricative
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dd_(digraph)" title="Dd (digraph)">
         dd
        </a>
        ⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ð/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
         voiced dental fricative
        </a>
        ), like the English ⟨th⟩ in
        <i>
         then
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ff_(digraph)" title="Ff (digraph)">
         ff
        </a>
        ⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f/
        </span>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
         voiceless labiodental fricative
        </a>
        ), like English ⟨f⟩, since Welsh ⟨f⟩ is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /v/
        </span>
        like an English ⟨v⟩.
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨
        <a href="/wiki/Ll" title="Ll">
         ll
        </a>
        ⟩ represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɬ/
        </span>
        (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_lateral_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar lateral fricative">
         voiceless alveolar lateral fricative
        </a>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The digraphs listed above represent distinct phonemes. On the other hand, the digraphs ⟨
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mh_(digraph)" title="Mh (digraph)">
        mh
       </a>
       ⟩, ⟨
       <a href="/wiki/Nh_(digraph)" title="Nh (digraph)">
        nh
       </a>
       ⟩, and the trigraph ⟨
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ngh_(letter)" title="Ngh (letter)">
        ngh
       </a>
       ⟩, which stand for
       <a href="/wiki/Voice_(phonetics)" title="Voice (phonetics)">
        voiceless consonants
       </a>
       , but only occur at the beginning of words as a result of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Welsh_morphology" title="Welsh morphology">
        nasal mutation
       </a>
       , are not included in the alphabet.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Daighi_tongiong_pingim" title="Daighi tongiong pingim">
        Daighi tongiong pingim
       </a>
       , a transcription system used for
       <a href="/wiki/Taiwanese_Hokkien" title="Taiwanese Hokkien">
        Taiwanese Hokkien
       </a>
       , includes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Or_(Digraph)" title="Or (Digraph)">
        or
       </a>
       which represents
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ə/
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Mid_central_vowel" title="Mid central vowel">
        mid central vowel
       </a>
       ) or
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid back rounded vowel">
        close-mid back rounded vowel
       </a>
       ), as well as other digraphs.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cyrillic">
        Cyrillic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Cyrillic">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Cyrillic_digraphs" title="Cyrillic digraphs">
        Cyrillic digraphs
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Modern Slavic languages written in the Cyrillic alphabet make little use of digraphs apart from ⟨дж⟩ for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dʐ/
       </span>
       , ⟨дз⟩ for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /dz/
       </span>
       (in Ukrainian, Belarusian, and Bulgarian), and ⟨жж⟩ and ⟨зж⟩ for the uncommon Russian phoneme
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʑː/
       </span>
       . In Russian, the sequences ⟨дж⟩ and ⟨дз⟩ do occur (mainly in loanwords), but are pronounced as combinations of an implosive (sometimes treated as an affricate) and a fricative; implosives are treated as allophones of the plosive /d̪/, so these sequences are not considered to be digraphs. Cyrillic only has large numbers of digraphs when used to write non-Slavic languages, especially
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caucasian_languages" title="Caucasian languages">
        Caucasian languages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Arabic">

       </i>
       were used for
       <i>
        š,
       </i>
       then the sequence
       <i>
        sh
       </i>
       could mean either
       <i>
        ša
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        saha.
       </i>

        aspirated

        murmured consonants

        h-
       </i>

       <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
        South Asia
       </a>
       such as
       <a href="/wiki/Urdu" title="Urdu">
        Urdu
       </a>

       <i>
        h
       </i>

      <p>
       In the

       , the digraph
       <i>

        </a>

        /u/

       <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">

       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Georgian">
        Georgian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Georgian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Georgian_alphabet" title="Georgian alphabet">
        Georgian alphabet
       </a>
       uses a few diacritics when writing other languages. For example, in
       <a href="/wiki/Svan_language" title="Svan language">
        Svan
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ø/
       </span>
       is ჳე "we", and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       is ჳი "wi".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Greek">
        Greek
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Greek">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Modern Greek
       </a>
       has the following digraphs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         αι
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         ai
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e̞/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ει
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         ei
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         οι
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         oi
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         ου
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         oy
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /u/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         υι
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         yi
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These are called "diphthongs" in Greek; in classical times most of them did represent
       <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
        diphthongs
       </a>
       , and the name has stuck.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         γγ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         gg
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋɡ/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         τσ
        </i>
        represents the affricate
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ts/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         τζ
        </i>
        represents the affricate
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dz/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Initial
        <i>
         γκ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         gk
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Initial
        <i>
         μπ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         mp
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /b/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Initial
        <i>
         ντ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         nt
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /d/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Ancient Greek also had the "diphthongs" listed above although their pronunciation in ancient times is disputed. In addition Ancient Greek also used the letter γ combined with a velar stop to produce the following digraphs:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         γγ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         gg
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋɡ/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         γκ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         gk
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋɡ/
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         γχ
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         gkh
        </i>
        ) represents
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ŋkʰ/
        </span>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Tsakonian_language" title="Tsakonian language">
        Tsakonian
       </a>
       has a few additional digraphs:
       <i>
        ρζ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʒ/
       </span>
       (historically perhaps a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fricative_trill" title="Fricative trill">
        fricative trill
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        κχ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /kʰ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        τθ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /tʰ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        πφ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /pʰ/
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        σχ
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʃ/
       </span>
       . In addition,
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
        palatal consonants
       </a>

       <i>
        ι
       </i>

       </i>

       </i>

        λλ
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Bactrian_language" title="Bactrian language">
        Bactrian
       </a>
       , the digraphs
       <i>
        ββ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        δδ
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        γγ
       </i>
       were used for
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">

       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /d/
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ŋg/
       </span>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indic">
        Indic
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       Most
       <a href="/wiki/Brahmic_scripts" title="Brahmic scripts">

      </p>

           </span>

          </td>

           </span>

           </span>

         </tr>

          </td>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japanese">
        Japanese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Japanese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Two
       <a href="/wiki/Kana" title="Kana">
        kana
       </a>
       may be combined into a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
         C
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
         V
        </a>
       </i>
       syllable by subscripting the second; this convention cancels the vowel of the first. This is commonly done for
       <i>
        CyV
       </i>
       syllables called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Y%C5%8Don" title="Yōon">
         yōon
        </a>
       </i>
       , as in ひょ
       <i>
        hyo
       </i>
       ⟨hi
       <sub>
        yo
       </sub>
       ⟩. These are not digraphs, as they retain the normal sequential reading of the two glyphs. However, some obsolete sequences no longer retain that reading, as in くゎ
       <i>
        kwa,
       </i>
       ぐゎ
       <i>
        gwa,
       </i>
       and むゎ
       <i>
        mwa,
       </i>
       now pronounced
       <i>
        ka, ga, ma.
       </i>
       In addition, non-sequenceable digraphs are used for foreign loans that do not follow normal Japanese
       <a href="/wiki/Assibilation" title="Assibilation">
        assibilation
       </a>
       patterns, such as ティ
       <i>
        ti,
       </i>
       トゥ
       <i>
        tu,
       </i>
       チェ
       <i>
        tye / che,
       </i>
       スェ
       <i>
        swe,
       </i>
       ウィ
       <i>
        wi,
       </i>
       ツォ
       <i>
        tso,
       </i>
       ズィ
       <i>
        zi.
       </i>
       (See
       <a href="/wiki/Katakana" title="Katakana">
        Katakana
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Transcription_into_Japanese" title="Transcription into Japanese">
        Transcription into Japanese
       </a>
       for complete tables.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Long vowels are written by adding the kana for that vowel, in effect doubling it. However, long
       <i>
        ō
       </i>
       may be written either
       <i>
        oo
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        ou
       </i>
       , as in とうきょう
       <i>
        toukyou
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [toːkjoː]
       </span>
       'Tōkyō'. For dialects which do not distinguish
       <i>
        ē
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ei
       </i>
       , the latter spelling is used for a long
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       , as in へいせい
       <i>
        heisei
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [heːseː]
       </span>
       'Heisei'.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are several conventions of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Okinawan_writing_system" title="Okinawan writing system">
        Okinawan kana
       </a>
       which involve subscript digraphs or ligatures. For instance, in the University of the Ryukyus system, ウ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʔu/
       </span>
       , ヲ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /o/
       </span>
       , but ヲゥ is
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /u/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Korean">
        Korean
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Korean">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As was the case in Greek, Korean has vowels descended from diphthongs that are still written with two letters. These digraphs, ㅐ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɛ/
       </span>
       and ㅔ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /e/
       </span>
       (also ㅒ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jɛ/
       </span>
       , ㅖ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /je/
       </span>
       ), and in some dialects ㅚ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ø/
       </span>
       and ㅟ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /y/
       </span>
       , all end in historical ㅣ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Hangul" title="Hangul">
        Hangul
       </a>
       was designed with a digraph series to represent the "
       <a href="/wiki/Slack_voice" title="Slack voice">
        muddy
       </a>
       " consonants: ㅃ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[b]
       </span>
       , ㄸ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[d]
       </span>
       , ㅉ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[dz]
       </span>
       , ㄲ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[ɡ]
       </span>
       , ㅆ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[z]
       </span>
       , ㆅ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        *[ɣ]
       </span>
       ; also ᅇ, with an uncertain value. These values are now obsolete, but most of these doubled letters were resurrected in the 19th century to write consonants which had not existed when hangul was devised: ㅃ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /p͈/
       </span>
       , ㄸ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͈/
       </span>
       , ㅉ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t͈ɕ/
       </span>
       , ㄲ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /k͈/
       </span>
       , ㅆ
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s͈/
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_Unicode">
        In Unicode
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Digraph_(orthography)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: In Unicode">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Generally, a digraph is simply represented using two characters in
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, for various reasons, Unicode sometimes provides a separate
       <a href="/wiki/Code_point" title="Code point">
        code point
       </a>
       for a digraph, encoded as a single character.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Dz_(digraph)" title="Dz (digraph)">
        DZ
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/IJ_(digraph)" title="IJ (digraph)">
        IJ
       </a>
       digraphs and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaj%27s_Latin_Alphabet#Digraphs" title="Gaj's Latin Alphabet">
        Serbian/Croatian digraphs
       </a>
       DŽ, LJ, and NJ have separate code points in Unicode.
      </p>
