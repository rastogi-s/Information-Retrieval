      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Parmigiano dialect
       </b>
       , sometimes the
       <b>
        Parmesan dialect
       </b>
       , (or
       <i>
        al djalètt pramzàn
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Parma" title="Province of Parma">
        Province of Parma
       </a>
       , the western-central portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Emilia-Romagna" title="Emilia-Romagna">
        Emilia-Romagna
       </a>
       administrative region.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Terminology">
        Terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Although the term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
         dialect
        </a>
       </i>
       is commonly used in reference to all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minority_languages" title="Minority languages">
        minority languages
       </a>
       native to Italy, most of them are not
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Standard_Italian" title="Standard Italian">
        Standard Italian
       </a>
       and have developed independently from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       . Parmigiano is no exception and so is a dialect of Emiliano-Romagnolo, not Italian.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian language
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo">
        Emiliano-Romagnolo
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Parmigiano is a subdialect of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian
       </a>
       , which is itself a dialect the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emiliano-Romagnolo_language" title="Emiliano-Romagnolo language">
        Emiliano-Romagnolo language
       </a>
       , which is identified as "definitely endangered" by UNESCO.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UNESCO_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UNESCO-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is a high degree of
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutual intelligibility
       </a>
       among the various Emilian-Romagnol dialects. Emiliano-Romagnolo is part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic" title="Gallo-Italic">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       family, which also includes
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmontese_language" title="Piedmontese language">
        Piedmontese
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard language
       </a>
       . Ligurian, in particular, has influenced Parmigiano.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmigiano has much of the history as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian
       </a>
       , but at some point, it diverged from other versions of that linguistic group. It now lies somewhere between Western Emilian, which includes Piancentino, and Central Emilian, which includes Reggiano and Modenese. Like the other Emilian dialects, it has fewer speakers than ever because of political, social and economic factors, but
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Repubblica" title="La Repubblica">
         La Repubblica
        </a>
       </i>
       has suggested that it is changing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repubblica_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Repubblica-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is still declining but more slowly, as parents are keen to preserve their ancestral roots.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its origins are with
       <a href="/wiki/Gauls" title="Gauls">
        Gauls
       </a>
       , who occupied the Parma area in around 400
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AD" title="AD">
        AD
       </a>
       , who had stayed there after the invasion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Rome" title="Ancient Rome">
        Romans
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Lexicon" title="Lexicon">
        lexicon
       </a>
       was therefore a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       influenced by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaulish" title="Gaulish">
        Gaulish
       </a>
       . The Gauls, or
       <a href="/wiki/Celts" title="Celts">
        Celts
       </a>
       , left their mark on modern Parmigiano in some words today, such as
       <i>
        gozèn
       </i>
       "pig",
       <i>
        scrana
       </i>
       "chair" and
       <i>
        sôga
       </i>
       "rope". As a result of Spanish and especially French invasions, Parmigiani began to use words which came from a French language that had Latin roots. That is seen in
       <i>
        tirabusòn
       </i>
       "corkscrew" (similar to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_French" title="Modern French">
        Modern French
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        tire-bouchon
       </i>
       )
       <i>
        vert
       </i>
       "open" (French:
       <i>
        ouvert
       </i>
       ),
       <i>
        pòmmm da téra
       </i>
       "potato" (French:
       <i>
        pomme de terre
       </i>
       ) and many other words.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_distribution">
        Geographic distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geographic distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmigiano is mainly spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parma_(province)" title="Parma (province)">
        province of Parma
       </a>
       . The vocabulary and vowels vary across the region, particularly between the urban and rural dialects, as there was once little mobility from within to outside the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/City_walls" title="City walls">
        city walls
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repubblica_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Repubblica-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The dialect spoken outside Parma is often called Arioso or Parmense within the city itself, but variation is less pronounced than it once was. The dialect spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Casalmaggiore" title="Casalmaggiore">
        Casalmaggiore
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Cremona" title="Province of Cremona">
        Province of Cremona
       </a>
       to the north of Parma is closely related to Parmigiano. Parmigiano subdialects have three forms:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Low Parmigiano
        </b>
        , which is native to a northern part of the province that lies between the
        <a href="/wiki/Po_(river)" title="Po (river)">
         Po
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Via_Aemilia" title="Via Aemilia">
         Via Aemilia
        </a>
        and whose largest town is
        <a href="/wiki/Colorno" title="Colorno">
         Colorno
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Western Parmigiano
        </b>
        , which is heard around
        <a href="/wiki/Fidenza" title="Fidenza">
         Fidenza
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Salsomaggiore_Terme" title="Salsomaggiore Terme">
         Salsomaggiore Terme
        </a>
        and has been strongly influenced by Piacentino, another
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
         Emilian language
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         High Parmigiano
        </b>
        , which has been affected by
        <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
         Ligurian
        </a>
        and is spoken in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apennines" title="Apennines">
         Apennine
        </a>
        region to the south.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       An example of the variation is the word
       <i>
        bombèn
       </i>
       "very well". In 1861, the popular forms were
       <i>
        moltbein
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        monbén
       </i>
       , but it has also taken these forms:
       <i>
        montben
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        mondbén
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        moltbén
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        moltbein
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        monbén
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        mombén
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repubblica_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Repubblica-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Official_status">
        Official status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Official status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Parmigiano is not recognised as a minority dialect in the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       or in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ; the same is true for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian_language" title="Emilian language">
        Emilian
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emilian-Romagnol" title="Emilian-Romagnol">
        Emilian-Romagnol
       </a>
       . Since 27 June 2000, Italy has been a signatory of the
       <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Europe" title="Council of Europe">
        Council of Europe
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/European_Charter_for_Regional_or_Minority_Languages" title="European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages">
        European Charter for Regional or Minority Languages
       </a>
       , which aims to protect and promote historical
       <a href="/wiki/Regional_language" title="Regional language">
        regional
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Minority_language" title="Minority language">
        minority languages
       </a>
       in Europe, but it has not ratified it.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_system">
        Writing system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Parmigiano is written using the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin alphabet
       </a>
       , but spelling can vary within a dialect. It has never been standardised, and the language is rarely written.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Repubblica_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Repubblica-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Still, a number of Parmigani-Italian dictionaries have been published.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Angelo Mazza and
       <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">
        translator
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Clemente_Bondi" title="Clemente Bondi">
        Clemente Bondi
       </a>
       were prolific writers of poetry in Parmigiano. Most of the works were first published in the late 1700s or the early to mid-1800s.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grammar">
        Grammar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Grammar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Parmigiano is a
       <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_language" title="Synthetic language">
        synthetic language
       </a>
       like
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       (but has much less so than
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Latin" title="Classical Latin">
        Classical Latin
       </a>
       ) and shares several notable features with most other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        loss of Latin's
        <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
         declension
        </a>
        and the neuter
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
         grammatical gender
        </a>
        for nouns
       </li>
       <li>
        development of grammatical
        <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
         articles
        </a>
        from Latin
        <a href="/wiki/Demonstrative" title="Demonstrative">
         demonstratives
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        new
        <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
         tenses
        </a>
        formed from auxiliaries
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        Nouns
       </a>
       and most pronouns are inflected for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_number" title="Grammatical number">
        number
       </a>
       (singular or plural);
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjectives
       </a>
       , for the number and gender (masculine or feminine) of their nouns;
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        personal pronouns
       </a>
       , for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_person" title="Grammatical person">
        person
       </a>
       , number, gender, and
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_case" title="Grammatical case">
        case
       </a>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verbs
       </a>
       , for
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_mood" title="Grammatical mood">
        mood
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_tense" title="Grammatical tense">
        tense
       </a>
       , and the person and number of their
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subjects
       </a>
       . Case is primarily marked using
       <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">
        word order
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        prepositions
       </a>
       , and certain verb features are marked using
       <a href="/wiki/Auxiliary_verb" title="Auxiliary verb">
        auxiliary verbs
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Negation">
        Negation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parmigiano_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Negation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Parmigiano expresses negation in two parts, with the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_particle" title="Grammatical particle">
        particle
       </a>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       attached to the verb and one or more negative words (
       <a href="/wiki/Connegative" title="Connegative">
        connegatives
       </a>
       ) that modify the verb or one of its
       <a href="/wiki/Argument" title="Argument">
        arguments
       </a>
       . Negation encircles a conjugated verb with
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       after the subject and the negative adverb after the conjugated verb, For example, the simple verbal negation is expressed by
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       before the finite verb (and any object pronouns) and the adverb
       <i>
        brisa
       </i>
       ['briza] after the finite verb. That is a feature it has in common with
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       , which uses
       <i>
        ne
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        pas
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Pas
       </i>
       derives from the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        passus
       </i>
       "step", and
       <i>
        brisa
       </i>
       "crumb" also derives from a small quantity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
