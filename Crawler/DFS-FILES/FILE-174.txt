      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Tropic of Cancer
       </b>
       , also referred to as the
       <b>
        Northern Tropic
       </b>
       , is currently 23°26′13.0″ (or 23.43694°) north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       . It is the most northerly circle of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       at which the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       can be
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        directly overhead
       </a>
       . This occurs on the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       , when the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       is tilted toward the Sun to its maximum extent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Its
       <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
        Southern Hemisphere
       </a>
       counterpart, marking the most southerly position at which the Sun can be directly overhead, is the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       . These tropics are two of the five major
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        circles of latitude
       </a>
       that mark maps of Earth; which also includes the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Circle" title="Arctic Circle">
        Arctic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">
        Antarctic Circles
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       . The positions of these two circles of latitude (relative to the Equator) are dictated by the tilt of Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis" title="Rotation around a fixed axis">
        axis of rotation
       </a>
       relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        plane of its orbit
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropic_of_Cancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When this line of latitude was named some 2,000 years ago, the Sun was in the
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellation
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cancer_(constellation)" title="Cancer (constellation)">
        Cancer
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Crab" title="Crab">
         crab
        </a>
       </i>
       ) at the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       , the time each year that the Sun reaches its zenith at this latitude. Due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">
        precession of the equinoxes
       </a>
       , this is no longer the case; today the Sun is in
       <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">
        Taurus
       </a>
       at the June solstice. The word "tropic" itself comes from the Greek "trope (τροπή)", meaning turn (change of direction, or circumstances), inclination, referring to the fact that the Sun appears to "turn back" at the solstices.
      </p>

      <p>
       The Tropic of Cancer's position is not fixed, but varies in a complicated manner over time. Currently, it gradually drifts south almost half an arcsecond (0.468″) of latitude, or 15 metres, per year (it was at exactly 23° 27′ in 1917 and will be at 23° 26' in 2045).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude#Movement_of_the_Tropical_and_Polar_circles" title="Circle of latitude">
        circles of latitude
       </a>
       for further information.
      </p>
      <p>
       North of the tropic are the
       <a href="/wiki/Subtropics" title="Subtropics">
        subtropics
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperate_climate" title="Temperate climate">
        North Temperate Zone
       </a>
       . The equivalent line of latitude south of the Equator is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn" title="Tropic of Capricorn">
        Tropic of Capricorn
       </a>
       , and the region between the two, centered on the Equator, is the
       <a href="/wiki/Tropics" title="Tropics">
        tropics
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropic_of_Cancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="186" data-file-width="512" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/400px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/600px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/800px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Relationship between Earth's axial tilt (ε) to the tropical and polar circles
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Excluding cooler highland regions in China, the climate at the Tropic of Cancer is generally hot and dry except for easterly coastal areas where
       <a href="/wiki/Precipitation_types#Orographic" title="Precipitation types">
        orographic rainfall
       </a>
       can be very heavy, in some places reaching 4 metres (160 in). Most regions on the Tropic of Cancer experience two distinct seasons: an extremely hot summer with temperatures often reaching 45 °C (113 °F) and a warm winter with maxima around 22 °C (72 °F). Much land on or near the Tropic of Cancer is part of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sahara_Desert" title="Sahara Desert">
        Sahara Desert
       </a>
       , whilst to the east the climate is torrid
       <a href="/wiki/Monsoon" title="Monsoon">
        monsoonal
       </a>
       with a short wet season from June to September and very little rainfall for the rest of the year.
      </p>
      <p>
       The highest mountain on or adjacent to the Tropic of Cancer is
       <a href="/wiki/Yushan_(mountain)" title="Yushan (mountain)">
        Yushan
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Taiwan" title="Taiwan">
        Taiwan
       </a>
       ; though it had glaciers descending as low as 2,800 metres (9,190 ft) during the
       <a href="/wiki/Last_Glacial_Maximum" title="Last Glacial Maximum">
        Last Glacial Maximum
       </a>
       , none survive and at present no glaciers exist within 470 kilometres (290 mi) of the Tropic of Cancer; the nearest currently surviving are the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Minyong_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Minyong Glacier (page does not exist)">
        Minyong
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Baishui_Glacier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Baishui Glacier (page does not exist)">
        Baishui
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Himalayas" title="Himalayas">
        Himalayas
       </a>
       to the north and on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iztacc%C3%ADhuatl" title="Iztaccíhuatl">
        Iztaccíhuatl
       </a>
       to the south.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Circumnavigation">
        Circumnavigation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tropic_of_Cancer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Circumnavigation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to the rules of the
       <a href="/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale" title="Fédération Aéronautique Internationale">
        Fédération Aéronautique Internationale
       </a>
       , for a flight to compete for a round-the-world speed record, it must cover a distance no less than the length of the Tropic of Cancer, cross all
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
        meridians
       </a>
       , and end on the same airfield where it started.
      </p>
      <p>
       Length of the Tropic on 11 December 2015, at 23°26′14″N is
       <b>
        36,788 kilometres (22,859 mi)
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For an ordinary
       <a href="/wiki/Circumnavigation" title="Circumnavigation">
        circumnavigation
       </a>
       the rules are somewhat relaxed and the distance is set to a rounded value of at least 36,770 kilometres (22,850 mi).
      </p>
