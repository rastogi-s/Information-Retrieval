      <p>
       A number of
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole languages
       </a>
       are based on the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Palenquero" title="Palenquero">
        Palenquero
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Bozal_Spanish" title="Bozal Spanish">
        Bozal Spanish
       </a>
       . Spanish also influenced other creole languages like
       <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pichinglis" title="Pichinglis">
        Pichinglis
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Annobonese_Creole" title="Annobonese Creole">
        Annobonese
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Chavacano">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
         Chavacano
        </a>
       </b>
       (also Chabacano) refers to a number of Spanish-based creole language varieties spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       . Linguists have identified at least six varieties of these Spanish creole languages: Zamboangueño, Caviteño, Ternateño, and Ermitaño. The variety found in Zamboanga City has the most number of speakers and is considered to be the most stable while the other varieties are considered to be either endangered or extinct (i.e. Ermitaño)
      </p>
      <p>
       Chavacano are spoken in Cavite City and Ternate (both on Luzon); Zamboanga, Cotabato and Davao (on Mindanao),
       <a href="/wiki/Isabela,_Basilan" title="Isabela, Basilan">
        Isabela City
       </a>
       and other parts of Province of Basilan and elsewhere. According to a 2007
       <a href="/wiki/Census" title="Census">
        census
       </a>
       , there are 2,502,185 speakers in the Philippines. It is the major language of
       <a href="/wiki/Zamboanga_City" title="Zamboanga City">
        Zamboanga City
       </a>
       . Chavacano is also spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Cavite_City" title="Cavite City">
        Cavite City
       </a>
       and in parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Ternate,_Cavite" title="Ternate, Cavite">
        Ternate, Cavite
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sabah" title="Sabah">
        Sabah
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Malaysia" title="Malaysia">
        Malaysia
       </a>
       nearest to the Philippines, and even in Brunei and Latin America, because of recent migrations.
      </p>
      <p>
       The different varieties of chavacano are mostly intelligible to one another but differ slightly in certain aspects such as in the usage of certain words. Most of the vocabulary comes from
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       , while the
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       is mostly based on the Austronesian structure. It is used in
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_education" title="Primary education">
        primary education
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
        television
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       . Recently English words have been infiltrating the language and
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code-switching
       </a>
       is common among younger speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       The name of the language stems from the Spanish word
       <i>
        Chabacano
       </i>
       which roughly means "tasteless", "common", or "vulgar",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       this Spanish word, however, has lost its original meaning and carries no negative connotation among contemporary speakers.
      </p>
      <p>
       For more information see the article on
       <a href="/wiki/Chavacano" title="Chavacano">
        Chavacano
       </a>
       , or the
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue Report
       </a>
       on Chavacano.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethn_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ethn-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Palenquero">
        Palenquero
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Palenquero">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Palenquero1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="323" data-file-width="278" height="232" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Palenquero1.jpg/200px-Palenquero1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2b/Palenquero1.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Palenquero1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Palenquero
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Palenquero" title="Palenquero">
         Palenquero
        </a>
       </b>
       (also
       <a href="/wiki/Palenque" title="Palenque">
        Palenque
       </a>
       ) is a Spanish-based creole spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The ethnic group which speaks this creole consisted only of 2,500 people in 1989.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Colombia" title="Colombia">
        Colombia
       </a>
       , in the village of
       <a href="/wiki/San_Basilio_de_Palenque" title="San Basilio de Palenque">
        San Basilio de Palenque
       </a>
       which is south and east of
       <a href="/wiki/Cartagena,_Colombia" title="Cartagena, Colombia">
        Cartagena
       </a>
       , and in some neighborhoods of
       <a href="/wiki/Barranquilla" title="Barranquilla">
        Barranquilla
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The village was founded by fugitive slaves (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maroon_(slavery)" title="Maroon (slavery)">
        Maroons
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">
        Native Americans
       </a>
       . Since many slaves had been only slightly exposed to contact with
       <a href="/wiki/White_people" title="White people">
        white people
       </a>
       , the
       <i>
        palenqueros
       </i>
       spoke creole languages derived from Spanish and from their ancestral African languages.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish speakers are unable to understand Palenquero. There is some influence from the
       <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">
        Kongo
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of Congo">
        Democratic Republic of Congo
       </a>
       . In 1998, only 10% of the population younger than 25 spoke Palenquero. It is most commonly spoken by the elderly.
      </p>
      <p>
       For more information see the
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue Report
       </a>
       on Palenquero.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bozal_Spanish">
        Bozal Spanish
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Bozal Spanish">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Bozal_Spanish" title="Bozal Spanish">
        Bozal Spanish
       </a>
       is a possibly extinct Spanish-based creole language that may have been a mixture of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kongo_language" title="Kongo language">
        Congolese
       </a>
       , with
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       influences.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clements_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-clements-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Attestation is insufficient to indicate whether Bozal Spanish was ever a single, coherent or stable language, or if the term merely referred to any idiolect of Spanish that included African elements.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bozal Spanish was spoken by
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_slave_trade" title="Atlantic slave trade">
        African slaves
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clements_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-clements-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and other areas of
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       from the 17th century up until its possible extinction at around 1850.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lipski_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lipski-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Esmeralde.C3.B1o-Chota_Creole">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Esmeraldeño-Chota_Creole">
        Esmeraldeño-Chota Creole
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Esmeraldeño-Chota Creole">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Esmeraldeño-Chota Creole is a pidgin Spanish spoken by some of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Afro_Ecuadorian" title="Afro Ecuadorian">
        Afro Ecuadorian
       </a>
       populations in the
       <a href="/wiki/Esmeraldas_Province" title="Esmeraldas Province">
        Esmeraldas Province
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carchi_Province" title="Carchi Province">
        Carchi Province
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Imbabura_Province" title="Imbabura Province">
        Imbabura Province
       </a>
       the language could be classified as just a dialect of Spanish, but has some English influence from escaped slaves from the Caribbean. The language was developed by escaped slaves from the Colombian coast and the Caribbean also from slaves brought to the region and immigrants from the Caribbean that settled on the northern Ecuadorian coast. Because of the think jungles of Esmeraldas and the high mountains that surround the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Chota_valley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Chota valley (page does not exist)">
        Chota valley
       </a>
       the language was able to obtain
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Quechua" title="Quechua">
        Quechua
       </a>
       influences and keep their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Niger-Congo" title="Niger-Congo">
        Niger-Congo
       </a>
       influences. Today it is spoken by nearly 250,000 people in northwestern Ecuador
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Annobonese">
        Annobonese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Annobonese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annobonese_language" title="Annobonese language">
         Annobonese language
        </a>
       </b>
       , locally called
       <b>
        Fa d'Ambö
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        Fa d'Ambu
       </b>
       or even
       <b>
        Fá d'Ambô
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_creole" title="Portuguese creole">
        Portuguese-based creole
       </a>
       , similar to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Forro_language" title="Forro language">
        Forro
       </a>
       , with some borrowings from Spanish. It is spoken by 9,000 people on the islands of
       <a href="/wiki/Annob%C3%B3n" title="Annobón">
        Ano Bom
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bioko" title="Bioko">
        Bioko
       </a>
       , in
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       . In fact, Fa d'Ambu shares the same structure of Forro (82% of lexicon).
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 15th century, the island was uninhabited and discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       but, by the 18th century, Portugal exchanged it and some other territories in Africa for Uruguay with
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       . Spain wanted to get territory in Africa, and Portugal wanted to enlarge even more the territory that they saw as the "New Portugal" (Brazil). Nevertheless, the populace of Ano Bom was against the shift and was hostile toward the Spaniards. This hostility, combined with their isolation from mainland Equatorial Guinea and their proximity to
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">
        São Tomé and Príncipe
       </a>
       —just 400 km from the island—has assured the maintenance of its identity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Fa d'Ambu has gained some words of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       origin (10% of lexicon), but some words are dubious in origin because Spanish and Portuguese are closely related languages.
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_Equatorial_Guinea" title="History of Equatorial Guinea">
        History of Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Papiamento">
        Papiamento
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Papiamento">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Papiamento" title="Papiamento">
         Papiamento
        </a>
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese-based_creole_languages" title="Portuguese-based creole languages">
        Portuguese-based creole language
       </a>
       spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dutch_West_Indies" title="Dutch West Indies">
        Dutch West Indies
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with some influences from
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_languages_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous languages of the Americas">
        Indigenous American languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">
        Dutch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . Primarily spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Cura%C3%A7ao" title="Curaçao">
        Curaçao
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bonaire" title="Bonaire">
        Bonaire
       </a>
       by 179,000 people in 1998 and
       <a href="/wiki/Aruba" title="Aruba">
        Aruba
       </a>
       by 100,000 people in 2004.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . Today, Spanish influence on the language is strong, but, due to the similarities between the languages, it is difficult to ascertain whether a certain feature is derived from Portuguese or Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pichinglis">
        Pichinglis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spanish-based_creole_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Pichinglis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pichinglis" title="Pichinglis">
        Pichinglis
       </a>
       is spoken on Bioko island, Equatorial Guinea. It originated with the arrival of
       <a href="/wiki/Krio_language" title="Krio language">
        Krio
       </a>
       speakers from the mainland. Krio is a creole that derives
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/English-based_creole" title="English-based creole">
        most of its vocabulary from English
       </a>
       , but the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Guinea" title="Spanish Guinea">
        Spanish colonization of Guinea
       </a>
       exerted Spanish influence on its lexicon and grammar.
      </p>
