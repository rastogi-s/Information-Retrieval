         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       An
       <b>
        Earth ellipsoid
       </b>
       is a mathematical figure approximating the
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        shape of the Earth
       </a>
       , used as a
       <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">
        reference frame
       </a>
       for computations in geodesy, astronomy and the geosciences. Various different ellipsoids have been used as approximations.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ellipsoid_of_revolution" title="Ellipsoid of revolution">
        ellipsoid of revolution
       </a>
       , whose short (polar) axis (connecting the two flattest spots called geographical north and south poles) is approximately aligned with the rotation axis of the Earth. The ellipsoid is defined by the equatorial axis
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       and the polar axis
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       ; their difference is about 21 km or 0.335 percent. Additional parameters are the
       <a href="/wiki/Probability_mass_function" title="Probability mass function">
        mass function
       </a>
       <i>
        J2
       </i>
       , the correspondent
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       formula, and the rotation period (usually 86164 seconds).
      </p>
      <p>
       Many methods exist for determination of the axes of an Earth ellipsoid, ranging from
       <a href="/wiki/Meridian_arc" title="Meridian arc">
        meridian arcs
       </a>
       up to modern
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_geodesy" title="Satellite geodesy">
        satellite geodesy
       </a>
       or the analysis and interconnection of continental
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_network" title="Geodetic network">
        geodetic networks
       </a>
       . Amongst the different set of data used in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_survey" title="National survey">
        national surveys
       </a>
       are several of special importance: the
       <a href="/wiki/Bessel_ellipsoid" title="Bessel ellipsoid">
        Bessel ellipsoid
       </a>
       of 1841, the international
       <a href="/wiki/Hayford_ellipsoid" title="Hayford ellipsoid">
        Hayford ellipsoid
       </a>
       of 1924, and (for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       positioning) the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS84" title="WGS84">
        WGS84
       </a>
       ellipsoid.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mean_Earth_ellipsoid_and_reference_ellipsoids">
        <i>
         Mean
        </i>
        Earth ellipsoid and
        <i>
         reference
        </i>
        ellipsoids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mean Earth ellipsoid and reference ellipsoids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        Reference ellipsoid
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A data set which describes the global
       <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">
        average
       </a>
       of the Earth's surface curvature is called the
       <i>
        mean
       </i>
       Earth Ellipsoid. It refers to a theoretical coherence between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_latitude" title="Geographic latitude">
        geographic latitude
       </a>
       and the meridional curvature of the
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       . The latter is close to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
        mean sea level
       </a>
       , and therefore an ideal Earth ellipsoid has the same
       <a href="/wiki/Volume" title="Volume">
        volume
       </a>
       as the geoid.
      </p>
      <p>
       While the
       <i>
        mean
       </i>
       Earth ellipsoid is the ideal basis of global geodesy, for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Regional" title="Regional">
        regional
       </a>
       networks a so-called
       <i>
        reference ellipsoid
       </i>
       may be the better choice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When geodetic measurements have to be computed on a mathematical reference surface, this surface should have a similar curvature as the regional geoid - otherwise,
       <a href="/wiki/Reduction_(mathematics)" title="Reduction (mathematics)">
        reduction
       </a>
       of the measurements will get small distortions.
      </p>
      <p>
       This is the reason for the "long life" of former reference ellipsoids like the
       <a href="/wiki/Hayford_ellipsoid" title="Hayford ellipsoid">
        Hayford
       </a>
       or the
       <a href="/wiki/Bessel_ellipsoid" title="Bessel ellipsoid">
        Bessel ellipsoid
       </a>
       , despite the fact that their main axes deviate by several hundred meters from the modern values. Another reason is a judicial one: the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coordinate" title="Coordinate">
        coordinates
       </a>
       of millions of boundary stones should remain fixed for a long period. If their reference surface changes, the coordinates themselves also change.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, for
       <a href="/wiki/International" title="International">
        international
       </a>
       networks,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       positioning, or
       <a href="/wiki/Astronautics" title="Astronautics">
        astronautics
       </a>
       , these regional reasons are less relevant. As knowledge of
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        the Earth's figure
       </a>
       is increasingly accurate, the International Geoscientific Union
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/IUGG" title="IUGG">
        IUGG
       </a>
       usually adapts the axes of the Earth ellipsoid to the best available data.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_method_of_determining_the_ellipsoid">
        Historical method of determining the ellipsoid
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Earth_ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Historical method of determining the ellipsoid">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       High precision land surveys can be used to determine the distance between two places at nearly the same longitude by measuring a base line and a chain of triangles. (Suitable stations for the end points are rarely at the same longitude). The distance Δ along the meridian from one end point to a point at the same latitude as the second end point is then calculated by trigonometry. The surface distance Δ is reduced to Δ', the corresponding distance at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_sea_level" title="Mean sea level">
        mean sea level
       </a>
       . The intermediate distances to points on the meridian at the same latitudes as other stations of the survey may also be calculated.
      </p>
      <p>
       The geographic latitudes of both end points, φ
       <sub>
        s
       </sub>
       (standpoint) and φ
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       (forepoint) and possibly at other points are determined by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrogeodesy" title="Astrogeodesy">
        astrogeodesy
       </a>
       , observing the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zenith_distance" title="Zenith distance">
        zenith distances
       </a>
       of sufficient numbers of
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       . If latitudes are measured at end points only, the radius of curvature at the midpoint of the meridian arc can be calculated from R = Δ'/(|φ
       <sub>
        s
       </sub>
       -φ
       <sub>
        f
       </sub>
       |). A second meridian arc will allow the derivation of two parameters required to specify a
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid" title="Reference ellipsoid">
        reference ellipsoid
       </a>
       . Longer arcs with intermediate latitude determinations can completely determine the ellipsoid. In practice multiple arc measurements are used to determine the ellipsoid parameters by the method of least squares. The parameters determined are usually the semi-major axis,

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Earth_ellipsoids">
        Historical Earth ellipsoids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

      </h2>
      <p>
       The reference ellipsoid models listed below have had utility in geodetic work and many are still in use. The older ellipsoids are named for the individual who derived them and the year of development is given. In 1887 the English surveyor Colonel
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ross_Clarke" title="Alexander Ross Clarke">
        Alexander Ross Clarke
       </a>
       CB FRS RE was awarded the Gold Medal of the Royal Society for his work in determining the figure of the Earth. The international ellipsoid was developed by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Fillmore_Hayford" title="John Fillmore Hayford">
        John Fillmore Hayford
       </a>
       in 1910 and adopted by the International Union of Geodesy and Geophysics (IUGG) in 1924, which recommended it for international use.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the 1967 meeting of the IUGG held in Lucerne, Switzerland, the ellipsoid called GRS-67 (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_system" title="Geodetic system">
        Geodetic Reference System
       </a>
       1967) in the listing was recommended for adoption. The new ellipsoid was not recommended to replace the International Ellipsoid (1924), but was advocated for use where a greater degree of accuracy is required. It became a part of the GRS-67 which was approved and adopted at the 1971 meeting of the IUGG held in Moscow. It is used in Australia for the Australian Geodetic Datum and in South America for the South American Datum 1969.
      </p>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        geodetic datum
       </a>
       . For example, the older ED-50 (
       <a href="/wiki/ED50" title="ED50">
        European Datum 1950
       </a>
       ) is based on the Hayford or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/International_Ellipsoid" title="International Ellipsoid">
        International Ellipsoid
       </a>
       . WGS-84 is peculiar in that the same name is used for both the complete geodetic reference system and its component ellipsoidal model. Nevertheless, the two concepts—ellipsoidal model and geodetic reference system—remain distinct.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that the same ellipsoid may be known by different names. It is best to mention the defining constants for unambiguous identification.
      </p>
