      <p>
       Traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       defines the
       <b>
        object
       </b>
       in a sentence as the entity that is acted upon by the subject.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is thus a primary distinction between subjects and objects that is understood in terms of the action expressed by the verb, e.g.
       <i>
        Tom studies grammar
       </i>
       —
       <i>
        Tom
       </i>
       is the subject and
       <i>
        grammar
       </i>
       is the object. Traditional theories of sentence structure divide the simple sentence into a
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Predicate_(grammar)" title="Predicate (grammar)">
        predicate
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereby the object is taken to be part of the predicate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many modern theories of grammar (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammars
       </a>
       ), in contrast, take the object to be a verb
       <a href="/wiki/Argument_(linguistics)" title="Argument (linguistics)">
        argument
       </a>
       like the subject, the difference between them being mainly just their prominence; the subject is ranked higher than the object and is thus more prominent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The main verb in a clause determines whether and what objects are present.
       <a href="/wiki/Transitive_verb" title="Transitive verb">
        Transitive verbs
       </a>
       require the presence of an object, whereas
       <a href="/wiki/Intransitive_verb" title="Intransitive verb">
        intransitive verbs
       </a>
       block the appearance of an object.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">
         complement
        </a>
       </i>
       overlaps in meaning with
       <i>
        object
       </i>
       : all objects are complements, but not vice versa. The objects that verbs do and do not take is explored in detail in
       <a href="/wiki/Valency_(linguistics)" title="Valency (linguistics)">
        valency
       </a>
       theory.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_objects">
        Types of objects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Object_(grammar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Types of objects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Various object types are commonly acknowledged:
       <i>
        direct
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        indirect
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        prepositional
       </i>
       . These object types are illustrated in the following table:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <dl>
             <dd>
              <dl>
               <dd>
                <table class="wikitable">
                 <tr>
                  <th>
                   Type
                  </th>
                  <th>
                   Description
                  </th>
                  <th>
                   Example
                  </th>
                 </tr>
                 <tr>
                  <td>
                   Direct object
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   Entity acted upon
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   Sam fed
                   <b>
                    the dogs
                   </b>
                   .
                  </td>
                 </tr>
                 <tr>
                  <td>
                   Indirect object
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   Entity indirectly affected by the action
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   She sent
                   <b>
                    him
                   </b>
                   a present.
                  </td>
                 </tr>
                 <tr>
                  <td>
                   Prepositional object
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   Object introduced by a preposition
                  </td>
                  <td>
                   She is waiting
                   <b>
                    for Lucy
                   </b>
                   .
                  </td>
                 </tr>
                </table>
               </dd>
              </dl>
             </dd>
            </dl>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The descriptions "entity acted upon" and "entity indirectly affected by the action" are merely loose orientation points. Beyond basic examples such as those provided in the table, these orientation points are not much help when the goal is to determine whether a given object should be viewed as direct or indirect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One rule of thumb for English, however, is that an indirect object is not present unless a direct object is also present. A
       <i>
        prepositional object
       </i>
       is one that is introduced by a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       . Despite the difficulties with the traditional nomenclature, the terms
       <i>
        direct object
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        indirect object
       </i>
       are widespread.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oblique_object&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oblique object (page does not exist)">
         oblique object
        </a>
       </i>
       is also employed at times, although what exactly is meant varies from author to author. Some understand it to be an umbrella term denoting all objects (direct, indirect, and prepositional), whereas others use the term to denote just a prepositional object.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_language" title="Chinese language">
        Chinese
       </a>
       verbs can have two direct objects, one being more closely bound to the verb than the other; these may be called
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_grammar#Objects" title="Chinese grammar">
        "inner" and "outer" objects
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Identifying_objects">
        Identifying objects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Object_(grammar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Identifying objects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A number of criteria can be employed for identifying objects, e.g:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          1. Subject of passive sentence: Most objects in active sentences can become the subject in the corresponding passive sentences.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
           <a href="#cite_note-9">
            [9]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          2. Position occupied: In languages with strict
          <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">
           word order
          </a>
          , the subject and the object tend to occupy set positions in unmarked declarative clauses. The object follows the subject.
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          3. Morphological case: In languages that have case systems, objects are marked by certain cases (accusative, dative, genitive, instrumental, etc.).
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       Languages vary significantly with respect to these criteria. The first criterion identifies objects reliably most of the time in English, e.g.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          Fred gave me a book.
         </dd>
         <dd>
          a. A book was given (to) me.
          <small>
           —Passive sentence identifies
           <i>
            a book
           </i>
           as an object in the starting sentence.
          </small>
         </dd>
         <dd>
          b. I was given a book.
          <small>
           —Passive sentence identifies
           <i>
            me
           </i>
           as an object in the starting sentence.
          </small>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The second criterion is also a reliable criterion for
       <a href="/wiki/Isolating_language" title="Isolating language">
        isolating languages
       </a>
       such as English, since the relatively strict word order of English usually positions the object after the verb(s) in declarative sentences. The third criterion is less applicable to English, though, since English lacks morphological case, exceptions being the personal pronouns (
       <i>
        I/me
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        we/us
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        he/him
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        she/her
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        they/them
       </i>
       ). For languages that have case and thus freer word order, morphological case is the most readily available criterion for identifying objects. In Latin and related languages, direct objects are usually marked with the accusative case, and indirect objects with the dative case. Note as well that some objects are marked in telling ways in particular languages. In Spanish, for example, human objects have to be marked by the preposition
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ; the phenomenon is called
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_object_marking" title="Differential object marking">
        differential object marking
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Objects_in_sentence_structure">
        Objects in sentence structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Object_(grammar)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Objects in sentence structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Objects are distinguished from subjects in the syntactic trees that represent sentence structure. The subject appears (as high or) higher in the syntactic structure than the object. The following trees of a
       <a href="/wiki/Dependency_grammar" title="Dependency grammar">
        dependency grammar
       </a>
       illustrate the hierarchical positions of subjects and objects:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Grammatical_objects.svg" title="Grammatical objects">
           <img alt="Grammatical objects" data-file-height="355" data-file-width="640" height="355" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Grammatical_objects.svg/640px-Grammatical_objects.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Grammatical_objects.svg/960px-Grammatical_objects.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Grammatical_objects.svg/1280px-Grammatical_objects.svg.png 2x" width="640"/>
          </a>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The subject is in blue, and the object in orange. The subject is consistently a dependent of the
       <a href="/wiki/Finite_verb" title="Finite verb">
        finite verb
       </a>
       , whereas the object is a dependent of the lowest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Non-finite_verb" title="Non-finite verb">
        non-finite verb
       </a>
       if such a verb is present.
      </p>
