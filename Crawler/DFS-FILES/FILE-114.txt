      <p>
       <b>
        Continental drift
       </b>
       is the movement of the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continents
       </a>
       relative to each other, thus appearing to "drift" across the ocean bed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usghistory_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-usghistory-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The speculation that continents might have 'drifted' was first put forward by
       <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ortelius" title="Abraham Ortelius">
        Abraham Ortelius
       </a>
       in 1596. The concept was independently and more fully developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">
        Alfred Wegener
       </a>
       in 1912, but his theory was rejected by some for lack of a mechanism (though this was supplied later by
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmes" title="Arthur Holmes">
        Arthur Holmes
       </a>
       ). The idea of continental drift has been subsumed by the theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       , which explains how the continents move.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_the_development_of_tectonophysics_(before_1954)" title="Timeline of the development of tectonophysics (before 1954)">
        Timeline of the development of tectonophysics (before 1954)
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_history">
        Early history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Continental_drift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Ortelius" title="Abraham Ortelius">
        Abraham Ortelius
       </a>
       (
       <a href="#CITEREFOrtelius1596">
        Ortelius 1596
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Theodor Christoph Lilienthal (1756),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmeling2004_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-schmeling2004-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt" title="Alexander von Humboldt">
        Alexander von Humboldt
       </a>
       (1801 and 1845),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schmeling2004_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-schmeling2004-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Snider-Pellegrini" title="Antonio Snider-Pellegrini">
        Antonio Snider-Pellegrini
       </a>
       (
       <a href="#CITEREFSnider-Pellegrini1858">
        Snider-Pellegrini 1858
       </a>
       ), and others had noted earlier that the shapes of
       <a href="/wiki/Continent" title="Continent">
        continents
       </a>
       on opposite sides of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       (most notably, Africa and South America) seem to fit together.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       W. J. Kious described Ortelius' thoughts in this way:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Abraham Ortelius in his work Thesaurus Geographicus ... suggested that the Americas were "torn away from Europe and Africa ... by earthquakes and floods" and went on to say: "The vestiges of the rupture reveal themselves, if someone brings forward a map of the world and considers carefully the coasts of the three [continents]."
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Writing in 1889,
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Russel_Wallace" title="Alfred Russel Wallace">
        Alfred Russel Wallace
       </a>
       remarks, "It was formerly a very general belief, even amongst geologists, that the great features of the earth's surface, no less than the smaller ones, were subject to continual mutations, and that during the course of known geological time the continents and great oceans had again and again changed places with each other."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He quotes
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lyell" title="Charles Lyell">
        Charles Lyell
       </a>
       as saying, "Continents, therefore, although permanent for whole geological epochs, shift their positions entirely in the course of ages."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and claims that the first to throw doubt on this was
       <a href="/wiki/James_Dwight_Dana" title="James Dwight Dana">
        James Dwight Dana
       </a>
       in 1849.
      </p>
      <p>
       In his
       <i>
        Manual of Geology
       </i>
       , 1863, Dana says, "The continents and oceans had their general outline or form defined in earliest time. This has been proved with respect to North America from the position and distribution of the first beds of the Silurian – those of the Potsdam epoch. … and this will probably prove to the case in Primordial time with the other continents also".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Dana was enormously influential in America – his
       <i>
        Manual of Mineralogy
       </i>
       is still in print in revised form – and the theory became known as
       <i>
        Permanence theory
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This appeared to be confirmed by the exploration of the deep sea beds conducted by the
       <a href="/wiki/Challenger_expedition" title="Challenger expedition">
        Challenger expedition
       </a>
       , 1872-6, which showed that contrary to expectation, land debris brought down by rivers to the ocean is deposited comparatively close to the shore on what is now known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_shelf" title="Continental shelf">
        continental shelf
       </a>
       . This suggested that the oceans were a permanent feature of the Earth's surface, and did not change places
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (April 2017)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       with the continents.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wegener_and_his_predecessors">
        Wegener and his predecessors
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Continental_drift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Wegener and his predecessors">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="765" data-file-width="496" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg/170px-Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg/255px-Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg/340px-Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Alfred_Wegener_1910.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Alfred Wegener
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The idea that the American continents had once formed a single landmass together with Europe and Asia before assuming their present shapes and positions was speculated by several scientists before
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Wegener" title="Alfred Wegener">
        Alfred Wegener
       </a>
       's 1912 paper.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weg_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-weg-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although Wegener's theory was formed independently and was more complete than those of his predecessors, Wegener later credited a number of past authors with similar ideas:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wegb_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wegb-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Franklin Coxworthy (between 1848 and 1890),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Mantovani" title="Roberto Mantovani">
        Roberto Mantovani
       </a>
       (between 1889 and 1909),
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">
        William Henry Pickering
       </a>
       (1907)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bursley_Taylor" title="Frank Bursley Taylor">
        Frank Bursley Taylor
       </a>
       (1908).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition,
       <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Suess" title="Eduard Suess">
        Eduard Suess
       </a>
       had proposed a supercontinent
       <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">
        Gondwana
       </a>
       in 1885
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_Ocean" title="Tethys Ocean">
        Tethys Ocean
       </a>
       in 1893,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       assuming a
       <a href="/wiki/Land_bridge#Land_bridge_theory" title="Land bridge">
        land-bridge
       </a>
       between the present continents submerged in the form of a
       <a href="/wiki/Geosyncline" title="Geosyncline">
        geosyncline
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/John_Perry_(engineer)" title="John Perry (engineer)">
        John Perry
       </a>
       had written an 1895 paper proposing that the earth's interior was fluid, and disagreeing with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Kelvin" title="Lord Kelvin">
        Lord Kelvin
       </a>
       on the age of the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For example: the similarity of southern continent geological formations had led
       <a href="/wiki/Roberto_Mantovani" title="Roberto Mantovani">
        Roberto Mantovani
       </a>
       to conjecture in 1889 and 1909 that all the continents had once been joined into a
       <a href="/wiki/Supercontinent" title="Supercontinent">
        supercontinent
       </a>
       ; Wegener noted the similarity of Mantovani's and his own maps of the former positions of the southern continents. In Mantovani's conjecture, this continent broke due to
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">
        volcanic
       </a>
       activity caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_expansion" title="Thermal expansion">
        thermal expansion
       </a>
       , and the new continents drifted away from each other because of further expansion of the rip-zones, where the oceans now lie. This led Mantovani to propose an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Expanding_Earth_theory" title="Expanding Earth theory">
        Expanding Earth theory
       </a>
       which has since been shown to be incorrect.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Continental drift without expansion was proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Bursley_Taylor" title="Frank Bursley Taylor">
        Frank Bursley Taylor
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_obit_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Taylor_obit-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who suggested in 1908 (published in 1910) that the continents were moved into their present positions by a process of "continental creep".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a later paper he proposed that this occurred by their being dragged towards the equator by tidal forces during the hypothesized capture of the moon in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous" title="Cretaceous">
        Cretaceous
       </a>
       , resulting in "general crustal creep" toward the equator. Although his proposed mechanism was wrong, he was the first to realize the insight that one of the effects of continental motion would be the formation of mountains, and attributed the formation of the Himalayas to the collision between the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       with Asia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell2015_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Powell2015-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Wegener said that of all those theories, Taylor's, although not fully developed, had the most similarities to his own. In the mid-20th century, the theory of continental drift was referred to as the "Taylor-Wegener hypothesis",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_obit_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Taylor_obit-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Powell2015_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Powell2015-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although this terminology eventually fell out of common use.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Alfred Wegener first presented his hypothesis to the German Geological Society on 6 January 1912.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weg_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-weg-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His hypothesis was that the continents had once formed a single landmass, called
       <a href="/wiki/Pangaea" title="Pangaea">
        Pangaea
       </a>
       , before breaking apart and drifting to their present locations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wegener was the first to use the phrase "continental drift" (1912, 1915)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-weg_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-weg-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wegb_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wegb-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (in German "die Verschiebung der Kontinente" – translated into English in 1922) and formally publish the hypothesis that the continents had somehow "drifted" apart. Although he presented much evidence for continental drift, he was unable to provide a convincing explanation for the physical processes which might have caused this drift. His suggestion that the continents had been pulled apart by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(fictitious)" title="Centrifugal force (fictitious)">
        centrifugal pseudoforce
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Polflucht
       </i>
       ) of the Earth's rotation or by a small component of astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       was rejected, as calculations showed that the force was not sufficient.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Polflucht" title="Polflucht">
        Polflucht
       </a>
       hypothesis was also studied by
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Sophus_Epstein" title="Paul Sophus Epstein">
        Paul Sophus Epstein
       </a>
       in 1920 and found to be implausible.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Rejection_of_Wegener.27s_theory.2C_1910s.E2.80.931950s">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rejection_of_Wegener's_theory,_1910s–1950s">
        Rejection of Wegener's theory, 1910s–1950s
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Continental_drift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rejection of Wegener's theory, 1910s–1950s">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The theory of continental drift was not accepted for many years. One problem was that a plausible driving force was missing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usghistory_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-usghistory-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A second problem was that Wegener's estimate of the velocity of continental motion, 250 cm/year, was implausibly high.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (The currently accepted rate for the separation of the Americas from Europe and Africa is about 2.5 cm/year).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       And it did not help that Wegener was not a geologist. Other geologists also believed that the evidence that Wegener had provided was not sufficient. It is now accepted that the plates carrying the continents do move across the Earth's surface, although not as fast as Wegener believed; ironically one of the chief outstanding questions is the one Wegener failed to resolve: what is the nature of the forces propelling the plates?
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usghistory_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-usghistory-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The British geologist
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Holmes" title="Arthur Holmes">
        Arthur Holmes
       </a>
       championed the theory of continental drift at a time when it was deeply unfashionable. He proposed in 1931 that the Earth's mantle contained convection cells that dissipated radioactive heat and moved the crust at the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       His
       <i>
        Principles of Physical Geology
       </i>
       , ending with a chapter on continental drift, was published in 1944.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/David_Attenborough" title="David Attenborough">
        David Attenborough
       </a>
       , who attended university in the second half of the 1940s, recounted an incident illustrating its lack of acceptance then: "I once asked one of my lecturers why he was not talking to us about continental drift and I was told, sneeringly, that if I could prove there was a force that could move continents, then he might think about it. The idea was moonshine, I was informed."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Geological maps of the time showed huge land bridges spanning the Atlantic and Indian oceans to account for the similarities of fauna and flora and the divisions of the Asian continent in the Permian era but failing to account for glaciation in India, Australia and South Africa.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As late as 1953 – just five years before
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Warren_Carey" title="Samuel Warren Carey">
        Carey
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       introduced the theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       – the theory of continental drift was rejected by the physicist Scheidegger on the following grounds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        First, it had been shown that floating masses on a rotating
        <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
         geoid
        </a>
        would collect at the equator, and stay there. This would explain one, but only one, mountain building episode between any pair of continents; it failed to account for earlier
        <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">
         orogenic
        </a>
        episodes.
       </li>
       <li>
        Second, masses floating freely in a fluid substratum, like icebergs in the ocean, should be in
        <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
         isostatic
        </a>
        equilibrium (in which the forces of gravity and buoyancy are in balance). But gravitational measurements showed that many areas are not in isostatic equilibrium.
       </li>
       <li>
        Third, there was the problem of why some parts of the Earth's surface (crust) should have solidified while other parts were still fluid. Various attempts to explain this foundered on other difficulties.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Geophysicist
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Oliver_(scientist)" title="Jack Oliver (scientist)">
        Jack Oliver
       </a>
       is credited with providing seismologic evidence supporting plate tectonics which encompassed and superseded continental drift with the article "Seismology and the New Global Tectonics", published in 1968, using data collected from seismologic stations, including those he set up in the South Pacific.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is now known that there are two kinds of crust:
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_crust" title="Continental crust">
        continental crust
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanic_crust" title="Oceanic crust">
        oceanic crust
       </a>
       . Continental crust is inherently lighter and its composition is different from oceanic crust, but both kinds reside above a much deeper "
       <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">
        plastic
       </a>
       " mantle. Oceanic crust is created at
       <a href="/wiki/Seafloor_spreading" title="Seafloor spreading">
        spreading centers
       </a>
       , and this, along with
       <a href="/wiki/Subduction" title="Subduction">
        subduction
       </a>
       , drives the system of plates in a chaotic manner, resulting in continuous
       <a href="/wiki/Orogeny" title="Orogeny">
        orogeny
       </a>
       and areas of isostatic imbalance. The theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       explains all this, including the movement of the continents, better than Wegener's theory.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_of_continental_drift">
        Evidence of continental drift
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Continental_drift&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Evidence of continental drift">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        Plate tectonics
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="446" data-file-width="580" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg/220px-Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg/330px-Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg/440px-Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Snider-Pellegrini_Wegener_fossil_map.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Fossil patterns across continents (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gondwanaland" title="Gondwanaland">
          Gondwanaland
         </a>
         ).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mesosaurus.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="579" data-file-width="2010" height="63" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mesosaurus.png/220px-Mesosaurus.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mesosaurus.png/330px-Mesosaurus.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e7/Mesosaurus.png/440px-Mesosaurus.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mesosaurus.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Mesosaurus
         </i>
         skeleton, MacGregor, 1908.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Evidence for the movement of continents on tectonic plates is now extensive. Similar plant and animal
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil" title="Fossil">
        fossils
       </a>
       are found around the shores of different continents, suggesting that they were once joined. The fossils of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mesosaurus" title="Mesosaurus">
         Mesosaurus
        </a>
       </i>
       , a freshwater reptile rather like a small crocodile, found both in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       , are one example; another is the discovery of fossils of the land
       <a href="/wiki/Reptile" title="Reptile">
        reptile
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lystrosaurus" title="Lystrosaurus">
         Lystrosaurus
        </a>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rocks
       </a>
       of the same age at locations in
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is also living evidence—the same animals being found on two continents. Some
       <a href="/wiki/Earthworm" title="Earthworm">
        earthworm
       </a>
       families (e.g. Ocnerodrilidae, Acanthodrilidae, Octochaetidae) are found in South America and Africa, for instance.
      </p>
      <p>
       The complementary arrangement of the facing sides of South America and Africa is obvious, but is a temporary coincidence. In millions of years,
       <a href="/wiki/Slab_pull" title="Slab pull">
        slab pull
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ridge-push" title="Ridge-push">
        ridge-push
       </a>
       , and other forces of
       <a href="/wiki/Tectonophysics" title="Tectonophysics">
        tectonophysics
       </a>
       , will further separate and rotate those two continents. It was this temporary feature which inspired Wegener to study what he defined as continental drift, although he did not live to see his hypothesis generally accepted.
      </p>
      <p>
       Widespread distribution of
       <a href="/wiki/Permo-Carboniferous" title="Permo-Carboniferous">
        Permo-Carboniferous
       </a>
       glacial sediments in South America, Africa, Madagascar, Arabia, India, Antarctica and Australia was one of the major pieces of evidence for the theory of continental drift. The continuity of glaciers, inferred from oriented
       <a href="/wiki/Glacial_striation" title="Glacial striation">
        glacial striations
       </a>
       and deposits called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tillite" title="Tillite">
        tillites
       </a>
       , suggested the existence of the supercontinent of
       <a href="/wiki/Gondwana" title="Gondwana">
        Gondwana
       </a>
       , which became a central element of the concept of continental drift. Striations indicated glacial flow away from the equator and toward the poles, based on continents' current positions and orientations, and supported the idea that the southern continents had previously been in dramatically different locations, as well as being contiguous with each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wegb_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-wegb-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
