      <p>
       <b>
        Hispanism
       </b>
       (sometimes referred to as
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Hispanic" title="Hispanic">
         Hispanic
        </a>
        Studies
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Spanish Studies
       </b>
       ) is the study of the literature and culture of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       -speaking world, principally that of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       . It can also entail studying Spanish language and culture in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and in other presently or formerly Spanish-speaking countries in
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pacific" title="Pacific">
        Pacific
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_Guinea" title="Equatorial Guinea">
        Equatorial Guinea
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       A practicing scholar who specializes in this field is known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanist" title="Hispanist">
        Hispanist
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">
        Origins
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       During the 16th century, Spain was a motor of innovation in Europe, given its links to new lands, subjects, literary sorts and personages, dances, and fashions. This hegemonic status, also advanced by commercial and economic interests, generated interest in learning the Spanish language, as Spain was the dominant political power and was the first to develop an overseas empire in post-
       <a href="/wiki/Renaissance" title="Renaissance">
        Renaissance
       </a>
       Europe. In order to respond to that interest, some Spanish writers developed a new focus on the Spanish language as subject matter. In 1492
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Nebrija" title="Antonio de Nebrija">
        Antonio de Nebrija
       </a>
       published his
       <i>
        Gramática castellana
       </i>
       , the first published grammar of a modern European language.
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_de_Vald%C3%A9s" title="Juan de Valdés">
        Juan de Valdés
       </a>
       composed his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=lOoRAAAAIAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;source=gbs_ge_summary_r&amp;cad=0#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Diálogo de la lengua
        </i>
       </a>
       (1533) for his Italian friends, who were eager to learn Castilian. And the lawyer
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_de_Villal%C3%B3n" title="es:Cristóbal de Villalón">
        Cristóbal de Villalón
       </a>
       wrote in his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Ud07AAAAcAAJ&amp;pg=PT4&amp;dq=Villal%C3%B3n+Gram%C3%A1tica+castellana&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=kUbyTYGIBKSn0AGB3_3_Cw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Gramática castellana
        </i>
       </a>
       (Antwerp, 1558) that Castilian was spoken by Flemish, Italian, English, and French persons.
      </p>
      <p>
       For many years, especially between 1550 and 1670, European presses published a large number of Spanish grammars and dictionaries that linked Spanish to one or more other languages. Two of the oldest grammars were published anonymously in
       <a href="/wiki/Leuven" title="Leuven">
        Louvain
       </a>
       :
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Y02ycQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Util+y+breve+institucion&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=N1DyTf-cOcXt0gHz_aDBCw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;ved=0CEEQ6AEwBA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Útil y breve institución para aprender los Principios y fundamentos de la lengua Hespañola
        </i>
       </a>
       (1555) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=O1ddAAAAMAAJ&amp;q=gramatica+de+la+lengua+vulgar+de+espa%C3%B1a&amp;dq=gramatica+de+la+lengua+vulgar+de+espa%C3%B1a&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=7lHyTdLLM-fx0gH_0LWKDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CDIQ6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Gramática de la lengua vulgar de España
        </i>
       </a>
       (1559).
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the more outstanding foreign authors of Spanish grammars were the Italians
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Mario_Alessandri" title="Giovanni Mario Alessandri">
        Giovanni Mario Alessandri
       </a>
       (1560) and
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Miranda" title="Giovanni Miranda">
        Giovanni Miranda
       </a>
       (1566);
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-google-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the English
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Percivale" title="Richard Percivale">
        Richard Percivale
       </a>
       (1591),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-percivale+minsheu_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-percivale+minsheu-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/John_Minsheu" title="John Minsheu">
        John Minsheu
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-percivale+minsheu_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-percivale+minsheu-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (1599) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=9Q7BbwAACAAJ&amp;dq=Lewis+Owen&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=nBP1TYm1JoGbtwe0ldmDBw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=7&amp;ved=0CEYQ6AEwBg" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_Owen" title="es:Lewis Owen">
        Lewis Owen
       </a>
       (1605); the French
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Saulnier" title="es:Jean Saulnier">
        Jean Saulnier
       </a>
       (1608) and
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Doujat" title="Jean Doujat">
        Jean Doujat
       </a>
       (1644); the German
       <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Doergangk" title="Heinrich Doergangk">
        Heinrich Doergangk
       </a>
       (1614);
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-google2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Dutch
       <a href="/wiki/Carolus_Mulerius" title="Carolus Mulerius">
        Carolus Mulerius
       </a>
       (1630).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-google3_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-google3-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dictionaries were composed by the Italian
       <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Vittori" title="Girolamo Vittori">
        Girolamo Vittori
       </a>
       (1602), the Englishman
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Torius" title="es:John Torius">
        John Torius
       </a>
       (1590) and the Frenchmen
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Ledel" title="es:Jacques Ledel">
        Jacques Ledel
       </a>
       (1565),
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=KQnfTw_oT6UC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Jean+Palet&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=FkP1TZeGKqL10gGsy4iMAQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCwQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Palet" title="es:Jean Palet">
        Jean Palet
       </a>
       (1604) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=eEd8QwAACAAJ&amp;dq=Fran%C3%A7ois+Huillery&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=9UP1TYGiF4jL0QGy-8nsDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;ved=0CD0Q6AEwBA" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Fran%C3%A7ois_Huillery&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="François Huillery (page does not exist)">
        François Huillery
       </a>
       (1661). The lexicographical contribution of the German
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heinrich_Hornkens" title="Heinrich Hornkens">
        Heinrich Hornkens
       </a>
       (1599) and of the Franco-Spanish author
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pere_Lacavaller%C3%ADa" title="es:Pere Lacavallería">
        Pere Lacavallería
       </a>
       (1642) were also important to French Hispanism.
      </p>
      <p>
       Others combined grammars and dictionaries. The works of the Englishman
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Percivale" title="Richard Percivale">
        Richard Percivale
       </a>
       (1591), Frenchman
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Oudin" title="César Oudin">
        César Oudin
       </a>
       (1597, 1607), Italians
       <a href="/wiki/Lorenzo_Franciosini" title="Lorenzo Franciosini">
        Lorenzo Franciosini
       </a>
       (1620, 1624) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=LZMCAAAAQAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Arnaldo+de+la+Porte&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=b0X1Ta-vJZOn0AGFlrHpDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CDAQ6AEwATgK#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnaldo_de_la_Porte" title="es:Arnaldo de la Porte">
        Arnaldo de la Porte
       </a>
       (1659, 1669) and Austrian
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=LYu2sj0spQ0C&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Nicholas+Mez+von+Braidenbach&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=D0f1TYPMM-qv0AH4rtjsDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCwQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Mez_von_Braidenbach" title="es:Nicholas Mez von Braidenbach">
        Nicholas Mez von Braidenbach
       </a>
       (1666, 1670) were especially relevant. Franciosini and Oudin also translated
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
         Don Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       . This list is far from complete and the grammars and dictionaries in general had a great number of versions, adaptations, reprintings and even translations (Oudin's
       <i>
        Grammaire et observations de langue espagnolle
       </i>
       , for example, was translated into Latin and English). This is why it is not possible to exaggerate the great impact that the Spanish language had in the Europe of the 16th and 17th centuries.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 19th century, coinciding with the loss of the Spanish colonial empire and the birth of new Latin American republics, Europe and the United States showed a renewed interest in Hispanic history, literature and culture of the declining great power and its now independent former colonies.
      </p>
      <p>
       During
       <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
        the Romantic period
       </a>
       , the image of a Moorish and exotic medieval Spain, a picturesque country with a mixed cultural heritage, captured the imagination of many writers. This led many to become interested in Spanish literature, legends, and traditions. Travel books written at that time maintained and intensified that interest, and led to a more serious and scientific approach to the study of Spanish and Hispanic American culture. This field did not have a word coined to name it until the early 20th century, when it ended up being called Hispanism.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hispanism has traditionally been defined
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (September 2012)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       as the study of the Spanish and Spanish-American cultures, and particularly of their language by foreigners or people generally not educated in Spain. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervantes_Institute" title="Cervantes Institute">
        Cervantes Institute
       </a>
       has promoted the study of Spanish and Hispanic culture around the world, similar to the way in which institutions such as the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Council" title="British Council">
        British Council
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Alliance_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Alliance Française">
        Alliance Française
       </a>
       or the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goethe_Institute" title="Goethe Institute">
        Goethe Institute
       </a>
       have done for their own countries.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_the_world">
        Hispanism in the world
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Hispanism in the world">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Great_Britain_and_Ireland">
        Hispanism in Great Britain and Ireland
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Great Britain and Ireland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first Spanish book translated into English was the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Celestina" title="La Celestina">
         Celestina
        </a>
       </i>
       , as an adaptation in verse published in London between 1525 and 1530 by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Rastell" title="John Rastell">
        John Rastell
       </a>
       . It includes only the first four acts and is based on the Italian version of Alfonso de Ordóñez; it is often referred to as an
       <i>
        Interlude
       </i>
       , and its original title is
       <i>
        A New Comedy in English in Manner of an Interlude Right Elegant and Full of Craft of Rhetoric: Wherein is Shewed and Described as well the Beauty and Good Properties of Women, as Their Vices and Evil Conditions with a Moral Conclusion and Exhortation to Virtue.
       </i>
       . The Scottish poet
       <a href="/wiki/William_Drummond_of_Hawthornden" title="William Drummond of Hawthornden">
        William Drummond
       </a>
       (1585–1649) translated
       <a href="/wiki/Garcilaso_de_la_Vega_(poet)" title="Garcilaso de la Vega (poet)">
        Garcilaso de la Vega
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Juan_Bosc%C3%A1n" title="Juan Boscán">
        Juan Boscán
       </a>
       . The English knew the masterpieces of Castilian literature, from early translations of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Amad%C3%ADs_de_Gaula" title="Amadís de Gaula">
         Amadís de Gaula
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Garci_Rodriguez_de_Montalvo" title="Garci Rodriguez de Montalvo">
        Garci Rodriguez de Montalvo
       </a>
       and the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A1rcel_de_amor" title="Cárcel de amor">
         Cárcel de amor
        </a>
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Diego_de_San_Pedro" title="Diego de San Pedro">
        Diego de San Pedro
       </a>
       . Sir
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_Sidney" title="Philip Sidney">
        Philip Sidney
       </a>
       had read
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Siete_libros_de_la_Diana&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Siete libros de la Diana (page does not exist)">
         Siete libros de la Diana
        </a>
       </i>
       by the Hispano-Portuguese
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_de_Montemayor" title="Jorge de Montemayor">
        Jorge de Montemayor
       </a>
       , whose poetry influenced him greatly. John Bourchier translated
       <i>
        Libro de Marco Aurelio
       </i>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Guevara" title="Antonio de Guevara">
        Antonio de Guevara
       </a>
       .
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Rowland" title="es:David Rowland">
        David Rowland
       </a>
       translated
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lazarillo_de_Tormes" title="Lazarillo de Tormes">
         Lazarillo de Tormes
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1586, which may have inspired the first English
       <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">
        picaresque novel
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        The Unfortunate Traveller
       </i>
       (1594), by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Nashe" title="Thomas Nashe">
        Thomas Nashe
       </a>
       . By the end of the 16th century, the
       <i>
        Celestina
       </i>
       had been translated fully (in London, J. Wolf, 1591;
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Adam_Islip&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Adam Islip (page does not exist)">
        Adam Islip
       </a>
       , 1596;
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=William_Apsley&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="William Apsley (page does not exist)">
        William Apsley
       </a>
       , 1598; and others). Some of the translators of that time traveled or lived for some time in Spain, such as Lord Berners,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bartholomew_Yong" title="es:Bartholomew Yong">
        Bartholomew Yong
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Shelton_(translator)" title="Thomas Shelton (translator)">
        Thomas Shelton
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Digges_(writer)" title="Leonard Digges (writer)">
        Leonard Digges
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Mabbe" title="James Mabbe">
        James Mabbe
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/William_Cecil,_1st_Baron_Burghley" title="William Cecil, 1st Baron Burghley">
        William Cecil
       </a>
       (Lord Burghley; 1520–1598) owned the largest Spanish library in the United Kingdom.
      </p>
      <p>
       Elizabethan theater also felt the powerful influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">
        Spanish Golden Age
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/John_Fletcher_(playwright)" title="John Fletcher (playwright)">
        John Fletcher
       </a>
       , a frequent collaborator of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       , borrowed from
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
         Don Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       for his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cardenio" title="Cardenio">
         Cardenio
        </a>
       </i>
       , possibly written in collaboration with Shakespeare, who is thought to have read
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Luis_Vives" title="Juan Luis Vives">
        Juan Luis Vives
       </a>
       . Fletcher's frequent collaborator
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Beaumont" title="Francis Beaumont">
        Francis Beaumont
       </a>
       also imitated
       <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
        Don Quixote
       </a>
       in the more well-known
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Knight_of_the_Burning_Pestle" title="The Knight of the Burning Pestle">
         The Knight of the Burning Pestle
        </a>
       </i>
       . Fletcher also borrowed from Cervantes's
       <i>
        Los trabajos de Persiles y Segismunda
       </i>
       for his
       <a href="/wiki/The_Custom_of_the_Country_(play)" title="The Custom of the Country (play)">
        <i>
         The Custom of the Country
        </i>
       </a>
       and from Cervantes's
       <i>
        La ilustre fregona
       </i>
       for his beautiful young saleswoman.
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Middleton" title="Thomas Middleton">
        Thomas Middleton
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/William_Rowley" title="William Rowley">
        William Rowley
       </a>
       were inspired by Cervantes's
       <i>
        La gitanilla
       </i>
       (one of his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Novelas_ejemplares" title="Novelas ejemplares">
         Novelas ejemplares
        </a>
       </i>
       ) to write
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spanish_Gipsy&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Spanish Gipsy (page does not exist)">
         The Spanish Gipsy
        </a>
       </i>
       (1623).
      </p>
      <p>
       The first translation of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       into a foreign language was the English version by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Shelton_(translator)" title="Thomas Shelton (translator)">
        Thomas Shelton
       </a>
       (first part, 1612; second, 1620). And
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       was imitated in the satirical poem
       <i>
        Hudibras
       </i>
       (1663–78), composed by
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Butler_(poet)" title="Samuel Butler (poet)">
        Samuel Butler
       </a>
       . In addition, the works of some great Golden Age poets were translated into English by
       <a href="/wiki/Sir_Richard_Fanshawe,_1st_Baronet" title="Sir Richard Fanshawe, 1st Baronet">
        Richard Fanshawe
       </a>
       , who died in Madrid. As early as 1738, a luxurious London edition of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       in Spanish was published, prepared by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sephardic" title="Sephardic">
        Sephardic
       </a>
       Cervantist
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_Pineda" title="es:Pedro Pineda">
        Pedro Pineda
       </a>
       , with an introduction by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Mayans" title="es:Gregorio Mayans">
        Gregorio Mayans
       </a>
       and ornate engravings. Also in the 18th century two new translations of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       were published, one by the painter
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Jervas" title="Charles Jervas">
        Charles Jervas
       </a>
       (1742) and one by
       <a href="/wiki/Tobias_Smollett" title="Tobias Smollett">
        Tobias Smollett
       </a>
       , a writer of
       <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">
        picaresque novels
       </a>
       (1755). Smollet appears as an avid reader of Spanish narrative, and that influence is always present in his works. Meanwhile, the best work of the 17th-century writer
       <a href="/wiki/Charlotte_Lennox" title="Charlotte Lennox">
        Charlotte Lennox
       </a>
       is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Female_Quixote" title="The Female Quixote">
         The Female Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       (1752), which was inspired by Cervantes. Cervantes also was the inspiration for
       <i>
        The Spiritual Quixote
       </i>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Graves" title="Richard Graves">
        Richard Graves
       </a>
       . Thwe first critical and annotated edition of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       was that of the English clergyman
       <a href="/wiki/John_Bowle_(writer)" title="John Bowle (writer)">
        John Bowle
       </a>
       (1781). The novelists
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Fielding" title="Henry Fielding">
        Henry Fielding
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lawrence_Sterne" title="Lawrence Sterne">
        Lawrence Sterne
       </a>
       also were familiar with the works of Cervantes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the British travellers in Spain in the 18th century who left written testimony of their travels are (chronologically)
       <a href="/wiki/John_Durant_Breval" title="John Durant Breval">
        John Durant Breval
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_James" title="Thomas James">
        Thomas James
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Wyndham_Beawes&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Wyndham Beawes (page does not exist)">
        Wyndham Beawes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/James_Harris_(grammarian)" title="James Harris (grammarian)">
        James Harris
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Twiss_(writer)" title="Richard Twiss (writer)">
        Richard Twiss
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Francis_Carter_(writer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Francis Carter (writer) (page does not exist)">
        Francis Carter
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Dalrymple_(historian)" title="William Dalrymple (historian)">
        William Dalrymple
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Philip_Thicknesse" title="Philip Thicknesse">
        Philip Thicknesse
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Swinburne" title="Henry Swinburne">
        Henry Swinburne
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Talbot_Dillon" title="John Talbot Dillon">
        John Talbot Dillon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Jardine_(British_Army_officer)" title="Alexander Jardine (British Army officer)">
        Alexander Jardine
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Croker" title="Richard Croker">
        Richard Croker
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Cumberland_(dramatist)" title="Richard Cumberland (dramatist)">
        Richard Cumberland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Townsend" title="Joseph Townsend">
        Joseph Townsend
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Young_(writer)" title="Arthur Young (writer)">
        Arthur Young
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Thomas_Beckford" title="William Thomas Beckford">
        William Beckford
       </a>
       , John Macdonald (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=A5ky1nzR2nUC&amp;pg=PR10&amp;dq=Memoirs+of+an+eighteenth-century+footman&amp;hl=en&amp;sa=X&amp;ei=lOTxT_GJJ4fb6wGGyZWYBg&amp;ved=0CDsQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q=Memoirs%20of%20an%20eighteenth-century%20footman&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Memoirs of an Eighteenth-Century Footman
        </i>
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">
        Robert Southey
       </a>

       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=S5c-YgEACAAJ&amp;dq=Neville+Wyndham&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=QAH9TdXXJeqt0AGs-N3GAw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        Neville Wyndham
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Other English travel writers who straddled the 18th and 19th centuries include
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hookham_Frere" title="John Hookham Frere">
        John Hookham Frere
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Vassall-Fox,_3rd_Baron_Holland" title="Henry Vassall-Fox, 3rd Baron Holland">
        Henry Richard Vassall-Fox
       </a>
       , better known as Lord Holland (1773–1840), a great friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Gaspar_Melchor_de_Jovellanos" title="Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos">
        Gaspar Melchor de Jovellanos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Manuel_Jos%C3%A9_Quintana" title="Manuel José Quintana">
        Manuel José Quintana
       </a>
       , and benefactor of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Blanco_White" title="José María Blanco White">
        José María Blanco White
       </a>
       . Lord Holland visited Spain on numerous occasions and wrote his impressions about those trips. He also collected books and manuscripts and wrote a biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Vega" title="Lope de Vega">
        Lope de Vega
       </a>
       . His home was open to all Spaniards, but especially to the liberal émigrés who arrived in the London district of
       <a href="/wiki/Somers_Town,_London" title="Somers Town, London">
        Somers Town
       </a>
       in the 19th century, fleeing the absolutist repression of
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_VII_of_Spain" title="Ferdinand VII of Spain">
        King Ferdinand VII
       </a>
       and the religious and ideological dogmatism of the country. Many of them subsisted by translating or teaching their language to English people, most of whom were interested in conducting business with Spanish America, although others wished to learn about Spanish medieval literature, much in vogue among the Romantics. One of the émigrés,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Alcal%C3%A1_Galiano" title="es:Antonio Alcalá Galiano">
        Antonio Alcalá Galiano
       </a>
       , taught Spanish literature as a professor at the University of London in 1828 and published his notes. The publisher
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolph_Ackerman" title="es:Rudolph Ackerman">
        Rudolph Ackerman
       </a>
       established a great business publishing
       <i>
        Catecismos
       </i>
       (text books) on different matters in Spanish, many of them written by Spanish émigrés, for the new Spanish-American republics.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_G._Lewis" title="es:Matthew G. Lewis">
        Matthew G. Lewis
       </a>
       set some of his works in Spain. And the protagonist of
       <a href="/wiki/Jane_Austen" title="Jane Austen">
        Jane Austen
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Abbey of Northanger
       </i>
       is deranged by her excessive reading of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gothic_novel" title="Gothic novel">
        Gothic novels
       </a>
       , much as was Don Quixote with his books of
       <a href="/wiki/Knight-errant" title="Knight-errant">
        chivalry
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sir
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Scott" title="Walter Scott">
        Walter Scott
       </a>
       was an enthusiastic reader of Cervantes and tried his hand at translation. He dedicated his narrative poem
       <i>
        The Vision of Roderick
       </i>
       (1811) to Spain and its history.
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Rodd" title="Thomas Rodd">
        Thomas Rodd
       </a>
       translated some
       <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
        Spanish folk ballads
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Lord_Byron" title="Lord Byron">
        Lord Byron
       </a>
       also was greatly interested in Spain and was a reader of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       . He translated the ballad
       <i>
        Ay de mi Alhama
       </i>
       in part of his
       <i>
        Childe Harold
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Don Juan
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Chenevix_Trench" title="Richard Chenevix Trench">
        Richard Trench
       </a>
       translated
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Pedro Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       and was friends with some of the emigrated Spaniards, some of whom wrote in both English and Spanish, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Mar%C3%ADa_Blanco_White" title="José María Blanco White">
        José María Blanco White
       </a>
       and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telesforo_de_Trueba_y_Coss%C3%ADo" title="es:Telesforo de Trueba y Cossío">
        Telesforo de Trueba y Cossío
       </a>
       , and many of whom (including
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Calder%C3%B3n" title="Juan Calderón">
        Juan Calderón
       </a>
       , who held a chair of Spanish at
       <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">
        King's College
       </a>
       ), spread knowledge of the Spanish language and its literature.
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hookham_Frere" title="John Hookham Frere">
        John Hookham Frere
       </a>
       was a friend of the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%81ngel_de_Saavedra,_3rd_Duke_of_Rivas" title="es:Ángel de Saavedra, 3rd Duke of Rivas">
        Duke of Rivas
       </a>
       when the latter was in Malta, and Hookham translated some medieval and classical poetry into English. The brothers
       <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah_Holmes_Wiffen" title="Jeremiah Holmes Wiffen">
        Jeremiah Holmes Wiffen
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benjamin_B._Wiffen" title="Benjamin B. Wiffen">
        Benjamin B. Wiffen
       </a>
       were both scholars of Spanish culture. The
       <a href="/wiki/Lake_Poets" title="Lake Poets">
        "Lake Poet"
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Southey" title="Robert Southey">
        Robert Southey
       </a>
       , translated
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Amad%C3%ADs_de_Gaula" title="Amadís de Gaula">
         Amadís de Gaula
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palmer%C3%ADn_de_Inglaterra" title="es:Palmerín de Inglaterra">
         Palmerín de Inglaterra
        </a>
       </i>
       into English, among others works. English novelists were strongly influenced by Cervantes. Especially so was
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">
        Charles Dickens
       </a>
       , who created a quixotic pair in Mr. Pickwick and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sam_Weller_(fictional_character)" title="Sam Weller (fictional character)">
        Sam Weller
       </a>
       of
       <i>
        Posthumous Papers of the Pickwick Club
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/John_Ormsby_(translator)" title="John Ormsby (translator)">
        John Ormsby
       </a>
       translated the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cantar_de_Mio_Cid" title="Cantar de Mio Cid">
         Cantar de Mio Cid
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
         Don Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Percy_Bysshe_Shelley" title="Percy Bysshe Shelley">
        Percy Bysshe Shelley
       </a>
       left traces of his devotion to
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       in his work. The polyglot
       <a href="/wiki/John_Bowring" title="John Bowring">
        John Bowring
       </a>
       traveled to Spain in 1819 and published the observations of his trip. Other accounts of travel in Spain inclulde those of

        Richard Ford
       </a>

       <i>
        Handbook for Travellers in Spain
       </i>
       (1845) was republished in many editions, and

        </a>
       </i>

       <a class="external text" href="http://www.hispanists.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Germany, Austria and Switzerland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Aside from the imitation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Picaresque_novel" title="Picaresque novel">
        picaresque novel
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Hans_Jakob_Christoffel_von_Grimmelshausen" title="Hans Jakob Christoffel von Grimmelshausen">
        Hans Jakob Christoffel von Grimmelshausen
       </a>
       , Hispanism bloomed in Germany around the enthusiasm that German Romantics had for
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Baltasar_Graci%C3%A1n" title="Baltasar Gracián">
        Gracián
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Friedrich_Diez" title="Friedrich Diez">
        Friedrich Diez
       </a>
       (1794–1876) can be considered the first German philologist to give prominence to Spanish, in his
       <i>
        Grammatik der romanischen Sprachen
       </i>
       (1836–1843) and his
       <i>
        Etymologisches Wörterbuch der romanischen Sprachen
       </i>
       (1854). His first Spanish-related work,
       <i>
        Altspanische Romanzen
       </i>
       , was published in 1819.
      </p>
      <p>
       Important to the promotion of Hispanism in Germany was a group of Romantic writers that included
       <a href="/wiki/Ludwig_Tieck" title="Ludwig Tieck">
        Ludwig Tieck
       </a>
       , an orientalist and poet who translated
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
         Don Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       into German (1799–1801);
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bouterwek" title="Friedrich Bouterwek">
        Friedrich Bouterwek
       </a>
       , author of the unorthodox
       <i>
        Geschichte der Poesie und Beredsamkeit seit dem Ende des dreizehnten Jahrhunderts
       </i>
       and translator of Cervantes's short farce
       <i>
        El Juez de los Divorcios
       </i>
       ; and
       <a href="/wiki/August_Wilhelm_Schlegel" title="August Wilhelm Schlegel">
        August Wilhelm Schlegel
       </a>
       (1767–1845), who translated works of
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        Spanisches Theater
       </i>
       , 1803–1809) and Spanish classical poetry into German. The philologist and folklorist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jakob_Grimm" title="Jakob Grimm">
        Jakob Grimm
       </a>
       published
       <i>
        Silva de romances viejos
       </i>
       (Vienna, 1816) with a prologue in Spanish.
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Nicol%C3%A1s_B%C3%B6hl_de_Faber" title="Juan Nicolás Böhl de Faber">
        Juan Nicolás Böhl de Faber
       </a>
       , German consul in Spain, was a devoted student of
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón de la Barca
       </a>
       , of Spanish classical theater generally, and of traditional popular literature. The philologist
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_von_Humboldt" title="Wilhelm von Humboldt">
        Wilhelm von Humboldt
       </a>
       traveled through Spain taking notes and was interested especially in the Basque language, and the philosopher
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schopenhauer" title="Arthur Schopenhauer">
        Arthur Schopenhauer
       </a>
       was an avid reader and translator of
       <a href="/wiki/Baltasar_Graci%C3%A1n" title="Baltasar Gracián">
        Gracián
       </a>
       . Count
       <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Friedrich_von_Schack" title="Adolf Friedrich von Schack">
        Adolf Friedrich von Schack
       </a>
       (1815–1894) made a trip to Spain in 1852 to study the remnants of the Moorish civilization and became a devoted scholar of things Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hispanists of German, Austrian, and Swiss origins include
       <a href="/wiki/Franz_Grillparzer" title="Franz Grillparzer">
        Franz Grillparzer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Wendelin_F%C3%B6rster" title="Wendelin Förster">
        Wendelin Förster
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Vollm%C3%B6ller" title="Karl Vollmöller">
        Karl Vollmöller
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Tobler" title="es:Adolf Tobler">
        Adolf Tobler
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Morf" title="es:Heinrich Morf">
        Heinrich Morf
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Gr%C3%B6ber" title="Gustav Gröber">
        Gustav Gröber
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Baist" title="es:Gottfried Baist">
        Gottfried Baist
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Meyer-L%C3%BCbke" title="Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke">
        Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke
       </a>
       . Among them are two emigrants to Chile,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rodolfo_Lenz" title="es:Rodolfo Lenz">
        Rodolfo Lenz
       </a>
       (1863–1938), whose works include his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=IJoMAQAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=rodolfo+lenz+voces+chilenas&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=7M8ATrC8KIS2tgf4tszyDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Diccionario etimolójico de las voces chilenas derivadas de lenguas indíjenas americanas
        </i>
       </a>
       (1904) and
       <i>
        Chilenische Studien
       </i>
       (1891), as well as other works on grammar and the Spanish of the Americas; and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Hanssen" title="es:Friedrich Hanssen">
        Friedrich Hanssen
       </a>
       (1857–1919), author of
       <i>
        Spanische Grammatik auf historischer Grundlage
       </i>
       (1910; revised ed. in Spanish,
       <i>
        Gramática histórica de la lengua castellana
       </i>
       , 1913), as well as other works on
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       philology,
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       dialectology, and the Spanish of the Americas. The
       <i>
        Handbuch der romanischen Philologie
       </i>
       (1896) by
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Meyer-L%C3%BCbke" title="Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke">
        Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke
       </a>
       was a classic in Spain, as were his
       <i>
        Grammatik der romanischen Sprachen
       </i>
       (1890–1902),
       <i>
        Einführung in das Studium der romanischen Sprachwissenschaft
       </i>
       (1901) (translated into Spanish), and
       <i>
        Romanisches etymologisches Wörterbuch
       </i>
       (1935).
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Fastenrath" title="Johannes Fastenrath">
        Johannes Fastenrath
       </a>
       , through his translations and other works, spread the Spanish culture among his contemporaries; in addition, he created the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Premio_Fastenrath" title="es:Premio Fastenrath">
        prize
       </a>
       that bears his name in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Real_Academia_Espa%C3%B1ola" title="Real Academia Española">
        Spanish Royal Academy
       </a>
       , to reward the best works in Spanish poetry, fiction, and essays. The Austrian Romance scholar
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Wolf" title="es:Ferdinand Wolf">
        Ferdinand Wolf
       </a>
       , a friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Agust%C3%ADn_Dur%C3%A1n" title="Agustín Durán">
        Agustín Durán
       </a>
       , was particularly interested in the
       <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
        romancero
       </a>
       , in the lyric poetry of the medieval Spanish
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%ADrica_cancioneril" title="es:Lírica cancioneril">
        <i>
         cancioneros
        </i>
       </a>
       , and in other medieval folk poetry; he also studied Spanish authors who had resided in Vienna, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Crist%C3%B3bal_de_Castillejo" title="Cristóbal de Castillejo">
        Cristóbal de Castillejo
       </a>
       . The Swiss scholar
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Morf" title="es:Heinrich Morf">
        Heinrich Morf
       </a>
       edited the medieval
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poema_de_Yu%C3%A7uf" title="es:Poema de Yuçuf">
        <i>
         Poema de José
        </i>
       </a>
       (Leipzig, 1883). The works of
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Vossler" title="Karl Vossler">
        Karl Vossler
       </a>
       and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Pfandl" title="es:Ludwig Pfandl">
        Ludwig Pfandl
       </a>
       on linguistic idealism and literary stylistics were widely read in Spain.
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón
       </a>
       studies in Germany were advanced by the editions of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Krenkel" title="es:Max Krenkel">
        Max Krenkel
       </a>
       . Other important authors were
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Emil_Gessner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Emil Gessner (page does not exist)">
        Emil Gessner
       </a>
       , who wrote
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=JgOpQAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Emil+Gessner+altleonesische&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=xtMATpaHIIO4tgfmo9GoBg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Das Altleonesische
        </i>
       </a>
       (Old Leonese) (Berlin 1867);
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Baist" title="es:Gottfried Baist">
        Gottfried Baist
       </a>
       , who produced an edition of
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Manuel,_Prince_of_Villena" title="Juan Manuel, Prince of Villena">
        Don Juan Manuel
       </a>
       's
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=HccGAQAAIAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=baist+juan+manuel&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=Asj_TZz6I4y3twfK7YjMDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Libro de la caza
        </i>
       </a>
       (1880), as well as the outline of a historical grammar of Spanish,
       <i>
        Die spanische Sprache
       </i>
       , in the encyclopedia of Romance philology published by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Gr%C3%B6ber" title="es:Gustav Gröber">
        Gustav Gröber
       </a>
       in 1888;
       <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schuchardt" title="Hugo Schuchardt">
        Hugo Schuchardt
       </a>
       , known for his study of Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Flamenco" title="Flamenco">
        flamenco
       </a>
       music,
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=xPh8QwAACAAJ&amp;dq=Die+cantes+flamencos+Schuchardt&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=T9cATrKMGpO5twfOvqn-DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwATgK" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Die cantes flamencos
        </i>
       </a>
       ; and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Armin_Gassner&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Armin Gassner (page does not exist)">
        Armin Gassner
       </a>
       , who wrote
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=ORUTAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Armin+Gassner+altspanische+verbum&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=vbgATqO1BMaztwes6f3zDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Das altspanische Verbum
        </i>
       </a>
       (the Old Spanish verb) (1897), as well as a work on Spanish syntax (1890) and several articles on Spanish pronouns between 1893 and 1895. And
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Moritz_Goldschmidt&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Moritz Goldschmidt (page does not exist)">
        Moritz Goldschmidt
       </a>
       wrote
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=BccAAAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Goldschmidt:+Zur+Kritik+der+altgermanischen&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=k8wATu-dJ4uatwe06sWMDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Zur Kritik der altgermanischen Elemente im Spanischen
        </i>
       </a>
       (Bonn 1887), the first work on the influences of the Germanic languages on Spanish.
      </p>

      <p>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Kr%C3%BCger" title="es:Fritz Krüger">
        Fritz Krüger
       </a>
       created the famous "Hamburg School" (not to be confused with the
       <a href="/wiki/Hamburger_Schule" title="Hamburger Schule">
        pop music genre
       </a>
       of the 1980s, of the same name), which applied the principles of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rter_und_Sachen" title="Wörter und Sachen">
         Wörter und Sachen
        </a>
       </i>
       movement, founded earlier by Swiss and German philologists such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Schuchardt" title="Hugo Schuchardt">
        Hugo Schuchardt
       </a>
       , Ruduolf Meringer, and
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_Meyer-L%C3%BCbke" title="Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke">
        Wilhelm Meyer-Lübke
       </a>
       , aptly combining dialectology and ethnography. Between 1926 and 1944 Krüger directed the journal
       <i>
        Volkstum und Kultur der Romane
       </i>
       and its supplements (1930–1945). It totaled 37 volumes, in which many of his students published their works. Krüger wrote mainly on Hispanic dialectology, especially on that of western Spain (Extremadura and Leon) and the Pyrenees, and he traveled on foot to gather the materials for his monumental work
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.google.com/search?tbm=bks&amp;tbo=1&amp;q=Die+Hochpyrenaen+Kr%C3%BCger&amp;btnG=Search+Books#hl=en&amp;tbo=1&amp;tbm=bks&amp;sa=X&amp;ei=cVUCTvOzAZTTgQf0lenpDQ&amp;ved=0CCkQBSgA&amp;q=Die+Hochpyren%C3%A4en+Kr%C3%BCger&amp;spell=1&amp;bav=on.2,or.r_gc.r_pw.&amp;fp=6455a52e8f48720&amp;biw=1024&amp;bih=605" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Die Hochpyrenäen
        </i>
       </a>
       , in which he meticulously described the landscape, flora, fauna, material culture, popular traditions and dialects of the Central Pyrenees. The versatile Romance scholar
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerhard_Rohlfs" title="es:Gerhard Rohlfs">
        Gerhard Rohlfs
       </a>
       investigated the languages and the dialects of both sides of the Pyrenees and their elements in common, as well as pre-Roman substrate languages of the Iberian Peninsula and
       <a href="/wiki/Guanche_language" title="Guanche language">
        Guanche
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Loanwords" title="Loanwords">
        loanwords
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The works of
       <a href="/wiki/Karl_Vossler" title="Karl Vossler">
        Karl Vossler
       </a>
       , founder of the linguistic school of
       <a href="/wiki/Idealism" title="Idealism">
        idealism
       </a>
       , include interpretations of Spanish literature and reflections on the Spanish culture. Vossler, along with
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmut_Hatzfeld" title="es:Helmut Hatzfeld">
        Helmut Hatzfeld
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_Spitzer" title="Leo Spitzer">
        Leo Spitzer
       </a>
       , began a new school of stylistics based on aesthetics, which focused on the means of expression of various authors.
      </p>
      <p>
       The early twentieth century marked the founding of two German institutions dedicated to Hispanic Studies (including Catalan, Galician and the Portuguese), in Hamburg and Berlin respectively. The University of Hamburg's Iberoamerikanisches Forschungsinstitut (Ibero-American Research Institute) was, from its founding in 1919 until the 1960s, almost the only German university institution dedicated to Spanish and other languages of the Iberian Peninsula. The Institute published the journal
       <i>
        Volkstum und Kultur der Romanen
       </i>
       (1926–1944), devoted specifically to works on dialectology and popular culture, following, in general, patterns of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/W%C3%B6rter_und_Sachen" title="Wörter und Sachen">
         Wörter und Sachen
        </a>
       </i>
       school. Meanwhile, Berlin's
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.iai.spk-berlin.de/" rel="nofollow">
        Ibero-Amerikanisches Institut
       </a>
       was founded in 1930. Today, the Berlin institute houses Europe's largest library dedicated to studies of Spain, Portugal, and Latin America, and to the languages of these countries (including Catalan, Galician, Portuguese, Basque, and the indigenous languages of the Americas). The Ibero-Amerikanisches Institut in Berlin is engaged in research in the fields of literature, linguistics, ethnology, history, and art history.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the
       <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">
        Nazi regime
       </a>
       (1933–1945), German philology went through a difficult time. Some Romanists, through their work, praised and propagated the Nazi ideology. Meanwhile, others lost their professorships or underwent anti-Jewish persecution (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Malkiel" title="Yakov Malkiel">
        Yakov Malkiel
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_Spitzer" title="Leo Spitzer">
        Leo Spitzer
       </a>
       , both of whom emigrated), by falling into disfavor with the regime or actively opposing it (for example
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmut_Hatzfeld" title="es:Helmut Hatzfeld">
        Helmut Hatzfeld
       </a>
       , who fled from Germany, and Werner Krauss (not to be confused with the
       <a href="/wiki/Werner_Krauss" title="Werner Krauss">
        actor
       </a>
       of the same name), who lost his academic position in 1935).
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_France_and_Belgium">
        Hispanism in France and Belgium
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hispanism in France and Belgium">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hispanism in France dates back to the powerful influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">
        Spanish Golden Age
       </a>
       literature on authors such as
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Corneille" title="Pierre Corneille">
        Pierre Corneille
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scarron" title="Paul Scarron">
        Paul Scarron
       </a>
       . Spanish influence was also brought to France by Spanish Protestants who fled the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">
        Inquisition
       </a>
       , many of whom took up teaching of the Spanish language. These included
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_de_Luna" title="es:Juan de Luna">
        Juan de Luna
       </a>
       , author of a sequel to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lazarillo_de_Tormes" title="Lazarillo de Tormes">
         Lazarillo de Tormes
        </a>
       </i>
       . N. Charpentier's
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=8WcocgAACAAJ&amp;dq=Charpentier+Parfaicte+m%C3%A9thode&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=c0X2TYr1Eaby0gGbkNnsDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=8&amp;ved=0CE0Q6AEwBw" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Parfaicte méthode pour entendre, écrire et parler la langue espagnole
        </i>
       </a>
       (Paris: Lucas Breyel, 1597) was supplemented by the grammar of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Oudin" title="fr:César Oudin">
        César Oudin
       </a>
       (also from 1597) that served as a model to those that were later written in French.
       <a href="/wiki/Michel_de_Montaigne" title="Michel de Montaigne">
        Michel de Montaigne
       </a>
       read the chroniclers of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_colonization_of_the_Americas" title="Spanish colonization of the Americas">
        Spanish Conquest
       </a>
       and had as one of his models
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_de_Guevara" title="Antonio de Guevara">
        Antonio de Guevara
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Moli%C3%A8re" title="Molière">
        Molière
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Alain-Ren%C3%A9_Lesage" title="Alain-René Lesage">
        Alain-René Lesage
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Claris_de_Florian" title="Jean-Pierre Claris de Florian">
        Jean-Pierre Claris de Florian
       </a>
       borrowed plots and characters from Spanish literature.
      </p>
      <p>
       French travelers to Spain in the 19th century who left written and artistic testimony include painters such as
       <a href="/wiki/Eug%C3%A8ne_Delacroix" title="Eugène Delacroix">
        Eugène Delacroix
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Henri_Regnault" title="Henri Regnault">
        Henri Regnault
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">

       </a>

       </a>

        Stendhal
       </a>
       ,

       </a>

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prosper_Merim%C3%A9e" title="Prosper Merimée">
        Prosper Merimée
       </a>
       ; and other writers, including
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Bourgoing" title="Jean-François de Bourgoing">
        Jean-François de Bourgoing
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Charles_Davillier" title="fr:Jean Charles Davillier">

       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Didier_(writer)" title="Charles Didier (writer)">

       </a>

       </a>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">
        Victor Hugo
       </a>
       was in Spain accompanying his father in 1811 and 1813. He was proud to call himself a "
       <a href="/wiki/Grandee" title="Grandee">
        grandee
       </a>
       of Spain", and he knew the language well. In his works there are numerous allusions to
       <a href="/wiki/El_Cid" title="El Cid">
        El Cid
       </a>
       and the works of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cervantes" title="Cervantes">

       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Prosper_M%C3%A9rim%C3%A9e" title="Prosper Mérimée">
        Prosper Mérimée
       </a>
       , even before his repeated trips to Spain, had shaped his intuitive vision of the country in his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=I00SMVParGEC&amp;pg=PA412&amp;dq=la+Famille+de+Carvajal&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=NFb2TcPRLOyx0AGy8qXsDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=6&amp;ved=0CEMQ6AEwBQ#v=onepage&amp;q=la%20Famille%20de%20Carvajal&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Théatre de Clara Gazul
        </i>
       </a>
       (1825) and in
       <i>
        La Famille de Carvajal
       </i>
       (1828). Mérimée made many trips between 1830 and 1846, making numerous friends, among them the
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%81ngel_de_Saavedra,_3rd_Duke_of_Rivas" title="Ángel de Saavedra, 3rd Duke of Rivas">
        Duke of Rivas
       </a>
       and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Alcal%C3%A1_Galiano" title="es:Antonio Alcalá Galiano">
        Antonio Alcalá Galiano
       </a>
       . He wrote
       <i>
        Lettres addressées d'Espagne au directeur de la Revue de Paris
       </i>
       , which are
       <a href="/wiki/Costumbrismo" title="Costumbrismo">
        <i>
         costumbrista
        </i>
       </a>
       sketches that feature the description of a bullfight. Mérimée's short novels
       <i>
        Les âmes du purgatoire
       </i>
       (1834) and
       <i>
        Carmen
       </i>
       (1845) are classic works on Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Honor%C3%A9_de_Balzac" title="Honoré de Balzac">
        Honoré de Balzac
       </a>
       was a friend of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_la_Rosa" title="Francisco Martínez de la Rosa">
        Francisco Martínez de la Rosa
       </a>

       <i>
        El Verdugo
       </i>
       (1829) to him. (And Martínez de la Rosa's play
       <i>

       </i>

      </p>
      <p>
       The Spanish
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
         romancero
        </a>
       </i>
       is represented in the French
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=7AA0AAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Biblioth%C3%A8que+universelle+de+romans&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=kmj2TdqmBabr0gGi25XuDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q=Biblioth%C3%A8que%20universelle%20de%20romans&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Bibliothèque universelle des romans
        </i>
       </a>
       , which was published in 1774.
       <a href="/wiki/Auguste_Creuz%C3%A9_de_Lesser" title="Auguste Creuzé de Lesser">
        Auguste Creuzé de Lesser
       </a>
       published
       <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
        folk ballads
       </a>
       about
       <a href="/wiki/El_Cid" title="El Cid">
        El Cid
       </a>
       in 1814, comparing them (as
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Herder" title="Johann Gottfried Herder">
        Johann Gottfried Herder
       </a>
       had done before him) with the Greek epic tradition, and these were reprinted in 1823 and 1836, providing much raw material to the French Romantic movement. The journalist and publisher
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel_Hugo" title="fr:Abel Hugo">
        Abel Hugo
       </a>
       , brother of
       <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hugo" title="Victor Hugo">
        Victor Hugo
       </a>
       , emphasized the literary value of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
        romancero
       </a>
       , translating and publishing a collection of
       <i>
        romances
       </i>
       and a history of King Rodrigo in 1821, and
       <i>
        Romances historiques traduits de l'espagnol
       </i>
       in 1822. He also composed a stage review,
       <i>
        Les français en Espagne
       </i>
       (1823), inspired by the time he spent with his brother at the Seminario de Nobles in Madrid during the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Bonaparte" title="Joseph Bonaparte">
        Joseph Bonaparte
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Germaine_de_Sta%C3%ABl" title="Germaine de Staël">
        Madame de Stäel
       </a>
       contributed to the knowledge of Spanish Literature in France (as she did also for German literature), which helped introduce Romanticism to the country. To this end she translated volume IV of
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Bouterwek" title="Friedrich Bouterwek">
        Friedrich Bouterwek
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Geschichte der Poesie und Beredsamkeit seit dem Ende des dreizehnten Jahrhunderts
       </i>
       in 1812 and gave it the title of
       <i>
        Histoire de la littérature espagnole
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish literature was also promoted to readers of French by the Swiss author
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Charles_L%C3%A9onard_de_Sismondi" title="Jean Charles Léonard de Sismondi">
        Simonde de Sismondi
       </a>
       with his study
       <i>
        De la littérature du midi de l'Europe
       </i>
       (1813).
      </p>
      <p>
       Also important for French access to Spanish poetry was the two-volume
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=6NUAAAAAMAAJ&amp;pg=PA17&amp;dq=maury+Espagne+po%C3%A9tique&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=hKr3TYLzAoKutwfv4qS-Cg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Espagne poétique
        </i>
       </a>
       (1826–27), an anthology of post-15th-century Castilian poetry translated by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juan_Mar%C3%ADa_Maury" title="es:Juan María Maury">
        Juan María Maury
       </a>
       . In Paris, the publishing house Baudry published many works by Spanish Romantics and even maintained a collection of "best" Spanish authors, edited by
       <a href="/wiki/Eugenio_de_Ochoa" title="Eugenio de Ochoa">
        Eugenio de Ochoa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Images of Spain were offered by the travel books of
       <a href="/wiki/Madame_d%27Aulnoy" title="Madame d'Aulnoy">
        Madame d'Aulnoy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_de_Rouvroy,_duc_de_Saint-Simon" title="Louis de Rouvroy, duc de Saint-Simon">
        Saint-Simon
       </a>
       , as well as the poet
       <a href="/wiki/Th%C3%A9ophile_Gautier" title="Théophile Gautier">
        Théophile Gautier
       </a>
       , who travelled in Spain in 1840 and published
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=7YIMAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=gautier+espagne&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=L4r3TdXOLJK-sQPJ5uzFBw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Voyage en Espagne
        </i>
       </a>
       (1845) and
       <i>
        Espagne
       </i>
       (1845). These works are so full of color and the sense of the picturesque that they even served as inspirations to Spanish writers themselves (poets such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Zorrilla_y_Moral" title="José Zorrilla y Moral">
        José Zorrilla
       </a>
       and narrators such as those of the
       <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2798" title="Generation of '98">
        Generation of '98
       </a>
       ), as well as to
       <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Dumas" title="Alexandre Dumas">
        Alexandre Dumas
       </a>
       , who attended the production of Zorrilla's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Juan_Tenorio" title="Don Juan Tenorio">
         Don Juan Tenorio
        </a>
       </i>
       in Madrid. Dumas wrote his somewhat negative views of his experience in his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=8wgUAAAAQAAJ&amp;dq=dumas%20impressions%20de%20voyage&amp;source=gbs_similarbooks" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Impressions de voyage
        </i>
       </a>
       (1847–1848). In his play
       <i>
        Don Juan de Marana
       </i>
       , Dumas revived the legend of
       <a href="/wiki/Don_Juan" title="Don Juan">
        Don Juan
       </a>
       , changing the ending after having seen Zorrilla's version in the edition of 1864.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Ren%C3%A9_de_Chateaubriand" title="François-René de Chateaubriand">
        François-René de Chateaubriand
       </a>
       traveled through Iberia in 1807 on his return trip from Jerusalem, and later took part in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hundred_Thousand_Sons_of_Saint_Louis" title="Hundred Thousand Sons of Saint Louis">
        French intervention in Spain
       </a>
       in 1823, which he describes in his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=VyAQAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=chateaubriand+m%C3%A9moires+d%27outre+tombe&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=j5r3TdvXL8na0QGqvsDICw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CDMQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Mémoires d'Outre-tombe
        </i>
       </a>
       (1849–1850). It may have been at that time that he began to write
       <i>
        Les aventures du dernier Abencerraje
       </i>
       (1826), which exalted Hispano-Arabic chivalry. Another work that was widely read was the
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=SV87AAAAcAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Lettres+d%27un+espagnol+viardot&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=FJ33TZiJDYbs0gHj4q2VCw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Lettres d'un espagnol
        </i>
       </a>
       (1826), by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Viardot" title="fr:Louis Viardot">
        Louis Viardot
       </a>
       , who visited Spain in 1823.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Stendhal" title="Stendhal">
        Stendhal
       </a>
       included a chapter "De l'Espagne" in his essay
       <i>
        De l'amour
       </i>
       (1822). Later (1834) he visited the country.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/George_Sand" title="George Sand">
        George Sand
       </a>
       spent the winter of 1837–1838 with
       <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Chopin" title="Frédéric Chopin">
        Chopin
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Majorca" title="Majorca">
        Majorca
       </a>
       , installed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Valldemossa_Charterhouse" title="Valldemossa Charterhouse">
        Valldemossa Charterhouse
       </a>
       . Their impressions are captured in Sand's
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=lBs6AAAAcAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=un+hiver+au+midi+George+Sand&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=qrv3TdbYI8nZ0QGLi6HOCw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Un hiver au midi de l'Europe
        </i>
       </a>
       (1842) and in Chopin's
       <i>
        Memoirs
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish classical painting exerted a strong influence on
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Manet" title="Édouard Manet">
        Manet
       </a>
       , and more recently, painters such as
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Picasso" title="Pablo Picasso">
        Picasso
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD" title="Salvador Dalí">
        Dalí
       </a>
       have influenced modern painting generally.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spanish music has influenced composers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Georges_Bizet" title="Georges Bizet">
        Georges Bizet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Emmanuel_Chabrier" title="Emmanuel Chabrier">
        Emmanuel Chabrier
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%89douard_Lalo" title="Édouard Lalo">
        Édouard Lalo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Maurice_Ravel" title="Maurice Ravel">
        Maurice Ravel
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Claude_Debussy" title="Claude Debussy">
        Claude Debussy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       At present the most important centers for Hispanism in France are at the Universities of
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Bordeaux" title="University of Bordeaux">
        Bordeaux
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toulouse" title="University of Toulouse">
        Toulouse
       </a>
       , and in Paris, with the Institut des Études Hispaniques, founded in 1912. Journals include
       <i>
        Bulletin Hispanique
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Prominent Hispanists in Belgium include
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Groult" title="es:Pierre Groult">
        Pierre Groult
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Lucien-Paul_Thomas&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Lucien-Paul Thomas (page does not exist)">
        Lucien-Paul Thomas
       </a>
       . Groult studied
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=pv4LAQAAIAAJ&amp;q=Pierre+Groult&amp;dq=Pierre+Groult&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=LMT3TYK3DoXZ0QGe1J3VCw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=6&amp;ved=0CD8Q6AEwBQ" rel="nofollow">
        Castilian mysticism
       </a>
       in relation to its Flemish counterpart.
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=rkohIvfer3cC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Jacques+de+Bruyne+comprehensive&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=QnwSTqP8DcTDgQfwrbjiDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         A Comprehensive Spanish Grammar
        </i>
       </a>
       (1995)—an English translation of the original Dutch
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=yEywL6D-aNUC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Spaanse+Spraakkunst+jacques+de+bruyne&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=mIASTt2lJ8PcgQfTvOT3Aw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCwQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Spaanse Spraakkunst
        </i>
       </a>
       (1979)—was written by Jacques de Bruyne, a professor at
       <a href="/wiki/Ghent_University" title="Ghent University">
        Ghent University
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_the_United_States_and_Canada">
        Hispanism in the United States and Canada
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Hispanism in the United States and Canada">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Hispanism in the United States has a long tradition and is highly developed. To a certain extent this is a result of the United States's own history, which is tied closely to the Spanish empire and its former colonies, especially
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       . Historically, many Americans have romanticized the Spanish legacy and given a privileged position to the Castilian language and culture, while simultaneously downplaying or rejecting the Latin American and Caribbean dialects and cultures of the Spanish-speaking areas of U.S. influence. There are now more than thirty-five million Spanish-speakers in the United States, making Spanish the second most spoken language in the country and Latinos the largest national minority. Spanish is used actively in some of the most populous states, including
       <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
        California
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       , and in large cities such as
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">
        New York
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">
        Miami
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio" title="San Antonio">
        San Antonio
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/San_Francisco" title="San Francisco">
        San Francisco
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Teachers_of_Spanish_and_Portuguese" title="American Association of Teachers of Spanish and Portuguese">
        American Association of Teachers of Spanish
       </a>
       was founded in 1917 and holds a biennial congress outside the United States;
       <i>
        Hispania
       </i>
       is the association's official publication. (Since 1944, it is the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Teachers_of_Spanish_and_Portuguese" title="American Association of Teachers of Spanish and Portuguese">
        American Association of Teachers of Spanish and Portuguese
       </a>
       .) The
       <a href="/wiki/North_American_Academy_of_the_Spanish_Language" title="North American Academy of the Spanish Language">
        North American Academy of the Spanish Language
       </a>
       brings together Spanish speakers in North America.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first academic professorships of Spanish at United States universities were established at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harvard" title="Harvard">
        Harvard
       </a>
       (1819),
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Virginia" title="University of Virginia">
        Virginia
       </a>
       (1825), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yale" title="Yale">
        Yale
       </a>
       (1826). The U.S. consul in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valencia,_Spain" title="Valencia, Spain">
        Valencia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Obadiah_Rich" title="Obadiah Rich">
        Obadiah Rich
       </a>
       , imported numerous books and valuable manuscripts that became the Obadiah Rich Collection at the
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_Public_Library" title="New York Public Library">
        New York Public Library
       </a>
       , and numerous magazines, especially the
       <i>
        North American Review
       </i>
       , published translations. Many travelers published their impressions on Spain, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Slidell_Mackenzie" title="Alexander Slidell Mackenzie">
        Alexander Slidell Mackenzie
       </a>
       (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=_SDdqFhkfBEC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=slidell+year+spain&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=zB_hTZmzLqXj0QHN7aiRBw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         A Year in Spain
        </i>
       </a>
       [1836] and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=wFLxtgAACAAJ&amp;dq=slidell+spain+revisited&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=eCThTarGGIXx0gHswYTfDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Spain Revisited
        </i>
       </a>
       [1836]). These were read by
       <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">
        Washington Irving
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Allan_Poe" title="Edgar Allan Poe">
        Edgar Allan Poe
       </a>
       , and other travelers like the Sephardic journalist
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mordecai_M._Noah" title="Mordecai M. Noah">
        Mordecai M. Noah
       </a>
       and the diplomat
       <a href="/wiki/Caleb_Cushing" title="Caleb Cushing">
        Caleb Cushing
       </a>
       and his wife. Poe studied Spanish at the University of Virginia and some of his stories have Spanish settings. He also wrote scholarly articles on
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_literature" title="Spanish literature">
        Spanish literature
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The beginnings of Hispanism itself are found in the works of
       <a href="/wiki/Washington_Irving" title="Washington Irving">
        Washington Irving
       </a>
       , who met
       <a href="/wiki/Leandro_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Morat%C3%ADn" title="Leandro Fernández de Moratín">
        Leandro Fernández de Moratín
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Bordeaux" title="Bordeaux">
        Bordeaux
       </a>
       in 1825 and was in Spain in 1826 (when he frequented the social gatherings of another American,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Sarah_Maria_Theresa_McKean&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Sarah Maria Theresa McKean (page does not exist)">
        Sarah Maria Theresa McKean
       </a>
       (1780–1841), the marquise widow of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlos_Mart%C3%ADnez_de_Irujo_y_Tac%C3%B3n" title="Carlos Martínez de Irujo y Tacón">
        Casa Irujo
       </a>
       ), as well as in 1829. He went on to become ambassador between 1842 and 1846. Irving studied in Spanish libraries and met
       <a href="/wiki/Mart%C3%ADn_Fern%C3%A1ndez_de_Navarrete" title="Martín Fernández de Navarrete">
        Martín Fernández de Navarrete
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       , using one of the latter's works as a source for his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_History_of_the_Life_and_Voyages_of_Christopher_Columbus" title="A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus">
         A History of the Life and Voyages of Christopher Columbus
        </a>
       </i>
       (1828), and made friends and corresponded with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cecilia_B%C3%B6hl_de_Faber" title="Cecilia Böhl de Faber">
        Cecilia Böhl de Faber
       </a>
       , from where a mutual influence was born. His Romantic interest in Arab topics shaped his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=ZWUqAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Chronicle+of+the+Conquest+of+Granada+irving&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=vsjuTayQN5Gq0AGcrLnCCQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Chronicle of the Conquest of Granada
        </i>
       </a>
       (1829) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=y0EXAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=alhambra+washington+irving&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=FtLuTbiDNIzQsAP09ej_Bg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CDAQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Alhambra
        </i>
       </a>
       (1832). McKean's social gatherings were also attended by the children of the Bostonian of Irish origin John Montgomery, who was the consul of the United States in
       <a href="/wiki/Alicante" title="Alicante">
        Alicante
       </a>
       , and particularly by the Spanish-born writer
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Washington_Montgomery" title="es:George Washington Montgomery">
        George Washington Montgomery
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Wadsworth_Longfellow" title="Henry Wadsworth Longfellow">
        Henry Wadsworth Longfellow
       </a>
       's translations of Spanish classics also form part of the history of North American Hispanism; he went through
       <a href="/wiki/Madrid" title="Madrid">
        Madrid
       </a>
       in 1829 expressing his impressions in his letters, a diary and in
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=ZY9qU-4uJuEC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=longfellow+outre-mer&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=BIn1Td7_OIbh0QHu2MTsDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=3&amp;ved=0CDQQ6AEwAg#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Outre-Mer
        </i>
       </a>
       (1833–1834). A good connoisseur of the classics, Longfellow translated
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Manrique" title="Jorge Manrique">
        Jorge Manrique
       </a>
       's
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=6L9jmgwz7QcC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=longfellow+coplas+manrique&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=K-zkTZaWOoecgQeIw9TFBg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=book-thumbnail&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CDYQ6wEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        couplets
       </a>
       . In order to fulfill his duties as a Spanish professor, he composed his
       <i>
        Spanish Novels
       </i>
       (1830), which are story adaptations of Irving and published several essays on Spanish literature and a drama, including
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Qr89AAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Longfellow+the+spanish+student&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=mRv2TbPqJ-Hg0QGqqZzwDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         The Spanish Student
        </i>
       </a>
       (1842), where he imitates those of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">
        Spanish Golden Age
       </a>
       . In his anthology
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=rkeuNYqO7wUC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=longfellow+Poets+and+Poetry+of+Europe&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=fR72TZiIC6-z0AHO0qXrDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         The Poets and Poetry of Europe
        </i>
       </a>
       (1845) he includes the works of many Spanish poets.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Cullen_Bryant" title="William Cullen Bryant">
        William Cullen Bryant
       </a>
       translated
       <a href="/wiki/Morisco" title="Morisco">
        Morisco
       </a>
       romances and composed the poems "The Spanish Revolution" (1808) and "Cervantes" (1878). He was linked in New York to Spaniards and, as director of the
       <i>
        Evening Post
       </i>
       , included many articles on Iberian subjects in the magazine. He was in Spain in 1847, and narrated his impressions in
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=f0laAAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=cullen+bryant+letters+of+a+traveller&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ix_2TZa0Iaux0AH3o_SnCQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Letters of a traveller
        </i>
       </a>
       (1850–1857). In Madrid he met
       <a href="/wiki/Carolina_Coronado" title="Carolina Coronado">
        Carolina Coronado
       </a>
       , translating into English her poem "The Lost Bird" and novel
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=XuQMAQAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=coronado+jarilla&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=3x_2TYy4OaXu0gHqu_HrDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Jarilla
        </i>
       </a>
       , both of which were published in the
       <i>
        Evening Post
       </i>
       . But the most important group of Spanish scholars was one from Boston. The work of
       <a href="/wiki/George_Ticknor" title="George Ticknor">
        George Ticknor
       </a>
       , a professor of Spanish at Harvard who wrote
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=fbMGQNXUBooC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=ticknor+history+spanish+literature&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=FyjhTeSECOrt0gGv5cmtBw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         History of Spanish Literature
        </i>
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/William_H._Prescott" title="William H. Prescott">
        William H. Prescott
       </a>
       , who wrote historical works on the conquest of America, are without doubt contributions of the first order. Ticknor was a friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Pascual_de_Gayangos_y_Arce" title="Pascual de Gayangos y Arce">
        Pascual de Gayangos y Arce
       </a>
       , whom he met in
       <a href="/wiki/London" title="London">
        London
       </a>
       , and visited
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       in 1818, describing his impressions in
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=wSAWO9MCMr8C&amp;pg=PA402&amp;dq=ticknor+life+letters+journal&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=BCH2TcWDPMXa0QGBw-XrDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q=ticknor%20life%20letters%20journal&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Life, letters and journals
        </i>
       </a>
       (1876). In spite of significant difficulties with his vision, Prescott composed histories of the conquest of
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=nUbVAAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=prescott+history+conquest+mexico&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=byH2TZ2tMK-x0AHdqeHuDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        Mexico
       </a>
       and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=XDx8ojIE7T8C&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=prescott+history+conquest+peru&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=oCH2TafwCMf50gGCmOntDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        Peru
       </a>
       , as well as a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=a8MCAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA345&amp;dq=prescott+catholic+monarchs&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=0SH2TciNOoj00gG384juDA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC8Q6AEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        history of the reign of the Catholic Monarchs
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the United States there are important societies that are dedicated to the study, conservation and spread of Spanish culture, of which the
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_Society_of_America" title="Hispanic Society of America">
        Hispanic Society of America
       </a>
       is the best known. There are also libraries specialized in Hispanic matter, including ones at Tulane University, New Orleans. Important journals include
       <i>
        Hispanic Review
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Revista de las Españas
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Nueva Revista de Filología Hispánica
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Hispania
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Dieciocho
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Revista Hispánica Moderna
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Cervantes
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Russia">
        Hispanism in Russia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Russia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The history of Hispanism in
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       —before, during, and after the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       period—is long and deep, and it even survived the rupture of relations between Russia and Spain caused by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">
        Spanish Civil War
       </a>
       . This history started in the 18th century, and in the 19th century the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Literary_realism" title="Literary realism">
        realist
       </a>
       novelists (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Fyodor_Dostoyevsky" title="Fyodor Dostoyevsky">
        Dostoyevsky
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Turgenev" title="Ivan Turgenev">
        Turgenev
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leon_Tolstoy" title="Leon Tolstoy">
        Tolstoy
       </a>
       ) was profound.
      </p>
      <p>
       Romantic travellers, such as
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serg%C3%A9i_Sobolevski" title="es:Sergéi Sobolevski">
        Sergei Sobolevski
       </a>
       , accumulated great libraries of books in Spanish and helped Spanish writers who visited Russia, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Valera_y_Alcal%C3%A1-Galiano" title="Juan Valera y Alcalá-Galiano">
        Juan Valera
       </a>
       . The Russian realist dramatist
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Ostrovsky" title="Alexander Ostrovsky">
        Alexander Ostrovsky
       </a>
       translated the theater of
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón
       </a>
       and wrote texts on Spanish Golden Age theater.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yevgeni_Salias_de_Tournemir" title="es:Yevgeni Salias de Tournemir">
        Yevgeni Salias de Tournemir
       </a>
       visited Spain and published
       <i>
        Apuntes de viaje por España
       </i>
       (1874), shortly before
       <a href="/wiki/Emilio_Castelar" title="Emilio Castelar">
        Emilio Castelar
       </a>
       published his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=AXVBAAAAIAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         La Rusia contemporánea
        </i>
       </a>
       (1881).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Russian Association of Hispanists, founded in 1994, is currently supported by the
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">
        Russian Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       . The field of Spanish-American studies has undergone a great increase recently. A survey in 2003 revealed that there are at least four thousand students of Spanish in Russian universities.
      </p>
      <p>
       Twentieth-century Spanish scholars include
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serg%C3%A9i_Goncharenko" title="es:Sergéi Goncharenko">
        Sergei Goncharenko
       </a>
       (mentor of a whole generation of Spanish scholars), Victor Andreyev, Vladimir Vasiliev, Natalia Miod, Svetlana Piskunova, and Vsevolod Bagno (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=681dAAAAMAAJ&amp;q=Vsevolod+Bagno+quijote&amp;dq=Vsevolod+Bagno+quijote&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=C1QFTpjZPMyhtwfwn6DVDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CD4Q6AEwBA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         El Quijote vivido por los rusos
        </i>
       </a>
       ). Recently, a Russian Hernandian Circle was founded, devoted to studying the work of
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_Hern%C3%A1ndez" title="Miguel Hernández">
        Miguel Hernández
       </a>
       , who visited the USSR in September 1937.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Poland">
        Hispanism in Poland
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Poland">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Records of visits to Spain by Poles begin in the Middle Ages, with pilgrimages to
       <a href="/wiki/Santiago_de_Compostela" title="Santiago de Compostela">
        Santiago de Compostela
       </a>
       . According to one estimate, more than 100 Poles made the pilgrimage during that era.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 16th century, the humanist
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Dantiscus" title="Johannes Dantiscus">
        Jan Dantyszek
       </a>
       (1485–1548), ambassador of King
       <a href="/wiki/Sigismund_I_the_Old" title="Sigismund I the Old">
        Sigismund I the Old
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_V,_Holy_Roman_Emperor" title="Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor">
        Charles V, Holy Roman Emperor
       </a>
       , traveled to the Iberian Peninsula three times and remained there for nearly ten years, becoming friends with outstanding figures such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hern%C3%A1n_Cort%C3%A9s" title="Hernán Cortés">
        Hernán Cortés
       </a>
       and leaving letters of his travels. The bishop
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piotr_Dunin-Wolski" title="pl:Piotr Dunin-Wolski">
        Piotr Dunin-Wolski
       </a>
       took 300 Spanish books to Poland, and these were added to the
       <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_Library" title="Jagiellonian Library">
        Jagiellonian Library
       </a>
       of Kraków under the name of
       <i>
        Bibliotheca Volsciana
       </i>
       . Several professors from Spain worked in the Academy of Kraków (today known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Jagiellonian_University" title="Jagiellonian University">
        Jagiellonian University
       </a>
       ), including the Sevillian Garsías Cuadras and the Aragonese jurist
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pedro_Ruiz_de_Moros" title="es:Pedro Ruiz de Moros">
        Pedro Ruiz de Moros
       </a>
       (1506–1571), known in Poland as Roizjusz, who mainly wrote in Latin and was adviser to the king. The
       <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">
        Society of Jesus
       </a>
       was active in Poland, promoting not only Spanish ideas of theology, but also Spanish theater, which they considered a teaching tool.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the 16th century, the travelers
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_%C5%81aski" title="pl:Stanisław Łaski">
        Stanisław Łaski
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrzej_T%C4%99czy%C5%84ski" title="pl:Andrzej Tęczyński">
        Andrzej Tęczyński
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Tarnowski" title="pl:Jan Tarnowski">
        Jan Tarnowski
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanis%C5%82aw_Radziwi%C5%82%C5%82_(1559-1599)" title="pl:Stanisław Radziwiłł (1559-1599)">
        Stanisław Radziwiłł
       </a>
       , and Szymon Babiogórski visited Spain, among others. An anonymous traveler who arrived in Barcelona in August 1595 left an account of his impressions in a manuscript called
       <i>
        Diariusz z peregrynacji włoskiej, hiszpańskiej, portugalskiej
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        Diary of the Italian, Spanish and Portuguese Pilgrimages
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 17th century, the Polish nobleman
       <a href="/wiki/Jakub_Sobieski" title="Jakub Sobieski">
        Jakub Sobieski
       </a>
       made the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Way_of_St._James" title="Way of St. James">
        pilgrimage to Santiago de Compostela
       </a>
       and wrote an account of his journey. In the years 1674–1675, Canon
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrzej_Chryzostom_Za%C5%82uski" title="pl:Andrzej Chryzostom Załuski">
        Andrzej Chryzostom Załuski
       </a>
       , Jerzy Radziwiłł, and Stanisław Radziwiłł visited Spain, and all left written testimony of their travels.
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern Polish Hispanic Studies begin with the Romantic poet
       <a href="/wiki/Adam_Mickiewicz" title="Adam Mickiewicz">
        Adam Mickiewicz
       </a>
       . He was followed in the 19th century by
       <a href="/wiki/Joachim_Lelewel" title="Joachim Lelewel">
        Joachim Lelewel
       </a>
       , Wojciech Dzieduszycki,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Rettel" title="pl:Leonard Rettel">
        Leonard Rettel
       </a>
       , and Julian Adolf Swiecicki.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karol_Dembowski" title="es:Karol Dembowski">
        Karol Dembowski
       </a>
       wrote, in French, a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Q_cRRQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Karol+Dembowski&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=1IEHTvzMG6H50gH-x5z3Cg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=3&amp;ved=0CDkQ6AEwAg" rel="nofollow">
        book on his travels
       </a>
       in Spain and Portugal during
       <a href="/wiki/Carlist_Wars" title="Carlist Wars">
        First Carlist War
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_Rozanski" title="es:Félix Rozanski">
        Felix Rozanski
       </a>
       , Edward Porebowicz and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygmunt_Czerny" title="pl:Zygmunt Czerny">
        Zygmunt Czerny
       </a>
       were enthusiastic translators who taught in Poland at that time. Maria Strzałkowa wrote the first
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=VCJcHQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Maria+Strza%C5%82kowa&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=yYMHTuW8EKfk0QG5rvXdCw&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=10&amp;ved=0CE8Q6AEwCTgK" rel="nofollow">
        outline of history of Spanish literature
       </a>
       in Polish. Other important translators include Kazimierz Zawanowski, Zofia Szleyen, Kalina Wojciechowska, and Zofia Chądzyńska.
      </p>
      <p>
       The poet and Hispanist
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florian_Smieja" title="es:Florian Smieja">
        Florian Śmieja
       </a>
       taught Spanish and Spanish American literature in London, Ontario. In 1971 the first professorship of Hispanic Studies not subordinate to a department of Romance literature was created at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Warsaw" title="University of Warsaw">
        University of Warsaw
       </a>
       , and in the following year a degree program in Hispanic Studies was instituted there. Today it is called the Institute of Iberian and Latin American Studies. Those who have taught in it include Urszula Aszyk-Bangs, M.-Pierrette Malcuzynski (1948–2004), Robert Mansberger Amorós, Víctor Manuel Ferreras, and Carlos Marrodán Casas. In Kraków the first National Symposium of Spanish Scholars was held in 1985. The historians
       <a href="/wiki/Janusz_Tazbir" title="Janusz Tazbir">
        Janusz Tazbir
       </a>
       and Jan Kienewicz wrote on Spanish themes, as did the literary scholars Gabriela Makowiecka, Henryk Ziomek, Beata Baczynska,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pl.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florian_%C5%9Amieja" title="pl:Florian Śmieja">
        Florian Śmieja
       </a>
       , Piotr Sawicki, and Kazimierz Sabik. Grzegorz Bak studied the image of Spain in 19th-century Polish literature.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Portugal_and_Brazil">
        Hispanism in Portugal and Brazil
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Portugal and Brazil">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Brazil">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The integration of Brazil into
       <a href="/wiki/Mercosur" title="Mercosur">
        Mercosur
       </a>
       in 1991 created a need for closer relations between Brazil and the Hispanic world, as well as better knowledge of the Spanish language within Brazil. For this reason, Brazil has promoted the inclusion of Spanish as a required subject in the country's education system. A large core of Spanish scholars formed at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="University of São Paulo">
        University of São Paulo
       </a>
       , including
       <a class="extiw" href="https://pt.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidelino_de_Figueiredo" title="pt:Fidelino de Figueiredo">
        Fidelino de Figueiredo
       </a>
       , Luis Sánchez y Fernández, and José Lodeiro. The year 1991 also marks the creation of the
       <i>
        Anuario Brasileño de Estudios Hispánicos
       </i>
       , whose
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.mec.es/sgci/br/es/publicaciones/anuario/abeh2000s.pdf" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Suplemento: El hispanismo en Brasil
        </i>
        (2000)
       </a>
       , traces the history of Hispanic Studies in the country. In 2000 the first Congresso Brasileiro de Hispanistas took place, and its proceedings were published under the title
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=g9GBAAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Hispanismo+2000&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=BKYHTsnZJ66v0AHmv7H5Cg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC8Q6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Hispanismo 2000
        </i>
       </a>
       . At that meeting, the Associação Brasileira de Hispanistas was established. The organization's second congress took place in 2002, and since then it has been held every two years.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Portugal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Compared to Brazil, Portugal has shown less interest in Hispanism; it was not until 2005 that a national association for it was founded. Portuguese activities in this field are mostly of a
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">
        comparatist
       </a>
       nature and focus on Luso-Spanish topics, partly because of academic and administrative reasons. The journal
       <a class="external text" href="http://web.letras.up.pt/ibericos/peninsulaesp.htm" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Península
        </i>
       </a>
       is one of the most important Hispanist journals in the country. Portuguese Hispanism appears somewhat limited, and to an extent there is a mutual distrust between the two cultures, motivated by a history of conflicts and rivalry. Nevertheless, Portuguese writers of the Renaissance—such as the dramatist
       <a href="/wiki/Gil_Vicente" title="Gil Vicente">
        Gil Vicente
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jorge_de_Montemayor" title="Jorge de Montemayor">
        Jorge de Montemayor
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francisco_S%C3%A1_de_Miranda" title="Francisco Sá de Miranda">
        Francisco Sá de Miranda
       </a>
       , and the historian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dom_Francisco_Manuel_de_Mello" title="Dom Francisco Manuel de Mello">
        Francisco Manuel de Mello
       </a>
       —wrote in both Spanish and Portuguese.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Italy">
        Hispanism in Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The cultural relationship between Spain and Italy developed early in the Middle Ages, especially centered in
       <a href="/wiki/Naples" title="Naples">
        Naples
       </a>
       through the relation that it had with the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_of_Aragon" title="Crown of Aragon">
        Crown of Aragon
       </a>
       and Sicily, and intensified during the Spanish Pre-Renaissance and Renaissance through Castile.
       <a href="/wiki/Garcilaso_de_la_Vega_(poet)" title="Garcilaso de la Vega (poet)">
        Garcilaso de la Vega
       </a>
       engaged members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Accademia_Pontaniana" title="Accademia Pontaniana">
        Accademia Pontaniana
       </a>
       and introduced the
       <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
        Petrarchian
       </a>
       metrical style and themes to Spanish lyric poetry. This close relation extended throughout the periods of
       <a href="/wiki/Mannerism" title="Mannerism">
        Mannerism
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Baroque" title="Baroque">
        Baroque
       </a>
       in the 16th and 17th centuries. In the 18th century the poet Giambattista Conti (1741–1820) was perhaps the foremost Spanish scholar, translator and anthologist of Europe. Dramatist, critic, and theater historiographer
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pietro_Napoli_Signorelli" title="es:Pietro Napoli Signorelli">
        Pietro Napoli Signorelli
       </a>
       (1731–1815) defended Spanish literature against critics such as
       <a href="/wiki/Girolamo_Tiraboschi" title="Girolamo Tiraboschi">
        Girolamo Tiraboschi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saverio_Bettinelli" title="Saverio Bettinelli">
        Saverio Bettinelli
       </a>
       , who accused it of "bad taste", "corruption", and "barbarism".
       <a href="/wiki/Giacomo_Casanova" title="Giacomo Casanova">
        Giacomo Casanova
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Marc%27Antonio_Baretti" title="Giuseppe Marc'Antonio Baretti">
        Giuseppe Baretti
       </a>
       traveled throughout Spain, leaving interesting descriptions of their experiences: Baretti was fluent in Spanish. The critic Guido Bellico was in the
       <a href="/wiki/Colegio_Imperial_de_Madrid" title="Colegio Imperial de Madrid">
        Reales Estudios de San Isidro
       </a>
       with the eminent Arabist Mariano Pizzi. Among other prominent Italian Hispanists were Leonardo Capitanacci, Ignazio Gajone, Placido Bordoni, Giacinto Ceruti, Francesco Pesaro, Giuseppe Olivieri, Giovanni Querini and Marco Zeno.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 19th century, Italian Romanticism took great interest in the Spanish
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
         romancero
        </a>
       </i>
       , with translations by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=3w0WAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Giovanni+Berchet+1837&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=7xAIToY5kKW3B5H6xLsN&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Berchet" title="Giovanni Berchet">
        Giovanni Berchet
       </a>
       in 1837 and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=uH0SHQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Pietro+Monti+1855&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=hhAITp7KMIvBtgfX66DfDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CDAQ6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        Pietro Monti
       </a>
       in 1855.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmondo_de_Amicis" title="Edmondo de Amicis">
        Edmondo de Amicis
       </a>
       traveled throughout Spain and wrote a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=fg5FAAAAYAAJ&amp;pg=PA3&amp;dq=Edmundo+de+Amicis&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=XBEITq7pB4ugtweZopTRDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=7&amp;ved=0CFIQ6AEwBg#v=onepage&amp;q=Edmundo%20de%20Amicis&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        book
       </a>
       of his impressions.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonio_Restori" title="es:Antonio Restori">
        Antonio Restori
       </a>
       (1859–1928), a professor at the Universities of Messina and of Genoa, published some works of Lope de Vega and dedicated his
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=1wIzAAAAIAAJ&amp;dq=Saggi+di+bibliografia+teatrale+spagnuola&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=CxIITr6qKMebtwf6hMjlDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCwQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Saggi di bibliografia teatrale spagnuola
        </i>
       </a>
       (1927) to the bibliography of the Spanish theater; he also wrote
       <i>
        Il Cid, studio storico-critico
       </i>
       (1881) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=reQPAAAAQAAJ&amp;pg=PA256&amp;dq=La+gesta+del+Cid+Restori&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ohIITuqvEsKjtge_qsxd&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CDAQ6AEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q=La%20gesta%20del%20Cid%20Restori&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Le gesta del Cid
        </i>
       </a>
       (1890).
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernardo_Sanvisenti" title="es:Bernardo Sanvisenti">
        Bernardo Sanvisenti
       </a>
       , a professor of Spanish language and literature at the University of Milan, wrote
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=lccAAAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Manuale+di+letteratura+spagnuola+1907&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=LBMITu2EFYGbtwfpp9zJDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Manuale di letteratura spagnuola
        </i>
       </a>
       (1907), as well as a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=0HQNAAAAIAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=I+primi+influssi+di+Dante,+del+Petrarca&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=6xMITvG9OMu3twe36qG4DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCoQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        study
       </a>
       (1902) on the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Boccaccio" title="Giovanni Boccaccio">
        Boccaccio
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
        Petrarch
       </a>
       in Spanish literature.
      </p>
      <p>
       Italian Hispanism arose from three sources, already identifiable in the 19th century. The first of these was the Spanish hegemonic presence in the Italian peninsula, which sparked interest in the study of Spain and in the creation of works about Spain. Secondly, Italian Hispanism was encouraged by a
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative_literature" title="Comparative literature">
        comparatist
       </a>
       approach, and in fact the first Italian studies on literature in Spanish were of a comparative nature, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Benedetto_Croce" title="Benedetto Croce">
        Benedetto Croce
       </a>
       's
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=p1poAAAAMAAJ&amp;q=La+Spagna+nella+vita+italiana+durante+la+Rinascenza+1907&amp;dq=La+Spagna+nella+vita+italiana+durante+la+Rinascenza+1907&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=MpIITprxH5PUgAfdobncDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CD4Q6AEwBA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         La Spagna nella vita italiana durante la Rinascenza
        </i>
       </a>
       (1907) and the works of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arturo_Farinelli" title="es:Arturo Farinelli">
        Arturo Farinelli
       </a>
       and Bernardino Sanvisenti, which were dedicated to the relationships between Spain and Italy, Italy and Germany, and Spain and Germany. Thirdly, the development of Italian Hispanism was supported by Romance philology, especially through the works of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mario_Casella" title="es:Mario Casella">
        Mario Casella
       </a>
       (author of
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=KAmyAAAAMAAJ&amp;q=Cervantes+il+Chisciotte%27%27+1938&amp;dq=Cervantes+il+Chisciotte%27%27+1938&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=bZQIToeRF5K_gQeO48DWDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CC4Q6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Cervantes: Il Chisciotte
        </i>
       </a>
       [1938]),
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ezio_Levi" title="es:Ezio Levi">
        Ezio Levi
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvatore_Battaglia" title="es:Salvatore Battaglia">
        Salvatore Battaglia
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Maria_Bertini" title="es:Giovanni Maria Bertini">
        Giovanni Maria Bertini
       </a>
       (translator of Spanish modern poetry, especially the poems of
       <a href="/wiki/Federico_Garc%C3%ADa_Lorca" title="Federico García Lorca">
        Lorca
       </a>
       ).
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cesare_de_Lollis" title="es:Cesare de Lollis">
        Cesare de Lollis
       </a>
       also made important contributions to Cervantes studies.
      </p>
      <p>
       The field of modern Hispanic Studies originated in 1945, with the trio of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oreste_Macr%C3%AC" title="es:Oreste Macrì">
        Oreste Macrì
       </a>
       (editor of works of
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Machado" title="Antonio Machado">
        Antonio Machado
       </a>
       and of Fray
       <a href="/wiki/Luis_de_Le%C3%B3n" title="Luis de León">
        Luis de León
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guido_Mancini" title="es:Guido Mancini">
        Guido Mancini
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franco_Meregalli" title="es:Franco Meregalli">
        Franco Meregalli
       </a>
       . Eventually Spanish-American studies emerged as an area of independent of the literature of Spain. Between 1960 and 1970 the first professorships of Spanish-American language and literature were created, pioneered by Giovanni Meo Zilio, who occupied the first chair of that sort created at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florence" title="University of Florence">
        University of Florence
       </a>
       in 1968. He was followed by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Bellini" title="es:Giuseppe Bellini">
        Giuseppe Bellini
       </a>
       (historian of Spanish-American literature, translator of
       <a href="/wiki/Pablo_Neruda" title="Pablo Neruda">
        Pablo Neruda
       </a>
       , and student of
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_%C3%81ngel_Asturias" title="Miguel Ángel Asturias">
        Miguel Ángel Asturias
       </a>
       );
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roberto_Paoli" title="es:Roberto Paoli">
        Roberto Paoli
       </a>
       (Peruvianist and translator of
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Vallejo" title="César Vallejo">
        César Vallejo
       </a>
       ); and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dar%C3%ADo_Puccini" title="es:Darío Puccini">
        Dario Puccini
       </a>
       (student of the lyric poetry of Sor
       <a href="/wiki/Juana_In%C3%A9s_de_la_Cruz" title="Juana Inés de la Cruz">
        Juana Inés de la Cruz
       </a>
       , as well as that of the 20th century).
      </p>
      <p>

       <a class="external text" href="http://www.aispi.it/" rel="nofollow">
        Association of Italian Hispanists
       </a>
       (AISPI) was created in May 1973 and has held numerous congresses almost annually since then. Italian Hispanists include

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Israel">
        Hispanism in Israel
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Israel">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At the time of its founding in 1948, the modern state of Israel already included a substantial Spanish-speaking community. Their language,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judeo-Spanish" title="Judeo-Spanish">
        Judeo-Spanish
       </a>
       , was derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_Spanish" title="Old Spanish">
        Old Spanish
       </a>
       along a path of development that diverged from that of the Spanish of Spain and its empire, beginning in 1492, when the
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Jews
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Alhambra_Decree" title="Alhambra Decree">
        expelled
       </a>
       from Spain. Between the 16th and 20th centuries many of them lived in the old
       <a href="/wiki/Ottoman_Empire" title="Ottoman Empire">
        Ottoman Empire
       </a>
       and North Africa. There are some 100,000 speakers of Judeo-Spanish in Israel today.
      </p>
      <p>
       At present there are several Israeli media outlets in (standard Castilian) Spanish, some of which have a long history. The newsweekly
       <i>
        Aurora
       </i>
       , for example, was founded in the late 1960s, and today it also has an
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.aurora-israel.co.il/" rel="nofollow">
        online edition
       </a>
       . Israel has at least three radio stations that broadcast in Spanish.
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern Israeli Hispanists include
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Miklos_Stern" title="es:Samuel Miklos Stern">
        Samuel Miklos Stern
       </a>
       (the discoverer of the Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Kharja" title="Kharja">
        kharjas
       </a>
       and a student of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Inquisition" title="Spanish Inquisition">
        Spanish Inquisition
       </a>
       ), professor
       <a href="/wiki/Benzion_Netanyahu" title="Benzion Netanyahu">
        Benzion Netanyahu
       </a>
       , and Haim Beinart. Other Israeli scholars have studied the literature and history of Spain, frequently influenced by the theses of
       <a href="/wiki/Am%C3%A9rico_Castro" title="Américo Castro">
        Américo Castro
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Don_Quixote" title="Don Quixote">
         Don Quixote
        </a>
       </i>
       has been translated into Hebrew twice, first by Natan Bistritzky and Nahman Bialik (Jerusalem, Sifriat Poalim, 1958), and later (Tel Aviv: Hakibutz Hameuchad, 1994) by Beatriz Skroisky-Landau and Luis Landau, the latter a professor in the Department of Hebrew Literature at
       <a href="/wiki/Ben-Gurion_University_of_the_Negev" title="Ben-Gurion University of the Negev">
        Ben-Gurion University of the Negev
       </a>
       and author of
       <i>
        Cervantes and the Jews
       </i>
       (Beer Sheva: Ben-Gurion University Press, 2002). The historian
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yosef_Kaplan" title="es:Yosef Kaplan">
        Yosef Kaplan
       </a>
       has written numerous works and has translated
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Cardoso" title="Isaac Cardoso">
        Isaac Cardoso
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Las excelencias y calumnias de los hebreos
       </i>
       into Hebrew. The
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.ahisrael.org/SobreAHI.htm" rel="nofollow">
        Asociación de Hispanistas de Israel
       </a>
       was created on 21 June 2007 at the
       <a class="external text" href="http://telaviv.cervantes.es/es/default.shtm" rel="nofollow">
        Instituto Cervantes de Tel Aviv
       </a>
       , consisting of over thirty professors, researchers and intellectuals linked to the languages, literatures, history and cultures of Spain, Portugal, Latin America and the Judeo-Spanish Sephardic world. Its first meeting was convened by professors
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruth_Fine" title="es:Ruth Fine">
        Ruth Fine
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem" title="Hebrew University of Jerusalem">
        Hebrew University of Jerusalem
       </a>
       ), who was appointed the first president of the association; Raanán Rein (
       <a href="/wiki/Tel_Aviv_University" title="Tel Aviv University">
        Tel Aviv University
       </a>
       ); Aviva Dorón (
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Haifa" title="University of Haifa">
        University of Haifa
       </a>
       ); and Tamar Alexander (
       <a href="/wiki/Ben-Gurion_University_of_the_Negev" title="Ben-Gurion University of the Negev">
        Ben-Gurion University of the Negev
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_the_Arab_world">
        Hispanism in the Arab world
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Hispanism in the Arab world">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Spain's links with the Arab world began in the Middle Ages with the
       <a href="/wiki/Umayyad_conquest_of_Hispania" title="Umayyad conquest of Hispania">
        Moorish conquest of the Iberian Peninsula
       </a>
       . Arabic-speaking
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Moorish kingdoms
       </a>
       were present in Spain until 1492, when the
       <a href="/wiki/Reconquista" title="Reconquista">
        Reconquista
       </a>
       defeated the
       <a href="/wiki/Emirate_of_Granada" title="Emirate of Granada">
        Emirate of Granada
       </a>
       . Many Moors remained in Spain until their final expulsion in 1609. The
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Empire" title="Spanish Empire">
        Spanish Empire
       </a>
       , at its height, included a number of Arabic-speaking enclaves in the
       <a href="/wiki/Maghreb" title="Maghreb">
        Maghreb
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Sahara" title="Spanish Sahara">
        Spanish Sahara
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_Morocco" title="Spanish Morocco">
        Spanish Morocco
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moroccan historian
       <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Mohammed_al-Maqqari" title="Ahmed Mohammed al-Maqqari">
        Ahmed Mohammed al-Maqqari
       </a>
       (c. 1591 – 1632) wrote about the Muslim dynasties in Spain. The Egyptian poet
       <a href="/wiki/Ahmed_Shawqi" title="Ahmed Shawqi">
        Ahmed Shawqi
       </a>
       (1869–1932) spent six years of exile in Andalusia. Perhaps the first "scientific" Arab Hispanist was the Lebanese writer
       <a href="/wiki/Shakib_Arslan" title="Shakib Arslan">
        Shakib Arslan
       </a>
       (1869–1946), who wrote a book about his trips to Spain in three volumes. The Egyptian writer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Taha_Husayn" title="Taha Husayn">
        Taha Husayn
       </a>
       (1889–1973) promoted the renewal of relations with Spain, among other European countries of the Mediterranean, and led the creation of an edition of the great 12th-century Andalusian literary encyclopedia
       <i>
        Al-Dajira
       </i>
       , of
       <a href="/wiki/Ibn_Bassam" title="Ibn Bassam">
        Ibn Bassam
       </a>
       . Other important figures were 'Abd al-`Aziz al-Ahwani, 'Abd Allah `Inan, Husayn Mu'nis, Salih al-Astar, Mahmud Mekki, and Hamid Abu Ahmad. Linked to the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instituto_Egipcio_de_Estudios_Isl%C3%A1micos_en_Madrid" title="es:Instituto Egipcio de Estudios Islámicos en Madrid">
        Egyptian Institute of Madrid
       </a>
       are Ahmad Mukhtar al-'Abbadi (who specialized in the history of Moorish Granada), Ahmad Haykal, Salah Fadl, As'ad Sharif 'Umar, and Nagwa Gamal Mehrez. The
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.heg-eg.org/" rel="nofollow">
        Asociación de Hispanistas de Egipto
       </a>
       was formed in 1968. The First Colloquium of Arab Hispanism took place in Madrid in 1975.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_the_Netherlands">
        Hispanism in the Netherlands
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hispanism in the Netherlands">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In spite of
       <a href="/wiki/Dutch_Revolt" title="Dutch Revolt">
        a bitter war
       </a>
       between Spain and the United Provinces in the late 16th century, Hispanism has deep roots in the Netherlands. The influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Golden_Age" title="Spanish Golden Age">
        Spanish Golden Age
       </a>
       literature can be seen in the work of the Dutch poet and playwright
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gerbrand_Adriaensz_Bredero" title="Gerbrand Adriaensz Bredero">
        Gerbrand Bredero
       </a>
       and in the translations of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guilliam_de_Bay" title="es:Guilliam de Bay">
        Guilliam de Bay
       </a>
       in the 17th century. Nineteenth-century
       <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
        Romanticism
       </a>
       aroused Dutch curiosity about the exoticism of things Spanish. The Arabist
       <a href="/wiki/Reinhart_Dozy" title="Reinhart Dozy">
        Reinhart Dozy
       </a>
       (1820–1883) made important contributions to the study of the Moorish domination in Spain, including
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=k5pWDAFhIzQC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=dozy+Histoire+des+Musulmans+d%27Espagne&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=nswRTpzBDIS3twe_7aXVDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Histoire des Musulmans d'Espagne
        </i>
       </a>
       (1861) and the continuation
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=nsW93SQfEJkC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=dozy+Recherches+l%27Histoire+et+litt%C3%A9rature&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=6swRTvDuMMjd0QHNhtyuDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Recherches sur l'Histoire et littérature de l'Espagne
        </i>
       </a>
       , which was published in its definitive form in 1881. A few years later, the Dutch scholar
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fonger_de_Haan" title="es:Fonger de Haan">
        Fonger de Haan
       </a>
       (1859–1930) held the chair of Spanish literature at
       <a href="/wiki/Boston_University" title="Boston University">
        Boston University
       </a>
       . Two of his publications,
       <a class="external text" href="https://catalyst.library.jhu.edu/catalog/bib_605142" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Pícaros y ganapanes
        </i>
       </a>
       (1899) and
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=weoCAAAAYAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=haan+outline+of+the+history&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=5NARTtrxLZO6tgfyo5zhDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CC0Q6AEwAA#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         An Outline of the History of the
        </i>
        Novela Picaresca
        <i>
         in Spain
        </i>
       </a>
       (1903) still serve as starting points for research today. In 1918 he tried in vain to spark the interest of the State
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Groningen" title="University of Groningen">
        University of Groningen
       </a>
       in Hispanic Studies, but nevertheless donated his library of Hispanic Studies to it a few years later.
      </p>
      <p>
       Serious studies of literature gained new impetus thanks to the work of
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_te_Winkel" title="es:Jan te Winkel">
        Jan te Winkel
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Amsterdam" title="University of Amsterdam">
        University of Amsterdam
       </a>
       who, with his seven-volume
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=hwUoAAAAMAAJ&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Jan+te+Winkel+Ontwikkelingsgang&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=4tsRTpfAOcicgQew5t2FDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC0Q6AEwAQ#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         De Ontwikkelingsgang der Nederlandsche Letterkunde
        </i>
       </a>
       (1908–1921), drew attention to the influence that Spanish literature exerted on Dutch literature in the 17th century. Other researchers, such as
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Davids" title="es:William Davids">
        William Davids
       </a>
       (1918),
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Vles" title="es:Joseph Vles">
        Joseph Vles
       </a>
       (1926) and
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Anselmus_Vosters" title="es:Simon Anselmus Vosters">
        Simon Vosters
       </a>
       (1955), continued in the same direction as te Winkel. Two Romanists who were of great importance to Dutch Hispanism were Salverda de Grave and Sneyders de Vogel.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Jacques_Salverda_de_Grave" title="es:Jean Jacques Salverda de Grave">
        Jean Jacques Salverda de Grave
       </a>
       (1863–1947) became a professor of Romance philology at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Groningen" title="University of Groningen">
        University of Groningen
       </a>
       in 1907, and he was succeeded by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kornelis_Sneyders_de_Vogel" title="es:Kornelis Sneyders de Vogel">
        Kornelis Sneyders de Vogel
       </a>
       (1876–1958) in 1921. In 1906, for the first time since 1659, a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=PJZTtwAACAAJ&amp;dq=abraham+anthony+fokker&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=aeERTsShEqu50AHwla2GDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;ved=0CDgQ6AEwBDgU" rel="nofollow">
        Spanish/Dutch dictionary
       </a>
       was published, followed in 1912 by a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=Ti3QGwAACAAJ&amp;dq=abraham+anthony+fokker+1912&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ueERTurMIqTf0QHf3pSQAg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;ved=0CDoQ6AEwBA" rel="nofollow">
        Dutch/Spanish dictionary
       </a>
       , both composed by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._A._Fokker" title="es:A. A. Fokker">
        A. A. Fokker
       </a>
       . Since then many such dictionaries have been published, including one by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=suRqQwAACAAJ&amp;dq=%22cfa+van+dam%22+woordenboek+spaanse&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=xS0STr2eCIGugQf38_X7DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CEEQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        C. F. A. van Dam and H. C. Barrau
       </a>
       and another by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=pbD4PQAACAAJ&amp;dq=woordenboek+spaanse&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=KC8STuSTGOPW0QGH7eCzCQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=6&amp;ved=0CEwQ6AEwBTgo" rel="nofollow">
        S. A. Vosters
       </a>
       . Many Spanish grammars in Dutch also have been published, including
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=atKiOwAACAAJ&amp;dq=Gerardus+Johannes+Geers+1924&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ojcSTo-ODunf0QG1lP37DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CCwQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        a grammar
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Gerardus_Johannes_Geers" title="Gerardus Johannes Geers">
        Gerardus Johannes Geers
       </a>
       (1924), one by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=QLiAHAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Spaanse+grammatica&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ZTQSTruoGMjn0QGX3umwDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CEIQ6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        Jonas Andries van Praag
       </a>
       (1957) and one by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=lL7eSgAACAAJ&amp;dq=Spaanse+grammatica&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ZTQSTruoGMjn0QGX3umwDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CDwQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        Jos Hallebeek, Antoon van Bommel, and Kees van Esch
       </a>
       (2004). Doctor W. J. van Baalen was an important popularizer of the history, customs, and wealth of Spanish America, producing ten books in those areas. Along with C. F. A. Van Dam, he founded the Nederlandsch Zuid-Amerikaansch Instituut in order to promote commercial and cultural contact between both worlds. The Groningen poet
       <a href="/wiki/Hendrik_de_Vries" title="Hendrik de Vries">
        Hendrik de Vries
       </a>
       (1896–1989) travelled twelve times to Spain between 1924 and 1936 and—although his father, an eminent philologist and polyglot, always refused to study Spanish because of the
       <a href="/wiki/Eighty_Years%27_War" title="Eighty Years' War">
        Eighty Years' War
       </a>
       —the poet dedicated his book of poems
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=sVdAGQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Hendrik+de+Vries+iberia+1964&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=ezsSTtaZBYGSgQe1ppT-DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Iberia
        </i>
       </a>
       (1964) to Spain.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Netherlands, the Institute of Hispanic Studies at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Utrecht" title="University of Utrecht">
        University of Utrecht
       </a>
       was founded in 1951 by
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornelis_Frans_Adolf_van_Dam" title="es:Cornelis Frans Adolf van Dam">
        Cornelis Frans Adolf van Dam
       </a>
       (who was a student of
       <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Men%C3%A9ndez_Pidal" title="Ramón Menéndez Pidal">
        Ramón Menéndez Pidal
       </a>
       ) and has since been an important center for Spanish scholars. The Mexican Training Center at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Groningen" title="University of Groningen">
        University of Groningen
       </a>
       was established in 1993.
      </p>
      <p>
       Johan Brouwer, who wrote his thesis on Spanish mysticism, produced twenty-two books on Spanish subjects, as well as numerous translations.
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonas_Andries_van_Prague" title="es:Jonas Andries van Prague">
        Jonas Andries van Prague
       </a>
       , a professor at Groningen, studied Spanish Golden Age theater in the Netherlands and the
       <a href="/wiki/Generation_of_%2798" title="Generation of '98">
        Generation of '98
       </a>
       , as well as the
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardic
       </a>
       refugee writers in the Netherlands.
       <a href="/wiki/Cees_Nooteboom" title="Cees Nooteboom">
        Cees Nooteboom
       </a>
       has written books about travel to Spain, including
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=FC9fiEgbf_IC&amp;printsec=frontcover&amp;dq=Cees+Nooteboom&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=bY8STo7aNI6SgQeHyuSKCQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=3&amp;ved=0CD8Q6AEwAg#v=onepage&amp;q&amp;f=false" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Roads to Santiago
        </i>
       </a>
       .
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barber_van_de_Pol" title="es:Barber van de Pol">
        Barber van de Pol
       </a>
       produced a Dutch translation of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       in 1994, and Hispanism continues to be promoted by Dutch writers such as Rik Zaal (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=sk5-AAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Rik+Zaal&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=upISTrasB5OcgQeTpLzrAg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CC0Q6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Alles over Spanje
        </i>
        )
       </a>
       , Gerrit Jan Zwier, Arjen Duinker, Jean Pierre Rawie, Els Pelgrom (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=6eZLGwAACAAJ&amp;dq=Els+Pelgrom&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=6JMSTrSHAsPEgQeC76TdDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;ved=0CDEQ6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         The Acorn Eaters
        </i>
       </a>
       ), Chris van der Heijden (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=GrU-HQAACAAJ&amp;dq=Chris+van+der+Heijden&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=SpQSTv7qJZDqgAfMiMGHDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Scandinavia">
        Hispanism in Scandinavia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Scandinavia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Denmark">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       was translated into
       <a href="/wiki/Danish_language" title="Danish language">
        Danish
       </a>
       (1776–1777) by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Charlotte_Dorothea_Biehl" title="Charlotte Dorothea Biehl">
        Charlotte Dorothea Biehl
       </a>
       , who also translated Cervantes's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Novelas_ejemplares" title="Novelas ejemplares">
         Novelas ejemplares
        </a>
       </i>
       (1780–1781).
       <a href="/wiki/Hans_Christian_Andersen" title="Hans Christian Andersen">
        Hans Christian Andersen
       </a>
       made a trip to Spain and kept a diary about his experiences. Other prominent Danish Hispanists include
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knud_Togeby" title="es:Knud Togeby">
        Knud Togeby
       </a>
       ; Carl Bratli (
       <i>
        Spansk-dansk Ordbog
       </i>
       [Spanish/Danish dictionary], 1947); Johann Ludwig Heiberg (1791–1860,
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón
       </a>
       studies); Kristoffer Nyrop (1858–1931,

        <i>
         Spansk grammatik
        </i>
       </a>
       ); and Valdemar Beadle (Middle Ages and the Spanish and Italian Baroque).
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Norway">
        Norway
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Norway">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Hispanism was founded in
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       by professor
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnus_Gronvold" title="es:Magnus Gronvold">
        Magnus Gronvold
       </a>
       , who translated
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       into Norwegian in collaboration with
       <a href="/wiki/Nils_Kj%C3%A6r" title="Nils Kjær">
        Nils Kjær
       </a>
       . Leif Sletsjoe (author of
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=97hiAAAAMAAJ&amp;q=Leif+Sletsjoe&amp;dq=Leif+Sletsjoe&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=uGcTTsnsNsHd0QGMivX7DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Sancho Panza, hombre de bien
        </i>
       </a>
       ) and Kurt E. Sparre (a
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón
       </a>
       scholar) were both professors at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Oslo" title="University of Oslo">
        University of Oslo
       </a>
       . Currently there is a strong and renewed interest in Hispanism among Norwegian youth, and the 21st century has seen the publication of at least three Spanish grammars for Norwegians—one by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=ecwcQAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Spansk+grammatikk&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=J2kTTuijN6jx0gGW6JS1Dg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=2&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CDIQ6AEwAQ" rel="nofollow">
        Cathrine Grimseid
       </a>
       (2005); another by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=-zQeQAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Spansk+grammatikk&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=J2kTTuijN6jx0gGW6JS1Dg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=7&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CEgQ6AEwBg" rel="nofollow">
        Johan Falk, Luis Lerate, and Kerstin Sjölin
       </a>
       (2008); and one by
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=0c81QAAACAAJ&amp;dq=Spansk+grammatikk&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=qWoTTqG3Gcn40gGKsP2WDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=5&amp;ved=0CDcQ6AEwBDgK" rel="nofollow">
        Ana Beatriz Chiquito
       </a>
       (2008). There is an Association of Norwegian Hispanism, a National Association of Professors of Spanish, and several journals, including
       <i>
        La Corriente del Golfo (Revista Noruega de Estudios Latinoamericanos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Tribune
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        Romansk forum
       </i>
       .
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">

       <a href="/wiki/Helsinki" title="Helsinki">
        Helsinki

       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=IkgmAQAAIAAJ&amp;q=Eero+Neuvonen&amp;dq=Eero+Neuvonen&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=27ITTpT6I5CSgQeFrsH9BA&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=7&amp;ved=0CEIQ6AEwBg" rel="nofollow">

      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Romania">
        Hispanism in Romania
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Romania">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Romania, the initiator of Hispanism was Ştefan Virgolici, who translated a great part of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       into Romanian and published—under the title
       <i>
        Studies on Spanish Literature
       </i>
       (Jasi, 1868–1870)—works on
       <a href="/wiki/Pedro_Calder%C3%B3n_de_la_Barca" title="Pedro Calderón de la Barca">
        Calderón
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Lope_de_Vega" title="Lope de Vega">
        Lope de Vega
       </a>
       , which had appeared in the journal
       <i>
        Convorbiri literare
       </i>
       (Literary Conversations).
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.crispedia.ro/Alexandru_Popescu-Telega" rel="nofollow">
        Alexandru Popescu-Telega
       </a>
       (1889–1970) wrote a book on
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Unamuno" title="Miguel de Unamuno">
        Unamuno
       </a>
       (1924), a comparison between Romanian and Spanish
       <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
        folklore
       </a>
       (1927), a biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Miguel_de_Cervantes" title="Miguel de Cervantes">
        Cervantes
       </a>
       (1944), a translation from the
       <a href="/wiki/Romancero" title="Romancero">
        romancero
       </a>
       (1947), a
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=p9c8AAAAMAAJ&amp;q=Alexandru+Popescu-Telega+Unamuno&amp;dq=Alexandru+Popescu-Telega+Unamuno&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=P2wUTruQNMrt0gGCoeHlDQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=10&amp;sqi=2&amp;ved=0CFIQ6AEwCQ" rel="nofollow">
        book on Hispanic Studies in Romania
       </a>
       (1964), and an anthology in Romanian. Ileana Georgescu,
       <a href="/wiki/George_C%C4%83linescu" title="George Călinescu">
        George Călinescu
       </a>
       (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=JX7ZPQAACAAJ&amp;dq=George+C%C4%83linescu+cervantes&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=lG8UToa6CKmp0AGd3o2CDg&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=8&amp;ved=0CEYQ6AEwBw" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Iscusitul hidalgo Don Quijote de la Mancha
        </i>
       </a>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/Tudor_Vianu" title="Tudor Vianu">
        Tudor Vianu
       </a>
       (
       <a class="external text" href="https://books.google.com/books?id=eR21YgEACAAJ&amp;dq=Tudor+Vianu+cervantes&amp;hl=en&amp;ei=N3AUTvmJEqLv0gGZm8j_DQ&amp;sa=X&amp;oi=book_result&amp;ct=result&amp;resnum=1&amp;ved=0CCkQ6AEwAA" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Cervantes
        </i>
       </a>
       ) have published books on Cervantes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hispanism_in_Asia_and_the_Pacific">
        Hispanism in Asia and the Pacific
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Hispanism in Asia and the Pacific">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is an Asian Association of Spanish Scholars (
       <a class="external text" href="https://web.archive.org/web/20111002043007/http://hispanismo.cervantes.es/Asociaciones_ficha.asp?DOCN=12" rel="nofollow">
        Asociación Asiática de Hispanistas
       </a>
       ), which was founded in 1985 and meets every three years.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Philippines">
        The Philippines
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: The Philippines">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Hispanism in Asia and the Pacific is mostly related to the literature and languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       , where Spanish has a history as a colonial language. In 1900 a million Filipinos spoke Spanish as their mother tongue;
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_Philippines" title="Spanish language in the Philippines">
        estimates of the number of Filipinos whose first language is Spanish
       </a>
       today vary widely, ranging from 2,660 to over 400,000. Spanish remains alive in some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creole_languages" title="Creole languages">
        creole languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chavacano_language" title="Chavacano language">
        Chabacano
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Manila" title="Manila">
        Manila
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Instituto_Cervantes" title="Instituto Cervantes">
        Cervantes Institute
       </a>
       has given Spanish classes for years, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippine_Academy_of_the_Spanish_Language" title="Philippine Academy of the Spanish Language">
        Philippine Academy of the Spanish Language
       </a>
       is involved in the teaching and standard use of Spanish in the Philippines. But there is no institution or association that brings together and defends the interests of Hispanic Filipinos themselves. The most important Spanish scholars—aside from the national hero, poet and novelist
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Rizal" title="José Rizal">
        José Rizal
       </a>
       (who wrote in Spanish)—are Antonio M. Molina (not the composer
       <a href="/wiki/Antonio_Molina_(composer)" title="Antonio Molina (composer)">
        Antonio J. Molina
       </a>
       ), José María Castañer,
       <a href="/wiki/Edmundo_Farolan" title="Edmundo Farolan">
        Edmundo Farolan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_G%C3%B3mez_Rivera" title="Guillermo Gómez Rivera">
        Guillermo Gómez
       </a>
       , Miguel Fernández Passion, Alfonso Felix, and Lourdes Castrillo de Brillantes. The weekly
       <i>
        Nueva Era
       </i>
       , edited by
       <a href="/wiki/Guillermo_G%C3%B3mez_Rivera" title="Guillermo Gómez Rivera">
        Guillermo Gómez Rivera
       </a>
       , is the only newspaper in Spanish still published in the Philippines, although the quarterly journal
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.revista.carayanpress.com/" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Revista Filipina
        </i>
       </a>
       , edited by Edmundo Farolán, also exists, in print and online.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Japan">
        Japan
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Japan">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The first Japanese institution to offer Spanish language classes, in 1897, was the Language School of Tokyo, known today as the
       <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_University_of_Foreign_Studies" title="Tokyo University of Foreign Studies">
        Tokyo University of Foreign Studies
       </a>
       . There,
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gonzalo_Jim%C3%A9nez_de_la_Espada" title="es:Gonzalo Jiménez de la Espada">
        Gonzalo Jiménez de la Espada
       </a>
       mentored the first Japanese Hispanists, including Hirosada Nagata (1885–1973, now considered a "patriarch" of Hispanism in Japan) and Shizuo Kasai. Meanwhile, the
       <a href="/wiki/Osaka_University_of_Foreign_Studies" title="Osaka University of Foreign Studies">
        Osaka University of Foreign Studies
       </a>
       established Hispanic Studies in its curriculum in 1921, but most university Hispanic Studies departments were founded in the 1970s and '80s. Translations of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       into Japanese are at first incomplete and by way of an English version (e.g. one by Shujiro Watanabe in 1887, and others in 1893, 1901, 1902, and 1914). Japanese versions of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       in its entirety—although still based on an English translation—were published in 1915 (by Hogetsu Shimamura and Noburu Katakami) and in 1927-28 (by Morita). In 1948, Hirosada Nagata published a nearly-complete direct (from the Spanish) Japanese translation. It fell to Nagata's student, Masatake Takahashi (1908–1984), to complete that translation (published in 1977). Meanwhile, an entire, direct Japanese translation of
       <i>
        Don Quixote
       </i>
       was also produced (the two parts in 1958 and 1962) by Yu Aida
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (1903–1971).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.soc.nii.ac.jp/ajh/indexsp.htm" rel="nofollow">
        Asociación Japonesa de Hispanistas
       </a>
       was founded in Tokyo in 1955, consisting mostly of university professors. The association publishes the journal
       <a class="external text" href="http://wwwsoc.nii.ac.jp/ajh/hispanica_es.html" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Hispánica
        </i>
       </a>
       . The journal
       <a class="external text" href="http://hispanismo.cervantes.es/archivo.asp?DOCN=8" rel="nofollow">
        <i>
         Lingüística Hispánica
        </i>
       </a>
       is published by the Círculo de Lingüística Hispánica de Kansai.
      </p>
      <p>
       Japanese Hispanism was surveyed by Ryohei Uritani in the article "Historia del hispanismo en el Japón", which was published in the journal
       <i>
        Español actual: Revista de español vivo
       </i>
       (48 [1987], 69-92).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Korea">
        Korea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hispanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Korea">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The relations between Spain and
       <a href="/wiki/Korea" title="Korea">
        Korea
       </a>
       began with
       <a class="extiw" href="https://es.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_C%C3%A9spedes" title="es:Gregorio Céspedes">
        Gregorio Céspedes
       </a>
       in the 16th century, who was studied by
       <a class="external text" href="http://cvc.cervantes.es/ensenanza/biblioteca_ele/asele/pdf/10/10_0503.pdf" rel="nofollow">
        Chul Park
       </a>
       . Spanish education in Korea has continued for the past fifty years, and there is currently a strong demand for it. Since 2001, Spanish has been an optional language in secondary education. The Asociación Coreana de Hispanistas was founded in 1981 and holds two annual congresses, one in June and another in December. It also publishes the journal
       <i>
        Hispanic Studies
       </i>
       .
      </p>
