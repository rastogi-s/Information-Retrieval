      <p>
       <b>
        Rashi script
       </b>
       is a semi-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_cursive" title="Hebrew cursive">
        cursive
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Typeface" title="Typeface">
        typeface
       </a>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">
        Hebrew alphabet
       </a>
       . It is named for
       <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
        Rashi
       </a>
       , an author of
       <a href="/wiki/Rabbinic_literature" title="Rabbinic literature">
        rabbinic commentary
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
        Hebrew Bible
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Tanakh" title="Tanakh">
        Tanakh
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Talmud" title="Talmud">
        Talmud
       </a>
       , and it is customarily used for printing his
       <a href="/wiki/Rashi#Works" title="Rashi">
        commentaries
       </a>
       . The typeface (which was not used by Rashi himself) is based on 15th century
       <a href="/wiki/Sephardi_Jews" title="Sephardi Jews">
        Sephardic
       </a>
       semi-cursive
       <a href="/wiki/Handwriting" title="Handwriting">
        handwriting
       </a>
       . It was taken as a model by early Hebrew typographers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Garton" title="Abraham Garton">
        Abraham Garton
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Soncino_family_(printers)" title="Soncino family (printers)">
        Soncino family
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Bomberg" title="Daniel Bomberg">
        Daniel Bomberg
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
        Christian
       </a>
       printer in
       <a href="/wiki/Venice" title="Venice">
        Venice
       </a>
       , in their editions of commented texts (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Mikraot_Gedolot" title="Mikraot Gedolot">
        Mikraot Gedolot
       </a>
       and the Talmud, in which Rashi's commentaries prominently figure).
      </p>
      <p>
       The purpose was to distinguish the rabbinic commentary from the text itself for which a proper
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_alphabet" title="Hebrew alphabet">
        square typeface
       </a>
       was used.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Rashi typeface is also traditionally used for printed
       <a href="/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish" title="Judaeo-Spanish">
        Judaeo-Spanish
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rashi_script&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The initial development of typefaces for the
       <a href="/wiki/Printing_press" title="Printing press">
        printing press
       </a>
       was often anchored in a pre-existing
       <a href="/wiki/Manuscript_culture" title="Manuscript culture">
        manuscript culture
       </a>
       . In the case of the Hebrew press,
       <a href="/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews" title="Ashkenazi Jews">
        Ashkenazi
       </a>
       tradition prevailed and square or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Block_letter" title="Block letter">
        block letters
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Movable_type" title="Movable type">
        cast
       </a>
       for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Biblical" title="Biblical">
        Biblical
       </a>
       and other important works. Secondary religious text, such as rabbinic commentaries, was, however, commonly set with a semi-cursive form of Sephardic origin, ultimately normalised as the Rashi typeface.
      </p>
      <p>
       A corresponding but distinctive semicursive typeface was used for printing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yiddish_language#Printing" title="Yiddish language">
        Yiddish
       </a>
       . It was termed
       <i>
        vaybertaytsh
       </i>
       , the Yiddish word
       <i>
        vayber
       </i>
       meaning "women" and
       <i>
        taytsh
       </i>
       being an archaic word for "Yiddish". (Works printed in
       <i>
        vaybertaytsh
       </i>
       were largely intended for a female readership.)
      </p>
