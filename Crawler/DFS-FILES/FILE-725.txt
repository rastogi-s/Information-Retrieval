      <p>
       <b>
        Astur-Leonese
       </b>
       is a group of closely related Romance languages of the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Iberian_languages" title="West Iberian languages">
        West Iberian branch
       </a>
       , including:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">
         Asturian
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          asturianu
         </b>
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         <b>
          bable
         </b>
        </i>
        , encompassing the vernaculars spoken in the Spanish province of
        <a href="/wiki/Asturias" title="Asturias">
         Asturias
        </a>
        , except the westernmost ones, which are more often considered dialects of
        <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
         Galician
        </a>
        (see
        <i>
         Eonavian
        </i>
        below). This is the area with most speakers left.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Leonese_dialect" title="Leonese dialect">
         Leonese
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          llionés
         </b>
        </i>
        , encompassing the vernaculars spoken in northern and western parts of the province of
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Province of León">
         León
        </a>
        , and a few western areas in the provinces of
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Zamora" title="Province of Zamora">
         Zamora
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Salamanca" title="Province of Salamanca">
         Salamanca
        </a>
        , in Spain.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The distinction between
        <i>
         Asturian
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         Leonese
        </i>
        cannot be made in purely linguistic terms. Leonese was spoken in the past in a much larger area; however, it is now approaching extinction.
       </li>
       <li>
        The now-extinct dialects of the neighbour parishes of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_de_Onor" title="Rio de Onor">
         Rio de Onor
        </a>
        and
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Guadramil&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Guadramil (page does not exist)">
         Guadramil
        </a>
        , in the northern border of the
        <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_District" title="Bragança District">
         District of Bragança
        </a>
        , in Portugal;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Mirandese_language" title="Mirandese language">
         Mirandese
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          mirandés
         </b>
        </i>
        , in villages around the border town of
        <a href="/wiki/Miranda_do_Douro" title="Miranda do Douro">
         Miranda do Douro
        </a>
        , in the eastern border of the
        <a href="/wiki/Bragan%C3%A7a_District" title="Bragança District">
         District of Bragança
        </a>
        like
        <a href="/wiki/Angueira" title="Angueira">
         Angueira
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Macedo_de_Cavaleiros" title="Macedo de Cavaleiros">
         Macedo de Cavaleiros
        </a>
        and further south in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sendim" title="Sendim">
         Sendim
        </a>
        , Portugal.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Cantabrian_dialect" title="Cantabrian dialect">
         Cantabrian
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          cántabru
         </b>
        </i>
        , encompassing the vernaculars spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Cantabria" title="Cantabria">
         Cantabria
        </a>
        . Some of them, especially western ones, are further grouped with the traditional name
        <i>
         <b>
          montañés
         </b>
        </i>
        while eastern ones are included in
        <i>
         <b>
          pasiegu
         </b>
        </i>
        . There are different positions about whether these varieties are dialects of the
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish language
        </a>
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        , dialects of Astur-Leonese
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        or independent languages on their own right
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2013)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Extremaduran_language" title="Extremaduran language">
         Extremaduran language
        </a>
        ,
        <i>
         <b>
          estremeñu
         </b>
        </i>
        , spoken in northwestern
        <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
         Extremadura
        </a>
        (Spain) is more distantly related to the group.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Leonese (in the sense of the whole linguistic group) was once regarded as an informal dialect (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilect" title="Basilect">
        basilect
       </a>
       ) of Spanish, but in 1906,
       <a href="/wiki/Ram%C3%B3n_Men%C3%A9ndez_Pidal" title="Ramón Menéndez Pidal">
        Ramón Menéndez Pidal
       </a>
       showed it was the result of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       evolution in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        Kingdom of León
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As is noted by the Spanish scholar Inés Fernández Ordoñez,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Men%C3%A9ndez_Pidal" title="Menéndez Pidal">
        Menéndez Pidal
       </a>
       <i>
        always maintained the idea that the Spanish language (or the common Spanish language, "la lengua común española", as he sometimes called it) evolved from a
        <a href="/wiki/Old_Spanish_language" title="Old Spanish language">
         Castilian
        </a>
        base which would have absorbed, or merged with, Leonese and Aragonese
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In that sense, in his works,
       <i>
        "Historia de la Lengua Española"
       </i>
       (History of the Spanish language) and especially
       <i>
        "El español en sus primeros tiempos"
       </i>
       (Spanish in its early times), this author explains the stages of this process, taking into account the influence caused in the first steps of the Spanish language, both by the
       <a href="/wiki/Leonese_dialect" title="Leonese dialect">
        Leonese dialect
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese dialect
       </a>
       , and finally and in last place by Castilian, which, nowadays, due to the political integration process produced during the last eight centuries, has absorbed almost completely to the other two dialects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Leonese is officially recognised by the Autonomous Community of Castile and León (2006). In Asturias it is protected under the Autonomous Statute legislation and is an optional language at schools, where it is widely studied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In Portugal, the related Mirandese language is recognized by the
       <a href="/wiki/Assembly_of_the_Republic_(Portugal)" title="Assembly of the Republic (Portugal)">
        Assembly of the Republic
       </a>
       as a co-official language along with
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       for local matters, and it is taught in public schools in the areas where Mirandese is natively spoken. Initially thought to be a basilect of Portuguese,
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Leite_de_Vasconcelos" title="José Leite de Vasconcelos">
        José Leite de Vasconcelos
       </a>
       studied Mirandese and concluded it was a separate language from Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Astur-Leonese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The language developed from
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       with contributions from the pre-Roman languages which were spoken in the territory of the
       <a href="/wiki/Astures" title="Astures">
        Astures
       </a>
       , an ancient tribe of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Iberian_peninsula" title="Iberian peninsula">
        Iberian peninsula
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Castilian (Spanish)
       </a>
       came to the area later in the 14th century when the central administration sent emissaries and functionaries to occupy political and ecclesiastical offices.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Asturian">
        Asturian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Astur-Leonese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Asturian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Asturian_language" title="Asturian language">
        Asturian language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Much effort has been made since 1974 to protect and promote Asturian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1981 Asturian, or
       <i>
        Bable
       </i>
       , as the language is officially named,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was recognized as an area in need of special protection by the local government. In 1994 there were 100,000 first language speakers and 450,000 second language speakers able to speak or understand Asturian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the outlook for Asturian remains critical, with a large decline in the number of speakers in the last 100 years. At the end of the 20th century, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia_de_la_Llingua_Asturiana" title="Academia de la Llingua Asturiana">
        Academia de la Llingua Asturiana
       </a>
       underwent initiatives designed to provide the language with most of the tools needed to survive in the modern era: a
       <a href="/wiki/Grammar" title="Grammar">
        grammar
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Dictionary" title="Dictionary">
        dictionary
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periodicals" title="Periodicals">
        periodicals
       </a>
       . A new generation of Asturian writers has championed the language. These developments have given Asturian greater hope of survival.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Leonese">
        Leonese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Astur-Leonese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Leonese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonese_language" title="Leonese language">
        Leonese language
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif">
         <img alt="Linguistic map Southwestern Europe.gif" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="544" data-file-width="600" height="272" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/300px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif/450px-Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/4d/Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Linguistic_map_Southwestern_Europe.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Leonese was probably spoken in a much larger area in the Middle Ages, roughly corresponding to the old
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Le%C3%B3n" title="Kingdom of León">
        Kingdom of León
       </a>
       . As the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castilian_language" title="Castilian language">
        Castilian language
       </a>
       became the main language in Spain, the linguistic features of the Leonese language retreated progressively westwards.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the late 1990s several associations unofficially promoted Leonese language courses. In 2001 the
       <i>
        Universidad de León
       </i>
       (University of León) created a course for Leonese teachers, and local and provincial governments developed Leonese language courses for adults. Nowadays Leonese can be studied in the largest towns of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Le%C3%B3n_(province)" title="León (province)">
        León
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zamora_(province)" title="Zamora (province)">
        Zamora
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salamanca_(province)" title="Salamanca (province)">
        Salamanca
       </a>
       provinces.
      </p>
      <p>
       Leonese's desperate reality as a minority language has driven it to an apparent dead end, and it is considered a Seriously Endangered Language by
       <a href="/wiki/UNESCO" title="UNESCO">
        UNESCO
       </a>
       . There are some efforts at language revival aimed at the urban population (the Leonese Council has made campaign to encourage young people to learn Leonese). Some experts think Leonese will be dead in two generations.
      </p>
      <p>
       In spite of all these difficulties, the number of young people learning and using Leonese (mainly as a written language) has increased substantially in recent years. The Leonese City Council promotes Leonese language courses for adults. Leonese is taught in sixteen schools in Leon.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leonese_language" title="Leonese language">
        Leonese language
       </a>
       has special status in the Statute of Autonomy of
       <a href="/wiki/Castile_and_Le%C3%B3n" title="Castile and León">
        Castile and León
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mirandese">
        Mirandese
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Astur-Leonese_languages&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mirandese">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Mirandese_language" title="Mirandese language">
        Mirandese language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the 19th century, José Leite de Vasconcelos described Mirandese as "the language of the farms, work, home, and love between the Mirandese," noting that it was a completely separate language from Portuguese. Since 1986/1987 the language has been taught to students between the ages of 10 and 11, and Mirandese is now recovering. Today Mirandese has fewer than 5,000 speakers (but the figure go up to 15,000 if one includes second language speakers).
      </p>
      <p>
       Portugal took a further step in protecting Mirandese when the Portuguese Republic officially recognised the language in 1999. It is administrated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Anstituto_de_la_Lh%C3%A9ngua_Mirandesa" title="Anstituto de la Lhéngua Mirandesa">
        Anstituto de la Lhéngua Mirandesa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
