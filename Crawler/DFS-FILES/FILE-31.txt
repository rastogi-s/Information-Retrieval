      <p>
       The
       <b>
        elevation
       </b>
       of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic" title="Geographic">
        geographic
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Location_(geography)" title="Location (geography)">
        location
       </a>
       is its height above or below a fixed reference point, most commonly a reference
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       , a mathematical model of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth's
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       as an
       <a href="/wiki/Equipotential" title="Equipotential">
        equipotential
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equipotential_surface" title="Equipotential surface">
        surface
       </a>
       (see
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_system#Vertical_datum" title="Geodetic system">
        Geodetic system, vertical datum
       </a>
       ). Elevation, or
       <b>
        geometric height
       </b>
       , is mainly used when referring to points on the Earth's surface, while
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude" title="Altitude">
        altitude
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Geopotential_height" title="Geopotential height">
        geopotential height
       </a>
       is used for points above the surface, such as an aircraft in flight or a
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       in orbit, and
       <a href="/wiki/Three-dimensional_space" title="Three-dimensional space">
        depth
       </a>
       is used for points below the surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       Elevation is not to be confused with the distance from the center of the Earth; due to
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_bulge" title="Equatorial bulge">
        equatorial bulge
       </a>
       , the summits of
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Everest" title="Mount Everest">
        Mt. Everest
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chimborazo_(volcano)" title="Chimborazo (volcano)">
        Chimborazo
       </a>
       have, respectively, the largest elevation and the largest geocentric distance.
      </p>

      <p>
       GIS or
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        geographic information system
       </a>
       is a computer system that allows for visualizing, manipulating, capturing, and storage of data with associated attributes. GIS offers better understanding of patterns and relationships of the landscape at different scales. Tools inside the GIS allow for manipulation of data for spatial analysis or cartography.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">

         <a href="/wiki/Equirectangular_projection" title="Equirectangular projection">
          equirectangular projection
         </a>
         , normalized as 8-bit grayscale, where lighter values indicate higher elevation.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">
        topographical map
       </a>
       is the main type of
       <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">
        map
       </a>
       used to depict elevation, often through use of
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
        contour lines
       </a>
       . In a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_Information_System" title="Geographic Information System">
        Geographic Information System
       </a>
       (GIS),
       <a href="/wiki/Digital_elevation_model" title="Digital elevation model">
        digital elevation models
       </a>
       (DEM) are commonly used to represent the surface (topography) of a place, through a
       <a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">
        raster
       </a>
       (grid) dataset of elevations.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Digital_terrain_model" title="Digital terrain model">
        Digital terrain models
       </a>
       are another way to represent terrain in GIS.
      </p>
      <p>
       USGS (United States Geologic Survey) is developing a 3D Elevation Program (3DEP) to keep up with growing needs for high quality topographic data. 3DEP is a collection of enhanced elevation data in the form of high quality LiDAR data over the conterminous United States, Hawaii, and the U.S. territories. There are three bare earth DEM layers in 3DEP which are nationally seamless at the resolution of 1/3, 1, and 2 arcseconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hypsography">
        Hypsography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elevation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Hypsography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-move" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <img alt="" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="60" height="17" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Split-arrows.svg/50px-Split-arrows.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Split-arrows.svg/75px-Split-arrows.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Split-arrows.svg/100px-Split-arrows.svg.png 2x" width="50"/>
         </div>
        </td>
        <td class="mbox-text">
         <div class="mbox-text-span">
          It has been suggested that this section be
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Splitting" title="Wikipedia:Splitting">
           split
          </a>
          out into another article titled
          <i>
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypsography" title="Hypsography">
            Hypsography
           </a>
          </i>
          . (
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:Elevation" title="Talk:Elevation">
           Discuss
          </a>
          )
          <small>
           <i>
            (July 2014)
           </i>
          </small>
         </div>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       <b>
        Hypsography
       </b>
       is the study of the distribution of elevations on the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , although the term is sometimes also applied to other rocky planets such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       . The term originates from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       word
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        ὕψος
       </span>
       "hypsos" meaning height. Most often it is used only in reference to elevation of land but a complete description of Earth's solid surface requires a description of the seafloor as well. Related to the term
       <a href="/wiki/Hypsometry" title="Hypsometry">
        hypsometry
       </a>
       , the measurement of these elevations of a planet's solid surface are taken relative to
       <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">
        mean
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        datum
       </a>
       , except for Earth which is taken relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EarthHypso.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="530" data-file-width="1040" height="306" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/EarthHypso.png/600px-EarthHypso.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/EarthHypso.png/900px-EarthHypso.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/95/EarthHypso.png 2x" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EarthHypso.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Hypsography of the Earth. Notice that Earth has two peaks in elevation, one for the continents, the other for the ocean floors.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">
        Temperature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Elevation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Temperature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Troposphere" title="Troposphere">
        troposphere
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperatures" title="Temperatures">
        temperatures
       </a>
       decrease with altitude. This
       <a href="/wiki/Lapse_rate" title="Lapse rate">
        lapse rate
       </a>
       is approximately 6.5 °C/km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
