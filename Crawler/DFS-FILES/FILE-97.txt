      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        reference ellipsoid
       </b>
       is a mathematically defined surface that approximates the
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       , the truer
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        figure of the Earth
       </a>
       , or other planetary body. Because of their relative simplicity, reference ellipsoids are used as a preferred surface on which
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_network" title="Geodetic network">
        geodetic network
       </a>
       computations are performed and point coordinates such as
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       are defined.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsoid_parameters">
        Ellipsoid parameters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reference_ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ellipsoid parameters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1687
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       published the
       <a href="/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica" title="Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica">
        Principia
       </a>
       in which he included a proof
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-newton_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-newton-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (February 2012)">
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       that a rotating self-gravitating fluid body in equilibrium takes the form of an oblate
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoid
       </a>
       of revolution which he termed an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblate
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spheroid" title="Spheroid">
        spheroid
       </a>
       . Current practice
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-torge_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-torge-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flattening_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-flattening-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       uses the word 'ellipsoid' alone in preference to the full term 'oblate ellipsoid of revolution' or the older term 'oblate spheroid'. In the rare instances (some
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       ) where a more general ellipsoid shape is required as a model the term used is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triaxial_ellipsoid" title="Triaxial ellipsoid">
        triaxial
       </a>
       (or scalene) ellipsoid. A great many ellipsoids have been used with various sizes and centres but modern (post-
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       ) ellipsoids are centred at the actual
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       of the Earth or body being modeled.
      </p>
      <p>
       The shape of an (oblate) ellipsoid (of revolution) is determined by the shape parameters of that
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       which generates the ellipsoid when it is rotated about its minor axis. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       of the ellipse,
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       , is identified as the equatorial radius of the ellipsoid: the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-minor_axis" title="Semi-minor axis">
        semi-minor axis
       </a>
       of the ellipse,
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       , is identified with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographic_pole" title="Geographic pole">
        polar
       </a>
       distances (from the centre). These two lengths completely specify the shape of the ellipsoid but in practice geodesy publications classify reference ellipsoids by giving the semi-major axis and the
       <i>
        inverse
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Flattening" title="Flattening">
        flattening
       </a>
       ,
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         1
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         <i>
          f
         </i>
        </span>
       </span>
       , The flattening,
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       , is simply a measure of how much the symmetry axis is compressed relative to the equatorial radius:
      </p>

       A great many other parameters are used in
       <a href="/wiki/Geodesy" title="Geodesy">
        geodesy

       <i>
        a
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        b
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       . They are listed in
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       .

       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        Latitude
       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        Longitude
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A primary use of reference ellipsoids is to serve as a basis for a coordinate system of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       (north/south),
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       (east/west), and
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       (height). For this purpose it is necessary to identify a
       <i>
        zero
        <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
         meridian
        </a>
       </i>
       , which for Earth is usually the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prime_Meridian" title="Prime Meridian">
        Prime Meridian
       </a>
       . For other bodies a fixed surface feature is usually referenced, which for Mars is the meridian passing through the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Airy-0" title="Airy-0">
        Airy-0
       </a>
       . It is possible for many different coordinate systems to be defined upon the same reference ellipsoid.
      </p>
      <p>
       The longitude measures the rotational
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angle
       </a>
       between the zero meridian and the measured point. By convention for the Earth, Moon, and Sun it is expressed in degrees ranging from −180° to +180° For other bodies a range of 0° to 360° is used.
      </p>
      <p>
       The latitude measures how close to the poles or equator a point is along a meridian, and is represented as an angle from −90° to +90°, where 0° is the equator. The common or
       <i>
        geodetic latitude
       </i>
       is the angle between the equatorial plane and a line that is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surface_normal" title="Surface normal">
        normal
       </a>
       to the reference ellipsoid. Depending on the flattening, it may be slightly different from the
       <i>
        geocentric (geographic) latitude
       </i>
       , which is the angle between the equatorial plane and a line from the center of the ellipsoid. For non-Earth bodies the terms
       <i>
        planetographic
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        planetocentric
       </i>
       are used instead.
      </p>
      <p>
       The coordinates of a geodetic point are customarily stated as geodetic latitude and longitude, i.e., the direction in space of the geodetic normal containing the point, and the height
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       of the point over the reference ellipsoid. See
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geodetic_system" title="Geodetic system">
        geodetic system
       </a>
       for more detail. If these coordinates, i.e., latitude
       <i>
        ϕ
       </i>
       , longitude
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       and height
       <i>
        h
       </i>
       , are given, one can compute the

       </i>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_Earth_ellipsoids">
        Historical Earth ellipsoids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reference_ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Historical Earth ellipsoids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg/220px-WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg/330px-WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg/440px-WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WGS84_mean_Earth_radius.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Equatorial (
         <i>
          a
         </i>
         ), polar (
         <i>
          b
         </i>
         ) and mean Earth radii as defined in the 1984
         <a href="/wiki/World_Geodetic_System" title="World Geodetic System">
          World Geodetic System
         </a>
         revision (not to scale)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid#Historical_Earth_ellipsoids" title="Earth ellipsoid">
        Earth ellipsoid § Historical Earth ellipsoids
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Currently the most common reference ellipsoid used, and that used in the context of the Global Positioning System, is the one defined by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/WGS_84" title="WGS 84">
        WGS 84
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Traditional reference ellipsoids or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Geodetic_datum" title="Geodetic datum">
         geodetic datums
        </a>
       </i>
       are defined regionally and therefore non-geocentric, e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/ED50" title="ED50">
        ED50
       </a>
       . Modern geodetic datums are established with the aid of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       and will therefore be geocentric, e.g., WGS 84.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsoids_for_other_planetary_bodies">
        Ellipsoids for other planetary bodies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Reference_ellipsoid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ellipsoids for other planetary bodies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Reference ellipsoids are also useful for geodetic mapping of other planetary bodies including planets, their satellites, asteroids and comet nuclei. Some well observed bodies such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       now have quite precise reference ellipsoids.
      </p>
      <p>
       For rigid-surface nearly-spherical bodies, which includes all the rocky planets and many moons, ellipsoids are defined in terms of the axis of rotation and the mean surface height excluding any atmosphere. Mars is actually
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oval_(geometry)" title="Oval (geometry)">
        egg shaped
       </a>
       , where its north and south polar radii differ by approximately 6 km (4 miles), however this difference is small enough that the average polar radius is used to define its ellipsoid. The Earth's Moon is effectively spherical, having almost no bulge at its equator. Where possible, a fixed observable surface feature is used when defining a reference meridian.
      </p>
      <p>
       For gaseous planets like
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , an effective surface for an ellipsoid is chosen as the equal-pressure boundary of one
       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">
        bar
       </a>
       . Since they have no permanent observable features, the choices of prime meridians are made according to mathematical rules.
      </p>
      <p>
       Small moons, asteroids, and comet nuclei frequently have irregular shapes. For some of these, such as Jupiter's
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       , a scalene (triaxial) ellipsoid is a better fit than the oblate spheroid. For highly irregular bodies, the concept of a reference ellipsoid may have no useful value, so sometimes a spherical reference is used instead and points identified by planetocentric latitude and longitude. Even that can be problematic for
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">
        non-convex
       </a>
       bodies, such as
       <a href="/wiki/433_Eros" title="433 Eros">
        Eros
       </a>
       , in that latitude and longitude don't always uniquely identify a single surface location.
      </p>
