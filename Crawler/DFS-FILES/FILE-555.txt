      <p>
       <b>
        New Mexican Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        español neomexicano
       </i>
       ) is a variant of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language_in_the_United_States" title="Spanish language in the United States">
        Spanish spoken in the United States
       </a>
       , primarily in the northern part of the state of
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_New_Mexico" title="Northern New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       and the southern part of the state of
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado" title="Colorado">
        Colorado
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanos_of_New_Mexico" title="Hispanos of New Mexico">
        Hispanos of New Mexico
       </a>
       . Despite a continual influence from the Spanish spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       to the south by contact with Mexican migrants who fled to the U.S. from the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Revolution" title="Mexican Revolution">
        Mexican Revolution
       </a>
       , New Mexico's unique political history and relative geographical and political isolation from the time New Mexico was annexed by United States from Mexico has made New Mexican Spanish differ notably from Spanish spoken in other parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Hispanic_America" title="Hispanic America">
        Hispanic America
       </a>
       , with the exception of certain rural areas of northern Mexico and
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Speakers of New Mexican Spanish are mainly descendants of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spanish
       </a>
       colonists who arrived in New Mexico in the sixteenth through the eighteenth centuries. During this time, contact with the rest of Spanish America was limited, and New Mexican Spanish developed on its own course. In the meantime, Spanish colonists coexisted with and intermarried with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Puebloan_peoples" title="Puebloan peoples">
        Puebloan peoples
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Navajo_(people)" title="Navajo (people)">
        Navajos
       </a>
       . After the
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican%E2%80%93American_War" title="Mexican–American War">
        Mexican–American War
       </a>
       , New Mexico and all its inhabitants came under the governance of the English-speaking United States, and for the next hundred years, English-speakers increased in number.
      </p>
      <p>
       For these reasons, the main differences between New Mexican Spanish and other forms of Hispanic American Spanish are these: the preservation of forms and vocabulary from colonial-era Spanish (e.g., in some places
       <i>
        haiga
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        haya
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Yo seigo
       </i>
       instead of
       <i>
        Yo soy
       </i>
       ); the borrowing of words from Rio Grande Indian languages for indigenous vocabulary (in addition to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nahuatl" title="Nahuatl">
        Nahuatl
       </a>
       additions that the colonists had brought); a tendency to "re-coin" Spanish words for ones that had fallen into disuse (for example,
       <i>
        ojo,
       </i>
       whose literal meaning is "eye," was repurposed to mean "hot spring" as well); and a large proportion of English loan words, particularly for technological words (e.g.
       <i>
        bos
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        troca
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        telefón
       </i>
       ). Pronunciation also carries influences from colonial, Native American, and English sources. In recent years, speakers have developed a modern New Mexican Spanish, called
       <i>
        Renovador
       </i>
       , which contains more modern vocabulary because of the increasing popularity of Spanish-language broadcast media in the U.S. and intermarriage between New Mexicans and Mexican settlers; the modernized dialect contains
       <a href="/wiki/Mexican_Spanish" title="Mexican Spanish">
        Mexican Spanish
       </a>
       slang (
       <i>
        mexicanismos
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobos-1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cobos-1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The development of a culture of print media in the late nineteenth century allowed New Mexican Spanish to resist assimilation toward either American English or Mexican Spanish for many decades.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 1911
       <a href="/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica" title="Encyclopædia Britannica">
        Encyclopædia Britannica
       </a>
       , for instance, noted that "About one-tenth of the Spanish-American and Indian population [of New Mexico] habitually use the English language." Until the 1930s or 1940s, many speakers never came to learn English, and even after that time, most of their descendants were bilingual with English until the 1960s or 1970s. The advance of English-language broadcast media accelerated this decline. The increasing popularity of Spanish-language broadcast media in the U.S. and intermarriage of Mexican settlers and descendants of colonial Spanish settlers has somehow increased the speakers of New Mexican Spanish.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphological_variation">
        Morphological variation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Morphological variation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:841px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:NM-CO_Spanish.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="406" data-file-width="839" height="406" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/39/NM-CO_Spanish.png" width="839"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          The main grammatical differences between New Mexican Spanish and Standard Spanish, according to Cobos.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobos-1_1-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-Cobos-1-1">
            [1]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Besides a great deal of phonological variation, there are various morphological differences throughout New Mexican Spanish, usually in verb conjugations or endings:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Change from bilabial nasal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /m/
        </span>
        to alveolar nasal
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        in the first person plural (
        <i>
         nosotros
        </i>
        ) ending of
        <a href="/wiki/Imperfect" title="Imperfect">
         imperfect
        </a>
        :
        <i>
         nos bañábamos
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /nos baˈɲabamos/
        </span>
        is realized as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [nos baˈɲa.β̞a.nos]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Regularization_(linguistics)" title="Regularization (linguistics)">
         Regularization
        </a>
        of the following irregular verb conjugations:
        <ul>
         <li>
          Radical stem changes:
          <i>
           quiero
          </i>
          becomes
          <i>
           quero
          </i>
          , this is clearly an archaism still kept
          <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
           [
           <i>
            <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
             <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
              citation needed
             </span>
            </a>
           </i>
           ]
          </sup>
          . In fact, this feature still remains in some parts of the northwest of Spain such as Asturias and Galicia.
         </li>
         <li>
          Regularization of irregular present indicative 1st person singular:
          <i>
           salgo
          </i>
          becomes
          <i>
           salo
          </i>
          ,
          <i>
           vengo
          </i>
          becomes
          <i>
           veno
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          Subjunctive present of
          <i>
           haber
          </i>
          becomes
          <i>
           haiga
          </i>
          (instead of
          <i>
           haya
          </i>
          ).
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobos-1_1-2">
           <a href="#cite_note-Cobos-1-1">
            [1]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
         <li>
          Different forms of
          <i>
           haber
          </i>
          as an auxiliary verb: "nosotros
          <i>
           hamos
          </i>
          comido" instead of "nosotros
          <i>
           hemos
          </i>
          comido", "yo
          <i>
           ha
          </i>
          comido" instead of "yo
          <i>
           he
          </i>
          comido".
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cobos-1_1-3">
           <a href="#cite_note-Cobos-1-1">
            [1]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <ul>
       <li>
        New Mexican Spanish has
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
          seseo
         </a>
        </i>
        (orthographic &lt;c&gt; before /e/ and /i/ and &lt;z&gt; represent a single phoneme /s/, normally pronounced [s]). That is,
        <i>
         casa
        </i>
        ("house") and
        <i>
         caza
        </i>
        ("hunt") are homophones.
        <i>
         Seseo
        </i>
        is prevalent in nearly all of Hispanic America as well as in the Canary Islands, and some areas of southern Spain.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       New Mexican Spanish has been in contact with several indigenous American languages, most prominently those of the Pueblo and Navajo peoples the Spaniards and Mexicans coexisted with during colonial times. For an example of
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanword
       </a>
       phonological borrowing in
       <a href="/wiki/Taos_language" title="Taos language">

       </a>
       , see
       <a href="/wiki/Taos_phonology#Loanword_phonology" title="Taos phonology">
        Taos loanword phonology
       </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">
        Legal status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_Mexican_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Legal status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       New Mexico law grants Spanish a special status. For instance, constitutional amendments must be approved by
       <a href="/wiki/Referendum" title="Referendum">
        referendum
       </a>
       and must be printed on the ballot in both English and Spanish.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain legal notices must be published in English and Spanish, and the state maintains a list of newspapers for Spanish publication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Spanish was not used officially in the legislature after 1935.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C&amp;F_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-C&amp;F-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the New Mexico Constitution (1912) provided that laws would be published in both languages for twenty years and this practice was renewed several times, it ceased in 1949.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C&amp;F_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-C&amp;F-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Accordingly, some describe New Mexico as officially bilingual,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while others disagree.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C&amp;F_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-C&amp;F-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
