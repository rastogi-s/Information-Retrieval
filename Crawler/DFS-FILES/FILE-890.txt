      <p>
       <b>
        Portuguese dialects
       </b>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Mutual_intelligibility" title="Mutual intelligibility">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       variations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese language
       </a>
       over
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese-speaking_countries" title="Portuguese-speaking countries">
        Portuguese-speaking countries
       </a>
       and other areas holding some degree of cultural bound with the language. Portuguese has two standard forms of writing and numerous regional spoken variations (with often large phonological and lexical differences).
      </p>
      <p>
       The standard written form of Portuguese used in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       is regulated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Academia_Brasileira_de_Letras" title="Academia Brasileira de Letras">
        Brazilian Academy of Letters
       </a>
       and is sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       (although the term primarily means all dialects spoken in Brazil as a whole). In Portugal, the language is regulated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academia_das_Ci%C3%AAncias_de_Lisboa,_Classe_de_Letras" title="Academia das Ciências de Lisboa, Classe de Letras">
        Sciences Academy of Lisbon, Class of Letters
       </a>
       , which shapes the standard spelling set of norms associated with
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
       . This written variation is the one preferred by Portuguese African and Asian ex-colonies, including
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cabo_Verde" title="Cabo Verde">
        Cabo Verde
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">
        Mozambique
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">
        Angola
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Timor-Leste" title="Timor-Leste">
        Timor-Leste
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
        Macau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Goa" title="Goa">
        Goa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Differences between Brazilian and European written forms of Portuguese occur in a similar way (and are often compared to) those of
       <a href="/wiki/American_English" title="American English">
        American
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/British_English" title="British English">
        British English
       </a>
       , though spelling divergencies are generally believed to occur with a little greater frequency in the two Portuguese written dialects. Differences in syntax and word construction, not directly related with spelling, are also observed. Furthermore, there were attempts to unify the two written variations, the most recent of them being the
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_Language_Orthographic_Agreement_of_1990" title="Portuguese Language Orthographic Agreement of 1990">
        Orthographic Agreement of 1990
       </a>
       , which only began to take effect in the 2000s and is still under implementation in some countries. This and previous reforms faced criticism by people who say they are unnecessary or inefficient or even that they create more differences instead of reducing or eliminating them.
      </p>
      <p>
       The differences between the various spoken Portuguese dialects are mostly in phonology, in the frequency of usage of certain grammatical forms, and especially in the distance between the formal and informal levels of speech. Lexical differences are numerous but largely confined to "peripheral" words, such as plants, animals, and other local items, with little impact in the core lexicon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Dialectal deviations from the official grammar are relatively few. As a consequence, all Portuguese dialects are mutually intelligible although for some of the most extremely divergent pairs, the phonological changes may make it difficult for speakers to understand rapid speech.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Main_subdivisions">
        Main subdivisions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main subdivisions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Portugal">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:272px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png">
         <img alt="Portugués europeo.png" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="534" data-file-width="525" height="275" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png/270px-Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png/405px-Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/79/Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png 2x" width="270"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Portugu%C3%A9s_europeo.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The dialects of Portugal can be divided into two major groups:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <b>
         southern and central dialects
        </b>
        are broadly characterized by preserving the distinction between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">
          b
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">
          v
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        , and by the tendency to monophthongize
        <i>
         ei
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ou
        </i>
        to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Mid_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Mid front unrounded vowel">
          e̞
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a href="/wiki/Mid_back_rounded_vowel" title="Mid back rounded vowel">
          o̞
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        . They include the dialect of the capital,
        <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">
         Lisbon
        </a>
        , but it has some peculiarities of its own. Although the dialects of the Atlantic archipelagos of the
        <a href="/wiki/Azores" title="Azores">
         Azores
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Madeira" title="Madeira">
         Madeira
        </a>
        have unique characteristics, as well, they can also be grouped with the southern dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <b>
         northern dialects
        </b>
        are characterized by preserving the pronunciation of
        <i>
         ei
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         ou
        </i>
        as diphthongs
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ei̯]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ou̯]
        </span>
        , and by somewhat having sometimes merged
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">
          v
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /
         <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_stop" title="Voiced bilabial stop">
          b
         </a>
         /
        </span>
        (like in
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_phonology" title="Spanish phonology">
         Spanish
        </a>
        ). They include the dialect of
        <a href="/wiki/Porto" title="Porto">
         Porto
        </a>
        , Portugal's second largest city.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Within each of these regions, however, is further variation, especially in pronunciation. For example, in Lisbon and its vicinity, the diphthong
       <i>
        ei
       </i>
       is centralized to
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɐi̯]
       </span>
       instead of being monophthongized, as in the south.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is usually believed that the dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       derived mostly from those of central and southern Portugal.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Barranquenho">
        Barranquenho
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Barranquenho">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In the Portuguese town of
       <a href="/wiki/Barrancos" title="Barrancos">
        Barrancos
       </a>
       (in the border between
       <a href="/wiki/Extremadura" title="Extremadura">
        Extremadura
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Andalucia" title="Andalucia">
        Andalucia
       </a>
       and Portugal), a dialect of Portuguese heavily influenced by Southern Spanish dialects is spoken, known as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Barranquenho" title="Barranquenho">
         barranquenho
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Brazil">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Brazilian dialects are divided into northern and southern groups, the northern dialects tending to slightly more
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel#Articulation" title="Vowel">
        open
       </a>
       pre-
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(linguistics)" title="Stress (linguistics)">
        stressed
       </a>
       vowels. The economic and cultural dominance of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo_(city)" title="São Paulo (city)">
        São Paulo
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rio_de_Janeiro_(city)" title="Rio de Janeiro (city)">
        Rio de Janeiro
       </a>
       in Brazil made their dialects end up having some influence on the rest of the country. However, migration from the Northern states to the Southern states cause the influence to be a two-way phenomenon. Cultural issues also play their roles. Speakers of the
       <i>
        Gaúcho
       </i>
       accent, for example, usually have strong feelings about their own way of speaking and are largely uninfluenced by the other accents. Also, people of inner cities of the three southern states usually speak with a very notable German, Italian or Polish accent, and among the inhabitants of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(island)" title="Santa Catarina (island)">
        Santa Catarina island
       </a>
       (ie. insular
       <a href="/wiki/Florian%C3%B3polis" title="Florianópolis">
        Florianópolis
       </a>
       ), the Azorean Portuguese dialect, in its
       <a href="/wiki/Florianopolitan_dialect" title="Florianopolitan dialect">
        local variant
       </a>
       , predominates.
      </p>
      <p>
       Between Brazilian Portuguese, particularly in its most informal varieties, and European Portuguese, there can be considerable differences in grammar, aside from the differences in pronunciation and vocabulary. The most prominent ones concern the placement of clitic pronouns, and the use of subject pronouns as objects in the third person. Non-standard inflections are also common in colloquial Brazilian Portuguese.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Africa.2C_Asia_and_Oceania">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Africa,_Asia_and_Oceania">
        Africa, Asia and Oceania
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Africa, Asia and Oceania">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Portuguese_in_Africa" title="Portuguese in Africa">
        Portuguese in Africa
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language_in_Asia" title="Portuguese language in Asia">
        Portuguese language in Asia
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       For historical reasons, the dialects of Africa are generally closer to those of Portugal than the Brazilian dialects, but in some aspects of their phonology, especially the pronunciation of unstressed vowels, they resemble Brazilian Portuguese more than European Portuguese. They have not been studied as exhaustively as European and Brazilian Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>
       Asian Portuguese dialects are similar to the African ones and so are generally close to those of Portugal. In Macau, the syllable onset rhotic
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ʁ/
       </span>
       is pronounced as a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_uvular_fricative" title="Voiced uvular fricative">
        voiced uvular fricative
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʁ]
       </span>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Uvular_trill" title="Uvular trill">
        uvular trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ʀ]
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_features_of_some_dialects">
        Notable features of some dialects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notable features of some dialects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many dialects have special characteristics. Most of the differences are seen in phonetics and phonology, and here are some of the more prominent:
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conservative">
        Conservative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conservative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In some regions of northern Portugal and Brazil, the digraph
        <i>
         ou
        </i>
        still denotes a falling
        <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
         diphthong
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ou̯]
        </span>
        , but it has been monophthongized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [o]
        </span>
        by most speakers of Portuguese.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the dialects of Alto-Minho and Trás-os-Montes (northern Portugal), the digraph
        <i>
         ch
        </i>
        still denotes the
        <a href="/wiki/Affricate_consonant" title="Affricate consonant">
         affricate
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        , as in
        <a href="/wiki/Galicia_(Spain)" title="Galicia (Spain)">
         Galicia
        </a>
        , but for most speakers, it has merged with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʃ/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Some dialects of northern Portugal still contrast the
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Predorsodental&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Predorsodental (page does not exist)">
         predorsodental
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sibilant_consonant" title="Sibilant consonant">
         sibilants
        </a>
        <i>
         c/ç
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         z
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        with
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apicoalveolar" title="Apicoalveolar">
         apicoalveolar
        </a>
        sibilants
        <i>
         s(s)
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s̺/
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         s
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z̺/
        </span>
        , with
        <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
         minimal pairs
        </a>
        such as
        <i>
         passo
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /pas̺u/
        </span>
        "step" and
        <i>
         paço
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /pasu/
        </span>
        "palace" or
        <i>
         coser
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kuz̺eɾ/
        </span>
        "to sew" and
        <i>
         cozer
        </i>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /kuzeɾ/
        </span>
        "to cook", which are
        <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
         homophones
        </a>
        in most dialects. The other dialects of northern Portugal that have lost this distinction have apicoalveolar sibilants instead of the predorsodental fricatives, found in all southern dialects of Portugal as well as in Brazil. In those dialects, they also appear in syllable codas instead of the
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        realizations that can be observed in all southern dialects.
       </li>
       <li>
        In northern Portugal, the pronoun
        <i>
         vós
        </i>
        and its associated verb forms are still in use.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Alentejo and parts of the Algarve (southern Portugal), one finds word-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [i]
        </span>
        where standard EP has
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɨ]
        </span>
        , a feature shared with BP.
       </li>
       <li>
        Also in Brazil, Alentejo and Algarve, progressive constructions are formed with the gerund form of verbs instead of
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        followed by the infinitive that one finds in most dialects of Portugal:
        <i>
         está chovendo
        </i>
        vs.
        <i>
         está a chover
        </i>
        ("it's raining").
       </li>
       <li>
        In Brazil, original voiced intervocalic stops are still pronounced as such,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [vidɐ], [kabu]
        </span>
        instead of the normal Portugal pronunciation
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [viðɐ], [kaβu]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In Brazil, all five vowels
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [a e i o u]
        </span>
        are usually pronounced clearly in unstressed pretonic syllables like in stressed syllables, while in Portugal they are generally reduced to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɐ ɨ i u u]
        </span>
        . However, some words in some Brazilian accents (esp. in Rio) have pretonic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [e o]
        </span>
        raised to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [i u]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Innovative">
        Innovative
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Innovative">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In central and southern Portugal (except the city of Lisbon and its vicinity), the diphthong
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ei̯/
        </span>
        is monophthongized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [e]
        </span>
        . The nasal diphthong
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ẽi̯/
        </span>
        is often monophthongized to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ẽ]
        </span>
        as well.
       </li>
       <li>
        In and near Lisbon,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ei̯/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ẽi̯/
        </span>
        are pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɐi̯]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɐ̃i̯]
        </span>
        , respectively. Furthermore, stressed
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɐ]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɐi̯]
        </span>
        before a
        <a href="/wiki/Palato-alveolar_consonant" title="Palato-alveolar consonant">
         palato-alveolar
        </a>
        or a
        <a href="/wiki/Palatal_consonant" title="Palatal consonant">
         palatal consonant
        </a>
        followed by another vowel.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the dialect of the Beiras (
        <a href="/wiki/Beira_Interior_Norte" title="Beira Interior Norte">
         Beira Interior Norte
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Cova_da_Beira_Subregion" title="Cova da Beira Subregion">
         Cova da Beira
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Beira_Interior_Sul" title="Beira Interior Sul">
         Beira Interior Sul
        </a>
        ) in central Portugal, the sibilant
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʒ/
        </span>
        occurs at the end of words, before another word which starts with a vowel, instead of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In northern Portugal, the phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /m/
        </span>
        has a
        <a href="/wiki/Velar_nasal" title="Velar nasal">
         velar
        </a>
        allophone
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        at the end of words.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the dialects of Portalegre, Castelo Branco, Algarve (Barlavento area) and São Miguel Island (Azores), the
        <a href="/wiki/Near-close_near-front_rounded_vowel" title="Near-close near-front rounded vowel">
         near-front rounded vowel
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʏ]
        </span>
        replaces
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /u/
        </span>
        , in a process similar to the one that originated the
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        <i>
         u
        </i>
        . The dialect of São Miguel has also the
        <a href="/wiki/Close-mid_front_rounded_vowel" title="Close-mid front rounded vowel">
         front rounded vowel
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ø]
        </span>
        replacing
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        , as in
        <i>
         outra
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         boi
        </i>
        . This is a unique distinction in Azorean-Portuguese and is not the case in the way the population of the other eight islands in the Azores speak. This is the result of emigration trends; S. Miguel was at one time used as a refuse for convicts who were exiled by France. The other islands were largely populated by Flemish merchants who established cities such as Horta to cultivate the archipelagos riches and resources.
       </li>
       <li>
        In northern Portugal, the close vowels
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        may be pronounced as diphthongs, such as in "Porto", pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpwoɾtu]
        </span>
        , "quê":
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kje]
        </span>
        , "hoje":
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈwoi̯ʒɨ]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈwoʒɨ]
        </span>
        or even
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈwoi̯ʒɨ]
        </span>
       </li>
       <li>
        Some dialects of southern Portugal have gerund forms that inflect for person and number:
        <i>
         em chegandos
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         when you arrive
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         em chegândemos
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         when we arrive
        </i>
        ),
        <i>
         em chegandem
        </i>
        (
        <i>
         when you/they arrive
        </i>
        ). They are not used in writing.
       </li>
       <li>
        There are some dialectal differences in how word final [u] is realized. In Brazilian Portuguese, it is always pronounced. In Portugal, it is usually most audible when at the end of an utterance. In other contexts, it may be realized not at all or as mere
        <a href="/wiki/Labialization" title="Labialization">
         labialization
        </a>
        of the preceding consonant. The northern dialects tend to maintain it in most contexts. For instance, a sentence like
        <i>
         o meu irmão comprou um carro novo
        </i>
        ("my brother bought a new car") would be pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u ˈmew iɾˈmɐ̃w̃ kõˈpɾow u~ ˈkaʁu ˈnovu]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u ˈmew iɾˈmɐ̃w̃ kõˈpɾow ũ ˈkaʁʷ ˈnovu]
        </span>
        in those dialects. In the Lisbon dialect the last two words would instead be pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaʁʷ ˈnovu]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaʁʷ ˈnovʷ]
        </span>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaʁ ˈnovu]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈkaʁ ˈnovʷ]
        </span>
        . In southern Portugal, word final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [w]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [w̃]
        </span>
        are also affected so in Alentejo, the same sentence would sound
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u ˈme iɾˈmɐ̃ kõˈpɾo ũ ˈkaʁ ˈnovu]
        </span>
        (in that dialect, utterance final vowels are also noticeably very prolonged so a more accurate transcription might be
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈnovuː]
        </span>
        for this example). In the southernmost region of the country, the Algarve, the vowel is completely lost:
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [u ˈme iɾˈmɐ̃ kõˈpɾo ũ ˈkaʁ ˈnov]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In most of Brazil, syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/L-vocalization#Romance_languages" title="L-vocalization">
         vocalized
        </a>
        to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /w/
        </span>
        , which causes
        <i>
         mau
        </i>
        "bad" and
        <i>
         mal
        </i>
        "badly" to become homophones (although Brazil tends to use
        <i>
         ruim
        </i>
        in place of
        <i>
         mau
        </i>
        ). Similarly,
        <i>
         degrau
        </i>
        "step" and
        <i>
         jornal
        </i>
        "journal" rhyme, which results in false plurals such as
        <i>
         degrais
        </i>
        "steps" (vs. correct
        <i>
         degraus
        </i>
        ), by analogy with correct plural
        <i>
         jornais
        </i>
        . In the
        <a href="/wiki/Caipira" title="Caipira">
         <i>
          caipira
         </i>
         dialect
        </a>
        , and in parts of Goiás and Minas Gerais, syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /l/
        </span>
        is instead merged with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɾ/
        </span>
        , pronounced as an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         alveolar approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɹ]
        </span>
        in the Caipira way.
       </li>
       <li>
        The pronunciation of syllable-initial and syllable-final
        <i>
         r
        </i>
        varies considerably with dialect. See
        <a href="/wiki/Guttural_R#Portuguese" title="Guttural R">
         Guttural R in Portuguese
        </a>
        , for details. Syllable-initial ⟨r⟩ and doubled ⟨rr⟩ are pronounced as a guttural
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʁ]
        </span>
        in most cities in Portugal, but as a traditional trill
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        in rural Portugal. In Brazil, the sound is normally pronounced as an
        <i>
         unvoiced
        </i>
        guttural (
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [x], [χ] or [h]
        </span>
        ), which is also used for ⟨r⟩ at the end of syllables (except in the
        <a href="/wiki/Caipira" title="Caipira">
         <i>
          caipira
         </i>
         dialect
        </a>
        , which uses an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
         alveolar approximant
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɹ]
        </span>
        , and the
        <a href="/wiki/Gaucho" title="Gaucho">
         <i>
          gaúcho
         </i>
         dialect
        </a>
        , which uses an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_flap" title="Alveolar flap">
         alveolar flap
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ɾ]
        </span>
        or
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
         trill
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [r]
        </span>
        ). ⟨r⟩ at the end of words, in Brazil, is normally silent or barely pronounced. In
        <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
         Macau
        </a>
        , where Portuguese is spoken mostly as a second language, initial and intervocalic "r" is sometimes replaced with a diphthong, and ⟨r⟩ at the end of words (esp. when final-stressed) is sometimes silent.
       </li>
       <li>
        The pronunciation of syllable-final
        <i>
         s/x/z
        </i>
        also varies with dialect. See
        <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_phonology#Fricatives" title="Portuguese phonology">
         Portuguese phonology
        </a>
        for details. Portugal and Rio de Janeiro favor
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        , both before a consonant and finally. Most other parts of Brazil favor
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        , but in the
        <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">
         Northeast
        </a>
        ,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ʃ]
        </span>
        is often heard before consonants, especially
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t/
        </span>
        (but not at the end of words).
       </li>
       <li>
        In the Northeast of Brazil and, to an increasing extent, in Rio de Janeiro and elsewhere,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [j]
        </span>
        is inserted before final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        in a final-stressed word, which makes
        <i>
         mas
        </i>
        "but" and
        <i>
         mais
        </i>
        "more" homonyms, both pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [majs]
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [majʃ]
        </span>
        . Other affected examples are
        <i>
         faz
        </i>
        "he does",
        <i>
         dez
        </i>
        "ten",
        <i>
         nós
        </i>
        "we",
        <i>
         voz
        </i>
        "voice",
        <i>
         luz
        </i>
        "light",
        <i>
         Jesus
        </i>
        "Jesus", etc. Related forms like
        <i>
         fazem
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vozes
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         nosso
        </i>
        are unaffected since
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is no longer final.
       </li>
       <li>
        In most of Brazil,
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /t/, /d/
        </span>
        are
        <a href="/wiki/Palatalization_(phonetics)" title="Palatalization (phonetics)">
         palatalized
        </a>
        to
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ], [dʒ]
        </span>
        when they are followed by
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        . Common sources of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        are the unstressed ending
        <i>
         -e
        </i>
        , as in
        <i>
         gente
        </i>
        "people"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈʒẽtʃi]
        </span>
        and
        <i>
         de
        </i>
        "of"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒi]
        </span>
        , and the epenthetic
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /i/
        </span>
        in words such as
        <i>
         advogado
        </i>
        "lawyer"
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [adʒivoˈɡadu]
        </span>
        . Pefixes
        <i>
         de-
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         des-
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         dez-
        </i>
        (such as
        <i>
         dezoito
        </i>
        "eighteen") vary from word to word and from speaker to speaker between
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [de], [des]/[dez]/etc.
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [dʒi], [dʒis]/[dʒiz]/etc.
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Informal Brazilian Portuguese makes major changes in its use of pronouns:
        <ul>
         <li>
          Informal
          <i>
           tu
          </i>
          is dropped entirely in most regions along with all second-person singular verbal inflections. When
          <i>
           tu
          </i>
          survives, it is used with third-person inflections.
         </li>
         <li>
          Clitic
          <i>
           te
          </i>
          <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
           [tʃi]
          </span>
          survives as the normal clitic object pronoun corresponding to
          <i>
           você
          </i>
          .
         </li>
         <li>
          Clitic pronouns almost always precede the verb. Post-verbal clitics are seen only in formal contexts, and mesoclisis (
          <i>
           amar-te-ei
          </i>
          "I will love you") is practically incomprehensible to most Brazilians.
         </li>
         <li>
          Possessives
          <i>
           seu, sua
          </i>
          virtually always mean "your". To say "his, her", constructions like
          <i>
           o carro dele
          </i>
          "his car" or
          <i>
           o carro dela
          </i>
          "her car" are used.
         </li>
         <li>
          Third-person clitics
          <i>
           o, a, os, as
          </i>
          and combined clitics like
          <i>
           mo, no-lo
          </i>
          are virtually never heard in speech. Instead, the clitics are simply omitted, especially to refer to objects; or a subject pronoun is placed
          <i>
           after
          </i>
          the verb:
          <i>
           Eu levo
          </i>
          "I'll get it";
          <i>
           Vi ele
          </i>
          "I saw him".
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>

        </i>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

       <i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <i>

       </i>

       <a href="/wiki/Close_front_unrounded_vowel" title="Close front unrounded vowel">

       <a href="/wiki/South_Region,_Brazil" title="South Region, Brazil">

       </a>

       </i>
       /

       <a href="/wiki/Close_back_rounded_vowel" title="Close back rounded vowel">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/South_Region,_Brazil" title="South Region, Brazil">

       </a>

       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">

        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">

           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>

        </i>

        <i>

       <i>

       </i>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Boa_and_voa">
        <i>
         Boa
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         voa
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Boa and voa">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Boa
       </i>
       means "good" (feminine) and
       <i>
        voa
       </i>
       , "he/she/it flies". Unlike most of the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Iberian_languages" title="West Iberian languages">
        West Iberian languages
       </a>
       , Portuguese usually differs between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_plosive" title="Voiced bilabial plosive">
        voiced bilabial plosive
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiced labiodental fricative">
        voiced labiodental fricative
       </a>
       , but the distinction used to be absent in the dialects of the northern half of Portugal, and in some dialects spoken in the border of Brazil or Portugal and Spanish-speaking countries. Both are realized indistinctly as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_plosive" title="Voiced bilabial plosive">
        voiced bilabial plosive
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_bilabial_fricative" title="Voiced bilabial fricative">
        voiced bilabial fricative
       </a>
       , like in Spanish.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="M.C3.A1s.2C_mas_and_mais">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Más,_mas_and_mais">
        <i>
         Más
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         mas
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         mais
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Más, mas and mais">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Más
       </i>
       means "bad ones" (feminine),
       <i>
        mas
       </i>
       means "but" and
       <i>
        mais
       </i>
       means "more" or "most". In
       <a href="/wiki/Northeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Northeast Region, Brazil">
        Northeastern Brazil
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carioca" title="Carioca">
        the metropolitan area of Rio de Janeiro
       </a>
       , the vowels followed by
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_consonant" title="Coronal consonant">
        coronal
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
        fricatives
       </a>
       in the same syllable have a
       <a href="/wiki/Palatal_approximant" title="Palatal approximant">
        palatal approximant
       </a>
       pronounced between both. The feature is very distinguishable since this combination appears in the plural forms.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="X.C3.A1_and_ch.C3.A1">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Xá_and_chá">
        <i>
         Xá
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         chá
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Xá and chá">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        Xá
       </i>
       means "
       <a href="/wiki/Shah" title="Shah">
        shah
       </a>
       ", and
       <i>
        chá
       </i>
       means
       <a href="/wiki/Tea" title="Tea">
        tea
       </a>
       . At the beginning of words, &lt;x&gt; and &lt;ch&gt; are usually
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar fricative">
        voiceless palato-alveolar fricatives
       </a>
       , but &lt;ch&gt; is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_palato-alveolar_affricate" title="Voiceless palato-alveolar affricate">
        voiceless palato-alveolar affricate
       </a>
       in northern Portugal. The sound happens in other cases in
       <a href="/wiki/Southeast_Region,_Brazil" title="Southeast Region, Brazil">
        Southeastern Brazil
       </a>
       but disappeared in the rest of the Portuguese-speaking world.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mixed_languages">
        Mixed languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mixed languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Portu%C3%B1ol" title="Portuñol">
        Portunhol/Portuñol
       </a>
       : In regions where Spanish and Portuguese coexist, various types of
       <a href="/wiki/Language_contact" title="Language contact">
        language contact
       </a>
       have occurred, ranging from improvised
       <a href="/wiki/Code-switching" title="Code-switching">
        code-switching
       </a>
       between monolingual speakers of each language to more or less stable
       <a href="/wiki/Mixed_language" title="Mixed language">
        mixed languages
       </a>
       . They are often designated by the common term
       <i>
        portunhol
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        portuñol
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Closely_related_languages">
        Closely related languages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Closely related languages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
        Galician language
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fala_language" title="Fala language">
        Fala language
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        This section does not cover
        <a href="/wiki/Galician_language" title="Galician language">
         Galician
        </a>
        , which is treated as a separate language from Portuguese by Galician official institutions, or
        <a href="/wiki/Fala_language" title="Fala language">
         Fala
        </a>
        . For a discussion of the controversy regarding the status of Galician with respect to Portuguese, see
        <a href="/wiki/Reintegrationism" title="Reintegrationism">
         Reintegrationism
        </a>
        .
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riverense_Portu%C3%B1ol_language" title="Riverense Portuñol language">
        Portunhol Riverense
       </a>
       is spoken in the region between
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       , particularly in the twin cities of
       <a href="/wiki/Rivera" title="Rivera">
        Rivera
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Santana_do_Livramento" title="Santana do Livramento">
        Santana do Livramento
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The language must not be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Portu%C3%B1ol" title="Portuñol">
        Portuñol
       </a>
       , since it is not a mixing of Spanish and Portuguese, but a variety of Portuguese language developed in Uruguay back in the time of its first settlers. It has since suffered influence from Uruguayan Spanish and Brazilian Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>
       In academic circles, the Portuguese used by the northern population of Uruguay received the name "Dialectos Portugueses del Uruguay" (Uruguayan Portuguese Dialects). There's still no consensus if the language(s) is (are) a dialect or a creole, although the name given by linguists uses the term "dialect". There is also no consensus on how many varieties it has, with some studies indicating that there are at least two varieties, an urban one and a rural one, while others say there are six varieties, of which Riverense Portuñol is one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This Portuguese spoken in Uruguay is also referred by its speakers, depending on the region that they live, as
       <i>
        Bayano
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Riverense
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Fronterizo
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Brasilero
       </i>
       or simply
       <i>
        Portuñol
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_comprehension">
        Mutual comprehension
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Portuguese_dialects&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Mutual comprehension">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The different
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialects
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Accent_(sociolinguistics)" title="Accent (sociolinguistics)">
        accents
       </a>
       do not block cross-understanding among the educated. Meanwhile, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Basilect" title="Basilect">
        basilects
       </a>
       have diverged more. The unity of the language is reflected in the fact that early imported
       <a href="/wiki/Sound_film" title="Sound film">
        sound films
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Dubbing_(filmmaking)" title="Dubbing (filmmaking)">
        dubbed
       </a>
       into one version for the entire Portuguese-speaking market. Currently, films not originally in Portuguese (usually
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">
        Hollywood
       </a>
       productions) are dubbed separately into two accents: one for Portugal and one for Brazilian (using without regionalisms). When dubbing an African character in cartoons and TV and film productions, Portuguese people usually mimic an Angolan accent, as it is also commonly seen as the African accent of Portuguese. The popularity of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Telenovela" title="Telenovela">
         telenovelas
        </a>
       </i>
       and music familiarizes the speakers with other accents of Portuguese.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prescription_and_description" title="Prescription and description">
        Prescription
       </a>
       and a common cultural and literary tradition, among other factors, have contributed to the formation of a Standard Portuguese, which is the preferred form in formal settings, and is considered indispensable in academic and literary writing, the media, etc. This standard tends to disregard local grammatical, phonetic and lexical peculiarities, and draws certain extra features from the commonly acknowledged
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/canon" title="wiktionary:canon">
        canon
       </a>
       , preserving (for example) certain verb tenses considered "bookish" or archaic in most other dialects. Portuguese has two official written standards, (i)
       <a href="/wiki/Brazilian_Portuguese" title="Brazilian Portuguese">
        Brazilian Portuguese
       </a>
       (used chiefly in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ) and (ii)
       <a href="/wiki/European_Portuguese" title="European Portuguese">
        European Portuguese
       </a>
       (used in
       <a href="/wiki/Portugal" title="Portugal">
        Portugal
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Angola" title="Angola">
        Angola
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Verde" title="Cape Verde">
        Cape Verde
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/East_Timor" title="East Timor">
        East Timor
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Guinea-Bissau" title="Guinea-Bissau">
        Guinea-Bissau
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Macau" title="Macau">
        Macau
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mozambique" title="Mozambique">
        Mozambique
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Tom%C3%A9_and_Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="São Tomé and Príncipe">
        São Tomé and Príncipe
       </a>
       ). The written standards slightly differ in spelling and vocabulary, and are legally regulated. Unlike the written language, however, there is no spoken-Portuguese official standard, but the European Portuguese reference pronunciation is the educated speech of
       <a href="/wiki/Lisbon" title="Lisbon">
        Lisbon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
