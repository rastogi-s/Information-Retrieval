      <p>
       <b>
        Genoese
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        Zeneize
       </i>
       ) is a dialect of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian language
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       (the principal city of the
       <a href="/wiki/Liguria" title="Liguria">
        Liguria
       </a>
       region in Northern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ligurian_(Romance_language)" title="Ligurian (Romance language)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       is listed by
       <a href="/wiki/Ethnologue" title="Ethnologue">
        Ethnologue
       </a>
       as a language in its own right, of the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance
       </a>
       branch, and not to be confused with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_(ancient_language)" title="Ligurian (ancient language)">
        ancient Ligurian language
       </a>
       . Like the languages of
       <a href="/wiki/Lombardy" title="Lombardy">
        Lombardy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">
        Piedmont
       </a>
       , and surrounding regions, it is
       <a href="/wiki/Gallo-Italic_languages" title="Gallo-Italic languages">
        Gallo-Italic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The language is far from dying out. While most remaining speakers of it are elderly, many young people still speak the language. Further, there are several associations dedicated to keeping the language alive, like "O Castello" in
       <a href="/wiki/Chiavari" title="Chiavari">
        Chiavari
       </a>
       and "A Compagna" in
       <a href="/wiki/Genoa" title="Genoa">
        Genoa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Written literature has been produced in Genoese since the 13th century, but the spelling has never been completely regularized. However, since 2008, there is an official orthography set up by the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Acad%C3%A9mia_Lig%C3%B9stica_do_Br%C3%A9nno&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Académia Ligùstica do Brénno (page does not exist)">
        Académia Ligùstica do Brénno
       </a>
       , which attempts to put its script in order based on citizen speech of the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Portoria&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Portoria (page does not exist)">
        Portoria
       </a>
       area. Their rules, which may be seen
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.zeneize.net/grafia/arfabeto.htm" rel="nofollow">
        here
       </a>
       , are useful to write in all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian language
       </a>
       varieties.
      </p>
      <p>
       Genoese
       <a href="/wiki/Phonology" title="Phonology">
        phonology
       </a>
       includes a number of similarities with French. One is the heavily nasalized vowels before nasal consonants (in VN(C) sequences). It also occurs when Genoese speakers speak standard Italian. There used to be an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
        alveolar approximant
       </a>
       (English-like)
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ɹ/
       </span>
       opposed to an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_trill" title="Alveolar trill">
        alveolar trill
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       (using the 18th century spelling:
       <i>
        caro
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkaːɹu]
       </span>
       "dear" vs.
       <i>
        carro
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈkaːru]
       </span>
       "cart"), but it is no longer heard in the city. It may still survive in some rural areas of Liguria, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Calizzano" title="Calizzano">
        Calizzano
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sassello" title="Sassello">
        Sassello
       </a>
       . Audio samples may be heard
       <a class="external text" href="http://www2.hu-berlin.de/Vivaldi/index.php?id=m3048&amp;lang=de" rel="nofollow">
        here
       </a>
       . By far the most widespread type of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       today is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_tap" title="Alveolar tap">
        alveolar tap
       </a>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ɾ]
       </span>
       (identical to unstressed Standard Italian
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /r/
       </span>
       ). There are several distinctive local
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accent_(dialect)" title="Accent (dialect)">
        accents
       </a>
       of Genoese. Nervi, Quinto and Quarto are heard to the east of Genoa. Voltri, Prà, Pegli and Sestri are heard to the west. There are also accents of the central
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polcevera_Valley" title="Polcevera Valley">
        Polcevera Valley
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Bisagno_(stream)" title="Bisagno (stream)">
        Bisagno
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Genoese is also an influence on the
       <a href="/wiki/Llanito" title="Llanito">
        Llanito
       </a>
       vernacular of
       <a href="/wiki/Gibraltar" title="Gibraltar">
        Gibraltar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tongue_twisters">
        Tongue twisters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Genoese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Tongue twisters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Sò asæ s'a sâ a sä asæ pe sâ a säsissa.
        </i>
        = I don't have a clue whether the salt is going to be enough to salt the sausage.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Scia scîe scignôa, sciando scia xêua inscî scî.
        </i>
        = Ski, madam, skying you fly on skis.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         A-o mêu nêuo gh'è nêue nâe nêue; a ciû nêua de nêue nâe nêue a n'êu anâ.
        </i>
        = At the new pier there are nine new ships; the newest of the nine new ships doesn't want to go.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Gi'angiai g'han gi'oggi gi'uegge gi'unge cume gi'atri?
        </i>
        = Do angels have eyes, ears, and (finger)nails like everyone else? (variant of the Cogorno
        <i>
         comune
        </i>
        )
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Expressions">
        Expressions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Genoese_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Expressions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Son zeneize, rizo ræo, strenzo i denti e parlo ciæo.
        </i>
        = "I'm Genoese, I seldom laugh, I grind my teeth, and I say what I mean" (literally, "speak clearly").
       </li>
       <li>
        The child complains:
        <i>
         Ò famme.
        </i>
        = I'm hungry. The mother answers:
        <i>
         Gràttite e zenogge e fatte e lasagne.
        </i>
        = Scratch your knees and make lasagna.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Chi veû vive da bon crestiàn, da-i begghìn o stagghe lontàn.
        </i>
        = If you want to live as a good Christian, stay away from those who pretend to be devout; a traditional warning to beware of fanatics and hypocrites.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Sciuscià e sciurbì nu se peu.
        </i>
        = You can't have or do two contradicting things at the same time (literally, "you can't inhale and exhale").
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Belìn
        </i>
        ! = Wow! or Damn! (very informal) (literally the word means "
        <a href="/wiki/Penis" title="Penis">
         penis
        </a>
        ", but it lost its obscene meaning and is currently used as an
        <a href="/wiki/Intensifier" title="Intensifier">
         intensifier
        </a>
        in a lot of different expressions).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
