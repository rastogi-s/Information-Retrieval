      <p>
       The
       <b>
        voiceless dental non-sibilant fricative
       </b>
       is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Consonant" title="Consonant">
        consonantal
       </a>
       sound used in some
       <a href="/wiki/Spoken_language" title="Spoken language">
        spoken languages
       </a>
       . It is familiar to English speakers as the 'th' in
       <i>
        thing
       </i>
       . Though rather rare as a phoneme in the world's inventory of languages, it is encountered in some of the most widespread and influential (see below). The symbol in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       that represents this sound is ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        θ
       </span>
       ⟩, and the equivalent
       <a href="/wiki/X-SAMPA" title="X-SAMPA">
        X-SAMPA
       </a>
       symbol is
       <code>
        T
       </code>
       . The IPA symbol is the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_alphabet" title="Greek alphabet">
        Greek
       </a>
       letter
       <a href="/wiki/Theta" title="Theta">
        theta
       </a>
       , which is used for this sound in post-classical
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       , and the sound is thus often referred to as "
       <b>
        theta
       </b>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       The dental non-sibilant fricatives are often called "
       <a href="/wiki/Interdental_consonant" title="Interdental consonant">
        interdental
       </a>
       " because they are often produced with the tongue between the upper and lower
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">
        teeth
       </a>
       , and not just against the back of the upper or lower teeth, as they are with other
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental consonants
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       This sound and its
       <a href="/wiki/Voiced_dental_fricative" title="Voiced dental fricative">
        voiced counterpart
       </a>
       are rare phonemes. Among the more than 60 languages with over 10 million speakers, only
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Modern_Standard_Arabic" title="Modern Standard Arabic">
        Modern Standard Arabic
       </a>
       , Standard European
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Burmese_language" title="Burmese language">
        Burmese
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       have the voiceless dental non-sibilant fricative.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Speakers of languages and dialects without the sound sometimes have difficulty producing or distinguishing it from similar sounds, especially if they have had no chance to acquire it in childhood, and typically replace it with a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
        voiceless alveolar fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /s/
       </span>
       ) (as in
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesian_language" title="Indonesian language">
        Indonesian
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Voiceless_dental_stop" title="Voiceless dental stop">
        voiceless dental stop
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /t/
       </span>
       ), or a
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_labiodental_fricative" title="Voiceless labiodental fricative">
        voiceless labiodental fricative
       </a>
       (
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /f/
       </span>
       ); known respectively as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Th-alveolarization" title="Th-alveolarization">
        th-alveolarization
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Th-stopping" title="Th-stopping">
        th-stopping
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Th-fronting" title="Th-fronting">
        th-fronting
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Among
       <a href="/wiki/Turkic_languages" title="Turkic languages">
        Turkic languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bashkir_language" title="Bashkir language">
        Bashkir
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkmen_language" title="Turkmen language">
        Turkmen
       </a>
       have the voiceless dental non-sibilant fricative.
      </p>
      <p>
       The sound is known to have disappeared from a number of languages, e.g. from most of the
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_languages" title="Germanic languages">
        Germanic
       </a>
       languages or dialects, where it is retained only in
       <a href="/wiki/Scots_language" title="Scots language">
        Scots
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Elfdalian" title="Elfdalian">
        Elfdalian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Icelandic_language" title="Icelandic language">
        Icelandic
       </a>
       , but it is
       <a href="/wiki/Voiceless_alveolar_fricative#Voiceless_alveolar_non-sibilant_fricative" title="Voiceless alveolar fricative">
        alveolar
       </a>
       in the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has likewise disappeared from many
       <a href="/wiki/Semitic_languages" title="Semitic languages">
        Semitic languages
       </a>
       , such as
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_language" title="Hebrew language">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       and many modern
       <a href="/wiki/Varieties_of_Arabic" title="Varieties of Arabic">
        varieties of Arabic
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voiceless_dental_fricative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Features of the voiceless dental non-sibilant fricative:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
         manner of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        , which means it is produced by constricting air flow through a narrow channel at the place of articulation, causing
        <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
         turbulence
        </a>
        . It does not have the grooved tongue and directed airflow, or the high frequencies, of a
        <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
         sibilant
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
         place of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
         dental
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated with either the tip or the blade of the tongue at the upper
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Teeth" title="Teeth">
         teeth
        </a>
        , termed respectively
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Apical_consonant" title="Apical consonant">
          apical
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
          laminal
         </a>
        </i>
        . Note that most stops and liquids described as dental are actually
        <a href="/wiki/Denti-alveolar_consonant" title="Denti-alveolar consonant">
         denti-alveolar
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
         phonation
        </a>
        is voiceless, which means it is produced without vibrations of the vocal cords. In some languages the vocal cords are actively separated, so it is always voiceless; in others the cords are lax, so that it may take on the voicing of adjacent sounds.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is an
        <a href="/wiki/Oral_consonant" title="Oral consonant">
         oral consonant
        </a>
        , which means air is allowed to escape through the mouth only.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is a
        <a href="/wiki/Central_consonant" title="Central consonant">
         central consonant
        </a>
        , which means it is produced by directing the airstream along the center of the tongue, rather than to the sides.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
         airstream mechanism
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">
         pulmonic
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the
        <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">
         lungs
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">
         diaphragm
        </a>
        , as in most sounds.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Voiceless_denti-alveolar_sibilant">
        Voiceless denti-alveolar sibilant
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voiceless_dental_fricative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Voiceless denti-alveolar sibilant">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="infobox" style="width:22em;width: 16em;">

           <th style="background:#FFFFE6; border:0;">

            <div class="center">
             <div class="floatnone">
              <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voiceless_denti-alveolar_sibilant.png">
               <img alt="Voiceless denti-alveolar sibilant.png" data-file-height="122" data-file-width="130" height="122" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/9b/Voiceless_denti-alveolar_sibilant.png" width="130"/>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        voiceless denti-alveolar sibilant
       </b>
       is the only sibilant fricative in some dialects of
       <a href="/wiki/Andalusian_Spanish" title="Andalusian Spanish">
        Andalusian Spanish
       </a>
       . It has no official symbol in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet" title="International Phonetic Alphabet">
        International Phonetic Alphabet
       </a>
       , though its features would be transcribed ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̻̪
       </span>
       ⟩ or ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̪̻
       </span>
       ⟩ (using the ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ◌̻
       </span>
       ⟩, the diacritic marking a
       <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
        laminal consonant
       </a>
       , and ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        ◌̪
       </span>
       ⟩, the diacritic marking a
       <a href="/wiki/Dental_consonant" title="Dental consonant">
        dental consonant
       </a>
       ). It is usually represented by an ad-hoc symbol such as ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̄
       </span>
       ⟩, ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        θˢ̣
       </span>
       ⟩, or ⟨
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        s̟
       </span>
       ⟩ (
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_articulation#Advanced_and_retracted" title="Relative articulation">
        advanced diacritic
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="#CITEREFDalbor1980">
        Dalbor (1980)
       </a>
       describes this sound as follows: "
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̄]
       </span>
       is a voiceless, corono-dentoalveolar groove fricative, the so-called
       <i>
        s coronal
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        s plana
       </i>
       because of the relatively flat shape of the tongue body.... To this writer, the coronal
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̄]
       </span>
       , heard throughout Andalusia, should be characterized by such terms as "soft," "fuzzy," or "imprecise," which, as we shall see, brings it quite close to one variety of
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /θ/
       </span>
       … Canfield has referred, quite correctly, in our opinion, to this
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [s̄]
       </span>
       as "the lisping coronal-dental," and Amado Alonso remarks how close it is to the post-dental
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θ̦]
       </span>
       , suggesting a combined symbol
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [θˢ̣]
       </span>
       to represent it."
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features_2">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Voiceless_dental_fricative&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Features of the voiceless denti-alveolar sibilant:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
         manner of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Sibilant" title="Sibilant">
         sibilant
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        , which means it is generally produced by channeling air flow along a
        <a href="/wiki/Sulcalization" title="Sulcalization">
         groove
        </a>
        in the back of the tongue up to the place of articulation, at which point it is focused against the sharp edge of the nearly clenched teeth, causing high-frequency
        <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
         turbulence
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Place_of_articulation" title="Place of articulation">
         place of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Denti-alveolar" title="Denti-alveolar">
         denti-alveolar
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated with a flat tongue against the
        <a href="/wiki/Alveolar_ridge" title="Alveolar ridge">
         alveolar ridge
        </a>
        and upper teeth.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is normally
        <a href="/wiki/Laminal_consonant" title="Laminal consonant">
         laminal
        </a>
        , which means it is pronounced with the blade of the tongue.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dalbor_12-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Dalbor-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Phonation" title="Phonation">
         phonation
        </a>
        is voiceless, which means it is produced without vibrations of the vocal cords. In some languages the vocal cords are actively separated, so it is always voiceless; in others the cords are lax, so that it may take on the voicing of adjacent sounds.
       </li>
       <li>
        It is an
        <a href="/wiki/Oral_consonant" title="Oral consonant">
         oral consonant
        </a>
        , which means air is allowed to escape through the mouth only.
       </li>
       <li>
        Its
        <a href="/wiki/Manner_of_articulation" title="Manner of articulation">
         manner of articulation
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Fricative_consonant" title="Fricative consonant">
         fricative
        </a>
        , which means it is produced by constricting air flow through a narrow channel at the place of articulation, causing
        <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
         turbulence
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Airstream_mechanism" title="Airstream mechanism">
         airstream mechanism
        </a>
        is
        <a href="/wiki/Egressive_sound" title="Egressive sound">
         pulmonic
        </a>
        , which means it is articulated by pushing air solely with the
        <a href="/wiki/Lung" title="Lung">
         lungs
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Thoracic_diaphragm" title="Thoracic diaphragm">
         diaphragm
        </a>
        , as in most sounds.
       </li>
      </ul>
