      <p>
       <b>
        Talian
       </b>
       (or
       <b>
        Brazilian Venetian
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated8_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated8-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ,
       <small>
        Venetian pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Venetian" title="Help:IPA/Venetian">
         [taliˈaŋ]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        Italian:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian" title="Help:IPA/Italian">
         [taliˈan]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        Portuguese:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">
         [tɐliˈɐ̃]
        </a>
       </span>
       but
       <small>
        locally
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Portuguese" title="Help:IPA/Portuguese">
         [taliˈɐŋ]
        </a>
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Dialect" title="Dialect">
        dialect
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian language
       </a>
       , spoken primarily in the
       <a href="/wiki/Serra_Ga%C3%BAcha" title="Serra Gaúcha">
        Serra Gaúcha
       </a>
       region in the northeast of the state of
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">
        Rio Grande do Sul
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       . It is also spoken in other parts of Rio Grande do Sul, as well as in parts of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Espirito_Santo" title="Espirito Santo">
        Espirito Santo
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated9_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated9-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated10_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated10-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and of
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">
        Santa Catarina
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite the similar names, Talian is not derived from standard
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       (usually called
       <i>
        <span lang="pt" xml:lang="pt">
         <i>
          italiano gramático
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       or "grammatical Italian" in Brazil), but is mainly a mix of
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       dialects influenced by other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallo-Italian_languages" title="Gallo-Italian languages">
        Gallo-Italian languages
       </a>
       as well as local
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Italians" title="Italians">
        Italian
       </a>
       settlers first began arriving into these regions in a wave of immigration lasting from approximately 1875 to 1914.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These settlers were mainly from
       <a href="/wiki/Veneto" title="Veneto">
        Veneto
       </a>
       , a region in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Italy" title="Northern Italy">
        Northern Italy
       </a>
       , where
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       was spoken, but also from
       <a href="/wiki/Trentino" title="Trentino">
        Trentino
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Friuli-Venezia_Giulia" title="Friuli-Venezia Giulia">
        Friuli-Venezia Giulia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated2_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated2-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated7_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated7-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the south of Brazil these immigrants settled as smallholders in the region of Encosta da Serra. There they created three settlements: Conde D'Eu (now,
       <a href="/wiki/Garibaldi,_Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Garibaldi, Rio Grande do Sul">
        Garibaldi, Rio Grande do Sul
       </a>
       ), Dona Isabel (now
       <a href="/wiki/Bento_Gon%C3%A7alves,_Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Bento Gonçalves, Rio Grande do Sul">
        Bento Gonçalves, Rio Grande do Sul
       </a>
       ), and Campo dos Bugres (now
       <a href="/wiki/Caxias_do_Sul" title="Caxias do Sul">
        Caxias do Sul
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As more people arrived, the Italian settlement expanded beyond these localities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Approximately 100,000 immigrants from Northern Italy arrived between 1875 and 1910. As time went by, a uniquely southern Brazilian dialect emerged. Veneto became the basis for Italian-Brazilian regionalism.
      </p>
      <p>
       Talian was very much influenced not only by other Italian languages but by
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       , the national language of Brazil. However, because its grammar and lexicon remain predominantly Venetian, Talian is not considered a
       <a href="/wiki/Creole_language" title="Creole language">
        creole language
       </a>
       , despite the preponderance of non-Venetian
       <a href="/wiki/Loanword" title="Loanword">
        loanwords
       </a>
       . It has been estimated that there have been 130 books published in Talian, including works of both poetry and prose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parlons_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Parlons-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Like
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Riograndenser_Hunsr%C3%BCckisch" title="Riograndenser Hunsrückisch">
        Riograndenser Hunsrückisch
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        hunsriqueano riograndense
       </i>
       ), the main
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       dialect spoken by southern Brazilians of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_people" title="German people">
        German
       </a>
       origin, Talian has suffered great deprecation since the 1940s. At that time, then-President
       <a href="/wiki/Get%C3%BAlio_Vargas" title="Getúlio Vargas">
        Getúlio Vargas
       </a>
       started a campaign of nationalization (similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nacionalismo_(Argentine_political_movement)" title="Nacionalismo (Argentine political movement)">
        Nacionalismo
       </a>
       of neighboring
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ) to try to force non-Portuguese speakers of Brazil to "better integrate" into the national mainstream culture. Speaking Talian or German in public, especially in education and press, was forbidden.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Current_status">
        Current status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Talian_dialect&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Current status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Talian is mainly spoken in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Southern_Region,_Brazil" title="Southern Region, Brazil">
        southern Brazilian states
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Rio_Grande_do_Sul" title="Rio Grande do Sul">
        Rio Grande do Sul
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Catarina_(state)" title="Santa Catarina (state)">
        Santa Catarina
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Paran%C3%A1_(state)" title="Paraná (state)">
        Paraná
       </a>
       , as well as in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Espirito_Santo" title="Espirito Santo">
        Espirito Santo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated4_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated5_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated5-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated6_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated6-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated9_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated9-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated10_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated10-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nowadays, there are approximately 3 million people of Italian ancestry in Rio Grande do Sul, about 30% of the local population,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and approximately 1.7 million people in Espirito Santo, which accounts for 65% of the local population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated11_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated11-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to some estimates, there are up to one million speakers of Talian today; Ethnologue reported 4,000,000 speakers in the year 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ethnologue.com_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ethnologue.com-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the "Estado Novo" period of the government of
       <a href="/wiki/Get%C3%BAlio_Vargas" title="Getúlio Vargas">
        Getúlio Vargas
       </a>
       , the use of Talian was declared illegal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result of the traumas of Vargas' policies, there is, even to this day, a stigma attached to speaking these languages. However, in 2009, the state of Rio Grande do Sul approved a law declaring the Talian language to be an integral part of the historical heritage of the state.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_10-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2009, the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Serafina_Corr%C3%AAa" title="Serafina Corrêa">
        Serafina Corrêa
       </a>
       , in the State of Rio Grande do Sul, elected Talian as co-official language, alongside
       <a href="/wiki/Portuguese_language" title="Portuguese language">
        Portuguese
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, in 2014 Talian was declared to be part of the cultural heritage of Brazil (
       <i>
        Língua e referência cultural brasileira
       </i>
       ). According to the figure given at this time, there would be about 500,000 active speakers in 133 towns.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Newspapers in the Talian-speaking region feature articles written in the language. There are approximately 500 radio programs broadcast in Talian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Parlons_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Parlons-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
