    Arctic Circle

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Arctic Circle
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">
        most northerly
       </a>
       of the five major
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude" title="Circle of latitude">
        circles
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       as shown on
       <a href="/wiki/Map" title="Map">
        maps
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . It marks the northernmost point at which the
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        noon
       </a>
       sun is just visible on the
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstice
       </a>
       and the southernmost point at which the
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        midnight sun
       </a>
       is just visible on the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June solstice
       </a>
       . The region north of this circle is known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       , and the zone just to the south is called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Temperateness" title="Temperateness">
        Northern Temperate Zone
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As seen from the Arctic, the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is above the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       for 24 continuous
       <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">
        hours
       </a>
       at least once per year (and therefore visible at
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight" title="Midnight">
        midnight
       </a>
       ) and below the horizon for 24 continuous hours at least once per year (and therefore not visible at
       <a href="/wiki/Noon" title="Noon">
        noon
       </a>
       ). This is also true in the
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       region, south of the equivalent
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic_Circle" title="Antarctic Circle">
        Antarctic Circle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The position of the Arctic Circle is not fixed; as of 28 January 2018, it runs 66°33′47.0″ north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        Equator
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its latitude depends on the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       , which
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">
        fluctuates
       </a>
       within a margin of 2° over a 40,000-year period, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       resulting from the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        orbit of the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Consequently, the Arctic Circle is currently
       <a href="/wiki/Circle_of_latitude#Movement_of_the_Tropical_and_Polar_Circles" title="Circle of latitude">
        drifting northwards
       </a>
       at a speed of about 15 metres (49 feet) per year.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The word
       <i>
        arctic
       </i>
       comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       word ἀρκτικός (
       <i>
        arktikos
       </i>
       : "near the Bear, northern")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that from the word ἄρκτος (
       <i>
        arktos
       </i>
       : "
       <a href="/wiki/Bear" title="Bear">
        bear
       </a>
       ").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Midnight_sun_and_polar_night">
        Midnight sun and polar night
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Midnight sun and polar night">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="186" data-file-width="512" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/400px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/600px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg/800px-Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg.png 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Axial_tilt_vs_tropical_and_polar_circles.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Relationship of Earth's axial tilt (ε) to the tropical and polar circles
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        Midnight sun
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        Polar night
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Arctic Circle is the southernmost latitude in the
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere" title="Northern Hemisphere">
        Northern Hemisphere
       </a>
       at which the sun can remain continuously above or below the horizon for twenty-four hours; as a result, at least once each year at any location within the Arctic Circle the sun is
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        visible at local midnight
       </a>
       , and at least once it is
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        not visible at local noon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Directly on the Arctic Circle these events occur, in principle, exactly once per year: at the
       <a href="/wiki/June_solstice" title="June solstice">
        June
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstices
       </a>
       , respectively. However, because of
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        atmospheric refraction
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mirage" title="Mirage">
        mirages
       </a>
       , and because the sun appears as a disk and not a point, part of the midnight sun may be seen on the night of the northern
       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">
        summer solstice
       </a>
       up to about 50
       <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">
        minutes (′)
       </a>
       (90 km (56 mi)) south of the Arctic Circle; similarly, on the day of the northern
       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       , part of the sun may be seen up to about 50′ north of the Arctic Circle. That is true at
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       ; those limits increase with elevation above sea level, although in mountainous regions there is often no direct view of the true horizon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_habitation">
        Human habitation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Human habitation">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="651" data-file-width="1300" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg/220px-World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg/330px-World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg/440px-World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World_map_with_arctic_circle.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cylindrical projection showing the Arctic Circle in red
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arctic_peoples" title="Arctic peoples">
        Arctic peoples
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Only four million people live north of the Arctic Circle due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_the_Arctic" title="Climate of the Arctic">
        severe climate
       </a>
       ; nonetheless, some areas have been settled for thousands of years by
       <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples" title="Indigenous peoples">
        indigenous peoples
       </a>
       , who today make up 10% of the region's population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tens of thousands of years ago, waves of people migrated from eastern Siberia across the
       <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">
        Bering Strait
       </a>
       into North America to settle. Much later, in the historic period, there has been migration into some Arctic areas by Europeans and other immigrants.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The largest communities north of the Arctic Circle are situated in
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       :
       <a href="/wiki/Murmansk" title="Murmansk">
        Murmansk
       </a>
       (population 307,257),
       <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">
        Norilsk
       </a>
       (175,365),
       <a href="/wiki/Troms%C3%B8" title="Tromsø">
        Tromsø
       </a>
       (71,295) and
       <a href="/wiki/Vorkuta" title="Vorkuta">
        Vorkuta
       </a>
       (59,231).
       <a href="/wiki/Rovaniemi" title="Rovaniemi">
        Rovaniemi
       </a>
       (61,329) in
       <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
        Finland
       </a>
       is the largest settlement in the immediate vicinity of the Arctic Circle, lying 6 kilometres (4 miles) south of the line.
      </p>
      <p>
       In contrast, the largest North American community north of the Arctic Circle,
       <a href="/wiki/Sisimiut" title="Sisimiut">
        Sisimiut
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       ), has approximately 5,000 inhabitants. Of the
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canadian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       Arctic communities,
       <a href="/wiki/Barrow,_Alaska" title="Barrow, Alaska">
        Barrow, Alaska
       </a>
       is the largest settlement with about 4,000 inhabitants.
      </p>

      <p>
       The Arctic Circle is roughly 16,000 kilometres (9,900 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The area north of the Circle is about 20,000,000 km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       (7,700,000 sq mi) and covers roughly 4% of Earth's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Arctic Circle passes through the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic_Ocean" title="Arctic Ocean">
        Arctic Ocean
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Scandinavian_Peninsula" title="Scandinavian Peninsula">
        Scandinavian Peninsula
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/North_Asia" title="North Asia">
        North Asia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_America" title="Northern America">
        Northern America
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       . The land within the Arctic Circle is divided among 8 countries:
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
        Sweden
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
        Finland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Alaska" title="Alaska">
        Alaska
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Yukon" title="Yukon">
        Yukon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Territories" title="Northwest Territories">
        Northwest Territories
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nunavut" title="Nunavut">
        Nunavut
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Denmark" title="Denmark">
        Denmark
       </a>
       (Greenland) and
       <a href="/wiki/Iceland" title="Iceland">
        Iceland
       </a>
       (where it passes through the small offshore island of
       <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%ADmsey" title="Grímsey">
        Grímsey
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climate">
        Climate
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Arctic_Circle&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Climate">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Further information:
       <a href="/wiki/Climate_of_the_Arctic" title="Climate of the Arctic">
        Climate of the Arctic
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The climate inside the Arctic Circle is generally cold, but the coastal areas of Norway have a generally mild climate as a result of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_Stream" title="Gulf Stream">
        Gulf Stream
       </a>
       , which makes the ports of northern Norway and northwest Russia ice-free all year long. In the interior, summers can be quite warm, while winters are extremely cold. For example, summer temperatures in
       <a href="/wiki/Norilsk" title="Norilsk">
        Norilsk
       </a>
       , Russia will sometimes reach as high as 30 °C (86 °F), while the winter temperatures frequently fall below −50 °C (−58 °F).
      </p>
