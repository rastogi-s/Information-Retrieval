      <p>
       <b>
        Paraguayan Spanish
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="es" xml:lang="es">
         español paraguayo
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a version of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish language
       </a>
       spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">
        Paraguay
       </a>
       . In addition, it influences the speech of the
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentine
       </a>
       provinces of
       <a href="/wiki/Misiones_Province" title="Misiones Province">
        Misiones
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Corrientes_Province" title="Corrientes Province">
        Corrientes
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Formosa_Province" title="Formosa Province">
        Formosa
       </a>
       , and, to a lesser extent,
       <a href="/wiki/Chaco_Province" title="Chaco Province">
        Chaco
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Guarani_language" title="Guarani language">
        Guarani language
       </a>
       is co-official with Spanish in Paraguay,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-March_2012_NYT_Article_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-March_2012_NYT_Article-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and most Paraguayans speak both languages.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Guaraní is the home language of more than half the population of Paraguay, with higher proportions of its use in rural areas, and those who speak Spanish at home slightly in the majority in the cities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Swedish linguist Bertil Malmberg visited Paraguay in 1946 and observed several features of Spanish pronunciation that he attributed to Guaraní influence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Guaraní origin of many of these features, however, has been questioned by other researchers, who document them in dialects not in contact with Guaraní.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Paraguayan_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The unique features of Paraguayan Spanish developed in part due to Paraguay's early isolation; for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Jos%C3%A9_Gaspar_Rodr%C3%ADguez_de_Francia" title="José Gaspar Rodríguez de Francia">
        José Gaspar Rodríguez de Francia
       </a>
       , the country's president until 1840, sealed Paraguay's borders. Other experiences with geographic, political, and economic isolation relative to its neighbours allowed Spanish spoken in Paraguay to develop its own unique characteristics, even apart from the wide-ranging influence of Guarani.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-March_2012_NYT_Article_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-March_2012_NYT_Article-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Paraguayan Spanish shares many similarities with
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       (that is, the variety spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ) such as the use of the
       <a href="/wiki/Voseo" title="Voseo">
        voseo
       </a>
       and various words and phrases.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Like all American dialects of Spanish, Paraguayan Spanish has
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seseo" title="Seseo">
         seseo
        </a>
        : the traditional phoneme
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /θ/
        </span>
        merges with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Syllable-final
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /r/
        </span>
        is pronounced as
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alveolar_approximant" title="Alveolar approximant">
          ɹ
         </a>
         ]
        </span>
        (as in American English
        <i>
         <b>
          r
         </b>
         ed
        </i>
        ) before /l/ or /s/, influenced by a substrate from Native American languages;
        <i>
         perla
        </i>
        ('pearl') and
        <i>
         verso
        </i>
        ("verse") are pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈpeɹla]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ˈbeɹso]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Absence of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Ye%C3%ADsmo" title="Yeísmo">
          yeísmo
         </a>
        </i>
        , the historical merger of the
        <a href="/wiki/Phoneme" title="Phoneme">
         phoneme
        </a>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʎ/
        </span>
        (spelled &lt;ll&gt;) with
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ʝ ~ j/
        </span>
        (spelled &lt;y&gt;). For speakers with
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        , the verbs
        <i>
         cayó
        </i>
        's/he fell' and
        <i>
         calló
        </i>
        's/he fell silent' are
        <a href="/wiki/Homophone" title="Homophone">
         homophones
        </a>
        , both pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʝo ~ kaˈjo]
        </span>
        . (In dialects that lack
        <i>
         yeísmo
        </i>
        , maintaining the historical distinction, the two words are pronounced respectively
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʝo ~ kaˈjo]
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [kaˈʎo]
        </span>
        .)
        <i>
         Yeísmo
        </i>
        characterizes the speech of most Spanish-speakers both in Spain and in the Americas.
       </li>
      </ul>
