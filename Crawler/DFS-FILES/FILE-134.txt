      <p>
       The
       <b>
        transverse Mercator
       </b>
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        map projection
       </a>
       is an adaptation of the standard
       <a href="/wiki/Mercator_projection" title="Mercator projection">
        Mercator projection
       </a>
       . The transverse version is widely used in national and international mapping systems around the world, including the
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator_coordinate_system" title="Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system">
        UTM
       </a>
       . When paired with a suitable
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Datum_(geodesy)" title="Datum (geodesy)">
        geodetic datum
       </a>
       , the transverse Mercator delivers high accuracy in zones less than a few degrees in east-west extent.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Standard_and_transverse_aspects">
        Standard and transverse aspects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Standard and transverse aspects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The transverse Mercator projection is the
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection#Aspect_of_the_projection" title="Map projection">
        transverse aspect
       </a>
       of the standard (or
       <i>
        Normal
       </i>
       ) Mercator projection. They share the same underlying mathematical construction and consequently the transverse Mercator inherits many traits from the normal Mercator:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Both
        <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
         projections
        </a>
        are
        <a href="/wiki/Map_projection#Cylindrical" title="Map projection">
         cylindrical
        </a>
        : for the Normal Mercator, the axis of the cylinder coincides with the polar axis and the line of tangency with the equator. For the transverse Mercator, the axis of the cylinder lies in the equatorial plane, and the line of tangency is any chosen meridian, thereby designated the
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Meridian_(geography)" title="Meridian (geography)">
          central meridian
         </a>
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Both projections may be modified to secant forms, which means the scale has been reduced so that the cylinder slices through the model globe.
       </li>
       <li>
        Both exist in spherical and
        <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
         ellipsoidal
        </a>
        versions.
       </li>
       <li>
        Both projections are
        <a href="/wiki/Conformal_map" title="Conformal map">
         conformal
        </a>
        , so that the
        <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">
         point scale
        </a>
        is independent of direction and
        <i>
         local
        </i>
        shapes are well preserved;
       </li>
       <li>
        Both projections have constant scale on the line of tangency (the equator for the normal Mercator and the central meridian for the transverse).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Since the central meridian of the transverse Mercator can be chosen at will, it may be used to construct highly accurate maps (of narrow width) anywhere on the globe. The secant, ellipsoidal form of the transverse Mercator is the most widely applied of all projections for accurate large scale maps.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spherical_transverse_Mercator">
        Spherical transverse Mercator
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spherical transverse Mercator">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In constructing a map on any projection, a
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_Earth" title="Spherical Earth">
        sphere
       </a>
       is normally chosen to model the Earth when the extent of the mapped region exceeds a few hundred kilometers in length in both dimensions. For maps of smaller regions, an
       <a href="/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth" title="Figure of the Earth">
        ellipsoidal model
       </a>
       must be chosen if greater accuracy is required; see next section. The spherical form of the transverse Mercator projection was one of the seven new projections presented, in 1772, by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Lambert" title="Johann Heinrich Lambert">
        Johann Heinrich Lambert
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lambert_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lambert-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wangerin_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wangerin-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (The text is also available in a modern English translation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tobler_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tobler-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) Lambert did not name his projections; the name
       <i>
        transverse Mercator
       </i>
       dates from the second half of the nineteenth century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-flattening_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-flattening-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The principal properties of the transverse projection are here presented in comparison with the properties of the normal projection.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsoidal_transverse_Mercator">
        Ellipsoidal transverse Mercator
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ellipsoidal transverse Mercator">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ellipsoidal form of the transverse Mercator projection was developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss" title="Carl Friedrich Gauss">
        Carl Friedrich Gauss
       </a>
       in 1825
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-gauss1825_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-gauss1825-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and further analysed by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Heinrich_Louis_Kr%C3%BCger" title="Johann Heinrich Louis Krüger">
        Johann Heinrich Louis Krüger
       </a>
       in 1912.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kruger_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kruger-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The projection is known by several names: Gauss Conformal or Gauss-Krüger in Europe; the transverse Mercator in the US; or Gauss–Krüger transverse Mercator generally. The projection is conformal with a constant scale on the central meridian. (There are other conformal generalisations of the transverse Mercator from the sphere to the ellipsoid but only Gauss-Krüger has a constant scale on the central meridian.) Throughout the twentieth century the Gauss–Krüger transverse Mercator was adopted, in one form or another, by many nations (and international bodies);
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in addition it provides the basis for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator" title="Universal Transverse Mercator">
        Universal Transverse Mercator
       </a>
       series of projections. The Gauss–Krüger projection is now the most widely used projection in accurate large scale mapping.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The projection, as developed by Gauss and Krüger, was expressed in terms of low order power series which were assumed to diverge in the east-west direction, exactly as in the spherical version. This was proved to be untrue by British cartographer E. H. Thompson, whose unpublished exact (closed form) version of the projection, reported by L. P. Lee in 1976,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_exact_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lee_exact-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       showed that the ellipsoidal projection is finite (below). This is the most striking difference between the spherical and ellipsoidal versions of the transverse Mercator projection: Gauss–Krüger gives a reasonable projection of the
       <i>
        whole
       </i>
       ellipsoid to the plane, although its principal application is to accurate large scale mapping "close" to the central meridian.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">

         </a>

          <div class="magnify">

          Ellipsoidal transverse Mercator: a finite projection.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Near the central meridian (Greenwich in the above example) the projection has low distortion and the shapes of Africa, western Europe, the British Isles, Greenland, and Antarctica compare favourably with a globe.
       </li>
       <li>
        The central regions of the transverse projections on sphere and ellipsoid are indistinguishable on the small scale projections shown here.
       </li>
       <li>
        The meridians at 90° east and west of the chosen central meridian project to horizontal lines through the poles. The more distant hemisphere is projected above the north pole and below the south pole.
       </li>
       <li>
        The equator bisects Africa, crosses South America and then continues onto the complete outer boundary of the projection; the top and bottom edges and the right and left edges must be identified (i.e. they represent the same lines on the globe). (Indonesia is bisected.)
       </li>
       <li>
        Distortion increases towards the right and left boundaries of the projection but it does not increase to infinity. Note the Galapagos Islands where the 90° west meridian meets the equator at bottom left.
       </li>
       <li>
        The map is conformal. Lines intersecting at any specified angle on the ellipsoid project into lines intersecting at the same angle on the projection. In particular parallels and meridians intersect at 90°.
       </li>
       <li>
        The point scale factor is independent of direction at any point so that the shape of a
        <i>
         small
        </i>
        region is reasonably well preserved. The necessary condition is that the magnitude of scale factor must not vary too much over the region concerned. Note that while South America is distorted greatly the island of Ceylon is small enough to be reasonably shaped although it is far from the central meridian.
       </li>
       <li>
        The choice of central meridian greatly affects the appearance of the projection. If 90°W is chosen then the whole of the Americas is reasonable. If 145°E is chosen the Far East is good and Australia is oriented with north up.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In most applications the
       <a href="/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Kr%C3%BCger_coordinate_system" title="Gauss–Krüger coordinate system">
        Gauss–Krüger coordinate system
       </a>
       is applied to a narrow strip near the central meridians where the differences between the spherical and ellipsoidal versions are small, but nevertheless important in accurate mapping. Direct series for scale, convergence and distortion are functions of eccentricity and both latitude and longitude on the ellipsoid: inverse series are functions of
       <a href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics)#Ellipses" title="Eccentricity (mathematics)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       and both
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       on the projection. In the secant version the lines of true scale on the projection are no longer parallel to central meridian; they curve slightly. The convergence angle between projected meridians and the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       constant grid lines is no longer zero (except on the equator) so that a grid bearing must be corrected to obtain an azimuth from true north. The difference is small, but not negligible, particularly at high latitudes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Implementations_of_the_Gauss.E2.80.93Kr.C3.BCger_projection">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Implementations_of_the_Gauss–Krüger_projection">
        Implementations of the Gauss–Krüger projection
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Implementations of the Gauss–Krüger projection">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In his 1912
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kruger_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-kruger-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       paper, Krüger presented two distinct solutions, distinguished here by the expansion parameter:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Krüger–
         <i>
          n
         </i>
        </b>
        (paragraphs 5 to 8): Formulae for the direct projection, giving the coordinates
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        , are fourth order expansions in terms of the third flattening,
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        (the ratio of the difference and sum of the major and minor axes of the ellipsoid). The coefficients are expressed in terms of latitude (
        <i>
         φ
        </i>
        ), longitude (
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        ), major axis (
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        ) and eccentricity (
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        ). The inverse formulae for
        <i>
         φ
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        are also fourth order expansions in
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        but with coefficients expressed in terms of
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Krüger–
         <i>
          λ
         </i>
        </b>
        (paragraphs 13 and 14): Formulae giving the projection coordinates
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        are expansions (of orders 5 and 4 respectively) in terms of the longitude
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        , expressed in radians: the coefficients are expressed in terms of
        <i>
         φ
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        . The inverse projection for
        <i>
         φ
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        are sixth order expansions in terms of the ratio
        <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
         <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
          <i>
           x
          </i>
         </span>
         <span class="visualhide">
          /
         </span>
         <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
          <i>
           a
          </i>
         </span>
        </span>
        , with coefficients expressed in terms of
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         a
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         e
        </i>
        . (See
        <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Mercator:_Redfearn_series" title="Transverse Mercator: Redfearn series">
         Transverse Mercator: Redfearn series
        </a>
        .)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The Krüger–
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       series were the first to be implemented, possibly because they were much easier to evaluate on the hand calculators of the mid twentieth century.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Lee–Redfearn–OSGB
        </b>
        : In 1945, L. P. Lee
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_series_9-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-lee_series-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        confirmed the
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        expansions of Krüger and proposed their adoption by the OSGB
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osgb_10-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-osgb-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        but Redfearn (1948)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Redfearn_11-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Redfearn-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        pointed out that they were not accurate because of (a) the relatively high latitudes of Great Britain and (b) the great width of the area mapped, over 10 degrees of longitude. Redfearn extended the series to eighth order and examined which terms were necessary to attain an accuracy of 1 mm (ground measurement). The
        <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Mercator:_Redfearn_series" title="Transverse Mercator: Redfearn series">
         Redfearn series
        </a>
        are still the basis of the OSGB map projections.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-osgb_10-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-osgb-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Thomas–UTM
        </b>
        : The
        <i>
         λ
        </i>
        expansions of Krüger were also confirmed by Paul Thomas in 1952:
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        they are readily available in Snyder.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-snyder_13-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-snyder-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        His projection formulae, completely equivalent to those presented by Redfearn, were adopted by the United States Defence Mapping Agency as the basis for the
        <a href="/wiki/Universal_Transverse_Mercator_coordinate_system" title="Universal Transverse Mercator coordinate system">
         UTM
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-utm_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-utm-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        They are also incorporated into the Geotrans
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        coordinate converter made available by the United States National Geospatial-Intelligence Agency
        <a class="external autonumber" href="http://earth-info.nga.mil/GandG/geotrans/#zzzb1%7CNGA" rel="nofollow">
         [3]
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Other countries
        </b>
        : The Redfearn series are the basis for geodetic mapping in many countries: Australia, Germany, Canada, South Africa to name but a few. (A list is given in Appendix A.1 of Stuifbergen 2009.)
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Many variants of the Redfearn series have been proposed but only those adopted by national cartographic agencies are of importance. For an example of modifications which do not have this status see
        <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Mercator:_Bowring_series" title="Transverse Mercator: Bowring series">
         Transverse Mercator: Bowring series
        </a>
        ). All such modifications have been eclipsed by the power of modern computers and the development of high order
        <i>
         n
        </i>
        -series outlined below. The precise Redfearn series, although of low order, cannot be disregarded as they are still enshrined in the quasi-legal definitions of OSGB and UTM etc.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span id="Exact_Gauss.E2.80.93Kr.C3.BCger_and_accuracy_of_the_truncated_series">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exact_Gauss–Krüger_and_accuracy_of_the_truncated_series">
        Exact Gauss–Krüger and accuracy of the truncated series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Exact Gauss–Krüger and accuracy of the truncated series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An exact solution by E. H. Thompson is described by L. P. Lee.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lee_exact_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lee_exact-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is constructed in terms of elliptic functions (defined in chapters 19 and 22 of the NIST
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">

        </a>
       </sup>
       handbook) which can be calculated to arbitrary accuracy using algebraic computing systems such as Maxima.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such an implementation of the exact solution is described by Karney (2011).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-karney_23-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-karney-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The exact solution is a valuable tool in assessing the accuracy of the truncated
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       and λ series. For example, the original 1912 Krüger–
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       series compares very favourably with the exact values: they differ by less than 0.31 μm within 1000 km of the central meridian and by less than 1 mm out to 6000 km. On the other hand the difference of the Redfearn series used by Geotrans and the exact solution is less than 1 mm out to a longitude difference of 3 degrees, corresponding to a distance of 334 km from the central meridian at the equator but a mere 35 km at the northern limit of an UTM zone. Thus the Krüger–
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       series are very much better than the Redfearn λ series.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Redfearn series becomes much worse as the zone widens. Karney discusses Greenland as an instructive example. The long thin landmass is centred on 42W and, at its broadest point, is no more than 750 km from that meridian while the span in longitude reaches almost 50 degrees. Krüger–
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       is accurate to within 1 mm but the Redfearn version of the Krüger–
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       series has a maximum error of 1 kilometre.
      </p>
      <p>
       Karney's own 8th-order (in
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       ) series is accurate to 5 nm within 3900 km of the central meridian.
      </p>

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The convergence angle
       <i>
        γ
       </i>
       at a point on the projection is defined by the angle measured
       <i>
        from
       </i>
       the projected meridian, which defines true north,
       <i>
        to
       </i>
       a grid line of constant
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       , defining grid north. Therefore
       <i>
        γ
       </i>
       is positive in the quadrant north of the equator and east of the central meridian and also in the quadrant south of the equator and west of the central meridian. The convergence must be added to a grid bearing to obtain a bearing from true north. For the secant transverse Mercator the convergence may be expressed
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-merc_26-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-merc-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       either in terms of the geographical coordinates or in terms of the projection coordinates:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>

      <h2>
       <span id="Coordinates.2C_grids.2C_eastings_and_northings">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coordinates,_grids,_eastings_and_northings">
        Coordinates, grids, eastings and northings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transverse_Mercator_projection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Coordinates, grids, eastings and northings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The projection coordinates resulting from the various developments of the ellipsoidal transverse Mercator are Cartesian coordinates such that the central meridian corresponds to the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       axis and the equator corresponds to the
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       axis. Both
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       are defined for all values of
       <i>
        λ
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        ϕ
       </i>
       . The projection does not define a grid: the grid is an independent construct which could be defined arbitrarily. In practice the national implementations, and UTM, do use grids aligned with the Cartesian axes of the projection, but they are of finite extent, with origins which need not coincide with the intersection of the central meridian with the equator.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        true grid origin
       </b>
       is always taken on the central meridian so that grid coordinates will be negative west of the central meridian. To avoid such negative grid coordinates, standard practice defines a
       <b>
        false origin
       </b>
       to the west (and possibly north or south) of the grid origin: the coordinates relative to the false origin define
       <b>
        eastings
       </b>
       and
       <b>
        northings
       </b>
       which will always be positive. The
       <b>
        false easting
       </b>
       ,
       <i>
        E
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       , is the distance of the true grid origin east of the false origin. The
       <b>
        false northing
       </b>
       ,
       <i>
        N
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       , is the distance of the true grid origin north of the false origin. If the true origin of the grid is at latitude
       <i>
        φ
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       on the central meridian and the scale factor the central meridian is
       <i>
        k
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       then these definitions give eastings and northings by:
      </p>

      <p>
       The terms "eastings" and "northings" do not mean strict east and north directions. Grid lines of the transverse projection, other than the
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        y
       </i>
       axes, do not run north-south or east-west as defined by parallels and meridians. This is evident from the global projections shown above. Near the central meridian the differences are small but measurable. The difference between the north-south grid lines and the true meridians is the
       <a href="/wiki/Transverse_Mercator_projection#Convergence" title="Transverse Mercator projection">
        angle of convergence
       </a>
       .
      </p>
