      <p>
       <b>
        Sassarese
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        Sassaresu
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Turritanu
       </i>
       ) is an
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian language
       </a>
       and transitional between
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_language" title="Corsican language">
        Corsican
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       . It is regarded as a
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsican
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       language because of
       <a href="/wiki/Sassari" title="Sassari">
        Sassari
       </a>
       's historic ties (and neighborhood) with
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">
        Corsica
       </a>
       . Despite the heavy Sardinian influences (especially in the vocabulary and phonetics), it still keeps its
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan
       </a>
       roots, which closely relate it to
       <a href="/wiki/Gallurese_dialect" title="Gallurese dialect">
        Gallurese
       </a>
       . The latter is regarded as a Corsican dialect despite the geographic location, although this attribution is a matter of controversy. It can be considered a transitional language between
       <a href="/wiki/Italo-Dalmatian_languages" title="Italo-Dalmatian languages">
        Italo-Dalmatian languages
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cos_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cos-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cor_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cor-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Max-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinian_language" title="Sardinian language">
        Sardinian
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has several similarities to
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and in particular the old dialects of Italian from
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscany" title="Tuscany">
        Tuscany
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cos_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cos-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cor_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cor-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Max_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Max-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sassarese is spoken by approximately 100,000 people, out of a total population of 175,000, in the northwest coastal areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        Sardinia
       </a>
       , Italy. Large Sassarese-speaking communities are present in
       <a href="/wiki/Sassari" title="Sassari">
        Sassari
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Stintino" title="Stintino">
        Stintino
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sorso" title="Sorso">
        Sorso
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Porto_Torres" title="Porto Torres">
        Porto Torres
       </a>
       . Sassarese's transition varieties towards
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gallurese" title="Gallurese">
        Gallurese
       </a>
       , known as the
       <i>
        Castellanesi
       </i>
       dialects, can be heard in
       <a href="/wiki/Castelsardo" title="Castelsardo">
        Castelsardo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tergu" title="Tergu">
        Tergu
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Sedini" title="Sedini">
        Sedini
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sassarese emerged as an urban language of commerce in the latter part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Giudicati" title="Giudicati">
        age of the Giudicati
       </a>
       (13th–14th century); it is based on a mixture of different languages, namely
       <a href="/wiki/Corsican_language" title="Corsican language">
        Corsican
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tuscan_dialect" title="Tuscan dialect">
        Tuscan
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ligurian_language_(Romance)" title="Ligurian language (Romance)">
        Ligurian
       </a>
       ; a strong
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sardo_logudorese" title="Sardo logudorese">
        Logudorese
       </a>
       influence can be felt in its phonetics, syntax, and vocabulary; a minor influence in vocabulary was exercised by
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       . There exist many modern and older works both on and in Sassarese, and a number of cultural, social, and theatre events are held regularly in connection with it.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1943 the
       <a href="/wiki/Germans" title="Germans">
        German
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_linguists" title="List of linguists">
        linguist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Leopold_Wagner" title="Max Leopold Wagner">
        Max Leopold Wagner
       </a>
       wrote:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ... A dialect of the people which, by all indications, was formed gradually from the 16th century, after several very deadly plagues decimated the population of the city; the bulk of the survivors were of Pisan and Corsican origin, there were even Genovese. Thus was born this hybrid dialect which is now spoken in Sassari, Porto Torres and Sorso, whose base is Tuscan corrupted with traces of Genovese, in addition to not a few Sardinian words.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Max Leopold Wagner,
         <i>
          "The problem of the geographical region to be attributed to Gallurese and Sassarese" in "Neolatin Culture 3" (1943), pp. 243–267
         </i>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ... un dialetto plebeo che, secondo tutti gli indizi, si stava formando a poco a poco a partire dal sec. XVI, dopo che varie pestilenze mortalissime avevano decimato la popolazione della città; dei superstiti la massima parte era di origine pisana e còrsa, e non mancavano neanche i genovesi. Così nacque quel dialetto ibrido che oggi si parla a Sassari, a Porto Torres ed a Sorso, la cui base è un toscano corrotto con qualche traccia genovese, e con non pochi vocaboli sardi.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Max Leopold Wagner,
         <i>
          "La questione del posto da assegnare al gallurese e al sassarese" in "Cultura Neolatina 3", 1943, pp. 243–267
         </i>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Official_status">
        Official status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sassarese_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Official status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <b>
        Sassarese
       </b>
       is recognized as an official language by the
       <a href="/wiki/Sardinia" title="Sardinia">
        regional government of Sardinia
       </a>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The same value attributed to the Sardinian culture and language is recognised referring to the relative territory, to the culture and Catalan language of Alghero, the Tabarchino of the islands of Sulcis, the
        <b>
         Sassarese
        </b>
        and Gallurese dialect.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Autonomous Region of Sardinia.,
         <i>
          Art. 2, paragraph 4, Regional Law from 15 Oct 1997 about the "Promotion and valorization of the culture and language of Sardinia"
         </i>
         .
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
          <a href="#cite_note-8">
           [8]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
