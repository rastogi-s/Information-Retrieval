      <p>
       <b>
        Navarro-Aragonese
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Romance_language" title="Romance language">
        Romance language
       </a>
       once spoken in a large part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ebro" title="Ebro">
        Ebro
       </a>
       River basin, south of the middle
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       , although it is only currently spoken in a small portion of its original territory. The areas where it was spoken might have included most of Aragón, southern
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Navarre" title="Kingdom of Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/La_Rioja_(Spain)" title="La Rioja (Spain)">
        La Rioja
       </a>
       . It was also spoken across several towns of central Navarre in a multilingual environment with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan#Occitan_in_the_Iberian_Peninsula" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       , and Basque as the native language.
      </p>
      <p>
       Navarro-Aragonese gradually lost ground throughout most of its geographic areas to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castilian_language" title="Castilian language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       , with its last remnants being the dialects of the
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese language
       </a>
       still spoken in northern Aragon.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origins_and_distribution">
        Origins and distribution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarro-Aragonese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Origins and distribution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The language was not defined by clear-cut boundaries, but rather it was a continuum of the Romance language spoken on the stretch extending north of the
       <a href="/wiki/Taifa" title="Taifa">
        Muslim realms
       </a>
       of the Ebro, under the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Mozarabic_language" title="Mozarabic language">
        Mozarabic
       </a>
       and Basque, towards the Pyrenees.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Muladi" title="Muladi">
        Muladies
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Banu_Qasi" title="Banu Qasi">
        Banu Qasi
       </a>
       , lords of
       <a href="/wiki/Tudela,_Navarre" title="Tudela, Navarre">
        Tudela
       </a>
       in the 9th century, may have mostly spoken a variant of Navarro-Aragonese.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Early evidence of the language can be found in place-names like
       <i>
        Murillo el Fruto
       </i>
       attested as
       <i>
        Murello Freito
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Muriel Freito
       </i>
       (stemming from Latin "Murellus Fractus") and
       <i>
        Cascante
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Olite
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Urzante
       </i>
       with a typical restored -e ending after "t" in this area.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The language is also attested in major towns of
       <a href="/wiki/Navarre" title="Navarre">
        Navarre
       </a>
       (in
       <a href="/wiki/Estella-Lizarra" title="Estella-Lizarra">
        Estella
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pamplona" title="Pamplona">
        Pamplona
       </a>
       too) in a multilingual environment where
       <a href="/wiki/Basque_language" title="Basque language">
        Basque
       </a>
       was the natural language, used by most of the people,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       was spoken by the
       <i>
        Franks
       </i>
       in their ethnic boroughs, while
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew" title="Hebrew">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       was used for written purposes in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aljama" title="Aljama">
         aljamas
        </a>
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       along with Basque
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Navarro-Aragonese as
       <a href="/wiki/Vernacular" title="Vernacular">
        vernaculars
       </a>
       in their respective linguistic regions.
      </p>

      <p>
       At the westernmost tip of this middle Ebro stretch a Romance variant was developed in La Rioja, recorded in the
       <a href="/wiki/Glosas_Emilianenses" title="Glosas Emilianenses">
        Glosas Emilianenses
       </a>
       dating from roughly 1000 AD. They have been diversely classified from "cradle of Spanish" to Navarro-Aragonese variant, while it is widely accepted the glosses show more similarities with the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, political events were going to tip the scale in favour of an increasing assimilation to Castilian in the following centuries, especially after the disputed region was annexed to Castile in 1177 at the expense of Navarre. Another focal point for the emergence and expansion of Romance in High Aragon and eastern border of Navarre was the ancient Roman road and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Way_of_St._James" title="Way of St. James">
        Way of St. James
       </a>
       crossing the Pyrenees to the south from
       <a href="/wiki/Gascony" title="Gascony">
        Gascony
       </a>
       and extending west via Jaca through the Corridor of Berdún, while the territory was largely Basque-Romance bilingual back in 1349.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       However, early Navarro-Aragonese speaking communities may have ebbed and become assimilated in some spots on the strength of a predominant Basque-speaking population (overwhelmingly so in Navarre) north away from the Ebro plains, due to demographic, economic and political shifts, e.g. the eastern borders of Navarre in Leire,
       <a href="/wiki/Sang%C3%BCesa" title="Sangüesa">
        Sangüesa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Li%C3%A9dena" title="Liédena">
        Liédena
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romanzado" title="Romanzado">
        Romanzado
       </a>
       altogether, were densely Basque-speaking in mid and late 16th century.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Navarro-Aragonese had a strong Basque substratum and adstratum, the former being in close contact with Basque, which in turn was rapidly losing ground to the Romance language in the Kingdom of Aragon during the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages#High_Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        High and Late Middle Ages
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Status_and_written_language">
        Status and written language
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarro-Aragonese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Status and written language">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Navarro-Aragonese was chosen in the High Middle Ages by the Navarrese aristocracy and royal institutions for official records and documents in the 14th century
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
        Occitan
       </a>
       variants fell much in decay after the last devastating war among boroughs in Pamplona, dubbing it
       <i>
        ydiomate navarre terrae
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        lengoage de Navarra
       </i>
       (as opposed to the
       <i>
        lingua navarrorum
       </i>
       , the Basque language).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Navarro-Aragonese is a modern term coined for linguistic classification purposes, while its speakers may have referred to it as "Romanz(e) (Aragonés/Navarro)" in the Middle Ages.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:240px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="770" data-file-width="1200" height="153" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg/238px-San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg/357px-San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg/476px-San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg 2x" width="238"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <div align="center">
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Juan_de_la_Pe%C3%B1a" title="San Juan de la Peña">
           San Juan de la Peña
          </a>
          , a landmark in the expansion of Romance in Aragón
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The language's features at this last stage in the 14th and 15th century grew closer to those of
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       , showing a clear trend towards convergence, as attested in the telling opening sentence of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_II_of_Navarre" title="Charles II of Navarre">
        Charles II of Navarre
       </a>
       at his coronation ceremony (1350): "Nos Karlos, por la gracia de Dios, rey de Navarra et conté d'Evreux, juramos a nuestro pueblo de Navarra, es assaber, prelados, ricoshombres, cavailleros, hombres de buenas villas et a todo el pueblo de Navarra, todos lures fueros, usos, costumbres, franquezas, libertades."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eventual_development">
        Eventual development
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Navarro-Aragonese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Eventual development">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The language merged with
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       during the 15th and early 16th century in Navarre, while it further survived in Aragon, eventually developing into
       <a href="/wiki/Aragonese_language" title="Aragonese language">
        Aragonese
       </a>
       , expanding south along with the
       <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Aragon" title="Kingdom of Aragon">
        Kingdom of Aragon
       </a>
       's lands conquered to the kingdoms in
       <a href="/wiki/Al-Andalus" title="Al-Andalus">
        Al-Andalus
       </a>
       , and reaching at one point as far south as
       <a href="/wiki/Murcia" title="Murcia">
        Murcia
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while the Mediterranean coastal strip came to be settled by
       <a href="/wiki/Catalan_language" title="Catalan language">
        Catalan
       </a>
       speakers. These geo-linguistic gains could not prevent Navarro-Aragonese from gradually losing ground to
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Castilian
       </a>
       both territorially and socially after the
       <a href="/wiki/House_of_Trast%C3%A1mara" title="House of Trastámara">
        Trastámara dynasty
       </a>
       's access to the Aragonese crown
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the 1469 wedding between
       <a href="/wiki/Ferdinand_II_of_Aragon" title="Ferdinand II of Aragon">
        Ferdinand II of Aragon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Isabella_I_of_Castile" title="Isabella I of Castile">
        Isabella I of Castile
       </a>
       , who favoured Castilian (Spanish) in the royal court. However, the language has lasted, while keeping a low profile and increasingly confined to the
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrenees" title="Pyrenees">
        Pyrenees
       </a>
       , up to modern days.
      </p>
