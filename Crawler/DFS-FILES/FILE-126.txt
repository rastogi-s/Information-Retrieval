      <p>
       A
       <b>
        map
       </b>
       is a symbolic depiction emphasizing relationships between elements of some space, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_body" title="Physical body">
        objects
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Region" title="Region">
        regions
       </a>
       , or themes.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many maps are static, fixed to paper or some other durable medium, while others are dynamic or interactive. Although most commonly used to depict geography, maps may represent any
       <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
        space
       </a>
       , real or imagined, without regard to
       <a href="/wiki/Context_(language_use)" title="Context (language use)">
        context
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">
        scale
       </a>
       , such as in
       <a href="/wiki/Brain_mapping" title="Brain mapping">
        brain mapping
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">
        DNA
       </a>
       mapping, or computer network topology mapping. The space being mapped may be two dimensional, such as the surface of the earth, three dimensional, such as the interior of the earth, or even more abstract spaces of any dimension, such as arise in modeling phenomena having many independent variables.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the earliest maps known are of the heavens, geographic maps of territory have a very long tradition and exist from ancient times. The word "map" comes from the medieval Latin
       <i>
        Mappa mundi
       </i>
       , wherein
       <i>
        mappa
       </i>
       meant napkin or cloth and
       <i>
        mundi
       </i>
       the world. Thus, "map" became the shortened term referring to a two-dimensional representation of the surface of the world.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geographic_maps">
        Geographic maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Geographic maps">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3972" data-file-width="5812" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/220px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/330px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg/440px-Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Planisph%C3%A6ri_c%C5%93leste.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A celestial map from the 17th century, by the cartographer
         <a href="/wiki/Frederik_de_Wit" title="Frederik de Wit">
          Frederik de Wit
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        Cartography
       </a>
       or
       <i>
        map-making
       </i>
       is the study and practice of crafting representations of the Earth upon a flat surface (see
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_cartography" title="History of cartography">
        History of cartography
       </a>
       ), and one who makes maps is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartographer
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Road_atlas" title="Road atlas">
        Road maps
       </a>
       are perhaps the most widely used maps today, and form a subset of navigational maps, which also include aeronautical and
       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">
        nautical charts
       </a>
       , railroad network maps, and hiking and bicycling maps. In terms of quantity, the largest number of drawn map sheets is probably made up by local surveys, carried out by
       <a href="/wiki/Municipality" title="Municipality">
        municipalities
       </a>
       , utilities, tax assessors, emergency services providers, and other local agencies. Many national surveying projects have been carried out by the military, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        British
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ordnance_Survey" title="Ordnance Survey">
        Ordnance Survey
       </a>
       : a civilian government agency, internationally renowned for its comprehensively detailed work.
      </p>
      <p>
       In addition to location information maps may also be used to portray
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
        contour lines
       </a>
       indicating constant values of
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevation
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rain" title="Rain">
        rainfall
       </a>
       , etc.
      </p>

         <i>
          The
          <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi" title="Hereford Mappa Mundi">
           Hereford Mappa Mundi
          </a>
         </i>
         from about 1300,
         <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Cathedral" title="Hereford Cathedral">
          Hereford Cathedral
         </a>
         , England, is a classic "T-O" map with Jerusalem at centre, east toward the top, Europe the bottom left and Africa on the right.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The orientation of a map is the relationship between the directions on the map and the corresponding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compass_direction" title="Compass direction">
        compass directions
       </a>
       in reality. The word "
       <a href="/wiki/Orient" title="Orient">
        orient
       </a>
       " is derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        oriens
       </i>
       , meaning east. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       many maps, including the
       <a href="/wiki/T_and_O_map" title="T and O map">
        T and O maps
       </a>
       , were drawn with east at the top (meaning that the direction "up" on the map corresponds to East on the compass). The most common cartographic convention is that north is at the top of a map.
      </p>
      <p>
       Maps not oriented with north at the top:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Maps from non-Western traditions are oriented a variety of ways. Old maps of
        <a href="/wiki/Edo" title="Edo">
         Edo
        </a>
        show the
        <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Imperial_Palace" title="Tokyo Imperial Palace">
         Japanese imperial palace
        </a>
        as the "top", but also at the centre, of the map. Labels on the map are oriented in such a way that you cannot read them properly unless you put the imperial palace above your head.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2008)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
         Medieval
        </a>
        European
        <a href="/wiki/T_and_O_map" title="T and O map">
         T and O maps
        </a>
        such as the
        <a href="/wiki/Hereford_Mappa_Mundi" title="Hereford Mappa Mundi">
         Hereford Mappa Mundi
        </a>
        were centred on
        <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
         Jerusalem
        </a>
        with East at the top. Indeed, prior to the reintroduction of
        <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
         Ptolemy
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geographia" title="Geographia">
          Geography
         </a>
        </i>
        to Europe around 1400, there was no single convention in the West.
        <a href="/wiki/Portolan_chart" title="Portolan chart">
         Portolan charts
        </a>
        , for example, are oriented to the shores they describe.
       </li>
       <li>
        Maps of cities bordering a sea are often conventionally oriented with the sea at the top.
       </li>
       <li>
        Route and channel maps have traditionally been oriented to the road or waterway they describe.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Azimuthal_equidistant_projection" title="Azimuthal equidistant projection">
         Polar maps
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
         Arctic
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
         Antarctic
        </a>
        regions are conventionally centred on the pole; the direction North would be towards or away from the centre of the map, respectively. Typical maps of the Arctic have 0° meridian towards the bottom of the page; maps of the Antarctic have the 0° meridian towards the top of the page.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reversed_map" title="Reversed map">
         Reversed maps
        </a>
        , also known as
        <i>
         Upside-Down maps
        </i>
        or
        <i>
         South-Up maps
        </i>
        , reverse the
        <i>
         North is up
        </i>
        convention and have south at the top.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Buckminster_Fuller" title="Buckminster Fuller">
         Buckminster Fuller
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Dymaxion_map" title="Dymaxion map">
         Dymaxion maps
        </a>
        are based on a projection of the Earth's
        <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
         sphere
        </a>
        onto an
        <a href="/wiki/Icosahedron" title="Icosahedron">
         icosahedron
        </a>
        . The resulting triangular pieces may be arranged in any order or orientation.
       </li>
       <li>
        Modern digital
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GIS" title="GIS">
         GIS
        </a>
        maps such as
        <a href="/wiki/ArcMap" title="ArcMap">
         ArcMap
        </a>
        typically project north at the top of the map, but use math degrees (0 is east, degrees increase counter-clockwise), rather than compass degrees (0 is north, degrees increase clockwise) for orientation of transects. Compass decimal degrees can be converted to math degrees by subtracting them from 450; if the answer is greater than 360, subtract 360.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_and_accuracy">
        Scale and accuracy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scale and accuracy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blank_globe.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="210" data-file-width="210" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blank_globe.svg/220px-Blank_globe.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blank_globe.svg/330px-Blank_globe.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Blank_globe.svg/440px-Blank_globe.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blank_globe.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A 'global view map' of Europe, Western Asia and Africa.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Many maps are drawn to a
       <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">
        scale
       </a>
       expressed as a
       <a href="/wiki/Ratio" title="Ratio">
        ratio
       </a>
       , such as 1:10,000, which means that 1 unit of
       <a href="/wiki/Measurement" title="Measurement">
        measurement
       </a>
       on the map corresponds to 10,000 of that same unit on the ground. The scale statement can be accurate when the region mapped is small enough for the
       <a href="/wiki/Curvature" title="Curvature">
        curvature
       </a>
       of the Earth to be neglected, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/City_map" title="City map">
        city map
       </a>
       . Mapping larger regions, where curvature cannot be ignored, requires
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        projections
       </a>
       to map from the curved surface of the Earth to the plane. The impossibility of flattening the
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
        plane
       </a>
       without distortion means that the map cannot have constant scale. Rather, on most projections the best that can be attained is accurate scale along one or two paths on the projection. Because scale differs everywhere, it can only be measured meaningfully as
       <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">
        point scale
       </a>
       per location. Most maps strive to keep point scale variation within narrow bounds. Although the scale statement is nominal it is usually accurate enough for most purposes unless the map covers a large fraction of the earth. At the scope of a world map, scale as a single number is practically meaningless throughout most of the map. Instead, it usually refers to the scale along the equator.
      </p>
      <p>
       Large scale maps, (e.g. 1:10,000), cover relatively small regions in great detail and small scale maps, (e.g. 1:10,000,000), cover large regions such as nations, continents and the whole
       <a href="/wiki/Globe" title="Globe">
        globe
       </a>
       . The large/small terminology arose from the practice of writing scales as numerical fractions: 1/10,000 is larger than 1/10,000,000. There is no exact dividing line between large and small but 1/100,000 might well be considered as a medium scale. Examples of large scale maps are the 1:25,000 maps produced for hikers; on the other hand maps intended for motorists at 1:250,000 or 1:1,000,000 are small scale.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is important to recognize that even the most accurate maps sacrifice a certain amount of accuracy in scale to deliver a greater visual usefulness to its user. For example, the width of roads and small streams are exaggerated when they are too narrow to be shown on the map at true scale; that is, on a printed map they would be narrower than could be perceived by the naked eye. The same applies to computer maps where the smallest unit is the
       <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">
        pixel
       </a>
       . A narrow stream say must be shown to have the width of a pixel even if at the map scale it would be a small fraction of the pixel width.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1534" data-file-width="1536" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/220px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/330px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG/440px-EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:EU_Pop2008_1024.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Cartogram: The
         <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
          EU
         </a>
         distorted to show population distributions.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some maps, called
       <a href="/wiki/Cartogram" title="Cartogram">
        cartograms
       </a>
       , have the scale deliberately distorted to reflect information other than land area or distance. For example, this map (at the right) of
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       has been distorted to show population distribution, while the rough shape of the continent is still discernible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example of distorted scale is the famous
       <a href="/wiki/Tube_map" title="Tube map">
        London Underground map
       </a>
       . The basic geographical structure is respected but the tube lines (and the
       <a href="/wiki/River_Thames" title="River Thames">
        River Thames
       </a>
       ) are smoothed to clarify the relationships between stations. Near the center of the map stations are spaced out more than near the edges of map.
      </p>
      <p>
       Further inaccuracies may be deliberate. For example, cartographers may simply omit military installations or remove features solely in order to enhance the clarity of the map. For example, a road map may not show railroads, smaller waterways or other prominent non-road objects, and even if it does, it may show them less clearly (e.g. dashed or dotted lines/outlines) than the main roads. Known as decluttering, the practice makes the subject matter that the user is interested in easier to read, usually without sacrificing overall accuracy. Software-based maps often allow the user to toggle decluttering between ON, OFF and AUTO as needed. In AUTO the degree of decluttering is adjusted as the user changes the scale being displayed.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Map_types_and_projections">
        Map types and projections
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Map types and projections">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable selfref" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Category:Map_types" title="Category:Map types">
        Category:Map types
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_gravity_map.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="597" data-file-width="1010" height="130" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/220px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/330px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Ocean_gravity_map.gif/440px-Ocean_gravity_map.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ocean_gravity_map.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of large underwater features. (1995,
         <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">
          NOAA
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Maps of the world or large areas are often either 'political' or 'physical'. The most important purpose of the political map is to show territorial
       <a href="/wiki/Border" title="Border">
        borders
       </a>
       ; the purpose of the physical is to show features of
       <a href="/wiki/Geography" title="Geography">
        geography
       </a>
       such as mountains, soil type or land use including infrastructure such as roads, railroads and buildings.
       <a href="/wiki/Topographic_map" title="Topographic map">
        Topographic maps
       </a>
       show
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevations
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">
        relief
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Contour_line" title="Contour line">
        contour lines
       </a>
       or shading.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_map" title="Geological map">
        Geological maps
       </a>
       show not only the physical surface, but characteristics of the underlying rock,
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">
        fault
       </a>
       lines, and subsurface structures. Maps that depict the surface of the Earth also use a
       <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
        projection
       </a>
       , a way of translating the three-dimensional real surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Geoid" title="Geoid">
        geoid
       </a>
       to a two-dimensional picture. Perhaps the best-known world-map projection is the
       <a href="/wiki/Mercator_projection" title="Mercator projection">
        Mercator projection
       </a>
       , originally designed as a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Nautical_chart" title="Nautical chart">
        nautical chart
       </a>
       . Aeroplane pilots use
       <a href="/wiki/Aeronautical_chart" title="Aeronautical chart">
        aeronautical charts
       </a>
       based on a
       <a href="/wiki/Lambert_conformal_conic_projection" title="Lambert conformal conic projection">
        Lambert conformal conic projection
       </a>
       , in which a cone is laid over the section of the earth to be mapped. The cone intersects the sphere (the earth) at one or two parallels which are chosen as standard lines. This allows the pilots to plot a great-circle route approximation on a flat, two-dimensional chart.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Map_projection#Azimuthal_(projections_onto_a_plane)" title="Map projection">
         Azimuthal
        </a>
        or
        <a href="/wiki/Gnomonic_projection" title="Gnomonic projection">
         Gnomonic
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Map_projection" title="Map projection">
         map projections
        </a>
        are often used in planning air routes due to their ability to represent
        <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
         great circles
        </a>
        as straight lines.
       </li>
       <li>
        Richard Edes Harrison produced a striking series of maps during and after
        <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
         World War II
        </a>
        for
        <a href="/wiki/Fortune_(magazine)" title="Fortune (magazine)">
         Fortune magazine
        </a>
        . These used "bird's eye" projections to emphasise globally strategic "fronts" in the air age, pointing out proximities and barriers not apparent on a conventional rectangular projection of the world.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Electronic_maps">
        Electronic maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electronic maps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/220px-Topographic_map_example.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/330px-Topographic_map_example.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Topographic_map_example.png/440px-Topographic_map_example.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Topographic_map_example.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A
         <a href="/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey" title="United States Geological Survey">
          USGS
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Digital_raster_graphic" title="Digital raster graphic">
          digital raster graphic
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the last quarter of the 20th century, the indispensable tool of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cartographer" title="Cartographer">
        cartographer
       </a>
       has been the computer. Much of cartography, especially at the data-gathering
       <a href="/wiki/Surveying" title="Surveying">
        survey
       </a>
       level, has been subsumed by
       <a href="/wiki/Geographic_information_system" title="Geographic information system">
        Geographic Information Systems
       </a>
       (GIS). The functionality of maps has been greatly advanced by technology simplifying the superimposition of spatially located variables onto existing geographical maps. Having local information such as rainfall level, distribution of wildlife, or demographic data integrated within the map allows more efficient analysis and better decision making. In the pre-electronic age such
       <a href="/wiki/Superimposition" title="Superimposition">
        superimposition
       </a>
       of data led
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Snow_(physician)" title="John Snow (physician)">
        Dr. John Snow
       </a>
       to identify the location of an outbreak of
       <a href="/wiki/Cholera" title="Cholera">
        cholera
       </a>
       . Today, it is used by agencies of the human kind, as diverse as wildlife conservationists and militaries around the world.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="399" data-file-width="497" height="177" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/220px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/330px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png/440px-Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Maps-for-free_Sierra_Nevada.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Relief map Sierra Nevada
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Even when GIS is not involved, most cartographers now use a variety of computer graphics programs to generate new maps.
      </p>
      <p>
       Interactive, computerised maps are commercially available, allowing users to
       <i>
        zoom in
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        zoom out
       </i>
       (respectively meaning to increase or decrease the scale), sometimes by replacing one map with another of different scale, centered where possible on the same point. In-car
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Global_navigation_satellite_system" title="Global navigation satellite system">
        global navigation satellite systems
       </a>
       are computerised maps with route-planning and advice facilities which monitor the user's position with the help of satellites. From the computer scientist's point of view, zooming in entails one or a combination of:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        replacing the map by a more detailed one
       </li>
       <li>
        enlarging the same map without enlarging the
        <a href="/wiki/Pixel" title="Pixel">
         pixels
        </a>
        , hence showing more detail by removing less information compared to the less detailed version
       </li>
       <li>
        enlarging the same map with the pixels enlarged (replaced by rectangles of pixels); no additional detail is shown, but, depending on the quality of one's vision, possibly more detail can be seen; if a computer display does not show adjacent pixels really separate, but overlapping instead (this does not apply for an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liquid_crystal_display" title="Liquid crystal display">
         LCD
        </a>
        , but may apply for a
        <a href="/wiki/Cathode_ray_tube" title="Cathode ray tube">
         cathode ray tube
        </a>
        ), then replacing a pixel by a rectangle of pixels does show more detail. A variation of this method is
        <a href="/wiki/Interpolation" title="Interpolation">
         interpolation
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ol>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:World.pdf">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1275" data-file-width="1650" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-220px-World.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-330px-World.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/World.pdf/page1-440px-World.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:World.pdf" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A world map in PDF format.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Typically (2) applies to a
        <a href="/wiki/Portable_Document_Format" title="Portable Document Format">
         Portable Document Format
        </a>
        (PDF) file or other format based on
        <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">
         vector graphics
        </a>
        . The increase in detail is limited to the information contained in the file: enlargement of a curve may eventually result in a series of standard geometric figures such as straight lines, arcs of circles or
        <a href="/wiki/Spline_(mathematics)" title="Spline (mathematics)">
         splines
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        (2) may apply to text and (3) to the outline of a map feature such as a forest or building.
       </li>
       <li>
        (1) may apply to the text as needed (displaying labels for more features), while (2) applies to the rest of the image. Text is not necessarily enlarged when zooming in. Similarly, a road represented by a double line may or may not become wider when one zooms in.
       </li>
       <li>
        The map may also have layers which are partly
        <a href="/wiki/Raster_graphics" title="Raster graphics">
         raster graphics
        </a>
        and partly
        <a href="/wiki/Vector_graphics" title="Vector graphics">
         vector graphics
        </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climatic_maps">
        Climatic maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climatic maps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The maps that reflect the territorial distribution of climatic conditions based on the results of long-term observations are climatic maps. Climatic maps can be compiled both for individual climatic features (temperature, precipitation, humidity) and for combinations of them at the earth’s surface and in the upper layers of the atmosphere. Climatic maps afford a very convenient overview of the climatic features in a large region and permit values of climatic features to be compared in different parts of the region. Through interpolation the maps can be used to determine the values of climatic features in any particular spot.
      </p>
      <p>
       Climatic maps generally apply to individual months and to the year as a whole, sometimes to the four seasons, to the growing period, and so forth. On maps compiled from the observations of ground meteorological stations, atmospheric pressure is converted to sea level. Air temperature maps are compiled both from the actual values observed on the surface of the earth and from values converted to sea level. The pressure field in free atmosphere is represented either by maps of the distribution of pressure at different standard altitudes—for example, at every kilometer above sea level—or by maps of baric topography on which altitudes (more precisely geopotentials) of the main isobaric surfaces (for example, 900, 800, and 700 millibars) counted off from sea level are plotted. The temperature, humidity, and wind on aeroclimatic maps may apply either to standard altitudes or to the main isobaric surfaces.
      </p>
      <p>
       Isolines are drawn on maps of such climatic features as the long-term mean values (of atmospheric pressure, temperature, humidity, total precipitation, and so forth) to connect points with equal values of the feature in question—for example, isobars for pressure, isotherms for temperature, and isohyets for precipitation. Isoamplitudes are drawn on maps of amplitudes (for example, annual amplitudes of air temperature—that is, the differences between the mean temperatures of the warmest and coldest month). Isanomals are drawn on maps of anomalies (for example, deviations of the mean temperature of each place from the mean temperature of the entire latitudinal zone). Isolines of frequency are drawn on maps showing the frequency of a particular phenomenon (for example, annual number of days with a thunderstorm or snow cover). Isochrones are drawn on maps showing the dates of onset of a given phenomenon (for example, the first frost and appearance or disappearance of the snow cover) or the date of a particular value of a meteorological element in the course of a year (for example, passing of the mean daily air temperature through zero). Isolines of the mean numerical value of wind velocity or isotachs are drawn on wind maps (charts); the wind resultants and directions of prevailing winds are indicated by arrows of different length or arrows with different plumes; lines of flow are often drawn. Maps of the zonal and meridional components of wind are frequently compiled for the free atmosphere. Atmospheric pressure and wind are usually combined on climatic maps. Wind roses, curves showing the distribution of other meteorological elements, diagrams of the annual course of elements at individual stations, and the like are also plotted on climatic maps.
      </p>
      <p>
       Maps of climatic regionalization, that is, division of the earth’s surface into climatic zones and regions according to some classification of climates, are a special kind of climatic map.
      </p>
      <p>
       Climatic maps are often incorporated into climatic atlases of varying geographic range (globe, hemispheres, continents, countries, oceans) or included in comprehensive atlases. Besides general climatic maps, applied climatic maps and atlases have great practical value. Aeroclimatic maps, aeroclimatic atlases, and agroclimatic maps are the most numerous.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Conventional_signs">
        Conventional signs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Conventional signs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The various features shown on a map are represented by conventional
       <a href="/wiki/Sign" title="Sign">
        signs
       </a>
       or symbols. For example, colors can be used to indicate a classification of roads. Those signs are usually explained in the margin of the map, or on a separately published characteristic sheet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some cartographers prefer to make the map cover practically the entire screen or sheet of paper, leaving no room "outside" the map for information about the map as a whole. These cartographers typically place such information in an otherwise "blank" region "inside" the map—
       <a href="/wiki/Cartouche_(cartography)" title="Cartouche (cartography)">
        cartouche
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Legend_(map)" title="Legend (map)">
        map legend
       </a>
       , title,
       <a href="/wiki/Compass_rose" title="Compass rose">
        compass rose
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bar_scale" title="Bar scale">
        bar scale
       </a>
       , etc. In particular, some maps contain smaller "sub-maps" in otherwise blank regions—often one at a much smaller scale showing the whole globe and where the whole map fits on that globe, and a few showing "regions of interest" at a larger scale in order to show details that wouldn't otherwise fit. Occasionally sub-maps use the same scale as the large map—a few maps of the
       <a href="/wiki/Contiguous_United_States" title="Contiguous United States">
        contiguous United States
       </a>
       include a sub-map to the same scale for each of the two non-contiguous states.
      </p>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_map_symbols" title="Japanese map symbols">
        Japanese map symbols
       </a>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Labeling">
        Labeling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Labeling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       To communicate spatial information effectively, features such as rivers, lakes, and cities need to be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Labeling_(map_design)" title="Labeling (map design)">
        labeled
       </a>
       . Over centuries cartographers have developed the art of placing names on even the densest of maps. Text placement or name placement can get mathematically very complex as the number of labels and map density increases. Therefore, text placement is time-consuming and labor-intensive, so cartographers and GIS users have developed
       <a href="/wiki/Automatic_label_placement" title="Automatic label placement">
        automatic label placement
       </a>
       to ease this process.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-geographical_spatial_maps">
        Non-geographical spatial maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Non-geographical spatial maps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Maps exist of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , and other cosmological features such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Star_map" title="Star map">
        star maps
       </a>
       . In addition maps of other bodies such as the Moon and other planets are technically not
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geospatial" title="Geospatial">
         geo
        </a>
       </i>
       graphical maps.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non_spatial_maps">
        Non spatial maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non spatial maps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Diagrams such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schematic_diagram" title="Schematic diagram">
        schematic diagrams
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gantt_chart" title="Gantt chart">
        Gantt charts
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Treemap" title="Treemap">
        treemaps
       </a>
       display logical relationships between items, and do not display spatial relationships at all.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some maps, for example the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/London_Underground_map" title="London Underground map">
        London Underground map
       </a>
       , are
       <a href="/wiki/Topological_map" title="Topological map">
        topological maps
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Topological" title="Topological">
        Topological
       </a>
       in nature, the distances are completely unimportant; only the connectivity is significant.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="General-purpose_maps">
        General-purpose maps
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: General-purpose maps">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       General-purpose maps provide many types of information on one map. Most atlas maps, wall maps, and road maps fall into this category. The following are some features that might be shown on general-purpose maps: bodies of water, roads, railway lines, parks, elevations, towns and cities, political boundaries, latitude and longitude, national and provincial parks. These maps give a broad understanding of location and features of an area. The reader may gain an understanding of the type of landscape, the location of urban places, and the location of major transportation routes all at once.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_regulation">
        Legal regulation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Map&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Legal regulation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some countries required that all published maps represent their national claims regarding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Border_dispute" title="Border dispute">
        border disputes
       </a>
       . For example:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Within Russia, Google Maps shows
        <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">
         Crimea
        </a>
        as part of Russia.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Both the Republic of India and the People's Republic of China require that all maps show areas subject to the
        <a href="/wiki/Sino-Indian_border_dispute" title="Sino-Indian border dispute">
         Sino-Indian border dispute
        </a>
        in their own favor.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In 2010, the People's Republic of China began requiring that all online maps served from within China be hosted there, making them subject to Chinese laws.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
