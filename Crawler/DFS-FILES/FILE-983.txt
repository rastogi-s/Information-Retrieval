      <p>
       <b>
        Friulian
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        Friulan
       </b>
       (
       <i>
        <span class="unicode haudio">
         <span class="fn">
          <span style="white-space:nowrap">
           <a href="/wiki/File:Furlan.ogg" title="About this sound">
            <img alt="About this sound" data-file-height="20" data-file-width="20" height="11" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/11px-Loudspeaker.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/17px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Loudspeaker.svg/22px-Loudspeaker.svg.png 2x" width="11"/>
           </a>
          </span>
          <a class="internal" href="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/13/Furlan.ogg" title="Furlan.ogg">
           furlan
          </a>
         </span>
         <small class="metadata audiolinkinfo" style="cursor:help;">
          (
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Media_help" title="Wikipedia:Media help">
           <span style="cursor:help;">
            help
           </span>
          </a>
          ·
          <a href="/wiki/File:Furlan.ogg" title="File:Furlan.ogg">
           <span style="cursor:help;">
            info
           </span>
          </a>
          )
         </small>
        </span>
       </i>
       or, affectionately,
       <i>
        <span lang="fur" xml:lang="fur">
         marilenghe
        </span>
       </i>
       in Friulian,
       <i>
        <span lang="it" xml:lang="it">
         friulano
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="de" xml:lang="de">
         Furlanisch
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <span lang="sl" xml:lang="sl">
         furlanščina
        </span>
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       ; also
       <i>
        Friulian
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance language
       </a>
       belonging to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
        Rhaeto-Romance
       </a>
       family, spoken in the
       <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">
        Friuli
       </a>
       region of northeastern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       . Friulian has around 600,000 speakers, the vast majority of whom also speak
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       . It is sometimes called
       <b>
        Eastern Ladin
       </b>
       since it shares the same roots as
       <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">
        Ladin
       </a>
       , but, over the centuries, it has diverged under the influence of surrounding languages, including
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
        Slovene
       </a>
       . Documents in Friulian are attested from the 11th century and poetry and literature date as far back as 1300. By the 20th century, there was a revival of interest in the language that has continued to this day. In North America the more common English form is Friulan for both the language and the ethnic identity of the people living in Friuli and abroad. Friulan follows the example of Friulano in Italian. See the reference,
       <i>
        Friulan Dictionary: English-Friulan / Friulan-English.
       </i>
       Eds. Gianni Nazzi &amp; Deborah Saidero. Udine: Ent Friul tal Mond. 2000.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="624" data-file-width="928" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg/220px-Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg/330px-Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg/440px-Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Bandiere_dal_Fri%C3%BBl.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Historical flag of Friûl
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A question which causes many debates is the influence of the Latin spoken in
       <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">
        Aquileia
       </a>
       and surrounding areas. Some claim that it had peculiar features that later passed into Friulian. Epigraphs and inscriptions from that period show some variants if compared to the standard Latin language, but most of them are common to other areas of the Roman Empire; often, it is cited that Fortunatianus, the bishop of
       <a href="/wiki/Aquileia" title="Aquileia">
        Aquileia
       </a>
       342-c. 357, wrote a commentary to the
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel" title="Gospel">
        Gospel
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        sermo rusticus
       </i>
       , in the language spoken by the people, which, therefore, would have been quite different from Standard
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Latin_language" title="Latin language">
        Latin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The text itself did not survive so its language cannot be examined, but its attested existence testifies to a shift of languages while, for example, other important communities of Northern Italy were still speaking Latin. The language spoken before the arrival of the Romans in 181 BC was of Celtic origin since the inhabitants belonged to the
       <a href="/wiki/Carni" title="Carni">
        Carni
       </a>
       , a Celtic population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In modern Friulian, the words of Celtic origins are many (names referring to mountains, woods, plants, animals) and much influence of the original population is shown in toponyms (names of villages with
       <i>
        -acco
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        -icco
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Even influences from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lombard_language" title="Lombard language">
        Lombard language
       </a>
       - Friuli was one of their strongholds - are very frequent.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Friulian there are also a lot of German, Slovenian and Venetian words. From that evidence, scholars today agree that the formation of Friulian dates back to around 1000, at the same time as other dialects derived from Latin (see
       <a href="/wiki/Vulgar_Latin" title="Vulgar Latin">
        Vulgar Latin
       </a>
       ). The first written records of Friulian have been found in administrative acts of the 13th century, but the documents became more frequent in the following century, when literary works also emerged (
       <i>
        Frammenti letterari
       </i>
       for example). The main centre at that time was
       <a href="/wiki/Cividale_del_Friuli" title="Cividale del Friuli">
        Cividale
       </a>
       . The Friulian language has never acquired official status: legal statutes were first written in Latin, then in Venetian and finally in Italian.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="The_.22Ladin_Question.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='The_"Ladin_Question"'>
        The "Ladin Question"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title='Edit section: The "Ladin Question"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ascoli.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="353" data-file-width="230" height="261" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Ascoli.jpg/170px-Ascoli.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/2d/Ascoli.jpg 1.5x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ascoli.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Historical linguist
         <a href="/wiki/Graziadio_Isaia_Ascoli" title="Graziadio Isaia Ascoli">
          Graziadio Isaia Ascoli
         </a>
         presented the theory that Ladin, Romansh and Friulian are from the same family.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The idea of unity among
       <a href="/wiki/Ladin_language" title="Ladin language">
        Ladin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Romansh_language" title="Romansh language">
        Romansh
       </a>
       and Friulian comes from the Italian
       <a href="/wiki/Historical_linguistics" title="Historical linguistics">
        historical linguist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Graziadio_Isaia_Ascoli" title="Graziadio Isaia Ascoli">
        Graziadio Isaia Ascoli
       </a>
       , who was born in
       <a href="/wiki/Gorizia" title="Gorizia">
        Gorizia
       </a>
       . In 1871, he presented his theory that these three languages are part of one family, which in the past stretched from
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Muggia" title="Muggia">
        Muggia
       </a>
       and perhaps also
       <a href="/wiki/Istria" title="Istria">
        Istria
       </a>
       . The three languages are the only survivors of this family and all developed differently. Friulian was much less influenced by
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       . The scholar Francescato claimed subsequently that until the 14th century, the
       <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
        Venetian language
       </a>
       shared many phonetic features with Friulian and Ladin and so he thought that Friulian was a much more
       <a href="/wiki/Conservative_(language)" title="Conservative (language)">
        conservative
       </a>
       language. Many features that Ascoli thought were peculiar to the Rhaeto-Romance languages can, in fact, be found in other languages of Northern
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Areas">
        Areas
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Areas">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Italy">
        Italy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Italy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Friulan_language.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="741" data-file-width="988" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Friulan_language.PNG/220px-Friulan_language.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Friulan_language.PNG/330px-Friulan_language.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Friulan_language.PNG/440px-Friulan_language.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Friulan_language.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Spread of the Friulian language in Italy
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Today, Friulian is spoken in the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Udine" title="Province of Udine">
        Udine
       </a>
       , including the area of the Carnia
       <a href="/wiki/Alps" title="Alps">
        Alps
       </a>
       , but as well throughout the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Pordenone" title="Pordenone">
        Pordenone
       </a>
       , in half of the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Gorizia" title="Province of Gorizia">
        Gorizia
       </a>
       , and in the eastern part of the province of
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Venice" title="Province of Venice">
        Venice
       </a>
       . In the past, the language borders were wider since in
       <a href="/wiki/Trieste" title="Trieste">
        Trieste
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Muggia" title="Muggia">
        Muggia
       </a>
       , local variants of Friulian were spoken. The main document about the dialect of Trieste, or
       <i>
        tergestino
       </i>
       , is "Dialoghi piacevoli in dialetto vernacolo triestino", published by G. Mainati in 1828.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="World">
        World
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: World">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Friuli was, until the 1960s, an area of deep poverty, causing a large number of Friulian speakers to emigrate. Most went to
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Switzerland" title="Switzerland">
        Switzerland
       </a>
       or outside
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       , to
       <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">
        Canada
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venezuela" title="Venezuela">
        Venezuela
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Africa" title="South Africa">
        South Africa
       </a>
       . In those countries, there are associations of Friulian immigrants (called
       <i>
        Fogolâr furlan
       </i>
       ) that try to protect their traditions and language.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first texts in Friulian date back to the 13th century and are mainly commercial or juridical acts. The examples show that Friulian was used together with Latin, which was still the administrative language. The main examples of literature that have survived (much from this period has been
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_works" title="Lost works">
        lost
       </a>
       ) are poems from the 14th century and are usually dedicated to the theme of love and are probably inspired by the Italian poetic movement
       <a href="/wiki/Dolce_Stil_Novo" title="Dolce Stil Novo">
        Dolce Stil Novo
       </a>
       . The most notable work is
       <i>
        Piruç myò doç inculurit
       </i>
       (which means "My sweet, coloured pear"); it was composed by an anonymous author from Cividale del Friuli, probably in 1380.
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <tr>
        <th>
         Original text
        </th>
        <th>
         Version in modern Friulian
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         Piruç myò doç inculurit
         <p>
          quant yò chi viot, dut stoi ardit
         </p>
        </td>
        <td>
         Piruç gno dolç inculurît
         <p>
          cuant che jo cj viôt, dut o stoi ardît
         </p>
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       There are few differences in the first two rows, which demonstrates that there has not been a great evolution in the language except for several words which are no longer used (for example,
       <i>
        dum(n) lo
       </i>
       , a word which means "child", which was used to be more frequently). A modern Friulian speaker can understand these texts with only a little difficulty.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second important period for Friulian literature is the 16th century. The main author of this period was
       <a href="/wiki/Ermes_di_Color%C3%AAt" title="Ermes di Colorêt">
        Ermes di Colorêt
       </a>
       , who composed over 200 poems.
      </p>

      <p>
       Notes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /m, p, b/
        </span>
        are
        <a href="/wiki/Bilabial_consonant" title="Bilabial consonant">
         bilabial
        </a>
        , whereas
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /f, v/
        </span>
        are
        <a href="/wiki/Labiodental_consonant" title="Labiodental consonant">
         labiodental
        </a>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /w/
        </span>
        is
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Velarized" title="Velarized">
         labiovelar
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Note that, in the standard language, a phonemic distinction exists between true palatal stops
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [c ɟ]
        </span>
        and palatoalveolar affricates
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ dʒ]
        </span>
        . The former (written ⟨cj gj⟩) originate from Latin ⟨k g⟩ before ⟨a⟩, whereas the latter (written ⟨c/ç z⟩, where ⟨c⟩ is found before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩, and ⟨ç⟩ is found elsewhere) originate primarily from Latin ⟨k g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩. The palatalization of Latin ⟨k⟩ and ⟨g⟩ before ⟨a⟩ is characteristic of the
        <a href="/wiki/Rhaeto-Romance_languages" title="Rhaeto-Romance languages">
         Rhaeto-Romance languages
        </a>
        and is also found in
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        and some
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
         Occitan
        </a>
        varieties. In some Friulian dialects (e.g. Western dialects), corresponding to Central
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [c ɟ tʃ dʒ]
        </span>
        are found
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ dʒ s z]
        </span>
        . Note in addition that, due to various sound changes, these sounds are all now phonemic; note, for example, the minimal pair
        <i>
         <span lang="fur" xml:lang="fur">
          cjoc
         </span>
        </i>
        "drunk" vs.
        <i>
         <span lang="fur" xml:lang="fur">
          çoc
         </span>
        </i>
        "log".
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orthography">
        Orthography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Orthography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some notes on orthography (from the perspective of the standard, i.e. Central, dialect):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Long vowels are indicated with a
        <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">
         circumflex
        </a>
        : ⟨â ê î ô û⟩.
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨e⟩ is used for both
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɛ/
        </span>
        (which only occurs in stressed syllables) and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /e/
        </span>
        ; similarly, ⟨o⟩ is used for both
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɔ/
        </span>
        and
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /o/
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /j/
        </span>
        is spelled ⟨j⟩ word-initially, and ⟨i⟩ elsewhere.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /w/
        </span>
        occurs primarily in diphthongs, and is spelled ⟨u⟩.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /s/
        </span>
        is normally spelled ⟨s⟩, but is spelled ⟨ss⟩ between vowels (in this context, a single ⟨s⟩ is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /z/
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɲ/
        </span>
        is spelled ⟨gn⟩, which can also occur word-finally.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [ŋ]
        </span>
        is an allophone of
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /n/
        </span>
        , found word-finally, before word-final
        <i>
         -s
        </i>
        , and often in the prefix
        <i>
         in-
        </i>
        . Both sounds are spelled ⟨n⟩.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /k/
        </span>
        is normally spelled ⟨c⟩, but ⟨ch⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩, as in Italian.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ɡ/
        </span>
        is normally spelled ⟨g⟩, but ⟨gh⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩, again as in Italian.
       </li>
       <li>
        The palatal stops
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /c ɟ/
        </span>
        are spelled ⟨cj gj⟩. Note that in some dialects, these sounds are pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [tʃ dʒ]
        </span>
        , as described above.
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /tʃ/
        </span>
        is spelled ⟨c⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩, ⟨ç⟩ elsewhere. Note that in some dialects, this sound is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [s]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dʒ/
        </span>
        is spelled ⟨z⟩. Note that in some dialects, this sound is pronounced
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         [z]
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨z⟩ can also represent
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /ts/
        </span>
        or
        <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
         /dz/
        </span>
        in certain words (e.g.
        <i>
         nazion
        </i>
        "nation",
        <i>
         lezion
        </i>
        "lesson").
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨h⟩ is silent.
       </li>
       <li>
        ⟨q⟩ is no longer used except in the traditional spelling of certain proper names; similarly for ⟨g⟩ before ⟨e⟩ and ⟨i⟩.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Long_vowels_and_their_origin">
        Long vowels and their origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Long vowels and their origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Long vowels are typical of the Friulian language and greatly influence the Friulian pronunciation of Italian.
      </p>
      <p>
       Friulian distinguishes between short and long vowels: in the following
       <a href="/wiki/Minimal_pair" title="Minimal pair">
        minimal pairs
       </a>
       (long vowels are marked in the official orthography with a
       <a href="/wiki/Circumflex" title="Circumflex">
        circumflex
       </a>
       accent):
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         lat
        </i>
        (milk)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         lât
        </i>
        (gone)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         fis
        </i>
        (fixed, dense)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         fîs
        </i>
        (sons)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         lus
        </i>
        (luxury)
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         lûs
        </i>
        (light n.)
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <p>
       Note that the vowels
       <i>
        î
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        û
       </i>
       in the standard language (based on the Central dialects) correspond to two different sounds in the Western dialects (including Codroipo). These sounds are not distributed randomly but correspond to different origins: Latin short
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         E
        </span>
       </span>
       in an open syllable produces Western
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ei]
       </span>
       but Central
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [iː]
       </span>
       , whereas Latin long
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         Ī
        </span>
       </span>
       produces
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [iː]
       </span>
       in both dialects. Similarly, Latin short
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         O
        </span>
       </span>
       in an open syllable produces Western
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ou]
       </span>
       but Central
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [uː]
       </span>
       , whereas Latin long
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         Ū
        </span>
       </span>
       produces
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [uː]
       </span>
       in both dialects. The word
       <i>
        mûr
       </i>
       , for example, means both "wall" (Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         MŪRUM
        </span>
       </span>
       ) and "(he, she, it) dies" (Vulgar Latin *
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         MORIT
        </span>
       </span>
       from Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         MORITUR
        </span>
       </span>
       ); both words are pronounced
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [muːr]
       </span>
       in Central dialects, but respectively
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [muːr]
       </span>
       and
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [mour]
       </span>
       in Western dialects.
      </p>
      <p>
       Long consonants (ll, rr, and so on), frequently used in Italian, are usually absent in Friulian.
      </p>
      <p>
       Friulian long vowels originate primarily from vowel lengthening in stressed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_syllable" title="Open syllable">
        open syllables
       </a>
       when the following vowel was lost.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pilar_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-pilar-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Friulian vowel length has no relation to vowel length in Classical Latin. For example, Latin
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        valet
       </span>
       yields
       <i>
        vâl
       </i>
       "it is worth" with a long vowel, but Latin
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        vallem
       </span>
       yields
       <i>
        val
       </i>
       "valley" with a short vowel. Long vowels aren't found when the following vowel is preserved, e.g.:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        before final -e &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         -a
        </span>
        , cf. short
        <i>
         nuve
        </i>
        "new (fem. sg.)" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         nova
        </span>
        vs. long
        <i>
         nûf
        </i>
        "new (masc. sg.)" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         novum
        </span>
        ;
       </li>
       <li>
        before a non-final preserved vowel, cf.
        <i>
         tivit
        </i>
        /ˈtivit/ "tepid, lukewarm" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         tepidum
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         zinar
        </i>
        /ˈzinar/ "son-in-law" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         generum
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         ridi
        </i>
        /ˈridi/ "to laugh" &lt; Vulgar Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         *rīdere
        </span>
        (Classical
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         rīdēre
        </span>
        ).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       It is quite possible that vowel lengthening occurred originally in all stressed open syllables, and was later lost in non-final syllables.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-loporcaro_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-loporcaro-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Evidence of this is found, for example, in the divergent outcome of Vulgar Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */ɛ/
       </span>
       , which becomes
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jɛ/
       </span>
       in originally closed syllables but
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i(ː)/
       </span>
       in Central Friulian in originally open syllables, including when non-finally. Examples:
       <i>
        siet
       </i>
       "seven" &lt; Vulgar Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */sɛtte/
       </span>
       &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         SEPTEM
        </span>
       </span>
       , word-final
       <i>
        pît
       </i>
       "foot" &lt; Vulgar Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */pɛde/
       </span>
       &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         PEDEM
        </span>
       </span>
       , non-word-final
       <i>
        tivit
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /ˈtivit/
       </span>
       "tepid, lukewarm" &lt; Vulgar Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */tɛpedu/
       </span>
       &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         TEPIDUM
        </span>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       An additional source of vowel length is
       <a href="/wiki/Compensatory_lengthening" title="Compensatory lengthening">
        compensatory lengthening
       </a>
       before lost consonants in certain circumstances, cf.
       <i>
        pâri
       </i>
       "father" &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        patrem
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        vôli
       </i>
       "eye" &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        oc(u)lum
       </span>
       ,
       <i>
        lîre
       </i>
       "pound" &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
        libra
       </span>
       . This produces long vowels in non-final syllables, and was apparently a separate, later development than the primary lengthening in open syllables. Note, for example, the development of Vulgar Latin
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */ɛ/
       </span>
       in this context:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */ɛ/
       </span>
       &gt;
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        */jɛ/
       </span>
       &gt;
       <i>
        iê
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /jeː/
       </span>
       , as in
       <i>
        piêre
       </i>
       "stone" &lt; Latin
       <span class="smallcaps">
        <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
         PETRAM
        </span>
       </span>
       , differing from the outcome
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        /i(ː)/
       </span>
       in originally open syllables (see above).
      </p>
      <p>
       Additional complications:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Central Friulian has lengthening before /r/ even in originally closed syllables, cf.
        <i>
         cjâr
        </i>
        /caːr/ "cart" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         carrum
        </span>
        (homophonous with
        <i>
         cjâr
        </i>
        "dear (masc. sg.)" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         cārum
        </span>
        ). This represents a late, secondary development, and some conservative dialects have the expected length distinction here.
       </li>
       <li>
        Lengthening doesn't occur before
        <a href="/wiki/Nasal_consonant" title="Nasal consonant">
         nasal consonants
        </a>
        even in originally open syllables, cf.
        <i>
         pan
        </i>
        /paŋ/ "bread" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         panem
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         prin
        </i>
        /priŋ/ "first" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant:small-caps;">
         prīmum
        </span>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Special developments produced absolutely word-final long vowels and length distinctions, cf.
        <i>
         fi
        </i>
        "fig" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          FĪCUM
         </span>
        </span>
        vs.
        <i>
         fî
        </i>
        "son" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          FĪLIUM
         </span>
        </span>
        ,
        <i>
         no
        </i>
        "no" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          NŌN
         </span>
        </span>
        vs.
        <i>
         nô
        </i>
        "we" &lt; Latin
        <span class="smallcaps">
         <span style="font-variant: small-caps; text-transform: lowercase;">
          NŌS
         </span>
        </span>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchrony_(linguistics)" title="Synchrony (linguistics)">
        Synchronic
       </a>
       analyses of vowel length in Friulian often claim that it occurs predictably in final syllables before an underlying voiced
       <a href="/wiki/Obstruent" title="Obstruent">
        obstruent
       </a>
       , which is then devoiced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-torres_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-torres-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analyses of this sort have difficulty with long-vowel contrasts that occur non-finally (e.g.
       <i>
        pâri
       </i>
       "father" mentioned above) or not in front of obstruents (e.g.
       <i>
        fi
       </i>
       "fig" vs.
       <i>
        fî
       </i>
       "son",
       <i>
        val
       </i>
       "valley" vs.
       <i>
        vâl
       </i>
       "it is worth").
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Morphology">
        Morphology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Morphology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Friulian is quite different from Italian in its morphology; it is, in many respects, closer to
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nouns">
        Nouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Nouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Friulian as in other
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Noun" title="Noun">
        nouns
       </a>
       are either
       <a href="/wiki/Grammatical_gender" title="Grammatical gender">
        masculine or feminine
       </a>
       (for example, "
       <i>
        il mûr
       </i>
       " ("the wall", masculine), "
       <i>
        la cjadree
       </i>
       " ("the chair", feminine).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Feminine">
        Feminine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Feminine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Most feminine nouns end in
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       , which is pronounced, unlike in Standard French:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjase
        </i>
        = house (from Latin "casa, -ae" hut)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lune
        </i>
        = moon (from Latin "luna, -ae")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         scuele
        </i>
        = school (from Latin "schola, -ae")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some feminine nouns, however, end in a consonant, including those ending in
       <i>
        -zion
       </i>
       , which are from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         man
        </i>
        = hand (from Latin "manŭs, -ūs" f)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lezion
        </i>
        = lesson (from Latin "lectio, -nis" f)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Masculine">
        Masculine
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Masculine">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Most masculine nouns end either in a consonant or in
       <i>
        -i
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjan
        </i>
        = dog
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         gjat
        </i>
        = cat
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fradi
        </i>
        = brother
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         libri
        </i>
        = book
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A few masculine nouns end in
       <i>
        -e
       </i>
       , including
       <i>
        sisteme
       </i>
       (system) and
       <i>
        probleme
       </i>
       (problem). They are usually words coming from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       . However, because most masculine nouns end in a consonant, it is common to find the forms
       <i>
        sistem
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        problem
       </i>
       instead, more often in print than in speech.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also a number of masculine nouns borrowed intact from
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       , with a final
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       , like
       <i>
        treno
       </i>
       (train). Many of the words have been fully absorbed into the language and even form their plurals with the regular Friulian
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       rather than the Italian
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Desinence" title="Desinence">
        desinence
       </a>
       changing. Still, there are some purists, including those influential in Friulian publishing, who frown on such words and insist that the "proper" Friulian terms should be without the final
       <i>
        -o
       </i>
       . Despite the fact that one almost always hears
       <i>
        treno
       </i>
       , it is almost always written
       <i>
        tren
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Articles">
        Articles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Articles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Friulian
       <a href="/wiki/Article_(grammar)" title="Article (grammar)">
        definite article
       </a>
       (which corresponds to "the" in English) is derived from the Latin
       <i>
        ille
       </i>
       and takes the following forms:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Definite_article" title="Definite article">
         Definite articles
        </a>
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Number
        </th>
        <th>
         Masculine
        </th>
        <th>
         Feminine
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Singular
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         il
        </td>
        <td>
         la
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Plural
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         i
        </td>
        <td>
         lis
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Before a vowel, both
       <i>
        il
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        la
       </i>
       can be abbreviated to l'
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wikipedia:AUDIENCE" title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE">
          <span title="Wikipedia:AUDIENCE">
           example needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       in the standard forms. In the spoken language, various other articles are used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The indefinite article in Friulian (which corresponds to "a" and
       <i>
        an
       </i>
       in English) derives from the Latin
       <i>
        unus
       </i>
       and varies according to gender:
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indefinite_article" title="Indefinite article">
         Indefinite articles
        </a>
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Masculine
        </th>
        <td>
         un
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Feminine
        </th>
        <td>
         une
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       A partitive article also exists:
       <b>
        des
       </b>
       for feminine and
       <b>
        dai
       </b>
       for masculine:
       <i>
        des vacjis
       </i>
       –
       <i>
        some cows
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        dai libris
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        some books
       </i>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adjectives">
        Adjectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Adjectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A Friulian
       <a href="/wiki/Adjective" title="Adjective">
        adjective
       </a>
       must agree in gender and number with the noun it qualifies. Most adjectives have four forms for singular (masculine and feminine) and plural (masculine and feminine):
      </p>
      <table class="wikitable">
       <caption>
        Declination
       </caption>
       <tr>
        <th>
         Number
        </th>
        <th>
         Masculine
        </th>
        <th>
         Feminine
        </th>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Singular
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         brut
        </td>
        <td>
         brute
        </td>
       </tr>
       <tr>
        <td>
         <b>
          Plural
         </b>
        </td>
        <td>
         bruts
        </td>
        <td>
         brutis
        </td>
       </tr>
      </table>
      <p>
       Note that in some areas, the feminine is pronounced with different vowels: plural
       <i>
        brutes
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        brutas
       </i>
       , or singular
       <i>
        bruta
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        bruto
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       To form the plural, the normal rules are followed, but in the feminine is formed in severe ways from the masculine:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        in most cases, all that is needed is -e (
        <i>
         curt
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         curte
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        if the final letter is a -c, the feminine can end with -cje, -gje, -che, -ghe
       </li>
       <li>
        if the final letter is a -f, the feminine can end with -ve
       </li>
       <li>
        if the final letter is a -p, the feminine can end with -be
       </li>
       <li>
        if the final letter is a -t, the feminine can end with -de
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plurals">
        Plurals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Plurals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       To form the plural of masculine and feminine nouns ending in -e, the -e is changed to -is.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         taule
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         taulis
        </i>
        = table, tables
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjase
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cjasis
        </i>
        = house, houses
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lune
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         lunis
        </i>
        = moon, moons
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         scuele
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         scuelis
        </i>
        = school, schools
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         sisteme
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         sistemis
        </i>
        = system, systems
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         manece
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         manecis
        </i>
        = glove, gloves
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         gnece
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         gnecis
        </i>
        = niece, nieces
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The plural of almost all other nouns is just -s. It is always pronounced as voiceless [s], as in
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       <i>
        cats
       </i>
       , never as voiced [z], as in
       <i>
        dogs
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         man
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         mans
        </i>
        = hand, hands
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         lezion
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         lezions
        </i>
        = lesson, lessons
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjan
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cjans
        </i>
        = dog, dogs
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         gjat
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         gjats
        </i>
        = cat, cats
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fradi
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         fradis
        </i>
        = brother, brothers
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         libri
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         libris
        </i>
        = book, books
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         tren
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         trens
        </i>
        = train, trains
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         braç
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         braçs
        </i>
        = arm, arms (from Latin "bracchium")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         guant
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         guants
        </i>
        = glove, gloves (compare English "gauntlet")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In some Friulian dialects, there are many words whose final consonant becomes silent when the -s is added. The words include just about all those whose singular form ends in -t. The plural of
       <i>
        gjat
       </i>
       , for example, is written as
       <i>
        gjats
       </i>
       but is pronounced in much of Friuli as if it were
       <i>
        gjas
       </i>
       . The plural of
       <i>
        plat
       </i>
       'dish', though written as
       <i>
        plats
       </i>
       , is often pronounced as
       <i>
        plas
       </i>
       . Other words in this category include
       <i>
        clâf
       </i>
       (key) and
       <i>
        clap
       </i>
       (stone), whose plural forms, clâfs and claps, are often pronounced with no f or p, respectively (clâs, clas) so the longer a in the former is all that distinguishes it from the latter. Note also that a final -ç, which is pronounced either as the
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       "-ch" (in central Friulian) or as "-s", is pluralized in writing as -çs, regardless of whether the pluralized pronunciation is "-s" or "-ts" (it varies according to dialect): messaç / messaçs (message).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">
        Exceptions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Exceptions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Masculine nouns ending in -l or -li form their plurals by palatalising final -l or -li to -i.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjaval
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cjavai
        </i>
        = horse, horses (from Latin "caballus")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         fîl
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         fîi
        </i>
        = string, strings (from Latin "filum")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjapiel
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cjapiei
        </i>
        = hat, hats
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         cjaveli
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cjavei
        </i>
        = hair, hairs
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         voli
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         voi
        </i>
        = eye, eyes
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         zenoli
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         zenoi
        </i>
        = knee, knees (from Latin "genu")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Notice how these very often correspond to French nouns that form an irregular plural in -
       <i>
        x
       </i>
       :
       <i>
        cheval-chevaux, chapeau-chapeaux, cheveu-cheveux, oeil-yeux, genou-genoux
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Feminine nouns ending in -l have regular plurals.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         piel
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         piels
        </i>
        = skin, skins
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         val
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vals
        </i>
        (in northern Friulian also "tal", "tals") = valley, valleys
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Masculine nouns ending in -st form their plurals by palatalising the final -t to -cj
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         cavalarist
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         cavalariscj
        </i>
        = military horseman, military horsemen
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         test
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         tescj
        </i>
        = text, texts
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Some masculine nouns ending in -t form their plurals by palatalising the final -t to -cj:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         dint
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         dincj
        </i>
        = tooth, teeth (from Latin "dens, -tis")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         dut
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         ducj
        </i>
        = all (of one thing), all (of several things) (from Latin "totus")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Nouns ending in "s" do not change spelling in the plural, but some speakers may pronounce the plural -s differently from the singular -s.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         vues
        </i>
        = bone, bones
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         pes
        </i>
        = fish (singular or plural) (from Latin "piscis")
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         mês
        </i>
        = month, months (from Latin "mensis")
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The plural of
       <i>
        an
       </i>
       (year) has several forms depending on dialect, including
       <i>
        ain
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        ains
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        agn
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        agns
       </i>
       . Regardless of pronunciation, the written form is
       <i>
        agns
       </i>
       . The same happens for the adjective
       <i>
        bon
       </i>
       (good), as its plural is
       <i>
        bogns
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Clitic_subject_pronouns">
        Clitic subject pronouns
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Clitic subject pronouns">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A feature of Friulian are the clitic
       <a href="/wiki/Subject_(grammar)" title="Subject (grammar)">
        subject
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pronoun" title="Pronoun">
        pronouns
       </a>
       . Known in Friulian as
       <i>
        pleonastics
       </i>
       , are never stressed; they are used together with the verbs to express the subject and can be found before the
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Sentence_(linguistics)#Classification_by_purpose" title="Sentence (linguistics)">
        declarative sentences
       </a>
       or immediately after it in case of
       <a href="/wiki/Question" title="Question">
        interrogative
       </a>
       or vocative (
       <i>
        optative
       </i>
       ) sentences.
      </p>

        </td>

        </td>
        <td>

       <i>
        jo o lavori

       <i>

       means "Do I work?", while
       <i>
        lavorassio
       </i>
       means "I wish I worked".
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Adverbs">
        Adverbs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Adverbs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An adjective can be made into an
       <a href="/wiki/Adverb" title="Adverb">
        adverb
       </a>
       by adding -mentri to the ending of the feminine singular form of the adjective (
       <i>
        lente
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        lentementri
       </i>
       , slowly), but it can sometimes
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       lose the -e of the adjective (
       <i>
        facile
       </i>
       becomes
       <i>
        facilmentri
       </i>
       , easily). It is more common in the written language; in the spoken language people frequently use other forms or locutions (
       <i>
        a planc
       </i>
       for slowly).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Vocabulary">
        Vocabulary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Vocabulary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most vocabulary is derived from Latin, with substantial phonological and morphological changes throughout its history. Therefore, many words are shared with the
       <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">
        Romance languages
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but other languages have contributed:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Celtic_languages" title="Celtic languages">
         Celtic
        </a>
        words are many, because the substrate of the Vulgar Latin spoken in Friuli, was the Karn-Celtic language. ("bâr", wood; "clap/crap", stone;"cjâr", plow; "crot", frog)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
         German
        </a>
        words were introduced in particular in the
        <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
         Middle Ages
        </a>
        , during the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patrie_dal_Fri%C3%BBl" title="Patrie dal Friûl">
         Patrie dal Friûl
        </a>
        , when the influence from this culture was quite strong (
        <i>
         bearç
        </i>
        , backyard).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
         Slavic
        </a>
        words were brought by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slavic_peoples" title="Slavic peoples">
         Slavic
        </a>
        (mostly
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpine_Slavs" title="Alpine Slavs">
         Alpine Slavic
        </a>
        ) immigrants called several times to
        <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">
         Friuli
        </a>
        to repopulate lands devastated by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hungarian_people" title="Hungarian people">
         Hungarian
        </a>
        invasions in the 10th century (
        <i>
         cjast
        </i>
        , barn;
        <i>
         zigâ
        </i>
        , to shout). Furthermore, many Slavic words have entered Friulian through the centuries-long neighbouring between Friulians and
        <a href="/wiki/Slovenes" title="Slovenes">
         Slovenes
        </a>
        , especially in north-eastern Friuli (
        <a href="/wiki/Slavia_Friulana" title="Slavia Friulana">
         Slavia Friulana
        </a>
        ) and in the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorizia_and_Gradisca" title="Gorizia and Gradisca">
         Gorizia and Gradisca
        </a>
        area. Words such as
        <i>
         colaç
        </i>
        (cake),
        <i>
         cudiç
        </i>
        (devil) and
        <i>
         cos
        </i>
        (basket) are all of
        <a href="/wiki/Slovene_language" title="Slovene language">
         Slovene
        </a>
        origin. There are also many toponyms with Slavic roots.
       </li>
       <li>
        There are many words that have Germanic (probably
        <a href="/wiki/Lombardic_language" title="Lombardic language">
         Lombardic
        </a>
        origins) and Celtic roots (what still remained of the languages spoken before the Romans came). Examples of the first category are
        <i>
         sbregâ
        </i>
        , to tear;
        <i>
         sedon
        </i>
        , spoon;
        <i>
         taponâ
        </i>
        , to cover. For the latter category,
        <i>
         troi
        </i>
        , path;
        <i>
         bragons
        </i>
        , trousers.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Venetian_language" title="Venetian language">
         Venetian language
        </a>
        influenced Friulian vocabulary:
        <i>
         canucje
        </i>
        , straw.
       </li>
       <li>
        Scientific terms are often of Greek origin, and there are also some
        <a href="/wiki/Arabic_languages" title="Arabic languages">
         Arabic
        </a>
        terms in Friulian (
        <i>
         lambic
        </i>
        , still)
       </li>
       <li>
        Some
        <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
         French
        </a>
        words entered the Friulian vocabulary:
        <i>
         pardabon
        </i>
        , really and
        <i>
         gustâ
        </i>
        , to have lunch.
       </li>
       <li>
        Many
        <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
         English
        </a>
        words (such as computer, monitor, mouse and so on) have entered the Friulian vocabulary through Italian.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
         Italian
        </a>
        itself has a growing influence on Friulian vocabulary, especially as far as neologisms are concerned (
        <i>
         treno
        </i>
        meaning train,
        <i>
         aereo
        </i>
        meaning airplane). Such neologisms are currently used even if they not accepted in the official dictionary.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Present_condition">
        Present condition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Present condition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nowadays, Friulian is officially recognized in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       , supported by law 482/1999, which protects linguistic minorities. Therefore, optional teaching of Friulian has been introduced in many primary schools. An online newspaper is active, and there are also a number of musical groups singing in Friulian and some theatrical companies. Recently, two movies have been made in Friulian (
       <i>
        Tierç lion
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Lidrîs cuadrade di trê
       </i>
       ), with positive reviews in Italian newspapers. In about 40% of the communities in the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Udine" title="Province of Udine">
        Province of Udine
       </a>
       , road signs are in both Friulian and Italian. There is also an official translation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Bible" title="Bible">
        Bible
       </a>
       . In 2005, a notable brand of beer used Friulian for one of its commercials.
      </p>
      <p>
       The main association to foster the use and development of Friulian is the
       <i>
        Societât filologjiche furlane
       </i>
       , founded in
       <a href="/wiki/Gorizia" title="Gorizia">
        Gorizia
       </a>
       in 1919.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Toponyms">
        Toponyms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: Toponyms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Friulian_place_names" title="List of Friulian place names">
        List of Friulian place names

      <p>
       Every city and village in Friuli has two
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Friulian_place_names" title="List of Friulian place names">
        names
       </a>
       , one in
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian
       </a>
       and one in Friulian. Only the Italian is official and used in administration, but it is widely expected that the Friulian ones will receive partial acknowledgement in the near future. For example, the city of
       <a href="/wiki/Udine" title="Udine">
        Udine
       </a>
       is called
       <i>
        Udin
       </i>
       in Friulian, the town of
       <a href="/wiki/Tolmezzo" title="Tolmezzo">
        Tolmezzo
       </a>
       <i>
        Tumieç
       </i>
       and the town of
       <a href="/wiki/Aviano" title="Aviano">
        Aviano
       </a>
       is called both Avian and Pleif.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Standardisation">
        Standardisation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Standardisation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A challenge that Friulian shares with other minorities is to create a standard language and a unique writing system. The regional law 15/1996 approved a standard orthography, which represents the basis of a common variant and should be used in toponyms, official acts, written documents. The standard is based on Central Friulian, which was traditionally the language used in literature already in 1700 and afterwards (the biggest examples are probably Pieri Çorut's works) but with some changes:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the
        <a href="/wiki/Diphthong" title="Diphthong">
         diphthong
        </a>
        <i>
         ie
        </i>
        replaces
        <i>
         ia
        </i>
        :
        <i>
         fier (iron)
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         fiar
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         tiere (soil, earth)
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         tiare
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        the use of
        <i>
         vu
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         u
        </i>
        at the beginning of word:
        <i>
         vueli (oil)
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ueli
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         vueit (empty)
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ueit
        </i>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        the use of
        <i>
         i
        </i>
        between vocals:
        <i>
         ploie (rain)
        </i>
        instead of
        <i>
         ploe
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Standard Friulian is called in Friulian
       <i>
        furlan standard
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        furlan normalizât
       </i>
       or from
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Koin%C3%A9_language" title="Koiné language">
         coinè
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">
        Criticism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Criticism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There have been several critics of the standardisation of Friulian, mainly from speakers of local variants that differ substantially from the proposed standard; they also argue that the standard could eventually kill local variants. The supporters of standardisation refer to the various advantages that a unique form can bring to the language. Above all, it can help to stop the influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">
        Italian language
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">
        neologisms
       </a>
       , which pose a serious threat to Friulian's future development. They also point out that it is a written standard without affecting pronunciation, which can follow local variants.
      </p>
      <p>
       Opponents of the standardisation, on the other hand, insist that the standard language, being artificially created, is totally inadequate to represent the local variations, particularly from differences in the phonetic pronunciation of the words in each variant that may, in some cases, even require special and different diacritics for writing a single variant.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variants_of_Friulian">
        Variants of Friulian
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Variants of Friulian">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Four dialects of Friulian can be at least distinguished, all
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mutually_intelligible_languages" title="Mutually intelligible languages">
        mutually intelligible
       </a>
       . They are usually distinguished by the last vowel of many parts of speech (including nouns, adjectives, adverbs), following this scheme:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Central Friulian, spoken around
        <a href="/wiki/Udine" title="Udine">
         Udine
        </a>
        has words ending with -e. It is used in official documents and generally considered standard. Some people see it as the least original but one of the most recent variants since it does not show interesting features found in other variants, as it has Venetian influence.
       </li>
       <li>
        Northern Friulian, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Carnia" title="Carnia">
         Carnia
        </a>
        , has several variants. The language can vary with the valleys and words can end in -o, -e or -a. It is the most archaic variant.
       </li>
       <li>
        Southeastern Friulian, spoken in
        <a href="/wiki/Bassa_Friulana" title="Bassa Friulana">
         Bassa Friulana
        </a>
        and Isontino and in the area along the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isonzo" title="Isonzo">
         Isonzo
        </a>
        River (the area of the old
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gorizia_and_Gradisca" title="Gorizia and Gradisca">
         Contea di Gorizia e Gradisca
        </a>
        ) has words that end with -a. This variant has been known since the origins of the language and was used as official literary language by the Friulians of the Austrian Empire. It was influenced by German and Slavic.
       </li>
       <li>
        Western Friulian, including Pordenonese, is spoken in the
        <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Pordenone" title="Province of Pordenone">
         Province of Pordenone
        </a>
        and is also called
        <i>
         concordiese
        </i>
        , from
        <a href="/wiki/Concordia_Sagittaria" title="Concordia Sagittaria">
         Concordia Sagittaria
        </a>
        . Words end with -a or -e, but the strong Venetian influence, makes it be considered one of the most corrupted variants.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The word for
       <i>
        home
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        cjase
       </i>
       in Central Friulian and
       <i>
        cjasa
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        cjaso
       </i>
       in other areas.
       <a href="/wiki/Pier_Paolo_Pasolini" title="Pier Paolo Pasolini">
        Pier Paolo Pasolini
       </a>
       wrote his works in Western Friulian since he learned the language from his mother who was from
       <a href="/wiki/Casarsa_della_Delizia" title="Casarsa della Delizia">
        Casarsa/Cjasarsa
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Pordenone" title="Pordenone">
        Pordenone
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 13th century, early literary works in Friulian were based on the language spoken in Cividale del Friuli, which was the most important town in Friuli. The endings in -o, which, interestingly, now is restricted to some villages in
       <a href="/wiki/Carnia" title="Carnia">
        Carnia
       </a>
       . Later, the main city of
       <a href="/wiki/Friuli" title="Friuli">
        Friuli
       </a>
       became
       <a href="/wiki/Udine" title="Udine">
        Udine
       </a>
       and the most common ending was -a; only from the 16th century on, -e endings were used in standard Friulian.
      </p>

      <p>
       In the official writing system, approved by the
       <a href="/wiki/Province_of_Udine" title="Province of Udine">
        Province of Udine
       </a>
       and used in official documents, Friulian is written using the
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_script" title="Latin script">
        Latin script
       </a>
       with the c-
       <a href="/wiki/Cedilla" title="Cedilla">
        cedilla
       </a>
       (ç). The letter q is used only for personal names and historical toponyms, and in every other case, it is replaced by c. Besides that, k, x, w, and y appear only in loan words so they are not considered part of the alphabet.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Aa Bb Cc Çç Dd Ee Ff Gg Hh Ii Jj Ll Mm Nn Oo Pp Qq Rr Ss Tt Uu Vv Zz
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       There are also
       <a href="/wiki/Grave_accent" title="Grave accent">
        grave accents
       </a>
       (à, è, ì, ò and ù) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Circumflex_accent" title="Circumflex accent">
        circumflex accents
       </a>
       (â, ê, î, ô, and û), which are put above the
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel" title="Vowel">
        vowels
       </a>
       to distinguish between homophonic words or to show stress (the former) and show long vowels (the latter).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_systems">
        Other systems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Friulian_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: Other systems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An alternative system is called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Faggin-Nazzi" title="Faggin-Nazzi">
        Faggin-Nazzi
       </a>
       from the names of the scholars who proposed it. It is less common, probably also because it is more difficult for a beginner for its use of letters, such as č, that are typical of
       <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">
        Slavic languages
       </a>
       but seem foreign to native Italian speakers.
      </p>
