      <p>
       <b>
        Stress in
        <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
         Spanish
        </a>
       </b>
       is functional: to change the placement of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stressed_syllable" title="Stressed syllable">
        stress
       </a>
       changes the meaning of a sentence or phrase: for example,
       <i>
        célebre
       </i>
       ('famous'),
       <i>
        celebre
       </i>
       ('[that] he/she celebrates'), and
       <i>
        celebré
       </i>
       ('I celebrated') contrast by stress. There is some variance between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spanish_dialects" title="Spanish dialects">
        Spanish dialects
       </a>
       ; a speaker of
       <a href="/wiki/Rioplatense_Spanish" title="Rioplatense Spanish">
        Rioplatense Spanish
       </a>
       will pronounce
       <i>
        boina
       </i>
       ('beret') as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈboi.na]
        </a>
       </span>
       while a speaker of
       <a href="/wiki/Colombian_Spanish" title="Colombian Spanish">
        Colombian Spanish
       </a>
       will pronounce it as
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [boˈina]
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transcription">
        Transcription
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stress_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transcription">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spanish has only two degrees of stress. In traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Phonetic_transcription" title="Phonetic transcription">
        transcription
       </a>
       , primary stress is marked with an
       <a href="/wiki/Acute_accent" title="Acute accent">
        acute accent
       </a>
       (´) over the vowel. Unstressed parts of a word are emphasized by placing a
       <a href="/wiki/Breve" title="Breve">
        breve
       </a>
       (˘) over the vowel if a mark is needed, or it is left unmarked.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Position">
        Position
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stress_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Position">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Stress usually occurs in three positions in Spanish: on the final syllable (
       <a href="/wiki/Oxytone" title="Oxytone">
        oxytone
       </a>
       ), the penultimate syllable (
       <a href="/wiki/Paroxytone" title="Paroxytone">
        paroxytone
       </a>
       ), and the antepenultimate syllable (
       <a href="/wiki/Proparoxytone" title="Proparoxytone">
        proparoxytone
       </a>
       ). (In very rare cases, it can come on the fourth last syllable in compound words.) Vowel-final words and those ending in
       <i>
        -s
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        -n
       </i>
       are usually stressed on the penultimate syllable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That accounts for around 80% of Spanish vocabulary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Creating_contrasts">
        Creating contrasts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stress_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Creating contrasts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All Spanish words have at least one stressed syllable when they are used in isolation. The word
       <i>
        para
       </i>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [ˈpaɾa]
        </a>
       </span>
       can be a
       <a href="/wiki/Verb" title="Verb">
        verb
       </a>
       (the
       <a href="/wiki/Personal_pronoun" title="Personal pronoun">
        singular pronoun
       </a>
       form of "stop") or a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Preposition" title="Preposition">
        preposition
       </a>
       (in order to, for). When words are used the stress can be dropped depending on the part of speech.
       <i>
        Para el coche
       </i>
       can mean "stop the car" if the stress remains. If the stress is removed, it means "for the car".
      </p>
      <p>
       In English, contrasts are made by
       <a href="/wiki/Vowel_reduction" title="Vowel reduction">
        reducing
       </a>
       vowels, changing the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volume_(sound)" title="Volume (sound)">
        volume
       </a>
       of the word, or changing the
       <a href="/wiki/Intonation_(linguistics)" title="Intonation (linguistics)">
        intonation
       </a>
       of the phrase. For example,
       <i>
        this is
        <b>
         her
        </b>
        car
       </i>
       emphasizes the owner of the car. If the stress is changed to say
       <i>
        this is her
        <b>
         car
        </b>
       </i>
       , the emphasis is on showing what object belongs to a specific person. In Spanish, the stress is almost always changed by reordering the words. Using the same example
       <i>
        este coche es suyo
       </i>
       emphasizes the owner and
       <i>
        éste es su coche
       </i>
       emphasizes the object.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Word_stress_categories">
        Word stress categories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Stress_in_Spanish&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Word stress categories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All Spanish words can be classified into one of four groups based on the position of their stress. If the last syllable is stressed it falls into the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oxytone" title="Oxytone">
         aguda
        </a>
       </i>
       category.
       <i>
        Aguda
       </i>
       words generally end in a consonant other than
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       , or are a conjugated verb that ends in an accented, stressed vowel. If the stress falls on the second to last syllable, it is classified as a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Paroxytone" title="Paroxytone">
         llana
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        grave
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        Llanas
       </i>
       typically are words that end in
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       , or a vowel. Any exceptions have a written accent. If the stress is placed on the third to last or the fourth to last syllable, they are categorized as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Proparoxytone" title="Proparoxytone">
         esdrújulas
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        sobresdrújulas
       </i>
       , respectively. In either of the last two categories, the stressed syllable must be accented to break the rules of the first two categories. No single Spanish word is classified as a
       <i>
        sobresdrújula
       </i>
       , only compound verbs like
       <i>
        diciéndonosla
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        diciendo-nos-la
       </i>
       ; telling it to us).
      </p>
