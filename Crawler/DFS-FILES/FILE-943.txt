      <p>
       <b>
        Middle French
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       :
       <i>
        <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr">
         moyen français
        </span>
       </i>
       ) is a historical division of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French language
       </a>
       that covers the period from the 14th to the early 17th centuries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is a period of transition during which:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the French language became clearly distinguished from the other competing
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O%C3%AFl_languages" title="Oïl languages">
         Oïl languages
        </a>
        , which are sometimes subsumed within the concept of
        <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">
         Old French
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         ancien français
        </i>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        the French language was imposed as the
        <a href="/wiki/Official_language" title="Official language">
         official language
        </a>
        of the kingdom of France in place of
        <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
         Latin
        </a>
        and other Oïl and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occitan" title="Occitan">
         Occitan
        </a>
        languages
       </li>
       <li>
        the literary development of French prepared the vocabulary and grammar for the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_French" title="Classical French">
         Classical French
        </a>
        (
        <i>
         français classique
        </i>
        ) spoken in the 17th and 18th centuries.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most important change found in Middle French is the complete disappearance of the noun
       <a href="/wiki/Declension" title="Declension">
        declension
       </a>
       system (already underway for centuries). There is no longer a distinction between
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nominative" title="Nominative">
        nominative
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oblique_case" title="Oblique case">
        oblique
       </a>
       forms of nouns, and plurals are indicated simply with an
       <i>
        s
       </i>
       . This transformation necessitates an increased reliance on the
       <a href="/wiki/Word_order" title="Word order">
        order of words
       </a>
       in the sentence, which becomes more or less the
       <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">
        syntax
       </a>
       of modern French (although there is a continued reliance on the verb in the second position of a sentence, or "
       <a href="/wiki/V2_word_order" title="V2 word order">
        verb-second structure
       </a>
       ", until the 16th century).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Among the elites, Latin was still the language of education, administration, and bureaucracy; this changed in 1539, with the
       <a href="/wiki/Ordinance_of_Villers-Cotter%C3%AAts" title="Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts">
        Ordinance of Villers-Cotterêts
       </a>
       , in which
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_I_of_France" title="Francis I of France">
        François I
       </a>
       made French alone the language for legal acts. Regional differences were still extremely pronounced throughout France: In the south of France,
       <a href="/wiki/Occitan_language" title="Occitan language">
        Occitan
       </a>
       languages dominated; in east central France,
       <a href="/wiki/Franco-Proven%C3%A7al_language" title="Franco-Provençal language">
        Franco-Provençal
       </a>
       languages were predominant; while, in the north of France, Oïl languages other than
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francien" title="Francien">
        Francien
       </a>
       continued to be spoken.
      </p>
      <p>
       The fascination with classical texts led to numerous borrowings from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       . Numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Neologism" title="Neologism">
        neologisms
       </a>
       based on Latin roots were introduced, and some scholars modified the spelling of French words to bring them into conformity with their Latin roots, sometimes erroneously. This often produced a radical difference between a word's spelling and the way it was pronounced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The French wars in
       <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">
        Italy
       </a>
       and the presence of Italians in the French court brought the French into contact with Italian
       <a href="/wiki/Humanism" title="Humanism">
        humanism
       </a>
       . Many words dealing with military (
       <i>
        alarme
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        cavalier
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        espion
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        infanterie
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        camp
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        canon
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        soldat
       </i>
       ) and artistic (especially architectural:
       <i>
        arcade
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        architrave
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        balcon
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        corridor
       </i>
       ; also literary:
       <i>
        sonnet
       </i>
       ) practices were borrowed from Italian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These tendencies would continue through Classical French.
      </p>
      <p>
       There were also some borrowings from
       <a href="/wiki/Spanish_language" title="Spanish language">
        Spanish
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        casque
       </i>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">
        German
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        reître
       </i>
       ) and from the Americas (
       <i>
        cacao
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        hamac
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        maïs
       </i>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Norman_language" title="Anglo-Norman language">
        Anglo-Norman language
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English
       </a>
       had left words of French and
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_language" title="Norman language">
        Norman
       </a>
       origin in England. Some words of Romance origin now found their way back into French as
       <a href="/wiki/Doublet_(linguistics)" title="Doublet (linguistics)">
        doublets
       </a>
       through war and trading contacts.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also, the meaning and usage of many words from Old French were transformed.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spelling and punctuation in this period are extremely variable. The introduction of printing in 1470 highlighted the need for
       <a href="/wiki/Reforms_of_French_orthography" title="Reforms of French orthography">
        reform in spelling
       </a>
       . One proposed reform came from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques_Peletier_du_Mans" title="Jacques Peletier du Mans">
        Jacques Peletier du Mans
       </a>
       , who developed a phonetic spelling system and introduced new typographic signs (1550); but this attempt at spelling reform was not followed.
      </p>
      <p>
       This period saw the publication of the first French grammars and of the French-Latin dictionary of
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Estienne" title="Robert Estienne">
        Robert Estienne
       </a>
       (1539).
      </p>
      <p>
       At the beginning of the 17th century, French would see the continued unification of French, the suppression of certain forms, and the prescription of rules, leading to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Classical_French" title="Classical French">
        Classical French
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Middle_French&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Middle French is the language found in the writings of
       <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Villon" title="François Villon">
        François Villon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cl%C3%A9ment_Marot" title="Clément Marot">
        Clément Marot
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rabelais" title="Rabelais">
        Rabelais
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montaigne" title="Montaigne">
        Montaigne
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ronsard" title="Ronsard">
        Ronsard
       </a>
       , and the poets of the
       <a href="/wiki/La_Pl%C3%A9iade" title="La Pléiade">
        Pléiade
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The affirmation and glorification of French finds its greatest manifestation in the "Defense and Illustration of the French Language" (1549) by the poet
       <a href="/wiki/Joachim_du_Bellay" title="Joachim du Bellay">
        Joachim du Bellay
       </a>
       , which maintained that French (like the Tuscan of
       <a href="/wiki/Petrarch" title="Petrarch">
        Petrarch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">
        Dante
       </a>
       ) was a worthy language for literary expression and which promulgated a program of linguistic production and purification (including the imitation of Latin genres).
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Canada" title="French Canada">
        French Canada
       </a>
       's history, Middle French is almost only encountered when reading the travels of
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cartier" title="Jacques Cartier">
        Jacques Cartier
       </a>
       in their original versions, after which the history usually fast-forwards 65 years to
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_de_Champlain" title="Samuel de Champlain">
        Samuel de Champlain
       </a>
       , whose French is better characterised as Classical.
      </p>
