      <p>
       <b>
        Libertad Lamarque
       </b>
       (
       <small>
        Spanish pronunciation:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Spanish" title="Help:IPA/Spanish">
         [liβerˈtað laˈmarke]
        </a>
       </span>
       ); (
       <a href="/wiki/Rosario,_Santa_Fe" title="Rosario, Santa Fe">
        Rosario
       </a>
       , November 24, 1908 –
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       , December 12, 2000) was an
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentine
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Actor" title="Actor">
        actress
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Singer" title="Singer">
        singer
       </a>
       , one of the icons of the
       <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_Argentina#1930s–1950s:_The_Golden_Age" title="Cinema of Argentina">
        Golden Age of Argentine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Golden_Age_of_Mexican_cinema" title="Golden Age of Mexican cinema">
        Mexican
       </a>
       cinema. She achieved fame throughout
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       , and became known as “La Novia de América” (“The Sweetheart of the Americas”). By the time she died in 2000, she had appeared in 65 films (21 filmed in Argentina, 45 in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       and one in
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       ) and six soap operas, had recorded over 800 songs and had made innumerable theatrical appearances.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Libertad Lamarque was born November 24, 1908 in
       <a href="/wiki/Rosario,_Santa_Fe" title="Rosario, Santa Fe">
        Rosario
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Santa_Fe_Province" title="Santa Fe Province">
        Santa Fe Province
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
        Argentina
       </a>
       to Gaudencio Lamarque
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Josefa Bouza
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       . She was named Libertad (Liberty) because at the time of her birth, her father, a Uruguayan anarchist of French descent, was imprisoned and pleading for release.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_career">
        Early career
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early career">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At the age of 7, Lamarque won first prize in a stage competition,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and participated with a group of street singers that made tours of nearby cities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_career_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Early_career-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1923, she appeared in her first professional role, the stage show
       <i>
        "Madre Tierra"
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Her local acclaim convinced her parents that the family should relocate to
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       , where Libertad's chances of a career would be better. The family hung their hopes on a letter of introduction from a local journalist to the owner of the National Theatre, Pascual Carcavallo. It proved successful, as in 1926 Libertad was hired to sing in the choir and given a one-year contract. Her debut was in a play called
       <i>
        La muchacha de Montmartre
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Girl from Montmartre
       </i>
       ) by José A. Saldías, where she sang as part of a trio with
       <a href="/wiki/Olinda_Boz%C3%A1n" title="Olinda Bozán">
        Olinda Bozán
       </a>
       and Antonia Volpe, to the guitar accompaniment of Rafael Iriarte. Within a couple of months, she had begun singing on Radio Prieto, and was signed for record production with
       <a href="/wiki/Victor_Talking_Machine_Company" title="Victor Talking Machine Company">
        Victor Records
       </a>
       , which released her first album,
       <i>
        Gaucho Sol
       </i>
       , on 26 September 1926, as well as the single
       <i>
        Chilenito
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_career_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Early_career-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1929 she began working in
       <a href="/wiki/Alberto_Vaccarezza" title="Alberto Vaccarezza">
        Alberto Vaccarezza
       </a>
       's,
       <i>
        El conventillo de la Paloma
       </i>
       ("The Tenement of the Dove"), which was about the life of a girl Doce Pesos, living in an immigrant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tenement_house" title="Tenement house">
        tenement house
       </a>
       . After two years and 1,000 performances, Lamarque quit the show to focus on her music career. She traveled through several provinces of Argentina and through neighboring
       <a href="/wiki/Paraguay" title="Paraguay">
        Paraguay
       </a>
       accompanied by the guitarists
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gregorio_Rivero&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gregorio Rivero (page does not exist)">
        Gregorio Rivero
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Angel_Las_Heras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Angel Las Heras (page does not exist)">
        Angel Las Heras
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nicol%C3%A1s_Ferrari&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nicolás Ferrari (page does not exist)">
        Nicolás Ferrari
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_career_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Early_career-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1930,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetheart_of_America_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sweetheart_of_America-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       following this tour she entered a competition held at the Teatro Colón in Buenos Aires, won first prize for her performances of the tangos
       <i>
        "La cumparsita"
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        "Tocaneando"
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_tribute_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UK_tribute-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and earned the title "Queen of Tango".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_career_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Early_career-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       She capped this cycle by performing
       <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Straus_(composer)" title="Oscar Straus (composer)">
        Oscar Straus
       </a>
       's

        Tres valses
       </i>
       (Three Waltzes), an adaptation of the operetta, with Chilean singer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Choly_Mur&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Choly Mur (page does not exist)">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Bandoneon" title="Bandoneon">

       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=H%C3%A9ctor_Mar%C3%ADa_Artola&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Héctor María Artola (page does not exist)">

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enrique_Santos_Discepolo" title="Enrique Santos Discepolo">
        Enrique Santos Discepolo
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sweetheart_of_America_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sweetheart_of_America-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adi%C3%B3s,_Argentina" title="Adiós, Argentina">

        <a href="/wiki/%C2%A1Tango!" title="¡Tango!">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-3">

        </a>

      <h3>
       <span id="Legendary_rift_with_Eva_Per.C3.B3n">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legendary_rift_with_Eva_Perón">
        Legendary rift with Eva Perón
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Legendary rift with Eva Perón">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Peron-Lamarque.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="430" data-file-width="604" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Peron-Lamarque.jpg/220px-Peron-Lamarque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Peron-Lamarque.jpg/330px-Peron-Lamarque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Peron-Lamarque.jpg/440px-Peron-Lamarque.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Peron-Lamarque.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">
          Eva Perón
         </a>
         and Libertad Lamarque in film
         <i>
          <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_cabalgata_del_circo" title="La cabalgata del circo">
           La cabalgata del circo
          </a>
         </i>
         (1945).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Legend has it that Lamarque left Argentina because she had been blacklisted by its First Lady,
       <a href="/wiki/Eva_Per%C3%B3n" title="Eva Perón">
        Eva Perón
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_tribute_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-UK_tribute-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Marysa Navarro and Nicholas Frasier, authors of
       <i>
        Evita: The Real Life of Eva Perón
       </i>
       , however, contend that this is not likely. They argue that Lamarque moved to Mexico, where she was already adored by the audiences, because Mexican cinema was in a better state during the 1940s/50s than Argentine cinema. The authors also point out that Lamarque traveled freely between Argentina and Mexico during the lifetime of Eva Perón and beyond, which does not support the blacklisting legend.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fraser/Navarro_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fraser/Navarro-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lamarque herself denied during her lifetime certain aspects of the legend, especially the reports that she had slapped Eva on the set of
       <i>
        La cabalgata del circo
       </i>
       . In her 1986 autobiography she flatly denied the allegations and explained that she was simply mortified by Eva's lack of discipline during production of the film.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pigna_(2007)_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pigna_(2007)-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Lamarque, Eva refused to take her work seriously and always arrived late or stalled the filming for trivial personal reasons. Complaints to either the producer or the director produced no result, as they were giving Eva preferential treatment as the girlfriend of
       <a href="/wiki/Juan_Per%C3%B3n" title="Juan Perón">
        Juan Perón
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peron_dispute_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Peron_dispute-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By 1945, Eva and Juan Perón were ensconced in the Presidential Palace,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peron_in_power_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Peron_in_power-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       rumors circulated that Evita had forbidden radio stations and film studios to play Lamarque's music or hire her,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_tribute_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-UK_tribute-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Lamarque's films, music and publicity in Argentina seemed to have come to an end.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relocation_to_Mexico">
        Relocation to Mexico
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Relocation to Mexico">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Between January and February 1946, Lamarque appeared in her first tour of Cuba, which was listed as the artistic event of the season. On 7 January, she debuted in the Teatro América with a varied repertoire, but closed the show with "Facundo" by Cuban composer
       <a href="/wiki/Eliseo_Grenet" title="Eliseo Grenet">
        Eliseo Grenet
       </a>
       to much applause. She performed shows daily, sometimes twice a day and on her final performance at Teatro América, January 20, she held three performances. She performed at Camaguey, Ciego de Avila, Santa Clara, Holguin, and Santiago de Cuba; visited hospitals and schools; and her farewell performance at the Municipal Amphitheatre of Havana was said to have been attended by 20,000 fans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuba_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuba-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was on this trip that the Cuban press first dubbed her America's Sweetheart.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Just before the Cuban trip, Hollywood had offered Lamarque a seven-year contract but she refused it as she did not speak English and was afraid she would be taken advantage of.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuba_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuba-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite her fears of working in the US, Lamarque sold out
       <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Hall" title="Carnegie Hall">
        Carnegie Hall
       </a>
       for a 1947 performance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NY_Times_Movie_Bio_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NY_Times_Movie_Bio-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When Mexico, on the other hand, offered her a picture deal to appear with legendary Spanish filmmaker
       <a href="/wiki/Luis_Bu%C3%B1uel" title="Luis Buñuel">
        Luis Buñuel
       </a>
       , she agreed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gran_Casino_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gran_Casino-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and moved to Mexico in 1946.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_tribute_5-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-UK_tribute-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The picture,
       <i>
        Gran Casino
       </i>
       , co-starring
       <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Negrete" title="Jorge Negrete">
        Jorge Negrete
       </a>
       was a flop,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gran_Casino_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gran_Casino-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but other roles followed, such as
       <i>
        Soledad
       </i>
       (1947),
       <i>
        La dama del velo
       </i>
       (1948),
       <i>
        Huellas de un pasado
       </i>
       (1950),
       <i>
        Mujeres sin lágrimas
       </i>
       (1951),
       <i>
        Nunca es tarde para amar
       </i>
       (1952),
       <i>
        Ansiedad
       </i>
       (1952), and
       <i>
        Rostros olvidados
       </i>
       (1952).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Movies_list_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Movies_list-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some of her best work during this period was in
       <i>
        Otra primavera
       </i>
       , filmed in 1949,
       <i>
        La loca
       </i>
       (1951) and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuando_me_vaya" title="Cuando me vaya">
         Cuando me vaya
        </a>
       </i>
       (1953); for each of these she was nominated for a Best actress
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_Award" title="Ariel Award">
        Ariel Award
       </a>
       in 1951, 1953, and 1955, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ariel_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ariel-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rosas_blancas_para_mi_hermana_negra&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rosas blancas para mi hermana negra (page does not exist)">
         Rosas blancas para mi hermana negra

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peron_in_power_10-1">

       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Eusebia_Cosme&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Eusebia Cosme (page does not exist)">
        Eusebia Cosme
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eusebia_Cosme_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eusebia_Cosme-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and her last two Argentine films,
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=La_sonrisa_de_mam%C3%A1&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="La sonrisa de mamá (page does not exist)">
         La sonrisa de mamá
        </a>
       </i>
       (1972) and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_mam%C3%A1_de_la_novia" title="La mamá de la novia">
         La mamá de la novia
        </a>
       </i>
       (1978).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-later_career_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-later_career-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But as she wound down her movie career, she began touring with music again. In the late 1950s, she did a concert tour with Puerto Rican singer
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jes%C3%BAs_Qui%C3%B1ones_Ledesma&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jesús Quiñones Ledesma (page does not exist)">
        Jesús Quiñones Ledesma
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jesús_Quiñones_Ledesma_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jesús_Quiñones_Ledesma-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and worked in Chile, Puerto Rico, Venezuela, Dominican Republic, Guatemala, El Salvador, Honduras and returned to Cuba to do theater and record a several albums.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       She returned to Argentina after Evita's death and was the first person to bring a Spanish version of "
       <a href="/wiki/Hello,_Dolly!_(musical)" title="Hello, Dolly! (musical)">
        Hello Dolly
       </a>
       " to Latin audiences
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       at a 1967 performance at the Teatro Nacional in Buenos Aires,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which she also later performed in Mexico in 1968 staged by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Manolo_Fabreg%C3%A1s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Manolo Fabregás (page does not exist)">
        Manolo Fabregás
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fabregas_obit_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fabregas_obit-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1982, she starred in the musical revue,
       <i>
        "Libertad Lamarque, ¿es una mujer de suerte?"
       </i>
       at the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Teatro_Lola_Membrives&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Teatro Lola Membrives (page does not exist)">
        Teatro Lola Membrives
       </a>
       in Buenos Aires. She wrote the script, which was adapted by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nicol%C3%A1s_Carreras&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nicolás Carreras (page does not exist)">
        Nicolás Carreras
       </a>
       under the musical direction of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Oscar_Cardozo_Ocampo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Oscar Cardozo Ocampo (page does not exist)">
        Oscar Cardozo Ocampo
       </a>
       . In 1988, Lamarque participated in the season at
       <a href="/wiki/Mar_del_Plata" title="Mar del Plata">
        Mar del Plata
       </a>
       's Teatro Opera with the musical
       <i>
        "A todo tango II"
       </i>
       under the direction of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jos%C3%A9_Col%C3%A1ngelo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="José Colángelo (page does not exist)">
        José Colángelo
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-later_career_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-later_career-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the 1960s she appeared in several episodes of a television show called "Saturday Circular" with
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nicholas_Mancera&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nicholas Mancera (page does not exist)">
        Nicholas Mancera
       </a>
       and in 1961, she filmed
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=As%C3%AD_era_mi_madre&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Así era mi madre (page does not exist)">
         Así era mi madre
        </a>
       </i>
       , her only Spanish film. Her first venture into soap operas came in Venezuela, when she was offered a role in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esmeralda_(telenovela)" title="Esmeralda (telenovela)">
         Esmeralda
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1972. That was followed by another Venezuelan production called
       <i>
        Mamá
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-later_career_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-later_career-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1980, she began the first of her Mexican
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telenovelas" title="Telenovelas">
        telenovelas
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Soledad
       </i>
       , followed by her role in
       <i>
        Carita de Angel
       </i>
       at the age of 91 where she played a Mother superior. Her last role, as la abuela Piedad in
       <i>
        La Usurpadora
       </i>
       (
       <i>
        The Usurper
       </i>
       ) was in 1998 - two years before her death.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2018)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Libertadlamarque.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="601" data-file-width="581" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Libertadlamarque.jpg/220px-Libertadlamarque.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Libertadlamarque.jpg/330px-Libertadlamarque.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Libertadlamarque.jpg/440px-Libertadlamarque.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>

      <p>
       Lamarque received an award in 1978 from Venezuelan President
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carlos_Andres_Perez" title="Carlos Andres Perez">
        Carlos Andres Perez
       </a>
       for her contributions to Latin American culture and in 1980, and shared a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Critic%27s_Choice_Award" title="Critic's Choice Award">
        Critic's Choice Award
       </a>
       with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_F%C3%A9lix" title="Maria Félix">
        Maria Félix
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello_Dolly_in_Argentina_12-3">

         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1985, she received the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Konex_Platinum_Award&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Konex Platinum Award (page does not exist)">
        Konex Platinum Award
       </a>
       for Best Tango Singer in Argentina. In 1988, she put her hand prints on the "Walk of Fame of the Hermitage Hotel".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

           citation needed

        ]
       </sup>

      <p>
       In 1989, she was honored at the Festival of San Sebastian, Spain, for her film achievements and was recognized by the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Caesar_Awards&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Caesar Awards (page does not exist)">
        Caesar Awards
       </a>
       given by the Association of American theater to Latin American artists in
       <a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles" title="Los Angeles">
        Los Angeles
       </a>
       . That same year, a tile bearing her name placed in the "Sidewalk of Latin Stars" in
       <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">
        Miami
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-later_career_18-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-later_career-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lamarque was honored in 1993 by
       <i>
        "Celebrando Magazine"
       </i>
       , a Spanish-language publication which is nationally distributed in the US, for her 70 years in film, theater and music and her philanthropy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carnegie_Hall_2-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carnegie_Hall-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       In 1998, she was appointed as Honorary Cultural Advisor and designated as a Cultural Legend in Buenos Aires.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cultural_legend_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cultural_legend-21">
         [21]
        </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Last_Years_and_Death">
        Last Years and Death
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Last Years and Death">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1996 Libertad moved to the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and settled in her
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coral_Gables" title="Coral Gables">
        Coral Gables
       </a>
       home in
       <a href="/wiki/Miami" title="Miami">
        Miami
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       , but often flew to
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       to attend her professional appointments, and to
       <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">
        Buenos Aires
       </a>
       as well, once her daughter, grandsons and great-grandsons lived there. Ms. Lamarque had a notorious vitality, despite reaching 90 years old.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1998 Libertad Lamarque featured the soap opera
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/La_Usurpadora" title="La Usurpadora">
        La Usurpadora
       </a>
       , where she played Ms. Piedad Bracho. The soap opera was a huge success in Mexico and in many countries in
       <a href="/wiki/Latin_America" title="Latin America">
        Latin America
       </a>
       . Her last role on the TV was on the
       <a href="/wiki/Telenovela" title="Telenovela">
        telenovela
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carita_de_%C3%81ngel" title="Carita de Ángel">
        Carita de Ángel
       </a>
       , as the Mother Superior. She was overjoyed by taking part on these projects.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the early days of December 2000, Libertad Lamarque was rushed to Santa Elena Hospital after feeling sick and having difficulties to breathe. She died aged 92 in December 12, 2000, in
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico_City" title="Mexico City">
        Mexico City
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mexico" title="Mexico">
        Mexico
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Pneumonia" title="Pneumonia">
        pneumonia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Her only daughter, Mirtha Libertad Lamarque Romero Deluca, died in October 19, 2014, aged 86.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">
        Personal life
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Libertad_Lamarque&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Personal life">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1926, Lamarque married Emilio Romero and had a daughter, Mirtha, with him before divorcing in 1945. Divorce was not possible at that time in Argentina and though the marriage was quickly over, it would take 12 years to officially be ended.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-obit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1935, she suffered several personal crises which led to a suicide attempt in Chile. She attempted to throw herself out of a hotel window, but an awning broke her fall. Shortly thereafter, her estranged husband kidnapped their daughter and took Mirtha to
       <a href="/wiki/Uruguay" title="Uruguay">
        Uruguay
       </a>
       : A group of friends, including Alfredo Malerba, and her attorney were able to help her regain custody.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Early_career_3-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Early_career-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-suicide_and_kidnapping_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-suicide_and_kidnapping-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lamarque's second husband was Alfredo Malerba, with whom she was married for nearly 50 years, until his death.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuba_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuba-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
