   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Conjunction (astronomy)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        conjunction
       </b>
       occurs when two
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        astronomical objects
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       have either the same
       <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">
        right ascension
       </a>
       or the same
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic longitude
       </a>
       , usually as observed from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_symbol" title="Astronomical symbol">
        astronomical symbol
       </a>
       for conjunction is ☌ (in
       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">
        Unicode
       </a>
       U+260C) and handwritten
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png">
        <img alt="Astronomical conjunction symbol.png" data-file-height="53" data-file-width="61" height="17" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png/20px-Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png/30px-Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png/40px-Astronomical_conjunction_symbol.png 2x" width="20"/>
       </a>
       . The conjunction symbol is not used in modern astronomy. It continues to be used in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrological_symbols#Aspects" title="Astrological symbols">
        astrology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When two objects always appear close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       – such as two
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and a planet, or the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and a planet – this fact implies an apparent close approach between the objects as seen on the sky. A related word,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Appulse" title="Appulse">
         appulse
        </a>
       </i>
       , is the minimum apparent separation on the sky of two astronomical objects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Conjunctions involve either two objects in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       or one object in the Solar System and a more distant object, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       . A conjunction is an apparent phenomenon caused by the observer's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">
        perspective
       </a>
       : the two objects involved are not actually close to one another in
       <a href="/wiki/Space" title="Space">
        space
       </a>
       . Conjunctions between two bright objects close to the ecliptic, such as two bright planets, can be seen with the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        naked eye
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Passing_close">
        Passing close
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Passing close">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2655" data-file-width="4089" height="143" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg/220px-Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg/330px-Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg/440px-Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Planetary_Conjunction_over_Paranal.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A conjunction of Mars and Jupiter in the morning of 1 May 2011, when, about an hour before sunrise, five of our Solar System's eight planets and the Moon could be seen from
         <a href="/wiki/Cerro_Paranal" title="Cerro Paranal">
          Cerro Paranal
         </a>
         , Chile.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
          <a href="#cite_note-4">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       More generally, in the particular case of two
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       , it means that they merely have the same
       <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">
        right ascension
       </a>
       (and hence the same
       <a href="/wiki/Hour_angle" title="Hour angle">
        hour angle
       </a>
       ). This is called conjunction in right ascension. However, there is also the term conjunction in ecliptic longitude. At such conjunction both objects have the same ecliptic longitude. Conjunction in right ascension and conjunction in ecliptic longitude do not normally take place at the same time, but in most cases nearly at the same time. However, at
       <a href="/wiki/Triple_conjunction" title="Triple conjunction">
        triple conjunctions
       </a>
       , it is possible that a conjunction only in right ascension (or ecliptic length) occurs. At the time of conjunction – it does not matter if in right ascension or in ecliptic longitude – the involved planets are close together upon the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       . In the vast majority of such cases, one of the planets will appear to pass north or south of the other.
      </p>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_(VLT)_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2432" data-file-width="3632" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg/220px-ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg/330px-ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg/440px-ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_%28VLT%29_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:ESO%E2%80%99s_Very_Large_Telescope_(VLT)_observatory_at_Paranal.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         In the night sky over
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESO" title="ESO">
          ESO
         </a>
         's
         <a href="/wiki/Very_Large_Telescope" title="Very Large Telescope">
          Very Large Telescope
         </a>
         (VLT) observatory at
         <a href="/wiki/Paranal_Observatory" title="Paranal Observatory">
          Paranal
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         shines along with two bright companions:
         <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
          Venus
         </a>
         and
         <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
          Jupiter
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       However, if two celestial bodies attain the same
       <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">
        declination
       </a>
       at the time of a conjunction in
       <a href="/wiki/Right_ascension" title="Right ascension">
        right ascension
       </a>
       (or the same ecliptic latitude at a conjunction in ecliptic longitude), the one that is closer to the Earth will pass in front of the other. In such a case, a
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       takes place. If one object moves into the shadow of another, the event is an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       . For example, if the Moon passes into the shadow of Earth and disappears from view, this event is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       . If the visible disk of the nearer object is considerably smaller than that of the farther object, the event is called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
        transit
       </a>
       . When Mercury passes in front of the Sun, it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">
        transit of Mercury
       </a>
       , and when Venus passes in front of the Sun, it is a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       . When the nearer object appears larger than the farther one, it will completely obscure its smaller companion; this is called an
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultation
       </a>
       . An example of an occultation is when the Moon passes between Earth and the Sun, causing the Sun to disappear either entirely or partially. This phenomenon is commonly known as a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       . Occultations in which the larger body is neither the Sun nor the Moon are very rare. More frequent, however, is an occultation of a planet by the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Several such events are visible every year from various places on Earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Position_of_the_observer">
        Position of the observer
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Position of the observer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A conjunction, as a phenomenon of perspective, is an event that involves two astronomical bodies seen by an observer on the Earth. Times and details depend only very slightly on the observer's location on the Earth's surface, with the differences being greatest for conjunctions involving the Moon because of its relative closeness, but even for the Moon the time of a conjunction never differs by more than a few hours.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Superior_and_inferior">
        Superior and inferior
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Superior and inferior">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       As seen from a planet that is
       <a href="/wiki/Inferior_and_superior_planets" title="Inferior and superior planets">
        superior
       </a>
       , if an
       <a href="/wiki/Inferior_and_superior_planets" title="Inferior and superior planets">
        inferior
       </a>
       planet is on the opposite side of the Sun, it is in
       <b>
        superior conjunction
       </b>
       with the Sun. An
       <b>
        inferior conjunction
       </b>
       occurs when the two planets lie in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Line_(mathematics)" title="Line (mathematics)">
        line
       </a>
       on the same side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . In an inferior conjunction, the superior planet is "in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_opposition" title="Astronomical opposition">
        opposition
       </a>
       " to the Sun as seen from the inferior planet.
      </p>
      <div style="float:left">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Positional_astronomy.svg">
        <img alt="Positional astronomy.svg" data-file-height="375" data-file-width="429" height="375" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Positional_astronomy.svg/429px-Positional_astronomy.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Positional_astronomy.svg/644px-Positional_astronomy.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Positional_astronomy.svg/858px-Positional_astronomy.svg.png 2x" width="429"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The terms "inferior conjunction" and "superior conjunction" are used in particular for the planets
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , which are
       <a href="/wiki/Inferior_and_superior_planets" title="Inferior and superior planets">
        inferior planets
       </a>
       as seen from the Earth. However, this definition can be applied to any pair of planets, as seen from the one farther from the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       A planet (or
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       ) is simply said to be in conjunction, when it is in conjunction with the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , as seen from the Earth. The
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is in conjunction with the Sun at
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        New Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quasiconjunction">
        Quasiconjunction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Quasiconjunction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a quasiconjunction, a planet in
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_retrograde_motion" title="Apparent retrograde motion">
        retrograde motion
       </a>
       — always either
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , from the point of view of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Earth" title="The Earth">
        the Earth
       </a>
       — will "drop back" in right ascension until it almost allows another planet to overtake it, but then the former planet will resume its forward motion and thereafter appear to draw away from it again. This will occur in the morning sky, before dawn. The reverse may happen in the evening sky after dusk, with Mercury or Venus entering retrograde motion just as it is about to overtake another planet (often Mercury
       <i>
        and
       </i>
       Venus are
       <i>
        both
       </i>
       of the planets involved, and when this situation arises they may remain in very close visual proximity for several days or even longer). The quasiconjunction is reckoned as occurring at the time the distance in right ascension between the two planets is smallest, even though, when declination is taken into account, they may appear closer together shortly before or after this.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1899">
        1899
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: 1899">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In early December 1899 the Sun and the naked-eye planets appeared to lie within a band 35 degrees wide along the ecliptic as seen from the Earth. As a consequence, over the period 1–4 December 1899, the Moon reached conjunction with, in order, Jupiter, Uranus, the Sun, Mercury, Mars, Saturn and Venus. Most of these conjunctions would not have been visible because of the glare of the Sun.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1962">
        1962
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 1962">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Over the period 4–6 February 1962, in a rare series of events, Mercury and Venus reached conjunction as observed from the Earth, followed by Venus and Jupiter, then by Mars and Saturn. Conjunctions took place between the Moon and, in turn, Mars, Saturn, the Sun, Mercury, Venus and Jupiter. Mercury also reached inferior conjunction with the Sun. The conjunction between the Moon and the Sun at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_Moon" title="New Moon">
        new Moon
       </a>
       produced a total solar eclipse visible in Indonesia and the Pacific Ocean,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       when these five naked-eye planets would have been visible in the vicinity of the Sun in the sky.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1987">
        1987
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 1987">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       separately reached conjunction with each other, and each separately with the Sun, within a 7-day period in August 1987 as seen from the Earth. The Moon also reached conjunction with each of these bodies on 24 August. However, none of these conjunctions would have been observable due to the glare of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meeus_astrontables1983_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-meeus_astrontables1983-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2000">
        2000
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 2000">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In May 2000, in a very rare event, several planets lay in the vicinity of the Sun in the sky as seen from the Earth, and a series of conjunctions took place. Jupiter, Mercury and Saturn each reached conjunction with the Sun in the period 8–10 May. These three planets in turn were in conjunction with each other and with Venus over a period of a few weeks. However, most of these conjunctions would not have been visible from the Earth because of the glare from the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meeus_astrontables1983_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-meeus_astrontables1983-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       NASA referred to May 5 as the date of the conjunction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2002">
        2002
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </a>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Venus, Mars and Saturn appeared close together in the evening sky in early May 2002, with a conjunction of Mars and Saturn occurring on 4 May. This was followed by a conjunction of Venus and Saturn on 7 May, and another of Venus and Mars on 10 May when their angular separation was only 18 arcminutes. A series of conjunctions between the Moon and, in order, Saturn, Mars and Venus took place on 14 May, although it would not have been possible to observe all these in darkness from any single location on the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-meeus_astrontables1983_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-meeus_astrontables1983-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="2007">
        2007
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: 2007">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A conjunction of the Moon and Mars took place on 24 December 2007, very close to the time of the full Moon and at the time when Mars was at opposition to the Sun. Mars and the full Moon would have appeared close together in the sky worldwide, with an occultation of Mars occurring for observers in some far northern locations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A similar conjunction took place on 21 May 2016.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2016">
        2016
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: 2016">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On the morning of January 9th,
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       came together in a conjunction
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On August 27,
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       were in conjunction, followed by a conjunction of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , meaning that the three planets were very close together in the evening sky.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2017">
        2017
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Conjunction_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: 2017">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On the morning of November 13th,
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       were in conjunction, meaning that they appeared close together in the morning sky.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="2018">
        2018
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

         ]

      </h3>

      <p>
       On the early hours of January 7th,
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       were in conjunction. The pair was only 0.25 degrees apart in the sky at its closest.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
