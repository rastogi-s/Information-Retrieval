   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Diameter
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

         <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">

         </a>
         (C) in black, diameter (D) in cyan, radius (R) in red, and centre or origin (O) in magenta.

       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Geometry" title="Geometry">
        geometry
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        diameter
       </b>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Circle" title="Circle">
        circle
       </a>
       is any straight
       <a href="/wiki/Line_segment" title="Line segment">
        line segment
       </a>
       that passes through the center of the circle and whose endpoints lie on the circle. It can also be defined as the longest
       <a href="/wiki/Chord_(geometry)" title="Chord (geometry)">
        chord
       </a>
       of the circle. Both definitions are also valid for the diameter of a
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere" title="Sphere">
        sphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In more modern usage, the length of a diameter is also called the diameter. In this sense one speaks of
       <i>
        the
       </i>
       diameter rather than
       <i>
        a
       </i>
       diameter (which refers to the line itself), because all diameters of a circle or sphere have the same length, this being twice the
       <a href="/wiki/Radius" title="Radius">
        radius
       </a>
       <b>
        r
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle d=2r\quad \Rightarrow \quad r={\frac {d}{2}}.}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
              <mi>
               d
              </mi>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mn>
               2
              </mn>
              <mi>
               r
              </mi>
              <mspace width="1em">
              </mspace>
              <mo stretchy="false">
               ⇒
               <!-- ⇒ -->
              </mo>
              <mspace width="1em">
              </mspace>
              <mi>
               r
              </mi>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mfrac>
                <mi>
                 d
                </mi>
                <mn>
                 2
                </mn>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
              <mo>
               .
              </mo>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle d=2r\quad \Rightarrow \quad r={\frac {d}{2}}.}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="d = 2r \quad \Rightarrow \quad r = \frac{d}{2}." aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5caf206ac522afad4b92d8278fc56dec5a8a60e5" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:21.631ex; height:5.343ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       For a
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">
        convex shape
       </a>
       in the plane, the diameter is defined to be the largest distance that can be formed between two opposite
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Parallel_lines" title="Parallel lines">
        parallel lines
       </a>
       tangent to its boundary, and the
       <i>
        width
       </i>
       is often defined to be the smallest such distance. Both quantities can be calculated efficiently using
       <a href="/wiki/Rotating_calipers" title="Rotating calipers">
        rotating calipers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For a
       <a href="/wiki/Curve_of_constant_width" title="Curve of constant width">
        curve of constant width
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Reuleaux_triangle" title="Reuleaux triangle">
        Reuleaux triangle
       </a>
       , the width and diameter are the same because all such pairs of parallel tangent lines have the same distance.
      </p>
      <p>
       <span id="Ellipse">
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       For an
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipse" title="Ellipse">
        ellipse
       </a>
       , the standard terminology is different. A diameter of an ellipse is any
       <a href="/wiki/Chord_(geometry)" title="Chord (geometry)">
        chord
       </a>
       passing through the center of the ellipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example,
       <a href="/wiki/Conjugate_diameters" title="Conjugate diameters">
        conjugate diameters
       </a>
       have the property that a tangent line to the ellipse at the endpoint of one of them is parallel to the other one. The longest diameter is called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_axis" title="Major axis">
        major axis
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The word "diameter" is derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Greek
       </a>
       διάμετρος (
       <i>
        diametros
       </i>
       ), "diameter of a circle", from διά (
       <i>
        dia
       </i>
       ), "across, through" and μέτρον (
       <i>
        metron
       </i>
       ), "measure".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is often abbreviated
       <b>
        DIA
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        dia
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        d
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        ⌀
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Generalizations">
        Generalizations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Diameter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Generalizations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The definitions given above are only valid for circles, spheres and convex shapes. However, they are special cases of a more general definition that is valid for any kind of
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       -dimensional convex or non-convex object, such as a
       <a href="/wiki/Hypercube" title="Hypercube">
        hypercube
       </a>
       or a set of scattered points. The
       <b>
        diameter
       </b>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Subset" title="Subset">
        subset
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_space" title="Metric space">
        metric space
       </a>
       is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supremum" title="Supremum">
        least upper bound
       </a>
       of the set of all distances between pairs of points in the subset. So, if
       <i>
        A
       </i>
       is the subset, the diameter is
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supremum" title="Supremum">
         sup
        </a>
        { d(
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        ) |
        <i>
         x
        </i>
        ,
        <i>
         y
        </i>
        ∈
        <i>
         A
        </i>
        } .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       If the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Distance_function" title="Distance function">
        distance function
       </a>
       d is viewed here as having
       <a href="/wiki/Codomain" title="Codomain">
        codomain
       </a>
       <b>
        R
       </b>
       (the set of all
       <a href="/wiki/Real_number" title="Real number">
        real numbers
       </a>
       ), this implies that the diameter of the
       <a href="/wiki/Empty_set" title="Empty set">
        empty set
       </a>
       (the case
       <span class="nowrap">
        <i>
         A
        </i>
        = ∅
       </span>
       ) equals −∞ (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Negative_infinity" title="Negative infinity">
        negative infinity
       </a>
       ). Some authors prefer to treat the empty set as a special case, assigning it a diameter equal to 0,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which corresponds to taking the codomain of d to be the set of nonnegative reals.
      </p>
      <p>
       For any solid object or set of scattered points in n-dimensional
       <a href="/wiki/Euclidean_space" title="Euclidean space">
        Euclidean space
       </a>
       , the diameter of the object or set is the same as the diameter of its
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_hull" title="Convex hull">
        convex hull
       </a>
       . In medical
       <a href="/wiki/Idiom#Parlance" title="Idiom">
        parlance
       </a>
       concerning a lesion or in geology concerning a rock, the diameter of an object is the supremum of the set of all distances between pairs of points in the object.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_geometry" title="Differential geometry">
        differential geometry
       </a>
       , the diameter is an important global
       <a href="/wiki/Riemannian_geometry" title="Riemannian geometry">
        Riemannian
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics)" title="Invariant (mathematics)">
        invariant
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In plane geometry, a diameter of a
       <a href="/wiki/Conic_section" title="Conic section">
        conic section
       </a>
       is typically defined as any chord which passes through the conic's centre; such diameters are not necessarily of uniform length, except in the case of the circle, which has
       <a href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics)" title="Eccentricity (mathematics)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       = 0.
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Unicode" title="Unicode">

        </span>
       </span>

       </span>

        <span class="smallcaps" style="font-variant: small-caps; font-size: smaller;">
         Diameter sign
        </span>
       </a>
       (HTML
       <code>
        &amp;#8960;
       </code>
       ). On an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apple,_Inc." title="Apple, Inc.">
        Apple
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Macintosh" title="Macintosh">
        Macintosh
       </a>
       , the diameter symbol can be entered via the character palette (this is opened by pressing
       <kbd class="keyboard-key nowrap" style="border: 1px solid #aaa; -moz-border-radius: 0.2em; -webkit-border-radius: 0.2em; border-radius: 0.2em; -moz-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); -webkit-box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); box-shadow: 0.1em 0.1em 0.2em rgba(0,0,0,0.1); background-color: #f9f9f9; background-image: -moz-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -o-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: -webkit-linear-gradient(top, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); background-image: linear-gradient(to bottom, #eee, #f9f9f9, #eee); padding: 0.1em 0.3em; font-family: inherit; font-size: 0.85em;">

       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="monospaced" style="font-family: monospace, monospace;">

        </span>

       <span style="font-size:125%">
        ø

        ·
       </b>

        &amp;oslash;

      <p>
       In

      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/LaTeX" title="LaTeX">

       </a>

      </p>
      <p>
       The diameter symbol ⌀ is distinct from the
       <a href="/wiki/Empty_set" title="Empty set">

       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phi_(letter)" title="Phi (letter)">
        phi
       </a>
       <i>
        Φ
       </i>
       , and from the Nordic vowel
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%98" title="Ø">
        Ø
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       See also
       <a href="/wiki/Slashed_zero" title="Slashed zero">
        slashed zero
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/German_(language)" title="German (language)">
        German
       </a>

       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durchmesserzeichen" title="de:Durchmesserzeichen">

        </a>
       </i>
       ) is also used as an
       <a href="/wiki/Average" title="Average">
        average
       </a>
       symbol (
       <i>
        Durchschnittszeichen
       </i>
       ).
      </p>
