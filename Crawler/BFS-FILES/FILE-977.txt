       <b>
        Hinode
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Japanese_language" title="Japanese language">
        Japanese
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="ja" xml:lang="ja">
        ひので
       </span>
       ,
       <small>
        IPA:
       </small>
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/Japanese" title="Help:IPA/Japanese">
         [çinode]
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <b>
        Sunrise
       </b>
       ), formerly
       <b>
        Solar-B
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Solar
       </a>
       mission with
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       collaboration. It is the follow-up to the
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       (Solar-A) mission and it was launched on the final flight of the
       <a href="/wiki/M-V" title="M-V">
        M-V-7 rocket
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Uchinoura_Space_Center" title="Uchinoura Space Center">
        Uchinoura Space Center
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japan
       </a>
       on 22 September 2006 at 21:36
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        UTC
       </a>
       (23 September, 06:36
       <a href="/wiki/Japan_Standard_Time" title="Japan Standard Time">
        JST
       </a>
       ). Initial orbit was perigee height 280 km, apogee height 686 km, inclination 98.3 degrees. Then the satellite maneuvered to the quasi-circular
       <a href="/wiki/Sun-synchronous_orbit" title="Sun-synchronous orbit">
        sun-synchronous orbit
       </a>
       over the day/night
       <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
        terminator
       </a>
       , which allows near-continuous observation of the Sun. On 28 October 2006, the probe's instruments captured their first images.
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norwegian

        Svalsat

       station, operated by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kongsberg_Gruppen" title="Kongsberg Gruppen">
         Kongsberg
        </a>
       </i>
       a few kilometres west of
       <a href="/wiki/Longyearbyen" title="Longyearbyen">
        Longyearbyen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Svalbard" title="Svalbard">
        Svalbard
       </a>
       . From there, data was transmitted by
       <a href="/wiki/Telenor" title="Telenor">
        Telenor
       </a>
       through a fibre-optic
       <a href="/wiki/Svalbard_Undersea_Cable_System" title="Svalbard Undersea Cable System">
        network
       </a>
       to mainland Norway at
       <a href="/wiki/Harstad" title="Harstad">
        Harstad
       </a>
       , and on to data users in North America, Europe and Japan.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">
        Mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hinode&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Hinode was planned as a three-year mission to explore the magnetic fields of the Sun. It consists of a coordinated set of optical,
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet" title="Extreme ultraviolet">
        extreme ultraviolet
       </a>
       (EUV), and
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        x-ray
       </a>
       instruments to investigate the interaction between the Sun's magnetic field and its corona. The result will be an improved understanding of the mechanisms that power the solar atmosphere and drive solar eruptions. The EUV imaging spectrometer ( EIS ) was built by a consortium led by the Mullard Space Science Laboratory (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MSSL" title="MSSL">
        MSSL
       </a>
       ) in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">
        UK
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       , the space agency of the United States, was involved with three science instrument components: the Focal Plane Package (FPP), the X-Ray Telescope (XRT), and the Extreme Ultraviolet Imaging Spectrometer (EIS) and shares operations support for science planning and instrument command generation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">
        Instruments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

      <p>
       Hinode carries three main instruments to study the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        SOT (Solar Optical Telescope)
       </dt>
       <dd>
        A 0.5 meter Gregorian
        <a href="/wiki/Optical_telescope" title="Optical telescope">
         optical telescope
        </a>
        with an
        <a href="/wiki/Angular_resolution" title="Angular resolution">
         angular resolution
        </a>
        of about 0.2
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
         arcsecond
        </a>
        over the
        <a href="/wiki/Field_of_view" title="Field of view">
         field of view
        </a>
        of about 400 x 400 arcsec. At the SOT
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Focal_plane" title="Focal plane">
         focal plane
        </a>
        , the Focal Plane Package (FPP) built by the
        <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Solar_and_Astrophysics_Laboratory" title="Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory">
         Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">
         Palo Alto, California
        </a>
        consists of three optical instruments:
        <ul>
         <li>
          the Broadband Filter Imager (BFI) which produces images of the solar photosphere and chromosphere in six wide-band interference filters;
         </li>
         <li>
          the Narrowband Filter Imager (NFI) which is a tunable Lyot-type birefringent filter capable of producing magnetogram and dopplergram images of the solar surface;
         </li>
         <li>
          and the Spectropolarimeter (SP) which produces the most sensitive
          <a href="/wiki/Vector_magnetograph" title="Vector magnetograph">
           vector magnetograph
          </a>
          maps of the photosphere to date.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        The FPP also includes a Correlation Tracker (CT) which locks onto
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_granulation" title="Solar granulation">
         solar granulation
        </a>
        to stabilize the SOT images to a fraction of an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
         arcsecond
        </a>
        . The spatial resolution of the SOT is a factor of 5 improvement over previous space-based solar telescopes (e.g., the MDI instrument on the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SOHO
        </a>
        ).
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        XRT (X-ray Telescope)
       </dt>
       <dd>
        A modified Wolter I telescope design that uses grazing incidence optics to image the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
         solar corona
        </a>
        's hottest components (0.5 to 10 Million K) with an angular resolution consistent with 1 arcsec pixels at the CCD. The telescope has an imaging field of view of 34 arcminutes. It is capable of capturing an image of the full sun when pointed at the center of the solar disk. The telescope was designed and built by
        <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
         Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
        </a>
        (SAO), which, with the Harvard College Observatory (HCO) form the Harvard-Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics (CfA). The camera was developed by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NAOJ" title="NAOJ">
         NAOJ
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">
         JAXA
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dt>
        EIS (Extreme-Ultraviolet Imaging Spectrometer)
       </dt>
       <dd>
        A normal incidence extreme ultraviolet (EUV)
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
         spectrometer
        </a>
        that obtains spatially resolved spectra in two wavelength bands: 17.0-21.2 and 24.6-29.2 nm.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Spatial resolution is around 2 arcsec, and the field of view is up to 560 x 512 arcsec
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        . The emission lines in the EIS wavelength bands are emitted at temperatures ranging from 50,000 K to 20 million K. EIS is used to identify the physical processes involved in heating the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
         solar corona
        </a>
        .
       </dd>
      </dl>
