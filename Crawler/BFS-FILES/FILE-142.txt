   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Predictive power
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         This article deals with predictive power in the context of the philosophy of science. For a statistical measure of predictive power, see
         <a href="/wiki/Mean_squared_prediction_error" title="Mean squared prediction error">
          mean squared prediction error
         </a>
         .
        </i>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Einstein_theory_triumphs.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="578" data-file-width="307" height="282" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Einstein_theory_triumphs.png/150px-Einstein_theory_triumphs.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Einstein_theory_triumphs.png/225px-Einstein_theory_triumphs.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Einstein_theory_triumphs.png/300px-Einstein_theory_triumphs.png 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Einstein_theory_triumphs.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
           The New York Times
          </a>
         </i>
         of November 10, 1919, reported on Einstein's confirmed prediction of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
          gravitation
         </a>
         on space, called the
         <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_lens" title="Gravitational lens">
          gravitational lens
         </a>
         effect.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The concept of
       <b>
        predictive power
       </b>
       differs from
       <a href="/wiki/Explanatory_power" title="Explanatory power">
        explanatory
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Descriptive_power&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Descriptive power (page does not exist)">
        descriptive power
       </a>
       (where phenomena that are already known are retrospectively explained or described by a given theory) in that it allows a prospective test of theoretical understanding.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Predictive_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A classic example of the predictive power of a theory is the
       <a href="/wiki/Discovery_of_Neptune" title="Discovery of Neptune">
        discovery of Neptune
       </a>
       as a result of predictions made by mathematicians
       <a href="/wiki/John_Couch_Adams" title="John Couch Adams">
        John Couch Adams
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier" title="Urbain Le Verrier">
        Urbain Le Verrier
       </a>
       , based on Newton's theory of gravity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other examples of predictive power of theories or models include
       <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev" title="Dmitri Mendeleev">
        Dmitri Mendeleev
       </a>
       's use of his
       <a href="/wiki/Periodic_table" title="Periodic table">
        periodic table
       </a>
       to predict previously undiscovered
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical elements
       </a>
       and their properties (though largely correct, he misjudged the relative atomic masses of
       <a href="/wiki/Tellurium" title="Tellurium">
        tellurium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Iodine" title="Iodine">
        iodine
       </a>
       ), and
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">
        Charles Darwin
       </a>
       's use of his knowledge of
       <a href="/wiki/Evolution" title="Evolution">
        evolution
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_selection" title="Natural selection">
        natural selection
       </a>
       to predict that because there existed a plant (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Angraecum" title="Angraecum">
         Angraecum
        </a>
       </i>
       ) with a long spur in its flowers, a complementary animal with a 30 cm
       <a href="/wiki/Proboscis" title="Proboscis">
        proboscis
       </a>
       must also exist to feed on and pollinate it (twenty years after his death
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Xanthopan_morganii" title="Xanthopan morganii">
        Xanthopan morganii
       </a>
       , a form of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hawk_moth" title="Hawk moth">
        hawk moth
       </a>
       , was found which did just that).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another example of predictive power is the prediction of
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_theory_of_relativity" title="General theory of relativity">
        general theory of relativity
       </a>
       that the path of light would bend in the presence of a strong gravitational field. This was
       <a href="/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity#Deflection_of_light_by_the_Sun" title="Tests of general relativity">
        experimentally verified
       </a>
       by an expedition to
       <a href="/wiki/Sobral,_Cear%C3%A1" title="Sobral, Ceará">
        Sobral
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic
       </a>
       island of
       <a href="/wiki/Pr%C3%ADncipe" title="Príncipe">
        Príncipe
       </a>
       to measure star positions during the solar eclipse of May 29, 1919, when observations made by the astrophysicist
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">
        Arthur Eddington
       </a>
       seemed to confirm Einstein's predictions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the measurements have been criticized by some as utilizing flawed methodology,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       modern reanalysis of the data
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggests that Eddington's analysis of the data was accurate. Later, more precise measurements taken by
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">
        radio
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Interferometry" title="Interferometry">
        interferometry
       </a>
       confirmed the predictions to a high degree of accuracy.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Applications">
        Applications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Predictive_power&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Applications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The predictive power of a theory is closely related to applications.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (April 2013)">
           according to whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       General relativity not only predicts the bending of light, but also predicts several other phenomena. Recently, the calculation of
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_time" title="Proper time">
        proper time
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       has been a successfully-measured prediction, now incorporated into the method used to calculate positions via
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPS" title="GPS">
        GPS
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       If a theory has no predictive power, it cannot be used for applications.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
