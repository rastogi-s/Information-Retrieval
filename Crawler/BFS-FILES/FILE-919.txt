      <p>
       <b>
        Cambridge University Press
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        CUP
       </b>
       ) is the publishing business of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        University of Cambridge
       </a>
       . Granted
       <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">
        letters patent
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">
        King Henry VIII
       </a>
       in 1534, it is the world's oldest publishing house and the second-largest university press in the world (after
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">
        Oxford University Press
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It also holds letters patent as the
       <a href="/wiki/Queen%27s_Printer" title="Queen's Printer">
        Queen's Printer
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The press's mission is "To further the University's mission by disseminating knowledge in the pursuit of education, learning and research at the highest international levels of excellence."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cambridge University Press is a department of the University of Cambridge and is both an academic and educational
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Publisher" title="Publisher">
        publisher
       </a>
       . With a global sales presence, publishing hubs, and offices in more than 40
       <a href="/wiki/Country" title="Country">
        countries
       </a>
       , it publishes over 50,000 titles by authors from over 100 countries. Its publishing includes
       <a href="/wiki/Academic_journal" title="Academic journal">
        academic journals
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Monograph" title="Monograph">
        monographs
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Reference_work" title="Reference work">
        reference works
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Textbook" title="Textbook">
        textbooks
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/English_language" title="English language">
        English-language
       </a>
       teaching and learning publications. Cambridge University Press is a charitable enterprise that transfers part of its annual surplus back to the university.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridge_University_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Cambridge University Press is both the oldest publishing house in the world and the oldest university press. It originated from
       <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">
        letters patent
       </a>
       granted to the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        University of Cambridge
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">
        Henry VIII
       </a>
       in 1534, and has been producing books continuously since the first University Press book was printed. Cambridge is one of the two
       <a href="/wiki/Privileged_presses" title="Privileged presses">
        privileged presses
       </a>
       (the other being
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_University_Press" title="Oxford University Press">
        Oxford University Press
       </a>
       ). Authors published by Cambridge have included
       <a href="/wiki/John_Milton" title="John Milton">
        John Milton
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Harvey" title="William Harvey">
        William Harvey
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Bertrand_Russell" title="Bertrand Russell">
        Bertrand Russell
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Hawking" title="Stephen Hawking">
        Stephen Hawking
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       University
       <a href="/wiki/Printing" title="Printing">
        printing
       </a>
       began in Cambridge when the first practising University Printer,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Thomas_Thomas_(printer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Thomas Thomas (printer) (page does not exist)">
        Thomas Thomas
       </a>
       , set up a printing house on the site of what became the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Senate_House_(University_of_Cambridge)" title="Senate House (University of Cambridge)">
        Senate House
       </a>
       lawn – a few yards from where the press's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bookshop" title="Bookshop">
        bookshop
       </a>
       now stands. In those days, the
       <a href="/wiki/Worshipful_Company_of_Stationers_and_Newspaper_Makers" title="Worshipful Company of Stationers and Newspaper Makers">
        Stationers' Company
       </a>
       in London jealously guarded its monopoly of printing, which partly explains the delay between the date of the university's letters patent and the printing of the first book.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1591, Thomas's successor, John Legate, printed the first Cambridge Bible, an
       <a href="/wiki/Octavo" title="Octavo">
        octavo
       </a>
       edition of the popular
       <a href="/wiki/Geneva_Bible" title="Geneva Bible">
        Geneva Bible
       </a>
       . The London Stationers objected strenuously, claiming that they had the monopoly on Bible printing. The university's response was to point out the provision in its charter to print "all manner of books". Thus began the press's tradition of publishing the Bible, a tradition that has endured for over four centuries, beginning with the Geneva Bible, and continuing with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Authorized_Version" title="Authorized Version">
        Authorized Version
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Revised_Version" title="Revised Version">
        Revised Version
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/New_English_Bible" title="New English Bible">
        New English Bible
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Revised_English_Bible" title="Revised English Bible">
        Revised English Bible
       </a>
       . The restrictions and compromises forced upon Cambridge by the dispute with the London Stationers did not really come to an end until the scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Bentley" title="Richard Bentley">
        Richard Bentley
       </a>
       was given the power to set up a 'new-style press' in 1696. In July 1697 the
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Seymour,_6th_Duke_of_Somerset" title="Charles Seymour, 6th Duke of Somerset">
        Duke of Somerset
       </a>
       made a loan of £200 to the university "towards the printing house and presse" and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Halman" title="James Halman">
        James Halman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Registrary" title="Registrary">
        Registrary
       </a>
       of the University, lent £100 for the same purpose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was in Bentley's time, in 1698, that a body of senior scholars ('the Curators', known from 1733 as 'the Syndics') was appointed to be responsible to the university for the press's affairs. The Press Syndicate's publishing committee still meets regularly (eighteen times a year), and its role still includes the review and approval of the press's planned output.
       <a href="/wiki/John_Baskerville" title="John Baskerville">
        John Baskerville
       </a>
       became University Printer in the mid-eighteenth century. Baskerville's concern was the production of the finest possible books using his own type-design and printing techniques.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:CUPPress2.jpg">
         <img alt="University Printing House" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="720" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a6/CUPPress2.jpg/220px-CUPPress2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a6/CUPPress2.jpg/330px-CUPPress2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/a/a6/CUPPress2.jpg/440px-CUPPress2.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:CUPPress2.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The University Printing House, on the main site of the press
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Baskerville wrote, "The importance of the work demands all my attention; not only for my own (eternal) reputation; but (I hope) also to convince the world, that the University in the honour done me has not entirely misplaced their favours." Caxton would have found nothing to surprise him if he had walked into the press's printing house in the eighteenth century: all the type was still being set by hand; wooden presses, capable of producing only 1,000 sheets a day at best, were still in use; and books were still being individually bound by hand. A technological breakthrough was badly needed, and it came when Lord Stanhope perfected the making of stereotype plates. This involved making a mould of the whole surface of a page of type and then casting plates from that mould. The press was the first to use this technique, and in 1805 produced the technically successful and much-reprinted Cambridge Stereotype Bible.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1170" data-file-width="1694" height="138" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8b/Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg/200px-Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8b/Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg/300px-Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8b/Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg/400px-Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cambridge_University_Press_Letters_Patent.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a href="/wiki/Letters_patent" title="Letters patent">
          letters patent
         </a>
         of Cambridge University Press by
         <a href="/wiki/Henry_VIII_of_England" title="Henry VIII of England">
          Henry VIII
         </a>
         allow the press to print "all manner of books". The fine initial with the king's portrait inside it and the large first line of script are still discernible.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       By the 1850s the press was using steam-powered machine presses, employing two to three hundred people, and occupying several buildings in the Silver Street and Mill Lane area, including the one that the press still occupies, the Pitt Building (1833), which was built specifically for the press and in honour of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Pitt_the_Younger" title="William Pitt the Younger">
        William Pitt the Younger
       </a>
       . Under the stewardship of C. J. Clay, who was University Printer from 1854 to 1882, the press increased the size and scale of its academic and educational publishing operation. An important factor in this increase was the inauguration of its list of schoolbooks (including what came to be known as the 'Pitt Press Series'). During Clay's administration, the press also undertook a sizeable co-publishing venture with Oxford: the Revised Version of the Bible, which was begun in 1870 and completed in 1885. It was in this period as well that the Syndics of the press turned down what later became the
       <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
        Oxford English Dictionary
       </a>
       —a proposal for which was brought to Cambridge by
       <a href="/wiki/James_Murray_(lexicographer)" title="James Murray (lexicographer)">
        James Murray (lexicographer)
       </a>
       before he turned to Oxford.
      </p>
      <p>
       The appointment of R. T. Wright as Secretary of the Press Syndicate in 1892 marked the beginning of the press's development as a modern publishing business with a clearly defined editorial policy and administrative structure. It was Wright (with two great historians,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lord_Acton" title="Lord Acton">
        Lord Acton
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F._W._Maitland" title="F. W. Maitland">
        F. W. Maitland
       </a>
       ) who devised the plan for one of the most distinctive Cambridge contributions to publishing—the Cambridge Histories.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_Modern_History" title="Cambridge Modern History">
         Cambridge Modern History
        </a>
       </i>
       was published between 1902 and 1912. Nine years later the press issued the first volumes of the freshly edited complete works of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       , a project of nearly equal scope that was not finished until 1966. The press's list in science and mathematics began to thrive, with men of the stature of
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Albert Einstein
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford" title="Ernest Rutherford">
        Ernest Rutherford
       </a>
       subsequently becoming Press authors. The press's impressive contribution to journal publishing began in 1893, and today it publishes over 300 journals.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1992 the press opened its own bookshop at 1
       <a href="/wiki/Trinity_Street,_Cambridge" title="Trinity Street, Cambridge">
        Trinity Street
       </a>
       , in the centre of Cambridge. Books have been sold continuously on this site since at least 1581, perhaps even as early as 1505, making it the oldest known bookshop site in Britain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2008 the shop expanded into 27 Market Hill where their specialist Education and English Language Teaching shop opened the following year.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2012 the press decided to end the tradition of printing after 428 years and now uses third parties to provide all of its print publications.
      </p>

      <p>
       The Press has, since 1698, been governed by the Press 'Syndics' (originally known as the 'Curators'),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-historyofcup_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-historyofcup-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       made up of 18 senior members of the University of Cambridge who represent a wide variety of subjects and areas of expertise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-statute_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-statute-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Syndicate has delegated its powers to a Press &amp; Assessment Board, which has an Audit Committee, Remuneration Committee and Nominations Committee (all shared with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_Assessment" title="Cambridge Assessment">
        Cambridge Assessment
       </a>
       ); and to an Academic Publishing Committee and an English Language Teaching &amp; Education Publishing Committee. The Press &amp; Assessment Board oversees the Press's financial, strategic and operational affairs, while the two Publishing Committees provide quality assurance and formal approval of the publishing strategy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Chair of the Syndicate is currently Professor
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Toope" title="Stephen Toope">
        Stephen Toope
       </a>
       (Vice-Chancellor of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Cambridge" title="University of Cambridge">
        University of Cambridge
       </a>
       ). The operational responsibility of the Press is delegated by the Syndics to the Press's Chief Executive, Peter Phillips, and the Press Board.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alms_for_Jihad">
        Alms for Jihad
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cambridge_University_Press&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Alms for Jihad">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Alms_for_Jihad" title="Alms for Jihad">
        Alms for Jihad
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 2007, controversy arose over CUP's decision to destroy all remaining copies of its 2006 book,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Alms_for_Jihad" title="Alms for Jihad">
         Alms for Jihad: Charity and Terrorism in the Islamic World
        </a>
       </i>
       , by Burr and Collins, as part of the settlement of a lawsuit brought by Saudi billionaire
       <a href="/wiki/Khalid_bin_Mahfouz" title="Khalid bin Mahfouz">
        Khalid bin Mahfouz
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Within hours,
       <i>
        Alms for Jihad
       </i>
       became one of the 100 most sought after titles on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">
        Amazon.com
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/EBay" title="EBay">
        eBay
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       . CUP sent a letter to libraries asking them to remove copies from circulation. CUP subsequently sent out copies of an "errata" sheet for the book.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/American_Library_Association" title="American Library Association">
        American Library Association
       </a>
       issued a recommendation to libraries still holding
       <i>
        Alms for Jihad
       </i>
       : "Given the intense interest in the book, and the desire of readers to learn about the controversy first hand, we recommend that U.S. libraries keep the book available for their users." The publisher's decision did not have the support of the book's authors and was criticized by some who claimed it was incompatible with freedom of speech and with freedom of the press and that it indicated that English libel laws were excessively strict.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="New York Times Book Review">
         New York Times Book Review
        </a>
       </i>
       (7 October 2007),
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_House_of_Representatives" title="United States House of Representatives">
        United States Congressman
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Wolf_(politician)" title="Frank Wolf (politician)">
        Frank R. Wolf
       </a>
       described Cambridge's settlement as "basically a book burning".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       CUP pointed out that, at that time, it had already sold most of its copies of the book.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cambridge defended its actions, saying it had acted responsibly and that it is a global publisher with a duty to observe the laws of many different countries.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Mao_Zedong" title="Mao Zedong">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">

        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:ASA_conference_2008_-_13.JPG">

      <p>
       The press has been recognized on several occasions for its commitment to community involvement and social responsibility, and it has stated that public engagement is an important part of the press's role, by undertaking educational projects and fundraising.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The press partnered with Bookshare in 2010 to make their books accessible to people with qualified
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Print_disabilities" title="Print disabilities">
        print disabilities
       </a>
       . Under the terms of the digital rights licence agreement, the press delivers academic and scholarly books from all of its regional publishing centres on the world to Bookshare for conversion into accessible formats. People with qualified print disabilities around the world can download the books for a nominal Bookshare membership fee and read them using a computer or other assistive technology, with voice generated by text-to-speech technology, as well as options for digital Braille.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Knowledge_Unlatched" title="Knowledge Unlatched">

       </a>
       pilot, a global library consortium approach to funding
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Open_access_book" title="Open access book">
        open access books
       </a>
       .
