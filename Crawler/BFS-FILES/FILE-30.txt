      <p>
       <b>
        Twilight
       </b>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Diffuse_sky_radiation" title="Diffuse sky radiation">
        illumination
       </a>
       of the lower
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       when the Sun itself is not directly visible because it is below the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       . Twilight is produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Sunlight" title="Sunlight">
        sunlight
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_scattering" title="Light scattering">
        scattering
       </a>
       in the upper atmosphere, illuminating the lower atmosphere so that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_surface" title="Earth's surface">
        Earth's surface
       </a>
       is neither completely lit nor completely dark. The word
       <i>
        twilight
       </i>
       is also used to denote the periods of time when this illumination occurs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USNO-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The farther the Sun is below the horizon, the dimmer the twilight (other things such as atmospheric conditions being equal). When the Sun reaches 18 degrees below the horizon, the twilight's brightness is nearly zero, and evening twilight becomes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nighttime" title="Nighttime">
        nighttime
       </a>
       . When the Sun again reaches 18° below the horizon,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nighttime" title="Nighttime">
        nighttime
       </a>
       becomes morning twilight. Owing to its distinctive quality, primarily the absence of shadows and the appearance of objects
       <a href="/wiki/Silhouette" title="Silhouette">
        silhouetted
       </a>
       against the bright sky, twilight has long been popular with photographers, who refer to it as 'sweet light', and painters, who refer to it as the
       <a href="/wiki/Blue_hour" title="Blue hour">
        blue hour
       </a>
       , after the
       <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">
        French
       </a>
       expression,
       <i>
        l'heure bleue
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Twilight should not be confused with
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        auroras
       </a>
       , which can have a similar appearance in the night sky at high latitudes. By analogy with evening twilight, the word
       <i>
        twilight
       </i>
       is also sometimes used
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metaphorically" title="Metaphorically">
        metaphorically
       </a>
       , to imply that something is losing strength and approaching its end. For example, very old people may be said to be "in the twilight of their lives". The
       <a href="/wiki/Collateral_adjective" title="Collateral adjective">
        collateral adjective
       </a>
       for
       <i>
        twilight
       </i>
       is
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Crepuscular" title="Crepuscular">
         crepuscular
        </a>
       </i>
       which is sometimes applied to
       <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">
        insects
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fish" title="Fish">
        fish
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mammal" title="Mammal">
        mammals
       </a>
       that are most active during that time.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions_by_geometry">
        Definitions by geometry
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions by geometry">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Twilight is defined according to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_elevation_angle" title="Solar elevation angle">
        solar elevation angle
       </a>
       <i>
        θ
        <sub>
         s
        </sub>
       </i>
       , which is the position of the geometric center of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        sun
       </a>
       relative to the horizon. There are three established and widely accepted
       <i>
        subcategories
       </i>
       of twilight: civil twilight (nearest the horizon), nautical twilight, and astronomical twilight (farthest from the horizon).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-USNO-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       There are three established and widely accepted
       <i>
        subcategories
       </i>
       of twilight: civil twilight (brightest), nautical twilight, and astronomical twilight (darkest).

        <a href="#cite_note-USNO-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Civil_twilight">
        Civil twilight
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Civil twilight">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Morning civil twilight begins when the geometric center of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        sun
       </a>
       is 6° below the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and ends at
       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">
        sunrise
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Evening civil twilight begins at
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sunset
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and ends when the geometric center of the sun reaches 6° below the horizon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the United States' military, the
       <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">
        initialisms
       </a>
       <b>
        BMCT
       </b>
       (begin morning civil twilight,
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       civil dawn) and
       <b>
        EECT
       </b>
       (end evening civil twilight,
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       civil dusk) are used to refer to the start of morning civil twilight and the end of evening civil twilight, respectively. Civil dawn is preceded by morning nautical twilight and civil dusk is followed by evening nautical twilight.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under clear weather conditions, civil twilight approximates the limit at which solar illumination suffices for the human eye to clearly distinguish terrestrial objects. Enough illumination renders artificial sources unnecessary for most outdoor activities. At civil dawn and at civil dusk sunlight clearly defines the horizon while the brightest stars and planets can appear. As observed from the Earth (see
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       ), sky-gazers know
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , the brightest planet, as the "morning star" or "evening star” because they can see it during civil twilight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lawmakers have enshrined the concept of civil twilight. Such statutes typically use a fixed period after sunset or before sunrise (most commonly 20–30 minutes), rather than how many degrees the sun is below the horizon. Examples include the following periods: when drivers of automobiles must turn on their headlights (called
       <a href="/wiki/Lighting-up_time" title="Lighting-up time">
        lighting-up time
       </a>
       in the UK); when hunting is restricted; when the crime of
       <a href="/wiki/Burglary" title="Burglary">
        burglary
       </a>
       is to be treated as nighttime burglary, which carries stiffer penalties in some jurisdictions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The period may affect when pilots require a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Night_rating" title="Night rating">
        night rating
       </a>
       to fly aircraft. In the US, civil twilight for aviation is defined in Part 1.1 of the
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Regulations" title="Federal Aviation Regulations">
        Federal Aviation Regulations
       </a>
       (FARs)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as the time listed in the American Air Almanac.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_twilight">
        Nautical twilight
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Nautical twilight">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
        Definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Morning nautical twilight begins (nautical dawn) when the geometric center of the sun is 12° below the horizon in the morning and ends when the geometric center of the sun is 6° below the horizon in the morning. Evening nautical twilight begins when the geometric center of the sun is 6° below the horizon in the evening and ends (nautical dusk) when the geometric center of the sun is 12° below the horizon in the evening.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nautical_dawn_and_dusk">
        Nautical dawn and dusk
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nautical dawn and dusk">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Nautical dawn is the moment when the geometric center of the Sun is 12 degrees below the horizon in the morning. It is preceded by morning astronomical twilight and followed by morning nautical twilight. Nautical dusk is the moment when the geometric center of the Sun is 12 degrees below the horizon in the evening. It marks the beginning of evening astronomical twilight and the end of evening nautical twilight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PanoSantPere.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="2756" height="91" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/PanoSantPere.jpg/250px-PanoSantPere.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/PanoSantPere.jpg/375px-PanoSantPere.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/PanoSantPere.jpg/500px-PanoSantPere.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PanoSantPere.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         At the beginning of nautical twilight, artificial lighting must be used to see terrestrial objects clearly.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

         <a href="/wiki/Lake_Ontario" title="Lake Ontario">

         </a>

         <a href="/wiki/Canada" title="Canada">

         </a>

      <p>
       Before nautical dawn and after nautical dusk, sailors cannot navigate via the horizon at sea.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Low_precision_formulae_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Low_precision_formulae-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At nautical dawn and nautical dusk, the human eye finds it difficult, if not impossible, to discern traces of illumination near the sunset or sunrise point of the horizon ("first light” after nautical dawn but before civil dawn and "nightfall" after civil dusk but before nautical dusk).
      </p>
      <p>
       Sailors can take reliable star sightings of well-known stars, during the stage of nautical twilight when they can distinguish a visible horizon for reference. Under good atmospheric conditions with the absence of other illumination, during nautical twilight, the human eye may distinguish general outlines of ground objects but cannot participate in detailed outdoor operations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Nautical twilight has military considerations as well. The initialisms
       <b>
        BMNT
       </b>
       (begin morning nautical twilight,
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       nautical dawn) and
       <b>
        EENT
       </b>
       (end evening nautical twilight,
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       nautical dusk) are used and considered when planning military operations. A military unit may treat BMNT and EENT with heightened security, e.g. by "standing to", in which everyone assumes a defensive position. This is partially due to tactics dating back to the
       <a href="/wiki/French_and_Indian_War" title="French and Indian War">
        French and Indian War
       </a>
       (part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Seven_Years%27_War" title="Seven Years' War">
        Seven Years' War
       </a>
       of 1756–1763), when combatants on both sides would launch attacks at nautical dawn or dusk.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_twilight">
        Astronomical twilight
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Astronomical twilight">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical_dawn_and_dusk">
        Astronomical dawn and dusk
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Astronomical dawn and dusk">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Astronomical dawn is the moment when the geometric center of the Sun is 18 degrees below the horizon in the morning. Astronomical dusk is the moment when the geometric center of the Sun is 18 degrees below the horizon in the evening. After astronomical dusk and before astronomical dawn, the sky is not illuminated by the sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TAPN2002_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-TAPN2002-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USNO_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-USNO-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition_2">
        Definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Morning astronomical twilight begins (astronomical dawn) when the geometric center of the sun is 18° below the horizon in the morning and ends when the geometric center of the sun is 12° below the horizon in the morning. Evening astronomical twilight begins when the geometric center of the sun is 12° below the horizon in the evening and ends (astronomical dusk) when the geometric center of the sun is 18° below the horizon in the evening.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GOMN_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-GOMN-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cambridge_5-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cambridge-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In some places (away from urban
       <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
        light pollution
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moonlight" title="Moonlight">
        moonlight
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        auroras
       </a>
       , and other sources of light), where the sky is dark enough for nearly all astronomical observations, astronomers can easily make observations of point sources such as stars both during and after astronomical twilight in the evening and both before and during astronomical twilight in the morning. However, some critical observations, such as of faint diffuse items such as
       <a href="/wiki/Nebula" title="Nebula">
        nebulae
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
        galaxies
       </a>
       , may require observation beyond the limit of astronomical twilight. Theoretically, the faintest stars detectable by the naked eye (those of approximately the sixth magnitude) will become visible in the evening at
       <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">
        astronomical dusk
       </a>
       , and become invisible at
       <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">
        astronomical dawn
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Astronomical_Dusk.jpg" title="Enlarge">

         <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">

      <p>
       However, in other places, especially those with
       <a href="/wiki/Skyglow" title="Skyglow">
        skyglow
       </a>
       , astronomical twilight may be almost indistinguishable from night. In the evening, even when astronomical twilight has yet to end and in the morning when astronomical twilight has already begun, most casual observers would consider the entire sky fully dark. Because of
       <a href="/wiki/Light_pollution" title="Light pollution">
        light pollution
       </a>
       , observers in some localities, generally in large cities, may never have the opportunity to view even fourth-magnitude stars, irrespective of the presence of any twilight at all, and to experience truly dark skies.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Times_of_occurrence">
        Times of occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Between_day_and_night">
        Between day and night
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Between day and night">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Observers within 48.5 degrees of the Equator can view twilight twice each day on every date of the year between astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Dawn" title="Dawn">
        dawn
       </a>
       , nautical dawn, or civil dawn, and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunrise" title="Sunrise">
        sunrise
       </a>
       as well as between
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sunset
       </a>
       and civil dusk, nautical dusk, or astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Dusk" title="Dusk">
        dusk
       </a>
       . This also occurs for most observers at higher latitudes on many dates throughout the year, except those around the summer solstice. However, at latitudes closer than 9 degrees to either Pole, the Sun cannot rise above the horizon nor sink more than 18 degrees below it on the same day on any date, this example of twilight cannot occur because the angular difference between solar noon and solar midnight elevates less than 18 degrees.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lasting_from_one_day_to_the_next">
        Lasting from one day to the next
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Lasting from one day to the next">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At latitudes greater than about 48.5 degrees North or South, on dates near the summer solstice, twilight can last from sunset to sunrise, since the Sun does not go more than 18 degrees below the horizon, so complete darkness does not occur even at solar midnight. These latitudes include many densely populated regions of the Earth, including the entire
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       and other countries in
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
        northern Europe
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Between_one_night_and_the_next">
        Between one night and the next
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Between one night and the next">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       latitudes in wintertime, the
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        polar night
       </a>
       only rarely produces complete darkness for 24 hours each day. This can occur only at locations within 5.5 degrees of latitude of the Pole, and there only on dates close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       . At all other latitudes and dates, the polar night includes a daily period of twilight, when the Sun is not far below the horizon. Around winter solstice, when the solar declination changes slowly, complete darkness lasts several weeks at the Pole itself, e.g., from May 11 to July 31 at
       <a href="/wiki/Amundsen%E2%80%93Scott_South_Pole_Station" title="Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station">
        Amundsen–Scott South Pole Station
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       North Pole has the experience of this from November 12 to January 30.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lasting_for_24_hours">
        Lasting for 24 hours
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Lasting for 24 hours">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       At latitudes within 9 degrees of either Pole, as the sun's angular elevation difference is less than 18 degrees, twilight can last for the entire 24 hours. This occurs for one day at latitudes near 9 degrees from the Pole and extends up to several weeks the further towards the Pole one goes. The only permanent settlement to experience this condition is
       <a href="/wiki/Alert,_Nunavut" title="Alert, Nunavut">
        Alert, Nunavut
       </a>
       , Canada, where it occurs for a week in late February, and again in late October.
      </p>

      <p>
       The apparent travel of the sun occurs at the rate of 15 degrees per hour (360° per day), but sunrise and sunset happen typically at oblique angles to the horizon and the actual duration of any twilight period will be a function of that angle, being longer for more oblique angles. This angle of the sun's motion with respect to the horizon changes with
       <a href="/wiki/Latitude" title="Latitude">
        latitude
       </a>
       as well as the time of year (affecting the angle of the Earth's axis with respect to the sun).
      </p>
      <p>
       At
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greenwich,_England" title="Greenwich, England">
        Greenwich, England
       </a>
       (51.5°N), the duration of civil twilight will vary from 33 minutes to 48 minutes, depending on the time of year. At the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , conditions can go from day to night in as little as 20–25 minutes. This is true because at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Low_latitudes" title="Low latitudes">
        low latitudes
       </a>
       the sun's apparent movement is
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to the observer's horizon. But at the poles, civil twilight can be as long as 2–3 weeks. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Arctic" title="Arctic">
        Arctic
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">
        Antarctic
       </a>
       regions, twilight (if there is any) can last for several hours. There is no astronomical twilight at the poles near the
       <a href="/wiki/Winter_solstice" title="Winter solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       (for about 74 days at the North Pole and about 80 days at the South Pole). As one gets closer to the Arctic and Antarctic circles, the sun's disk moves toward the observer's horizon at a lower angle. The observer's earthly location will pass through the various twilight zones less directly, taking more time.
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Polar_circle" title="Polar circle">
        polar circles
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        twenty-four-hour daylight
       </a>
       is encountered in summer, and in regions very close to the poles, twilight can last for weeks on the winter side of the
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinoxes
       </a>
       . Outside the polar circles, where the angular distance from the polar circle is less than the angle which defines twilight (see above), twilight can continue through local midnight near the
       <a href="/wiki/Summer_solstice" title="Summer solstice">
        summer solstice
       </a>
       . The precise position of the polar circles, and the regions where twilight can continue through local midnight, varies slightly from year to year with Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       . The lowest latitudes at which the various twilights can continue through local midnight are approximately 60.561° (60°33′43″) for civil twilight, 54.561° (54°33′43″) for nautical twilight and 48.561° (48°33′43″) for astronomical twilight.

      <p>
       Although Helsinki, Oslo, Stockholm, Tallinn, and Saint Petersburg also enter into nautical twilight after sunset, they do have noticeably lighter skies at night during the summer solstice than other locations mentioned in their category above, because they do not go far into nautical twilight. A
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_night_(astronomy)" title="White night (astronomy)">
        white night
       </a>
       is a night with only civil twilight which lasts from sunset to sunrise.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the winter solstice within the polar circle, twilight can extend through solar noon at latitudes below 72.561° (72°33′43″) for civil twilight, 78.561° (78°33′43″) for nautical twilight, and 84.561° (84°33′43″) for astronomical twilight.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="On_other_planets">
        On other planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: On other planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Twilight on
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       is longer than on Earth, lasting for up to two hours before sunrise or after sunset. Dust high in the atmosphere scatters light to the night side of the planet. Similar twilights are seen on Earth following major
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">
        volcanic eruptions
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_religion">
        In religion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: In religion">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Christianity">
        Christianity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Christianity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg/170px-BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg/255px-BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg/340px-BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:BenedictineEasterVigil.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
          American
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Order_of_Saint_Benedict" title="Order of Saint Benedict">
          Benedictine
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Monk" title="Monk">
          monks
         </a>
         around an
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_fire" title="Easter fire">
          Easter fire
         </a>
         preparing to light the
         <a href="/wiki/Paschal_candle" title="Paschal candle">
          Paschal candle
         </a>
         prior to Easter Vigil
         <a href="/wiki/Mass_(liturgy)" title="Mass (liturgy)">
          mass
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Christian" title="Christian">
        Christian
       </a>
       practice, "
       <a href="/wiki/Vigil" title="Vigil">
        vigil
       </a>
       " observances often occur during twilight on the evening before major feast days or holidays. For example, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Easter_Vigil" title="Easter Vigil">
         Easter Vigil
        </a>
       </i>
       is held in the hours of darkness between sunset on
       <a href="/wiki/Holy_Saturday" title="Holy Saturday">
        Holy Saturday
       </a>
       and sunrise on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Easter_Day" title="Easter Day">
        Easter Day
       </a>
       — most commonly in the evening of Holy Saturday or midnight — and is the first celebration of Easter, days traditionally being considered to begin at sunset.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Hinduism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Twilight is sacred in
       <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">
        Hinduism
       </a>
       . It is called गोधूळिवेळ
       <i>
        gōdhūḷivēḷ
       </i>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Marathi_language" title="Marathi language">
        Marathi
       </a>
       or गोधूलिवेला
       <i>
        godhūlivelā
       </i>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindi_language" title="Hindi language">
        Hindi
       </a>
       , గొధూళివేళ "godhoolivela" in Telugu, literally "cow dust time". Many
       <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">
        rituals
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Sandhyavandanam" title="Sandhyavandanam">
        Sandhyavandanam
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Puja_(Hinduism)" title="Puja (Hinduism)">
        Puja
       </a>
       , are performed at twilight hour. Eating of food is not advised during this time. Sometimes it is referred to as Asurasandhya vela. It is believed that
       <a href="/wiki/Asura" title="Asura">
        Asuras
       </a>
       are active during these hours. One of the avatars of Lord
       <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">
        Vishnu
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Narasimha" title="Narasimha">
        Narasimha
       </a>
       , is closely associated with the twilight period. According to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hindu_scriptures" title="Hindu scriptures">
        Hindu scriptures
       </a>
       , a daemonic king,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hiranakashipa" title="Hiranakashipa">
        Hiranakashipa
       </a>
       , performed penance and obtained a boon from
       <a href="/wiki/Brahma" title="Brahma">
        Brahma
       </a>
       that he could not be killed during day or night and neither by human nor animal. Lord
       <a href="/wiki/Vishnu" title="Vishnu">
        Vishnu
       </a>
       appeared in a half-man half-lion form (neither human nor animal), ended the life of Hiranakashipa during twilight (neither day nor night).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Islam">
        Islam
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Islam">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Twilight is important in
       <a href="/wiki/Islam" title="Islam">
        Islam
       </a>
       as it determines when certain
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salat" title="Salat">
        universally obligatory prayers
       </a>
       are to be recited. Morning twilight is when morning prayers (
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fajr" title="Fajr">
         Fajr
        </a>
       </i>
       ) are done, while evening twilight is the time for evening prayers (
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Maghrib_prayer" title="Maghrib prayer">
         Maghrib prayer
        </a>
       </i>
       ). Suhoor (early morning meal) time for fasting (during Ramadan and other times) ends at morning twilight (at dawn). Fasting is from the beginning of dawn to sunset. There is also an important discussion in Islamic jurisprudence between "true dawn" and "
       <a href="/wiki/Zodiacal_light#Importance_to_Islam" title="Zodiacal light">
        false dawn
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Judaism">
        Judaism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Twilight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Judaism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In Judaism, twilight is considered neither day nor night; consequently it is treated as a safeguard against encroachment upon either. For example, the twilight of Friday is reckoned as
       <a href="/wiki/Sabbath" title="Sabbath">
        Sabbath
       </a>
       eve, and that of Saturday as Sabbath day; and the same rule applies to festival days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
