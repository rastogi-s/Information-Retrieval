      <p>
       A
       <b>
        geographical pole
       </b>
       is either of the two points on a rotating body (
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       , sphere...etc) where its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">
        axis of rotation
       </a>
       intersects its surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As with
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Poles
       </a>
       , they are usually called that body's "north pole" and "south pole", one lying 90
       <a href="/wiki/Degree_(angle)" title="Degree (angle)">
        degrees
       </a>
       in one direction from the body's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       and the other lying 90 degrees in the opposite direction from the equator.
      </p>
      <p>
       Every planet has geographical poles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If, like
       <a href="/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field" title="Earth's magnetic field">
        the Earth
       </a>
       , a body generates a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       , it will also possess
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        magnetic poles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perturbations in a body's rotation mean that geographical poles wander slightly on its surface. The Earth's North and South Poles, for example, move by a few metres over periods of a few years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As
       <a href="/wiki/Cartography" title="Cartography">
        cartography
       </a>
       requires exact and unchanging coordinates, the averaged
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       locations of geographical poles are taken as fixed
       <i>
        cartographic poles
       </i>
       and become the points where the body's
       <a href="/wiki/Great_circle" title="Great circle">
        great circles
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Longitude" title="Longitude">
        longitude
       </a>
       intersect.
      </p>
