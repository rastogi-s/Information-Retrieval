   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    High Resolution Coronal Imager
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="450" data-file-width="674" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg/220px-HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg/330px-HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg/440px-HI-C_telescope_payload.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        High Resolution Coronal Imager
       </b>
       <b>
        (Hi-C)
       </b>
       is a sub-orbital telescope designed to take high-resolution images of the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . It was launched on July 11, 2012 aboard a
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Brant_(rocket)" title="Black Brant (rocket)">
        Black Brant
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Sounding_rocket" title="Sounding rocket">
        sounding rocket
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/White_Sands_Missile_Range" title="White Sands Missile Range">
        White Sands Missile Range
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCD_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CCD-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The images taken were the highest resolution photos ever of the Sun's corona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Telescope_description">
        Telescope description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Telescope description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The telescope weighs 464 pounds (210 kg), and is 10 feet (3.0 m) long.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mirrors are approximately 9.5 inches (24 cm) across. Its optics were designed at the
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Huntsville,_Alabama" title="Huntsville, Alabama">
        Huntsville, Alabama
       </a>
       with assistance from the
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
        Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       and L-3Com/Tinsley Laboratories of
       <a href="/wiki/Richmond,_California" title="Richmond, California">
        Richmond, California
       </a>
       . Dr. Jonathan Cirtain, from MSFL said: "These mirrors were to be the finest pieces of glass ever fabricated for solar astrophysics."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Imaging_system">
        Imaging system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Imaging system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The imaging system was designed by Apogee Imaging Systems with a resolution of 0.1 arcsec/pixel (14 times higher resolution than the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
       ). It was based on a customized version of the E2V CCD203 from
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">
        Lockheed Martin
       </a>
       , which is a very large 4 channel back illuminated 4,000 × 4,000 pixel
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        charge-coupled device
       </a>
       (CCD).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CCD_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CCD-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">
        Mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1000" data-file-width="1000" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg/220px-Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg/330px-Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg/440px-Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun-corona-magnetic-braids.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Images of the Sun's million degree corona, including images of the magnetic braids (left hand side).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The flight lasted for 10 minutes, reached an altitude of 283 kilometres (176 mi) and the telescope captured 165 images of a large
       <a href="/wiki/Corona#Active_regions" title="Corona">
        active region
       </a>
       . It imaged the Sun in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_light" title="Ultraviolet light">
        ultraviolet light
       </a>
       at 19.3 nm wavelength.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZonano_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AZonano-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The total cost of the mission was $5 million.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Findings">
        Findings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Findings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The experiment revealed never-before-seen "magnetic braids" of plasma roiling in the Sun's outer layers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the first time scientists were able to directly observe
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       in braids, which may be the primary sources of heating in the active solar corona.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AZonano_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-AZonano-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cirtain2013_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cirtain2013-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
