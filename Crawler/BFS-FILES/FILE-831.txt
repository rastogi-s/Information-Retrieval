      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         The Spectator
        </b>
       </i>
       is a weekly
       <a href="/wiki/Conservatism_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Conservatism in the United Kingdom">
        British conservative
       </a>
       magazine on politics, culture, and current affairs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was first published on 6 July 1828.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is currently owned by
       <a href="/wiki/David_and_Frederick_Barclay" title="David and Frederick Barclay">
        David and Frederick Barclay
       </a>
       who also own
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       newspaper, via
       <a href="/wiki/Press_Holdings" title="Press Holdings">
        Press Holdings
       </a>
       . Its principal subject areas are politics and culture. Its editorial outlook is generally supportive of the
       <a href="/wiki/Conservative_Party_(UK)" title="Conservative Party (UK)">
        Conservative Party
       </a>
       , although regular contributors include some outside that fold, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Field_(British_politician)" title="Frank Field (British politician)">
        Frank Field
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rod_Liddle" title="Rod Liddle">
        Rod Liddle
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Bright" title="Martin Bright">
        Martin Bright
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The magazine also contains arts pages on books, music, opera, and film and TV reviews. In late 2008,
       <i>
        Spectator Australia
       </i>
       was launched. This offers 12 pages of "Unique Australian Content" (including a separate editorial page) in addition to the full UK contents.
      </p>
      <p>
       Editorship of
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       has often been a step on the ladder to high office in the Conservative Party in the UK – past editors include
       <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">
        Boris Johnson
       </a>
       (1999–2005), the incumbent
       <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Foreign_and_Commonwealth_Affairs" title="Secretary of State for Foreign and Commonwealth Affairs">
        Foreign Secretary
       </a>
       , and former cabinet members
       <a href="/wiki/Iain_Macleod" title="Iain Macleod">
        Iain Macleod
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Gilmour" title="Ian Gilmour">
        Ian Gilmour
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Lawson" title="Nigel Lawson">
        Nigel Lawson
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_years">
        Early years
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s founding editor, the
       <a href="/wiki/Dundee" title="Dundee">
        Dundonian
       </a>
       reformer
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Stephen_Rintoul" title="Robert Stephen Rintoul">
        Robert Stephen Rintoul
       </a>
       , launched the paper on 5 December 1828. Almost certainly (there is no precise evidence) he revived the title from the
       <a href="/wiki/The_Spectator_(1711)" title="The Spectator (1711)">
        1711 publication by Addison &amp; Steele
       </a>
       . As he had long been determined "to edit a perfect newspaper",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beach_Thomas_1928_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beach_Thomas_1928-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rintoul initially insisted on "absolute power"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Beach_Thomas_1928_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Beach_Thomas_1928-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       over content, commencing a long-lasting tradition of the paper’s editor and proprietor being one and the same person.
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s political outlook in its first thirty years reflected Rintoul’s liberal-radical agenda.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Despite its political stance it was widely regarded and respected for its non-partisanship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Under Rintoul
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       came out strongly for the
       <a href="/wiki/Reform_Act_1832" title="Reform Act 1832">
        Great Reform Act of 1832
       </a>
       , coining the well-known phrase, "The Bill, the whole Bill and nothing but the Bill", in its support. It also objected to the appointment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington" title="Arthur Wellesley, 1st Duke of Wellington">
        Duke of Wellington
       </a>
       as Prime Minister, condemning him as "a Field Marshal whose political career proves him to be utterly destitute of political principle – whose military career affords ample evidence of his stern and remorseless temperament."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The magazine was vocal in its opposition to the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Opium_War" title="First Opium War">
        First Opium War
       </a>
       (1839–1842), commenting: "all the alleged aims of the expedition against China are vague, illimitable, and incapable of explanation, save only that of making the Chinese pay the opium-smugglers."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and "There does not appear to be much glory gained in a contest so unequal that hundreds are killed on one side and none on the other. What honour is there in going to shoot men, certain that they cannot hurt you? The cause of the war, be it remembered, is as disreputable as the strength of the parties is unequal. The war is undertaken in support of a co-partnery of opium-smugglers, in which the Anglo-Indian Government may be considered as the principal partner."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1853 it published an anonymous and unfavourable review of
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">
        Charles Dickens
       </a>
       ’
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bleak_House" title="Bleak House">
         Bleak House
        </a>
       </i>
       , later revealed to be by
       <a href="/wiki/George_Brimley" title="George Brimley">
        George Brimley
       </a>
       , typical of the paper's enduring contempt for him as a "popular" writer "amusing the idle hours of the greatest number of readers; not, we may hope, without improvement to their hearts, but certainly without profoundly affecting their intellects or deeply stirring their emotions."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Rintoul died in April 1858 and the magazine, whose circulation was falling, was sold. Thereafter, it went into an accelerated period of decline. Records are scarce but it appears that it was briefly owned by a Mr Scott and then bought for £4200 in December 1858 by two London-based Americans, James McHenry and
       <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Moran" title="Benjamin Moran">
        Benjamin Moran
       </a>
       . McHenry was a businessman and Moran was an Assistant Secretary to the ambassador,
       <a href="/wiki/George_M._Dallas" title="George M. Dallas">
        George M. Dallas
       </a>
       ; they saw their purchase as a means to influence British opinion on American affairs. The editor was
       <a href="/wiki/Thornton_Leigh_Hunt" title="Thornton Leigh Hunt">
        Thornton Hunt
       </a>
       , a friend of Moran who had also worked for Rintoul. Hunt was also nominally the purchaser, having been given the necessary monies in an attempt by McHenry and Moran to disguise the American ownership. Circulation declined with this loss of independence and inspirational leadership, and the views of
       <a href="/wiki/James_Buchanan" title="James Buchanan">
        James Buchanan
       </a>
       , the then president of the US, came to the fore. Within weeks,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the editorial line followed Buchanan's pronouncements in being "...neither pro-slavery nor pro-abolitionist. To unsympathetic observers Buchanan's policy seemed to apportion blame for the impasse on the slavery question equally on pro-slavery and abolitionist factions - and rather than work out a solution, simply to argue that a solution would take time. The Spectator now would publicly support that 'policy.'".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This set it at odds with most of the British press but gained it the sympathy of ex-patriate Americans in the country. Richard Fulton notes that from then until 1861, "... the
       <i>
        Spectator's
       </i>
       commentary on American affairs read like a Buchanan administration propaganda sheet." and that this represented a
       <i>
        volte-face
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Meredith_Townsend.2C_Richard_Holt_Hutton_and_John_Strachey">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meredith_Townsend,_Richard_Holt_Hutton_and_John_Strachey">
        Meredith Townsend, Richard Holt Hutton and John Strachey
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Meredith Townsend, Richard Holt Hutton and John Strachey">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       On 19 January 1861,
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       was bought by a journalist,
       <a href="/wiki/Meredith_Townsend" title="Meredith Townsend">
        Meredith Townsend
       </a>
       , for £2000. The need to promote the Buchanan position in Britain had been reduced as British papers such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_Review_(London)" title="Saturday Review (London)">
         The Saturday Review
        </a>
       </i>
       turned in his favour, fearing the potential effects of a split in the Union.
       <a href="/wiki/Abraham_Lincoln" title="Abraham Lincoln">
        Abraham Lincoln
       </a>
       had also replaced the vacillating Buchanan and Moran's position in London was in doubt now that Dallas had been removed as ambassador. In addition, the owners had been pumping money into a loss-making publication and were increasingly reluctant to continue the practice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From the outset, Townsend took up an anti-Buchanan, anti-slavery position, arguing that his unwillingness to act decisively had been a weakness and a contributor to the problems apparent in the US.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fulton_5-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fulton-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He soon went into partnership with
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Holt_Hutton" title="Richard Holt Hutton">
        Richard Holt Hutton
       </a>
       , a theologian whose friend
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Gladstone" title="William Gladstone">
        William Gladstone
       </a>
       later called him "the first critic of the nineteenth century".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Townsend’s writing in
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       confirmed him as one of the finest journalists of his day, and he has since been called "the greatest leader writer ever to appear in the English Press."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The two men remained co-proprietors and joint editors for 25 years, taking a strong stand on some of the most controversial issues of their day. They supported the Federalists against the South in the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Civil_War" title="American Civil War">
        American Civil War
       </a>
       , an unpopular position which, at the time, did some damage to the paper’s circulation, though gained readers in the long run when the North won.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They also launched an all-out assault on
       <a href="/wiki/Benjamin_Disraeli" title="Benjamin Disraeli">
        Benjamin Disraeli
       </a>
       , accusing him in a series of leaders of jettisoning ethics for politics by ignoring the atrocities committed against Bulgarian civilians by Turkey in the 1870s.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1887 Townsend was succeeded by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Strachey_(journalist)" title="John Strachey (journalist)">
        John St Loe Strachey
       </a>
       , a young aristocrat who had replaced
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/H.H._Asquith" title="H.H. Asquith">
        H.H. Asquith
       </a>
       (the future Prime Minister) as a leader-writer during the previous year. As well as being
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s sole proprietor and editor, he also became its chief leader-writer, general manager and literature critic. The paper’s circulation doubled under Strachey’s leadership, becoming "the most influential of all the London weeklies"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before 1914. After falling ill in 1925, Strachey finally sold his controlling interest in the paper to his business manager,
       <a href="/wiki/Evelyn_Wrench" title="Evelyn Wrench">
        Sir Evelyn Wrench
       </a>
       , and retired, dying two years later in 1928.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="1928.E2.80.931975">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1928–1975">
        1928–1975
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: 1928–1975">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Aberdare_miners_and_1930s_Europe">
        Aberdare miners and 1930s Europe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Aberdare miners and 1930s Europe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Perhaps Wrench’s most remembered achievement as editor of
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       was his campaign to ease unemployment in the mining town of
       <a href="/wiki/Aberdare" title="Aberdare">
        Aberdare
       </a>
       , one of the worst hit by the crisis of 1928, when joblessness reached 40% in South Wales. Within three months, the paper’s appeal for the town’s relief raised over £12,000 (the equivalent of about £500,000 today).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A statuette presented in gratitude to
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       , of an Aberdare miner, still sits in the editor’s office, bearing the inscription: "From the Townsfolk of Aberdare in Grateful Recognition: 'The Greatest of These is Love'".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Wrench retired as editor in 1932 (though he remained the magazine's proprietor), appointing
       <a href="/wiki/Wilson_Harris_(journalist)" title="Wilson Harris (journalist)">
        Wilson Harris
       </a>
       his successor. Under Harris
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       became increasingly outspoken on developing international politics in the 1930s, in particular on the rise of
       <a href="/wiki/Fascism" title="Fascism">
        fascism
       </a>
       . Beneath a reader’s letter referring to the
       <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Party" title="Nazi Party">
        Nazi Party
       </a>
       as "peaceful, orderly and kindly", Harris printed the following reply:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        No facts in recent history are established more incontestably? ... than the numerous cases of murder, assault, and various forms of intimidation for which the National Socialist Party in Germany has been responsible ... The organized economic boycott of the Jews is the climax.
        <i>
         The Spectator
        </i>
        has consistently shown itself a friend of Germany, but it is a friend of freedom first. Resort to violence is not condoned by styling it revolution.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       In general however, Harris supported
       <a href="/wiki/Neville_Chamberlain" title="Neville Chamberlain">
        Neville Chamberlain
       </a>
       ’s policy of appeasement, praising the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Munich_agreement" title="Munich agreement">
        Munich agreement
       </a>
       , explaining later that he believed "even the most desperate attempt to save the peace was worthwhile".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ian_Gilmour">
        Ian Gilmour
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Ian Gilmour">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Wrench sold
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       in 1954 to barrister
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Gilmour" title="Ian Gilmour">
        Ian Gilmour
       </a>
       . Assuming the editorship himself from 1954 to 1959, Gilmour adopted a
       <a href="/wiki/Libertarianism" title="Libertarianism">
        libertarian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pro-Europeanism" title="Pro-Europeanism">
        pro-European
       </a>
       outlook, and "enlivened the paper and injected a new element of irreverence, fun and controversy".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was critical of
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">
        Harold Macmillan
       </a>
       's government, and while supporting the Conservatives was also friendly to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hugh_Gaitskell" title="Hugh Gaitskell">
        Hugh Gaitskell
       </a>
       wing of the Labour Party.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-royjenkins-auto_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-royjenkins-auto-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Gilmour famously lent
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s voice to the campaign to end
       <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment" title="Capital punishment">
        capital punishment
       </a>
       in Britain, writing an incensed leader attacking the hanging of
       <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Ellis" title="Ruth Ellis">
        Ruth Ellis
       </a>
       in 1955, in which he claimed "Hanging has become the national sport", and that the home secretary
       <a href="/wiki/Gwilym_Lloyd_George" title="Gwilym Lloyd George">
        Gwilym Lloyd George
       </a>
       , for not reprieving the sentence, "has now been responsible for the hanging of two women over the past eight months".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       opposed Britain’s involvement in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Suez_crisis" title="Suez crisis">
        Suez crisis
       </a>
       in 1956, strongly criticizing the government’s handling of the debacle. The paper went on to oppose Macmillan’s government’s
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1959" title="United Kingdom general election, 1959">
        re-election in 1959
       </a>
       , complaining: "The continued Conservative pretence that Suez was a good, a noble, a wise venture has been too much to stomach ... the Government is taking its stand on a solid principle: 'Never admit a mistake.'"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The paper also gave its support to the proposals of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolfenden_Committee" title="Wolfenden Committee">
        Wolfenden Committee
       </a>
       in 1957, condemning the "utterly irrational and illogical" old laws on homosexuality: "Not only is the law unjust in conception, it is almost inevitably unjust in practice".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_1999_11-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld_1999-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In March the same year,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jenny_Nicholson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jenny Nicholson (page does not exist)">
        Jenny Nicholson
       </a>
       , a frequent contributor, wrote a piece on the
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Socialist_Party" title="Italian Socialist Party">
        Italian Socialist Party
       </a>
       congress in Venice, which mentioned three Labour Party politicians (
       <a href="/wiki/Aneurin_Bevan" title="Aneurin Bevan">
        Aneurin Bevan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Crossman" title="Richard Crossman">
        Richard Crossman
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Phillips" title="Morgan Phillips">
        Morgan Phillips
       </a>
       ) "who puzzled the Italians by filling themselves like tanks with whisky and coffee "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All three sued for libel, the case went to trial and
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       was forced to make a large payment in damages and costs, a sum well over the equivalent of £150,000 today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has since emerged that "all three plaintiffs, to a greater or lesser degree, perjured themselves in court".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id=".22The_Tory_Leadership.22_article">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"The_Tory_Leadership"_article'>
        "The Tory Leadership" article
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title='Edit section: "The Tory Leadership" article'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In 1963, Gilmour offered the editorship to
       <a href="/wiki/Iain_Macleod" title="Iain Macleod">
        Iain Macleod
       </a>
       , the politician who had recently resigned his cabinet seat in objection to the controversial appointment of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_Alec_Douglas-Home" title="Sir Alec Douglas-Home">
        Sir Alec Douglas-Home
       </a>
       as Prime Minister in succession to
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Macmillan" title="Harold Macmillan">
        Harold Macmillan
       </a>
       . The decision caused enormous controversy, especially after Macleod used the paper to explain his recent resignation. In an article entitled "The Tory Leadership", ostensibly a review of a new book by
       <a href="/wiki/Randolph_Churchill" title="Randolph Churchill">
        Randolph Churchill
       </a>
       , Macleod laid out his version of events in great detail.
      </p>
      <p>
       In disclosing, from the horse’s mouth, the mysterious circumstances of Douglas-Home’s appointment, the article caused an immediate sensation. Churchill’s book was all but obliterated by the review, which said that "four fifths" of it "could have been compiled by anyone with a pair of scissors, a pot of paste and a built-in prejudice against Mr Butler and
       <a href="/wiki/William_Haley" title="William Haley">
        Sir William Haley
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That week’s edition, bearing the headline "Iain Macleod, What Happened", sold a record number of copies.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Nigel_Lawson.2C_George_Gale_and_Harry_Creighton">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nigel_Lawson,_George_Gale_and_Harry_Creighton">
        Nigel Lawson, George Gale and Harry Creighton
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Nigel Lawson, George Gale and Harry Creighton">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The "Tory Leadership" article prompted a furious response from many
       <i>
        Spectator
       </i>
       readers and caused Macleod, for a time, to be shunned by political colleagues. He eventually regained his party’s favour, however, and rejoined the shadow cabinet in the same year. On his appointment as Shadow Chancellor in 1965, he stepped down as editor on the last day of the year, to be replaced by
       <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Lawson" title="Nigel Lawson">
        Nigel Lawson
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sometimes called "The Great Procrastinator" because of his tendency to leave writing leaders until the last minute,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lawson had been City editor for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">
         The Sunday Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       and Alec Douglas-Home’s personal assistant during the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1964" title="United Kingdom general election, 1964">
        1964 general election
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Largely thanks to Lawson, in 1966
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       opposed America’s increasing military commitment in
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">
        Vietnam
       </a>
       . In a signed article he estimated "the risks involved in an American withdrawal from Vietnam are less than the risks in escalating a bloody and brutal war".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1967 Ian Gilmour, who by then had joined parliament and was already finding the proprietorship less of a help than a hindrance in political life, sold
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Creighton" title="Harold Creighton">
        Harry Creighton
       </a>
       for £75,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1970, Creighton replaced Lawson as editor (there had been growing resentment between the two men)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/George_Gale_(journalist)" title="George Gale (journalist)">
        George Gale
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Gale shared Creighton’s political outlook,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Blake_4-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Blake-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in particular his strong opposition to the Common Market, and much of the next five years was spent attacking the pro-
       <a href="/wiki/European_Economic_Community" title="European Economic Community">
        EEC
       </a>
       prime minister
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Heath" title="Edward Heath">
        Edward Heath
       </a>
       , treating his eventual defeat by
       <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">
        Margaret Thatcher
       </a>
       with undisguised delight.
      </p>
      <p>
       Gale’s almost obsessive opposition to the EEC and antagonistic attitude towards Heath began to lose the magazine readers. In 1973 Creighton took over the editorship himself, but was, if possible, even less successful in stemming the losses. Circulation fell from 36,000 in 1966 to below 17,000. As one journalist who joined
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       at that time said: "It gave the impression, an entirely accurate one, of a publication surviving on a shoestring".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       George Gale later remarked that Creighton had only wanted the job to get into
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Who%27s_Who_(UK)" title="Who's Who (UK)">
         Who’s Who
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="1975.E2.80.932005">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="1975–2005">
        1975–2005
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 1975–2005">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Henry_Keswick_and_Alexander_Chancellor">
        Henry Keswick and Alexander Chancellor
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Henry Keswick and Alexander Chancellor">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In 1975 Creighton sold
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Keswick_(businessman)" title="Henry Keswick (businessman)">
        Henry Keswick
       </a>
       , again for £75,000 (Creighton sold the 99 Gower Street premises separately, so the magazine moved to offices in Doughty Street).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Keswick was chairman of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jardine_Matheson_Holdings" title="Jardine Matheson Holdings">
        Jardine Matheson
       </a>
       multinational corporation. He was drawn to the paper partly because he harboured political aspirations (the paper’s perk as a useful stepping stone to Westminster was, by now, well established), but also because his father had been a friend of
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Fleming_(writer)" title="Peter Fleming (writer)">
        Peter Fleming
       </a>
       , its well-known columnist.
      </p>
      <p>
       Keswick gave the job of editor to "the only journalist he knew",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Chancellor" title="Alexander Chancellor">
        Alexander Chancellor
       </a>
       , an old family friend and his mother’s godson, with whom he had been at
       <a href="/wiki/Eton_College" title="Eton College">
        Eton
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cambridge_University" title="Cambridge University">
        Cambridge
       </a>
       . Before then, Chancellor had worked at
       <a href="/wiki/Reuters" title="Reuters">
        Reuters
       </a>
       news agency and had been a scriptwriter and reporter for
       <a href="/wiki/ITN" title="ITN">
        ITN
       </a>
       . In spite of his relative inexperience, he was to become known as "one of the best editors in the history of
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Chancellor’s editorship of the paper relied principally on a return to earlier values. He adopted a new format and a more traditional weekly style, with the front page displaying five cover lines above the leader. Most significantly, he recognised the need "to bring together a number of talented writers and, with the minimal of editorial interference, let them write".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       To this end he persuaded
       <a href="/wiki/Auberon_Waugh" title="Auberon Waugh">
        Auberon Waugh
       </a>
       (who had been sacked by Nigel Lawson) to return from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Statesman" title="New Statesman">
         New Statesman
        </a>
       </i>
       , and enticed
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_West_(journalist)" title="Richard West (journalist)">
        Richard West
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Bernard" title="Jeffrey Bernard">
        Jeffrey Bernard
       </a>
       from the same magazine. Another columnist recruited by Chancellor was
       <a href="/wiki/Taki_Theodoracopulos" title="Taki Theodoracopulos">
        Taki Theodoracopulos
       </a>
       whose column ‘High Life’ was then printed beside Bernard’s ‘Low Life’. Taki's column, frequently criticised for its content by the press,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       remains in the paper.
      </p>
      <p>
       In September 1978, a 96-page issue was released to mark
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s 150th anniversary.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Rees-Mogg" title="William Rees-Mogg">
        William Rees-Mogg
       </a>
       congratulated the paper in a
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         Times
        </a>
        's
       </i>
       leading article, praising it in particular for its important part in "the movement away from collectivism".
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Charles_Moore">
        Charles Moore
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Charles Moore">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Chancellor was replaced by the 28-year-old
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moore_(journalist)" title="Charles Moore (journalist)">
        Charles Moore
       </a>
       in February 1984 after the magazine’s then owner,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Algy_Cluff&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Algy Cluff (page does not exist)">
        Algy Cluff
       </a>
       , had become concerned that
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       was "lacking in political weight" and considered Chancellor to be "commercially irresponsible".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Moore had been a leader writer at
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       before Chancellor recruited him to
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       as political commentator. The paper under Moore became more political than it had been under Chancellor. The new editor adopted an approach that was, in general, pro-
       <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Thatcher" title="Margaret Thatcher">
        Margaret Thatcher
       </a>
       , while showing no restraint in opposing her on certain issues. The paper called the
       <a href="/wiki/Anglo-Irish_Agreement" title="Anglo-Irish Agreement">
        Anglo-Irish Agreement
       </a>
       "a fraudulent prospectus" in 1985, came out against the
       <a href="/wiki/Single_European_Act" title="Single European Act">
        Single European Act
       </a>
       , and, in 1989, criticised the
       <a href="/wiki/Transfer_of_sovereignty_over_Hong_Kong" title="Transfer of sovereignty over Hong Kong">
        handover
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Hong_Kong" title="Hong Kong">
        Hong Kong
       </a>
       to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
        China
       </a>
       . Moore wrote that, if Britain failed to allow the city’s UK passport holders right of abode in Britain, "we shall have to confess that, for the first time in our history, we have forced Britons to be slaves."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He also introduced several new contributors, including a restaurant column by
       <a href="/wiki/Nigella_Lawson" title="Nigella Lawson">
        Nigella Lawson
       </a>
       (the former editor’s daughter), and a humorous column by
       <a href="/wiki/Craig_Brown_(satirist)" title="Craig Brown (satirist)">
        Craig Brown
       </a>
       . When Taki was briefly imprisoned for cocaine possession Moore refused to accept his resignation, explaining publicly: "We expect our High Life columnist to be high some of the time."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       changed hands again in 1985, by which time it had accumulated an overdraft of over £300,000 and it was facing financial meltdown. Cluff had reached the conclusion that the paper "would be best secured in the hands of a publishing group", and sold it to an Australian company,
       <a href="/wiki/Fairfax_Media" title="Fairfax Media">
        John Fairfax Ltd
       </a>
       , who promptly paid off the overdraft. With the support of its new proprietor, the paper was able to widen its readership through subscription drives and advertising, reaching a circulation of 30,000 in 1986, exceeding the circulation of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Statesman" title="New Statesman">
         New Statesman
        </a>
       </i>
       for the first time. The magazine was again sold in 1988, after an uncertain period during which several candidates, including
       <a href="/wiki/Rupert_Murdoch" title="Rupert Murdoch">
        Rupert Murdoch
       </a>
       , attempted to buy the magazine. Moore wrote to Murdoch, saying: "Most of our contributors and many of our readers would be horrified at the idea of your buying
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       . They believe you are autocratic and that you have a bad effect on journalism of quality – they cite
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       as the chief example."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Courtauld_16-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Courtauld-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the end
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       was bought by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph_Group" title="Telegraph Group">
        Telegraph Group
       </a>
       , of which
       <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Black" title="Conrad Black">
        Conrad Black
       </a>
       then had a controlling interest.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dominic_Lawson_and_Frank_Johnson">
        Dominic Lawson and Frank Johnson
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Dominic Lawson and Frank Johnson">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Moore gave up the editorship in 1990 to become deputy editor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       , though he continues to write a column for the magazine. He was replaced by his own deputy editor,
       <a href="/wiki/Dominic_Lawson" title="Dominic Lawson">
        Dominic Lawson
       </a>
       —the former editor’s son.
      </p>
      <p>
       Shortly after becoming editor, Lawson became responsible for the resignation of a cabinet minister when he interviewed the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_Trade_and_Industry" title="Secretary of State for Trade and Industry">
        Secretary of State for Trade and Industry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Ridley,_Baron_Ridley_of_Liddesdale" title="Nicholas Ridley, Baron Ridley of Liddesdale">
        Nicholas Ridley
       </a>
       . During the interview Ridley described the proposed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Economic_and_Monetary_Union" title="Economic and Monetary Union">
        Economic and Monetary Union
       </a>
       as "a German racket designed to take over the whole of Europe",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and seemed to draw comparisons between the German Chancellor,
       <a href="/wiki/Helmut_Kohl" title="Helmut Kohl">
        Helmut Kohl
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Adolf_Hitler" title="Adolf Hitler">
        Adolf Hitler
       </a>
       . The interview appeared in the issue of 14 July 1990, whose cover showed a cartoon by
       <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_Garland" title="Nicholas Garland">
        Nicholas Garland
       </a>
       , of Ridley painting onto a poster of Kohl a crude comb-over and a Hitler moustache. Ridley resigned from Thatcher’s government immediately.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       caused controversy in 1994 when it printed an article entitled "Kings of the Deal" on a claimed Jewish influence in Hollywood, written by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_Cash_(journalist)" title="William Cash (journalist)">
        William Cash
       </a>
       , who at the time was based in Los Angeles and working mainly for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <i>
        The Telegraph
       </i>
       had considered the article too risky to publish, but Lawson thought Cash's idea was as old as Hollywood itself and that his (Lawson's) being Jewish would mitigate adverse reactions to publication. There was, however, considerable controversy. Although owner
       <a href="/wiki/Conrad_Black" title="Conrad Black">
        Conrad Black
       </a>
       did not personally rebuke Lawson,
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Hastings" title="Max Hastings">
        Max Hastings
       </a>
       , then editor of
       <i>
        The Daily Telegraph
       </i>
       , wrote with regard to Black, who also owned
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Jerusalem_Post" title="The Jerusalem Post">
         The Jerusalem Post
        </a>
       </i>
       at the time, "It was one of the few moments in my time with Conrad when I saw him look seriously rattled: 'You don't understand, Max. My entire interests in the United States and internationally could be seriously damaged by this'."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The article was defended by some conservatives.
       <a href="/wiki/John_Derbyshire" title="John Derbyshire">
        John Derbyshire
       </a>
       , who says he has "complicated and sometimes self-contradictory feelings about Jews", wrote on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Review_Online" title="National Review Online">
        National Review Online
       </a>
       regarding what he saw as the Jewish overreaction to the article that "It was a display of arrogance, cruelty, ignorance, stupidity, and sheer bad manners by rich and powerful people towards a harmless, helpless young writer, and the Jews who whipped up this preposterous storm should all be thoroughly ashamed of themselves".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lawson left in 1995 to become editor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">
         The Sunday Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       , and was replaced by a deputy editor of the same newspaper,
       <a href="/wiki/Frank_Johnson_(journalist)" title="Frank Johnson (journalist)">
        Frank Johnson
       </a>
       . After the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_1997" title="United Kingdom general election, 1997">
        1997 election
       </a>
       , Johnson averted a decline in
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s sales by recruiting "
       <a href="/wiki/New_Labour" title="New Labour">
        New Labour
       </a>
       contributors", and shifting the magazine’s direction slightly away from politics. In 1996 the paper featured an interview with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Spice_Girls" title="The Spice Girls">
        The Spice Girls
       </a>
       , in which the band members gave their "Euro-sceptic and generally anti-labour" views on politics. Shortly before her death
       <a href="/wiki/Diana,_Princess_of_Wales" title="Diana, Princess of Wales">
        Diana, Princess of Wales
       </a>
       was depicted on the magazine’s cover as the figurehead of
       <a href="/wiki/Mohamed_Al-Fayed" title="Mohamed Al-Fayed">
        Mohamed Al-Fayed
       </a>
       ’s boat, the Jonikal.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Boris_Johnson">
        Boris Johnson
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Boris Johnson">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Before joining
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       as editor,
       <a href="/wiki/Boris_Johnson" title="Boris Johnson">
        Johnson
       </a>
       had worked for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       , the Wolverhampton
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolverhampton_Express_and_Star" title="Wolverhampton Express and Star">
         Express &amp; Star
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       . He had also briefly been political commentator for
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       under Dominic Lawson, but Frank Johnson replaced him with
       <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Anderson_(columnist)" title="Bruce Anderson (columnist)">
        Bruce Anderson
       </a>
       in 1995. Succeeding Frank Johnson in 1999, Johnson soon established himself as a competent and "colourful"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       editor.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom_general_election,_2001" title="United Kingdom general election, 2001">
        2001 general election
       </a>
       he was elected MP for
       <a href="/wiki/Henley_(UK_Parliament_constituency)" title="Henley (UK Parliament constituency)">
        Henley
       </a>
       , and by 2004 had been made vice-chairman of the Conservative party, with a place in
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_Howard" title="Michael Howard">
        Michael Howard
       </a>
       ’s shadow cabinet. In 2003 he explained his editorial policy for
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       would "always be roughly speaking in favour of getting rid of Saddam, sticking up for Israel, free-market economics, expanding choice" and that the magazine was "not necessarily a Thatcherite Conservative or a neo-conservative magazine, even though in our editorial coverage we tend to follow roughly the conclusions of those lines of arguments".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In October 2004, a
       <i>
        Spectator
       </i>
       editorial suggested that the death of the hostage
       <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Bigley" title="Kenneth Bigley">
        Kenneth Bigley
       </a>
       was being over-sentimentalized by the people of Liverpool, accusing them of indulging in a "vicarious victimhood" and of possessing a "deeply unattractive psyche".’
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Johnson had not written the leader but, as editor, took full responsibility for it. Michael Howard subsequently ordered him to visit Liverpool on a "penitential pilgrimage".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At this time the paper began jokingly to be referred to as the ‘Sextator’ – a nickname for which Johnson himself was more than a little responsible – owing to the number of sex scandals connected with the magazine during his editorship. These included an affair between columnist
       <a href="/wiki/Rod_Liddle" title="Rod Liddle">
        Rod Liddle
       </a>
       and the magazine’s receptionist, and Johnson’s own affair with another columnist,
       <a href="/wiki/Petronella_Wyatt" title="Petronella Wyatt">
        Petronella Wyatt
       </a>
       . Johnson at first denied the relationship, dismissing the allegations as "an inverted pyramid of piffle", but was sacked from the Shadow Cabinet in November 2004 when they turned out to be true. In the same year
       <a href="/wiki/David_Blunkett" title="David Blunkett">
        David Blunkett
       </a>
       , the Home Secretary, resigned from the government after it emerged he had been having an affair with the publisher of
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kimberly_Quinn" title="Kimberly Quinn">
        Kimberly Quinn
       </a>
       , and had fast-tracked her nanny’s visa application.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Circulation under Johnson reached record levels – as high as 70,000 by the time he left the magazine in 2005 to join
       <a href="/wiki/David_Cameron" title="David Cameron">
        David Cameron
       </a>
       ’s Shadow Cabinet as Shadow Minister for Higher Education. On the announcement of his departure,
       <a href="/wiki/Andrew_Neil" title="Andrew Neil">
        Andrew Neil
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       CEO paid tribute to his editorship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">

      <h3>
       <span id="2006.E2.80.93present">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2006–present">
        2006–present
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [

      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span id="Matthew_d.E2.80.99Ancona">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Matthew_d’Ancona">
        Matthew d’Ancona
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Matthew d’Ancona">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_d%27Ancona" title="Matthew d'Ancona">
        D’Ancona
       </a>
       had been Deputy Editor at
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Telegraph" title="The Sunday Telegraph">
         The Sunday Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       , and before that an assistant editor at
       <i>
        The Times
       </i>
       . During his four years as editor of
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       , he made several editorial and structural changes to the magazine, "not all of which were universally popular with readers".
      </p>
      <p>
       He ended the traditional summary of the week’s events, "Portrait of the Week", and, in 2006, launched a new lifestyle section entitled "You Earned It". He removed
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Oborne" title="Peter Oborne">
        Peter Oborne
       </a>
       as political editor, and appointed
       <a href="/wiki/Fraser_Nelson" title="Fraser Nelson">
        Fraser Nelson
       </a>
       in his place. He decided not to appoint a new media columnist to succeed
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Glover" title="Stephen Glover">
        Stephen Glover
       </a>
       , explaining, "I do not think
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       needs a media columnist. Our pages are precious and I do not think the internal wranglings of our trade are high on the list of
       <i>
        Spectator
       </i>
       readers’ priorities."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perhaps the magazine's most important innovation under d’Ancona was the Coffee House blog, led by
       <a href="/wiki/Peter_Hoskin" title="Peter Hoskin">
        Peter Hoskin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/James_Forsyth_(journalist)" title="James Forsyth (journalist)">
        James Forsyth
       </a>
       , launched in May 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-the_new_Editor_of_The_Spectator_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-the_new_Editor_of_The_Spectator-33">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2007
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       moved its offices from Doughty Street, which had been its home for 31 years, to 22 Old Queen Street in Westminster, leaving Bloomsbury for the first time since the paper’s founding in 1828.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fraser_Nelson">
        Fraser Nelson
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Fraser Nelson">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       ’s current editor is
       <a href="/wiki/Fraser_Nelson" title="Fraser Nelson">
        Fraser Nelson
       </a>
       , who replaced d’Ancona in August 2009.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2010 he unveiled a slight redesign of the paper, shrinking the cover illustration slightly, shifting the cover lines, in general, to the bottom, and spreading the contents section over a double-page. Playing down the changes, Nelson described the new look as "a tidy-up ... rather like restoring an old painting."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An article in November 2011 by
       <a href="/wiki/Rod_Liddle" title="Rod Liddle">
        Rod Liddle
       </a>
       on the trial of two men eventually convicted for the murder of
       <a href="/wiki/Murder_of_Stephen_Lawrence" title="Murder of Stephen Lawrence">
        Stephen Lawrence
       </a>
       led to the
       <a href="/wiki/Crown_Prosecution_Service" title="Crown Prosecution Service">
        Crown Prosecution Service
       </a>
       (CPS) deciding to prosecute the magazine for breaching reporting restrictions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The magazine chose not to contest the case,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the publisher Spectator 1828 Ltd pleaded guilty at the court hearing at Westminster Magistrates Court on 7 June 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The magazine was fined £3,000, with £2,000 compensation awarded to Stephen Lawrence's parents and £625 costs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to Nelson, readers' most common reaction to the columnist was "don't tone down Rod", but "our non-readers don't like" him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In June 2013, The Spectator Archive was launched,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       containing 1.5 million pages from 180 years of published articles.
      </p>
      <p>
       In August 2015,
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       received media attention and criticism after publishing an article by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Moore_(journalist)" title="Charles Moore (journalist)">
        Charles Moore
       </a>
       regarding the
       <a href="/wiki/Labour_Party_(UK)_leadership_election,_2015" title="Labour Party (UK) leadership election, 2015">
        2015 Labour Party leadership election
       </a>
       titled "Have Yvette Cooper and Liz Kendall got the looks for a leadership contest?", in which he wrote "there is an understanding that no leader - especially, despite the age of equality, a woman - can look grotesque on television and win a general election" and discussed the looks of the two female candidates in detail. The article was condemned by
       <a href="/wiki/Liz_Kendall" title="Liz Kendall">
        Liz Kendall
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/First_Minister_of_Scotland" title="First Minister of Scotland">
        First Minister of Scotland
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nicola_Sturgeon" title="Nicola Sturgeon">
        Nicola Sturgeon
       </a>
       ; Candidate for Labour nomination for
       <a href="/wiki/Mayor_of_London" title="Mayor of London">
        Mayor of London
       </a>
       and former Minister and MP
       <a href="/wiki/Tessa_Jowell" title="Tessa Jowell">
        Tessa Jowell
       </a>
       ; along with several journalists and MPs from various parties
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Policy_positions">
        Policy positions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Policy positions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Like its sister publication
       <i>
        The Daily Telegraph
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       is generally
       <a href="/wiki/Atlanticism" title="Atlanticism">
        Atlanticist
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Euroscepticism" title="Euroscepticism">
        Eurosceptic
       </a>
       in outlook, favouring close ties with the United States rather than with the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Union" title="European Union">
        European Union
       </a>
       , and supportive of
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       It also strongly opposes
       <a href="/wiki/Scottish_independence" title="Scottish independence">
        Scottish independence
       </a>
       . However, it has expressed strong doubts about the
       <a href="/wiki/Iraq_War" title="Iraq War">
        Iraq War
       </a>
       , and some of its contributors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Parris" title="Matthew Parris">
        Matthew Parris
       </a>
       and Stuart Reid, express a more old-school conservative position. Some contributors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Irwin_Stelzer" title="Irwin Stelzer">
        Irwin Stelzer
       </a>
       , argue from an American
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neoconservative" title="Neoconservative">
        neoconservative
       </a>
       position.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Unlike much of the British press it is approving of the unilateral extradition treaty that allowed the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natwest_three" title="Natwest three">
        Natwest three
       </a>
       to be extradited, and in July 2006 the magazine devoted a leading article to praising the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Senate" title="US Senate">
        US Senate
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cultural_positions">
        Cultural positions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Spectator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Cultural positions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       is one of the few British publications that tends to be cautious of idolising examples of
       <a href="/wiki/Popular_culture" title="Popular culture">
        popular culture
       </a>
       , in the way that (for example)
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Telegraph" title="The Daily Telegraph">
         The Daily Telegraph
        </a>
       </i>
       did under
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Deedes" title="Bill Deedes">
        Bill Deedes
       </a>
       , or
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Times" title="The Times">
         The Times
        </a>
       </i>
       did under
       <a href="/wiki/William_Haley" title="William Haley">
        William Haley
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2009)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The magazine coined the phrase "
       <a href="/wiki/Young_fogey" title="Young fogey">
        young fogey
       </a>
       " in 1984.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability">
          <span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (September 2016)">
           not in citation given
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        The Spectator
       </i>
       does have a popular music column, though it only appears every four weeks, while a cinema column contains a review of one film each week by
       <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Ross_(journalist)" title="Deborah Ross (journalist)">
        Deborah Ross
       </a>
       . By contrast, opera, fine art, books, poetry and classical music all receive extensive weekly coverage.
      </p>
