   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Double planet
   </h1>

      <p>
       In astronomy, a
       <b>
        double planet
       </b>
       (also
       <b>
        binary planet
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_system_(astronomy)" title="Binary system (astronomy)">
        binary system
       </a>
       where both
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        objects
       </a>
       are of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planemo" title="Planemo">
        planetary mass
       </a>
       . The term is not recognized by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU) and is therefore not an official classification. At its 2006 General Assembly, the International Astronomical Union considered a proposal that
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       be reclassified as a double planet,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draft_Resolution_5_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Draft_Resolution_5-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the proposal was abandoned in favor of the current definition of
       <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet" title="IAU definition of planet">
        planet
       </a>
       . In promotional materials advertising the
       <a href="/wiki/SMART-1" title="SMART-1">
        SMART-1
       </a>
       mission and pre-dating the IAU planet definition, the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       once referred to the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       –
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       system as a double planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-esa-double_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-esa-double-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_asteroid" title="Binary asteroid">
        binary asteroids
       </a>
       with components of roughly equal mass are sometimes informally referred to as double
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_group" title="Asteroid group">
        minor planets
       </a>
       . These include binary asteroids
       <a href="/wiki/69230_Hermes" title="69230 Hermes">
        69230 Hermes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/90_Antiope" title="90 Antiope">
        90 Antiope
       </a>
       and binary
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt_object" title="Kuiper belt object">
        Kuiper belt objects
       </a>
       (KBOs)
       <a href="/wiki/79360_Sila%E2%80%93Nunam" title="79360 Sila–Nunam">
        79360 Sila–Nunam
       </a>
       and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/1998_WW31" title="1998 WW31">
         1998 WW
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           31
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>

       <span id="Definition_of_.22double_planet.22">
       </span>

      </h2>

      <p>
       There is debate as to what criteria should be used to distinguish "double planet" from a "planet–moon system". The following are considerations.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Both_bodies_satisfy_planet_criterion">
        Both bodies satisfy planet criterion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Both bodies satisfy planet criterion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A definition proposed in the Astronomical Journal calls for both bodies to individually satisfy an orbit-clearing criterion in order to be called a double planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_ratios_closer_to_1">
        Mass ratios closer to 1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mass ratios closer to 1">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One important consideration in defining "double planet" is the ratio of the masses of the two bodies. A mass ratio of 1 would indicate bodies of equal mass, and bodies with mass ratios closer to 1 are more attractive to label as "doubles." Using this definition, the satellites of Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune can all easily be excluded; they all have masses less than 0.00025 (​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         4000
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) of the planets around which they revolve. Some
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planets
       </a>
       , too, have satellites substantially less massive than the dwarf planets themselves.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most notable exception is the Pluto–Charon system. The Charon-to-Pluto mass ratio of 0.117 (≈ ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         17
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) is close enough to 1 that Pluto and Charon have frequently been described by many scientists as "double dwarf planets" ("double planets" prior to the 2006 definition of "planet"). The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU) currently calls Charon a satellite of Pluto, but has explicitly expressed a willingness to reconsider the bodies double dwarf planets at a future time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon-to-Earth mass ratio of 0.01230 (≈ ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         81
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) is also notably close to 1 when compared to all other satellite-to-planet ratios. Consequently, some scientists view the Earth-Moon system as a double planet as well, though this is a minority view.
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       's lone satellite,
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       , has a radius somewhere around ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </span>
       that of
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       ; assuming similar densities (Dysnomia's compositional make-up may or may not differ substantially from Eris's), the mass ratio would be near ​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         40
        </sub>
       </span>
       , a value intermediate to the Moon–Earth and Charon–Pluto ratios.
      </p>
      <p>
       The next criteria both attempt to answer the question "How close to 1 must the mass ratio be?"
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Center-of-mass_position">
        Center-of-mass position
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Center-of-mass position">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Currently, the most commonly proposed definition for a double-planet system is one in which the
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycenter
       </a>
       , around which both bodies orbit, lies outside both bodies. Under this definition, Pluto and Charon are double (dwarf) planets, since they orbit a point clearly outside of Pluto, as visible in animations created from images of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       space probe in June 2015.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under this definition, the Earth–Moon system is not currently a double planet; although the Moon is massive enough to cause the Earth to make a noticeable revolution around this center of mass, this point nevertheless lies well within Earth. However, the Moon migrates outward from Earth at a rate of approximately 3.8 cm (1.5 in) per year; in a few hundred million years, the Earth–Moon system's center of mass will lie outside Earth, which would make it a double-planet system.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is interesting to note that the center of mass of the Jupiter–Sun system lies outside the surface of the Sun, though arguing that Jupiter is a double star is
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       analogous to arguing Charon is a double planet; the problem is that one cannot argue that Jupiter is even a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       —even a
       <a href="/wiki/Brown_dwarf" title="Brown dwarf">
        brown dwarf
       </a>
       —due to its low mass and associated inability to support any type of fusion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tug-of-war_value">
        Tug-of-war value
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tug-of-war value">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
        Isaac Asimov
       </a>
       suggested a distinction between planet–moon and double-planet structures based in part on what he called a "
       <a href="/wiki/Tug_of_war_(astronomy)" title="Tug of war (astronomy)">
        tug-of-war
       </a>
       " value, which does not consider their relative sizes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asimov_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Asimov-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This quantity is simply the ratio of the force exerted on the smaller body by the larger (primary) body to the force exerted on the smaller body by the Sun. This
       <a href="/wiki/Tug_of_war_(astronomy)#Law_of_universal_gravitation" title="Tug of war (astronomy)">
        can be shown
       </a>
       to equal
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle {\text{tug-of-war value}}={\frac {m_{p}}{m_{s}}}\cdot \left({\frac {d_{s}}{d_{p}}}\right)^{2}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mtext>
                tug-of-war value
               </mtext>
              </mrow>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mfrac>
                <msub>
                 <mi>
                  m
                 </mi>

                  <mi>
                   p
                  </mi>
                 </mrow>
                </msub>
                <msub>
                 <mi>
                  m
                 </mi>
                 <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                  <mi>
                   s
                  </mi>
                 </mrow>
                </msub>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
              <mo>
               ⋅
               <!-- ⋅ -->
              </mo>
              <msup>
               <mrow>
                <mo>
                 (
                </mo>
                <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                 <mfrac>
                  <msub>
                   <mi>
                    d
                   </mi>
                   <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                    <mi>
                     s
                    </mi>
                   </mrow>
                  </msub>
                  <msub>
                   <mi>
                    d
                   </mi>
                   <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">

                    </mi>

                  </msub>

                </mrow>

                </mo>
               </mrow>
               <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                <mn>
                 2

               </mrow>
              </msup>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle {\text{tug-of-war value}}={\frac {m_{p}}{m_{s}}}\cdot \left({\frac {d_{s}}{d_{p}}}\right)^{2}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="\text{tug-of-war value} = \frac{m_p}{m_s} \cdot \left( \frac{d_s}{d_p} \right)^2" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0634efb5654051453266a38828474d07c0c4717f" style="vertical-align: -2.505ex; width:32.77ex; height:6.509ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       where m
       <sub>
        p
       </sub>
       is the mass of the primary (the larger body), m
       <sub>
        s
       </sub>
       is the mass of the Sun, d
       <sub>
        s
       </sub>
       is the distance between the smaller body and the Sun, and d
       <sub>
        p
       </sub>
       is the distance between the smaller body and the primary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asimov_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Asimov-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Note that the tug-of-war value does not rely on the mass of the satellite (the smaller body).
      </p>
      <p>
       This formula actually reflects the relation of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational
       </a>
       effects on the smaller body from the larger body and from the Sun. The tug-of-war figure for Saturn's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       is 380, which means that Saturn's hold on Titan is 380 times as strong as the Sun's hold on Titan. Titan's tug-of-war value may be compared with that of Saturn's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , which has a tug-of-war value of just 3.5. So Saturn's hold on Phoebe is only 3.5 times as strong as the Sun's hold on Phoebe.
      </p>
      <p>
       Asimov calculated tug-of-war values for several satellites of the planets. He showed that even the largest gas giant, Jupiter, had only a slightly better hold than the Sun on its outer captured satellites, some with tug-of-war values not much higher than one. In nearly every one of Asimov's calculations the tug-of-war value was found to be greater than one, so in those cases the Sun loses the tug-of-war with the planets. The one exception was Earth's Moon, where the Sun wins the tug-of-war with a value of 0.46, which means that Earth's hold on the Moon is less than half that of the Sun's. Asimov included this with his other arguments that Earth and the Moon should be considered a binary planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asimov_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Asimov-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        We might look upon the Moon, then, as neither a true satellite of the Earth nor a captured one, but as a planet in its own right, moving about the Sun in careful step with the Earth. From within the Earth–Moon system, the simplest way of picturing the situation is to have the Moon revolve about the Earth; but if you were to draw a picture of the orbits of the Earth and Moon about the Sun exactly to scale, you would see that the Moon's orbit is everywhere concave toward the Sun. It is always "falling toward" the Sun. All the other satellites, without exception, "fall away" from the Sun through part of their orbits, caught as they are by the superior pull of their primary planets – but not the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Asimov_5-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Asimov-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aslaksen_6-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Aslaksen-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PoV_7-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-PoV-7">
          [Footnote 1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Isaac Asimov
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       See the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit_of_the_Moon#Path_of_Earth_and_Moon_around_Sun" title="Orbit of the Moon">
        Path of Earth and Moon around Sun
       </a>
       section in the "Orbit of the Moon" article for a more detailed explanation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that this definition of double planet depends on the pair's distance from the Sun. If the Earth–Moon system happened to orbit farther away from the Sun than it does now, then Earth would win the tug of war. For example, at the orbit of Mars, the Moon's tug-of-war value would be 1.05. Also, several tiny moons discovered since Asimov's proposal would qualify as double planets by this argument. Neptune's small outer moons
       <a href="/wiki/Neso_(moon)" title="Neso (moon)">
        Neso
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Psamathe_(moon)" title="Psamathe (moon)">
        Psamathe
       </a>
       , for example, have tug-of-war values of 0.42 and 0.44, less than that of Earth's Moon. Yet their masses are tiny compared to Neptune's, with an estimated ratio of 1.5
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         9
        </span>
       </sup>
       (​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         700,000,000
        </sub>
       </span>
       ) and 0.4
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         9
        </span>
       </sup>
       (​
       <span class="frac nowrap">
        <sup>
         1
        </sup>
        ⁄
        <sub>
         2,500,000,000
        </sub>
       </span>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_the_system">
        Formation of the system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Double_planet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation of the system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A final consideration is the way in which the two bodies came to form a system. Both the Earth-Moon and Pluto-Charon systems are thought to have been formed as a result of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impacts
       </a>
       : one body was impacted by a second body, resulting in a debris disk, and through accretion, either two new bodies formed or one new body formed, with the larger body remaining (but changed). However, a giant impact is not a sufficient condition for two bodies being "double planets" because such impacts can also produce tiny satellites, such as the four, small, outer satellites of Pluto.
      </p>
      <p>
       A now-abandoned hypothesis for the
       <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_Moon" title="Origin of the Moon">
        origin of the Moon
       </a>
       was actually called the "double-planet hypothesis"; the idea was that the Earth and the Moon formed in the same region of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
        solar system
       </a>
       's proto-planetary disk, forming a system under gravitational interaction. This idea, too, is a problematic condition for defining two bodies as "double planets" because planets can "capture" moons through gravitational interaction. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Mars" title="Moons of Mars">
        moons of Mars
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       ) are thought to be asteroids captured long ago by
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . Such a weak definition would also deem Neptune-Triton a double planet, since
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       was a
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       body the same size and of similar composition to Pluto, later captured by
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       .
      </p>
