   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Internal structure of the Moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Olivine_Basalt_15555_from_Apollo_15_in_National_Museum_of_Natural_History.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
          Olivine
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
          basalt
         </a>
         collected by
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
          Apollo 15
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Having a
       <a href="/wiki/Arithmetic_mean" title="Arithmetic mean">
        mean
       </a>
       density of 3,346.4
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilogram_per_metre_cubed" title="Kilogram per metre cubed">
        kg/m³
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">
        differentiated
       </a>
       body, being composed of a
       <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">
        geochemically
       </a>
       distinct
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_core" title="Planetary core">
        planetary core
       </a>
       . This structure is believed to have resulted from the
       <a href="/wiki/Fractional_crystallization_(geology)" title="Fractional crystallization (geology)">
        fractional crystallization
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">
        magma ocean
       </a>
       shortly after its formation about 4.5 billion years ago. The energy required to melt the outer portion of the Moon is commonly attributed to a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impact
       </a>
       event that is postulated to have formed the Earth-Moon system, and the subsequent reaccretion of material in Earth orbit. Crystallization of this magma ocean would have given rise to a mafic mantle and a
       <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">
        plagioclase
       </a>
       -rich crust.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geochemical mapping from orbit implies that the crust of the Moon is largely
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthositic
       </a>
       in composition,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-L06_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-L06-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       consistent with the magma ocean hypothesis. In terms of elements, the lunar crust is composed primarily of
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Silicon" title="Silicon">
        silicon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Magnesium" title="Magnesium">
        magnesium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">
        calcium
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
        aluminium
       </a>
       , but important minor and trace elements such as
       <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">
        titanium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">
        uranium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">
        thorium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       are present as well. Based on geophysical techniques, the crust is estimated to be on average about 50 km thick.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W06_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-W06-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Partial melting within the mantle of the Moon gave rise to the eruption of mare basalts on the lunar surface. Analyses of these basalts indicate that the mantle is composed predominantly of the minerals
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthopyroxene" title="Orthopyroxene">
        orthopyroxene
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clinopyroxene" title="Clinopyroxene">
        clinopyroxene
       </a>
       , and that the lunar mantle is more iron-rich than that of the Earth. Some lunar basalts contain high abundances of titanium (present in the mineral
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       ), suggesting that the mantle is highly heterogeneous in composition.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moonquake" title="Moonquake">
        Moonquakes
       </a>
       have been found to occur deep within the mantle of the Moon about 1,000 km below the surface. These occur with monthly periodicities and are related to tidal stresses caused by the eccentric orbit of the Moon about the Earth. A few shallow moonquakes with hypocenters located about 100 km below the surface have also been detected, but these occur more infrequently and appear to be unrelated to the lunar tides.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W06_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-W06-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Core">
        Core
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Internal_structure_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Core">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="547" data-file-width="840" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg/350px-Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg/525px-Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg/700px-Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg.png 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_Schematic_Cross_Section.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Schematic illustration of the internal structure of the Moon
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Several lines of evidence imply that the lunar core is small, with a radius of about 350 km or less.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W06_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-W06-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The size of the lunar core is only about 20% the size of the Moon itself, in contrast to about 50% as is the case for most other terrestrial bodies. The composition of the lunar core is not well constrained, but most believe that it is composed of metallic iron alloyed with a small amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nickel" title="Nickel">
        nickel
       </a>
       . Analyses of the Moon's time-variable rotations indicate that the core is at least partly molten.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2010, a reanalysis of the old
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package" title="Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package">
        Apollo
       </a>
       seismic data on the deep
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moonquake" title="Moonquake">
        moonquakes
       </a>
       using modern processing methods confirmed that the Moon has an iron rich core with a radius of
       <span class="nowrap">
        330 ± 20 km
       </span>
       . The same reanalysis established that the solid inner core made of pure iron has the radius of
       <span class="nowrap">
        240 ± 10 km
       </span>
       . The core is surrounded by the partially (10 to 30%) melted layer of the lower mantle with a radius of
       <span class="nowrap">
        480 ± 20 km
       </span>
       (thickness ~150 km). These results imply that 40% of the core by volume has solidified. The density of the liquid outer core is about 5 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       and it could contain as much 6%
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       by weight. The temperature in the core is probably about 1600–1700 K.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Weber2010_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Weber2010-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
