      <p>
       <b>
        Dynamics
       </b>
       is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Branch_(academia)#Physics" title="Branch (academia)">
        branch
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Applied_mathematics" title="Applied mathematics">
        applied mathematics
       </a>
       (specifically
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_mechanics" title="Classical mechanics">
        classical mechanics
       </a>
       ) concerned with the study of
       <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
        forces
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torques
       </a>
       and their effect on
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">
        motion
       </a>
       , as opposed to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kinematics" title="Kinematics">
         kinematics
        </a>
       </i>
       , which studies the motion of objects without reference to its causes.
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Isaac Newton
       </a>
       defined the fundamental
       <a href="/wiki/Physical_law" title="Physical law">
        physical laws
       </a>
       which govern dynamics in physics, especially his
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Second_law_of_motion" title="Second law of motion">
        second law of motion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Principles">
        Principles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamics_(mechanics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Principles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Generally speaking, researchers involved in dynamics study how a physical system might develop or alter over time and study the causes of those changes. In addition, Newton established the fundamental physical laws which govern dynamics in physics. By studying his system of mechanics, dynamics can be understood. In particular, dynamics is mostly related to Newton's second law of motion. However, all three laws of motion are taken into account because these are interrelated in any given observation or experiment.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Linear_and_rotational_dynamics">
        Linear and rotational dynamics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamics_(mechanics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Linear and rotational dynamics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The study of dynamics falls under two categories: linear and rotational. Linear dynamics pertains to objects moving in a line and involves such quantities as
       <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
        force
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Inertia#Mass_and_inertia" title="Inertia">
        inertia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Displacement_(vector)" title="Displacement (vector)">
        displacement
       </a>
       (in units of distance),
       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
        velocity
       </a>
       (distance per unit time),
       <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
        acceleration
       </a>
       (distance per unit of time squared) and
       <a href="/wiki/Momentum" title="Momentum">
        momentum
       </a>
       (mass times unit of velocity). Rotational dynamics pertains to objects that are rotating or moving in a curved path and involves such quantities as
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torque
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">
        moment of inertia
       </a>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_inertia" title="Rotational inertia">
        rotational inertia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_displacement" title="Angular displacement">
        angular displacement
       </a>
       (in radians or less often, degrees),
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_velocity" title="Angular velocity">
        angular velocity
       </a>
       (radians per unit time),
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_acceleration" title="Angular acceleration">
        angular acceleration
       </a>
       (radians per unit of time squared) and
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       (moment of inertia times unit of angular velocity). Very often, objects exhibit linear and rotational motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       For classical
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetism" title="Electromagnetism">
        electromagnetism
       </a>
       , it is
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">
        Maxwell's equations
       </a>
       that describe the dynamics. And the dynamics of classical systems involving both mechanics and electromagnetism are described by the combination of Newton's laws, Maxwell's equations, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">
        Lorentz force
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Force">
        Force
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamics_(mechanics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Force">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Force" title="Force">
        Force
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       From Newton, force can be defined as an exertion or
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       which can cause an object to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accelerate" title="Accelerate">
        accelerate
       </a>
       . The concept of force is used to describe an influence which causes a
       <a href="/wiki/Free_body" title="Free body">
        free body
       </a>
       (object) to accelerate. It can be a push or a pull, which causes an object to change direction, have new
       <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
        velocity
       </a>
       , or to
       <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">
        deform
       </a>
       temporarily or permanently. Generally speaking, force causes an object's
       <a href="/wiki/Motion_(physics)" title="Motion (physics)">
        state of motion
       </a>
       to change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phys-tut-force_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Phys-tut-force-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Newton.27s_laws">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Newton's_laws">
        Newton's laws
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Dynamics_(mechanics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Newton's laws">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">
        Newton's laws of motion
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Newton described force as the ability to cause a mass to accelerate. His three laws can be summarized as follows:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <b>
         First law
        </b>
        : If there is no net force on an object, then its
        <a href="/wiki/Velocity" title="Velocity">
         velocity
        </a>
        is constant. The object is either at rest (if its velocity is equal to zero), or it moves with constant speed in a single direction.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first-law-shaums_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-first-law-shaums-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-first-law-dmmy_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-first-law-dmmy-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Second law
        </b>
        : The rate of change of linear momentum
        <b>
         P
        </b>
        of an object is equal to the net force
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         net
        </sub>
        , i.e., d
        <b>
         P
        </b>
        /dt =
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         net
        </sub>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Third law
        </b>
        : When a first body exerts a force
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        on a second body, the second body simultaneously exerts a force
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        = −
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        on the first body. This means that
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
        and
        <b>
         F
        </b>
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        are equal in magnitude and opposite in direction.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       Newton's Laws of Motion are valid only in an inertial frame of reference
      </p>
