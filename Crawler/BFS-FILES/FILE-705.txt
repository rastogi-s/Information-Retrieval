   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Solar maximum
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

         The current prediction for Sunspot Cycle 24 gives a smoothed sunspot number maximum of about 66 in the Summer of 2013. The smoothed sunspot number has already reached 67 (in February 2012) due to the strong peak in late 2011 so the official maximum will be at least this high. The smoothed sunspot number has been flat over the last four months. We are currently over four years into Cycle 24. The current predicted and observed size makes this the smallest sunspot cycle since Cycle 14 which had a maximum of 64.2 in February 1906.
         <a class="external text" href="http://solarscience.msfc.nasa.gov/predict.shtml" rel="nofollow">
          NASA
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Solar maximum
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        solar max
       </b>
       is a regular period of greatest
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       activity during the 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       . During solar maximum, large numbers of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       appear, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_irradiance" title="Solar irradiance">
        solar irradiance
       </a>
       output grows by about 0.07%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solar-climate_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-solar-climate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The increased energy output of solar maxima can impact Earth's global
       <a href="/wiki/Climate" title="Climate">
        climate
       </a>
       , and recent studies have shown some correlation with regional weather patterns.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At solar maximum, the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       lines are the most distorted due to the magnetic field on the solar equator rotating at a slightly faster pace than at the solar poles.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       On average, the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       takes about 11 years to go from one solar maximum to the next, with duration observed varying from 9 to 14 years.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar-cycle-data.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="466" data-file-width="700" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png/400px-Solar-cycle-data.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png/600px-Solar-cycle-data.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0d/Solar-cycle-data.png 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar-cycle-data.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Three recent solar cycles
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Large
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       often occur during a maximum. For example, the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        solar storm of 1859
       </a>
       struck the Earth with such intensity that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
        northern lights
       </a>
       were visible as far from the poles as
       <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">
        Cuba
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Hawaii" title="Hawaii">
        Hawaii
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Predictions">
        Predictions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Predictions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Predictions of a future maximum's timing and strength are very difficult; predictions vary widely. There was a solar maximum in 2000. In 2006
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       initially expected a solar maximum in 2010 or 2011, and thought that it could be the strongest since
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_19" title="Solar cycle 19">
        1958
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the solar maximum was not declared to have occurred until 2014, and even then was ranked among the weakest on record.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solarminimax_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-solarminimax-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Grand_solar_minima_and_maxima">
        Grand solar minima and maxima
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_maximum&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Grand solar minima and maxima">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_variation" title="Solar variation">
        Solar variation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Grand solar maxima occur when several solar cycles exhibit greater than average activity for decades or centuries. Solar cycles still occur during these grand solar maximum periods but the intensity of those cycles is greater. Grand solar maxima have shown some correlation with global and regional climate changes.
      </p>
