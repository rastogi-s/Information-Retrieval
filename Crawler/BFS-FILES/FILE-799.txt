      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       has been the subject of many works of art and literature and the inspiration for countless others. It is a motif in the visual arts, the performing arts, poetry, prose and music.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fantasy">
        Fantasy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fantasy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In many
       <a href="/wiki/Fantasy" title="Fantasy">
        fantasy
       </a>
       stories, a full moon means
       <a href="/wiki/Vampire" title="Vampire">
        vampires
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Werewolf" title="Werewolf">
        werewolves
       </a>
       running loose.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary">
        Literary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tale_of_the_Bamboo_Cutter" title="The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter">
         The Tale of the Bamboo Cutter
        </a>
        , a 10th-century
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_folktale" title="Japanese folktale">
         Japanese folktale
        </a>
        , tells of a mysterious Moon Princess growing up on Earth as the adopted daughter of a bamboo cutter and his wife, dazzling human Princes and the Emperor himself with her beauty, and finally going back to her people at "The Capital of the Moon" (Tsuki-no-Miyako 月の都), leaving many broken hearts on Earth. It is among the first texts of any culture assuming the Moon to be an inhabited world and describing travel between it and the Earth.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/John_Heywood" title="John Heywood">
         John Heywood
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         Proverbes
        </i>
        (1546) coined the famous phrase that "The moon is made of a greene cheese", "greene" meaning "not aged", but Heywood was probably being sarcastic.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        One of the earliest fictional flights to the Moon took place on the pages of
        <a href="/wiki/Ludovico_Ariosto" title="Ludovico Ariosto">
         Ludovico Ariosto
        </a>
        's well-known
        <a href="/wiki/Italian_literature" title="Italian literature">
         Italian
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epic_poem" title="Epic poem">
         epic poem
        </a>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Orlando_Furioso" title="Orlando Furioso">
          Orlando Furioso
         </a>
        </i>
        (1516). The protagonist Orlando, having been thwarted in love, goes mad with despair and rampages through Europe and Africa, destroying everything in his path. The English knight
        <a href="/wiki/Astolfo" title="Astolfo">
         Astolfo
        </a>
        , seeking to find a cure for Orlando's madness, flies up to the Moon in
        <a href="/wiki/Elijah" title="Elijah">
         Elijah
        </a>
        's flaming chariot. In this depiction, the Moon is where everything lost on earth is to be found, including Orlando's wits, and Astolfo brings them back in a bottle and makes Orlando sniff them, thus restoring him to sanity.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Edward_Young" title="Edward Young">
         Edward Young
        </a>
        's poem entitled
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Night-Thoughts" title="Night-Thoughts">
          The Complaint, and the Consolation; or, Night Thoughts
         </a>
        </i>
        (1742-1745), was a favorite of poets and painters of
        <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">
         Romanticism
        </a>
        including
        <a href="/wiki/William_Blake" title="William Blake">
         William Blake
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Palmer" title="Samuel Palmer">
         Samuel Palmer
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attlee_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Attlee-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Attlee_2011_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Attlee_2011-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Great_Moon_Hoax" title="Great Moon Hoax">
         Great Moon Hoax
        </a>
        of 1835, a newspaper reporter concocted a series of stories purporting to describe the discovery of life on the Moon, talking of such creatures as winged humanoids and goats.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Liudmyla_Starytska-Cherniakhivska" title="Liudmyla Starytska-Cherniakhivska">
         Liudmyla Starytska-Cherniakhivska’s
        </a>
        <i>
         Living Grave: A Ukrainian Legend
        </i>
        [Zhyva Mohyla: Ukrainska Lehenda], first published in 1889, the moon is often referred as the '
        <a href="/wiki/Cossacks" title="Cossacks">
         kozak
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Cossacks" title="Cossacks">
         cossack
        </a>
        ) sun'.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Johnny_Gruelle" title="Johnny Gruelle">
         Johnny Gruelle
        </a>
        's 1922 children's book,
        <i>
         The Magical Land of Noom
        </i>
        , relates the adventures of two Earth children among the inhabitants of the
        <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
         far side of the Moon
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Roverandom" title="Roverandom">
          Roverandom
         </a>
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">
         J. R. R. Tolkien
        </a>
        was written in 1925 to console his son Michael, then four years old, for the loss of a beloved toy dog. In the story, the dog has flown to the Moon and had a whole series of amusing adventures there. The story was only published posthumously. In addition,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_(Middle-earth)" title="Moon (Middle-earth)">
         Isil
        </a>
        and the guidesman
        <a href="/wiki/Tilion" title="Tilion">
         Tilion
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">
         J. R. R. Tolkien
        </a>
        's fictional
        <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">
         Middle-earth
        </a>
        cosmology are based in Tolkien's familiarity with Norse and Gaelic myths of the moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Dolittle_in_the_Moon" title="Doctor Dolittle in the Moon">
          Doctor Dolittle in the Moon
         </a>
        </i>

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Lucian" title="Lucian">
        Lucian
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Icaromenippus&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Icaromenippus (page does not exist)">
         Icaromenippus
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/True_History" title="True History">
         True History
        </a>
       </i>
       , written in the 2nd century AD, deal with
       <a href="/wiki/Imaginary_voyage" title="Imaginary voyage">
        imaginary voyages
       </a>
       to the moon such as on a fountain after going past the
       <a href="/wiki/Pillars_of_Hercules" title="Pillars of Hercules">
        Pillars of Hercules
       </a>
       . The theme did not become popular until the 17th century, however, when the invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       hastened the popular acceptance of the concept of "a world in the Moon", that is, that the Moon was an inhabitable planet, which might be reached via some sort of aërial carriage. The concept of another world, close to our own and capable of looking down at it from a distance, provided ample scope for satirical comments on the manners of the Earthly world. Among the early stories dealing with this concept are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>

        </i>

        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Juan_Maldonado_(humanist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Juan Maldonado (humanist) (page does not exist)">
         Juan Maldonado
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Somnium_(novel)" title="Somnium (novel)">
          The Dream (Somnium)
         </a>
        </i>
        (1634) by
        <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">
         Johannes Kepler
        </a>
        (written before 1610, but not published during Kepler's life). An Icelandic voyager is transported to the Moon by aërial demons; an occasion for Kepler to offer some of his astronomical theories in the guise of fiction.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Man_in_the_Moone" title="The Man in the Moone">
          The Man in the Moone
         </a>
        </i>
        (1638) by
        <a href="/wiki/Francis_Godwin" title="Francis Godwin">
         Francis Godwin
        </a>

       </li>

         John Wilkins
        </a>

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>

       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.gutenberg.org/etext/7089" rel="nofollow">

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_voyage">
        First voyage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: First voyage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The first flight to the Moon was a popular topic of science fiction before the actual landing in 1969.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/From_the_Earth_to_the_Moon" title="From the Earth to the Moon">
          From the Earth to the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1865) and its sequel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
          Around the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1870) by
        <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
         Jules Verne
        </a>
        , in which a projectile is launched from
        <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
         Florida
        </a>
        and lands in the
        <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
         Pacific Ocean
        </a>
        , not unlike in the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
         Apollo program
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_orbit_rendezvous" title="Lunar orbit rendezvous">
         lunar orbit rendezvous
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         Les Exilés de la Terre
        </i>
        (Exiled from Earth, 1887), by
        <a href="/wiki/Paschal_Grousset" title="Paschal Grousset">
         Paschal Grousset
        </a>
        (writing as André Laurie), a
        <a href="/wiki/Sudan" title="Sudan">
         Sudanese
        </a>
        mountain composed of pure
        <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
         iron
        </a>
        ore is converted into a huge
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electro-magnet" title="Electro-magnet">
         electro-magnet
        </a>
        and catapulted to the Moon where the protagonists have various adventures.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
          The First Men in the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1901) by
        <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
         H. G. Wells
        </a>
        in which a spaceship gets to the moon with the aid of Cavorite -a material which shields out
        <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
         gravity
        </a>
        . It is inhabited by insect-like Selenites who are ruled by a Grand Lunar, and who prevent Cavor from returning to Earth after learning of humanity's warlike nature.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Na srebrnym globie
        </i>
        [The Silver Globe] (1903), by Polish writer
        <a href="/wiki/Jerzy_%C5%BBu%C5%82awski" title="Jerzy Żuławski">
         Jerzy Żuławski
        </a>
        in which a first expedition from Earth gives birth to a lunar society. The story was continued in
        <i>
         Zwycięzca
        </i>
        [The Conqueror] (1910) and
        <i>
         Stara Ziemia
        </i>
        [The Old Earth] (1911). This so-called
        <i>
         Lunar Trilogy
        </i>
        was the first modern Polish SF story. It was adapted to the screen as
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/On_the_Silver_Globe_(film)" title="On the Silver Globe (film)">
          On the Silver Globe
         </a>
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Andrzej_%C5%BBu%C5%82awski" title="Andrzej Żuławski">
         Andrzej Żuławski
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trends_(Asimov)" title="Trends (Asimov)">
          Trends
         </a>
        </i>
        is a 1939 short story by
        <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
         Isaac Asimov
        </a>
        in which religious fanatics oppose a fictional first flight to the Moon in the 1970s.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Prelude_to_Space" title="Prelude to Space">
          Prelude to Space
         </a>
        </i>
        is a 1951 novel by
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
         Arthur C. Clarke
        </a>
        recounting the events leading up to a fictional first flight to the Moon in 1978.
       </li>

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Robert_A._Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Robert A. Heinlein">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       wrote extensively, prolifically, and inter-connectedly about first voyages and colonization of the Moon, which he most often called Luna.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also was involved with the films
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Project_Moonbase" title="Project Moonbase">
         Project Moonbase
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Requiem_(short_story)" title="Requiem (short story)">
         Requiem
        </a>
        " 1940. A lyrical story about Harriman, the man who financed the first Moon landing (see also "The Man Who Sold the Moon", below).
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Ship_Galileo" title="Rocket Ship Galileo">
          Rocket Ship Galileo
         </a>
        </i>
        1947. A physicist and several prodigy teenagers convert a sub-orbital rocket ship to reach the moon where they are profoundly surprised and have to act quickly to deal with a malignant menace.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Columbus_Was_a_Dope" title="Columbus Was a Dope">
         Columbus Was a Dope
        </a>
        ", as Lyle Monroe, 1947. In a bar on the Moon, a chance encounter reveals both deep and practical attitudes about space exploration.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Long_Watch" title="The Long Watch">
         The Long Watch
        </a>
        " (aka "Rebellion on the Moon", 1948). An officer in charge of a nuclear arsenal on the Moon makes tough decisions.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Gentlemen,_Be_Seated!" title="Gentlemen, Be Seated!">
         Gentlemen, Be Seated!
        </a>
        ", 1948. A dangerous leak develops in a lunar tunnel and the men devise a unique way to deal with it until a repair can be made.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Black_Pits_of_Luna" title="The Black Pits of Luna">
         The Black Pits of Luna
        </a>
        ", 1948. A Boy Scout visits cities on the Moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon" title="The Man Who Sold the Moon">
         The Man Who Sold the Moon
        </a>
        ", a 1949 short story, first published in 1951. In this story, a
        <a href="/wiki/Prequel" title="Prequel">
         prequel
        </a>
        to "Requiem" (above), events revolve around a fictional first Moon landing in 1978.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/Nothing_Ever_Happens_on_the_Moon" title="Nothing Ever Happens on the Moon">
         Nothing Ever Happens on the Moon

      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inhabited_Moon">
        Inhabited Moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Inhabited Moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The moon is sometimes imagined as having, now or in the distant past, indigenous life and civilization.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
          The First Men in the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1901) by
        <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
         H. G. Wells
        </a>
        , the moon is inhabited by insectoid "Selenites."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Lost Paradise
        </i>
        (1936) by
        <a href="/wiki/C._L._Moore" title="C. L. Moore">
         C. L. Moore
        </a>
        . This
        <a href="/wiki/Northwest_Smith" title="Northwest Smith">
         Northwest Smith
        </a>
        story tells how the once-fertile Moon became an airless wasteland.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/C._S._Lewis" title="C. S. Lewis">
         C. S. Lewis
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/That_Hideous_Strength" title="That Hideous Strength">
          That Hideous Strength
         </a>
        </i>
        (1945), the moon (Sulva) is described as being home to a race of extreme
        <a href="/wiki/Eugenics" title="Eugenics">
         eugenicists
        </a>
        . On the near side, the elite caste seems to have dispensed with organic existence altogether, by some means never clearly described; the only holdouts against this trend are an embattled minority on the far side. The response of the characters to this state of affairs varies according to their status: Professor Filostrato, of the wicked N.I.C.E., considers the Sulvans "[a] great race, further advanced than we", while the
        <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">
         Christian
        </a>
        champion
        <a href="/wiki/Elwin_Ransom" title="Elwin Ransom">
         Elwin Ransom
        </a>
        describes them as "an accursed people, full of pride and lust."
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         Badger's Moon
        </i>
        (1949) by
        <a href="/wiki/Elleston_Trevor" title="Elleston Trevor">
         Elleston Trevor
        </a>
        , four animals travel to the moon by rocket ship and meet the inhabitants.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         The ″Lomokome″ Papers
        </i>
        (1968) by
        <a href="/wiki/Herman_Wouk" title="Herman Wouk">
         Herman Wouk
        </a>
        . Lt. Daniel Butler is left marooned on the moon. A rescue ship finds a manuscript written by Lt. Butler where he tells a story of how he was held captive by people who live beneath the surface of the moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Looney" title="Matthew Looney">
          Matthew Looney
         </a>
        </i>
        series of children's books by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerome_Beatty_Jr" title="Jerome Beatty Jr">
         Jerome Beatty Jr
        </a>
        (written 1961 - 1978) is an amusing set of stories about an inhabited moon whose government is intent on invading the Earth.
       </li>
      </ul>

        </i>

        <a href="/wiki/Evangelion_(mecha)#Evangelion_Mark.06" title="Evangelion (mecha)">
         Evangelion Mark.06
        </a>
        .

        <i>

         </a>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Luna_(My_Little_Pony)" title="Princess Luna (My Little Pony)">
         Luna
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Princess_Celestia" title="Princess Celestia">
         Celestia
        </a>
        , respectively. A thousand years prior to the first episode, Luna grew jealous that the ponies living in the world slept during her night, and tried to make the night last forever, taking the name 'Nightmare Moon'. Celestia subsequently banished her to the moon, and arranged for the show's main characters to assist in redeeming her.

Cut to the Moon covered in dynamite seconds before it explodes, shocking the other Titans Starfire laughs/gargles before she really does down the milk carton, telling them that she only did it because Robin loved the Moon so much.

      <ul>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/DC_Universe" title="DC Universe">
         DC Universe
        </a>
        , the Moon is the location of the
        <a href="/wiki/Justice_League_Watchtower" title="Justice League Watchtower">
         Justice League Watchtower
        </a>
        until its destruction by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alexander_Luthor" title="Alexander Luthor">
         Alexander Luthor
        </a>
        and also a former home of
        <a href="/wiki/Eclipso" title="Eclipso">
         Eclipso
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        In an early
        <a href="/wiki/Ibis_the_Invincible" title="Ibis the Invincible">
         Ibis the Invincible
        </a>
        story the Moon has members of a humanoid race composed of stone that competed with humanity over the Earth and were exiled to the Moon thousands of years ago where they are frozen. A Professor makes a rocket ship to go to the Moon with Taia, and Ibis follows them. Two of the creatures are taken on the ship, and revive on a journey back to Earth, but are killed when the spaceship crashes.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/De_Avonturen_van_Pa_Pinkelman" title="De Avonturen van Pa Pinkelman">
          De Avonturen van Pa Pinkelman
         </a>
        </i>
        (1945) by
        <a href="/wiki/Godfried_Bomans" title="Godfried Bomans">
         Godfried Bomans
        </a>
        and drawn by
        <a href="/wiki/Carol_Voges" title="Carol Voges">
         Carol Voges
        </a>
        has the characters set foot on the moon, where they spent a long time and meet an entire society, even with his own national anthem.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Herg%C3%A9" title="Hergé">
         Hergé
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(comics)" title="Destination Moon (comics)">
         <i>
          Destination Moon
         </i>
        </a>
        and
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Explorers_on_the_Moon" title="Explorers on the Moon">
          Explorers on the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        (1953–1954),
        <a href="/wiki/Tintin_(character)" title="Tintin (character)">
         Tintin
        </a>
        and his companions make the first voyage to the moon and Tintin becomes the first explorer on the Moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Nero" title="The Adventures of Nero">
          The Adventures of Nero
         </a>
        </i>
        story "De Daverende Pitteleer" (1959) by
        <a href="/wiki/Marc_Sleen" title="Marc Sleen">
         Marc Sleen
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Nero_(comic_book_character)" title="Nero (comic book character)">
         Nero
        </a>
        and his friends accidentally land on the Moon. They meet a moon man there too, before continuing their flight to their original destination on Earth. The Moon is depicted just like Earth, with the characters walking around without having to use a space helmet or undergoing any effect of gravity loss.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Nero" title="The Adventures of Nero">
          The Adventures of Nero
         </a>
        </i>
        story "De Paarse Futen" (1968) Nero and his friends travel at sea and pick up a pair of American astronauts who crash-landed in the ocean after their attempt to travel to the moon once again failed.
        <a href="/wiki/Adhemar_(comic_book_character)" title="Adhemar (comic book character)">
         Adhemar
        </a>
        uses a magic wand to send them to the moon and says: "This time the Americans beat the Russians." Near the end of the story a US military official arrives to congratulate Adhemar for what he has done and awards him a medal.
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a href="/wiki/Marvel_Universe" title="Marvel Universe">
         Marvel Universe
        </a>
        , the Moon contains the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blue_Area_of_the_Moon" title="Blue Area of the Moon">
         Blue Area
        </a>
        , the home of the
        <a href="/wiki/Inhumans" title="Inhumans">
         Inhumans
        </a>
        . It was built by the Skrull race, in events which led to their Inter-galactic war with the Kree race. The powerful Watcher, Uatu, watches the Solar System from a base on the Moon. In FF #13 the Fantastic Four make the first landing on the Moon (this was published before 1969), and battle the communist villain the
        <a href="/wiki/Red_Ghost" title="Red Ghost">
         Red Ghost
        </a>
        and his Super-apes.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Judge_Dredd" title="Judge Dredd">
          Judge Dredd
         </a>
        </i>
        the moon is the site of a small colony named Luna City One.
       </li>
      </ul>
