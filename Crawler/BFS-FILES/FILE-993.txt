      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_physics" title="Atomic physics">
        atomic physics
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        fine structure
       </b>
       describes the splitting of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atoms" title="Atoms">
        atoms
       </a>
       due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_spin" title="Electron spin">
        electron spin
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Special_relativity" title="Special relativity">
        relativistic corrections
       </a>
       to the non-relativistic
       <a href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation" title="Schrödinger equation">
        Schrödinger equation
       </a>
       . It was first measured precisely for the
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">
        hydrogen atom
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_A._Michelson" title="Albert A. Michelson">
        Albert A. Michelson
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_W._Morley" title="Edward W. Morley">
        Edward W. Morley
       </a>
       in 1887
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       laying the basis for the theoretical treatment by
       <a href="/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld" title="Arnold Sommerfeld">
        Arnold Sommerfeld
       </a>
       , introducing the
       <a href="/wiki/Fine-structure_constant" title="Fine-structure constant">
        fine-structure constant
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fine_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gross_structure">
        Gross structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fine_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gross structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        gross structure
       </b>
       of line spectra is the line spectra predicted by the quantum mechanics of non-relativistic electrons with no spin. For a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrogenic" title="Hydrogenic">
        hydrogenic
       </a>
       atom, the gross structure energy levels only depend on the
       <a href="/wiki/Principal_quantum_number" title="Principal quantum number">
        principal quantum number
       </a>
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       . However, a more accurate model takes into account relativistic and spin effects, which break the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Degenerate_energy_level" title="Degenerate energy level">
        degeneracy
       </a>
       of the energy levels and split the spectral lines. The scale of the fine structure splitting relative to the gross structure energies is on the order of (
       <i>
        Zα
       </i>
       )
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       , where
       <i>
        Z
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_number" title="Atomic number">
        atomic number
       </a>
       and
       <i>
        α
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Fine-structure_constant" title="Fine-structure constant">
        fine-structure constant
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity" title="Dimensionless quantity">
        dimensionless number
       </a>
       equal to approximately
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle 1/137}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
             <mn>
              1
             </mn>
             <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
              <mo>
               /
              </mo>
             </mrow>
             <mn>
              137
             </mn>
            </mstyle>
           </mrow>
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\displaystyle 1/137}
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="1/137" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00ae806487762d558c1790d1d3aaed9eb4c5be28" style="vertical-align: -0.838ex; width:5.812ex; height:2.843ex;"/>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Relativistic_corrections">
        Relativistic corrections
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fine_structure&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Relativistic corrections">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The fine structure energy corrections can be obtained by using
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)">
        perturbation theory
       </a>
       . To perform this calculation one must add the three corrective terms to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics)" title="Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)">
        Hamiltonian
       </a>
       : the leading order relativistic correction to the kinetic energy, the correction due to the spin-orbit coupling, and the Darwin term coming from the quantum fluctuating motion or
       <a href="/wiki/Zitterbewegung" title="Zitterbewegung">
        zitterbewegung
       </a>
       of the electron.
      </p>
      <p>
       These corrections can also be obtained from the non-relativistic limit of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dirac_equation" title="Dirac equation">
        Dirac equation
       </a>
       , since Dirac's theory naturally incorporates relativity and
       <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
        spin
       </a>
       interactions.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kinetic_energy_relativistic_correction">

      </p>
      <dl>

         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

      <p>
       This expression, which contains all higher order terms that were left out in the other calculations, expands to first order to give the energy corrections derived from perturbation theory. However, this equation does not contain the
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperfine_structure" title="Hyperfine structure">
        hyperfine structure
       </a>
       corrections, which are due to interactions with the nuclear spin. Other corrections from
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_field_theory" title="Quantum field theory">
        quantum field theory
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Lamb_shift" title="Lamb shift">
        Lamb shift
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment" title="Anomalous magnetic dipole moment">
        anomalous magnetic dipole moment
       </a>
       of the electron are not included.
