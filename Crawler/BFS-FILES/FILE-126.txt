      <p>
       The
       <b>
        chromosphere
       </b>
       (literally, "sphere of color") is the second of the three main layers in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's atmosphere and is roughly 3,000 to 5,000 kilometers deep. The chromosphere's rosy red color is only apparent during eclipses. The chromosphere sits just above the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       and below the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        solar transition region
       </a>
       . The layer of the chromosphere atop the photosphere is homogeneous. A forest of hairy appearing
       <a href="/wiki/Spicule_(solar_physics)" title="Spicule (solar physics)">
        spicules
       </a>
       rise from the homogeneous layer some of which extend 10,000 km into the corona above.
      </p>
      <p>
       The density of the chromosphere is only 10
       <sup>
        −4
       </sup>
       times that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       , the layer beneath, and 10
       <sup>
        −8
       </sup>
       times that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       at sea level. This makes the chromosphere normally invisible and it can be seen only during a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_eclipse" title="Total eclipse">
        total eclipse
       </a>
       , where its reddish color is revealed. The color hues are anywhere between pink and red.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Without special equipment, the chromosphere cannot normally be seen due to the overwhelming brightness of the photosphere beneath.
      </p>
      <p>
       The density of the chromosphere decreases with distance from the center of the Sun. This decreases logarithmically from 10
       <sup>
        17
       </sup>
       particles per cubic centimeter, or approximately
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6996200000000000000♠
        </span>
        2
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −4
        </sup>
        kg/m
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       to under
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6989160000000000000♠
        </span>
        1.6
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −11
        </sup>
        kg/m
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       at the outer boundary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The temperature decreases from the inner boundary at about 6,000 K
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to a minimum of approximately 3,800 K,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before increasing to upwards of 35,000 K
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at the outer boundary with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition layer
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Chromospheres have been observed also in stars other than the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The sun's chromosphere has always been and will continue to be a body hard to examine and decipher. Although, since it is a point of interest to several individuals, we attempt to observe the chromosphere with the help of the electromagnetic spectrum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comparing_chromosphere_and_photosphere">
        Comparing chromosphere and photosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Comparing chromosphere and photosphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Whilst the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       has an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines#Types_of_line_spectra" title="Spectral lines">
        absorption line
       </a>
       spectrum, the chromosphere's
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       is dominated by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines#Types_of_line_spectra" title="Spectral lines">
        emission lines
       </a>
       . In particular, one of its strongest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_lines" title="Spectral lines">
        lines
       </a>
       is the
       <i>
        H
       </i>
       <sub>
        α
       </sub>
       at a
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of 656.3 nm; this line is emitted by a
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_atom" title="Hydrogen atom">
        hydrogen atom
       </a>
       whenever its
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electron
       </a>
       makes a transition from the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       =3 to the
       <i>
        n
       </i>
       =2
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_level" title="Energy level">
        energy level
       </a>
       . A
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of 656.3 nm is in the red part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       , which causes the chromosphere to have its characteristic reddish colour.
      </p>
      <p>
       By analysing the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       of the chromosphere, it was found that the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       of this layer of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       atmosphere increases with increasing height in the chromosphere itself. The temperature at the top of
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       is only about 4,400 K, while at the top of chromosphere, some 2,000 km higher, it reaches 25,000 K.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is however the opposite of what we find in the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       , where the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       drops with increasing height. It is not yet fully understood what phenomenon causes the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       of the chromosphere to paradoxically increase further from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's interior. However, it seems likely to be explained, partially or totally, by
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Features">
        Features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chromosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Many interesting phenomena can be observed in the chromosphere, which is very complex and dynamic:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Filaments (and prominences, which are filaments viewed from the side) underlie many
        <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
         coronal mass ejections
        </a>
        and hence are important to the prediction of
        <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
         space weather
        </a>
        .
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
          Solar prominences
         </a>
        </i>
        rise up through the chromosphere from the photosphere, sometimes reaching altitudes of 150,000 km. These gigantic plumes of gas are the most spectacular of solar phenomena, aside from the less frequent
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
         solar flares
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The most common feature is the presence of
        <a href="/wiki/Spicule_(solar_physics)" title="Spicule (solar physics)">
         spicules
        </a>
        , long thin fingers of luminous gas which appear like the blades of a huge field of fiery
        <a href="/wiki/Poaceae" title="Poaceae">
         grass
        </a>
        growing upwards from the photosphere below. Spicules rise to the top of the chromosphere and then sink back down again over the course of about 10 minutes. Similarly, there are horizontal wisps of gas called
        <i>
         fibrils
        </i>
        , which last about twice as long as spicules.
       </li>
       <li>
        Images taken in typical chromospheric lines show the presence of brighter cells, usually called as
        <i>
         network
        </i>
        , while the surrounding darker regions are named
        <i>
         internetwork
        </i>
        . They look similar to
        <a href="/wiki/Granule_(solar_physics)" title="Granule (solar physics)">
         granules
        </a>
        commonly observed on the
        <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
         photosphere
        </a>
        due to the heat
        <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
         convection
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Periodic oscillations have been found since the first observations with the instrument SUMER on board
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SOHO
        </a>
        with a frequency from 3 mHz to 10 mHz, corresponding to a characteristic periodic time of three minutes.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Oscillations of the radial component of the plasma velocity are typical of the high chromosphere. Now we know that the photospheric granulation pattern has usually no oscillations above 20 mHz while higher frequency waves (100 mHz or a 10 s period) were detected in the solar atmosphere (at temperatures typical of the transition region and corona) by
        <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
         TRACE
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         Cool loops
        </i>
        can be seen at the border of the solar disk. They are different from prominences because they look as concentric arches with maximum temperature of the order 0,1 MK (too low to be considered coronal features). These cool loops show an intense variability: they appear and disappear in some UV lines in a time less than an hour, or they rapidly expand in 10–20 minutes. Foukal
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        studied these cool loops in detail from the observations taken with the EUV spectrometer on
        <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
         Skylab
        </a>
        in 1976. Otherwise, when the plasma temperature of these loops becomes coronal (above 1 MK), these features appear more stable and evolve on longer times.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       See the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.atkinsopht.com/ast/astrpage.htm" rel="nofollow">
        flash spectrum
       </a>
       of the solar chromosphere (Eclipse of March 7, 1970).
      </p>
