      <p>
       In physics, a
       <b>
        fluid
       </b>
       is a substance that continually
       <a href="/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics)" title="Deformation (mechanics)">
        deforms
       </a>
       (flows) under an applied
       <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
        shear stress
       </a>
       . Fluids are a subset of the
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_(matter)" title="Phase (matter)">
        phases
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        matter
       </a>
       and include
       <a href="/wiki/Liquid" title="Liquid">
        liquids
       </a>
       , gases,
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasmas
       </a>
       , and to some extent,
       <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">
        plastic solids
       </a>
       . Fluids are
       <a href="/wiki/Matter" title="Matter">
        substances
       </a>
       that have zero
       <a href="/wiki/Shear_modulus" title="Shear modulus">
        shear modulus
       </a>
       , or, in simpler terms, a fluid is a substance which cannot resist any
       <a href="/wiki/Shear_force" title="Shear force">
        shear force
       </a>
       applied to it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the term "fluid" includes both the liquid and gas phases, in common usage, "fluid" is often used as a synonym for "liquid", with no implication that gas could also be present. For example, "
       <a href="/wiki/Brake_fluid" title="Brake fluid">
        brake fluid
       </a>
       " is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydraulic_oil" title="Hydraulic oil">
        hydraulic oil
       </a>
       and will not perform its required
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Incompressible" title="Incompressible">
        incompressible
       </a>
       function if there is gas in it. This colloquial usage of the term is also common in medicine and in nutrition ("take plenty of fluids").
      </p>
      <p>
       Liquids form a
       <a href="/wiki/Free_surface" title="Free surface">
        free surface
       </a>
       (that is, a surface not created by the container) while gases do not. The distinction between
       <a href="/wiki/Solid" title="Solid">
        solids
       </a>
       and fluid is not entirely obvious. The distinction is made by evaluating the
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscosity
       </a>
       of the substance.
       <a href="/wiki/Silly_Putty" title="Silly Putty">
        Silly Putty
       </a>
       can be considered to behave like a solid or a fluid, depending on the time period over which it is observed. It is best described as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Viscoelastic" title="Viscoelastic">
        viscoelastic
       </a>
       fluid. There are many examples of substances proving difficult to classify. A particularly interesting one is
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_(resin)" title="Pitch (resin)">
        pitch
       </a>
       , as demonstrated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Pitch_drop_experiment" title="Pitch drop experiment">
        pitch drop experiment
       </a>
       currently running at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Queensland" title="University of Queensland">
        University of Queensland
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">
        Physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Fluids display properties such as:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        not resisting permanent deformation, resisting only
        <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
         relative rates of deformation
        </a>
        in a dissipative, frictional manner, and
       </li>
       <li>
        the ability to flow (also described as the ability to take on the shape of the container).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These properties are typically a function of their inability to support a
       <a href="/wiki/Shear_stress" title="Shear stress">
        shear stress
       </a>
       in static
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">
        equilibrium
       </a>
       . In contrast, solids respond to shear with
       <a href="/wiki/Elasticity_(physics)" title="Elasticity (physics)">
        a spring-like restoring force
       </a>
       , which means that small deformations, whether shear or normal, are reversible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Solids respond with restoring forces to both shear stresses, and to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_stress" title="Normal stress">
        normal stresses
       </a>
       —both
       <a href="/wiki/Compressive_stress" title="Compressive stress">
        compressive
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_stress" title="Tensile stress">
        tensile
       </a>
       . In contrast, ideal fluids only respond with restoring forces to normal stresses, called
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       : fluids can be subjected to both compressive stress, corresponding to positive pressure, and to tensile stress, corresponding to
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure#Negative_pressure" title="Pressure">
        negative pressure
       </a>
       . Both solids and liquids also have tensile strengths, which when exceeded in solids makes
       <a href="/wiki/Plasticity_(physics)" title="Plasticity (physics)">
        irreversible deformation
       </a>
       and fracture, and in liquids causes the onset of
       <a href="/wiki/Cavitation" title="Cavitation">
        cavitation
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
        Gases
       </a>
       do not have tensile strength, and freely expand in response to changes in pressure.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both solids and liquids have free surfaces, which cost some amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamic_free_energy" title="Thermodynamic free energy">
        free energy
       </a>
       to form. In the case of solids, the amount of free energy to form a given unit of surface area is called
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_energy" title="Surface energy">
        surface energy
       </a>
       , whereas for liquids the same quantity is called
       <a href="/wiki/Surface_tension" title="Surface tension">
        surface tension
       </a>
       . The ability of liquids to flow results in very different behaviour in response to surface tension than in solids, although in equilibrium both will try to
       <a href="/wiki/Wulff_construction" title="Wulff construction">
        minimise their surface energy
       </a>
       : liquids tend to form rounded
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Droplets" title="Droplets">
        droplets
       </a>
       , whereas pure solids tend to form
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystals" title="Crystals">
        crystals
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
        Gases
       </a>
       do not have free surfaces, and freely
       <a href="/wiki/Diffusion#Elementary_theory_of_diffusion_coefficient_in_gases" title="Diffusion">
        diffuse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modelling">
        Modelling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Fluid&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Modelling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">
        Fluid mechanics
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In a solid, shear stress is a function of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strain_(materials_science)" title="Strain (materials science)">
        strain
       </a>
       , but in a fluid,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stress_(physics)" title="Stress (physics)">
        shear stress
       </a>
       is a function of
       <a href="/wiki/Strain_rate" title="Strain rate">
        strain rate
       </a>
       . A consequence of this behavior is
       <a href="/wiki/Pascal%27s_law" title="Pascal's law">
        Pascal's law
       </a>
       which describes the role of
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       in characterizing a fluid's state.
      </p>
      <p>
       Depending on the relationship between shear stress, and the rate of strain and its
       <a href="/wiki/Derivative" title="Derivative">
        derivatives
       </a>
       , fluids can be characterized as one of the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Newtonian_fluid" title="Newtonian fluid">
         Newtonian fluids
        </a>
        : where stress is directly proportional to rate of strain
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Non-Newtonian_fluid" title="Non-Newtonian fluid">
         Non-Newtonian fluids
        </a>
        : where stress is not proportional to rate of strain, its higher powers and derivatives.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The behavior of fluids can be described by the
       <a href="/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations" title="Navier–Stokes equations">
        Navier–Stokes equations
       </a>
       —a set of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_differential_equations" title="Partial differential equations">
        partial differential equations
       </a>
       which are based on:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        continuity (
        <a href="/wiki/Continuity_equation#Fluid_dynamics" title="Continuity equation">
         conservation of mass
        </a>
        ),
       </li>
       <li>
        conservation of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_momentum" title="Linear momentum">
         linear momentum
        </a>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        conservation of
        <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
         angular momentum
        </a>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Conservation_of_energy" title="Conservation of energy">
         conservation of energy
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The study of fluids is
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid_mechanics" title="Fluid mechanics">
        fluid mechanics
       </a>
       , which is subdivided into
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
        fluid dynamics
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fluid_statics" title="Fluid statics">
        fluid statics
       </a>
       depending on whether the fluid is in motion.
      </p>
