   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Retrograde and prograde motion
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Retrogradeorbit.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="289" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/Retrogradeorbit.gif/220px-Retrogradeorbit.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/65/Retrogradeorbit.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>

         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         Retrograde orbit: the satellite (red) orbits in the direction opposite to the rotation of its primary (blue/black)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <b>
        Retrograde motion
       </b>
       is motion in the direction opposite to the movement of something else and the contrary of
       <b>
        direct
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        prograde motion
       </b>
       . This
       <i>
        motion
       </i>
       can be the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       of one body about another body or about some other point, or the
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">
        rotation
       </a>
       of a single body about its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Axis_of_rotation" title="Axis of rotation">
        axis
       </a>
       , or other phenomena such as
       <a href="/wiki/Precession" title="Precession">
        precession
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Nutation" title="Nutation">
        nutation
       </a>
       of the axis. In reference to celestial
       <i>
        systems
       </i>
       , retrograde motion
       <i>
        usually
       </i>
       means motion that is contrary to the
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation" title="Rotation">
        rotation
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Primary_(astronomy)" title="Primary (astronomy)">
        primary
       </a>
       , that is, the object that forms the system's hub. Rotation is determined with respect to an
       <a href="/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference" title="Inertial frame of reference">
        inertial frame of reference
       </a>
       , such as distant
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        fixed stars
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In our
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , all of the
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       and most of the other objects that orbit the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , with the exception of many
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       , do so in the "prograde" direction, i.e. the same sense as the rotation of the Sun. In addition, the rotations of most planets are prograde, with the exceptions of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       , which have retrograde rotations. Most
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       of planets revolve around their planets in the prograde sense. (In the case of the satellites of Uranus, this means they revolve in the same sense as Uranus's rotation, which is retrograde relative to the Sun.) There are some satellites that orbit in the retrograde sense, but these are generally small and distant from their planets, except for
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       's satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       , which is large and close. It is thought that these retrograde satellites, including Triton, are bodies that have been captured into orbit around their planets, having been formed elsewhere.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation_of_celestial_systems">
        Formation of celestial systems
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation of celestial systems">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When a
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
        galaxy
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_system" title="Planetary system">
        planetary system
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis" title="Nebular hypothesis">
        forms
       </a>
       , its material takes the shape of a disk. Most of the material orbits and rotates in one direction. This uniformity of motion is due to the collapse of a gas cloud.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_Aug_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS_Aug-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The nature of the collapse is explained by the principle called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">
        conservation of angular momentum
       </a>
       . In 2010 the discovery of several
       <a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">
        hot Jupiters
       </a>
       with backward orbits called into question the theories about the formation of planetary systems.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010question_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2010question-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This can be explained by noting that stars and their planets do not form in isolation but in
       <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">
        star clusters
       </a>
       that contain
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">
        molecular clouds
       </a>
       . When a
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       collides with or steals material from a cloud this can result in retrograde motion of a disk and the resulting planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steal_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-steal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natural-misalign_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-natural-misalign-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_parameters">
        Orbital parameters
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbital parameters">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inclination">
        Inclination
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inclination">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A celestial object's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       indicates whether the object's
       <i>
        orbit
       </i>
       is prograde or retrograde. The inclination of a celestial object is the
       <a href="/wiki/Angle" title="Angle">
        angle
       </a>
       between its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        orbital plane
       </a>
       and another reference frame such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_plane" title="Equatorial plane">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       of the object's primary. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , inclination of the planets is measured from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_plane" title="Ecliptic plane">
        ecliptic plane
       </a>
       , which is the
       <a href="/wiki/Plane_(geometry)" title="Plane (geometry)">
        plane
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's orbit around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The inclination of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moons" title="Moons">
        moons
       </a>
       is measured from the equator of the planet they orbit. An object with an inclination between 0 and 90 degrees is orbiting or revolving in the same direction as the primary is rotating. An object with an inclination of exactly 90 degrees has a perpendicular orbit that is neither prograde nor retrograde. An object with an inclination between 90 degrees and 180 degrees is in a retrograde orbit.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Axial_tilt">
        Axial tilt
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Axial tilt">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A celestial object's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       indicates whether the object's
       <i>
        rotation
       </i>
       is prograde or retrograde. Axial tilt is the angle between an object's rotation axis and a line
       <a href="/wiki/Perpendicular" title="Perpendicular">
        perpendicular
       </a>
       to its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy)" title="Orbital plane (astronomy)">
        orbital plane
       </a>
       passing through the object's centre. An object with an axial tilt up to 90 degrees is rotating in the same direction as its primary. An object with an axial tilt of exactly 90 degrees has a perpendicular rotation that is neither prograde nor retrograde. An object with an axial tilt between 90 degrees and 180 degrees is rotating in the opposite direction to its orbital direction.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planets">
        Planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All eight planets in the Solar System orbit the Sun in the direction that the Sun is rotating, which is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Counterclockwise" title="Counterclockwise">
        counterclockwise
       </a>
       when viewed from above the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Geographic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        north pole
       </a>
       . Six of the planets also rotate about their axis in this same direction. The exceptions—the planets with retrograde rotation—are
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . Venus's axial tilt is 177 degrees, which means it is spinning almost exactly in the opposite direction to its orbit. Uranus has an axial tilt of 97.77 degrees, so its axis of rotation is approximately parallel with the plane of the Solar System. The reason for Uranus's unusual axial tilt is not known with certainty, but the usual speculation is that during the formation of the Solar System, an Earth-sized
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">
        protoplanet
       </a>
       collided with Uranus, causing the skewed orientation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is unlikely that Venus was formed with its present slow retrograde rotation, which takes 243 days. Venus probably began with a fast prograde rotation with a period of several hours much like most of the planets in the solar system. Venus is close enough to the Sun to experience significant gravitational
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal dissipation
       </a>
       , and also has a thick enough atmosphere to create thermally driven atmospheric tides that create a retrograde
       <a href="/wiki/Torque" title="Torque">
        torque
       </a>
       . Venus' present slow retrograde rotation is in
       <a href="/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium" title="Mechanical equilibrium">
        equilibrium
       </a>
       balance between gravitational tides trying to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_lock" title="Tidally lock">
        tidally lock
       </a>
       Venus to the Sun and atmospheric tides trying to spin Venus in a retrograde direction. In addition to maintaining this present day equilibrium, tides are also sufficient to account for evolution of Venus's rotation from a primordial fast prograde direction to its present-day slow retrograde rotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TidalEvoExop_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TidalEvoExop-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the past various other alternative hypotheses have been proposed to explain Venus' retrograde rotation, such as collisions or it having originally formed that way.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Mercury is closer to the Sun than Venus but Mercury is not tidally locked because it has entered a
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)#Spin–orbit_resonance" title="Mercury (planet)">
        3:2 spin–orbit resonance
       </a>
       due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of its orbit. Mercury's prograde rotation is slow enough that due to its eccentricity, its angular orbital velocity exceeds its angular rotational velocity near
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       , causing the motion of the sun in Mercury's sky to temporarily reverse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-strom_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-strom-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rotations of Earth and Mars are also affected by tidal forces with the Sun, but they have not reached an equilibrium state like Mercury and Venus because they are further out from the Sun where tidal forces are weaker. The
       <a href="/wiki/Gas_giant" title="Gas giant">
        gas giants
       </a>
       of the solar system are too massive and too far from the Sun for tidal forces to slow down their rotations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TidalEvoExop_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-TidalEvoExop-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarf_planets">
        Dwarf planets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Dwarf planets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All known
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planets
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_possible_dwarf_planets" title="List of possible dwarf planets">
        dwarf planet candidates
       </a>
       have prograde orbits around the Sun, but some have retrograde rotation.
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       has retrograde rotation; its axial tilt is approximately 120 degrees.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pluto and its moon
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       are both tidally locked to each other. It is suspected that the Plutonian satellite system was created by a
       <a href="/wiki/Collisional_family" title="Collisional family">
        massive collision
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Canup_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Canup-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stern_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stern-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Earth.27s_atmosphere">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earth's_atmosphere">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Earth's atmosphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Retrograde motion, or retrogression, within the Earth's atmosphere is seen in weather systems that move from east to west through the
       <a href="/wiki/Westerlies" title="Westerlies">
        Westerlies
       </a>
       or from west to east through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trade_wind" title="Trade wind">
        trade wind
       </a>
       easterlies.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_satellites_and_rings">
        Natural satellites and rings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Natural satellites and rings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RetrogradeBaan.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="240" data-file-width="320" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/RetrogradeBaan.gif/220px-RetrogradeBaan.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/82/RetrogradeBaan.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:RetrogradeBaan.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The orange moon is in a retrograde orbit.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       If formed in the gravity-field of a planet as the planet is forming, a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       will orbit the planet in the same direction as the planet is rotating and is a
       <a href="/wiki/Regular_moon" title="Regular moon">
        regular moon
       </a>
       . If an object is formed elsewhere and later captured into orbit by a planet's gravity, it can be captured into a retrograde or prograde orbit depending on whether it first approaches the side of the planet that is rotating towards or away from it. This is an
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the Solar System, many of the asteroid-sized moons have retrograde orbits, whereas all the large moons except
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       (the largest of Neptune's moons) have prograde orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The particles in Saturn's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Phoebe_ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Phoebe ring
       </a>
       are thought to have a retrograde orbit because they originate from the irregular moon
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       All retrograde satellites experience
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal deceleration
       </a>
       to some degree. The only satellite in the Solar System for which this effect is non-negligible is Neptune's moon Triton. All the other retrograde satellites are on distant orbits and tidal forces between them and the planet are negligible.
      </p>
      <p>
       Within the
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       , the region of stability for retrograde orbits at a large distance from the primary is larger than that for prograde orbits. This has been suggested as an explanation for the preponderance of retrograde moons around Jupiter. Because Saturn has a more even mix of retrograde/prograde moons, however, the underlying causes appear to be more complex.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astakhov2003_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Astakhov2003-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With the exception of
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       all the known
       <a href="/wiki/Regular_moon" title="Regular moon">
        regular planetary natural satellites
       </a>
       in the Solar System are
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       to their host planet, so they have zero rotation relative to their host planet, but have the same type of rotation relative to the Sun as their host planet, because they have prograde orbits around their host planet. That is to say, they all have prograde rotation relative to the Sun except those of Uranus.
      </p>
      <p>
       If there is a collision, material could be ejected in any direction and coalesce into either prograde or retrograde moons, which may be the case for the moons of dwarf planet
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Haumea_(dwarf_planet)" title="Haumea (dwarf planet)">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , although Haumea's rotation direction is not known.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Small_solar_system_bodies">
        Small solar system bodies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Small solar system bodies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Asteroids">
        Asteroids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Asteroids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroids" title="Asteroids">
        Asteroids
       </a>
       usually have a prograde orbit around the Sun. Only a few dozen
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_notable_asteroids#Retrograde_and_highly_inclined" title="List of notable asteroids">
        asteroids in retrograde orbits
       </a>
       are known.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some asteroids with retrograde orbits may be burnt-out comets,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_May_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS_May-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but some may acquire their retrograde orbit due to gravitational interactions with
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-greenstreet_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-greenstreet-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to their small size and their large distance from Earth it is difficult to
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescopically
       </a>
       analyse the rotation of most asteroids. As of 2012, data is available for less than 200 asteroids and the different methods of determining the orientation of
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_pole" title="Orbital pole">
        poles
       </a>
       often result in large discrepancies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The asteroid spin vector catalog at Poznan Observatory
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       avoids use of the phrases "retrograde rotation" or "prograde rotation" as it depends which reference plane is meant and asteroid coordinates are usually given with respect to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ecliptic_plane" title="Ecliptic plane">
        ecliptic plane
       </a>
       rather than the asteroid's orbital plane.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Asteroids with satellites, also known as binary asteroids, make up about 15% of all asteroids less than 10 km in diameter in the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">
        main belt
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_asteroids" title="Near-Earth asteroids">
        near-Earth
       </a>
       population and most are thought to be formed by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YORP_effect" title="YORP effect">
        YORP effect
       </a>
       causing an asteroid to spin so fast that it breaks up.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2012, and where the rotation is known, all
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">
        satellites of asteroids
       </a>
       orbit the asteroid in the same direction as the asteroid is rotating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Most known objects that are in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       are orbiting in the same direction as the objects they are in resonance with, however a few retrograde asteroids have been found in resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morais2013_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morais2013-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Comets">
        Comets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Comets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Comets" title="Comets">
        Comets
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Oort_cloud" title="Oort cloud">
        Oort cloud
       </a>
       are much more likely than asteroids to be retrograde.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_May_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS_May-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet" title="Halley's Comet">
        Halley's Comet
       </a>
       has a retrograde orbit around the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kuiper_belt">

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Kuiper belt">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Most
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects have prograde orbits around the Sun. The first Kuiper belt object discovered to have a retrograde orbit is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2008_KV42" title="2008 KV42">
         2008 KV
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           42
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_Sep_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS_Sep-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Meteoroids">
        Meteoroids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Meteoroids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteoroids" title="Meteoroids">
        Meteoroids
       </a>
       in a retrograde orbit around the Sun hit the Earth with a faster relative speed than prograde meteoroids and tend to burn up in the atmosphere and are more likely to hit the side of the Earth facing away from the Sun (i.e. at night) whereas the prograde meteoroids have slower closing speeds and more often land as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorites" title="Meteorites">
        meteorites
       </a>
       and tend to hit the sun-facing side of the Earth. Most meteoroids are prograde.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sun">
        The Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: The Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Sun's motion about the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass#Barycenter_in_astrophysics_and_astronomy" title="Center of mass">
        centre of mass
       </a>
       of the Solar System is complicated by perturbations from the planets. Every few hundred years this motion switches between prograde and retrograde.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exoplanets">
        Exoplanets
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Exoplanets">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Stars and planetary systems tend to be born in
       <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">
        star clusters
       </a>
       rather than forming in isolation.
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        Protoplanetary disks
       </a>
       can collide with or steal material from
       <a href="/wiki/Molecular_cloud" title="Molecular cloud">
        molecular clouds
       </a>
       within the cluster and this can lead to disks and their resulting planets having inclined or retrograde orbits around their stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-steal_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-steal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-natural-misalign_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-natural-misalign-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Retrograde motion may also result from gravitational interactions with other celestial bodies in the same system (See
       <a href="/wiki/Kozai_mechanism" title="Kozai mechanism">
        Kozai mechanism
       </a>
       ) or a near-collision with another planet,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS_Aug_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS_Aug-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or it may be that the star itself flipped over early in their system's formation due to interactions between the star's magnetic field and the planet-forming disk.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       of the protostar
       <a href="/wiki/IRAS_16293-2422" title="IRAS 16293-2422">
        IRAS 16293-2422
       </a>
       has parts rotating in opposite directions. This is the first known example of a counterrotating accretion disk. If this system forms planets, the inner planets will likely orbit in the opposite direction to the outer planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/WASP-17b" title="WASP-17b">
        WASP-17b
       </a>
       was the first
       <a href="/wiki/Exoplanet" title="Exoplanet">
        exoplanet
       </a>
       that was discovered to be orbiting its star opposite to the direction the star is rotating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2010_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2010-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A second such planet was announced just a day later:
       <a href="/wiki/HAT-P-7b" title="HAT-P-7b">
        HAT-P-7b
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In one study more than half of all the known
       <a href="/wiki/Hot_Jupiter" title="Hot Jupiter">
        hot Jupiters
       </a>
       had orbits that were misaligned with the rotation axis of their parent stars, with six having backwards orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010question_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-2010question-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The last few
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
        giant impacts
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/Nebular_hypothesis#Formation_of_planets" title="Nebular hypothesis">
        planetary formation
       </a>
       tend to be the main determiner of a
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial planet
       </a>
       's rotation rate. During the giant impact stage, the thickness of a
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       is far larger than the size of planetary embryos so collisions are equally likely to come from any direction in three dimensions. This results in the
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        axial tilt
       </a>
       of accreted planets ranging from 0 to 180 degrees with any direction as likely as any other with both prograde and retrograde spins equally probable. Therefore, prograde spin with small axial tilt, common for the solar system's terrestrial planets except for Venus, is not common for terrestrial planets in general.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stars">
        Stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The pattern of stars appears fixed in the sky, but that is only because they are so far away that their motion isn't visible to the naked eye; actually, they are orbiting the centre of the galaxy. Stars with a retrograde orbit are more likely to be found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Galactic_halo" title="Galactic halo">
        galactic halo
       </a>
       than in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galactic_disk" title="Galactic disk">
        galactic disk
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
        Milky Way
       </a>
       's outer halo has many
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Globular_clusters" title="Globular clusters">
        globular clusters
       </a>
       with a retrograde orbit
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and with a retrograde or zero rotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The structure of the halo is topic of an ongoing debate. Several studies claimed to find a halo consisting of two distinct components.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These studies find a "dual" halo, with an inner, more metal-rich, prograde component (i.e. stars orbit the galaxy on average with the disk rotation), and a metal-poor, outer, retrograde (rotating against the disc) component. However, these findings have been challenged by other studies,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
        <a href="#cite_note-41">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       arguing against such a duality. These studies demonstrate that the observational data can be explained without a duality, when employing an improved statistical analysis and accounting for measurement uncertainties.
      </p>
      <p>
       The nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Kapteyn%27s_Star" title="Kapteyn's Star">
        Kapteyn's Star
       </a>
       is thought to have ended up with its high-velocity retrograde orbit around the galaxy as a result of being ripped from a dwarf galaxy that merged with the Milky Way.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Galaxies">
        Galaxies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Galaxies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_galaxies">
        Satellite galaxies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Satellite galaxies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Close-flybys and mergers of galaxies within
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_cluster" title="Galaxy cluster">
        galaxy clusters
       </a>
       can pull material out of galaxies and create small satellite galaxies in either prograde or retrograde orbits around larger galaxies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
        <a href="#cite_note-43">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A galaxy called Complex H, which was orbiting the Milky Way in a retrograde direction relative to the Milky Way's rotation, is colliding with the Milky Way.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Counter-rotating_bulges">
        Counter-rotating bulges
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Counter-rotating bulges">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/NGC_7331" title="NGC 7331">
        NGC 7331
       </a>
       is an example of a galaxy that has a bulge that is rotating in the opposite direction to the rest of the disk, probably as a result of infalling material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Central_black_holes">
        Central black holes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Retrograde_and_prograde_motion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Central black holes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The center of a spiral galaxy contains at least one
       <a href="/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole" title="Supermassive black hole">
        supermassive black hole
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Merritt2005_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Merritt2005-47">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A retrograde black hole – one whose spin is opposite to that of its disk – spews jets much more powerful than those of a prograde black hole, which may have no jet at all. Scientists have produced a theoretical framework for the formation and evolution of retrograde black holes based on the gap between the inner edge of an accretion disk and the black hole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
