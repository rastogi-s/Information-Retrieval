   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Black moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       The term
       <b>
        black moon
       </b>
       refers to an additional
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       that appears in a
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        month
       </a>
       or in a
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        season
       </a>
       . It may also refer to the absence of a
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       or of a new moon in a month.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Definitions.2C_frequency.2C_dates">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions,_frequency,_dates">
        Definitions, frequency, dates
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definitions, frequency, dates">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Month_with_two_new_moons">
        Month with two new moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Month with two new moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One use of the term is for the occurrence of a second new moon in a calendar month. This is analogous to the by-month definition of a
       <a href="/wiki/Blue_moon" title="Blue moon">
        blue moon
       </a>
       as the second full moon in a month. February is too short for a second new moon to occur. This event occurs about every 29 months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfaq_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfaq-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harper-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odenwald_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Odenwald-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cain_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cain-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rao-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morrison_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morrison-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witchipedia_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-witchipedia-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The assignment of a
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar_date" title="Calendar date">
        calendar date
       </a>
       to a new moon, and in which month a second new moon occurs, depends on the
       <a href="/wiki/Time_zone" title="Time zone">
        time zone
       </a>
       . For example, the new moon of 2016-10-01T00:11 UTC occurs on 1 October for
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oceania" title="Oceania">
        Oceania
       </a>
       , making it the first of two new moons in October. However, for the
       <a href="/wiki/Americas" title="Americas">
        Americas
       </a>
       the date is still 30 September, making this the second new moon of September.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rao-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Calculated in UTC, instances of a second new moon in a calendar month between 2010 and 2020 are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usno_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-usno-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        2011-07-30
       </li>
       <li>
        2014-01-30
       </li>
       <li>
        2014-03-30
       </li>
       <li>
        2016-10-30
       </li>
       <li>
        2019-08-30
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Season_with_four_new_moons">
        Season with four new moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Season with four new moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another use of the term is for the third new moon in a
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        season
       </a>
       that has four new moons. This is analogous to the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Farmers%27_Almanac" title="Farmers' Almanac">
         Farmers' Almanac
        </a>
       </i>
       definition of a blue moon as the third full moon in a season with four full moons. A season lasts about three months and usually has three new moons. This event occurs about every 33 months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harper-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There is no dependency on time zones in this definition as the seasons are tied to the winter solstice. Instances of four new moons in a season are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harper-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usno_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-usno-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        2012-05-20
       </li>
       <li>
        2015-02-18
       </li>
       <li>
        2017-08-21
       </li>
       <li>
        2020-05-22
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Month_without_full_moon">
        Month without full moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Month without full moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another use of the term is for the absence of the full moon from a calendar month. This can occur only in February; it happens about every 20 years. When February is without full moon, then the preceding January or December and the following March or April have two full moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfaq_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfaq-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witchipedia_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-witchipedia-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As with the case of two new moons in a month, whether a black moon by this definition occurs depends on the time zone. Calculated in UTC, instances of a month without full moon between 1990 and 2040 are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usno_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-usno-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        February 1999
       </li>
       <li>
        February 2018
       </li>
       <li>
        February 2037
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Month_without_new_moon">
        Month without new moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Month without new moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Another use of the term is for the absence of the new moon in a calendar month. This can occur only in February; it happens about every 20 years. When February is without new moon, then the preceding January or December and the following March or April have two new moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonfaq_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonfaq-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As with the case of two new moons in a month, whether a black moon by this definition occurs depends on the time zone. Calculated in UTC, instances of a month without new moon between 1990 and 2040 are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usno_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-usno-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        February 1995
       </li>
       <li>
        February 2014
       </li>
       <li>
        February 2033
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Paganism">
        Paganism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Paganism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In some aspects of
       <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">
        paganism
       </a>
       , particularly amongst
       <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">
        Wiccans
       </a>
       , the black moon is considered to be a special time when any rituals, spells, or other workings are considered to be more powerful and effective. Others do not believe any rituals or workings should be conducted at these times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harper-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-witchipedia_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-witchipedia-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-celtic_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-celtic-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism_of_terminology">
        Criticism of terminology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Black_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criticism of terminology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The term
        <i>
         black moon
        </i>
        is not formally established in astronomy and is used at best in the
        <a href="/wiki/Popular_science" title="Popular science">
         popularization
        </a>
        of astronomy.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        There is no single definition of the term
        <i>
         black moon
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
         new moon
        </a>
        itself cannot be observed.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timeanddate_1-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-timeanddate-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harper_3-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Harper-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Odenwald_4-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Odenwald-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rao_6-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-Rao-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The lack of a new or
        <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
         full moon
        </a>
        in February can only be assigned to the month, not to any particular date; as such, this is also not observable.
       </li>
       <li>
        The event of a black moon is an artefact of how the
        <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
         Gregorian calendar
        </a>
        or the seasons map onto
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
         lunations
        </a>
        . There is no physical or geometric difference between a black moon and other instances of a new moon.
       </li>
      </ul>
