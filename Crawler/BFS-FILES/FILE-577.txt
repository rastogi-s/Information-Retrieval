      <p>
       The
       <b>
        solar eclipse of January 15, 2010
       </b>
       was an
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Types" title="Solar eclipse">
        annular eclipse
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">
        magnitude
       </a>
       of 0.9190. A
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurs when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. An annular solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       is smaller than the Sun's, blocking most of the Sun's light and causing the Sun to look like an
       <a href="/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics)" title="Annulus (mathematics)">
        annulus
       </a>
       (ring). An annular eclipse appears as a partial eclipse over a region of the Earth thousands of kilometres wide. It was the longest annular
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       of the millennium,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Eclipse.gsfc.nasa.gov-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the longest until December 23, 3043, with the length of maximum eclipse of 11 minutes, 7.8 seconds, and the longest duration of 11 minutes, 10.7 seconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Besselian_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Besselian-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is about 4 minutes longer than total solar eclipses could ever get. (The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_January_4,_1992" title="Solar eclipse of January 4, 1992">
        solar eclipse of January 4, 1992
       </a>
       , was longer, at 11 minutes, 41 seconds, occurring in the middle of the Pacific Ocean.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Timeanddate.com_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Timeanddate.com-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse was visible as only a partial eclipse in much of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Asia" title="Asia">
        Asia
       </a>
       . It was seen as an annular eclipse within a narrow stretch of 300 km (190 mi) width across
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Africa" title="Central Africa">
        Central Africa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">
        Maldives
       </a>
       , South
       <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">
        Kerala
       </a>
       (India), South
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">
        Tamil Nadu
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">
        Sri Lanka
       </a>
       and parts of
       <a href="/wiki/Bangladesh" title="Bangladesh">
        Bangladesh
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burma" title="Burma">
        Burma
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China" title="People's Republic of China">
        China
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_annular_eclipse_of_January_15,_2010_in_Bangui,_Central_African_Republic.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         January 15, 2010's sunrise in Bangui, Central African Republic
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The eclipse started in the
       <a href="/wiki/Central_African_Republic" title="Central African Republic">
        Central African Republic
       </a>
       near the border with
       <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">
        Chad
       </a>
       , traversed
       <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Republic_of_the_Congo" title="Democratic Republic of the Congo">
        DR Congo
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Uganda" title="Uganda">
        Uganda
       </a>
       , passed through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nairobi,_Kenya" title="Nairobi, Kenya">
        Nairobi, Kenya
       </a>
       , the northern tip of
       <a href="/wiki/Tanzania" title="Tanzania">
        Tanzania
       </a>
       , southwestern
       <a href="/wiki/Somalia" title="Somalia">
        Somalia
       </a>
       and three islands of
       <a href="/wiki/Seychelles" title="Seychelles">
        Seychelles
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Bird_Island,_Seychelles" title="Bird Island, Seychelles">
        Bird
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Denis_Island" title="Denis Island">
        Denis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aride_Island" title="Aride Island">
        Aride
       </a>
       ), and passed over the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Ocean" title="Indian Ocean">
        Indian Ocean
       </a>
       , where it reached its greatest visibility. It then entered
       <a href="/wiki/Maldives" title="Maldives">
        Maldives
       </a>
       , where it was the longest on land with 10.8 viewable minutes. The annular eclipse at
       <a href="/wiki/Mal%C3%A9" title="Malé">
        Malé
       </a>
       , the capital city of Maldives, started at 12:20:20 and ended at 12:30:06 Maldives local time (UTC+5). This was also the longest duration of any eclipse with an international airport in its track.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_SE2010Jan15A_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_SE2010Jan15A-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At approximately 13:20
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Standard_Time" title="Indian Standard Time">
        IST
       </a>
       , the annular solar eclipse entered India at
       <a href="/wiki/Thiruvananthapuram" title="Thiruvananthapuram">
        Thiruvananthapuram
       </a>
       (Trivandrum),
       <a href="/wiki/Kerala" title="Kerala">
        Kerala
       </a>
       and exited India at
       <a href="/wiki/Rameswaram" title="Rameswaram">
        Rameswaram
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tamil_Nadu" title="Tamil Nadu">
        Tamil Nadu
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse was viewable for 10.4 minutes in India. After Rameswaram, it entered
       <a href="/wiki/Sri_Lanka" title="Sri Lanka">
        Sri Lanka
       </a>
       at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delft_Island" title="Delft Island">
        Delft Island
       </a>
       , exited at
       <a href="/wiki/Jaffna" title="Jaffna">
        Jaffna
       </a>
       in Sri Lanka, crossed the
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">
        Bay of Bengal
       </a>
       and re-entered India in
       <a href="/wiki/Mizoram" title="Mizoram">
        Mizoram
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse5.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="394" data-file-width="383" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dd/Eclipse5.jpg/220px-Eclipse5.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/d/dd/Eclipse5.jpg/330px-Eclipse5.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/d/dd/Eclipse5.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse5.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Eclipse picture from
         <a href="/wiki/Thiruvananthapuram" title="Thiruvananthapuram">
          Thiruvananthapuram
         </a>
         , India where the eclipse was 94%
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Thiruvananthapuram, which was the entry point of the eclipse in India, was equipped with telescopes and announced facilities for the public to view the eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FacilitiesinTvm_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FacilitiesinTvm-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Vikram_Sarabhai_Space_Centre" title="Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre">
        Vikram Sarabhai Space Centre
       </a>
       , situated in Trivandrum, analysed the atmospheric-ionospheric parameters during the eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VSSC_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-VSSC-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many scientists camped in the city to witness and study the eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tvmeclipse_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tvmeclipse-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At Rameswaram, the sunrise was not visible due to thick clouds, but it started getting clear at around 9 AM local time and became almost totally clear by the time the eclipse began. The sky had a thin layer of
       <a href="/wiki/Cirrus_cloud" title="Cirrus cloud">
        cirrus clouds
       </a>
       till 2:30PM. Among the eclipse-watchers was Sky Watchers' Association of North Bengal (SWAN) from
       <a href="/wiki/Siliguri" title="Siliguri">
        Siliguri
       </a>
       at the foothills of
       <a href="/wiki/Bengal" title="Bengal">
        West Bengal
       </a>
       and Tamil Nadu Astronomical Association.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Dhanushkodi" title="Dhanushkodi">
        Dhanushkodi
       </a>
       , which falls on the central line of the eclipse, was a good place to view the eclipse. The northernmost limit of shadow in India was
       <a href="/wiki/Cuddalore" title="Cuddalore">
        Cuddalore
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Neyveli" title="Neyveli">
        Neyveli
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Erode" title="Erode">
        Erode
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kodaikanal" title="Kodaikanal">
        Kodaikanal
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Madurai" title="Madurai">
        Madurai
       </a>
       . Other prime viewing locations in Tamil Nadu include
       <a href="/wiki/Thoothukudi" title="Thoothukudi">
        Thoothukudi
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cape_Comorin" title="Cape Comorin">
        Cape Comorin
       </a>
       , 22 km north of the center line. The exact location of the line is between the NH end and the Dhanushkodi ruins. Dhanushkodi is about 2 km east of the central line. The degree difference is about 0.2 between the central line – with Kodandaramar Temple and Dhanushkodi ruins vice versa. Dhanushkodi is about 5 km from the Kodandaramar Temple.
      </p>
      <p>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/South_Asia" title="South Asia">
        South Asia
       </a>
       , annularity passed
       <a href="/wiki/Myanmar" title="Myanmar">
        Myanmar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       before leaving the Earth.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_141">
        Saros 141
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_January_15,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Saros 141">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_141" title="Solar Saros 141">
        Solar Saros 141
       </a>
       repeats every 18 years, 11 days and contains 70 events. The series started with partial solar eclipse on May 19, 1613. It contains annular eclipses from August 4, 1739 through October 14, 2460. There are no total eclipses in this series. The series ends at member 70 as a partial eclipse on June 13, 2857.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_series">
        Metonic series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_January_15,_2010&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Metonic series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        metonic series
       </a>
       repeats eclipses every 19 years (6939.69 days), lasting about 5 cycles. Eclipses occur in nearly the same calendar date. In addition, the octon subseries repeats 1/5 of that or every 3.8 years (1387.94 days).
