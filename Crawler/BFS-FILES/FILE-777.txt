   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Origin of the Moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cara-oculta-luna.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="720" data-file-width="702" height="226" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cara-oculta-luna.jpg/220px-Cara-oculta-luna.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cara-oculta-luna.jpg/330px-Cara-oculta-luna.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Cara-oculta-luna.jpg/440px-Cara-oculta-luna.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cara-oculta-luna.jpg" title="Enlarge">

         </div>
         The Moon's heavily cratered
         <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
          far-side
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        origin of the Moon
       </b>
       is usually thought to be that a
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       -sized body struck the Earth, making a debris ring that eventually collected into a single
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , but there are a number of variations on this
       <a href="/wiki/Giant-impact_hypothesis" title="Giant-impact hypothesis">
        giant-impact hypothesis
       </a>
       , as well as alternate explanations, and research into how the Moon came to be continues.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140907_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140907-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other proposed scenarios include captured body, fission, formed together (condensation theory),
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimal
       </a>
       collisions (formed from asteroid-like bodies), and collision theories.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-theories_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-theories-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The standard giant-impact hypothesis suggests the Mars-sized body, called
       <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
        Theia
       </a>
       , impacted Earth, creating a large debris ring around Earth, which then accreted to form the Moon. This collision also resulted in the 23.5° tilted axis of the earth, thus causing the seasons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_oxygen" title="Isotopes of oxygen">
        oxygen isotopic
       </a>
       ratios seem to be essentially identical to Earth's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oxygen isotopic ratios, which may be measured very precisely, yield a unique and distinct signature for each solar system body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scott010312_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-scott010312-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If Theia had been a separate
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">
        protoplanet
       </a>
       , it probably would have had a different oxygen isotopic signature from Earth, as would the ejected mixed material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moonwalk_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moonwalk-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also, the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanium_isotope" title="Titanium isotope">
        titanium isotope
       </a>
       ratio (
       <sup>
        50
       </sup>
       Ti/
       <sup>
        47
       </sup>
       Ti) appears so close to the Earth's (within 4 ppm) that little if any of the colliding body's mass could likely have been part of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zhang-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        Giant impact hypothesis
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="quotebox pullquote" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:22em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
       <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
        <div>
         "One of the challenges to the longstanding theory of the collision, is that a Mars-sized impacting body, whose composition likely would have differed substantially from that of Earth, likely would have left Earth and the moon with different chemical compositions, which they are not."
        </div>
       </div>
       <cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">
        —NASA
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2383" data-file-width="3000" height="175" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg/220px-Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg/330px-Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg/440px-Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Sample_61016_-_Big_Muley.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Lunar sample 61016, better known as "
         <a href="/wiki/Big_Muley" title="Big Muley">
          Big Muley
         </a>
         "
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Some theories have been stated that presume the Earth had no large moons early in the formation of the Solar System, 4.6 billion years ago, Earth being basically rock and lava. Theia, an early
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanet" title="Protoplanet">
        protoplanet
       </a>
       the size of Mars, hit Earth in such a way that it ejected a considerable amount of material away from Earth. Some proportion of this ejecta
       <a href="/wiki/Escape_velocity" title="Escape velocity">
        escaped
       </a>
       into space, but the rest consolidated into a single body in orbit about Earth, creating the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       The hypothesis requires a collision between a body about 90% of the present size of Earth, and another the diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       (half of the terrestrial radius and a tenth of its mass). The colliding body has sometimes been referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
        Theia
       </a>
       , the mother of
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       , the Moon
       <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">
        goddess
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       . This size ratio is needed in order for the resulting system to have sufficient angular momentum to match the current orbital configuration. Such an impact would have put enough material into orbit around Earth to have eventually accumulated to form the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Computer_simulation" title="Computer simulation">
        Computer simulations
       </a>
       show a need for a glancing blow, which causes a portion of the collider to form a long arm of material that then shears off. The asymmetrical shape of the Earth following the collision then causes this material to settle into an orbit around the main mass. The energy involved in this collision is impressive: possibly trillions of tons of material would have been vaporized and melted. In parts of the Earth, the
       <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
        temperature
       </a>
       would have risen to 10,000 °C (18,000 °F).
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon's relatively small
       <a href="/wiki/Iron" title="Iron">
        iron
       </a>
       core is explained by Theia's core accreting into that of Earth. The lack of volatiles in the lunar samples is also explained in part by the energy of the collision. The energy liberated during the reaccreation of material in orbit around Earth would have been sufficient to melt a large portion of the Moon, leading to the generation of a
       <a href="/wiki/Magma" title="Magma">
        magma
       </a>
       ocean.
      </p>
      <p>
       The newly formed Moon orbited at about one-tenth the distance that it does today, and spiraled outward because of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_friction" title="Tidal friction">
        tidal friction
       </a>
       transferring angular momentum from the rotations of both bodies to the Moon's orbital motion. Along the way, the Moon's rotation became
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       to Earth, so that one side of the Moon continually faces toward Earth. Also, the Moon would have collided with and incorporated any small pre-existing satellites of Earth, which would have shared the Earth's composition, including isotopic abundances. The geology of the Moon has since been more independent of the Earth. Although this hypothesis explains many aspects of the Earth–Moon system, there are still a few unresolved problems, such as the Moon's volatile
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        elements
       </a>
       not being as depleted as expected from such an energetic impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another issue is lunar and Earth isotope comparisons. In 2001, the most precise measurement yet of the
       <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">
        isotopic signatures
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_rock" title="Moon rock">
        moon rocks
       </a>
       was published.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Surprisingly, the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       lunar samples carried an isotopic signature identical to Earth rocks, but different from other Solar system bodies. Because most of the material that went into orbit to form the Moon was thought to come from Theia, this observation was unexpected. In 2007, researchers from Caltech showed that the likelihood of Theia having an identical isotopic signature as the Earth is very small (&lt;1 percent).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ps2007_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ps2007-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Published in 2012, an analysis of titanium isotopes in Apollo lunar samples showed that the Moon has the same composition as Earth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-test-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis#Difficulties" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        conflicts
       </a>
       with the Moon forming far from Earth's orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       To help resolve these problems, a new theory published in 2012 posits that two bodies—each five times the size of Mars—collided, then re-collided, forming a large disc of debris that eventually formed Earth and the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The paper was called “Forming a Moon with an Earth-like composition via a Giant Impact,” by R.M Canup.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa1_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A 2012 study on the depletion of
       <a href="/wiki/Zinc" title="Zinc">
        zinc
       </a>
       isotopes on the Moon supported the giant-impact origin for Earth and the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2013, a study was released that indicated that water in lunar magma is indistinguishable from
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonaceous_chondrite" title="Carbonaceous chondrite">
        carbonaceous chondrites
       </a>
       and nearly the same as that of Earth in
       <a href="/wiki/Isotopic_signature" title="Isotopic signature">
        isotopic composition
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The giant-impact hypothesis was again challenged in September 2013,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with a growing sense that lunar origins are more complicated.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness" title="Wikipedia:Vagueness">
          <span title="This information is too vague. (May 2016)">
           vague
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clery_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-clery-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another possibility is that before the giant impact, Earth had one or more normal satellites that shared its composition. Following the impact, the Moon formed closer to Earth than these satellites, and then spiraled outward, colliding with them. (If the Moon was more massive than the other satellites, its tidal effect on Earth would have been greater, making it spiral outward faster.) This led to the Moon being covered with material with the same composition as the satellites, and so Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2017, planetary researchers at Weizmann Institute of Science in Rehovot, Israel offered a new theory that suggests the moon was forged in a brutal rain of cosmic debris that repeatedly hammered the fledgling Earth over millions of years. They determined that a series of smaller impacts, which were likely more common in the early solar system, could blast enough Earth rocks and dirt into orbit to form a small moonlet. As repeated impacts created more balls of debris, the moonlets could merge over time into one large moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSJ-20170109_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WSJ-20170109-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Capture">
        Capture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Capture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This hypothesis states that the Moon was captured by the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was popular until the 1980s, and some things in favor of this model include the Moon's size, orbit, and tidal locking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One problem is understanding the capture mechanism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A close encounter with Earth typically results in either collision or altered trajectories. For this hypothesis to function, there might have been a large
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       around the primitive Earth, which would slow the movement of the Moon by natural
       <a href="/wiki/Aerobraking" title="Aerobraking">
        aerobraking
       </a>
       before it could escape. That hypothesis may also explain the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       orbits of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This hypothesis also has difficulty explaining the essentially identical
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
        isotope
       </a>
       ratios of the two bodies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wiechert_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-wiechert-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:802px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth-moon-to-scale.svg">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="160" data-file-width="3200" height="40" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Earth-moon-to-scale.svg/800px-Earth-moon-to-scale.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Earth-moon-to-scale.svg/1200px-Earth-moon-to-scale.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/Earth-moon-to-scale.svg/1600px-Earth-moon-to-scale.svg.png 2x" width="800"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth-moon-to-scale.svg" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Earth and Moon to scale, 500 km per pixel
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fission">
        Fission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This is the now discredited hypothesis that an ancient, rapidly spinning Earth expelled a piece of its mass.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was proposed by
       <a href="/wiki/George_Darwin" title="George Darwin">
        George Darwin
       </a>
       (son of the famous biologist
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Darwin" title="Charles Darwin">
        Charles Darwin
       </a>
       ) in the 1800s and retained some popularity until Apollo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Austrian geologist Otto Ampherer in 1925 also suggested the emerging of the Moon as cause for
       <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">
        continental drift
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was proposed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific Ocean
       </a>
       represented the scar of this event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_18-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-origin-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Today it is known that the oceanic crust that makes up this ocean basin is relatively young, about 200 million years old or less, whereas the Moon is much older. The Moon does not consist of oceanic crust but of mantle material, which originated inside the proto-Earth in the Precambrian.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Zhang_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Zhang-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Accretion">
        Accretion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Accretion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The hypothesis of accretion suggests that the Earth and the Moon formed together as a double system from the primordial
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Formation_of_the_Moon_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Formation_of_the_Moon-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The problem with this hypothesis is that it does not explain the angular momentum of the Earth-Moon system or why the Moon has a relatively small iron core compared to the Earth (25% of its radius compared to 50% for the Earth).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-The_Formation_of_the_Moon_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-The_Formation_of_the_Moon-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2011">
        2011
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2011, it was theorized that a second moon existed 4.5 billion years ago, and later had an impact with the Moon, as a part of the accretion process in the formation of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2013">
        2013
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: 2013">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       One hypothesis, presented only as a possibility, was that the Earth took the Moon from Venus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taken_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-taken-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="2017">
        2017
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Origin_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: 2017">
