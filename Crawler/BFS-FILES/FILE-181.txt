      <p>
       A
       <b>
        transit of Phobos
       </b>
       across the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       takes place when
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       passes directly between the Sun and a point on the surface of Mars, obscuring a large part of the Sun's disc for an observer on Mars. During a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
        transit
       </a>
       , Phobos can be seen from Mars as a large black disc rapidly moving across the face of the Sun. At the same time, the shadow (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antumbra" title="Antumbra">
        antumbra
       </a>
       ) of Phobos moves across the Martian surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       The event could also be regarded as a particularly quick and shallow
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annular_solar_eclipse" title="Annular solar eclipse">
        annular solar eclipse
       </a>
       by Phobos.
      </p>

      <p>
       A transit of Phobos from Mars usually lasts only thirty seconds or so, due to the moon's very rapid
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       of about 7.6 hours.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Phobos orbits close to Mars and in line with its
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       , transits of Phobos occur somewhere on Mars on most days of the Martian year. Its orbital inclination is 1.08°, so the latitude of its shadow projected onto the Martian surface shows a seasonal variation, moving from 70.4°S to 70.4°N and back again over the course of a Martian year. Phobos is so close to Mars that it is not visible south of 70.4°S or north of 70.4°N; for some days in the year, its shadow misses the surface entirely and falls north or south of Mars.
      </p>
      <p>
       At any given geographical location on the surface of Mars, there are two intervals in a Martian year when the shadow of Phobos or Deimos is passing through its latitude. During each such interval, about half a dozen transits of Phobos can be seen by observers at that geographical location (compared to zero or one
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars" title="Transit of Deimos from Mars">
        transits of Deimos
       </a>
       ). Transits of Phobos happen during Martian autumn and winter in the respective hemisphere; close to the equator they happen around the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" title="Autumnal equinox">
        autumnal equinox
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">
        vernal equinox
       </a>
       , while farther from the equator they happen closer to the
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Observers at high latitudes (but less than 70.4°) will see a noticeably smaller angular diameter for Phobos because they are considerably farther away from it than observers at Mars's equator. As a result, transits of Phobos for such observers will cover less of the Sun's disk. Because it orbits so close to Mars, Phobos cannot be seen north of 70.4°N or south of 70.4°S; observers at such latitudes will obviously not see transits, either.
      </p>

        </i>
        rover
       </a>
       photographed transits of Phobos on 7, 10 and 12 March 2004. In the captions below, the first row shows Earth time
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       and the second row shows
       <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars" title="Timekeeping on Mars">
        Martian local solar time
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Images_of_the_shadow">
        Images of the shadow
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Phobos_from_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Images of the shadow">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Viewed from orbit, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
        penumbral
       </a>
       shadow of Phobos can be seen to move rapidly over the Martian surface. This shadow on the Martian surface has been photographed on many occasions by
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">
        Mars Global Surveyor
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Viking_1">
        Viking 1
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>

      <p>
       In the 1970s, the
       <a href="/wiki/Viking_1" title="Viking 1">
        Viking 1
       </a>
       Lander and Orbiter photographed the shadow as well. The Lander detected the penumbral shadow of Phobos passing across it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was detected only as a slight dimming of the ambient light; the Viking Lander camera did not image the Sun. The shadow took about 20 seconds to pass over the Lander, moving at about 2 km/s. The shadow was simultaneously imaged from the Viking 1 Orbiter, which permitted locating the position of the lander in the orbiter pictures.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="26_August_1999_image">
        26 August 1999 image
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Phobos_from_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 26 August 1999 image">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_shadow.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="232" height="486" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Phobos_shadow.jpg/220px-Phobos_shadow.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/d/d1/Phobos_shadow.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_shadow.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
          penumbral
         </a>
         shadow of Phobos on the Martian landscape, as seen by
         <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">
          Mars Global Surveyor
         </a>
         on 26 August 1999. The center of the shadow was at 10.9°N 49.2°W at 04:00:33
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
          UTC
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Far more detailed images of the shadow were taken with the arrival in 1997 of the
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">
        Mars Global Surveyor
       </a>
       and its high-resolution Mars Orbital Camera. One such image was taken on 26 August 1999 and shows the shadow at high resolution. This image was featured in a 1 November 1999 NASA press release.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By examining maps of Mars
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       we see the shadow is centered at around 10.9°N 49.2°W.
      </p>
      <p>
       We can also look up the original image files at M04-03241 (red)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and M04-03242 (blue),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       part of the gallery of MOC Wide-Angle Images, Region Lunae Palus, Subphase M04.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The "image start time" was 03:26:13.01
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       , the "line integration time" is 80.4800 milliseconds, and the "downtrack summing" factor is 4. Since the shadow is centered at 6400 pixels from the bottom of the original 10800-pixel-high image (Mars Global Surveyor had a south-to-north Sun-synchronous orbit), we add (6400 × 0.08048 × 4) = 2060.3 seconds = 34 minutes 20.3 seconds to get a time of 04:00:33.3 UTC for the center of the shadow. Putting in the longitude/latitude/altitude coordinate values -310.8,10.9,0 into JPL Horizons
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       we see that the predicted time of mid-transit was 04:00:36
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       , in perfect agreement within the error of determining the exact longitude and latitude of the center of the shadow. This was about 14:41 Martian local solar time, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's altitude was 46.5° above the horizon. JPL Horizons also shows that on 26 August 1999 the distance between the Earth and Mars was 9.6 light-minutes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_images">
        Other images
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Phobos_from_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Other images">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_shadow_1999-09-01.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="288" height="391" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Phobos_shadow_1999-09-01.gif/220px-Phobos_shadow_1999-09-01.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/3b/Phobos_shadow_1999-09-01.gif 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_shadow_1999-09-01.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
          penumbral
         </a>
         shadow of
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         on
         <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
          Mars
         </a>
         on 1 September 1999 as imaged by
         <a href="/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor" title="Mars Global Surveyor">
          Mars Global Surveyor
         </a>
         . The center of the shadow was at 14°N 236°W at 20:49:02.4
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
          UTC
         </a>
         . The text label of "20:13:05" represents the image start time.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Many dozens of other images of the shadow exist, but these are much lower resolution (by a factor of 27/4). Three such images are shown in the 1 November 1999 NASA press release.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Note the timestamps printed on the photos in the NASA press release do
       <i>
        not
       </i>
       correspond to the actual time that the shadow was imaged, rather they represent the "image start time" of a vertically much larger original image. Mars Global Surveyor orbits Mars in a Sun-synchronous polar orbit with
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       117.65 minutes, moving from south pole to north pole, and continuously points its camera straight down. The result is an image in the form of a very long thin vertical strip, where the pixels in the top part of the image are imaged nearly one hour after those in the bottom part of the image. In principle the image could be as much as 43200 pixels high, but "downtrack summing" is used to merge adjacent lines. For instance, a downtrack summing factor of 27 causes every 27 lines to be merged into one, resulting in an image 1600 pixels high. Thus to determine the actual time that the Phobos shadow was imaged, it is necessary to locate the original image and measure how many pixels from the bottom of the image the shadow is found and add the corresponding offset to the image start time.
      </p>
      <p>
       For example, we examine the image labeled with the timestamp 1 September 1999 20:13:05 (UTC). We have the original images M07-00166 (red)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and M07-00167 (blue),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       part of the gallery of MOC Global-Map Images, Subphase M07.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shadow is situated at roughly 14°N 236°W.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In this case the image start time is 20:13:04.69
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       , the line integration time is 80.48 milliseconds, and the downtrack summing factor is 27. The shadow is about 8 pixels high, centered at 993 pixels from the bottom of the original 1600-pixel-high image. We add (993 × 0.08048 × 27) = 2157.75 seconds = 35 minutes 57.75 seconds to get a time of 20:49:02.4 UTC for the center of the shadow.
      </p>
      <p>
       Putting in the longitude/latitude/altitude coordinate values -124,14,0 into JPL Horizons gives an expected time of 20:49 for the transit, which is in good agreement once again. On 1 September 1999 the distance between the Earth and Mars was 9.9 light-minutes.
      </p>
