      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Parker Solar Probe
        </b>
       </i>
       (previously
       <i>
        <b>
         Solar Probe
        </b>
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        <b>
         Solar Probe Plus
        </b>
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        <b>
         Solar Probe+
        </b>
       </i>
       ) is a planned
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       robotic spacecraft to probe the outer
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20170531_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20170531-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It will approach to within 8.5
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">
        solar radii
       </a>
       (5.9 million kilometers or 3.67 million miles) to the 'surface' (
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       ) of the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The project was announced in the fiscal 2009 budget year.
       <a href="/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University" title="Johns Hopkins University">
        Johns Hopkins University
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Applied_Physics_Laboratory" title="Applied Physics Laboratory">
        Applied Physics Laboratory
       </a>
       designed and built the spacecraft, which was originally scheduled to launch in 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The launch date has since been rescheduled to the summer of 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-launch_date_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-launch_date-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was the first time a NASA spacecraft was named after a living person, honoring physicist
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">
        Eugene Parker
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Parker Solar Probe
       </i>
       concept originates from a predecessor
       <i>
        Solar Orbiter
       </i>
       project conceived in the 1990s. Similar in design and objectives, the
       <i>
        Solar Probe
       </i>
       mission served as one of the centerpieces of the eponymous Outer Planet/Solar Probe (OPSP) program formulated by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       . The first three missions of the program were planned to be the
       <i>
        Solar Orbiter
       </i>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       reconnaissance mission
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pluto_Kuiper_Express" title="Pluto Kuiper Express">
         Pluto Kuiper Express
        </a>
       </i>
       , and the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Europa_Orbiter" title="Europa Orbiter">
         Europa Orbiter
        </a>
       </i>
       astrobiology mission focused on
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Following the appointment of
       <a href="/wiki/Sean_O%27Keefe" title="Sean O'Keefe">
        Sean O'Keefe
       </a>
       as
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Administrators_and_Deputy_Administrators_of_NASA" title="List of Administrators and Deputy Administrators of NASA">
        Administrator of NASA
       </a>
       , the entirety of the OPSP program was cancelled as part of President
       <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">
        George W. Bush
       </a>
       's request for the
       <a href="/wiki/2003_United_States_federal_budget" title="2003 United States federal budget">
        2003 United States federal budget
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com-1_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-1-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Administrator O'Keefe cited a need for a restructuring of NASA and its projects, falling in line with the Bush Administration's wish for NASA to refocus on "research and development, and addressing management shortcomings."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com-1_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-1-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The cancellation of the program also resulted in the initial cancellation of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       , the mission that eventually won the competition to replace
       <i>
        Pluto Kuiper Express
       </i>
       in the former OPSP program.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That mission, which would eventually be launched as the first mission of the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Frontiers_program" title="New Frontiers program">
        New Frontiers program
       </a>
       , a conceptual successor to the OPSP program, would undergo a lengthy political battle to secure funding for its launch, which occurred in 2006.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Plans for the
       <i>
        Solar Probe
       </i>
       mission would later manifest as the
       <i>
        Solar Probe Plus
       </i>
       in the early 2010s.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In May 2017, the
       <i>
        Solar Probe Plus
       </i>
       was renamed
       <i>
        Parker Solar Probe
       </i>
       in honor of astrophysicist
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">
        Eugene Parker
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun%27s_Apparent_Size_as_Seen_From_Earth_vs_From_Solar_Probe_Plus%27s_orbit.png">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

          </a>
         </div>
         Apparent size of the Sun as seen from the orbit of the
         <i>
          Parker Solar Probe
         </i>
         compared to its apparent size seen from Earth.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/220px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/330px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg/440px-Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Voyager_2_velocity_vs_distance_from_sun.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Plot of Voyager 2's heliocentric velocity against its distance from the Sun, illustrating the use of gravity assist to accelerate the spacecraft by Jupiter, Saturn and Uranus.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
          <a href="#cite_note-17">
           [17]
          </a>
         </sup>
         Using the same technique but with Venus,
         <i>
          Parker Solar Probe
         </i>
         will decelerate to lower its orbit.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Solar Parker Probe
       </i>
       will be the first spacecraft to fly into the low
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       . It will determine the structure and dynamics of the Sun's coronal magnetic field, understand how the solar corona and wind are heated and accelerated, and determine what processes accelerate energetic particles. The
       <i>
        Parker Solar Probe
       </i>
       mission design uses repeated
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">
        gravity assists
       </a>
       at Venus to incrementally decrease its orbital
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       to achieve multiple passes of the Sun at approximately 8.5
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_radii" title="Solar radii">
        solar radii
       </a>
       , or about 6 million km (3.7 million mi; 0.040 AU).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The spacecraft's systems are designed to endure the extreme radiation and heat near the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , where the incident solar intensity is approximately 520 times the intensity at
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       orbit, by the use of a solar shadow-shield. The solar shield is 11.4 cm (4.5 in) thick and is made of
       <a href="/wiki/Reinforced_carbon%E2%80%93carbon" title="Reinforced carbon–carbon">
        reinforced carbon–carbon
       </a>
       composite, which is designed to withstand temperatures outside the spacecraft of about 1,377 °C (2,511 °F).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shield is hexagonal and is mounted at the Sun-facing side of the spacecraft. The spacecraft systems and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_instruments" title="Scientific instruments">
        scientific instruments
       </a>
       are located in the central portion of the shield's shadow, where direct radiation from the Sun is fully blocked. The primary power for the mission will be a dual system of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">
        solar panels
       </a>
       (photovoltaic array). A primary photovoltaic array, used for the portion of the mission outside
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7010373994676750000♠
        </span>
        0.25 AU
       </span>
       , is retracted behind the shadow shield during the close approach to the Sun, and a much smaller secondary array powers the spacecraft through closest approach. This secondary array uses pumped-fluid cooling to maintain
       <a href="/wiki/Operating_temperature" title="Operating temperature">
        operating temperature
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trajectory">
        Trajectory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Trajectory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The spacecraft trajectory will include seven Venus flybys over nearly seven years to gradually shrink its elliptical orbit around the Sun, for a total of 24 orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-extreme_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-extreme-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The science phase will take place during those 7 years, focusing on the periods when the spacecraft is closest to the Sun. The near Sun radiation environment is predicted to cause both spacecraft charging effects, radiation damage in materials and electronics, and communication interruptions, so the orbit will be highly elliptical with short times spent near the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The trajectory requires high launch energy, so the probe will be launched on a Delta IV Heavy class launch vehicle and an upper stage based on the STAR-48B solid rocket motor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Interplanetary
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">
        gravity assists
       </a>
       will provide further deceleration relative to its
       <a href="/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit" title="Heliocentric orbit">
        heliocentric orbit
       </a>
       , which may result in a heliocentric speed record at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delta_4_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delta_4-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As the probe passes around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , it will achieve a velocity of up to 200 km/s (120 mi/s), which will temporarily make it the fastest manmade object, almost three times as fast as the current record holder,
       <a href="/wiki/Helios_(spacecraft)#Helios-B" title="Helios (spacecraft)">
        Helios-B
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Like every object in an orbit, due to gravity the spacecraft will accelerate as it nears perihelion, then slow down again afterwards until it reaches its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">
        aphelion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_goals">
        Scientific goals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Scientific goals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The goals of the mission are:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Determine the structure and dynamics of the
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
         magnetic fields
        </a>
        at the sources of
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
         solar wind
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Trace the flow of energy that heats the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
         corona
        </a>
        and accelerates the solar wind.
       </li>
       <li>
        Determine what mechanisms accelerate and transport energetic particles.
       </li>
       <li>
        Explore dusty
        <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
         plasma
        </a>
        near the Sun and its influence on
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
         solar wind
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">
         solar energetic particles
        </a>
        formation.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Investigations">
        Investigations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Parker_Solar_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Investigations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In order to achieve these goals, the mission will perform five major experiments or investigations:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_2016_21-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox_2016-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Electromagnetic Fields Investigation (
        <b>
         FIELDS
        </b>
        ) — This investigation will make direct measurements of
        <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">
         electric
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
         magnetic fields
        </a>
        , radio waves,
        <a href="/wiki/Poynting_vector" title="Poynting vector">
         Poynting flux
        </a>
        , absolute
        <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
         plasma
        </a>
        density, and electron temperature. Its main instrument is a
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetometer#Fluxgate_magnetometer" title="Magnetometer">
         flux-gate magnetometer
        </a>
        . The Principal investigator is Stuart Bale, at the
        <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
         University of California, Berkeley
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Integrated Science Investigation of the Sun (
        <b>
         ISOIS
        </b>
        )— This investigation will measure energetic electrons, protons and heavy ions. The instrument suite is composed of two independent instruments, EPI-Hi and EPI-Lo. The Principal investigator is David McComas, at the
        <a href="/wiki/Princeton_University" title="Princeton University">
         Princeton University
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Wide-field Imager for Solar PRobe (
        <b>
         WISPR
        </b>
        )— These optical telescopes will acquire images of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
         solar corona
        </a>
        and inner
        <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
         heliosphere
        </a>
        . The Principal Investigator is Russell Howard, at the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="Naval Research Laboratory">
         Naval Research Laboratory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Solar Wind Electrons Alphas and Protons (
        <b>
         SWEAP
        </b>
        )— This investigation will count the electrons, protons and helium ions, and measure their properties such as velocity, density, and temperature. Its main instruments are two
        <a href="/wiki/Electrostatic_analyzer" title="Electrostatic analyzer">
         electrostatic analyzers
        </a>
        and a
        <a href="/wiki/Faraday_cup" title="Faraday cup">
         Faraday cup
        </a>
        . The Principal Investigator is Justin Kasper at the
        <a href="/wiki/University_of_Michigan" title="University of Michigan">
         University of Michigan
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
         Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        Heliospheric origins with Solar Probe Plus (
        <b>
         HeliOSPP
        </b>
        ) is a theory and modeling investigation to maximize the scientific return from the mission. The Principal Investigator is Marco Velli.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       After the first Venus fly-by, the probe will be in an elliptical orbit with a period of 150 days (two-thirds the period of Venus), making three orbits while Venus makes two. On the second fly-by, the period shortens to 130 days. After less than two orbits (only 198 days later) it encounters Venus a third time at a point earlier in the orbit of Venus. This encounter shortens its period to half of that of Venus, or about 112.5 days. After two orbits it meets Venus a fourth time at about the same place, shortening its period to about 102 days. After 237 days it meets Venus for the fifth time and its period is shortened to about 96 days, three-sevenths that of Venus. It then makes seven orbits while Venus makes three. The sixth encounter, almost two years after the fifth, brings its period down to 92 days, two-fifths that of Venus. After five more orbits (two orbits of Venus) it meets Venus for the seventh and last time, decreasing its period to 88 or 89 days and allowing it to approach close to the Sun.
