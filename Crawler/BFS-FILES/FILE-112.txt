      <p>
       An
       <b>
        optical spectrometer
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        spectrophotometer
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        spectrograph
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        spectroscope
       </b>
       ) is an instrument used to measure properties of
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       over a specific portion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       , typically used in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_analysis" title="Spectroscopic analysis">
        spectroscopic analysis
       </a>
       to identify materials.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The variable measured is most often the light's
       <a href="/wiki/Intensity_(physics)" title="Intensity (physics)">
        intensity
       </a>
       but could also, for instance, be the
       <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">
        polarization
       </a>
       state. The independent variable is usually the
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelength
       </a>
       of the light or a unit directly proportional to the
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photon
       </a>
       energy, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Wavenumber" title="Wavenumber">
        reciprocal centimeters
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electron_volt" title="Electron volt">
        electron volts
       </a>
       , which has a reciprocal relationship to wavelength.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       is used in
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       for producing
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       and measuring their
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       and intensities. Spectrometers may also operate over a wide range of non-optical wavelengths, from
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
        gamma rays
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-rays
       </a>
       into the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">
        far infrared
       </a>
       . If the instrument is designed to measure the spectrum in
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Absolute_units&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Absolute units (page does not exist)">
        absolute units
       </a>
       rather than
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Relative_units&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Relative units (page does not exist)">
        relative units
       </a>
       , then it is typically called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectrophotometer" title="Spectrophotometer">
        spectrophotometer
       </a>
       . The majority of spectrophotometers are used in spectral regions near the visible spectrum.
      </p>
      <p>
       In general, any particular instrument will operate over a small portion of this total range because of the different techniques used to measure different portions of the spectrum. Below optical frequencies (that is, at
       <a href="/wiki/Microwave" title="Microwave">
        microwave
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       frequencies), the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum_analyzer" title="Spectrum analyzer">
        spectrum analyzer
       </a>
       is a closely related electronic device.
      </p>
      <p>
       Spectrometers are used in many fields. For example, they are used in astronomy to analyze the radiation from astronomical objects and deduce chemical composition. The spectrometer uses a prism or a grating to spread the light from a distant object into a spectrum. This allows astronomers to detect many of the chemical elements by their characteristic spectral fingerprints. If the object is glowing by itself, it will show spectral lines caused by the glowing gas itself. These lines are named for the elements which cause them, such as the hydrogen alpha, beta, and gamma lines. Chemical compounds may also be identified by absorption. Typically these are dark bands in specific locations in the spectrum caused by energy being absorbed as light from other objects passes through a gas cloud. Much of our knowledge of the chemical makeup of the universe comes from spectra.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <p>
       Spectroscopes are often used in
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and some branches of
       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
        chemistry
       </a>
       . Early spectroscopes were simply
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triangular_prism_(optics)" title="Triangular prism (optics)">
        prisms
       </a>
       with graduations marking wavelengths of light. Modern spectroscopes generally use a
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction_grating" title="Diffraction grating">
        diffraction grating
       </a>
       , a movable
       <a href="/wiki/Diffraction#Single-slit_diffraction" title="Diffraction">
        slit
       </a>
       , and some kind of
       <a href="/wiki/Photodetector" title="Photodetector">
        photodetector
       </a>
       , all automated and controlled by a
       <a href="/wiki/Computer" title="Computer">
        computer
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">
        Joseph von Fraunhofer
       </a>
       developed the first modern spectroscope by combining a prism, diffraction slit and
       <a href="/wiki/Refracting_telescope" title="Refracting telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       in a manner that increased the spectral resolution and was reproducible in other laboratories. Fraunhofer also went on to invent the first diffraction spectroscope.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_37_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brand_37-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gustav_Robert_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Robert Kirchhoff">
        Gustav Robert Kirchhoff
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Bunsen" title="Robert Bunsen">
        Robert Bunsen
       </a>
       discovered the application of spectroscopes to chemical analysis and used this approach to discover
       <a href="/wiki/Caesium" title="Caesium">
        caesium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">
        rubidium
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Kirchhoff and Bunsen's analysis also enabled a chemical explanation of
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy#Stars_and_their_properties" title="Astronomical spectroscopy">
        stellar spectra
       </a>
       , including
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
        Fraunhofer lines
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brand_63_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brand_63-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When a material is heated to
       <a href="/wiki/Incandescence" title="Incandescence">
        incandescence
       </a>
       it emits
       <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
        light
       </a>
       that is characteristic of the atomic makeup of the material. Particular light frequencies give rise to sharply defined bands on the scale which can be thought of as fingerprints. For example, the element
       <a href="/wiki/Sodium" title="Sodium">
        sodium
       </a>
       has a very characteristic double yellow band known as the Sodium D-lines at 588.9950 and 589.5924 nanometers, the color of which will be familiar to anyone who has seen a low pressure
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_lamp" title="Sodium vapor lamp">
        sodium vapor lamp
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the original spectroscope design in the early 19th century, light entered a slit and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Collimating_lens" title="Collimating lens">
        collimating lens
       </a>
       transformed the light into a thin beam of parallel rays. The light then passed through a prism (in hand-held spectroscopes, usually an
       <a href="/wiki/Amici_prism" title="Amici prism">
        Amici prism
       </a>
       ) that
       <a href="/wiki/Refraction" title="Refraction">
        refracted
       </a>
       the beam into a spectrum because different wavelengths were refracted different amounts due to
       <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
        dispersion
       </a>
       . This image was then viewed through a tube with a scale that was transposed upon the spectral image, enabling its direct measurement.
      </p>
      <p>
       With the development of
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_film" title="Photographic film">
        photographic film
       </a>
       , the more accurate
       <a href="#Spectrographs">
        spectrograph
       </a>
       was created. It was based on the same principle as the spectroscope, but it had a camera in place of the viewing tube. In recent years, the electronic circuits built around the
       <a href="/wiki/Photomultiplier" title="Photomultiplier">
        photomultiplier
       </a>
       tube have replaced the camera, allowing real-time spectrographic analysis with far greater accuracy. Arrays of photosensors are also used in place of film in spectrographic systems. Such spectral analysis, or spectroscopy, has become an important scientific tool for analyzing the composition of unknown material and for studying astronomical phenomena and testing astronomical theories.
      </p>
      <p>
       In modern spectrographs in the UV, visible, and near-IR spectral ranges, the spectrum is generally given in the form of photon number per unit wavelength (nm or μm), wavenumber (μm
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       , cm
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ), frequency (THz), or energy (eV), with the units indicated by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abscissa" title="Abscissa">
        abscissa
       </a>
       . In the mid- to far-IR, spectra are typically expressed in units of Watts per unit wavelength (μm) or wavenumber (cm
       <sup>
        −1
       </sup>
       ). In many cases, the spectrum is displayed with the units left implied (such as "digital counts" per spectral channel).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectrographs">
        Spectrographs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Optical_spectrometer&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spectrographs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrograph" title="Spectrograph">
        spectrograph
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Simple_spectroscope.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3185" data-file-width="2000" height="319" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/200px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/300px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Simple_spectroscope.jpg/400px-Simple_spectroscope.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Simple_spectroscope.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A very simple spectroscope based on a prism
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A spectrograph is an instrument that separates an incoming wave into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Frequency_spectrum" title="Frequency spectrum">
        frequency spectrum
       </a>
       . There are several kinds of machines referred to as
       <i>
        spectrographs
       </i>
       , depending on the precise nature of the waves. The first spectrographs used
       <a href="/wiki/Photographic_paper" title="Photographic paper">
        photographic paper
       </a>
       as the detector. The star
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        spectral classification
       </a>
       and discovery of the
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main sequence
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble%27s_law" title="Hubble's law">
        Hubble's law
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_sequence" title="Hubble sequence">
        Hubble sequence
       </a>
       were all made with spectrographs that used photographic paper. The plant pigment
       <a href="/wiki/Phytochrome" title="Phytochrome">
        phytochrome
       </a>
       was discovered using a spectrograph that used living plants as the detector. More recent spectrographs use electronic detectors, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Charge-coupled_device" title="Charge-coupled device">
        CCDs
       </a>
       which can be used for both visible and
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        UV
       </a>
       light. The exact choice of detector depends on the wavelengths of light to be recorded.
      </p>
      <p>
       A spectrograph is sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Polychromator" title="Polychromator">
        polychromator
       </a>
       , as an analogy to
       <a href="/wiki/Monochromator" title="Monochromator">
        monochromator
       </a>
       .
      </p>
