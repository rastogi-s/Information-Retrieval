      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Hertzsprung–Russell diagram
       </b>
       , abbreviated
       <b>
        H–R diagram
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        HRD
       </b>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Scatter_plot" title="Scatter plot">
        scatter plot
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       showing the relationship between the stars'
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">
        absolute magnitudes
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Luminosity" title="Luminosity">
        luminosities
       </a>
       versus their
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        stellar classifications
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        effective temperatures
       </a>
       . More simply, it plots each star on a graph measuring the star's brightness against its temperature (color). It does not map any locations of stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       The diagram was created circa 1910 by
       <a href="/wiki/Ejnar_Hertzsprung" title="Ejnar Hertzsprung">
        Ejnar Hertzsprung
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Norris_Russell" title="Henry Norris Russell">
        Henry Norris Russell
       </a>
       and represents a major step towards an understanding of
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">
        stellar evolution
       </a>
       or "the way in which stars undergo sequences of dynamic and radical changes over time".
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_background">
        Historical background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the nineteenth-century large-scale photographic spectroscopic surveys of stars were performed at
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard_College_Observatory" title="Harvard College Observatory">
        Harvard College Observatory
       </a>
       , producing spectral classifications for tens of thousands of stars, culminating ultimately in the
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Draper_Catalogue" title="Henry Draper Catalogue">
        Henry Draper Catalogue
       </a>
       . In one segment of this work
       <a href="/wiki/Antonia_Maury" title="Antonia Maury">
        Antonia Maury
       </a>
       included divisions of the stars by the width of their
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral lines
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HCAn28_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HCAn28-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hertzsprung noted that stars described with narrow lines tended to have smaller
       <a href="/wiki/Proper_motion" title="Proper motion">
        proper motions
       </a>
       than the others of the same spectral classification. He took this as an indication of greater luminosity for the narrow-line stars, and computed
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_parallax#Other_baselines" title="Stellar parallax">
        secular parallaxes
       </a>
       for several groups of these, allowing him to estimate their absolute magnitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hertzsprung1909_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hertzsprung1909-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1910 Hans Rosenberg published a diagram plotting the apparent magnitude of stars in the Pleiades cluster against the strengths of the
       <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">
        Calcium
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/K-line_(spectrometry)" title="K-line (spectrometry)">
        K line
       </a>
       and two
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        Hydrogen
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Balmer_line" title="Balmer line">
        Balmer lines
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenberg_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rosenberg-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These spectral lines serve as a proxy for the temperature of the star, an early form of spectral classification. The apparent magnitude of stars in the same cluster is equivalent to their absolute magnitude and so this early diagram was effectively a plot of luminosity against temperature. The same type of diagram is still used today as a means of showing the stars in clusters without having to initially know their distance and luminosity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cluster_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cluster-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hertzsprung had already been working with this type of diagram, but his first publications showing it were not until 1911. This was also the form of the diagram using apparent magnitudes of a cluster of stars all at the same distance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hertzsprung_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hertzsprung-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Russell's early (1913) versions of the diagram included Maury's giant stars identified by Hertzsprung, those nearby stars with parallaxes measured at the time, stars from the
       <a href="/wiki/Hyades_(star_cluster)" title="Hyades (star cluster)">
        Hyades
       </a>
       (a nearby
       <a href="/wiki/Open_cluster" title="Open cluster">
        open cluster
       </a>
       ), and several
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_groups" title="Moving groups">
        moving groups
       </a>
       , for which the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moving_cluster_method" title="Moving cluster method">
        moving cluster method
       </a>
       could be used to derive distances and thereby obtain absolute magnitudes for those stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Russ1914PA_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Russ1914PA-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Forms_of_diagram">
        Forms of diagram
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Forms of diagram">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are several forms of the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram, and the
       <a href="/wiki/Nomenclature" title="Nomenclature">
        nomenclature
       </a>
       is not very well defined. All forms share the same general layout: stars of greater luminosity are toward the top of the diagram, and stars with higher surface temperature are toward the left side of the diagram.
      </p>
      <p>
       The original diagram displayed the spectral type of stars on the horizontal axis and the
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">
        absolute visual magnitude
       </a>
       on the vertical axis. The spectral type is not a numerical quantity, but the sequence of spectral types is a
       <a href="/wiki/Monotonic_function" title="Monotonic function">
        monotonic series
       </a>
       that reflects the stellar surface temperature. Modern observational versions of the chart replace spectral type by a
       <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">
        color index
       </a>
       (in diagrams made in the middle of the 20th Century, most often the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B-V_color" title="B-V color">
        B-V color
       </a>
       ) of the stars. This type of diagram is what is often called an observational Hertzsprung–Russell diagram, or specifically a color-magnitude diagram (CMD), and it is often used by observers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In cases where the stars are known to be at identical distances such as within a star cluster, a color-magnitude diagram is often used to describe the stars of the cluster with a plot in which the vertical axis is the
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of the stars. For cluster members, by assumption there is a single additive constant difference between their apparent and absolute magnitudes, called the
       <a href="/wiki/Distance_modulus" title="Distance modulus">
        distance modulus
       </a>
       , for all of that cluster of stars. Early studies of nearby open clusters (like the Hyades and
       <a href="/wiki/Pleiades" title="Pleiades">
        Pleiades
       </a>
       ) by Hertzsprung and Rosenberg produced the first CMDs, antedating by a few years Russell's influential synthesis of the diagram collecting data for all stars for which absolute magnitudes could be determined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenberg_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-rosenberg-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hertzsprung_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-hertzsprung-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another form of the diagram plots the
       <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
        effective surface temperature
       </a>
       of the star on one axis and the luminosity of the star on the other, almost invariably in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Log-log_plot" title="Log-log plot">
        log-log plot
       </a>
       . Theoretical calculations of
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_structure" title="Stellar structure">
        stellar structure
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution#Models" title="Stellar evolution">
        evolution of stars
       </a>
       produce plots that match those from observations. This type of diagram could be called
       <i>
        temperature-luminosity diagram
       </i>
       , but this term is hardly ever used; when the distinction is made, this form is called the
       <i>
        theoretical Hertzsprung–Russell diagram
       </i>
       instead. A peculiar characteristic of this form of the H–R diagram is that the temperatures are plotted from high temperature to low temperature, which aids in comparing this form of the H–R diagram with the observational form.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the two types of diagrams are similar, astronomers make a sharp distinction between the two. The reason for this distinction is that the exact transformation from one to the other is not trivial. To go between effective temperature and color requires a
       <a href="/wiki/Color_temperature#Color_temperature_in_astronomy" title="Color temperature">
        color-temperature relation
       </a>
       , and constructing that is difficult; it is known to be a function of
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_chemistry" title="Stellar chemistry">
        stellar composition
       </a>
       and can be affected by other factors like
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_rotation" title="Stellar rotation">
        stellar rotation
       </a>
       . When converting luminosity or absolute
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolometric_magnitude" title="Bolometric magnitude">
        bolometric magnitude
       </a>
       to apparent or absolute visual magnitude, one requires a
       <a href="/wiki/Bolometric_correction" title="Bolometric correction">
        bolometric correction
       </a>
       , which may or may not come from the same source as the color-temperature relation. One also needs to know the distance to the observed objects (
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       , the distance modulus) and the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">
        interstellar obscuration
       </a>
       , both in the color (reddening) and in the apparent magnitude (where the effect is called "extinction"). Color distortion (including reddening) and extinction (obscuration) are also apparent in stars having significant
       <a href="/wiki/Circumstellar_dust" title="Circumstellar dust">
        circumstellar dust
       </a>
       . The ideal of direct comparison of theoretical predictions of stellar evolution to observations thus has additional uncertainties incurred in the conversions between theoretical quantities and observations.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation">
        Interpretation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Interpretation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most of the stars occupy the region in the diagram along the line called the
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main sequence
       </a>
       . During the stage of their lives in which stars are found on the main sequence line, they are
       <a href="/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain_reaction" title="Proton–proton chain reaction">
        fusing hydrogen
       </a>
       in their cores. The next concentration of stars is on the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_branch" title="Horizontal branch">
        horizontal branch
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helium_fusion" title="Helium fusion">
        helium fusion
       </a>
       in the core and hydrogen burning in a shell surrounding the core). Another prominent feature is the
       <a href="/wiki/Hertzsprung_gap" title="Hertzsprung gap">
        Hertzsprung gap
       </a>
       located in the region between A5 and G0 spectral type and between +1 and −3 absolute magnitudes (
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       between the top of the main sequence and the giants in the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizontal_branch" title="Horizontal branch">
        horizontal branch
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">
        RR Lyrae variable
       </a>
       stars can be found in the left of this gap on a section of the diagram called the
       <a href="/wiki/Instability_strip" title="Instability strip">
        instability strip
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">
        Cepheid variables
       </a>
       also fall on the instability strip, at higher luminosities.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HR-diag-instability-strip.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="800" data-file-width="700" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HR-diag-instability-strip.svg/220px-HR-diag-instability-strip.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HR-diag-instability-strip.svg/330px-HR-diag-instability-strip.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/HR-diag-instability-strip.svg/440px-HR-diag-instability-strip.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:HR-diag-instability-strip.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An HR diagram with the
         <a href="/wiki/Instability_strip" title="Instability strip">
          instability strip
         </a>
         and its components highlighted.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The H-R diagram can be used by scientists to roughly measure how far away a
       <a href="/wiki/Star_cluster" title="Star cluster">
        star cluster
       </a>
       is from Earth. This can be done by comparing the apparent magnitudes of the stars in the cluster to the absolute magnitudes of stars with known distances (or of model stars). The observed group is then shifted in the vertical direction, until the two main sequences overlap. The difference in magnitude that was bridged in order to match the two groups is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Distance_modulus" title="Distance modulus">
        distance modulus
       </a>
       and is a direct measure for the distance (ignoring
       <a href="/wiki/Extinction_(astronomy)" title="Extinction (astronomy)">
        extinction
       </a>
       ). This technique is known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main_sequence_fitting" title="Main sequence fitting">
        main sequence fitting
       </a>
       and is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopic_parallax" title="Spectroscopic parallax">
        spectroscopic parallax
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Diagram.27s_role_in_the_development_of_stellar_physics">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagram's_role_in_the_development_of_stellar_physics">
        Diagram's role in the development of stellar physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Hertzsprung%E2%80%93Russell_diagram&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Diagram's role in the development of stellar physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis" title="Stellar nucleosynthesis">
        stellar nucleosynthesis
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Contemplation of the diagram led astronomers to speculate that it might demonstrate
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">
        stellar evolution
       </a>
       , the main suggestion being that stars collapsed from red giants to dwarf stars, then moving down along the line of the main sequence in the course of their lifetimes. Stars were thought therefore to radiate energy by converting gravitational energy into radiation through the
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_mechanism" title="Kelvin–Helmholtz mechanism">
        Kelvin–Helmholtz mechanism
       </a>
       . This mechanism resulted in an age for the Sun of only tens of millions of years, creating a conflict over the age of the Solar System between astronomers, and biologists and geologists who had evidence that the Earth was far older than that. This conflict was only resolved in the 1930s when nuclear fusion was identified as the source of stellar energy.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, following Russell's presentation of the diagram to a meeting of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">
        Royal Astronomical Society
       </a>
       in 1912,
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Eddington" title="Arthur Eddington">
        Arthur Eddington
       </a>
       was inspired to use it as a basis for developing ideas on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_physics" title="Stellar physics">
        stellar physics
       </a>
       . In 1926, in his book
       <i>
        The Internal Constitution of the Stars
       </i>
       he explained the physics of how stars fit on the diagram.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eddington-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The paper anticipated the later discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        nuclear fusion
       </a>
       and correctly proposed that the star's source of power was the combination of hydrogen into helium, liberating enormous energy. This was a particularly remarkable jump of insight, since at that time the source of a star's energy was still unsolved,
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        thermonuclear energy
       </a>
       had not been proven to exist, and even that stars are largely composed of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">
        metallicity
       </a>
       ), had not yet been discovered. Eddington managed to sidestep this problem by concentrating on the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermodynamics" title="Thermodynamics">
        thermodynamics
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_radiation" title="Thermal radiation">
        radiative transport
       </a>
       of energy in stellar interiors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-eddington2_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-eddington2-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eddington predicted that dwarf stars remain in an essentially static position on the main sequence for most of their lives. In the 1930s and 1940s, with an understanding of hydrogen fusion, came an evidence-backed theory of evolution to red giants following which were speculated cases of explosion and implosion of the remnants to white dwarfs. The term
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova_nucleosynthesis" title="Supernova nucleosynthesis">
        supernova nucleosynthesis
       </a>
       is used to describe the creation of elements during the evolution and explosion of a pre-supernova star, a concept put forth by
       <a href="/wiki/Fred_Hoyle" title="Fred Hoyle">
        Fred Hoyle
       </a>
       in 1954.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The pure mathematical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quantum_mechanic" title="Quantum mechanic">
        quantum mechanics
       </a>
       and classical mechanical models of stellar processes enable the Hertzsprung–Russell diagram to be annotated with known conventional paths known as stellar sequences — there continue to be added rarer and more anomalous examples as more stars are analysed and mathematical models considered.
      </p>
