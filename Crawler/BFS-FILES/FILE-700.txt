      <p>
       A
       <b>
        coronal mass ejection
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        CME
       </b>
       ) is a significant release of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . They often follow
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       and are normally present during a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        solar prominence
       </a>
       eruption. The plasma is released into the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       , and can be observed in
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       imagery.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-helios-cme_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-helios-cme-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-msfc-cme_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-msfc-cme-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-noaa-cme_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-noaa-cme-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections are often associated with other forms of solar activity, but a broadly accepted theoretical understanding of these relationships has not been established. CMEs most often originate from active regions on the Sun's surface, such as groupings of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       associated with frequent flares. Near
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maxima
       </a>
       , the Sun produces about three CMEs every day, whereas near
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minima
       </a>
       , there is about one CME every five days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fox_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fox-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections release large quantities of matter and electromagnetic radiation into space above the Sun's surface, either near the corona (sometimes called a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        solar prominence
       </a>
       ), or farther into the planetary system, or beyond (interplanetary CME). The ejected material is a
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        magnetized plasma
       </a>
       consisting primarily of
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">
        protons
       </a>
       . While solar flares are very fast (being electromagnetic radiation), CMEs are relatively slow.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20140921_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20140921-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections are associated with enormous changes and disturbances in the coronal
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       . They are usually observed with a white-light
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        coronagraph
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">
        Cause
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Scientific research
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       has shown that the phenomenon of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       is closely associated with CMEs and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamic
       </a>
       theory, the sudden rearrangement of magnetic field lines when two oppositely directed magnetic fields are brought together is called "magnetic reconnection". Reconnection releases energy stored in the original stressed magnetic fields. These magnetic field lines can become twisted in a helical structure, with a 'right-hand twist' or a 'left hand twist'. As the Sun's magnetic field lines become more and more twisted, CMEs appear to be a 'valve' to release the magnetic energy being built up, as evidenced by the helical structure of CMEs, that would otherwise renew itself continuously each solar cycle and eventually rip the Sun apart.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Green2014_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Green2014-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On the Sun, magnetic reconnection may happen on solar arcades—a series of closely occurring loops of magnetic lines of force. These lines of force quickly reconnect into a low arcade of loops, leaving a helix of magnetic field unconnected to the rest of the arcade. The sudden release of energy during this process causes the solar flare and ejects the CME. The helical magnetic field and the material that it contains may violently expand outwards forming a CME.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciam1_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sciam1-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This also explains why CMEs and solar flares typically erupt from what are known as the active regions on the Sun where magnetic fields are much stronger on average.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_on_Earth">
        Impact on Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Impact on Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When the ejection is directed towards
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and reaches it as an interplanetary CME (ICME), the
       <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
        shock wave
       </a>
       of traveling mass causes a
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       that may disrupt Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       , compressing it on the day side and extending the night-side
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere#Magnetic_tails" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetic tail
       </a>
       . When the magnetosphere
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        reconnects
       </a>
       on the nightside, it releases
       <a href="/wiki/Power_(physics)" title="Power (physics)">
        power
       </a>
       on the order of
       <a href="/wiki/Watt" title="Watt">
        terawatt
       </a>
       scale, which is directed back toward Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere" title="Celestial body atmosphere">
        upper atmosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">
        Solar energetic particles
       </a>
       can cause particularly strong
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        aurorae
       </a>
       in large regions around Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Magnetic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        magnetic poles
       </a>
       . These are also known as the
       <i>
        Northern Lights
       </i>
       (aurora borealis) in the northern hemisphere, and the
       <i>
        Southern Lights
       </i>
       (aurora australis) in the southern hemisphere. Coronal mass ejections, along with solar flares of other origin, can disrupt
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio_transmission" title="Radio transmission">
        radio transmissions
       </a>
       and cause damage to
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_transmission_line" title="Electrical transmission line">
        electrical transmission line
       </a>
       facilities, resulting in potentially massive and long-lasting
       <a href="/wiki/Power_outage" title="Power outage">
        power outages
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awst20130114_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-awst20130114-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Energetic protons released by a CME can cause an increase in the number of free electrons in the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       , especially in the high-latitude polar regions. The increase in free electrons can enhance radio wave absorption, especially within the D-region of the ionosphere, leading to Polar Cap Absorption (PCA) events.
      </p>
      <p>
       Humans at high altitudes, as in airplanes or space stations, risk exposure to relatively intense
       <a href="/wiki/Cosmic_ray" title="Cosmic ray">
        cosmic rays
       </a>
       . The energy absorbed by astronauts is not reduced by a typical spacecraft shield design and, if any protection is provided, it would result from changes in the microscopic inhomogeneity of the energy absorption events.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa-tm4444_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-tm4444-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       A typical coronal mass ejection may have any or all of three distinctive features: a cavity of low electron density, a dense core (the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        prominence
       </a>
       , which appears on coronagraph images as a bright region embedded in this cavity), and a bright leading edge.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most ejections originate from active regions on the Sun's surface, such as groupings of
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       associated with frequent flares. These regions have closed magnetic field lines, in which the magnetic field strength is large enough to contain the plasma. These field lines must be broken or weakened for the ejection to escape from the Sun. However, CMEs may also be initiated in quiet surface regions, although in many cases the quiet region was recently active. During
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minimum
       </a>
       , CMEs form primarily in the coronal streamer belt near the solar magnetic equator. During
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maximum
       </a>
       , they originate from active regions whose latitudinal distribution is more homogeneous.
      </p>
      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections reach velocities from 20 to 3,200 km/s (12 to 1,988 mi/s) with an average speed of 489 km/s (304 mi/s), based on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LASCO_Large_Angle_and_Spectrometric_Coronagraph" title="LASCO Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph">
        LASCO
       </a>
       measurements between 1996 and 2003. These speeds correspond to transit times from the Sun out to the mean radius of Earth's orbit of about 13 hours to 86 days (extremes), with about 3.5 days as the average. The average mass ejected is 1.6
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        12
       </sup>
       kg (3.5
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        12
       </sup>
       lb). However, the estimated mass values for CMEs are only lower limits, because coronagraph measurements provide only two-dimensional data. The frequency of ejections depends on the phase of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       : from about one every fifth day near the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minimum
       </a>
       to 3.5 per day near the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maximum
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These values are also lower limits because ejections propagating away from Earth (backside CMEs) usually cannot be detected by coronagraphs.
      </p>
      <p>
       Current knowledge of coronal mass ejection kinematics indicates that the ejection starts with an initial pre-acceleration phase characterized by a slow rising motion, followed by a period of rapid acceleration away from the Sun until a near-constant velocity is reached. Some
       <i>
        balloon
       </i>
       CMEs, usually the slowest ones, lack this three-stage evolution, instead accelerating slowly and continuously throughout their flight. Even for CMEs with a well-defined acceleration stage, the pre-acceleration stage is often absent, or perhaps unobservable.
      </p>

      <p>
       Coronal mass ejections are often associated with other forms of solar activity, most notably:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
         Solar flares
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Eruptive prominence and X-ray
        <a href="/wiki/Sigmoid_function" title="Sigmoid function">
         sigmoids
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tomczak2012_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Tomczak2012-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Coronal dimming (long-term brightness decrease on the solar surface)
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Moreton_wave" title="Moreton wave">
         Moreton waves
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        Coronal waves (bright fronts propagating from the location of the eruption)
       </li>
       <li>
        Post-eruptive arcades
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The association of a CME with some of those phenomena is common but not fully understood. For example, CMEs and flares are normally closely related, but there was confusion about this point caused by the events originating beyond the limb. For such events no flare could be detected. Most weak flares do not have associated CMEs; most powerful ones do. Some CMEs occur without any flare-like manifestation, but these are the weaker and slower ones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is now thought that CMEs and associated flares are caused by a common event (the CME peak acceleration and the flare impulsive phase generally coincide). In general, all of these events (including the CME) are thought to be the result of a large-scale restructuring of the magnetic field; the presence or absence of a CME during one of these restructures would reflect the coronal environment of the process (i.e., can the eruption be confined by overlying magnetic structure, or will it simply break through and enter the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Theoretical_models">
        Theoretical models
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Theoretical models">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It was first postulated that CMEs might be driven by the heat of an explosive flare. However, it soon became apparent that many CMEs were not associated with flares, and that even those that were often started before the flare. Because CMEs are initiated in the solar corona (which is dominated by magnetic energy), their energy source must be magnetic.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the energy of CMEs is so high, it is unlikely that their energy could be directly driven by emerging magnetic fields in the photosphere (although this is still a possibility). Therefore, most models of CMEs assume that the energy is stored up in the coronal magnetic field over a long period of time and then suddenly released by some instability or a loss of equilibrium in the field. There is still no consensus on which of these release mechanisms is correct, and observations are not currently able to constrain these models very well. These same considerations apply equally well to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       , but the observable signatures of these phenomena differ.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interplanetary_coronal_mass_ejections">
        Interplanetary coronal mass ejections
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Interplanetary coronal mass ejections">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="729" data-file-width="1080" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg/220px-Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg/330px-Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg/440px-Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Coronal_mass_ejection_moving_toward_heliopause.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Illustration of a coronal mass ejection moving beyond the planets toward the
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy)" title="Heliopause (astronomy)">
          heliopause
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       CMEs typically reach Earth one to five days after leaving the Sun. During their propagation, CMEs interact with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">
        interplanetary magnetic field
       </a>
       (IMF). As a consequence, slow CMEs are accelerated toward the speed of the solar wind and fast CMEs are decelerated toward the speed of the solar wind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manoharan2006_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manoharan2006-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The strongest deceleration or acceleration occurs close to the Sun, but it can continue even beyond Earth orbit (1
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        AU
       </a>
       ), which was observed e.g. using measurements at
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(spacecraft)" title="Ulysses (spacecraft)">
        Ulysses spacecraft
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . CMEs faster than about 500 km/s (310 mi/s) eventually drive a
       <a href="/wiki/Shock_wave" title="Shock wave">
        shock wave
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wilkinson2012_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wilkinson2012-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This happens when the speed of the CME in the
       <a href="/wiki/Frame_of_reference" title="Frame of reference">
        frame of reference
       </a>
       moving with the solar wind is faster than the local fast
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosonic_wave" title="Magnetosonic wave">
        magnetosonic
       </a>
       speed. Such shocks have been observed directly by coronagraphs
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the corona, and are related to type II radio bursts. They are thought to form sometimes as low as 2 R
       <sub>
        s
       </sub>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_radius" title="Solar radius">
        solar radii
       </a>
       ). They are also closely linked with the acceleration of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_energetic_particles" title="Solar energetic particles">
        solar energetic particles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_mission_Wind">
        NASA mission
        <i>
         Wind
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: NASA mission Wind">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 1 November 1994,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       launched the
       <a href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">
        WIND
       </a>
       spacecraft as a solar wind monitor to orbit Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       Lagrange point as the interplanetary component of the Global Geospace Science (GGS) Program within the International Solar Terrestrial Physics (ISTP) program. The spacecraft is a spin axis-stabilized satellite that carries eight instruments measuring solar wind particles from thermal to &gt;MeV energies, electromagnetic radiation from DC to 13 MHz radio waves, and gamma-rays. Though the WIND spacecraft is over two decades old, it still provides the highest time, angular, and energy resolution of any of the solar wind monitors. It continues to produce relevant research as its data has contributed to over 150 publications since 2008 alone.

        [
        <i>

        ]

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="NASA_mission_STEREO">
        NASA mission
        <i>
         STEREO
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       On 25 October 2006, NASA launched
       <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">
        STEREO
       </a>
       , two near-identical spacecraft which, from widely separated points in their orbits, are able to produce the first
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stereoscopic" title="Stereoscopic">
        stereoscopic
       </a>
       images of CMEs and other solar activity measurements. The spacecraft orbit the Sun at distances similar to that of Earth, with one slightly ahead of Earth and the other trailing. Their separation gradually increased so that after four years they were almost diametrically opposite each other in orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_traces">
        First traces
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: First traces">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The largest recorded geomagnetic perturbation, resulting presumably from a CME, coincided with the first-observed
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flare
       </a>
       on 1 September 1859, and is now referred to as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carrington_Event" title="Carrington Event">
        Carrington Event
       </a>
       , or the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        solar storm of 1859
       </a>
       . The flare and the associated sunspots were visible to the naked eye (both as the flare itself appearing on a projection of the Sun on a screen and as an aggregate brightening of the solar disc), and the flare was independently observed by English astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">
        R. C. Carrington
       </a>
       and R. Hodgson. The
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       was observed with the recording magnetograph at
       <a href="/wiki/Kew_Gardens" title="Kew Gardens">
        Kew Gardens
       </a>
       . The same instrument recorded a
       <i>
        crochet
       </i>
       , an instantaneous perturbation of Earth's ionosphere by ionizing soft
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-rays
       </a>
       . This could not easily be understood at the time because it predated the discovery of X-rays by
       <a href="/wiki/Wilhelm_R%C3%B6ntgen" title="Wilhelm Röntgen">
        Röntgen
       </a>
       and the recognition of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_E._Kennelly" title="Arthur E. Kennelly">
        Kennelly
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside" title="Oliver Heaviside">
        Heaviside
       </a>
       . The storm took down parts of the recently created US telegraph network, starting fires and shocking some telegraph operators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-awst20130114_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-awst20130114-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Historical records were collected and new observations recorded in annual summaries by the Astronomical Society of the Pacific between 1953 and 1960.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ASOP_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ASOP-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="First_clear_detections">
        First clear detections
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: First clear detections">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first detection of a CME as such was made on 14 December 1971, by R. Tousey (1973) of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naval_Research_Laboratory" title="Naval Research Laboratory">
        Naval Research Laboratory
       </a>
       using the seventh Orbiting Solar Observatory (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OSO-7" title="OSO-7">
        OSO-7
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery image (256 × 256 pixels) was collected on a Secondary Electron Conduction (SEC)
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vidicon" title="Vidicon">
        vidicon
       </a>
       tube, transferred to the instrument computer after being digitized to 7
       <a href="/wiki/Bit" title="Bit">
        bits
       </a>
       . Then it was compressed using a simple run-length encoding scheme and sent down to the ground at 200 bit/s. A full, uncompressed image would take 44 minutes to send down to the ground. The
       <a href="/wiki/Telemetry" title="Telemetry">
        telemetry
       </a>
       was sent to ground support equipment (GSE) which built up the image onto
       <a href="/wiki/Instant_film" title="Instant film">
        Polaroid
       </a>
       print. David Roberts, an electronics technician working for NRL who had been responsible for the testing of the SEC-vidicon camera, was in charge of day-to-day operations. He thought that his camera had failed because certain areas of the image were much brighter than normal. But on the next image the bright area had moved away from the Sun and he immediately recognized this as being unusual and took it to his supervisor, Dr.
       <a href="/wiki/Guenter_Brueckner" title="Guenter Brueckner">
        Guenter Brueckner
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and then to the solar physics branch head, Dr. Tousey. Earlier observations of
       <i>
        coronal transients
       </i>
       or even phenomena observed visually during
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses" title="Solar eclipses">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       are now understood as essentially the same thing.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Recent_events">
        Recent events
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Recent events">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 9 March 1989 a
       <a href="/wiki/March_1989_geomagnetic_storm" title="March 1989 geomagnetic storm">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       occurred. On 13 March 1989 a severe geometric storm struck the Earth. It caused power failures in Quebec, Canada and short-wave radio interference.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 1 August 2010, during
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle_24#August_2010" title="Solar cycle 24">
        solar cycle 24
       </a>
       , scientists at the
       <a href="/wiki/Harvard%E2%80%93Smithsonian_Center_for_Astrophysics" title="Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics">
        Harvard–Smithsonian Center for Astrophysics
       </a>
       (CfA) observed a series of four large CMEs emanating from the Earth-facing hemisphere of the Sun. The initial CME was generated by an eruption on 1 August that was associated with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">
        NOAA
       </a>
       Active Region 1092, which was large enough to be seen without the aid of a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_telescope" title="Solar telescope">
        solar telescope
       </a>
       . The event produced significant
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        aurorae
       </a>
       on Earth three days later.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 23 July 2012, a massive, and potentially damaging,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        solar superstorm
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flare
       </a>
       , CME,
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_electromagnetic_pulse#E3" title="Nuclear electromagnetic pulse">
        solar EMP
       </a>
       ) barely missed Earth, according to NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140723a_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140723a-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140429_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140429-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 31 August 2012 a CME connected with Earth's magnetic environment, or
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       , with a glancing blow causing aurora to appear on the night of 3 September.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        Geomagnetic storming
       </a>
       reached the G2 (
       <a href="/wiki/K-index" title="K-index">
        Kp
       </a>
       =6) level on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">
        NOAA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Weather_Prediction_Center" title="Space Weather Prediction Center">
        Space Weather Prediction Center
       </a>
       scale of geomagnetic disturbances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The 14 October 2014 ICME was photographed by the Sun-watching spacecraft
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PROBA2" title="PROBA2">
        PROBA2
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        ESA
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        Solar and Heliospheric Observatory
       </a>
       (ESA/NASA), and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        Solar Dynamics Observatory
       </a>
       (NASA) as it left the Sun, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/STEREO-A" title="STEREO-A">
        STEREO-A
       </a>
       observed its effects directly at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7011149597870700000♠
        </span>
        1
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
         AU
        </a>
       </span>
       . ESA's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Venus_Express" title="Venus Express">
         Venus Express
        </a>
       </i>
       gathered data. The CME reached
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       on 17 October and was observed by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mars_Express" title="Mars Express">
         Mars Express
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/MAVEN" title="MAVEN">
        MAVEN
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mars_Odyssey" title="Mars Odyssey">
         Mars Odyssey
        </a>
       </i>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Science_Laboratory" title="Mars Science Laboratory">
        Mars Science Laboratory
       </a>
       missions. On 22 October, at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7011463753399170000♠
        </span>
        3.1
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
         AU
        </a>
       </span>
       , it reached comet
       <a href="/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko" title="67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko">
        67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko
       </a>
       , perfectly aligned with the Sun and Mars, and was observed by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rosetta_(spacecraft)" title="Rosetta (spacecraft)">
         Rosetta
        </a>
       </i>
       . On 12 November, at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7012148101891993000♠
        </span>
        9.9
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
         AU
        </a>
       </span>
       , it was observed by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Horizons" title="New Horizons">
         New Horizons
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft was at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7012472729271412000♠
        </span>
        31.6
        <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
         AU
        </a>
       </span>
       approaching
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       when the CME passed three months after the initial eruption, and it may be detectable in the data.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       has data that can be interpreted as the passing of the CME, 17 months after. The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
         Curiosity
        </a>
       </i>
       rover's
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_assessment_detector" title="Radiation assessment detector">
        RAD instrument
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        Mars Odyssey
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Rosetta
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       showed a sudden decrease in galactic cosmic rays (
       <a href="/wiki/Forbush_decrease" title="Forbush decrease">
        Forbush decrease
       </a>
       ) as the CME's protective bubble passed by.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2017-08-14__Witasse_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2017-08-14_Witasse-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2017-08-17_SD_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-2017-08-17_SD-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Future_risk">
        Future risk
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Future risk">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       According to a report published in 2012 by physicist Pete Riley of Predictive Science Inc., the chance of Earth being hit by a Carrington-class storm between 2012 and 2022 is 12%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140723a_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140723a-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Riley2012_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Riley2012-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_coronal_mass_ejections">
        Stellar coronal mass ejections
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Stellar coronal mass ejections">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There have been a small number of CMEs observed on other stars, all of which as of 2016
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coronal_mass_ejection&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       have been found on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M_dwarf" title="M dwarf">
        M dwarfs
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Korhonen2016_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Korhonen2016-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These have been detected by spectroscopy, most often by studying
       <a href="/wiki/Balmer_series" title="Balmer series">
        Balmer lines
       </a>
       : the material ejected toward the observer causes asymmetry in the blue wing of the line profiles due to
       <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
        Doppler shift
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vida2016_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vida2016-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This enhancement can be seen in absorption when it occurs on the stellar disc (the material is cooler than its surrounding), and in emission when it is outside the disc. The observed projected velocities of CMEs range from ≈84 to 5,800 km/s (52 to 3,600 mi/s).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leitzinger2011_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Leitzinger2011-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Houdebine1990_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Houdebine1990-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Compared to activity on the Sun, CME activity on other stars seems to be far less common.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vida2016_37-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vida2016-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Leitzinger2014_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Leitzinger2014-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
