      <p>
       A
       <b>
        variable star
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       whose brightness as seen from Earth (its
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       ) fluctuates.
      </p>
      <p>
       This variation may be caused by a change in emitted light or by something partly blocking the light, so variable stars are classified as either:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Intrinsic variables, whose luminosity actually changes; for example, because the star periodically swells and shrinks.
       </li>
       <li>
        Extrinsic variables, whose apparent changes in brightness are due to changes in the amount of their light that can reach Earth; for example, because the star has an orbiting companion that sometimes eclipses it.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Many, possibly most, stars have at least some variation in luminosity: the energy output of our
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , for example, varies by about 0.1% over an 11-year
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An ancient Egyptian calendar of lucky and unlucky days composed some 3,200 years ago may be the oldest preserved historical document of the discovery of a variable star, the eclipsing binary
       <a href="/wiki/Algol" title="Algol">
        Algol
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the modern astronomers, the first variable star was identified in 1638 when
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Phocylides_Holwarda" title="Johannes Phocylides Holwarda">
        Johannes Holwarda
       </a>
       noticed that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Omicron_Ceti" title="Omicron Ceti">
        Omicron Ceti
       </a>
       (later named Mira) pulsated in a cycle taking 11 months; the star had previously been described as a nova by
       <a href="/wiki/David_Fabricius" title="David Fabricius">
        David Fabricius
       </a>
       in 1596. This discovery, combined with
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">
        supernovae
       </a>
       observed in 1572 and 1604, proved that the starry sky was not eternally invariable as
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       and other ancient philosophers had taught. In this way, the discovery of variable stars contributed to the astronomical revolution of the sixteenth and early seventeenth centuries.
      </p>
      <p>
       The second variable star to be described was the eclipsing variable Algol, by
       <a href="/wiki/Geminiano_Montanari" title="Geminiano Montanari">
        Geminiano Montanari
       </a>
       in 1669;
       <a href="/wiki/John_Goodricke" title="John Goodricke">
        John Goodricke
       </a>
       gave the correct explanation of its variability in 1784.
       <a href="/wiki/Chi_Cygni" title="Chi Cygni">
        Chi Cygni
       </a>
       was identified in 1686 by
       <a href="/wiki/Gottfried_Kirch" title="Gottfried Kirch">
        G. Kirch
       </a>
       , then
       <a href="/wiki/R_Hydrae" title="R Hydrae">
        R Hydrae
       </a>
       in 1704 by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Maraldi" title="Giovanni Domenico Maraldi">
        G. D. Maraldi
       </a>
       . By 1786 ten variable stars were known. John Goodricke himself discovered
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">
        Delta Cephei
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Lyrae" title="Beta Lyrae">
        Beta Lyrae
       </a>
       . Since 1850 the number of known variable stars has increased rapidly, especially after 1890 when it became possible to identify variable stars by means of photography.
      </p>
      <p>
       The latest edition of the
       <a href="/wiki/General_Catalogue_of_Variable_Stars" title="General Catalogue of Variable Stars">
        General Catalogue of Variable Stars
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (2008) lists more than 46,000 variable stars in the Milky Way, as well as 10,000 in other galaxies, and over 10,000 'suspected' variables.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detecting_variability">
        Detecting variability
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Detecting variability">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most common kinds of variability involve changes in brightness, but other types of variability also occur, in particular changes in the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       . By combining
       <a href="/wiki/Light_curve" title="Light curve">
        light curve
       </a>
       data with observed spectral changes, astronomers are often able to explain why a particular star is variable.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variable_star_observations">
        Variable star observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Variable star observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1434" data-file-width="1509" height="209" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg/220px-New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg/330px-New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg/440px-New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:New_View_of_the_Great_Nebula_in_Carina.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A photogenic variable star,
         <a href="/wiki/Eta_Carinae" title="Eta Carinae">
          Eta Carinae
         </a>
         , embedded in the
         <a href="/wiki/Carina_Nebula" title="Carina Nebula">
          Carina Nebula
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Variable stars are generally analysed using
       <a href="/wiki/Photometry_(astronomy)" title="Photometry (astronomy)">
        photometry
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrophotometry" title="Spectrophotometry">
        spectrophotometry
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       . Measurements of their changes in brightness can be plotted to produce
       <a href="/wiki/Light_curve" title="Light curve">
        light curves
       </a>
       . For regular variables, the
       <a href="/wiki/Frequency" title="Frequency">
        period
       </a>
       of variation and its
       <a href="/wiki/Amplitude" title="Amplitude">
        amplitude
       </a>
       can be very well established; for many variable stars, though, these quantities may vary slowly over time, or even from one period to the next. Peak brightnesses in the light curve are known as
       <b>
        maxima
       </b>
       , while troughs are known as
       <b>
        minima
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_astronomy" title="Amateur astronomy">
        Amateur astronomers
       </a>
       can do useful scientific study of variable stars by visually comparing the star with other stars within the same
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescopic
       </a>
       field of view of which the magnitudes are known and constant. By estimating the variable's magnitude and noting the time of observation a visual lightcurve can be constructed. The
       <a href="/wiki/American_Association_of_Variable_Star_Observers" title="American Association of Variable Star Observers">
        American Association of Variable Star Observers
       </a>
       collects such observations from participants around the world and shares the data with the scientific community.
      </p>
      <p>
       From the
       <b>
        light curve
       </b>
       the following data are derived:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        are the brightness variations periodical, semiperiodical, irregular, or unique?
       </li>
       <li>
        what is the
        <b>
         period
        </b>
        of the brightness fluctuations?
       </li>
       <li>
        what is the
        <b>
         shape
        </b>
        of the light curve (symmetrical or not, angular or smoothly varying, does each cycle have only one or more than one minima, etcetera)?
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       From the
       <b>
        spectrum
       </b>
       the following data are derived:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        what kind of star is it: what is its temperature, its
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Luminosity_class" title="Luminosity class">
         luminosity class
        </a>
        (
        <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_star" title="Dwarf star">
         dwarf star
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">
         giant star
        </a>
        ,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">
         supergiant
        </a>
        , etc.)?
       </li>
       <li>
        is it a single star, or a binary? (the combined spectrum of a binary star may show elements from the spectra of each of the member stars)
       </li>
       <li>
        does the spectrum change with time? (for example, the star may turn hotter and cooler periodically)
       </li>
       <li>
        changes in brightness may depend strongly on the part of the spectrum that is observed (for example, large variations in visible light but hardly any changes in the infrared)
       </li>
       <li>
        if the wavelengths of spectral lines are shifted this points to movements (for example, a periodical swelling and shrinking of the star, or its rotation, or an expanding gas shell) (
        <a href="/wiki/Doppler_effect" title="Doppler effect">
         Doppler effect
        </a>
        )
       </li>
       <li>
        strong magnetic fields on the star betray themselves in the spectrum
       </li>
       <li>
        abnormal emission or absorption lines may be indication of a hot stellar atmosphere, or gas clouds surrounding the star.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In very few cases it is possible to make pictures of a stellar disk. These may show darker spots on its surface.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretation_of_observations">
        Interpretation of observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Interpretation of observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Combining light curves with spectral data often gives a clue as to the changes that occur in a variable star. For example, evidence for a pulsating star is found in its shifting spectrum because its surface periodically moves toward and away from us, with the same frequency as its changing brightness.
      </p>
      <p>
       About two-thirds of all variable stars appear to be pulsating. In the 1930s astronomer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arthur_Stanley_Eddington" title="Arthur Stanley Eddington">
        Arthur Stanley Eddington
       </a>
       showed that the mathematical equations that describe the interior of a star may lead to instabilities that cause a star to pulsate. The most common type of instability is related to oscillations in the degree of ionization in outer, convective layers of the star.
      </p>
      <p>
       Suppose the star is in the swelling phase. Its outer layers expand, causing them to cool. Because of the decreasing temperature the degree of ionization also decreases. This makes the gas more transparent, and thus makes it easier for the star to radiate its energy. This in turn will make the star start to contract. As the gas is thereby compressed, it is heated and the degree of ionization again increases. This makes the gas more opaque, and radiation temporarily becomes captured in the gas. This heats the gas further, leading it to expand once again. Thus a cycle of expansion and compression (swelling and shrinking) is maintained.
      </p>
      <p>
       The pulsation of cepheids is known to be driven by oscillations in the ionization of
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       (from He
       <sup>
        ++
       </sup>
       to He
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
       and back to He
       <sup>
        ++
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nomenclature">
        Nomenclature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Nomenclature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Variable_star_designation" title="Variable star designation">
        Variable star designation
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In a given constellation, the first variable stars discovered were designated with letters R through Z, e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/R_Andromedae" title="R Andromedae">
        R Andromedae
       </a>
       . This system of
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions" title="Astronomical naming conventions">
        nomenclature
       </a>
       was developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Argelander" title="Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander">
        Friedrich W. Argelander
       </a>
       , who gave the first previously unnamed variable in a constellation the letter R, the first letter not used by
       <a href="/wiki/Bayer_designation" title="Bayer designation">
        Bayer
       </a>
       . Letters RR through RZ, SS through SZ, up to ZZ are used for the next discoveries, e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae" title="RR Lyrae">
        RR Lyrae
       </a>
       . Later discoveries used letters AA through AZ, BB through BZ, and up to QQ through QZ (with J omitted). Once those 334 combinations are exhausted, variables are numbered in order of discovery, starting with the prefixed V335 onwards.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
        Classification
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Classification">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Variable stars may be either
       <i>
        intrinsic
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        extrinsic
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         Intrinsic variable stars
        </b>
        : stars where the variability is being caused by changes in the physical properties of the stars themselves. This category can be divided into three subgroups.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Pulsating variables, stars whose radius alternately expands and contracts as part of their natural evolutionary ageing processes.
         </li>
         <li>
          Eruptive variables, stars who experience eruptions on their surfaces like flares or mass ejections.
         </li>
         <li>
          Cataclysmic or explosive variables, stars that undergo a cataclysmic change in their properties like
          <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">
           novae
          </a>
          and
          <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">
           supernovae
          </a>
          .
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Extrinsic variable stars
        </b>
        : stars where the variability is caused by external properties like rotation or eclipses. There are two main subgroups.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Eclipsing binaries,
          <a href="/wiki/Double_star" title="Double star">
           double stars
          </a>
          where, as seen from
          <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
           Earth
          </a>
          's vantage point the stars occasionally eclipse one another as they orbit.
         </li>
         <li>
          Rotating variables, stars whose variability is caused by phenomena related to their rotation. Examples are stars with extreme "sunspots" which affect the apparent brightness or stars that have fast rotation speeds causing them to become ellipsoidal in shape.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       These subgroups themselves are further divided into specific types of variable stars that are usually named after their prototype. For example, dwarf novae are designated
       <i>
        U Geminorum
       </i>
       stars after the first recognized star in the class,
       <i>
        U Geminorum
       </i>
       .
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:HR-vartype.svg">

        </div>
       </div>

      <p>
       Examples of types within these divisions are given below.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pulsating_variable_stars">
        Pulsating variable stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Pulsating variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stellar_pulsations" title="Stellar pulsations">
        Stellar pulsations
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The pulsating stars swell and shrink, affecting their brightness and spectrum. Pulsations are generally split into:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radial_pulsations" title="Radial pulsations">
        radial
       </a>
       , where the entire star expands and shrinks as a whole; and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Non-radial&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Non-radial (page does not exist)">
        non-radial
       </a>
       , where one part of the star expand while another part shrinks. Some scientists consider non-radial pulsations to encompass everything, with radial pulsations as a special case, but considering them as mutually exclusive is convenient since they generally vary with one type or the other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-asteroseismology_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-asteroseismology-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Depending on the type of pulsation and its location within the star, there is a natural or
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">
        fundamental frequency
       </a>
       which determines the period of the star. Stars may also pulsate in a
       <a href="/wiki/Harmonic" title="Harmonic">
        harmonic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Overtone" title="Overtone">
        overtone
       </a>
       which is a higher frequency, corresponding to a shorter period. Pulsating variable stars sometimes have a single well-defined period, but often they pulsate simultaneously with multiple frequencies and complex analysis is required to determine the separate
       <a href="/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation)" title="Interference (wave propagation)">
        interfering
       </a>
       periods. In some cases, the pulsations do not have a defined frequency, causing a random variation, referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Stochastic" title="Stochastic">
        stochastic
       </a>
       . The study of stellar interiors using their pulsations is
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroseismology" title="Asteroseismology">
        asteroseismology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A pulsation in a star must be caused by an unbalanced driving force with a feedback mechanism. In pulsating variable stars the driving force is the internal energy of the star, usually from
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        nuclear fusion
       </a>
       , but in some cases just from stored energy, as it propagates outwards. At certain locations on the HR diagram, corresponding to particular combinations of temperatures, size, and internal chemistry, the outward flow of energy by radiation varies strongly with the density or temperature of the material through which it is passing. When the
       <a href="/wiki/Opacity_(optics)" title="Opacity (optics)">
        opacity
       </a>
       of a layer is high, that layer blocks the radiation, absorbing it and hence it grows hotter and expands. As the layer expands it eventually cools, its ionization drops and becomes more transparent to radiation, allowing it to cool further, until it cools enough to become more dense and fall back into the star, thereby increasing its temperature and starting the cycle again, resulting in regular pulsations. This generally occurs as the ionisation level of the material changes, for example the ionisation of helium in yellow stars on the instability strip.
      </p>
      <p>
       The expansion phase of a pulsation is caused by the blocking of the internal energy flow by material with a high opacity, but this must occur at a particular depth of the star to create visible pulsations. If the expansion occurs below a convective zone then no variation will be visible at the surface. If the expansion occurs too close to the surface the restoring force will be too weak to create a pulsation. The restoring force to create the contraction phase of a pulsation can be pressure if the pulsation occurs in a non-degenerate layer deep inside a star, and this is called an
       <a href="/wiki/Acoustics" title="Acoustics">
        acoustic
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Pressure" title="Pressure">
        pressure
       </a>
       mode of pulsation, abbreviated to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-mode_star" title="P-mode star">
        p-mode
       </a>
       . In other cases, the restoring force is simple
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       and this is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_wave" title="Gravity wave">
        g-mode
       </a>
       . Pulsating variable stars typically pulsate in only one of these modes.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cepheids_and_cepheid-like_variables">
        Cepheids and cepheid-like variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Cepheids and cepheid-like variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Cepheid_variable" title="Cepheid variable">
        Cepheid variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       This group consists of several kinds of pulsating stars, all found on the
       <a href="/wiki/Instability_strip" title="Instability strip">
        instability strip
       </a>
       , that swell and shrink very regularly caused by the star's own mass
       <a href="/wiki/Resonance" title="Resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       , generally by the
       <a href="/wiki/Fundamental_frequency" title="Fundamental frequency">
        fundamental frequency
       </a>
       . Generally the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/%CE%9A_mechanism" title="Κ mechanism">
        Eddington valve
       </a>
       mechanism for pulsating variables is believed to account for cepheid-like pulsations. Each of the subgroups on the instability strip has a fixed relationship between period and absolute magnitude, as well as a relation between period and mean density of the star. The period-luminosity relationship was first established for Delta Cepheids, and makes the high luminosity Cepheids very useful for determining distances to galaxies within the
       <a href="/wiki/Local_Group" title="Local Group">
        Local Group
       </a>
       and beyond.
       <a href="/wiki/Edwin_Hubble" title="Edwin Hubble">
        Edwin Hubble
       </a>
       used this method to prove that the so-called spiral nebulae are in fact distant galaxies.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note that the Cepheids are named only for
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">
        Delta Cephei
       </a>
       , while a completely separate class of variables is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Cephei" title="Beta Cephei">
        Beta Cephei
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical_Cepheid_variables">
        Classical Cepheid variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Classical Cepheid variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Cepheid_variable" title="Classical Cepheid variable">
        Classical Cepheid variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Classical Cepheids (or Delta Cephei variables) are population I (young, massive, and luminous) yellow supergiants which undergo pulsations with very regular periods on the order of days to months. On September 10, 1784,
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Pigott" title="Edward Pigott">
        Edward Pigott
       </a>
       detected the variability of
       <a href="/wiki/Eta_Aquilae" title="Eta Aquilae">
        Eta Aquilae
       </a>
       , the first known representative of the class of Cepheid variables. However, the namesake for classical Cepheids is the star
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_Cephei" title="Delta Cephei">
        Delta Cephei
       </a>
       , discovered to be variable by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Goodricke" title="John Goodricke">
        John Goodricke
       </a>
       a few months later.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Type_II_Cepheids">
        Type II Cepheids
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Type II Cepheids">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Type_II_Cepheids" title="Type II Cepheids">
        Type II Cepheids
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Type II Cepheids (historically termed W Virginis stars) have extremely regular light pulsations and a luminosity relation much like the δ Cephei variables, so initially they were confused with the latter category. Type II Cepheids stars belong to older
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">
        Population II
       </a>
       stars, than do the type I Cepheids. The Type II have somewhat lower
       <a href="/wiki/Metallicity" title="Metallicity">
        metallicity
       </a>
       , much lower mass, somewhat lower luminosity, and a slightly offset period verses luminosity relationship, so it is always important to know which type of star is being observed.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="RR_Lyrae_variables">
        RR Lyrae variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: RR Lyrae variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/RR_Lyrae_variable" title="RR Lyrae variable">
        RR Lyrae variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These stars are somewhat similar to Cepheids, but are not as luminous and have shorter periods. They are older than type I Cepheids, belonging to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Population_II" title="Population II">
        Population II
       </a>
       , but of lower mass than type II Cepheids. Due to their common occurrence in
       <a href="/wiki/Globular_cluster" title="Globular cluster">
        globular clusters
       </a>
       , they are occasionally referred to as
       <i>
        cluster Cepheids
       </i>
       . They also have a well established period-luminosity relationship, and so are also useful as distance indicators. These A-type stars vary by about 0.2–2 magnitudes (20% to over 500% change in luminosity) over a period of several hours to a day or more.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Delta_Scuti_variables">
        Delta Scuti variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Delta Scuti variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_Scuti_variable" title="Delta Scuti variable">
        Delta Scuti variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Delta Scuti (δ Sct) variables are similar to Cepheids but much fainter and with much shorter periods. They were once known as
       <i>
        Dwarf Cepheids
       </i>
       . They often show many superimposed periods, which combine to form an extremely complex light curve. The typical δ Scuti star has an amplitude of 0.003–0.9 magnitudes (0.3% to about 130% change in luminosity) and a period of 0.01–0.2 days. Their
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_classification" title="Stellar classification">
        spectral type
       </a>
       is usually between A0 and F5.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SX_Phoenicis_variables">
        SX Phoenicis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: SX Phoenicis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/SX_Phoenicis_variable" title="SX Phoenicis variable">
        SX Phoenicis variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These stars of spectral type A2 to F5, similar to δ Scuti variables, are found mainly in globular clusters. They exhibit fluctuations in their brightness in the order of 0.7 magnitude (about 100% change in luminosity) or so every 1 to 2 hours.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rapidly_oscillating_Ap_variables">
        Rapidly oscillating Ap variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Rapidly oscillating Ap variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Rapidly_oscillating_Ap_star" title="Rapidly oscillating Ap star">
        Rapidly oscillating Ap star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These stars of spectral type A or occasionally F0, a sub-class of δ Scuti variables found on the main sequence. They have extremely rapid variations with periods of a few minutes and amplitudes of a few thousandths of a magnitude.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Long_period_variables">
        Long period variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Long period variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Long_period_variable" title="Long period variable">
        Long period variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The long period variables are cool evolved stars that pulsate with periods in the range of weeks to several years.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mira_variables">
        Mira variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Mira variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Mira_variable" title="Mira variable">
        Mira variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Mira variables are AGB red giants. Over periods of many months they fade and brighten by between 2.5 and 11
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        magnitudes
       </a>
       , a sixfold to 30 thousandfold change in luminosity.
       <a href="/wiki/Mira" title="Mira">
        Mira
       </a>
       itself, also known as Omicron Ceti (ο Cet), varies in brightness from almost 2nd magnitude to as faint as 10th magnitude with a period of roughly 332 days. The very large visual amplitudes are mainly due to the shifting of energy output between visual and infra-red as the temperature of the star changes. In a few cases, Mira variables show dramatic period changes over a period of decades, thought to be related to the thermal pulsing cycle of the most advanced AGB stars.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Semiregular_variables">
        Semiregular variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Semiregular variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semiregular_variable" title="Semiregular variable">
        Semiregular variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giants" title="Red giants">
        red giants
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_supergiant" title="Red supergiant">
        supergiants
       </a>
       . Semiregular variables may show a definite period on occasion, but more often show less well-defined variations that can sometimes be resolved into multiple periods. A well-known example of a semiregular variable is
       <a href="/wiki/Betelgeuse" title="Betelgeuse">
        Betelgeuse
       </a>
       , which varies from about magnitudes +0.2 to +1.2 (a factor 2.5 change in luminosity). At least some of the semi-regular variables are very closely related to Mira variables, possibly the only difference being pulsating in a different harmonic.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slow_irregular_variables">
        Slow irregular variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Slow irregular variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Slow_irregular_variable" title="Slow irregular variable">
        Slow irregular variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_giants" title="Red giants">
        red giants
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_supergiant" title="Red supergiant">
        supergiants
       </a>
       with little or no detectable periodicity. Some are poorly studied semiregular variables, often with multiple periods, but others may simply be chaotic.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Long_secondary_period_variables">
        Long secondary period variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Long secondary period variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Long-period_variable_star#Long_secondary_periods" title="Long-period variable star">
        Long-period variable star § Long secondary periods
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Many variable red giants and supergiants show variations over several hundred to several thousand days. The brightness may change by several magnitudes although it is often much smaller, with the more rapid primary variations are superimposed. The reasons for this type of variation are not clearly understood, being variously ascribed to pulsations, binarity, and stellar rotation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-messina_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-messina-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Beta_Cephei_variables">
        Beta Cephei variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Beta Cephei variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Cephei_variable" title="Beta Cephei variable">
        Beta Cephei variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Beta Cephei (β Cep) variables (sometimes called
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Canis_Majoris" title="Beta Canis Majoris">
        Beta Canis Majoris
       </a>
       variables, especially in Europe)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       undergo short period pulsations in the order of 0.1–0.6 days with an amplitude of 0.01–0.3 magnitudes (1% to 30% change in luminosity). They are at their brightest during minimum contraction. Many stars of this kind exhibits multiple pulsation periods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Slowly_pulsating_B-type_stars">
        Slowly pulsating B-type stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Slowly pulsating B-type stars">

      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Slowly_pulsating_B-type_star" title="Slowly pulsating B-type star">
        Slowly pulsating B-type star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Slowly pulsating B (APB) stars are hot main-sequence stars slightly less luminous than the Beta Cephei stars, with longer periods and larger amplitudes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spb_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spb-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Very_rapidly_pulsating_hot_.28subdwarf_B.29_stars">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Very_rapidly_pulsating_hot_(subdwarf_B)_stars">
        Very rapidly pulsating hot (subdwarf B) stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Very rapidly pulsating hot (subdwarf B) stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Subdwarf_B_star#Variables" title="Subdwarf B star">
        Subdwarf B star § Variables
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The prototype of this rare class is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V361_Hydrae" title="V361 Hydrae">
        V361 Hydrae
       </a>
       , a 15th magnitude
       <a href="/wiki/Subdwarf_B_star" title="Subdwarf B star">
        subdwarf B star
       </a>
       . They pulsate with periods of a few minutes and may simultaneous pulsate with multiple periods. They have amplitudes of a few hundredths of a magnitude and are given the GCVS acronym RPHS. They are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/P-mode_star" title="P-mode star">
        p-mode
       </a>
       pulsators.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kilkenny_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-kilkenny-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="PV_Telescopii_variables">
        PV Telescopii variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: PV Telescopii variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/PV_Telescopii_variable" title="PV Telescopii variable">
        PV Telescopii variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Stars in this class are type Bp supergiants with a period of 0.1–1 day and an amplitude of 0.1 magnitude on average. Their spectra are peculiar by having weak
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       while on the other hand
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       lines are extra strong, a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Extreme_helium_star" title="Extreme helium star">
        Extreme helium star
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="RV_Tauri_variables">
        RV Tauri variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=25" title="Edit section: RV Tauri variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/RV_Tauri_variable" title="RV Tauri variable">
        RV Tauri variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These are yellow supergiant stars (actually low mass post-AGB stars at the most luminous stage of their lives) which have alternating deep and shallow minima. This double-peaked variation typically has periods of 30–100 days and amplitudes of 3–4 magnitudes. Superimposed on this variation, there may be long-term variations over periods of several years. Their spectra are of type F or G at maximum light and type K or M at minimum brightness. They lie near the instability strip, cooler than type I Cepheids more luminous than type II Cepheids. Their pulsations are caused by the same basic mechanisms related to helium opacity, but they are at a very different stage of their lives.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alpha_Cygni_variables">
        Alpha Cygni variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=26" title="Edit section: Alpha Cygni variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_Cygni_variable" title="Alpha Cygni variable">
        Alpha Cygni variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Alpha Cygni (α Cyg) variables are nonradially pulsating supergiants of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_class" title="Spectral class">
        spectral classes
       </a>
       B
       <sub>
        ep
       </sub>
       to A
       <sub>
        ep
       </sub>
       Ia. Their periods range from several days to several weeks, and their amplitudes of variation are typically of the order of 0.1 magnitudes. The light changes, which often seem irregular, are caused by the superposition of many oscillations with close periods.
       <a href="/wiki/Deneb" title="Deneb">
        Deneb
       </a>
       , in the constellation of
       <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation)" title="Cygnus (constellation)">
        Cygnus
       </a>
       is the prototype of this class.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma_Doradus_variables">
        Gamma Doradus variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=27" title="Edit section: Gamma Doradus variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Doradus_variable" title="Gamma Doradus variable">
        Gamma Doradus variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Gamma Doradus (γ Dor) variables are non-radially pulsating main-sequence stars of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_classes" title="Spectral classes">
        spectral classes
       </a>
       F to late A. Their periods are around one day and their amplitudes typically of the order of 0.1 magnitudes.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pulsating_white_dwarfs">
        Pulsating white dwarfs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=28" title="Edit section: Pulsating white dwarfs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Pulsating_white_dwarf" title="Pulsating white dwarf">
        Pulsating white dwarf
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These non-radially pulsating stars have short periods of hundreds to thousands of seconds with tiny fluctuations of 0.001 to 0.2 magnitudes. Known types of pulsating white dwarf (or pre-white dwarf) include the
       <i>
        DAV
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        ZZ Ceti
       </i>
       , stars, with hydrogen-dominated atmospheres and the spectral type DA;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-physrev_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-physrev-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        DBV
       </i>
       , or
       <i>
        V777 Her
       </i>
       , stars, with helium-dominated atmospheres and the spectral type DB;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wden_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wden-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        GW Vir
       </i>
       stars, with atmospheres dominated by helium, carbon, and oxygen. GW Vir stars may be subdivided into
       <i>
        DOV
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        PNNV
       </i>
       stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-quirion_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-quirion-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar-like_oscillations">
        Solar-like oscillations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Solar-like oscillations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       oscillates with very low amplitude in a large number of modes having periods around 5 minutes. The study of these oscillations is known as
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       . Oscillations in the Sun are driven stochastically by
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       in its outer layers. The term
       <a href="/wiki/Solar-like_oscillations" title="Solar-like oscillations">
        solar-like oscillations
       </a>
       is used to describe oscillations in other stars that are excited in the same way and the study of these oscillations is one of the main areas of active research in the field of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroseismology" title="Asteroseismology">
        asteroseismology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="BLAP_variables">
        BLAP variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=30" title="Edit section: BLAP variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BLAP_(Blue_Large-Amplitude_Pulsators)" title="BLAP (Blue Large-Amplitude Pulsators)">
        BLAP (Blue Large-Amplitude Pulsators)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A Blue Large-Amplitude Pulsator (BLAP) is a pulsating star characterized by changes of 0.2 to 0.4 magnitudes with typical periods of 20 to 40 minutes.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eruptive_variable_stars">
        Eruptive variable stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=31" title="Edit section: Eruptive variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Eruptive variable stars show irregular or semi-regular brightness variations caused by material being lost from the star, or in some cases being accreted to it. Despite the name these are not explosive events, those are the cataclysmic variables.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Protostars">
        Protostars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=32" title="Edit section: Protostars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre%E2%80%93main-sequence_star" title="Pre–main-sequence star">
        Pre–main-sequence star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Protostars are young objects that have not yet completed the process of contraction from a gas nebula to a veritable star. Most protostars exhibit irregular brightness variations.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span id="Herbig_Ae.2FBe_stars">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Herbig_Ae/Be_stars">
        Herbig Ae/Be stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=33" title="Edit section: Herbig Ae/Be stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbig_Ae/Be_stars" title="Herbig Ae/Be stars">
        Herbig Ae/Be stars
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Variability of more massive (2–8
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       mass)
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herbig_Ae/Be_stars" title="Herbig Ae/Be stars">
        Herbig Ae/Be stars
       </a>
       is thought to be due to gas-dust clumps, orbiting in the circumstellar disks.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orion_variables">
        Orion variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Orion variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Orion_variable" title="Orion variable">
        Orion variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Orion variables are young, hot
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre%E2%80%93main-sequence_star" title="Pre–main-sequence star">
        pre–main-sequence stars
       </a>
       usually embedded in nebulosity. They have irregular periods with amplitudes of several magnitudes. A well-known subtype of Orion variables are the
       <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">
        T Tauri
       </a>
       variables. Variability of
       <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">
        T Tauri stars
       </a>
       is due to spots on the stellar surface and gas-dust clumps, orbiting in the circumstellar disks.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="FU_Orionis_variables">
        FU Orionis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=35" title="Edit section: FU Orionis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/FU_Orionis_star" title="FU Orionis star">
        FU Orionis star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These stars reside in reflection nebulae and show gradual increases in their luminosity in the order of 6 magnitudes followed by a lengthy phase of constant brightness. They then dim by 2 magnitudes (six times dimmer) or so over a period of many years.
       <i>
        V1057 Cygni
       </i>
       for example dimmed by 2.5 magnitude (ten times dimmer) during an eleven-year period. FU Orionis variables are of spectral type A through G and are possibly an evolutionary phase in the life of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/T_Tauri_star" title="T Tauri star">
         T Tauri
        </a>
       </i>
       stars.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Giants_and_supergiants">
        Giants and supergiants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Giants and supergiants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Large stars lose their matter relatively easily. For this reason variability due to eruptions and mass loss is fairly common among giants and supergiants.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Luminous_blue_variables">
        Luminous blue variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=37" title="Edit section: Luminous blue variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Luminous_blue_variable" title="Luminous blue variable">
        Luminous blue variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Also known as the
       <a href="/wiki/S_Doradus" title="S Doradus">
        S Doradus
       </a>
       variables, the most luminous stars known belong to this class. Examples include the
       <a href="/wiki/Hypergiant" title="Hypergiant">
        hypergiants
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Eta_Carinae" title="Eta Carinae">
        η Carinae
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/P_Cygni" title="P Cygni">
        P Cygni
       </a>
       . They have permanent high mass loss, but at intervals of years internal pulsations cause the star to exceed its Eddington limit and the mass loss increases hugely. Visual brightness increases although the overall luminosity is largely unchanged. Giant eruptions observed in a few LBVs do increase the luminosity, so much so that they have been tagged
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova_impostor" title="Supernova impostor">
        supernova impostors
       </a>
       , and may be a different type of event.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Yellow_hypergiants">
        Yellow hypergiants
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=38" title="Edit section: Yellow hypergiants">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Yellow_hypergiant" title="Yellow hypergiant">
        Yellow hypergiant
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These massive evolved stars are unstable due to their high luminosity and position above the instability strip, and they exhibit slow but sometimes large photometric and spectroscopic changes due to high mass loss and occasional larger eruptions, combined with secular variation on an observable timescale. The best known example is
       <a href="/wiki/Rho_Cassiopeiae" title="Rho Cassiopeiae">
        Rho Cassiopeiae
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="R_Coronae_Borealis_variables">
        R Coronae Borealis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=39" title="Edit section: R Coronae Borealis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/R_Coronae_Borealis_variable" title="R Coronae Borealis variable">
        R Coronae Borealis variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       While classed as eruptive variables, these stars do not undergo periodic increases in brightness. Instead they spend most of their time at maximum brightness, but at irregular intervals they suddenly fade by 1–9 magnitudes (2.5 to 4000 times dimmer) before recovering to their initial brightness over months to years. Most are classified as yellow supergiants by luminosity, although they are actually post-AGB stars, but there are both red and blue giant R CrB stars.
       <a href="/wiki/R_Coronae_Borealis" title="R Coronae Borealis">
        R Coronae Borealis
       </a>
       (R CrB) is the prototype star.
       <a href="/wiki/DY_Persei_variable" title="DY Persei variable">
        DY Persei variables
       </a>
       are a subclass of R CrB variables that have a periodic variability in addition to their eruptions.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Wolf.E2.80.93Rayet_variables">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Wolf–Rayet_variables">
        Wolf–Rayet variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=40" title="Edit section: Wolf–Rayet variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf%E2%80%93Rayet_star" title="Wolf–Rayet star">
        Wolf–Rayet star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Wolf–Rayet stars are massive hot stars that sometimes show variability, probably due to several different causes including binary interactions and rotating gas clumps around the star. They exhibit broad emission line spectra with
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       lines. Variations in some stars appear to be stochastic while others show multiple periods.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma_Cassiopeiae_variables">
        Gamma Cassiopeiae variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=41" title="Edit section: Gamma Cassiopeiae variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma_Cassiopeiae_variable" title="Gamma Cassiopeiae variable">
        Gamma Cassiopeiae variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_Cassiopeiae" title="Gamma Cassiopeiae">
        Gamma Cassiopeiae
       </a>
       (γ Cas) variables are non-supergiant fast-rotating B class emission line-type stars that fluctuate irregularly by up to 1.5 magnitudes (fourfold change in luminosity) due to the ejection of matter at their
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equatorial
       </a>
       regions caused by the rapid rotational.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flare_stars">
        Flare stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=42" title="Edit section: Flare stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Flare_star" title="Flare star">
        Flare star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In main-sequence stars major eruptive variability is exceptional. It is common only among the
       <a href="/wiki/Flare_star" title="Flare star">
        flare stars
       </a>
       , also known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_Ceti" title="UV Ceti">
        UV Ceti
       </a>
       variables, very faint main-sequence stars which undergo regular flares. They increase in brightness by up to two magnitudes (six times brighter) in just a few seconds, and then fade back to normal brightness in half an hour or less. Several nearby red dwarfs are flare stars, including
       <a href="/wiki/Proxima_Centauri" title="Proxima Centauri">
        Proxima Centauri
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_359" title="Wolf 359">
        Wolf 359
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="RS_Canum_Venaticorum_variables">
        RS Canum Venaticorum variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=43" title="Edit section: RS Canum Venaticorum variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/RS_Canum_Venaticorum_variable" title="RS Canum Venaticorum variable">
        RS Canum Venaticorum variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       These are close binary systems with highly active chromospheres, including huge sunspots and flares, believed to be enhanced by the close companion. Variability scales ranges from days, close to the orbital period and sometimes also with eclipses, to years as sunspot activity varies.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cataclysmic_or_explosive_variable_stars">
        Cataclysmic or explosive variable stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=44" title="Edit section: Cataclysmic or explosive variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Cataclysmic_variable_star" title="Cataclysmic variable star">
        Cataclysmic variable star
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">

       </a>
       , and

      </div>

       </div>
      </div>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Supernovae">
        Supernovae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=45" title="Edit section: Supernovae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova" title="Supernova">
        Supernovae
       </a>
       are the most dramatic type of cataclysmic variable, being some of the most energetic events in the universe. A supernova can briefly emit as much energy as an entire
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy" title="Galaxy">
        galaxy
       </a>
       , brightening by more than 20 magnitudes (over one hundred million times brighter). The supernova explosion is caused by a white dwarf or a star core reaching a certain mass/density limit, the
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrasekhar_limit" title="Chandrasekhar limit">
        Chandrasekhar limit
       </a>
       , causing the object to collapse in a fraction of a second. This collapse "bounces" and causes the star to explode and emit this enormous energy quantity. The outer layers of these stars are blown away at speeds of many thousands of kilometers an
       <a href="/wiki/Hour" title="Hour">
        hour
       </a>
       . The expelled matter may form nebulae called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Supernova_remnant" title="Supernova remnant">
         supernova remnants
        </a>
       </i>
       . A well-known example of such a nebula is the
       <a href="/wiki/Crab_Nebula" title="Crab Nebula">
        Crab Nebula
       </a>
       , left over from a supernova that was observed in
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North America
       </a>
       in 1054. The core of the star or the white dwarf may either become a
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">
        neutron star
       </a>
       (generally a
       <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">
        pulsar
       </a>
       ) or disintegrate completely in the explosion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Supernovae can result from the death of an extremely massive star, many times heavier than the Sun. At the end of the life of this massive star, a non-fusible iron core is formed from fusion ashes. This iron core is pushed towards the Chandrasekhar limit till it surpasses it and therefore collapses.
      </p>
      <p>
       A supernova may also result from mass transfer onto a
       <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">
        white dwarf
       </a>
       from a star companion in a double star system. The Chandrasekhar limit is surpassed from the infalling matter. The absolute luminosity of this latter type is related to properties of its light curve, so that these supernovae can be used to establish the distance to other galaxies. One of the most studied supernovae is
       <a href="/wiki/SN_1987A" title="SN 1987A">
        SN 1987A
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud" title="Large Magellanic Cloud">
        Large Magellanic Cloud
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Novae">
        Novae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=46" title="Edit section: Novae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">
        Nova
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Nova" title="Nova">
        Novae
       </a>
       are also the result of dramatic explosions, but unlike supernovae do not result in the destruction of the progenitor star. Also unlike supernovae, novae ignite from the sudden onset of thermonuclear fusion, which under certain high pressure conditions (
       <a href="/wiki/Degenerate_matter" title="Degenerate matter">
        degenerate matter
       </a>
       ) accelerates explosively. They form in close
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_system_(astronomy)" title="Binary system (astronomy)">
        binary systems
       </a>
       , one component being a white dwarf accreting matter from the other ordinary star component, and may recur over periods of decades to centuries or millennia. Novae are categorised as
       <i>
        fast
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        slow
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        very slow
       </i>
       , depending on the behaviour of their light curve. Several
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        naked eye
       </a>
       novae have been recorded,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nova_Cygni_1975" title="Nova Cygni 1975">
        Nova Cygni 1975
       </a>
       being the brightest in the recent history, reaching 2nd magnitude.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dwarf_novae">
        Dwarf novae
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=47" title="Edit section: Dwarf novae">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_nova" title="Dwarf nova">
        Dwarf nova
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Dwarf novae are double stars involving a
       <a href="/wiki/White_dwarf" title="White dwarf">
        white dwarf
       </a>
       in which matter transfer between the component gives rise to regular outbursts. There are three types of dwarf nova:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U_Geminorum_star" title="U Geminorum star">
         U Geminorum stars
        </a>
        , which have outbursts lasting roughly 5–20 days followed by quiet periods of typically a few hundred days. During an outburst they brighten typically by 2–6 magnitudes. These stars are also known as
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SS_Cygni_variable" title="SS Cygni variable">
         SS Cygni variables
        </a>
        after the variable in
        <a href="/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation)" title="Cygnus (constellation)">
         Cygnus
        </a>
        which produces among the brightest and most frequent displays of this variable type.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Z_Camelopardalis_star" title="Z Camelopardalis star">
         Z Camelopardalis stars
        </a>
        , in which occasional plateaux of brightness called
        <i>
         standstills
        </i>
        are seen, part way between maximum and minimum brightness.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SU_Ursae_Majoris_star" title="SU Ursae Majoris star">
         SU Ursae Majoris stars
        </a>
        , which undergo both frequent small outbursts, and rarer but larger
        <i>
         superoutbursts
        </i>
        . These binary systems usually have orbital periods of under 2.5 hours.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="DQ_Herculis_variables">
        DQ Herculis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=48" title="Edit section: DQ Herculis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Intermediate_polar" title="Intermediate polar">
        Intermediate polar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       DQ Herculis systems are interacting binaries in which a low-mass star transfers mass to a highly magnetic white dwarf. The white dwarf spin period is significantly shorter than the binary orbital period and can sometimes be detected as a photometric periodicity. An accretion disk usually forms around the white dwarf, but its innermost regions are magnetically truncated by the white dwarf. Once captured by the white dwarf's magnetic field, the material from the inner disk travels along the magnetic field lines until it accretes. In extreme cases, the white dwarf's magnetism prevents the formation of an accretion disk.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="AM_Herculis_variables">
        AM Herculis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=49" title="Edit section: AM Herculis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_(cataclysmic_variable_star)" title="Polar (cataclysmic variable star)">
        Polar (cataclysmic variable star)
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In these cataclysmic variables, the white dwarf's magnetic field is so strong that it synchronizes the white dwarf's spin period with the binary orbital period. Instead of forming an accretion disk, the accretion flow is channeled along the white dwarf's magnetic field lines until it impacts the white dwarf near a magnetic pole. Cyclotron radiation beamed from the accretion region can cause orbital variations of several magnitudes.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Z_Andromedae_variables">
        Z Andromedae variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=50" title="Edit section: Z Andromedae variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       These symbiotic binary systems are composed of a red giant and a hot blue star enveloped in a cloud of gas and dust. They undergo nova-like outbursts with amplitudes of some 4 magnitudes.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="AM_CVn_variables">
        AM CVn variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=51" title="Edit section: AM CVn variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/AM_Canum_Venaticorum_star" title="AM Canum Venaticorum star">
        AM Canum Venaticorum star
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       AM CVn variables are symbiotic binaries where a white dwarf is accreting helium-rich material from either another white dwarf, a helium star, or an evolved main-sequence star. They undergo complex variations, or at times no variations, with ultrashort periods.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extrinsic_variable_stars">
        Extrinsic variable stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=52" title="Edit section: Extrinsic variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are two main groups of extrinsic variables: rotating stars and eclipsing stars.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotating_variable_stars">
        Rotating variable stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=53" title="Edit section: Rotating variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Stars with sizeable
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       may show significant variations in brightness as they rotate, and brighter areas of the surface are brought into view. Bright spots also occur at the magnetic poles of magnetic stars. Stars with ellipsoidal shapes may also show changes in brightness as they present varying areas of their surfaces to the observer.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-spherical_stars">
        Non-spherical stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=54" title="Edit section: Non-spherical stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ellipsoidal_variables">
        Ellipsoidal variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=55" title="Edit section: Ellipsoidal variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       These are very close binaries, the components of which are non-spherical due to their mutual gravitation. As the stars rotate the area of their surface presented towards the observer changes and this in turn affects their brightness as seen from Earth.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Stellar_spots">
        Stellar spots
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=56" title="Edit section: Stellar spots">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The surface of the star is not uniformly bright, but has darker and brighter areas (like the sun's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sun_spot" title="Sun spot">
        solar spots
       </a>
       ). The star's
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       too may vary in brightness. As the star rotates we observe brightness variations of a few tenths of magnitudes.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="FK_Comae_Berenices_variables">
        FK Comae Berenices variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=57" title="Edit section: FK Comae Berenices variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       These stars rotate extremely fast (~100 km/s at the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       ); hence they are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ellipsoidal" title="Ellipsoidal">
        ellipsoidal
       </a>
       in shape. They are (apparently) single giant stars with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectral_type" title="Spectral type">
        spectral types
       </a>
       G and K and show strong
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromospheric
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_line" title="Emission line">
        emission lines
       </a>
       . Examples are
       <a href="/wiki/FK_Comae_Berenices" title="FK Comae Berenices">
        FK Com
       </a>
       , HD 199178 and UZ Lib. A possible explanation for the rapid rotation of FK Comae stars is that they are the result of the merger of a
       <a href="/wiki/Contact_binary" title="Contact binary">
        (contact) binary
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="BY_Draconis_variable_stars">
        <a href="/wiki/BY_Draconis_variable" title="BY Draconis variable">
         BY Draconis variable
        </a>
        stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=58" title="Edit section: BY Draconis variable stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <p>
       BY Draconis stars are of spectral class K or M and vary by less than 0.5 magnitudes (70% change in luminosity).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_fields">
        Magnetic fields
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=59" title="Edit section: Magnetic fields">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alpha-2_Canum_Venaticorum_variables">
        Alpha-2 Canum Venaticorum variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=60" title="Edit section: Alpha-2 Canum Venaticorum variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alpha-2_Canum_Venaticorum_variable" title="Alpha-2 Canum Venaticorum variable">
        Alpha-2 Canum Venaticorum variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Alpha-2 Canum Venaticorum (α
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       CVn) variables are
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main-sequence
       </a>
       stars of spectral class B8–A7 that show fluctuations of 0.01 to 0.1 magnitudes (1% to 10%) due to changes in their magnetic fields.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="SX_Arietis_variables">
        SX Arietis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=61" title="Edit section: SX Arietis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/SX_Arietis_variable" title="SX Arietis variable">
        SX Arietis variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Stars in this class exhibit brightness fluctuations of some 0.1 magnitude caused by changes in their magnetic fields due to high rotation speeds.
      </p>
      <h5>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Optically_variable_pulsars">
        Optically variable pulsars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=62" title="Edit section: Optically variable pulsars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h5>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">
        Pulsar
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Few
       <a href="/wiki/Pulsar" title="Pulsar">
        pulsars
       </a>
       have been detected in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       . These
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">
        neutron stars
       </a>
       change in brightness as they rotate. Because of the rapid rotation, brightness variations are extremely fast, from milliseconds to a few seconds. The first and the best known example is the
       <a href="/wiki/Crab_Pulsar" title="Crab Pulsar">
        Crab Pulsar
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eclipsing_binaries">
        Eclipsing binaries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=63" title="Edit section: Eclipsing binaries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star#Eclipsing_binaries" title="Binary star">
        Binary star § Eclipsing binaries
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:402px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="540" data-file-width="860" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg/400px-Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg/600px-Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg/800px-Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg 2x" width="400"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Light_curve_of_binary_star_Kepler-16.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         How eclipsing binaries vary in brightness
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Extrinsic variables have variations in their brightness, as seen by terrestrial observers, due to some external source. One of the most common reasons for this is the presence of a binary companion star, so that the two together form a
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star" title="Binary star">
        binary star
       </a>
       . When seen from certain angles, one star may
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       the other, causing a reduction in brightness. One of the most famous eclipsing binaries is
       <a href="/wiki/Algol" title="Algol">
        Algol
       </a>
       , or Beta Persei (β Per).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Algol_variables">
        Algol variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=64" title="Edit section: Algol variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Algol_variable" title="Algol variable">
        Algol variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Algol variables undergo eclipses with one or two minima separated by periods of nearly constant light. The prototype of this class is
       <a href="/wiki/Algol" title="Algol">
        Algol
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellation
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Perseus_(constellation)" title="Perseus (constellation)">
        Perseus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_Periodic_variables">
        Double Periodic variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=65" title="Edit section: Double Periodic variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Double_periodic_variable" title="Double periodic variable">
        Double periodic variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Double periodic variables exhibit cyclical mass exchange which causes the orbital period to vary predictably over a very long period. The best known example is
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=V393_Scorpii&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="V393 Scorpii (page does not exist)">
        V393 Scorpii
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Beta_Lyrae_variables">
        Beta Lyrae variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=66" title="Edit section: Beta Lyrae variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Lyrae_variable" title="Beta Lyrae variable">
        Beta Lyrae variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Beta Lyrae (β Lyr) variables are extremely close binaries, named after the star
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_Lyrae" title="Beta Lyrae">
        Sheliak
       </a>
       . The light curves of this class of eclipsing variables are constantly changing, making it almost impossible to determine the exact onset and end of each eclipse.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="W_Serpentis_variables">
        W Serpentis variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=67" title="Edit section: W Serpentis variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       W Serpentis is the prototype of a class of semi-detached binaries including a giant or supergiant transferring material to a massive more compact star. They are characterised, and distinguished from the similar β Lyr systems, by strong UV emission from accretions hotspots on a disc of material.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="W_Ursae_Majoris_variables">
        W Ursae Majoris variables
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=68" title="Edit section: W Ursae Majoris variables">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/W_Ursae_Majoris_variable" title="W Ursae Majoris variable">
        W Ursae Majoris variable
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The stars in this group show periods of less than a day. The stars are so closely situated to each other that their surfaces are almost in contact with each other.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planetary_transits">
        Planetary transits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Variable_star&amp;action=edit&amp;section=69" title="Edit section: Planetary transits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Stars with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">
        planets
       </a>
       may also show brightness variations if their planets pass between Earth and the star. These variations are much smaller than those seen with stellar companions and are only detectable with extremely accurate observations. Examples include
       <a href="/wiki/HD_209458" title="HD 209458">
        HD 209458
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/GSC_02652-01324" title="GSC 02652-01324">
        GSC 02652-01324
       </a>
       , and all of the planets and planet candidates detected by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kepler_Mission" title="Kepler Mission">
        Kepler Mission
       </a>
       .
      </p>
