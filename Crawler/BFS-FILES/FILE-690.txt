   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Stellar atmosphere
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3489" data-file-width="3543" height="246" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/250px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/375px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/500px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Photo taken in
         <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
          France
         </a>
         during the
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999" title="Solar eclipse of August 11, 1999">
          1999 eclipse
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        stellar atmosphere
       </b>
       is the outer region of the volume of a
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       , lying above the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_core" title="Solar core">
        stellar core
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_zone" title="Radiation zone">
        radiation zone
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection zone
       </a>
       . It is divided into several regions of distinct character:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
         photosphere
        </a>
        , which is the atmosphere's lowest and coolest layer, is normally its only visible part.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrightPS_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-BrightPS-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Light" title="Light">
         Light
        </a>
        escaping from the surface of the star stems from this region and passes through the higher layers. The
        <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
         Sun
        </a>
        's photosphere has a
        <a href="/wiki/Effective_temperature" title="Effective temperature">
         temperature
        </a>
        in the 5,770
        <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
         K
        </a>
        to 5,780 K range.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lowTemp_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-lowTemp-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-higherTemp_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-higherTemp-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Starspot" title="Starspot">
         Starspots
        </a>
        , cool regions of disrupted
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
         magnetic field
        </a>
        lie on the photosphere.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-higherTemp_3-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-higherTemp-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Above the photosphere lies the
        <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
         chromosphere
        </a>
        . This part of the atmosphere first cools down and then starts to heat up to about 10 times the temperature of the photosphere.
       </li>
       <li>
        Above the chromosphere lies the
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
         transition region
        </a>
        , where the temperature increases rapidly on a distance of only around 100 km.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The outermost part of the stellar atmosphere is the
        <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
         corona
        </a>
        , a tenuous
        <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
         plasma
        </a>
        which has a temperature above one million Kelvin.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        While all stars on the
        <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
         main sequence
        </a>
        feature transition regions and coronae, not all
        <a href="/wiki/Stellar_evolution" title="Stellar evolution">
         evolved stars
        </a>
        do so. It seems that only some
        <a href="/wiki/Giant_star" title="Giant star">
         giants
        </a>
        , and very few
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Supergiant" title="Supergiant">
         supergiants
        </a>
        , possess coronae.
        <a href="/wiki/Corona#Coronal_heating_problem" title="Corona">
         An unresolved problem
        </a>
        in stellar
        <a href="/wiki/Astrophysics" title="Astrophysics">
         astrophysics
        </a>
        is how the corona can be heated to such high temperatures. The answer lies in
        <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
         magnetic fields
        </a>
        , but the exact mechanism remains unclear.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       During a total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       , the photosphere of the Sun is
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        obscured
       </a>
       , revealing its atmosphere's other layers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrightPS_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrightPS-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observed during eclipse, the sun's chromosphere appears (briefly) as a thin pinkish
       <a href="/wiki/Arc_(geometry)" title="Arc (geometry)">
        arc
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and its corona is seen as a tufted
       <a href="/wiki/Halo_(optical_phenomenon)" title="Halo (optical phenomenon)">
        halo
       </a>
       . The same phenomenon in
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_star#Eclipsing_binaries" title="Binary star">
        eclipsing binaries
       </a>
       can make the chromosphere of giant stars visible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
