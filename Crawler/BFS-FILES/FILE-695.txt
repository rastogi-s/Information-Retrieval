   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Spicule (solar physics)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_physics" title="Solar physics">
        solar physics
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        spicule
       </b>
       is a dynamic jet of about 500 km diameter in the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . It moves upwards at about 20 km/s from the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       . They were discovered in 1877 by Father
       <a href="/wiki/Angelo_Secchi" title="Angelo Secchi">
        Angelo Secchi
       </a>
       of the Observatory of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Roman_Collegium&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Roman Collegium (page does not exist)">
        Roman Collegium
       </a>
       in Rome.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spicule_(solar_physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Spicules last for about 15 minutes;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_limb" title="Solar limb">
        solar limb
       </a>
       they appear elongated (if seen on the disk, they are known as "mottles" or "fibrils"). They are usually associated with regions of high
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">
        magnetic flux
       </a>
       ; their
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_flux" title="Mass flux">
        mass flux
       </a>
       is about 100 times that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . They rise at a rate of 20 km/s (or 72,000 km/h) and can reach several thousand kilometers in height before collapsing and fading away.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevalence">
        Prevalence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spicule_(solar_physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prevalence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are about 300,000 active spicules at any one time on the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       , amounting to about 1% of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Freedman_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Freedman-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An individual spicule typically reaches 3,000-10,000 km altitude above the photosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
        Causes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Spicule_(solar_physics)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Causes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bart_De_Pontieu&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bart De Pontieu (page does not exist)">
        Bart De Pontieu
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin_Solar_and_Astrophysics_Laboratory" title="Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory">
        Lockheed Martin Solar and Astrophysics Laboratory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Palo_Alto,_California" title="Palo Alto, California">
        Palo Alto, California
       </a>
       , United States),
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Robert_Erd%C3%A9lyi&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Robert Erdélyi (page does not exist)">
        Robert Erdélyi
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Stewart_James" title="Stewart James">
        Stewart James
       </a>
       (both from the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Sheffield" title="University of Sheffield">
        University of Sheffield
       </a>
       , United Kingdom) hypothesised in 2004 that spicules formed as a result of
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        P-mode oscillations
       </a>
       in the Sun's surface, sound waves with a period of about five minutes that causes the Sun's surface to rise and fall at several hundred meters per second (see
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       ). Magnetic flux tubes that tilted away from the vertical can focus and guide the rising material up into the solar atmosphere to form a spicule. There is still however some controversy about the issue in the solar physics community.
      </p>
