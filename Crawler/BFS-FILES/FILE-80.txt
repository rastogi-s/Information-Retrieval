      <p>
       The
       <b>
        chronology of the ancient Near East
       </b>
       provides a framework of dates for various events, rulers and dynasties. Individual inscriptions and texts customarily record events in terms of a succession of officials or rulers, taking forms like "in the year X of king Y". Thus by piecing together many records a
       <a href="/wiki/Relative_dating" title="Relative dating">
        relative chronology
       </a>
       is arrived at, relating dates in cities over a wide area. For the first millennium BC, the relative
       <a href="/wiki/Chronology" title="Chronology">
        chronology
       </a>
       can be tied to actual calendar years by identifying significant astronomical events. An inscription from the tenth year of Assyrian king
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashur-Dan_III" title="Ashur-Dan III">
        Ashur-Dan III
       </a>
       refers to an eclipse of the sun, and astronomical calculations among the range of possible dates identify the eclipse as having occurred 15 June 763 BCE. The date can be corroborated with other mentions of astronomical events and a secure
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_dating" title="Absolute dating">
        absolute chronology
       </a>
       established, that ties the relative chronologies into our calendar.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the third and second millennia, the correlation is not so fixed. A key document is the
       <a href="/wiki/Venus_tablet_of_Ammisaduqa" title="Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa">
        Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa
       </a>
       , preserving record of astronomical observations of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , as preserved in numerous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">
        cuneiform
       </a>
       tablets during the reign of the Babylonian king
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammisaduqa" title="Ammisaduqa">
        Ammisaduqa
       </a>
       , known to be the fourth ruler after
       <a href="/wiki/Hammurabi" title="Hammurabi">
        Hammurabi
       </a>
       in the
       <i>
        relative calendar
       </i>
       . In the series, the conjunction of the rise of Venus with the new moon provides a fixed point, or rather three fixed points, for the conjunction is a
       <a href="/wiki/Periodic_function" title="Periodic function">
        periodic
       </a>
       occurrence. Astronomical calculation can therefore fix, for example, the first dates of the reign of Hammurabi in this manner either as 1848, 1792, or 1736 BC, depending on whether the "high" (or "long"), "
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_chronology" title="Middle chronology">
        middle
       </a>
       " or "low (or short) chronology" is followed.
      </p>
      <p>
       For the
       <a href="/wiki/3rd_millennium_BC" title="3rd millennium BC">
        3rd
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/2nd_millennium_BC" title="2nd millennium BC">
        2nd
       </a>
       millennia BC, the following periods can be distinguished:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Bronze_Age" title="Early Bronze Age">
         Early Bronze Age
        </a>
        : A series of rulers and dynasties whose existence is based mostly on the
        <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">
         Sumerian King List
        </a>
        besides some that are attested
        <a href="/wiki/Epigraphy" title="Epigraphy">
         epigraphically
        </a>
        (e. g.,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/En-me-barage-si" title="En-me-barage-si">
         En-me-barage-si
        </a>
        ). No absolute dates within a certainty better than a century can be assigned to this period.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Middle_Bronze_Age" title="Middle Bronze Age">
         Middle
        </a>
        to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Late_Bronze_Age" title="Late Bronze Age">
         Late Bronze Age
        </a>
        : Beginning with the
        <a href="/wiki/Akkadian_Empire" title="Akkadian Empire">
         Akkadian Empire
        </a>
        around 2300 BC, the chronological evidence becomes internally more consistent. Essentially, for this period, a good picture can be drawn of who succeeded whom, and synchronisms between
        <a href="/wiki/Mesopotamia" title="Mesopotamia">
         Mesopotamia
        </a>
        , the
        <a href="/wiki/Levant" title="Levant">
         Levant
        </a>
        and the more robust
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronology_of_Ancient_Egypt" title="Chronology of Ancient Egypt">
         chronology of Ancient Egypt
        </a>
        can be established. The assignment of absolute dates is a matter of dispute; the conventional
        <i>
         middle chronology
        </i>
        fixes the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sack_of_Babylon" title="Sack of Babylon">
         sack of Babylon
        </a>
        at 1595 BC while the
        <i>
         short chronology
        </i>
        fixes it at 1531 BC.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bronze_Age_collapse" title="Bronze Age collapse">
         Bronze Age collapse
        </a>
        : a "Dark Age" begins with the fall of Babylonian Dynasty III (Kassite) around 1200 BC, the invasions of the
        <a href="/wiki/Sea_Peoples" title="Sea Peoples">
         Sea Peoples
        </a>
        and the collapse of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hittite_Empire" title="Hittite Empire">
         Hittite Empire
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Early_Iron_Age" title="Early Iron Age">
         Early Iron Age
        </a>
        : around 900 BC, historical data, written records become more numerous once more, with the rise of the
        <a href="/wiki/Neo-Assyrian_Empire" title="Neo-Assyrian Empire">
         Neo-Assyrian Empire
        </a>
        , enabling the certain assignment of absolute dates. Classical sources such as the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canon_of_Ptolemy" title="Canon of Ptolemy">
         Canon of Ptolemy
        </a>
        , the works of
        <a href="/wiki/Berossus" title="Berossus">
         Berossus
        </a>
        and the
        <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Bible" title="Hebrew Bible">
         Hebrew Bible
        </a>
        provide chronological support and synchronisms. An eclipse in 763 BC anchors the
        <a href="/wiki/Assyria" title="Assyria">
         Assyrian
        </a>
        list of imperial officials.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Variant_Bronze_Age_chronologies">
        Variant Bronze Age chronologies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Variant Bronze Age chronologies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1037" data-file-width="731" height="312" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png/220px-Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png/330px-Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png/440px-Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mesopotamian_Chronology_2-2011-29-03.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Middle chronology of the main dominations
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Due to the sparsity of sources throughout the "Dark Age", the history of the Near Eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Bronze_Age" title="Bronze Age">
        Bronze Age
       </a>
       down to the end of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Third_Babylonian_Dynasty" title="Third Babylonian Dynasty">
        Third Babylonian Dynasty
       </a>
       is a "floating chronology". In other words, it fits together internally as a "relative chronology" but not as an "absolute chronology".
      </p>
      <p>
       The major schools of thought on the length of the Dark Age are separated by 56 or 64 years. This is because the key source for their dates is the
       <a href="/wiki/Venus_tablet_of_Ammisaduqa" title="Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa">
        Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa
       </a>
       and the visibility of Venus has a 56/64
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (February 2009)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       year cycle. More recent work by
       <a href="/wiki/Vahe_Gurzadyan" title="Vahe Gurzadyan">
        Vahe Gurzadyan
       </a>
       has suggested that the fundamental 8-year cycle of Venus is a better metric.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultra_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ultra-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (see update in
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultra1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ultra1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) There have been other attempts to anchor the chronology using records of eclipses and other methods, but they are not yet widely supported. The alternative major chronologies are defined by the date of the 8th year of the reign of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ammisaduqa" title="Ammisaduqa">
        Ammisaduqa
       </a>
       , king of Babylon. This choice then defines the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Hammurabi" title="Hammurabi">
        Hammurabi
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Middle_chronology" title="Middle chronology">
         middle chronology
        </a>
       </i>
       (reign of Hammurabi 1792–1750 BC) is commonly encountered in literature, and many recent textbooks on the archaeology and history of the ancient Near East continue using it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The alternative
       <a href="/wiki/Short_chronology" title="Short chronology">
        "short" (or "low") chronology
       </a>
       is less commonly followed, and the "long" (or "high") and "ultra-short" (or "ultra-low")
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultra_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ultra-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       chronologies are clear minority views. There are also some scholars who discount the validity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Venus_tablet_of_Ammisaduqa" title="Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa">
        Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa
       </a>
       entirely. Early twenty-first century
       <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">
        dendrochronology
       </a>
       has essentially disproved the short chronology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For much of the period in question, middle chronology dates can be calculated by adding 64 years to the corresponding short chronology date (e.g. 1728 BC in short chronology corresponds to 1792 in middle chronology).
      </p>
      <p>
       The following table gives an overview of the competing proposals, listing some key dates and the deviation relative to the short chronology:

      <p>
       The chronologies of Mesopotamia, the Levant and Anatolia depend significantly on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chronology_of_Ancient_Egypt" title="Chronology of Ancient Egypt">
        chronology of Ancient Egypt
       </a>
       . To the extent that there are problems in the Egyptian chronology, these issues will be inherited in chronologies based on synchronisms with Ancient Egypt.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sources_of_chronological_data">
        Sources of chronological data
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Sources of chronological data">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inscriptional">
        Inscriptional
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inscriptional">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Thousands of cuneiform tablets have been found in an area running from Anatolia to Egypt. While many are the ancient equivalent of modern grocery receipts, these tablets, along with inscriptions on buildings and public monuments, provide the major source of chronological information for the ancient Middle East.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Underlying_issues">
        Underlying issues
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Underlying issues">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <ul>
       <li>
        State of materials
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       While there are some relatively pristine objects, such as you might see in the Louvre or the British Museum, the vast majority of recovered tables and inscriptions are in much worse condition. They have been broken with only portions found, intentionally defaced, and damaged by weather or the effects of being buried underground. Many tablets were not even baked in antiquity and have to be carefully handled until they are heated properly.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Provenance
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The site of an item's recovery is an important piece of information for archaeologists. Unfortunately, two factors impinge on this. First, in ancient times old materials were often reused either as building material or fill, sometimes at a great distance from the original location. Secondly, looting has been a fact of life for archaeological sites, dating at least back to Roman times. The provenance of a looted object is difficult or impossible to determine.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Multiple versions
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Over time, key documents like the
       <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">
        Sumerian King List
       </a>
       were repeatedly copied across the generations. As a result, there are sometimes multiple versions of a chronological source that differ from each other. It can be very hard to determine which version is correct.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Translation
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The translation of cuneiform documents is quite difficult, especially given the damaged nature of much source material. Additionally, our knowledge of the underlying languages, like Akkadian and Sumerian, have evolved over time, so a translation done now may be quite different than one done in AD 1900. The result of all this is that there can be honest disagreement what the document really says. Worse yet, many archaeological finds have not yet been published, much less translated. Those held in private collections may never be.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Slant
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Many of our important source documents, such as the Assyrian King List, are the products of government and religious establishments. They often have a built-in slant in favor of the king or god in charge. A king may even take credit for a battle or construction project of an earlier ruler. The Assyrians in particular have a literary tradition of always putting the best possible face on history. The tablets or inscriptions in question still have value, though one does have to keep the slant in mind.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="King_Lists">
        King Lists
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: King Lists">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Keeping historical lists of rulers was traditional in the ancient Near East.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Sumerian_King_List" title="Sumerian King List">
         Sumerian King List
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Covers rulers of Mesopotamia from a time "before the flood" up to the fall of the Isin Dynasty. For many early city-states it is the only source of chronological data. A major problem is that many early rulers are listed with fantastically long reigns. There has been some speculation that this stems from an error transcribing from the base 60 arithmetic of the Sumerians to the decimal-based system of the later Akkadians.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonian_King_List" title="Babylonian King List">
         Babylonian King List
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This list deals only with the rulers of Babylon. It has been found in two versions, Babylonian King List A and Babylonian King List B. The later dynasties in the list reflect the Kassite and Sealand periods of Babylon. There is also a Babylonian King List of the Hellenistic Period which covers the later part of the 1st millennium.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Assyrian_King_List" title="Assyrian King List">
         Assyrian King List
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Found in multiple differing copies, this tablet lists all the kings of Assyria and their regnal lengths back into the mists of time, with the portions with reasonable data beginning at around the 14th century BC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When combined with the various Assyrian chronicles, the Assyrian King List anchors the chronology of the 1st millennium.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Waddell%27s_chronology" title="Waddell's chronology">
         Indus Valley King List
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A list of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Indus Valley Civilization">
        Indus Valley Civilization
       </a>
       kings was compiled by
       <a href="/wiki/Laurence_Waddell" title="Laurence Waddell">
        Laurence Waddell
       </a>
       , but it is not generally accepted or well regarded by mainstream academia.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Macnaughton1932_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Macnaughton1932-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chronicles">
        Chronicles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Chronicles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Many chronicles have been recovered in the ancient Near East. Most are partial or fragmentary, but when combined with other sources, they provide a rich source of chronological data.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Synchronistic Chronicle
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Found in the library of Assurbanipal in Nineveh, it records the interaction of the Assyrian and Babylonian empires, from the Assyrian point of view. While useful, the consensus is that this chronicle should not be considered reliable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_P" title="Chronicle P">
         Chronicle P
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       While quite incomplete, this tablet provides the same type of information as the Assyrian Synchronistic Chronicle, but from the Babylonian point of view.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Royal Chronicle of Lagash
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The Sumerian King List omits any mention of
       <a href="/wiki/Lagash" title="Lagash">
        Lagash
       </a>
       , even though it was clearly a major power during the period covered by the list. The Royal Chronicle of Lagash appears to be an attempt to remedy that omission, listing the kings of Lagash in the form of a chronicle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some scholars believe the chronicle to be either a parody of the Sumerian King List or a complete fabrication.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Royal_inscriptions">
        Royal inscriptions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Royal inscriptions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In general, political figures in the ancient Near East liked to take credit for public works. Temples, buildings and statues built by a ruler are likely to have some sort of inscription mentioning his name. The kings also were sure to record major deeds like battles won, titles acquired, or gods appeased in some form of public inscription. These are very useful in tracking the reign of a ruler.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Year_lists">
        Year lists
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Year lists">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Unlike current calendars, most ancient calendars were based on how long the current ruler had been in power. A year might be "the 5th year in the reign of Hammurabi". As part of this, each royal year was given a title, like "the year Ur was defeated". Most often this reflected a deed of the ruler. The compilation of these years are called date lists.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Eponym_.28limmu.29_lists">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eponym_(limmu)_lists">
        Eponym (limmu) lists
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Eponym (limmu) lists">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In Assyria, a royal official or
       <a href="/wiki/Limmu" title="Limmu">
        limmu
       </a>
       was selected every year of a king's reign. Many copies of these lists have been found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Naturally, details are not always clear-cut. There are sometimes too many or few
       <i>
        limmū
       </i>
       for the length of a king's reign and sometimes the different versions of the eponym list disagree on a limmu.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span id="Trade.2C_diplomatic.2C_and_disbursement_records">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Trade,_diplomatic,_and_disbursement_records">
        Trade, diplomatic, and disbursement records
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Trade, diplomatic, and disbursement records">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       As is often the case in archaeology, it is everyday records that give the best picture of a civilization. Cuneiform tablets were constantly moving around the ancient Near East, offering alliances (sometimes including daughters for marriage), threatening war, acting as shipping documents for mundane supplies or settling accounts receivable. Most were tossed away after use the way that we would discard unwanted receipts. Fortunately for us, tablets are durable and many are well-preserved even when used as material for wall filler in new construction.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Amarna_letters" title="Amarna letters">
         Amarna letters
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The classic example. A number of cuneiform tablets were found at Amarna in Egypt, the city of the pharaoh
       <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">
        Akhenaten
       </a>
       . They were written mostly in Akkadian, the diplomatic language of the time. Several named rulers in the region including the kings of Assyria and Babylon. Assuming that the correct kings have been identified, it locks the chronology of the ancient Near East to that of Egypt, at least from the middle of the 2nd millennium.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Classical">
        Classical
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Classical">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some data sources are available to us from the classical period:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Berossus" title="Berossus">
         Berossus
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Berossus was a Babylonian astronomer living during the Hellenistic period. He wrote a history of Babylon which has not survived to modern times. Luckily, portions of this work were preserved by other classical writers.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Canon of Ptolemy or
        <a href="/wiki/Canon_of_Kings" title="Canon of Kings">
         Canon of Kings
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       This book provides a list of kings starting at around 750 BC in Babylon and forward through the Persian and Roman periods, in an astronomical context. It is used to help define the chronology of the 1st millennium.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Hebrew Bible
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Not having the benefit of being written into clay and buried, the records of the Hebrews have an additional layer of time to work through in being used as a source for chronology. On the other hand, the Hebrews did live pretty much in a territory directly in the crosshairs of Babylon, Assyria, Egypt and the Hittites, giving them a front row seat to actions in the area. Mainly of use in the 1st millennium and with the Assyrian New Kingdom.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Astronomical">
        Astronomical
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Astronomical">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Venus_tablet_of_Ammisaduqa" title="Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa">
         Venus tablet of Ammisaduqa
        </a>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A record of the movements of Venus during the reign of a king of the First Babylonian Dynasty. Using it, various scholars have proposed dates for the fall of Babylon based on the 56/64 year cycle of Venus. The mentioned recent work suggesting that the fundamental 8-year cycle of Venus is a better metric, led to the proposal of an "ultra-low" chronology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eclipses">
        Eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       A number of lunar and solar eclipses have been suggested for use in dating the ancient Near East. Many suffer from the vagueness of the original tablets in showing that an actual eclipse occurred. At that point, it becomes a question of using computer models to show when a given eclipse would have been visible at a site, complicated by difficulties in modeling the slowing rotation of the earth,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_T" title="Delta T">
        Delta T
       </a>
       . One important event is the
       <a href="/wiki/Assyrian_eclipse" title="Assyrian eclipse">
        Nineveh eclipse
       </a>
       , found in an Assyrian limmu list q.e. "Bur-Sagale of Guzana, revolt in the city of Ashur. In the month Simanu an eclipse of the sun took place." This eclipse is considered to be solidly dated to 15 June 763 BC. Another important event is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ur_III" title="Ur III">
        Ur III
       </a>
       Lunar/Solar Eclipse pair in the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Shulgi" title="Shulgi">
        Shulgi
       </a>
       . Most calculations for dating using eclipses have assumed that the Venus Tablet of Ammisaduqa is a legitimate source.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ultra_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ultra-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Dendrochronology">
        Dendrochronology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Dendrochronology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">
        Dendrochronology
       </a>
       attempts to use the variable growth pattern of trees, expressed in their rings, to build up a chronological timeline. At present, there are no continuous chronologies for the Near East. A floating chronology has been developed using trees in Anatolia for the Bronze and Iron Ages. Until a continuous sequence is developed, the usefulness for improving the chronology of the Ancient Near East is limited.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-="dendro_2016"_30-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-="dendro_2016"-30'>
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The difficulty in tying the chronology to the modern day lies primarily in the Roman period, for which few good wood samples have been found, and many of those turn out to be imported from outside the Near East.
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-="dendro_2016"_30-1'>
        <a href='#cite_note-="dendro_2016"-30'>
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radiocarbon_dating">
        Radiocarbon dating
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Radiocarbon dating">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       As in Egypt and the eastern Mediterranean, radiocarbon dates run one or two centuries earlier than the dates proposed by archaeologists. It is not at all clear which group is right, if either. Mechanisms have been proposed for explaining why radiocarbon dates in the region might be skewed. Equally logical arguments have been made suggesting that the archaeological dates are too late. Time will tell.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The spread of accelerator based carbon dating techniques may help clear up the issue. Another promising front is the dating of lime plaster from structures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recently, radiocarbon dates from the final destruction of
       <a href="/wiki/Ebla" title="Ebla">
        Ebla
       </a>
       have been shown to definitely favour the middle chronology (with the fall of Babylon and Aleppo at c. 1595 BCE), and do not seem to fall with the ultra-low chronology (same event at c. 1499 BCE), although it is emphasized that this is not to be seen as a decisive argument.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Synchronisms">
        Synchronisms
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Synchronisms">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Egypt">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       At least as far back as the reign of
       <a href="/wiki/Thutmose_I" title="Thutmose I">
        Thutmose I
       </a>
       , Egypt took a strong interest in the ancient Near East. At times they occupied portions of the region, a favor returned in later days by the Assyrians. Some key synchronisms:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Kadesh" title="Battle of Kadesh">
         Battle of Kadesh
        </a>
        , involving
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ramses_II" title="Ramses II">
         Ramses II
        </a>
        of Egypt (in his 5th year of reign) and
        <a href="/wiki/Muwatalli_II" title="Muwatalli II">
         Muwatalli II
        </a>
        of the Hittite empire. Recorded by both Egyptian and Hittite records.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
         <a href="#cite_note-34">
          [34]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Peace treaty between Ramses II of Egypt (in his 21st year of reign) and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hattusili_III" title="Hattusili III">
         Hattusili III
        </a>
        of the Hittites. Recorded by both Egyptian and Hittite records.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
         <a href="#cite_note-35">
          [35]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Amenhotep_III" title="Amenhotep III">
         Amenhotep III
        </a>
        (Amenophis III) marries the daughter of
        <a href="/wiki/Shuttarna_II" title="Shuttarna II">
         Shuttarna II
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/Mitanni" title="Mitanni">
         Mitanni
        </a>
        . There is also a record of messages from the pharaoh to
        <a href="/wiki/Kadashman-Enlil_I" title="Kadashman-Enlil I">
         Kadashman-Enlil I
        </a>
        of Babylon in the Amarna Letter (EA1–5). Other Amarna letters link Amenhotep III to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burnaburiash_II" title="Burnaburiash II">
         Burnaburiash II
        </a>
        of Babylon (EA6) and
        <a href="/wiki/Tushratta" title="Tushratta">
         Tushratta
        </a>
        of Mitanni (EA17–29) as well.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Akhenaten" title="Akhenaten">
         Akhenaten
        </a>
        (Amenhotep IV) married the daughter of Tushratta of Mitanni (as did his father Amenhotep III), leaving a number of records on the matter. He also corresponded with
        <a href="/wiki/Burna-Buriash_II" title="Burna-Buriash II">
         Burna-Buriash II
        </a>
        of Babylon (EA7–11, 15), and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ashuruballit_I" title="Ashuruballit I">
         Ashuruballit I
        </a>
        of Assyria (EA15–16)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Indus_Valley">
        Indus Valley
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Indus Valley">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       There is much evidence that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley_Civilization" title="Indus Valley Civilization">
        Harappan
       </a>
       civilization of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indus_Valley" title="Indus Valley">
        Indus Valley
       </a>
       traded with the region. This is demonstrated by clay seals found at Ur III and in the Persian Gulf.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In addition, if the land of
       <a href="/wiki/Meluhha" title="Meluhha">
        Meluhha
       </a>
       does indeed refer to the Indus Valley, then there are extensive trade records ranging from the Akkadian Empire until the Babylonian Dynasty I.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Thera_and_Eastern_Mediterranean">
        Thera and Eastern Mediterranean
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Chronology_of_the_ancient_Near_East&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Thera and Eastern Mediterranean">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Goods from Greece made their way into the ancient Near East, directly in Anatolia and via the island of
       <a href="/wiki/Cyprus" title="Cyprus">
        Cyprus
       </a>
       in the rest of the region and Egypt. A Hittite king, Tudhaliya IV, even captured Cyprus as part of an attempt to enforce a blockade of the Assyrians.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Minoan_eruption" title="Minoan eruption">
        eruption of the Thera volcano
       </a>
       provides a possible time marker for the region. A large eruption, it would have sent a plume of ash directly over Anatolia and filled the sea in the area with floating pumice. This pumice appeared in Egypt, apparently via trade. Current excavations in the Levant may also add to the timeline. The exact date of the volcanic eruption has been the subject of strong debate, with dates ranging between 1628 and 1520 BCE. Radiocarbon dating has placed it at between 1627 BCE and 1600 BCE with a 95% degree of probability.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Manning_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Manning-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Archaeologist Kevin Walsh, accepting the radiocarbon dating, suggests a possible date of 1628 and believes this to be the most debated event in Mediterranean archaeology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Walsh_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Walsh-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
