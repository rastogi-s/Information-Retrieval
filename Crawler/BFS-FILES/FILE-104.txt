   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Crucifixion darkness
   </h1>

        </div>
       </div>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Crucifixion darkness
       </b>
       is an episode in three of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canonical_gospels" title="Canonical gospels">
        canonical gospels
       </a>
       in which the sky becomes dark in daytime during the
       <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion_of_Jesus" title="Crucifixion of Jesus">
        crucifixion of Jesus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Ancient and medieval Christian writers treated this as a
       <a href="/wiki/Miracle" title="Miracle">
        miracle
       </a>
       , and believed it to be one of the few episodes from the
       <a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">
        New Testament
       </a>
       which were confirmed by non-Christian sources; modern scholars, however, have found no contemporary references to it outside the New Testament.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200588-96_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200588-96-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Modern scholarship, noting the way in which similar accounts were associated in ancient times with the deaths of notable figures, sees the phenomenon as a literary invention that attempts to convey a sense of the power of Jesus in the face of death, or a sign of God's displeasure with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_people" title="Jewish people">
        Jewish people
       </a>
       . Scholars have also noted the ways in which this episode appears to draw on earlier biblical accounts of darkness from the
       <a href="/wiki/Old_Testament" title="Old Testament">
        Old Testament
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </p>

           <li class="nv-edit">

      <p>
       The oldest biblical reference to the crucifixion darkness is found in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Mark" title="Gospel of Mark">
        Gospel of Mark
       </a>
       , written around the year 70.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWitherington200131_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWitherington200131-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHooker19918_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHooker19918-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In its account of the crucifixion, on the eve of
       <a href="/wiki/Passover" title="Passover">
        Passover
       </a>
       , it says that after Jesus was crucified at nine in the morning; darkness fell over all the land, or all the world (
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">
        γῆν
       </span>
       <i lang="grc-Latn" title="Ancient Greek-language transliteration" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
        gēn
       </i>
       can mean either), from around noon ("the sixth hour") until 3 o'clock ("the ninth hour").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It adds, immediately after the death of Jesus, that "the curtain of the temple was torn in two, from top to bottom".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Matthew" title="Gospel of Matthew">
        Gospel of Matthew
       </a>
       , written around the year 85 or 90, and using Mark as a source,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHarrington19918_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHarrington19918-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       has an almost identical wording: "From noon on, darkness came over the whole land [
       <i>
        or
       </i>
       , earth] until three in the afternoon."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The author includes dramatic details, including an earthquake and the raising of the dead, which were also common motifs in Jewish apocalyptic literature:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEYieh200465_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYieh200465-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFunk1998129–270_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFunk1998129–270-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "The earth shook, and the rocks were split. The tombs also were opened, and many bodies of the saints who had fallen asleep were raised."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Luke" title="Gospel of Luke">
        Gospel of Luke
       </a>
       , written around the year 90 and also using Mark as a source,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDavies2004xii_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies2004xii-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       has none of the details of the Matthew version, mentions the tearing of the temple veil immediately before the death of Jesus,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEvans2011308_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEvans2011308-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and provides the obscuring of the sun as the cause of the darkness:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenige2005150_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenige2005150-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFunk1998267–364_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFunk1998267–364-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        It was now about noon, and darkness came over the whole land [
        <i>
         or
        </i>
        , earth] until three in the afternoon, while the sun's light failed [
        <i>
         or
        </i>
        , the sun was eclipsed]; and the curtain of the temple was torn in two.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It appears that Luke's Gospel originally explained the event as an
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       . The majority of manuscripts of the Gospel of Luke have the Greek phrase
       <i>
        <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
         <i>
          eskotisthe ho helios
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("the sun was darkened"), but the earliest manuscripts say
       <i>
        <span lang="grc-Latn" xml:lang="grc-Latn">
         <i>
          tou heliou eklipontos
         </i>
        </span>
       </i>
       ("the sun's light failed" or "the sun was in eclipse").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELoader2002356_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELoader2002356-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This earlier version is likely to have been the original one, amended by later scribes to correct what they assumed was an error, since they knew that an eclipse was impossible during Passover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851517–1518_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851517–1518-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWallace2004_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWallace2004-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One early Christian commentator even suggested that the text had been deliberately corrupted by opponents of the Church to make it easier to attack.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200589_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200589-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the account of the crucifixion given in the
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_John" title="Gospel of John">
        Gospel of John
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarclay2001340_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarclay2001340-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       there is no mention of darkness, the tearing of the veil, or the raising of the dead.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBroadhead1994196_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBroadhead1994196-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Later_versions">
        Later versions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Later versions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apocryphal_writers">
        Apocryphal writers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apocryphal writers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A number of accounts in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Testament_apocrypha" title="New Testament apocrypha">
        apocryphal literature
       </a>
       built on the accounts of the crucifixion darkness. The
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Peter" title="Gospel of Peter">
        Gospel of Peter
       </a>
       , probably from the second century, expanded on the canonical gospel accounts in creative ways. As one writer puts it, "accompanying miracles become more fabulous and the apocalyptic portents are more vivid".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoster200997_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoster200997-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this version, the darkness which covers the whole of Judaea leads people to go about with lamps believing it to be night.
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_IX,_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf09.iii.i.html_"The_Gospel_of_Peter"]_5:15,_p._4_23-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_IX,_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf09.iii.i.html_"The_Gospel_of_Peter"]_5:15,_p._4-23'>
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The fourth century
       <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Nicodemus" title="Gospel of Nicodemus">
        Gospel of Nicodemus
       </a>
       describes how Pilate and his wife are disturbed by a report of what had happened, and the Judeans he has summoned tell him it was an ordinary solar eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBarnstone2005351,_368,_374,_378–379,_419_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnstone2005351,_368,_374,_378–379,_419-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another text from the fourth century, the purported
       <i>
        Report of Pontius Pilate to Tiberius
       </i>
       , claimed the darkness had started at the sixth hour, covered the whole world, and during the subsequent evening the full moon resembled blood for the entire night.
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_VIII,_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf08.vii.xix.html_"The_Report_of_Pontius_Pilate"],_pp._462–463_25-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_VIII,_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf08.vii.xix.html_"The_Report_of_Pontius_Pilate"],_pp._462–463-25'>
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a fifth- or sixth-century text by
       <a href="/wiki/Pseudo-Dionysius_the_Areopagite" title="Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite">
        Pseudo-Dionysius the Areopagite
       </a>
       , the author claims to have observed a solar eclipse from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Heliopolis_(Ancient_Egypt)" title="Heliopolis (Ancient Egypt)">
        Heliopolis
       </a>
       at the time of the crucifixion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEParker1897148–149,_182–183_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEParker1897148–149,_182–183-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_historians">
        Ancient historians
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ancient historians">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       No contemporary references to this darkness have been found outside of the New Testament. Later commentators speculated about a reference in a work by the chronicler
       <a href="/wiki/Thallus_(historian)" title="Thallus (historian)">
        Thallus
       </a>
       . In the ninth century, the Byzantine historian
       <a href="/wiki/George_Syncellus" title="George Syncellus">
        George Syncellus
       </a>
       quoted from the third-century Christian historian
       <a href="/wiki/Sextus_Julius_Africanus" title="Sextus Julius Africanus">
        Sextus Julius Africanus
       </a>
       , who remarked that "Thallos dismisses this darkness as a solar eclipse".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Africanus_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Africanus-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is not known when Thallus lived, and it is unclear whether he himself made any reference to the crucifixion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAlexander2005225_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlexander2005225-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Tertullian" title="Tertullian">
        Tertullian
       </a>
       , in his
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Apologeticus" title="Apologeticus">
         Apologeticus
        </a>
       </i>
       , told the story of the crucifixion darkness and suggested that the evidence must still be held in the Roman archives.
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_III,_"The_Apology"_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf03.iv.iii.i.html_chapter_21],_pp._34–36_29-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_III,_"The_Apology"_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf03.iv.iii.i.html_chapter_21],_pp._34–36-29'>
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Until the Enlightenment era, the crucifixion darkness story was often used by Christian apologists because they believed it was a rare example of the biblical account being supported by non-Christian sources. When the pagan critic
       <a href="/wiki/Celsus" title="Celsus">
        Celsus
       </a>
       claimed that Jesus could hardly be a God because he had performed no great deeds, the third-century Christian commentator
       <a href="/wiki/Origen" title="Origen">
        Origen
       </a>
       responded, in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Against_Celsus" title="Against Celsus">
         Against Celsus
        </a>
       </i>
       , by recounting the darkness, earthquake and opening of tombs. As proof that the incident had happened, he referred to a description by
       <a href="/wiki/Phlegon_of_Tralles" title="Phlegon of Tralles">
        Phlegon of Tralles
       </a>
       of an eclipse, accompanied by earthquakes felt in other parts of the Empire during the reign of Tiberius (probably that of 29 CE).
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_IV,_"Contra_Celsum",_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf03.iv.iii.i.html_Book_II,_chapter_23]_p._441_30-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTERobertsDonaldsonCoxe1896Volume_IV,_"Contra_Celsum",_[http://www.ccel.org/ccel/schaff/anf03.iv.iii.i.html_Book_II,_chapter_23]_p._441-30'>
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In his
       <i>
        Commentary on Matthew
       </i>
       , however, Origen offered a different approach. Answering criticisms that there was no mention of this incident in any of the many non-Christian sources, he insisted that it was local to Palestine, and therefore would have gone unnoticed outside. To suggestions it was merely an eclipse, Origen pointed out that this was impossible and suggested other explanations, such as heavy clouds, drawing only on the accounts given in Matthew and Mark, which make no mention of the sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200588–89_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200588–89-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Extra-Biblical_accounts">
        Extra-Biblical accounts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Extra-Biblical accounts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Book_of_Mormon">
        The Book of Mormon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: The Book of Mormon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Mormon" title="Book of Mormon">
        Book of Mormon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_the_Lamanite" title="Samuel the Lamanite">
        Samuel the Lamanite
       </a>
       prophesied around 5 BC:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        But behold, as I said unto you concerning another sign, a sign of his death, behold, in that day that he shall suffer death the sun shall be darkened and refuse to give his light unto you; and also the moon and the stars; and there shall be no light upon the face of this land, even from the time that he shall suffer death, for the space of three days, to the time that he shall rise again from the dead. Yea, at the time that he shall yield up the ghost there shall be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours, and the earth shall shake and tremble; and the rocks which are upon the face of this earth, which are both above the earth and beneath, which ye know at this time are solid, or the more part of it is one solid mass, shall be broken up; Yea, they shall be rent in twain, and shall ever after be found in seams and in cracks, and in broken fragments upon the face of the whole earth, yea, both above the earth and beneath. And behold, there shall be great tempests, and there shall be many mountains laid low, like unto a valley, and there shall be many places which are now called valleys which shall become mountains, whose height is great. And many highways shall be broken up, and many cities shall become desolate. And many graves shall be opened, and shall yield up many of their dead; and many saints shall appear unto many. And behold, thus hath the angel spoken unto me; for he said unto me that there should be thunderings and lightnings for the space of many hours. And he said unto me that while the thunder and the lightning lasted, and the tempest, that these things should be, and that darkness should cover the face of the whole earth for the space of three days. And the angel said unto me that many shall see greater things than these, to the intent that they might believe that these signs and these wonders should come to pass upon all the face of this land, to the intent that there should be no cause for unbelief among the children of men—
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
         <a href="#cite_note-32">
          [32]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       At the time of Jesus' death, the Book of Mormon records the fulfillment of this prophecy:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        And it came to pass in the thirty and fourth year, in the first month, on the fourth day of the month, there arose a great storm, such an one as never had been known in all the land. And there was also a great and terrible tempest; and there was terrible thunder, insomuch that it did shake the whole earth as if it was about to divide asunder. And there were exceedingly sharp lightnings, such as never had been known in all the land. And the city of Zarahemla did take fire. And the city of Moroni did sink into the depths of the sea, and the inhabitants thereof were drowned. And the earth was carried up upon the city of Moronihah, that in the place of the city there became a great mountain. And there was a great and terrible destruction in the land southward. But behold, there was a more great and terrible destruction in the land northward; for behold, the whole face of the land was changed, because of the tempest and the whirlwinds, and the thunderings and the lightnings, and the exceedingly great quaking of the whole earth; And the highways were broken up, and the level roads were spoiled, and many smooth places became rough. And many great and notable cities were sunk, and many were burned, and many were shaken till the buildings thereof had fallen to the earth, and the inhabitants thereof were slain, and the places were left desolate. And there were some cities which remained; but the damage thereof was exceedingly great, and there were many in them who were slain. And there were some who were carried away in the whirlwind; and whither they went no man knoweth, save they know that they were carried away. And thus the face of the whole earth became deformed, because of the tempests, and the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the quaking of the earth. And behold, the rocks were rent in twain; they were broken up upon the face of the whole earth, insomuch that they were found in broken fragments, and in seams and in cracks, upon all the face of the land. And it came to pass that when the thunderings, and the lightnings, and the storm, and the tempest, and the quakings of the earth did cease—for behold, they did last for about the space of three hours; and it was said by some that the time was greater; nevertheless, all these great and terrible things were done in about the space of three hours—and then behold, there was darkness upon the face of the land. And it came to pass that there was thick darkness upon all the face of the land, insomuch that the inhabitants thereof who had not fallen could feel the vapor of darkness; And there could be no light, because of the darkness, neither candles, neither torches; neither could there be fire kindled with their fine and exceedingly dry wood, so that there could not be any light at all; And there was not any light seen, neither fire, nor glimmer, neither the sun, nor the moon, nor the stars, for so great were the mists of darkness which were upon the face of the land. And it came to pass that it did last for the space of three days that there was no light seen; and there was great mourning and howling and weeping among all the people continually; yea, great were the groanings of the people, because of the darkness and the great destruction which had come upon them.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
         <a href="#cite_note-33">
          [33]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Revelation_of_the_Magi">
        Revelation of the Magi
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Revelation of the Magi">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revelation_of_the_Magi" title="Revelation of the Magi">
        Revelation of the Magi
       </a>
       by Brent Landau (containing a translation of a Syriac text purported to have been originally written by the Magi, and discovered recently in the Vatican library), the Magi prophesied that at the time of Christ's death, there would be earthquakes, lightnings and darkness covering the land, and that many of the dead would rise from their graves.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations">
        Explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Miracle">
        Miracle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Miracle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Because it was known in ancient and medieval times that a solar eclipse could not take place during Passover (solar eclipses require a
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       while Passover only takes place during a
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       ), it was considered a miraculous sign rather than a naturally occurring event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChambers1899129–130_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChambers1899129–130-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_de_Sacrobosco" title="Johannes de Sacrobosco">
        Johannes de Sacrobosco
       </a>
       wrote, in his
       <a href="/wiki/De_sphaera_mundi" title="De sphaera mundi">
        The Sphere of the World
       </a>
       , "the eclipse was not natural, but, rather, miraculous and contrary to nature".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBartlett200868–69_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBartlett200868–69-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Modern writers who regard this as a miraculous event tend either to see it as operating through a natural phenomenon—such as volcanic dust or heavy cloud cover—or avoid explanation completely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200568–69_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200568–69-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Reformation_Study_Bible" title="Reformation Study Bible">
        Reformation Study Bible
       </a>
       , for instance, simply states "This was a supernatural darkness."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESproul2010comment_on_Luke_23:44_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESproul2010comment_on_Luke_23:44-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Natural_explanations">
        Natural explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Natural explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3489" data-file-width="3543" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/220px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/330px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg/440px-Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_1999_4_NR.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Some commentators suggested the darkness was a
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
          solar eclipse
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Gospel of Luke account appears to describe the event as an eclipse, and some non-Christian writers dismissed it in these terms. However, the biblical details do not accord with an eclipse: a solar eclipse could not have occurred on or near the Passover, when Jesus was crucified, and would have been too brief to account for three hours of darkness. The maximum possible duration of a total solar eclipse is seven minutes and 31.1 seconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMeeus2003_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMeeus2003-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The only total eclipse visible in Jerusalem in this era fell later in the year, on 24 November 29 CE at 11:05 AM.
       <sup class="reference" id='cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEspenak"Total_Solar_Eclipse_of_0029_Nov_24"_39-0'>
        <a href='#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEspenak"Total_Solar_Eclipse_of_0029_Nov_24"-39'>
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Around the Sea of Galilee, it would have been visible for just one minute and forty-nine seconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For there to have been a partial or total eclipse of any duration while Jesus was crucified, then he would have had to have been killed on November 24, 29 CE, about half a year later than scholars currently believe.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1983,
       <a href="/wiki/Colin_Humphreys" title="Colin Humphreys">
        Colin Humphreys
       </a>
       and W. G. Waddington argued that the darkness could be accounted for by a partial
       <i>
        lunar
       </i>
       eclipse that had taken place on that day: lunar eclipses can last much longer than solar ones.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumphreysWaddington1985_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumphreysWaddington1985-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Astronomer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bradley_E._Schaefer" title="Bradley E. Schaefer">
        Bradley E. Schaefer
       </a>
       , on the other hand, pointed out that the eclipse would not have been visible during daylight hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchaefer1990_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchaefer1990-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchaefer1991_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchaefer1991-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Humphreys and Waddington speculated that the reference in the Luke Gospel to a solar eclipse must have been the result of a scribe wrongly amending the text, a claim historian
       <a href="/wiki/David_Henige" title="David Henige">
        David Henige
       </a>
       describes as "indefensible".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHenige2005150_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenige2005150-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some writers have explained the crucifixion darkness in terms of sunstorms, heavy cloud cover, the aftermath of a volcanic eruption,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown19941040_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown19941040-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/Khamsin" title="Khamsin">
        khamsin
       </a>
       dust storm that tends to occur from March to May.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHumphreys201184_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHumphreys201184-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A popular work of the nineteenth century described it as an 'oppressive gloom' and suggested this was a typical phenomenon related to earthquakes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ellicott_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ellicott-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_creation">
        Literary creation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Literary creation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A common view in modern scholarship is that the account in the synoptic gospels is a literary creation of the gospel writers, intended to heighten the importance of what they saw as a theologically significant event.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Burton_Mack" title="Burton Mack">
        Burton Mack
       </a>
       describes it as a fabrication by the author of the Gospel of Mark,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMack1988296_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMack1988296-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while
       <a href="/wiki/G._B._Caird" title="G. B. Caird">
        G. B. Caird
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Fitzmyer" title="Joseph Fitzmyer">
        Joseph Fitzmyer
       </a>
       conclude that the author did not intend the description to be taken literally.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECaird1980186_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECaird1980186-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851513_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851513-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/W._D._Davies" title="W. D. Davies">
        W. D. Davies
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dale_Allison" title="Dale Allison">
        Dale Allison
       </a>
       similarly conclude "It is probable that, without any factual basis, darkness was added in order to wrap the cross in a rich symbol and/or assimilate Jesus to other worthies".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDaviesAllison1997623_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDaviesAllison1997623-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The image of darkness over the land would have been understood by ancient readers as a cosmic sign, a typical element in the description of the death of kings and other major figures by writers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Philo" title="Philo">
        Philo
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dio_Cassius" title="Dio Cassius">
        Dio Cassius
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Virgil" title="Virgil">
        Virgil
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Plutarch" title="Plutarch">
        Plutarch
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">
        Josephus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGarland1999264_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGarland1999264-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9za_Vermes" title="Géza Vermes">
        Géza Vermes
       </a>
       describes the darkness account as "part of the Jewish eschatological imagery of the day of the Lord. It is to be treated as a literary rather than historical phenomenon notwithstanding naive scientists and over-eager television documentary makers, tempted to interpret the account as a datable eclipse of the sun. They would be barking up the wrong tree".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEVermes2005108–109_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVermes2005108–109-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Interpretations">
        Interpretations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Crucifixion_darkness&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Interpretations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1847" data-file-width="2536" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg/220px-Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg/330px-Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0d/Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg/440px-Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Meister_des_Rabula-Evangeliums_002.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The earliest crucifixion in an
         <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">
          illuminated manuscript
         </a>
         , from the
         <a href="/wiki/Syriac_language" title="Syriac language">
          Syriac
         </a>
         <a href="/wiki/Rabbula_Gospels" title="Rabbula Gospels">
          Rabbula Gospels
         </a>
         , 586 CE: note the sun and moon in the sky
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       This sequence plays an important part in the gospel's literary narrative. The author of Mark's gospel has been described as operating here "at the peak of his rhetorical and theological powers".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlack200542_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlack200542-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One suggestion is that the darkness is a deliberate inversion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Transfiguration_of_Jesus" title="Transfiguration of Jesus">
        transfiguration
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBlack200542_53-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlack200542-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
       alternately, Jesus's earlier discourse about a future tribulation mentions the sun being darkened,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and can be seen as foreshadowing this scene.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEHealy2008319_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHealy2008319-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Striking details such as the darkening of the sky and the tearing of the temple veil may be a way of focusing the reader away from the shame and humiliation of the crucifixion; one professor of biblical theology concluded, "it is clear that Jesus is not a humiliated criminal but a man of great significance. His death is therefore not a sign of his weakness but of his power."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWinn2008133_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWinn2008133-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When considering the theological meaning of the event, some authors have interpreted the darkness as a period of mourning by the cosmos itself at the death of Jesus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDonahue2002451–452_57-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDonahue2002451–452-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Others have seen it as a sign of God's judgement on the Jewish people, sometimes connecting it with the destruction of the city of Jerusalem in the year 70; or as symbolising shame, fear, or the mental suffering of Jesus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200597–102_58-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200597–102-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Fitzmyer compares the event to a contemporary description recorded in
       <a href="/wiki/Josephus" title="Josephus">
        Josephus
       </a>
       '
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Antiquities_of_the_Jews" title="Antiquities of the Jews">
         Antiquities of the Jews
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851518_59-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFitzmyer19851518-59">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which recounts "unlawful acts against the gods, from which we believe the very sun turned away, as if it too were loath to look upon the foul deed".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60">
        <a href="#cite_note-60">
         [60]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Many writers have adopted an
       <a href="/wiki/Intertextuality" title="Intertextuality">
        intertextual
       </a>
       approach, looking at earlier texts from which the author of the Mark Gospel may have drawn. In particular, parallels have often been noted between the darkness and the prediction in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Amos" title="Book of Amos">
        Book of Amos
       </a>
       of an earthquake in the reign of King
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uzziah_of_Judah" title="Uzziah of Judah">
        Uzziah of Judah
       </a>
       : "On that day, says the Lord God, I will make the sun go down at noon, and darken the earth in broad daylight".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61">
        <a href="#cite_note-61">
         [61]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Particularly in connection with this reference, read as a prophecy of the future, the darkness can be seen as portending the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_times" title="End times">
        end times
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison2005100–101_62-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison2005100–101-62">
         [62]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Another likely literary source is the plague narrative in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Exodus" title="Book of Exodus">
        Book of Exodus
       </a>
       , in which Egypt is covered by darkness for three days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63">
        <a href="#cite_note-63">
         [63]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been suggested that the author of Matthew's gospel changed the Marcan text slightly to more closely match this source.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison2005182–83_64-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison2005182–83-64">
         [64]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Commentators have also drawn comparisons with the description of darkness in the
       <a href="/wiki/Genesis_creation_narrative" title="Genesis creation narrative">
        Genesis creation narrative
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65">
        <a href="#cite_note-65">
         [65]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with a prophecy regarding mid-day darkness by
       <a href="/wiki/Jeremiah" title="Jeremiah">
        Jeremiah
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66">
        <a href="#cite_note-66">
         [66]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and with an end-times prophecy in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Zechariah" title="Book of Zechariah">
        Book of Zechariah
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67">
        <a href="#cite_note-67">
         [67]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAllison200583–84_68-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllison200583–84-68">
         [68]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Roman literary sources have also been postulated, namely the death of
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Caesar" title="Julius Caesar">
        Julius Caesar
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEickenberg20151–51,_118–133_69-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEickenberg20151–51,_118–133-69">
         [69]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
