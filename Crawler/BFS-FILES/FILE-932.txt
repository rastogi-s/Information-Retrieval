    Bengt Edlén

      <p>
       <b>
        Bengt Edlén
       </b>
       (2 November 1906, Gusum – 10 February 1993, Lund) was a Swedish professor of physics and astronomer who specialized in
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       . He was the first who identified the unknown coronal spectral lines in the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        Corona
       </a>
       , which was used to calculate the temperature of the corona.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">
        Biography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Bengt_Edl%C3%A9n&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Bengt Edlén was born 2 November 1906 in
       <a href="/wiki/Gusum" title="Gusum">
        Gusum
       </a>
       , Sweden. He graduated from high school in
       <a href="/wiki/Norrk%C3%B6ping" title="Norrköping">
        Norrköping
       </a>
       in 1926 and entered the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Uppsala" title="University of Uppsala">
        University of Uppsala
       </a>
       the same year.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He was awarded his
       <a href="/wiki/Bachelor%27s_degree" title="Bachelor's degree">
        Bachelor's degree
       </a>
       after three semester and graduated with a PhD in 1934 with his thesis about the spectra and energy of the elements in the beginning of the periodic system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He received international fame after finding unidentified spectral lines in the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrum" title="Spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       which were speculatively believed to originate from a hitherto unidentified
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        chemical element
       </a>
       termed
       <a href="/wiki/Coronium" title="Coronium">
        coronium
       </a>
       . Edlén later showed that those lines are from multiply ionized iron (
       <b>
        Fe-XIV
       </b>
       ). His discovery was not immediately accepted, since the alleged ionization required a temperature of millions of degrees. Later such
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       temperatures were verified.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also made an important contribution in analyzing spectra of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star" title="Wolf-Rayet star">
        Wolf-Rayet stars
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Edlén was professor at
       <a href="/wiki/Lund_University" title="Lund University">
        Lund University
       </a>
       from 1944 to 1973. He was elected a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Swedish_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences">
        Royal Swedish Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       in 1947. He received the
       <a href="/wiki/Gold_Medal_of_the_Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Gold Medal of the Royal Astronomical Society">
        Gold Medal
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Royal_Astronomical_Society" title="Royal Astronomical Society">
        Royal Astronomical Society
       </a>
       1945 for the solution of the Corona Mystery,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Howard_N._Potts_Medal" title="Howard N. Potts Medal">
        Howard N. Potts Medal
       </a>
       in 1946 for researches in the extreme ultraviolet,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Henry_Draper_Medal" title="Henry Draper Medal">
        Henry Draper Medal
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_National_Academy_of_Sciences" title="United States National Academy of Sciences">
        National Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Draper_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Draper-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in 1968.
      </p>
