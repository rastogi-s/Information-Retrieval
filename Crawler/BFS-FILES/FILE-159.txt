      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurred on March 28–29, 2006. A solar eclipse occurs when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       is larger than the Sun's, blocking all direct sunlight, turning day into darkness. Totality occurs in a narrow path across Earth's surface, with the partial solar eclipse visible over a surrounding region thousands of kilometres wide. It was visible from a narrow corridor which traversed half the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">
        magnitude
       </a>
       , that is, the ratio between the apparent sizes of the Moon and that of the Sun, was 1.052, and it was part of Saros 139.
      </p>

      <p>
       The path of totality of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's shadow began at sunrise in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       and extended across the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , traveling across
       <a href="/wiki/Ghana" title="Ghana">
        Ghana
       </a>
       , the southeastern tip of
       <a href="/wiki/Ivory_Coast" title="Ivory Coast">
        Ivory Coast
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Togo" title="Togo">
        Togo
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Benin" title="Benin">
        Benin
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nigeria" title="Nigeria">
        Nigeria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Niger" title="Niger">
        Niger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chad" title="Chad">
        Chad
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Libya" title="Libya">
        Libya
       </a>
       , and a small corner of northwest
       <a href="/wiki/Egypt" title="Egypt">
        Egypt
       </a>
       , from there across the
       <a href="/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea" title="Mediterranean Sea">
        Mediterranean Sea
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Greece" title="Greece">
        Greece
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kastell%C3%B3rizo" title="Kastellórizo">
        Kastellórizo
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       , then across the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
        Black Sea
       </a>
       via
       <a href="/wiki/Georgia_(country)" title="Georgia (country)">
        Georgia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
        Russia
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">
        Kazakhstan
       </a>
       to Western
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolia" title="Mongolia">
        Mongolia
       </a>
       , where it ended at sunset. A
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        partial eclipse
       </a>
       was seen from the much broader path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
        penumbra
       </a>
       , including the northern two-thirds of
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , the whole of
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        Europe
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">
        Central Asia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">
        Observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_29,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       People around the world gathered in areas where the eclipse was visible to view the event. The
       <a href="/wiki/Manchester_Astronomical_Society" title="Manchester Astronomical Society">
        Manchester Astronomical Society
       </a>
       , the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Malaysian_Space_Agency&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Malaysian Space Agency (page does not exist)">
        Malaysian Space Agency
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_Society_of_the_Pacific" title="Astronomical Society of the Pacific">
        Astronomical Society of the Pacific
       </a>
       , as well as dozens of tour groups met at the Apollo temple and the theater in
       <a href="/wiki/Side,_Turkey" title="Side, Turkey">
        Side
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       . The San Francisco
       <a href="/wiki/Exploratorium" title="Exploratorium">
        Exploratorium
       </a>
       featured a live webcast from the site, where thousands of observers were seated in the ancient, Roman-style theater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Almost all actively visited areas in the path of totality had perfect weather. Many observers reported an unusually beautiful eclipse, with many or all effects visible, and a very nice
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       , despite the proximity to the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_minimum" title="Solar minimum">
        solar minimum
       </a>
       . The partial phase of the eclipse was also visible from the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Space_Station" title="International Space Station">
        International Space Station
       </a>
       , where the astronauts on board took spectacular pictures of the moon's shadow on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's surface. It initially appeared as though an orbit correction set for the middle of March would bring the ISS into the path of totality, but this correction was postponed.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_failure">
        Satellite failure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_29,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Satellite failure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Optus_fleet_of_satellites#Failures" title="Optus fleet of satellites">
        Optus fleet of satellites § Failures
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The satellite responsible for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SKY_Network_Television" title="SKY Network Television">
        SKY Network Television
       </a>
       , a New Zealand pay TV company, failed the day after this eclipse at around 1900 local time. While SKY didn't directly attribute the failure to the eclipse, they said in a media release that it took longer to resolve the issue because of it, but this claim was refuted by astronomers. The main reason for the failure was because of an aging and increasingly faulty satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_eclipses">
        Related eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_29,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This solar eclipse was preceded by the
       <a href="/wiki/March_2006_lunar_eclipse" title="March 2006 lunar eclipse">
        penumbral lunar eclipse on March 14, 2006
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_139">
        Saros 139
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_March_29,_2006&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saros 139">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_139" title="Solar Saros 139">
        saros series 139
       </a>
       , repeating every 18 years, 11 days, 8 hours, containing 71 events. The series started with partial solar eclipse on May 17, 1501. It contains hybrid eclipses on August 11, 1627 through December 9, 1825 and total eclipses from December 21, 1843 through March 26, 2601. The series ends at member 71 as a partial eclipse on July 3, 2763. Members in the same column are one
       <a href="/wiki/Exeligmos" title="Exeligmos">
        exeligmos
       </a>
       apart and thus occur in the same geographic area.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_13,_2132" title="Solar eclipse of June 13, 2132">
        solar eclipse of June 13, 2132
       </a>
       will be the longest total solar eclipse since
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_11,_1991" title="Solar eclipse of July 11, 1991">
        July 11, 1991
       </a>
       at 6 minutes, 55 seconds.
      </p>
      <p>
       The longest duration of totality will be produced by member 39 at 7 minutes, 29 seconds on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_16,_2186" title="Solar eclipse of July 16, 2186">
        July 16, 2186
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is the longest solar eclipse computed between 4000BC and 6000AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_series">
        Metonic series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
