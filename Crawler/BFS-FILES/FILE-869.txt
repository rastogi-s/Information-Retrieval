   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Goonhilly Satellite Earth Station
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

      <p>
       <b>
        Goonhilly Satellite Earth Station
       </b>
       is a large
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiocommunication" title="Radiocommunication">
        radiocommunication
       </a>
       site located on
       <a href="/wiki/Goonhilly_Downs" title="Goonhilly Downs">
        Goonhilly Downs
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Helston" title="Helston">
        Helston
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/The_Lizard" title="The Lizard">
        Lizard peninsula
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">
        Cornwall
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       . Owned by Goonhilly Earth Station Ltd
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       under a 999-year lease from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/BT_Group_plc" title="BT Group plc">
        BT Group plc
       </a>
       , it was at one time the largest
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_station" title="Earth station">
        earth station
       </a>
       in the world, with more than 25 communications dishes in use and over 60 in total. The site also links into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Transatlantic_telephone_cable" title="Transatlantic telephone cable">
        undersea cable lines
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Its first dish, Antenna One (dubbed "Arthur"), was built in 1962 to link with
       <a href="/wiki/Telstar" title="Telstar">
        Telstar
       </a>
       . It was the first open
       <a href="/wiki/Parabolic_antenna" title="Parabolic antenna">
        parabolic design
       </a>
       and is 25.9 metres (85 feet) in diameter and weighs 1,118
       <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">
        tonnes
       </a>
       . After
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Centre_de_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9communication_par_satellite_de_Pleumeur-Bodou&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Centre de télécommunication par satellite de Pleumeur-Bodou (page does not exist)">
        Centre de télécommunication par satellite de Pleumeur-Bodou
       </a>
       <span class="noprint" style="font-size:85%; font-style: normal;">
        (
        <a class="extiw" href="https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centre_de_t%C3%A9l%C3%A9communication_par_satellite_de_Pleumeur-Bodou" title="fr:Centre de télécommunication par satellite de Pleumeur-Bodou">
         fr
        </a>
        )
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Brittany" title="Brittany">
        Brittany
       </a>
       ) which received the first live transatlantic television broadcasts from the United States via the Telstar satellite at 0H47 GMT on 11 July 1962, Arthur received his first video in the middle of the same day. It is now a Grade II
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Listed_structure" title="Listed structure">
        listed structure
       </a>
       and is therefore protected.
      </p>
      <p>
       The site has also played a key role in communications events such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Muhammad_Ali" title="Muhammad Ali">
        Muhammad Ali
       </a>
       fights, the
       <a href="/wiki/Olympic_Games" title="Olympic Games">
        Olympic Games
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       Moon landing, and 1985's
       <a href="/wiki/Live_Aid" title="Live Aid">
        Live Aid
       </a>
       concert.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Statistics">
        Statistics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Statistics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The site's largest dish, dubbed "Merlin", has a diameter of 32 metres (105 feet). Other dishes include Guinevere, Tristan, and Isolde after characters in
       <a href="/wiki/King_Arthur" title="King Arthur">
        Arthurian
       </a>
       legend, much of which takes place in Cornwall.
      </p>
      <p>
       The earth station is powered by the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Grid_(Great_Britain)" title="National Grid (Great Britain)">
        National Grid
       </a>
       . If power fails, all essential equipment will run off huge batteries for up to 20 minutes, during which time four one-megawatt diesel generators will take over. The nearby
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wind_generator" title="Wind generator">
        wind generator
       </a>
       farm is not part of the complex.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Closure">
        Closure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Closure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On 12 September 2006, BT announced it would shut down satellite operations at Goonhilly in 2008, and move them to
       <a href="/wiki/Madley_Communications_Centre" title="Madley Communications Centre">
        Madley Communications Centre
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Herefordshire" title="Herefordshire">
        Herefordshire
       </a>
       , making that centre BT's only earth station.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visitor_centre">
        Visitor centre
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Visitor centre">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Until Easter 2010 the site had a visitor centre inside which the
       <a href="/wiki/Connected_Earth" title="Connected Earth">
        Connected Earth
       </a>
       gallery told the history of satellite communications. There were many other interactive exhibits, a cafe, a shop and one of Britain's fastest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cybercaf%C3%A9" title="Cybercafé">
        cybercafés
       </a>
       (a one gigabit pipe and a theoretical maximum speed per computer of 100 Mbit). There were also tours around the main BT site and into the heart of Arthur.
      </p>
      <p>
       At its prime, the site attracted around 80,000 visitors a year, but in March 2010 BT announced that the visitor centre would be "Closed for Easter and beyond, until further notice."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Future">
        Future
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Future">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On 11 January 2011 it was announced that part of the site is to be sold to create a space science centre.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This will involve upgrading some of the dishes to make them suitable for "deep space communication with spacecraft missions". A new company has been formed to manage the operations, Goonhilly Earth Station Ltd.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The company will lease most of the antennas for at least three years with the option to buy the entire complex in the future. Goonhilly Earth Station Limited took ownership of the site in January 2014.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are plans to connect one or more of the Goonhilly dishes into global radio astronomy interferometer networks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are also plans to upgrade the former visitor centre into "an outreach centre promoting space and space science for visitors, including local residents and schools".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-autogenerated1_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-autogenerated1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In July 2015
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       has begun a 9-month feasibility study to examine if antenna Goonhilly 6 could be used to support
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission_1" title="Exploration Mission 1">
        Exploration Mission 1
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Orion_(spacecraft)" title="Orion (spacecraft)">
        Orion Multi-Purpose Crew Vehicle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The site is now (2017) a partner in the bid by
       <a href="/wiki/Cornwall_Airport_Newquay" title="Cornwall Airport Newquay">
        Cornwall Airport Newquay
       </a>
       to become the UK's first Spaceport. A decision may be announced in the summer of 2017.
      </p>
