   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Solar prominence
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="599" data-file-width="600" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg/220px-Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg/330px-Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg/440px-Sun_earth_jupiter_whole_600.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

         <div class="magnify">

         </div>
         Solar Prominence with images of Jupiter and Earth for size comparison.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        prominence
       </b>
       is a large, bright, gaseous feature extending outward from the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's surface, often in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
        loop
       </a>
       shape. Prominences are anchored to the Sun's surface in the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       , and extend outwards into the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . While the corona consists of extremely hot
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ionized" title="Ionized">
        ionized
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gas" title="Gas">
        gases
       </a>
       , known as
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , which do not emit much
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       , prominences contain much cooler plasma, similar in composition to that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       . The prominence plasma is typically a hundred times more lit and denser than the coronal plasma. A prominence forms over timescales of about a day and may persist in the corona for several weeks or months, looping hundreds of thousands of miles into space. Some prominences break apart and may then give rise to
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       . Scientists are currently researching how and why prominences are formed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The red-glowing looped material is plasma, a hot gas composed of electrically charged hydrogen and helium. The prominence plasma flows along a tangled and twisted structure of magnetic fields generated by the sun’s internal dynamo. An erupting prominence occurs when such a structure becomes unstable and bursts outward, releasing the plasma.
      </p>
      <p>
       A typical prominence extends over many thousands of kilometers; the largest on record was estimated at over 800,000 kilometres (500,000 mi) long
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-univtoday_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-univtoday-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       – roughly the radius of the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Historical">
        Historical
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_prominence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Historical">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first detailed description of a solar prominence was in 14th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Codex" title="Laurentian Codex">
        Laurentian Codex
       </a>
       , describing the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_1_May_1185" title="Solar eclipse of 1 May 1185">
        Solar eclipse of 1 May 1185
       </a>
       . They were described as "flame-like tongues of live embers."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montreal_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Montreal-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsletter_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Newsletter-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prominences_and_Filaments">
        Prominences and Filaments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_prominence&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Prominences and Filaments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When a prominence is viewed from a different perspective so that it is against the sun instead of against space, it appears darker than the surrounding background. This formation is instead called a solar filament.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-univtoday_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-univtoday-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is possible for a projection to be both a filament and a prominence. Some prominences are so powerful that they throw out matter from the Sun into space at speeds ranging from 600 km/s to more than 1000 km/s. Other prominences form huge loops or arching columns of glowing gases over sunspots that can reach heights of hundreds of thousands of kilometers. Prominences may last for a few days or even for a few months.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
