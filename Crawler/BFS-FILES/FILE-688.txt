   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Tachocline
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:299px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="314" data-file-width="297" height="314" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/86/Tachocline.gif" width="297"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tachocline.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Internal rotation in the Sun, showing differential rotation in the outer convective region and almost uniform rotation in the central radiative region. The transition between these regions is called the tachocline.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        tachocline
       </b>
       is the transition region of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       between the
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation_zone" title="Radiation zone">
        radiative interior
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Differential_rotation" title="Differential rotation">
        differentially rotating
       </a>
       outer
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convective zone
       </a>
       . It is in the outer third of the Sun (by radius). This causes the region to have a very large
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shear_(fluid)" title="Shear (fluid)">
        shear
       </a>
       as the rotation rate changes very rapidly. The convective exterior rotates as a normal fluid with differential rotation with the poles rotating slowly and the equator rotating quickly. The radiative interior exhibits solid-body rotation, possibly due to a
       <a href="/wiki/Fossil_stellar_magnetic_field" title="Fossil stellar magnetic field">
        fossil field
       </a>
       . The rotation rate through the interior is roughly equal to the rotation rate at mid-latitudes, i.e. in-between the rate at the slow poles and the fast equator. Recent results from
       <a href="/wiki/Helioseismology" title="Helioseismology">
        helioseismology
       </a>
       indicate that the tachocline is located at a radius of at most 0.70 times the Solar radius (measured from the core, i.e., the surface is at 1 solar radius), with a thickness of 0.04 times the solar radius. This would mean the area has a very large shear profile that is one way that large scale magnetic fields can be formed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The geometry and width of the tachocline are thought to play an important role in models of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_dynamo" title="Solar dynamo">
        solar dynamo
       </a>
       by winding up the weaker
       <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">
        poloidal
       </a>
       field to create a much stronger
       <a href="/wiki/Toroidal_and_poloidal" title="Toroidal and poloidal">
        toroidal
       </a>
       field. Recent radio observations of cooler stars and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Brown_dwarfs" title="Brown dwarfs">
        brown dwarfs
       </a>
       , which do not have a radiative core and only have a convective zone, demonstrate that they maintain large-scale, solar-strength magnetic fields and display solar-like activity despite the absence of tachoclines. This suggests that the convective zone alone may be responsible for the function of the solar dynamo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        tachocline
       </i>
       was coined in a paper by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Spiegel" title="Edward Spiegel">
        Edward Spiegel
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean-Paul_Zahn&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean-Paul Zahn (page does not exist)">
        Jean-Paul Zahn
       </a>
       in 1992
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by analogy to the oceanic
       <a href="/wiki/Thermocline" title="Thermocline">
        thermocline
       </a>
       .
      </p>
