    Solar eclipse of 1 May 1185
   </h1>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        solar eclipse of 1 May 1185
       </b>
       was a total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       visible in
       <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">
        Central America
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Northern_Europe" title="Northern Europe">
        Northern Europe
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">
        Kazakhstan
       </a>
       . The eclipse is number 30 in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_115" title="Solar Saros 115">
        Solar Saros 115
       </a>
       series. The eclipse shadow on the Earth's surface was at its greatest at 13:18:02
       <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
        Universal Time
       </a>
       . The sun was in
       <a href="/wiki/Taurus_(constellation)" title="Taurus (constellation)">
        Taurus
       </a>
       at this time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fred_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fred-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Eclipse_path">
        Eclipse path
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_1_May_1185&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Eclipse path">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The shadow axis passed between the Earth's center and the north pole. The minimum distance from the center of the Earth to the Moon's shadow cone axis was 3,357 kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-280KM_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-280KM-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The average length of the Moon's shadow was 373,320 kilometers. The distance from the Earth to the Moon on 1 May 1185 was 347,727 kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-280KM_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-280KM-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The apparent diameter of the Moon was 1.0736 times that of the sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-280KM_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-280KM-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shadow had a maximum width of 280 kilometers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-path_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-path-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It began on the west coast of Central America and crossed the Atlantic Ocean in a northeasterly direction. The shadow passed through present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Nicaragua" title="Nicaragua">
        Nicaragua
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Haiti" title="Haiti">
        Haiti
       </a>
       . The maximum duration of the eclipse occurred in the middle of the North Atlantic at coordinates 46°N, 37.2°W.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fred_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fred-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The maximum duration of totality was 5 minutes and 10 seconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TotalityDive_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TotalityDive-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The eclipse magnitude is 1.0736.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fred_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fred-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The eclipse was preceded two weeks earlier by a partial lunar eclipse on April 16.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fred_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fred-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A partial eclipse came ashore in Scotland, crossed the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Sea" title="North Sea">
        North Sea
       </a>
       , and entered
       <a href="/wiki/Norway" title="Norway">
        Norway
       </a>
       . According to astronomer Sheridan Williams, there was probably a drop in temperature at the time of the eclipse, around 2 pm in Scotland.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He says that it was Scotland's fifth longest total eclipse at 4 minutes and 40 seconds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Williams also notes that some older observers in Scotland may have also witnessed the previous eclipse, 52 years earlier.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965.9-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After passing through present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">
        Sweden
       </a>
       , the eclipse touched a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Finland" title="Finland">
        Finland
       </a>
       and covered the northern part of the Baltic Sea at the
       <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Finland" title="Gulf of Finland">
        Gulf of Finland
       </a>
       . The eclipse then entered Russia. The eclipse's shadow touched part of what would be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/St._Petersburg" title="St. Petersburg">
        St. Petersburg
       </a>
       , as well as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novgorod" title="Novgorod">
        Novgorod
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rostov" title="Rostov">
        Rostov
       </a>
       . As the shadow journeyed in a southeasterly direction, it passed through the modern sites of
       <a href="/wiki/Nizhny_Novgorod" title="Nizhny Novgorod">
        Nizhny Novgorod
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Kazan" title="Kazan">
        Kazan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ufa" title="Ufa">
        Ufa
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitogorsk" title="Magnitogorsk">
        Magnitogorsk
       </a>
       . The eclipse shadow started diminishing when it entered present-day
       <a href="/wiki/Kazakhstan" title="Kazakhstan">
        Kazakhstan
       </a>
       and extinguished completely about 30 miles before reaching
       <a href="/wiki/Astana" title="Astana">
        Astana
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASApath_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASApath-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965-9_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams19965-9-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FirstGov_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FirstGov-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_literature">
        In literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_1_May_1185&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: In literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Study of eclipse occurrences can create a precise timetable for historical events that are elusive and could not otherwise be accurately dated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [A]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse was mentioned in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kievan_Rus%27" title="Kievan Rus'">
        Kievan Rus'
       </a>
       epic poem
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tale_of_Igor%27s_Campaign" title="Tale of Igor's Campaign">
         Lay of the Host of Igor
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was seen by Prince
       <a href="/wiki/Igor_Svyatoslavich" title="Igor Svyatoslavich">
        Igor Svyatoslavich
       </a>
       and his army whilst on their campaign against the
       <a href="/wiki/Cumans" title="Cumans">
        Polovtsians
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETerras1985425_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETerras1985425-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was interpreted as a message from God prophesying trouble.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trouble_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-trouble-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hence frightening Igor's men who thought it a bad omen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUshinskiĭ1978543_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUshinskiĭ1978543-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WPZOOM_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WPZOOM-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to the
       <i>
        Lay
       </i>
       , after the eclipse Igor gave a long speech to his
       <a href="/wiki/Retinue" title="Retinue">
        retinue
       </a>
       to allay their fears before proceeding on his campaign.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTENabokov200971_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTENabokov200971-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse is mentioned in the 14th-century
       <a href="/wiki/Laurentian_Codex" title="Laurentian Codex">
        Laurentian Chronicle
       </a>
       with the first detailed description of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_prominence" title="Solar prominence">
        solar prominences
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-past_eclipses_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-past_eclipses-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NCAR_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NCAR-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They were described as flame-like tongues of live embers:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Montreal_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Montreal-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESandersonHarrington200641_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESandersonHarrington200641-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>
        On the first day of the month of May, on the day of the Saint Prophet Jeremiah, on Wednesday, during the evening service, there was a sign in the Sun. It became very dark, even the stars could be seen; it seemed to men as if everything were green, and the Sun became like a crescent of the Moon, from the horns of which a glow similar to that of red-hot charcoals was emanating. It was terrible to see this sign of the Lord.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newsletter_23-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Newsletter-23">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStephenson2008395_24-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStephenson2008395-24">
          [23]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_of_Melrose" title="Chronicle of Melrose">
        Melrose Chronicle
       </a>
       claims that "stars appeared" at
       <a href="/wiki/Melrose,_Scottish_Borders" title="Melrose, Scottish Borders">
        Melrose
       </a>
       in Scotland during the eclipse of 1185, but this is doubted by the writer Thomas Cooper who points out that scientific calculations indicate that the eclipse was not total at Melrose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Brut_y_Tywysogion" title="Brut y Tywysogion">
        Brut y Tywysogion
       </a>
       , the Welsh Chronicle of the Princes, says of the solar eclipse of 1 May 1185
       <i>
        Yn y ulwyddyn honno dyw Calan Mei y sumudawd yr heul y lliw; ac y dywat rei uot anei diffyc
       </i>
       , which translates as:
       <i>
        In that year on the day of the
        <a href="/wiki/Calends" title="Calends">
         Calends
        </a>
        of May the Sun changed its colour; and some said that it was under an eclipse.
       </i>
       Bryn Jones believes there was a total solar eclipse across the Scottish Highlands, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Isles" title="Western Isles">
        Western Isles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Orkney" title="Orkney">
        Orkney
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BrynJones_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BrynJones-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etana_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etana-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
