      <p>
       <b>
        KREEP
       </b>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Acronym" title="Acronym">
        acronym
       </a>
       built from the letters
       <b>
        K
       </b>
       (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chemical_symbol" title="Chemical symbol">
        atomic symbol
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       ),
       <b>
        REE
       </b>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Rare-earth_element" title="Rare-earth element">
        rare-earth elements
       </a>
       ) and
       <b>
        P
       </b>
       (for
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
        phosphorus
       </a>
       ), is a
       <a href="/wiki/Geochemistry" title="Geochemistry">
        geochemical
       </a>
       component of some lunar impact
       <a href="/wiki/Breccia" title="Breccia">
        breccia
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       rocks. Its most significant feature is somewhat enhanced concentration of a majority of so-called "incompatible" elements
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (those that are concentrated in the liquid phase during
       <a href="/wiki/Igneous_differentiation" title="Igneous differentiation">
        magma crystallization
       </a>
       ) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
        heat
       </a>
       -producing elements, namely
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Uranium" title="Uranium">
        uranium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thorium" title="Thorium">
        thorium
       </a>
       , and potassium (due to presence of the radioactive
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium-40" title="Potassium-40">
        <sup>
         40
        </sup>
        K
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Typical_composition">
        Typical composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=KREEP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Typical composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The typical composition of KREEP includes about one percent, by mass, of potassium and phosphorus oxides, 20 to 25 parts per million of
       <a href="/wiki/Rubidium" title="Rubidium">
        rubidium
       </a>
       , and a concentration of the
       <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">
        element
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lanthanum" title="Lanthanum">
        lanthanum
       </a>
       that is 300 to 350 times the concentrations found in carbonaceous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chondrites" title="Chondrites">
        chondrites
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_origin">
        Possible origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=KREEP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Possible origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Indirectly, it has been deduced that the origin of KREEP is contained in the origin of the Moon. This is now commonly thought to be the result of
       <a href="/wiki/Theia_(planet)" title="Theia (planet)">
        a rocky object
       </a>
       the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        struck the Earth
       </a>
       about 4.5 billion (4.5×10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       ) years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Belbruno_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Belbruno-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This collision threw a large amount of broken rock into orbit around the Earth. This ultimately
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        gathered together
       </a>
       to form the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Given the high energy such a collision would involve, it has been deduced that a large portion of the Moon would have been liquified, and this formed a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_magma_ocean" title="Lunar magma ocean">
        lunar magma ocean
       </a>
       . As the crystallization of this liquid rock proceeded, minerals such as
       <a href="/wiki/Olivine" title="Olivine">
        olivine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Pyroxene" title="Pyroxene">
        pyroxene
       </a>
       precipitated and sank to the bottom to form the lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       After the solidification was about 75% complete, the material
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthite" title="Anorthite">
        anorthositic plagioclase
       </a>
       began to crystallize, and because of its low density, it floated, forming a solid crust. Hence, elements that are usually incompatible (i.e., those that usually partition in the liquid phase) would have been progressively concentrated into the magma. Thus a KREEP-rich magma was formed that was sandwiched at first between the crust and mantle. The evidence for these processes comes from the highly anorthositic composition of the crust of the lunar highlands, as well as the presence of the rocks rich in KREEP.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_Prospector_measurements">
        Lunar Prospector measurements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=KREEP&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar Prospector measurements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Before the mission of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       , it was commonly thought that these KREEP materials had been formed in a widespread layer beneath the crust. However, the measurements from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamma-ray" title="Gamma-ray">
        gamma-ray
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometer
       </a>
       on-board this satellite showed that the KREEP-containing rocks are primarily concentrated underneath the
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Mare Imbrium
       </a>
       . This is a unique lunar geological province that is now known as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_terranes" title="Lunar terranes">
        Procellarum KREEP Terrane
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Basins far from this province that dug deeply into the crust (and possibly the mantle), such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Crisium" title="Mare Crisium">
        Mare Crisium
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Orientale" title="Mare Orientale">
        Mare Orientale
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin" title="South Pole–Aitken basin">
        South Pole–Aitken basin
       </a>
       , show only little or no enhancements of KREEP within their rims or ejecta. The enhancement of heat-producing radioactive elements within the crust (and/or the mantle) of the Procellarum KREEP Terrane is almost certainly responsible for the longevity and intensity of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare volcanism
       </a>
       on the nearside of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
