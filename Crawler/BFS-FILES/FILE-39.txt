      <p>
       The
       <b>
        photosphere
       </b>
       is a star's outer shell from which light is radiated. The term itself is derived from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       roots, φῶς, φωτός/
       <i>
        phos, photos
       </i>
       meaning "light" and σφαῖρα/
       <i>
        sphaira
       </i>
       meaning "sphere", in reference to the fact that it is a spherical surface that is perceived to emit light. It extends into a star's surface until the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       becomes opaque, equivalent to an
       <a href="/wiki/Optical_depth" title="Optical depth">
        optical depth
       </a>
       of approximately 2/3.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In other words, a photosphere is the deepest region of a luminous object, usually a star, that is transparent to photons of certain
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">
        Temperature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Temperature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The surface of a star is defined to have a temperature given by the effective temperature in the
       <a href="/wiki/Stefan%E2%80%93Boltzmann_law" title="Stefan–Boltzmann law">
        Stefan–Boltzmann law
       </a>
       . Stars, except
       <a href="/wiki/Neutron_star" title="Neutron star">
        neutron stars
       </a>
       , have no solid surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Therefore, the photosphere is typically used to describe the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's or another
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        star
       </a>
       's visual surface.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       is composed primarily of the chemical elements
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Helium" title="Helium">
        helium
       </a>
       ; they account for 74.9% and 23.8% of the mass of the Sun in the photosphere, respectively. All heavier elements, called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metals" title="Metals">
        metals
       </a>
       in astronomy, account for less than 2% of the mass, with oxygen (roughly 1% of the Sun's mass), carbon (0.3%), neon (0.2%), and iron (0.2%) being the most abundant.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sun">
        Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1246" data-file-width="877" height="242" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg/170px-Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg/255px-Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg/340px-Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_atmosphere" title="Solar atmosphere">
          Solar atmosphere
         </a>
         : temperature and density. See
         <a href="/wiki/File_talk:Sun_Atmosphere_Temperature_and_Density_SkyLab.jpg" title="File talk:Sun Atmosphere Temperature and Density SkyLab.jpg">
          here
         </a>
         for meanings of extra lines in the graph.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's photosphere has a temperature between 4,500 and 6,000
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       (4,230 and 5,730 °C)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (with an effective temperature of 5,777
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        K
       </a>
       (5,504 °C))
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       of about
       <a href="/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(density)#-4" title="Orders of magnitude (density)">
        2
       </a>
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         4
        </span>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">
        kg
       </a>
       /
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cubic_meter" title="Cubic meter">
        m
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       other stars may have hotter or cooler photospheres. The Sun's photosphere is composed of
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_cell" title="Convection cell">
        convection cells
       </a>
       called
       <a href="/wiki/Granule_(solar_physics)" title="Granule (solar physics)">
        granules
       </a>
       —cells of
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       each approximately 1000
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilometer" title="Kilometer">
        kilometers
       </a>
       in diameter
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with hot rising plasma in the center and cooler plasma falling in the narrow spaces between them. Each granule has a lifespan of only about eight minutes, resulting in a continually shifting "boiling" pattern. Grouping the typical granules are super granules up to 30,000 kilometers in diameter with lifespans of up to 24 hours. These details are too fine to see on other stars.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_layers">
        Other layers
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Photosphere&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other layers">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Sun's visible atmosphere has other layers above the photosphere: the 2,000 kilometer-deep
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       (typically observed by filtered light, for example
       <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">
        H-alpha
       </a>
       ) lies just between the photosphere and the much hotter but more tenuous
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . Other "surface features" on the photosphere are
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       .
      </p>
