    Magnitude of eclipse
   </h1>

      <p>
       <b>
        Magnitude of eclipse
       </b>
       is the fraction of the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial body
       </a>
       being
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipsed
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasapage_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasapage-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This applies to all celestial eclipses. The magnitude of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Partial_eclipse" title="Partial eclipse">
        partial
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse#Types" title="Solar eclipse">
        annular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       is always between 0.0 and 1.0, while the magnitude of a total solar eclipse is always at least 1.0.
      </p>
      <p>
       This measure should not be confused with the covered fraction of the apparent
       <i>
        area
       </i>
       (disk) of the eclipsed body, whereas the magnitude of an eclipse is strictly a ratio of diameters. Neither should it be confused with the astronomical
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">
        magnitude
       </a>
       scale of
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent brightness
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_the_magnitude_on_a_solar_eclipse">
        Effect of the magnitude on a solar eclipse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnitude_of_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Effect of the magnitude on a solar eclipse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg/220px-Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg/330px-Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg/440px-Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_eclipse_visualisation.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Each icon shows the view from the centre of its black spot, representing the moon (not to scale)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        apparent sizes
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       are both approximately 0.5°, or 30', but both vary because the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        distance
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and Moon varies. (The
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        distance
       </a>
       between Earth and Sun also varies, but the effect is slight in comparison.)
      </p>
      <p>
       In an annular solar eclipse, the magnitude of the eclipse is the ratio between the apparent
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameters
       </a>
       of the Moon and that of the Sun during the maximum eclipse, yielding a ratio less than 1.0. As the magnitude of eclipse is less than one, the disk of the Moon cannot completely cover the Sun. When the centers of the two disks are sufficiently aligned, a ring of sunlight remains visible around the Moon. This is called an annular eclipse, from
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       <i>
        annulus
       </i>
       , meaning "ring".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-happyliving_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-happyliving-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For a total solar eclipse to happen, the ratio of the apparent diameters of the Moon and of the Sun must be 1.0 or more, and the three celestial bodies (Sun, Earth and Moon) must be aligned centrally enough. When that is the case, the Moon's disk covers the Sun's disk in the sky completely. The path of totality (i.e. of the travelling shadow of the Moon cutting off all direct sunlight from reaching the Earth's surface) is a relatively narrow strip, at most a few hundreds of kilometers across.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a partial solar eclipse, the magnitude of the eclipse is the fraction of the Sun's diameter occulted by the Moon at the time of maximum eclipse. As seen from one location, the momentary eclipse magnitude varies, being exactly 0.0 at the start of the eclipse, rising to some maximum value, and then decreasing to 0.0 at the end of the eclipse. When one says "the magnitude of the eclipse" without further specification, one usually means the maximum value of the magnitude of the eclipse.
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse magnitude varies not only between eclipses, but also by viewing location. An eclipse may be annular in one location and total in another. These mixed-type eclipses are called hybrid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-happyliving_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-happyliving-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effect_of_the_magnitude_on_a_lunar_eclipse">
        Effect of the magnitude on a lunar eclipse
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnitude_of_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Effect of the magnitude on a lunar eclipse">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The effect on a lunar eclipse is quite similar, with a few differences. First, the eclipsed body is the Moon and the eclipsing 'body' is the Earth's shadow. Second, since the Earth's shadow at the Moon's distance always is considerably larger than the Moon, a lunar eclipse can never be annular but is always partial or total. Third, the Earth's shadow has two components: the dark
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       and the much brighter
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbra" title="Penumbra">
        penumbra
       </a>
       . A lunar eclipse will have two geometric magnitudes: the umbral magnitude and the penumbral magnitude. If the three bodies are not aligned enough, the Moon does not reach into the Earth's umbra - it may still pass through the Earth's penumbra though, and such an eclipse is called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penumbral_eclipse" title="Penumbral eclipse">
        penumbral eclipse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
