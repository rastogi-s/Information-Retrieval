      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectroscopy
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        forbidden mechanism
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        forbidden transition
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        forbidden line
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Spectral_line" title="Spectral line">
        spectral line
       </a>
       associated with absorption or emission of light by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_nuclei" title="Atomic nuclei">
        atomic nuclei
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atoms
       </a>
       , or
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecules
       </a>
       which undergo a transition that is not allowed by a particular
       <a href="/wiki/Selection_rule" title="Selection rule">
        selection rule
       </a>
       but is allowed if the approximation associated with that rule is not made.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BunkerJensen2006_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BunkerJensen2006-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, in a situation where, according to usual approximations (such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment" title="Electric dipole moment">
        electric-dipole approximation
       </a>
       for the interaction with light), the process cannot happen, but at a higher level of approximation (
       <i>
        e.g.
       </i>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole_moment" title="Magnetic dipole moment">
        magnetic dipole
       </a>
       , or, electric
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole" title="Quadrupole">
        quadrupole
       </a>
       ) the process is allowed but at a much lower rate.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example is
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorescence" title="Phosphorescence">
        phosphorescent
       </a>
       glow in the dark materials,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LisenskyPatel1996_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LisenskyPatel1996-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which absorb light and form an excited state whose decay involves a spin flip, and is therefore forbidden by electric dipole transitions. The result is emission of light slowly over minutes or hours.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the transitions are nominally forbidden, there is a small probability of their spontaneous occurrence, should an
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">
        atomic nucleus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Atom" title="Atom">
        atom
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Molecule" title="Molecule">
        molecule
       </a>
       be raised to an excited state. More precisely, there is a certain probability that such an excited entity will make a forbidden transition to a lower energy state per unit time; by definition, this probability is much lower than that for any transition permitted or allowed by the selection rules. Therefore, if a state can de-excite via a permitted transition (or otherwise, e.g. via collisions) it will almost certainly do so before any transition occurs via a forbidden route. Nevertheless, most forbidden transitions are only relatively unlikely: states that can only decay in this way (so-called
       <a href="/wiki/Metastability" title="Metastability">
        meta-stable
       </a>
       states) usually have lifetimes on the order milliseconds to seconds, compared to less than a microsecond for decay via permitted transitions. In some radioactive decay systems, multiple levels of forbiddenness can stretch life times by many orders of magnitude for each additional unit by which the system changes beyond what is most allowed under the selection rules.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Such excited states can last years, or even for many billions of years (too long to have been measured).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_radioactive_decay">
        In radioactive decay
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_mechanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: In radioactive decay">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gamma_decay">
        Gamma decay
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_mechanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Gamma decay">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The most common mechanism for suppression of the rate of gamma decay of excited atomic nuclei, and thus make possible the existence of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Metastable_isomer" title="Metastable isomer">
        metastable isomer
       </a>
       for the nucleus, is lack of a decay route for the excited state that will change nuclear angular momentum (along any given direction) by the most common (allowed) amount of 1 quantum unit
       <span class="mwe-math-element">
        <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
         <math alttext="{\displaystyle \hbar }" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
          <semantics>
           <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
            <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
             <mi class="MJX-variant">
              ℏ
              <!-- ℏ -->
             </mi>
            </mstyle>
           </mrow>
           <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
            {\displaystyle \hbar }
           </annotation>
          </semantics>
         </math>
        </span>
        <img alt="\hbar " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41" style="vertical-align: -0.338ex; width:1.306ex; height:2.176ex;"/>
       </span>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Spin_(physics)" title="Spin (physics)">
        spin
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum" title="Angular momentum">
        angular momentum
       </a>
       . Such a change is necessary to emit a gamma-ray photon, which has a spin of 1 unit in this system. Integral changes of 2, 3, 4, and more units in angular momentum are possible (the emitted photons carry off the additional angular momentum), but changes of more than 1 unit are known as forbidden transitions. Each degree of forbiddenness (additional unit of spin change larger than 1, that the emitted gamma ray must carry) inhibits decay rate by about 5 orders of magnitude.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The highest known spin change of 8 units occurs in the decay of
       <a href="/wiki/Isotopes_of_tantalum" title="Isotopes of tantalum">
        Ta-180m
       </a>
       , which suppresses its decay by a factor of 10
       <sup>
        35
       </sup>
       from that associated with 1 unit, so that instead of a natural gamma decay half life of 10
       <sup>
        −12
       </sup>
       seconds, it has a half life of more than 10
       <sup>
        23
       </sup>
       seconds, or at least 3 x 10
       <sup>
        15
       </sup>
       years, and thus has yet to be observed to decay.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although gamma decays with nuclear angular momentum changes of 2, 3, 4, etc., are forbidden, they are only relatively forbidden, and do proceed, but with a slower rate than the normal allowed change of 1 unit. However, gamma emission is absolutely forbidden when the nucleus begins in a zero-spin state, as such an emission would not conserve angular momentum. These transitions cannot occur by gamma decay, but must proceed by another route, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Beta_decay" title="Beta decay">
        beta decay
       </a>
       in some cases, or
       <a href="/wiki/Internal_conversion" title="Internal conversion">
        internal conversion
       </a>
       where beta decay is not favored.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Beta_decay">
        Beta decay
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_mechanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Beta decay">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Beta decays here are classified according to the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_momentum_operator" title="Angular momentum operator">
        <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
         L
        </span>
        -value
       </a>
       of the emitted radiation. Unlike gamma decays, beta decays may proceed from a nucleus with a spin of zero and even parity, to a nucleus also with a spin of zero and even parity (Fermi transition). This is possible because the electron and neutrino emitted may be of opposing spin (giving a radiation total angular momentum of zero), thus preserving angular momentum of the initial state even if the nucleus remains at spin-zero before and after emission. This type of emission is super-allowed meaning that it is the most rapid type of beta decay in nuclei that are susceptible to a change in proton/neutron ratios that accompanies a beta decay process.
      </p>
      <p>
       The next possible total angular momentum of the electron and neutrino emitted in beta decay is a combined spin of 1 (electron and neutrino spinning in the same direction), and is allowed. This type of emission (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gamow-Teller_transition" title="Gamow-Teller transition">
        Gamow-Teller transition
       </a>
       ) changes nuclear spin by a unit of 1 to compensate. States involving higher angular momenta of the emitted radiation (2, 3, 4, etc.) are forbidden, and are ranked in degree of forbiddenness by their increasing angular momentum.
      </p>
      <p>
       Specifically, when
       <span class="texhtml">
        <i>
         L
        </i>
        &gt; 0
       </span>
       , the decay is referred to as forbidden. Nuclear
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Selection_rules" title="Selection rules">
        selection rules
       </a>
       require L-values greater than two to be accompanied by changes in both
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_spin" title="Nuclear spin">
        nuclear spin
       </a>
       (
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        J
       </span>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Parity_(physics)" title="Parity (physics)">
        parity
       </a>
       (π). The selection rules for the
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic;">
        L
       </span>
       th forbidden transitions are:
      </p>

       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

      <p>
       Forbidden transitions in rare earth atoms such as
       <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">
        erbium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">
        neodymium
       </a>
       make them useful as dopants for solid-state lasing media. In such media, the atoms are held in a matrix which keeps them from de-exciting by collision, and the long half life of their excited states makes them easy to optically pump to create a large population of excited atoms. Neodymium doped glass derives its unusual coloration from forbidden
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       -
       <i>
        f
       </i>
       transitions within the neodymium atom, and is used in extremely high power
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid_state_laser" title="Solid state laser">
        solid state lasers
       </a>
       . Bulk
       <a href="/wiki/Semiconductor" title="Semiconductor">
        semiconductor
       </a>
       transitions can also be forbidden by symmetry, which change the functional form of the absorption spectrum, as can be shown in a
       <a href="/wiki/Tauc_plot" title="Tauc plot">
        Tauc plot
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_astrophysics_and_atomic_physics">
        In astrophysics and atomic physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Forbidden_mechanism&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: In astrophysics and atomic physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Forbidden emission lines have been observed in extremely low-
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       gases and
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasmas
       </a>
       , either in
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       or in the extreme
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Upper_atmosphere" title="Upper atmosphere">
        upper atmosphere
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EDoA_161_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EDoA_161-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In space environments, densities may be only a few atoms per
       <a href="/wiki/Cubic_centimetre" title="Cubic centimetre">
        cubic centimetre
       </a>
       , making atomic collisions unlikely. Under such conditions, once an atom or molecule has been excited for any reason into a meta-stable state, then it is almost certain to decay by emitting a forbidden-line photon. Since meta-stable states are rather common, forbidden transitions account for a significant percentage of the photons emitted by the ultra-low density gas in space. Forbidden transitions in
       <a href="/wiki/Highly_charged_ion" title="Highly charged ion">
        highly charged ions
       </a>
       resulting in the emission of visible, vacuum-ultraviolet, soft x-ray and x-ray photons are routinely observed in certain laboratory devices such as
       <a href="/wiki/Electron_beam_ion_trap" title="Electron beam ion trap">
        electron beam ion traps
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maeckel2011_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maeckel2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and ion
       <a href="/wiki/Storage_ring" title="Storage ring">
        storage rings
       </a>
       , where in both cases residual gas densities are sufficiently low for forbidden line emission to occur before atoms are
       <a href="/wiki/Collision" title="Collision">
        collisionally
       </a>
       de-excited. Using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Laser_spectroscopy" title="Laser spectroscopy">
        laser spectroscopy
       </a>
       techniques, forbidden transitions are used to stabilize
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">
        atomic clocks
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_clock" title="Quantum clock">
        quantum clocks
       </a>
       that have the highest accuracies currently available.
      </p>
      <p>
       Forbidden lines of
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       ([N II] at 654.8 and 658.4
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanometer" title="Nanometer">
        nm
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       ([S II] at 671.6 and 673.1 nm), and
       <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">
        oxygen
       </a>
       ([O II] at 372.7 nm, and [O III] at 495.9 and 500.7 nm) are commonly observed in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophysical_plasma" title="Astrophysical plasma">
        astrophysical plasmas
       </a>
       . These lines are important to the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy_economics" title="Energy economics">
        energy balance
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nebula" title="Planetary nebula">
        planetary nebulae
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/H_II_region" title="H II region">
        H II regions
       </a>
       . The forbidden
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen_line" title="Hydrogen line">
        21-cm hydrogen line
       </a>
       is particularly important for
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_astronomy" title="Radio astronomy">
        radio astronomy
       </a>
       as it allows very cold neutral hydrogen gas to be seen. Also, the presence of [O I] and [S II] forbidden lines in the spectra of T-tauri stars implies low gas density.
      </p>
