      <p>
       A
       <b>
        solar flare
       </b>
       is a sudden flash of increased
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's brightness, usually observed near its surface. Flares are often, but not always, accompanied by a
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejection
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kopp2005_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kopp2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar flares fall on a very broad spectrum of emissions, an energy release of typically 10
       <sup>
        20
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">
        joules
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       is considered to be the median for a well-observed event, while a major event can emit up to 10
       <sup>
        25
       </sup>
       joules
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The flare ejects clouds of electrons, ions, and atoms along with the electromagnetic waves through the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       into
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       . The phenomenon therefore provides an early example of
       <a href="/wiki/Multi-messenger_astronomy" title="Multi-messenger astronomy">
        multi-messenger astronomy
       </a>
       . If ejection is in the direction of the Earth the particles hitting the upper atmosphere can cause bright
       <a href="/wiki/Aurora" title="Aurora">
        auroras
       </a>
       , and may even disrupt long range radio communication. It usually takes a day or two for these clouds to reach
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term is also used to refer to similar phenomena in other stars, where the term
       <a href="/wiki/Flare_star" title="Flare star">
        stellar flare
       </a>
       applies.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physics">
        Physics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Physics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Solar flares affect all layers of the solar atmosphere (
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere#The_Sun" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       ), when the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       medium is heated to tens of millions of degrees
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       , while the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic-ray" title="Cosmic-ray">
        cosmic-ray
       </a>
       -like
       <a href="/wiki/Electron" title="Electron">
        electrons
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Proton" title="Proton">
        protons
       </a>
       , and heavier
       <a href="/wiki/Ion" title="Ion">
        ions
       </a>
       are accelerated to near the
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light" title="Speed of light">
        speed of light
       </a>
       . They produce
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       across the
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        electromagnetic spectrum
       </a>
       at all
       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       , from
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
        radio waves
       </a>
       to
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
        gamma rays
       </a>
       , although most of the energy is spread over frequencies outside the visual range and for this reason the majority of the flares are not visible to the naked eye and must be observed with special instruments. Flares occur in active regions around
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        sunspots
       </a>
       , where intense magnetic fields penetrate the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       to link the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       to the solar interior. Flares are powered by the sudden (timescales of minutes to tens of minutes) release of magnetic energy stored in the corona. The same energy releases may produce
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       (CME), although the relation between CMEs and flares is still not well established.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-rays
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UV_radiation" title="UV radiation">
        UV radiation
       </a>
       emitted by solar flares can affect Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       and disrupt long-range radio communications. Direct radio emission at decimetric wavelengths may disturb the operation of radars and other devices that use those frequencies.
      </p>
      <p>
       Solar flares were first observed on the Sun by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">
        Richard Christopher Carrington
       </a>
       and independently by Richard Hodgson in 1859
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carrington1859_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carrington1859-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       as localized visible brightenings of small areas within a sunspot group. Stellar flares can be inferred by looking at the lightcurves produced from the telescope or satellite data of variety of other stars.
      </p>
      <p>
       The frequency of occurrence of solar flares varies, from several per day when the Sun is particularly "active" to less than one every week when the Sun is "quiet", following the 11-year cycle (the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cycle" title="Solar cycle">
        solar cycle
       </a>
       ). Large flares are less frequent than smaller ones.
      </p>
      <p>
       On July 23, 2012, a massive, and potentially damaging,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_2012" title="Solar storm of 2012">
        solar superstorm
       </a>
       (solar flare, coronal mass ejection,
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_electromagnetic_pulse#E3" title="Nuclear electromagnetic pulse">
        solar EMP
       </a>
       ) barely missed Earth, according to NASA.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140723a_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140723a-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140429_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140429-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to NASA, there may be as much as a 12% chance of a similar event occurring between 2012 and 2022,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140723a_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140723a-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although because this particular figure was based on an extreme extrapolation of the calculated frequency of future storms, the actual probability of this is quite uncertain.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cause">
        Cause
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Cause">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Flares occur when sped up charged particles, mainly electrons, interact with the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       medium. Abundant evidence implicates that the phenomenon of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       (also describable as magnetic flux transfer) leads to this copious acceleration of charged particles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the Sun, magnetic reconnection may happen on solar arcades – a series of closely occurring loops following magnetic lines of force. These lines of force quickly reconnect into a low arcade of loops leaving a helix of magnetic field unconnected to the rest of the arcade. The sudden release of energy in this reconnection is the origin of the particle acceleration. The unconnected magnetic helical field and the material that it contains may violently expand outwards forming a coronal mass ejection.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciam1_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sciam1-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This also explains why solar flares typically erupt from what are known as the active regions on the Sun where magnetic fields are much stronger on average.
      </p>
      <p>
       Although there is a general agreement on the source of a flare's energy, the mechanisms involved are still not well known. It is not clear how the magnetic energy is transformed into the particle kinetic energy, nor is it known how some particles can accelerated to the GeV range (10
       <sup>
        12
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Electronvolt" title="Electronvolt">
        electron volt
       </a>
       ) and beyond. There are also some inconsistencies regarding the total number of accelerated particles, which sometimes seems to be greater than the total number in the coronal loop. Scientists are unable to forecast flares, even to this day.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

       The classification system for solar flares uses the letters A, B, C, M or X, according to the peak flux in watts per square metre (W/m

       <a href="/wiki/Wavelength" title="Wavelength">
        wavelengths
       </a>
       100 to 800
       <a href="/wiki/Picometre" title="Picometre">
        picometre
       </a>
       , as measured at the Earth's orbital distance by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">
        GOES
       </a>
       spacecraft.

       The strength of an event within a class is noted by a numerical suffix ranging from 1 to 9, which is also the factor for that event within the class. Hence, an X2 flare is twice the strength of an X1 flare, an X3 flare is three times as powerful as an X1, and only 50% more powerful than an X2

        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       . An X2 is four times more powerful than an M5 flare.

       <i>
        millionths
       </i>
       of the hemisphere and is described below. (The total hemisphere area
       <i>
        A
        <sub>

        </sub>
       </i>
       = 15.5 × 10
       <sup>
        12
       </sup>
       km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       .)
      </p>

      <p>
       Solar flares strongly influence the local
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weather" title="Space weather">
        space weather
       </a>
       in the vicinity of the Earth. They can produce streams of highly energetic particles in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Stellar_wind" title="Stellar wind">
        stellar wind
       </a>
       , known as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_proton_event" title="Solar proton event">
        solar proton event
       </a>
       . These particles can impact the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       (see main article at
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       ), and present
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       hazards to spacecraft and astronauts. Additionally, massive solar flares are sometimes accompanied by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       (CMEs) which can trigger
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storms" title="Geomagnetic storms">
        geomagnetic storms
       </a>
       that
       <a href="/wiki/March_1989_geomagnetic_storm" title="March 1989 geomagnetic storm">
        have been known
       </a>
       to disable satellites and knock out terrestrial electric power grids for extended periods of time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The soft
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       flux of X class flares increases the ionization of the upper atmosphere, which can interfere with short-wave radio communication and can heat the outer atmosphere and thus increase the drag on low orbiting satellites, leading to orbital decay. Energetic particles in the magnetosphere contribute to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">
        aurora borealis
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_australis" title="Aurora australis">
        aurora australis
       </a>
       . Energy in the form of hard x-rays can be damaging to spacecraft electronics and are generally the result of large plasma ejection in the upper chromosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       The radiation risks posed by solar flares are a major concern in discussions of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Manned_mission_to_Mars" title="Manned mission to Mars">
        manned mission to Mars
       </a>
       , the Moon, or other planets. Energetic protons can pass through the human body, causing
       <a href="/wiki/Ionizing_radiation#Biological_effects" title="Ionizing radiation">
        biochemical damage
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       presenting a hazard to astronauts during interplanetary travel. Some kind of physical or magnetic shielding would be required to protect the astronauts. Most proton storms take at least two hours from the time of visual detection to reach Earth's orbit. A solar flare on January 20, 2005 released the highest concentration of protons ever directly measured,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       giving astronauts as little as 15 minutes to reach shelter.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observations">
        Observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Flares produce radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum, although with different intensity. They are not very intense at
       <i>
        white light
       </i>
       , but they can be very bright at particular atomic lines. They normally produce
       <a href="/wiki/Bremsstrahlung" title="Bremsstrahlung">
        bremsstrahlung
       </a>
       in X-rays and
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation" title="Synchrotron radiation">
        synchrotron radiation
       </a>
       in radio.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        Optical observations
       </b>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">
        Richard Carrington
       </a>
       observed a flare for the first time on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        1 September 1859
       </a>
       projecting the image produced by an optical telescope through a broad-band filter. It was an extraordinarily intense
       <i>
        white light flare
       </i>
       . Since flares produce copious amounts of radiation at
       <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">
        Hα
       </a>
       , adding a narrow ( ≈1 Å) passband filter centered at this wavelength to the optical telescope, allows the observation of not very bright flares with small telescopes. For years Hα was the main, if not the only, source of information about solar flares. Other passband filters are also used.
      </p>
      <p>
       <br/>
       ...
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Radio observations
       </b>
       . During
       <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">
        World War II
       </a>
       , on 25 and 26 February 1942, British radar operators observed radiation that
       <a href="/wiki/James_Stanley_Hey" title="James Stanley Hey">
        Stanley Hey
       </a>
       interpreted as solar emission. Their discovery did not go public until the end of the conflict. The same year
       <a href="/wiki/George_Clark_Southworth" title="George Clark Southworth">
        Southworth
       </a>
       also observed the Sun in radio, but as with Hey, his observations were only known after 1945. In 1943
       <a href="/wiki/Grote_Reber" title="Grote Reber">
        Grote Reber
       </a>
       was the first to report radioastronomical observations of the Sun at 160 MHz. The fast development of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioastronomy" title="Radioastronomy">
        radioastronomy
       </a>
       revealed new peculiarities of the solar activity like
       <i>
        storms
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        bursts
       </i>
       related to the flares. Today ground-based radiotelescopes observe the Sun from ~15 MHz up to 400 GHz.
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Space telescopes
       </b>
       . Since the beginning of
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space exploration
       </a>
       , telescopes have been sent to space, where they work at wavelengths shorter than UV, which are completely absorbed by the atmosphere, and where flares may be very bright. Since the 1970s, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GOES" title="GOES">
        GOES
       </a>
       series of satellites observe the Sun in
       <i>
        soft
       </i>
       X-rays, and their observations became the
       <i>
        standard measure
       </i>
       of flares, diminishing the importance of the
       <a href="/wiki/H-alpha" title="H-alpha">
        Hα
       </a>
       classification.
       <i>
        Hard
       </i>
       X-rays were observed by many different instruments, the most important today being the Reuven Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RHESSI" title="RHESSI">
        RHESSI
       </a>
       ). Nonetheless, UV observations are today the
       <i>
        stars
       </i>
       of solar imaging with their incredible fine details that reveal the complexity of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       . Spacecraft may also bring radio detectors at extremely long wavelengths (as long as a few kilometers) that cannot propagate through the
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h3>

       <span class="mw-editsection">

      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nan%C3%A7ay_Radioheliographe&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nançay Radioheliographe (page does not exist)">
         Nançay Radioheliographe
        </a>
        (NRH) is an interferometer composed of 48 antennas observing at meter-decimeter wavelengths. The radioheliographe is installed at the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nan%C3%A7ay_Radio_Observatory" title="Nançay Radio Observatory">
         Nançay Radio Observatory
        </a>
        , France.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Owens_Valley_Solar_Array" title="Owens Valley Solar Array">
         Owens Valley Solar Array
        </a>
        (OVSA) is a radio interferometer operated by the New Jersey Institute of Technology consisting of 7 antennas, observing from 1 to 18 GHz in both left and right circular polarization. OVSA is located in
        <a href="/wiki/Owens_Valley" title="Owens Valley">
         Owens Valley
        </a>
        , California. It is now being improved, increasing to 15 the total number of antennas and upgrading its control system.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nobeyama_Radioheliograph&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nobeyama Radioheliograph (page does not exist)">
         Nobeyama Radioheliograph
        </a>
        (NoRH) is an interferometer installed at the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobeyama_Radio_Observatory" title="Nobeyama Radio Observatory">
         Nobeyama Radio Observatory
        </a>
        , Japan, formed by 84 small (80 cm) antennas, with receivers at 17 GHz (left and right polarization) and 34 GHz operating simultaneously. It continuously observes the Sun, producing daily snapshots.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Siberian_Solar_Radio_Telescope" title="Siberian Solar Radio Telescope">
         Siberian Solar Radio Telescope
        </a>
        (SSRT) is a special-purpose solar radio telescope designed for studying solar activity in the microwave range (5.7 GHz) where the processes occurring in the solar corona are accessible to observation over the entire solar disk. It is a crossed interferometer, consisting of two arrays of 128x128 parabolic antennas 2.5 meters in diameter each, spaced equidistantly at 4.9 meters and oriented in the E-W and N-S directions. It is located in a wooded valley separating two mountain ridges of the Eastern Sayan Mountains and Khamar-Daban, 220 km from Irkutsk, Russia.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
         <a href="#cite_note-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nobeyama_Radio_Polarimeters&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nobeyama Radio Polarimeters (page does not exist)">
         Nobeyama Radio Polarimeters
        </a>
        are a set of radio telescopes installed at the Nobeyama Radio Observatory that continuously observes the full Sun (no images) at the frequencies of 1, 2, 3.75, 9.4, 17, 35, and 80 GHz, at left and right circular polarization.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
         <a href="#cite_note-21">
          [21]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_Submillimeter_Telescope" title="Solar Submillimeter Telescope">
         Solar Submillimeter Telescope
        </a>
        is a single dish telescope, that observes continuously the Sun at 212 and 405 GHz. It is installed at
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complejo_Astronomico_El_Leoncito" title="Complejo Astronomico El Leoncito">
         Complejo Astronomico El Leoncito
        </a>
        in Argentina. It has a focal array composed by 4 beams at 212 GHz and 2 at 405 GHz, therefore it can instantaneously locate the position of the emitting source
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
         <a href="#cite_note-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        SST is the only solar submillimeter telescope currently in operation.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/POlarization_Emission_of_Millimeter_Activity_at_the_Sun" title="POlarization Emission of Millimeter Activity at the Sun">
         Polarization Emission of Millimeter Activity at the Sun
        </a>
        (POEMAS) is a system of two circular polarization solar radio telescopes, for observations of the Sun at 45 and 90 GHz. The novel characteristic of these instruments is the capability to measure circular right- and left-hand polarizations at these high frequencies. The system is installed at
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complejo_Astronomico_El_Leoncito" title="Complejo Astronomico El Leoncito">
         Complejo Astronomico El Leoncito
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">
         Argentina
        </a>
        . It started operations in November 2011. In November 2013 it went offline for repairs. It is expected to return to observing in January 2015.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Bleien_Radio_Observatory" title="Bleien Radio Observatory">
         Bleien Radio Observatory
        </a>
        is a set of radio telescopes operating near
        <a href="/wiki/Gr%C3%A4nichen" title="Gränichen">
         Gränichen
        </a>
        (Switzerland). They continuously observe the solar flare radio emission from 10 MHz (ionospheric limit) to 5 GHz. The broadband spectrometers are known as Phoenix and CALLISTO.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
         <a href="#cite_note-23">
          [23]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_telescopes">
        Space telescopes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Space telescopes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The following spacecraft missions have flares as their main observation target.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
         Yohkoh
        </a>
        – The Yohkoh (originally Solar A) spacecraft observed the Sun with a variety of instruments from its launch in 1991 until its failure in 2001. The observations spanned a period from one solar maximum to the next. Two instruments of particular use for flare observations were the Soft X-ray Telescope (SXT), a glancing incidence low energy X-ray telescope for photon energies of order 1 keV, and the Hard X-ray Telescope (HXT), a collimation counting instrument which produced images in higher energy X-rays (15-92 keV) by image synthesis.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/WIND_(spacecraft)" title="WIND (spacecraft)">
         WIND
        </a>
        – The Wind spacecraft is devoted to the study of the interplanetary medium. Since the Solar Wind is its main driver, solar flares effects can be traced with the instruments aboard Wind. Some of the WIND experiments are: a very low frequency spectrometer, (WAVES), particles detectors (EPACT, SWE) and a magnetometer (MFI).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_Operational_Environmental_Satellite" title="Geostationary Operational Environmental Satellite">
         GOES
        </a>
        – The GOES spacecraft are satellites in
        <a href="/wiki/Geostationary_orbit" title="Geostationary orbit">
         geostationary orbits
        </a>
        around the Earth that have measured the soft X-ray flux from the Sun since the mid-1970s, following the use of similar instruments on the
        <a href="/wiki/Galactic_Radiation_and_Background" title="Galactic Radiation and Background">
         Solrad
        </a>
        satellites. GOES X-ray observations are commonly used to classify flares, with A, B, C, M, and X representing different powers of ten – an X-class flare has a peak 1-8 Å flux above 0.0001 W/m
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Reuven_Ramaty_High_Energy_Solar_Spectroscopic_Imager" title="Reuven Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectroscopic Imager">
         RHESSI
        </a>
        – The Reuven Ramaty High Energy Solar Spectral Imager is designed to image solar flares in energetic photons from soft X rays (~3 keV) to gamma rays (up to ~20 MeV) and to provide high resolution spectroscopy up to gamma-ray energies of ~20 MeV. Furthermore, it has the capability to perform spatially resolved spectroscopy with high spectral resolution.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
         SOHO
        </a>
        – The Solar and Heliospheric Observatory is collaboration between the
        <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
         ESA
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
         NASA
        </a>
        which is in operation since December 1995. It carries 12 different instruments, among them the
        <a href="/wiki/Extreme_ultraviolet_Imaging_Telescope" title="Extreme ultraviolet Imaging Telescope">
         Extreme ultraviolet Imaging Telescope
        </a>
        (EIT), the
        <a href="/wiki/Large_Angle_and_Spectrometric_Coronagraph" title="Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph">
         Large Angle and Spectrometric Coronagraph
        </a>
        (LASCO) and the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Michelson_Doppler_Imager" title="Michelson Doppler Imager">
         Michelson Doppler Imager
        </a>
        (MDI). SOHO is in a halo orbit around the earth-sun
        <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
         L1
        </a>
        point.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
         TRACE
        </a>
        – The Transition Region and Coronal Explorer is a NASA
        <a href="/wiki/Small_Explorer_program" title="Small Explorer program">
         Small Explorer program
        </a>
        (SMEX) to image the solar corona and transition region at high angular and temporal resolution. It has passband filters at 173 Å, 195 Å, 284 Å, 1600 Å with a spatial resolution of 0.5 arc sec, the best at these wavelengths.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
         SDO
        </a>
        – The Solar Dynamics Observatory is a NASA project composed of 3 different instruments: the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Helioseismic_and_Magnetic_Imager" title="Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager">
         Helioseismic and Magnetic Imager
        </a>
        (HMI), the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_Imaging_Assembly" title="Atmospheric Imaging Assembly">
         Atmospheric Imaging Assembly
        </a>
        (AIA) and the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extreme_Ultraviolet_Variability_Experiment" title="Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment">
         Extreme Ultraviolet Variability Experiment
        </a>
        (EVE). It has been operating since February 2010 in a
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geosynchronous" title="Geosynchronous">
         geosynchronous
        </a>
        earth orbit.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [24]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
         Hinode
        </a>
        –The Hinode spacecraft, originally called Solar B, was launched by the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
         Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency
        </a>
        in September 2006 to observe solar flares in more precise detail. Its instrumentation, supplied by an international collaboration including Norway, the U.K., the U.S., and Africa focuses on the powerful magnetic fields thought to be the source of solar flares. Such studies shed light on the causes of this activity, possibly helping to forecast future flares and thus minimize their dangerous effects on satellites and astronauts.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
         <a href="#cite_note-25">
          [25]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">
         ACE
        </a>
        – The Advanced Composition Explorer was launched in 1997 into a halo orbit around the earth-sun
        <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
         L1
        </a>
        point. It carries spectrometers, magnetometers and charged particle detectors to analyze the solar wind. The Real Time Solar Wind (RTSW) beacon is continually monitored by a network of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NOAA" title="NOAA">
         NOAA
        </a>
        -sponsored ground stations to provide early warning of earth-bound CMEs.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/MAVEN" title="MAVEN">
         MAVEN
        </a>
        – The Mars Atmosphere and Volatile EvolutioN (MAVEN) mission, which launched from Cape Canaveral Air Force Station on November 18, 2013, is the first mission devoted to understanding the Martian upper atmosphere. The goal of MAVEN is to determine the role that loss of atmospheric gas to space played in changing the Martian climate through time. The Extreme Ultraviolet (EUV) monitor on MAVEN is part of the Langmuir Probe and Waves (LPW) instrument and measures solar EUV input and variability, and wave heating of the Martian upper atmosphere.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
         <a href="#cite_note-26">
          [26]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition to these solar observing facilities, many non-solar astronomical satellites observe flares either intentionally (e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/NuSTAR" title="NuSTAR">
        NuSTAR
       </a>
       ), or simply because the penetrating hard radiations coming from a flare can readily penetrate most forms of shielding.
      </p>

      <p>
       The most powerful flare ever observed was the first one to be observed,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasascience2008_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasascience2008-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on September 1, 1859, and was reported by British astronomer Richard Carrington and independently by an observer named Richard Hodgson. The event is named the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        Solar storm of 1859
       </a>
       , or the "Carrington event". The flare was visible to a naked eye (in
       <i>
        white light
       </i>
       ), and produced stunning auroras down to tropical latitudes such as Cuba or Hawaii, and set telegraph systems on fire.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasasci_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasasci-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The flare left a trace in
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       ice in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrate" title="Nitrate">
        nitrates
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Beryllium-10" title="Beryllium-10">
        beryllium-10
       </a>
       , which allow its strength to be measured today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cliver and Svalgaard
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       reconstructed the effects of this flare and compared with other events of the last 150 years. In their words:
       <i>
        While the 1859 event has close rivals or superiors in each of the above categories of space weather activity, it is the only documented event of the last ∼150 years that appears at or near the top of all of the lists
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In modern times, the largest solar flare measured with instruments occurred on November 4, 2003. This event saturated the GOES detectors, and because of this its classification is only approximate. Initially, extrapolating the GOES curve, it was estimated to be X28.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later analysis of the ionospheric effects suggested increasing this estimate to X45.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This event produced the first clear evidence of a new spectral component above 100 GHz.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Other large solar flares also occurred on April 2, 2001 (X20),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Soho1_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Soho1-35">

         [36]
        </a>

        <a href="#cite_note-Soho1-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       February 17, 2011 (X2),

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">

        </a>
       </sup>
       when an X1.1 solar flare fired out of the AR1515 sunspot. Another X1.4 solar flare from AR 1520 region of the Sun,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       second in the week, reached the Earth on July 15, 2012
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Geomagnetic_storm" title="Geomagnetic storm">
        geomagnetic storm
       </a>
       of G1–G2 level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A X1.8-class flare was recorded on October 24, 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There has been major solar flare activity in early 2013, notably within a 48-hour period starting on May 12, 2013, a total of four X-class solar flares were emitted ranging from an X1.2 and upwards of an X3.2,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">

        </a>
       </sup>
       Departing sunspot complex AR2035-AR2046 erupted on April 25, 2014 at 0032 UT, producing a strong X1.3-class solar flare and an HF communications blackout on the dayside of Earth. NASA's Solar Dynamics Observatory recorded
       <a class="external text" href="http://sdowww.lmsal.com/sdomedia/SunInTime/2014/04/25/f_211_193_171.jpg" rel="nofollow">
        a flash
       </a>
       of extreme ultraviolet radiation from the explosion.The Solar Dynamics Observatory recorded an X9.3-class flare at around 1200 UTC on September 6, 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53">
        <a href="#cite_note-53">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Flare_spray">
        Flare spray
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Flare spray">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Flare sprays are a type of eruption associated with solar flares.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moromoto_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moromoto-54">
         [54]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They involve faster ejections of material than eruptive prominences,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55">
        <a href="#cite_note-55">
         [55]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and reach velocities of 20 to 2000 kilometers per second.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56">
        <a href="#cite_note-56">
         [56]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction">
        Prediction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_flare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Prediction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Current methods of flare prediction are problematic, and there is no certain indication that an active region on the Sun will produce a flare. However, many properties of sunspots and active regions correlate with flaring. For example, magnetically complex regions (based on line-of-sight magnetic field) called delta spots produce the largest flares. A simple scheme of sunspot classification due to McIntosh, or related to fractal complexity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57">
        <a href="#cite_note-57">
         [57]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is commonly used as a starting point for flare prediction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58">
        <a href="#cite_note-58">
         [58]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Predictions are usually stated in terms of probabilities for occurrence of flares above M or X GOES class within 24 or 48 hours. The
       <a href="/wiki/National_Oceanic_and_Atmospheric_Administration" title="National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration">
        U.S. National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA)
       </a>
       issues forecasts of this kind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59">
        <a href="#cite_note-59">
         [59]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
