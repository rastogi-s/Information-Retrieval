   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Theia (planet)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Theia
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/θ/: 'th' in 'thigh'">
            θ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is a hypothesized ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary-mass_object" title="Planetary-mass object">
        planetary-mass object
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
        early Solar System
       </a>
       that, according to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        giant impact hypothesis
       </a>
       , collided with another planetary-mass object, Gaia (the
       <a href="/wiki/Early_Earth" title="Early Earth">
        early Earth
       </a>
       ) around 4.5 billion years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-agerefinement_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-agerefinement-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheiaPothesis_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TheiaPothesis-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to the
       <a href="/wiki/Hypothesis" title="Hypothesis">
        hypothesis
       </a>
       , Theia was an
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_trojan" title="Earth trojan">
        Earth trojan
       </a>
       about the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , with diameter of about 6,000 km (3,700 miles). Geologist Edward Young of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">
        University of California, Los Angeles
       </a>
       , drawing on an analysis of rocks collected by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       missions
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        12
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        15
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        17
       </a>
       , proposes that Theia collided head-on with Earth,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nace2016_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nace2016-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in contrast to the previous theory that suggested a glancing impact. Models of the impact indicate that Theia's debris gathered around Earth to form the early
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Some scientists think the material thrown into orbit originally formed two moons
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JutziAsphaug2011_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-JutziAsphaug2011-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Astronomy_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Astronomy-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that later merged to form the single moon we know today. The Theia hypothesis also explains why Earth's core is larger than would be expected for a body its size: according to the hypothesis, Theia's core and mantle mixed with Earth's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theia_(planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Theia is thought to have orbited in the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L4 or L5
       </a>
       configuration presented by the Earth–Sun system, where it would tend to remain. In that case, it would have grown, potentially to a size comparable to Mars.
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        Gravitational perturbations
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       could have eventually put it onto a collision course with the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stereo_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stereo-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template">
        <span style="white-space: nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Link_rot" title="Wikipedia:Link rot">
           <span title=" Dead link since February 2017">
            dead link
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </span>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theia_(planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Theia was named for the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        titaness
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Theia" title="Theia">
        Theia
       </a>
       , who in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       was the mother of
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       , the goddess of the moon;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       that echoes the planet Theia's collision with the early Earth, theorized to have created the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An alternative name, Orpheus, is also used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collision">
        Collision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theia_(planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Collision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Big_Splash_Theia.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="501" data-file-width="600" height="251" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Big_Splash_Theia.gif/300px-Big_Splash_Theia.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Big_Splash_Theia.gif/450px-Big_Splash_Theia.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/74/Big_Splash_Theia.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Big_Splash_Theia.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Animation of collision event between Earth and Theia.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       According to the giant-impact hypothesis, Theia orbited the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , nearly along the orbit of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Proto-Earth" title="Proto-Earth">
        (the proto-Earth)
       </a>
       , by staying close to one or the other of the Sun–Earth system's two more stable
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian points
       </a>
       (i.e. either L4 or L5).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Theia was eventually
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbed
       </a>
       away from that relationship by the gravitational influence of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       , resulting in a collision between Theia and Earth.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Originally, the hypothesis supposed that Theia had struck Earth with a glancing blow
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReuferMeier2012_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ReuferMeier2012-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and ejected many pieces of both the proto-Earth and Theia, those pieces either forming one body that became the Moon, or forming two moons that eventually merged to form the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JutziAsphaug2011_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-JutziAsphaug2011-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such accounts assumed that Theia striking the proto-Earth head-on would have led to the destruction of both planets, creating a short-lived second
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">
        asteroid belt
       </a>
       between the orbits of Venus and Mars.
      </p>
      <p>
       In contrast, evidence published in January 2016 suggests that the impact was indeed a head-on collision, and that Theia's remains can be found both in Earth and the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hypothesis">
        Hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Theia_(planet)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Origin_of_the_Moon" title="Origin of the Moon">
        Origin of the Moon
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       From the beginning of modern astronomy, there have been at least four hypotheses for the origin of the Moon:
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        that a single body split into Earth and the Moon;
       </li>
       <li>
        that the Moon was
        <a href="/wiki/Sphere_of_influence_(astrodynamics)" title="Sphere of influence (astrodynamics)">
         captured
        </a>
        by Earth's gravity (as most of the outer planets' smaller moons were captured);
       </li>
       <li>
        that Earth and the Moon formed at the same time when the
        <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
         protoplanetary disk
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
         accreted
        </a>
        ; and
       </li>
       <li>
        the Theia scenario.
       </li>
      </ol>
      <p>
       The lunar rock samples retrieved by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Apollo_astronauts" title="List of Apollo astronauts">
        astronauts
       </a>
       were found to be very similar in composition to Earth's crust, and so were likely removed from Earth in some violent event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nace2016_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nace2016-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HerwartzPack2014_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HerwartzPack2014-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeierReufer2014_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MeierReufer2014-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
