      <p>
       A
       <b>
        blue moon
       </b>
       is an additional
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       that appears in a subdivision of a year: either the third of four full moons in a season, or a second full moon in a month of the common calendar.
      </p>
      <p>
       The phrase has nothing to do with the actual color of the moon, although a literal "blue moon" (the moon appearing with a tinge of blue) may occur in certain atmospheric conditions: e.g., if volcanic eruptions or fires leave particles in the atmosphere of just the right size to preferentially scatter red light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
        Definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intercalary_month" title="Intercalary month">
        Intercalary month
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term has traditionally referred to an "extra" full moon, where a year which normally has 12 full moons has 13 instead. The "blue moon" reference is applied to the third full moon in a
       <a href="/wiki/Season#Mid-latitude_astronomical" title="Season">
        season
       </a>
       with four full moons,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel2_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel2-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       thus correcting the timing of the last month of a season that would have otherwise been expected too early. This happens every two to three years (seven times in the
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        Metonic cycle
       </a>
       of 19 years).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BadAstronomy2_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BadAstronomy2-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The March 1946 issue of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sky_%26_Telescope" title="Sky &amp; Telescope">
         Sky &amp; Telescope
        </a>
       </i>
       misinterpreted the traditional definition, which led to the modern colloquial misunderstanding that a blue moon is a second
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       in a single
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">
        solar calendar
       </a>
       month with no seasonal link.
      </p>
      <p>
       Owing to the rarity of a blue moon, the term "blue moon" is
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_idioms_of_improbability" title="List of idioms of improbability">
        used colloquially to mean a rare event
       </a>
       , as in the phrase "once in a blue moon".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       (an average lunar cycle) is 29.53
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       . There are about 365.24 days in a
       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
        tropical year
       </a>
       . Therefore, about 12.37 lunations (365.24 days divided by 29.53 days) occur in a tropical year. In the widely used
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       , there are 12
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        months
       </a>
       (the word
       <i>
        month
       </i>
       is derived from
       <i>
        moon
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) in a year, and normally there is one full moon each month. Each calendar year contains roughly 11 days more than the number of days in 12 lunar cycles. The extra days accumulate, so every two or three years (seven times in the 19-year
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        Metonic cycle
       </a>
       ), there is an extra full moon. The extra full moon necessarily falls in one of the four seasons, giving that season four full moons instead of the usual three, and, hence, a blue moon.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
         calculating
        </a>
        the dates for
        <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">
         Lent
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
         Easter
        </a>
        , Catholic
        <a href="/wiki/Clergy" title="Clergy">
         clergy
        </a>
        identified a Lenten moon. Historically, when the moons arrived too early, they called the early moon a "betrayer" (
        <i>
         belewe
        </i>
        )
        <i>
         moon
        </i>
        , so the Lenten moon came at its expected time.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
         Folklore
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rose_Moon" title="Rose Moon">
         named each of the 12 full moons in a year
        </a>
        according to its time of year. The occasional 13th full moon that came too early for its season was called a "blue moon", so the rest of the moons that year retained their customary seasonal names.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <i>
         Maine Farmers' Almanac
        </i>
        called the third full moon in a season that had four the "blue moon".
       </li>
       <li>
        The frequency of a blue moon can be calculated as follows. It is the period of time it would take for an extra
        <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period#Varieties_of_orbital_periods" title="Orbital period">
         synodic orbit
        </a>
        of the moon to occur in a year. Given that a year is approximately 365.2425 days and a synodic orbit is 29.5309 days,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        then there are about 12.368 synodic months in a year. For this to add up to another full month would take 1/0.368 years. Thus it would take about 2.716 years, or 2 years, 8 months and 18 days for another blue moon to occur.
       </li>
       <li>
        Using the common Sky &amp; Telescope misunderstanding, when one calendar month has two full moons; the second one is called a "blue moon". On rare occasions in a calendar year (as happened in 2010 in time zones east of UTC+07), both January and March each have two full moons, so that the second one in each month is called a "blue moon"; in this case, the month of February, with only 28 or 29 days, has no full moon. Under this misinterpretation a blue moon can be more frequent.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_of_the_term">
        Origin of the term
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin of the term">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="353" data-file-width="362" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg/220px-Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg/330px-Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/51/Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Blue_moon_of_31.08.2012.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Blue moon of August 31, 2012, viewed from
         <a href="/wiki/Slobozia" title="Slobozia">
          Slobozia
         </a>
         , Romania.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The suggestion has been made that the term "blue moon" for "intercalary month" arose by
       <a href="/wiki/Folk_etymology" title="Folk etymology">
        folk etymology
       </a>
       , the "blue" replacing the no-longer-understood
       <i>
        belewe
       </i>
       , 'to betray'. The original meaning would then have been "betrayer moon", referring to a full moon that would "normally" (in years without an intercalary month) be the full moon of spring, while in an intercalary year, it was "traitorous" in the sense that people would have had to continue fasting for another month in accordance with the season of
       <a href="/wiki/Lent" title="Lent">
        Lent
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest recorded English usage of the term
       <i>
        blue moon
       </i>
       is found in an anti-clerical pamphlet (attacking the Roman clergy, and cardinal
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Wolsey" title="Thomas Wolsey">
        Thomas Wolsey
       </a>
       in particular) by two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Protestant_Reformation" title="Protestant Reformation">
        converted
       </a>
       Greenwich friars, William Roy and Jerome Barlow, published in 1528 under the title
       <i>
        Rede me and be nott wrothe, for I say no thynge but trothe
       </i>
       . The relevant passage reads:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        O churche men are wyly foxes [...] Yf they say the mone is blewe / We must beleve that it is true / Admittynge their interpretacion.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It is not clear from the context that this refers to
       <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">
        intercalation
       </a>
       ; the context of the passage is a dialogue between two priest's servants, spoken by the character "Jeffrey" (
       <i>
        a brefe dialoge betwene two preste's servauntis, named Watkyn and Ieffraye
       </i>
       ). The intention may simply be that Jeffrey makes an absurd statement, "the moon is blue", to make the point that priests require laymen to believe in statements even if they are patently false. But in the above interpretation of "betrayer moon", Jeffrey may also be saying that it is up to the priests to say when Lent will be delayed, by announcing "blue moons" which laymen have no means to verify.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Maine_Farmers.27_Almanac_blue_moons">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Maine_Farmers'_Almanac_blue_moons">
        <i>
         Maine Farmers' Almanac
        </i>
        blue moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Maine Farmers' Almanac blue moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the 19th and early 20th centuries, the
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maine_Farmers%27_Almanac" title="Maine Farmers' Almanac">
         Maine Farmers' Almanac
        </a>
       </i>
       listed blue moon dates for farmers. These correspond to the third full moon in a quarter of the year when there were four full moons (normally a quarter year has three full moons).
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Farmers%27_Almanac_full_moon_names" title="Farmers' Almanac full moon names">
        Full moon names
       </a>
       were given to each lunation in a season. The seasons used were those of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean_tropical_year" title="Mean tropical year">
        mean tropical year
       </a>
       , equal in length, as opposed to the astronomical seasons which vary in length because the earth's speed in its orbit round the sun is not uniform.
      </p>
      <p>
       To compare, in 1983 the equal seasons began at 1.48 AM on 23 March, 9.15 AM on 22 June, 4.42 PM on 21 September and 12.10 AM on 22 December, while the astronomical seasons began at 4.39 AM on 21 March, 11.09 PM on 21 June, 2.42 PM on 23 September and 10.30 AM on 22 December (all times GMT). When a season has four full moons the third is called the "blue moon" so that the last can continue to be called with the proper name for that season.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel2_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel2-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sky_and_Telescope_calendar_misinterpretation">
        <i>
         Sky and Telescope
        </i>
        calendar misinterpretation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sky and Telescope calendar misinterpretation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The March 1946
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sky_and_Telescope" title="Sky and Telescope">
         Sky and Telescope
        </a>
       </i>
       article "Once in a Blue Moon" by James Hugh Pruett misinterpreted the 1937
       <i>
        Maine Farmers' Almanac
       </i>
       . "Seven times in 19 years there were – and still are – 13 full moons in a year. This gives 11 months with one full moon each and one with two. This second in a month, so I interpret it, was called Blue Moon." Widespread adoption of the definition of a "blue moon" as the second full moon in a month followed its use on the popular radio program
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/StarDate_(radio)" title="StarDate (radio)">
         StarDate
        </a>
       </i>
       on January 31, 1980
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel2_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel2-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in a question in the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Trivial_Pursuit" title="Trivial Pursuit">
         Trivial Pursuit
        </a>
       </i>
       game in 1986.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SkyTel1_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SkyTel1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visibly_blue_moon">
        Visibly blue moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Visibly blue moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most literal meaning of
       <i>
        blue moon
       </i>
       is when the moon (not necessarily a full moon) appears to a casual observer to be unusually bluish, which is a rare event. The effect can be caused by smoke or dust particles in the atmosphere, as has happened after forest fires in Sweden and Canada in 1950 and 1951,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and after the
       <a href="/wiki/1883_eruption_of_Krakatoa" title="1883 eruption of Krakatoa">
        eruption of Krakatoa in 1883
       </a>
       , which caused the moon to appear blue for nearly two years. Other less potent volcanoes have also turned the moon blue. People saw blue moons in 1983 after the eruption of the
       <a href="/wiki/El_Chich%C3%B3n" title="El Chichón">
        El Chichón
       </a>
       volcano in Mexico, and there are reports of blue moons caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_St._Helens" title="Mount St. Helens">
        Mount St. Helens
       </a>
       in 1980 and
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Pinatubo" title="Mount Pinatubo">
        Mount Pinatubo
       </a>
       in 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science.nasa.gov_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-science.nasa.gov-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the Antarctic diary of
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Falcon_Scott" title="Robert Falcon Scott">
        Robert Falcon Scott
       </a>
       for July 11, 1911 his entry says, "... the air thick with snow, and the moon a vague blue".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-coolantarctica.com_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-coolantarctica.com-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On that date the moon phase would have looked full.
      </p>
      <p>
       On September 23, 1950, several
       <a href="/wiki/Muskeg" title="Muskeg">
        muskeg
       </a>
       fires that had been smoldering for several years in Alberta, Canada, suddenly blew up into major—and very smoky—fires. Winds carried the smoke eastward and southward with unusual speed, and the conditions of the fire produced large quantities of oily droplets of just the right size (about 1
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">
        micrometre
       </a>
       in diameter) to scatter red and yellow light. Wherever the smoke cleared enough so that the sun was visible, it was lavender or blue. Ontario, Canada, and much of the east coast of the United States were affected by the following day, and two days later, observers in Britain reported an indigo sun in smoke-dimmed skies, followed by an equally blue moon that evening.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-science.nasa.gov_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-science.nasa.gov-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The key to a blue moon is having lots of particles slightly wider than the wavelength of red light (0.7 micrometer)—and no other sizes present. It is rare, but volcanoes sometimes produce such clouds, as do forest fires. Ash and dust clouds thrown into the atmosphere by fires and storms usually contain a mixture of particles with a wide range of sizes, with most smaller than 1 micrometer, and they tend to scatter blue light. This kind of cloud makes the moon turn red; thus red moons are far more common than blue moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
