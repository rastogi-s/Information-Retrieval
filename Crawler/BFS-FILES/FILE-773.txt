   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Late Heavy Bombardment
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </b>
       (abbreviated
       <b>
        LHB
       </b>
       and also known as the
       <b>
        lunar cataclysm
       </b>
       ) is an event thought to have occurred approximately 4.1 to 3.8 billion years (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ga_(unit)" title="Ga (unit)">
        Ga
       </a>
       ) ago,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-taylor1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at a time corresponding to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Neohadean" title="Neohadean">
        Neohadean
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Eoarchean" title="Eoarchean">
        Eoarchean
       </a>
       eras on Earth. During this interval, a disproportionately large number of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       are theorized to have collided with the early
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial planets
       </a>
       in the inner Solar System, including
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Late Heavy Bombardment happened after the Earth and other rocky planets had formed and
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        accreted
       </a>
       most of their mass, but still quite early in Earth's history.
      </p>
      <p>
       Evidence for the LHB derives from lunar samples brought back by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       astronauts.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isotopic_dating" title="Isotopic dating">
        Isotopic dating
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       rocks implies that most impact melts occurred in a rather narrow interval of time. Several hypotheses attempt to explain the apparent spike in the flux of impactors (i.e.
       <a href="/wiki/Astroid" title="Astroid">
        asteroids
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comets
       </a>
       ) in the inner
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , but no consensus yet exists. The
       <a href="/wiki/Nice_model" title="Nice model">
        Nice model
       </a>
       , popular among
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_science" title="Planetary science">
        planetary scientists
       </a>
       , postulates that the
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">
        giant planets
       </a>
       underwent
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_migration" title="Planetary migration">
        orbital migration
       </a>
       and in doing so, scattered objects in the
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid_belt" title="Asteroid belt">
        asteroid
       </a>
       and/or
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belts
       </a>
       into eccentric orbits, and into the path of the terrestrial planets. Other researchers argue that the lunar sample data do not require a cataclysmic cratering event near 3.9 Ga, and that the apparent clustering of impact-melt ages near this time is an artifact of sampling materials retrieved from a single large impact basin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor1_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-taylor1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They also note that the rate of impact cratering could differ significantly between the outer and inner zones of the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Evidence_for_a_cataclysm">
        Evidence for a cataclysm
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Evidence for a cataclysm">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main piece of evidence for a lunar cataclysm comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">
        radiometric ages
       </a>
       of impact melt rocks that were collected during the Apollo missions. The majority of these impact melts are believed to have formed during the collision of asteroids or comets tens of kilometers across, forming impact craters hundreds of kilometers in diameter. The
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        16
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        17
       </a>
       landing sites were chosen as a result of their proximity to the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Imbrium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Nectaris" title="Mare Nectaris">
        Nectaris
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">
        Serenitatis
       </a>
       basins respectively.
      </p>
      <p>
       Under study on Earth, the ages of impact melts collected at these sites clustered between about 3.8 and 4.1 Ga. The apparent clustering of ages of these led to postulation that the ages record an intense bombardment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They called it the "lunar cataclysm" and proposed that it represented a dramatic increase in the rate of bombardment of the Moon around 3.9 Ga. If these impact melts were derived from these three basins, then not only did these three prominent impact basins form within a short interval of time, but so did many others based on
       <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">
        stratigraphic
       </a>
       grounds. At the time, the conclusion was considered controversial.
      </p>
      <p>
       As more data has become available, particularly from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_meteorites" title="Lunar meteorites">
        lunar meteorites
       </a>
       , this theory, while still controversial, has gained in popularity. The lunar meteorites are believed to randomly sample the lunar surface, and at least some of these should have originated from regions far from the Apollo landing sites. Many of the
       <a href="/wiki/Feldspar" title="Feldspar">
        feldspathic
       </a>
       lunar meteorites probably originated from the lunar far side, and impact melts within these have recently been dated. Consistent with the cataclysm hypothesis, none of their ages was found to be older than about 3.9 Ga.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, the ages do not "cluster" at this date, but span between 2.5 and 3.9 Ga.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Dating of
       <a href="/wiki/Howardite" title="Howardite">
        howardite
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Eucrite" title="Eucrite">
        eucrite
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Diogenite" title="Diogenite">
        diogenite
       </a>
       (HED) meteorites and
       <a href="/wiki/H_chondrite" title="H chondrite">
        H chondrite
       </a>
       meteorites originating from the asteroid belt reveal numerous ages from 3.4–4.1 Ga and an earlier peak at 4.5 Ga. The 3.4–4.1 Ga ages has been interpreted as representing an increase in impact velocities as computer simulations using hydrocode reveal that the volume of impact melt increases 100–1000 times as the impact velocity increases from the current asteroid belt average of 5 km/s to 10 km/s. Impact velocities above 10 km/s require very high inclinations or the large eccentricities of asteroids on planet crossing orbits. Such objects are rare in the current asteroid belt but the population would be significantly increased by the sweeping of resonances due to giant planet migration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-March_etal_2013_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-March_etal_2013-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Studies of the highland crater size distributions suggest that the same family of projectiles struck Mercury and the Moon during the
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Strom_1979_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Strom_1979-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If the history of decay of late heavy bombardment on Mercury also followed the history of late heavy bombardment on the Moon, the youngest large basin discovered,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Caloris_basin" title="Caloris basin">
        Caloris
       </a>
       , is comparable in age to the youngest large lunar basins, Orientale and Imbrium, and all of the plains units are older than 3 billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASAsp467_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASAsp467-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Criticisms_of_the_cataclysm_hypothesis">
        Criticisms of the cataclysm hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Criticisms of the cataclysm hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       While the cataclysm hypothesis has recently gained in popularity, particularly among dynamicists who have identified possible causes for such a phenomenon, the cataclysm hypothesis is still controversial and based on debatable assumptions. Two criticisms are that (1) the "cluster" of impact ages could be an artifact of sampling a single basin's ejecta, and (2) that the lack of impact melt rocks older than about 4.1 Ga is related to all such samples having been pulverized, or their ages being reset.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first criticism concerns the origin of the impact melt rocks that were sampled at the Apollo landing sites. While these impact melts have been commonly attributed to having been derived from the
       <i>
        closest basin
       </i>
       , it has been argued that a large portion of these might instead be derived from the Imbrium basin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Imbrium impact basin is the youngest and largest of the multi-ring basins found on the central nearside of the Moon, and quantitative modeling shows that significant amounts of ejecta from this event should be present at all of the Apollo landing sites. According to this alternative hypothesis, the cluster of impact melt ages near 3.9 Ga simply reflects material being collected from a single impact event, Imbrium, and not several. Additional criticism also argues that the age spike at 3.9 Ga identified in 40Ar/39Ar dating could also be produced by an episodic early crust formation followed by partial 40Ar losses as the impact rate declined.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A second criticism concerns the significance of the lack of impact melt rocks older than about 4.1 Ga. One hypothesis for this observation that does not involve a cataclysm is that old melt rocks did exist, but that their ages have all been reset by the continuous effects of impact cratering over the past 4 billion years. Furthermore, it is possible that these putative samples could all have been pulverized to such small sizes that it is impossible to obtain age determinations using standard radiometric methods.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hartmann_2003_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hartmann_2003-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Latest reinterpretation of crater statistics suggests that the flux on the Moon and on Mars may have been lower in general. Thus, the recorded crater population can be explained without any peak in the earliest bombardment of the inner Solar System.
      </p>

      <p>
       If a
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/cataclysmic" title="wikt:cataclysmic">
        cataclysmic
       </a>
       cratering event truly occurred on the Moon, the Earth would have been affected as well. Extrapolating lunar cratering rates
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to Earth at this time suggests that the following number of craters would have formed:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        22,000 or more
        <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
         impact craters
        </a>
        with diameters &gt;20 km (12 mi),
       </li>
       <li>
        about 40
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impact_basin" title="Impact basin">
         impact basins
        </a>
        with diameters about 1,000 km (620 mi),
       </li>
       <li>
        several impact basins with diameters about 5,000 km (3,100 mi),
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Before the formulation of the LHB theory, geologists generally assumed that the Earth remained molten until about 3.8 Ga. This date could be found in many of the
       <a href="/wiki/Oldest_dated_rocks" title="Oldest dated rocks">
        oldest known rocks
       </a>
       from around the world, and appeared to represent a strong "cutoff point" beyond which older rocks could not be found. These dates remained fairly constant even across various dating methods, including the system considered the most accurate and least affected by environment,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranium-lead_dating" title="Uranium-lead dating">
        uranium-lead dating
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Zircon" title="Zircon">
        zircons
       </a>
       . As no older rocks could be found, it was generally assumed that the Earth had remained molten until this date, which defined the boundary between the earlier
       <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">
        Hadean
       </a>
       and later
       <a href="/wiki/Archean" title="Archean">
        Archean
       </a>
       eons. Nonetheless, more recently, in 1999, the oldest known rock on Earth was dated to be 4.031 ± 0.003 billion years old, and is part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Acasta_Gneiss" title="Acasta Gneiss">
        Acasta Gneiss
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slave_craton" title="Slave craton">
        Slave craton
       </a>
       in northwestern Canada.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bowring_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bowring-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Older rocks could be found, however, in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       fragments that fall to Earth as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorites" title="Meteorites">
        meteorites
       </a>
       . Like the rocks on Earth, asteroids also show a strong cutoff point, at about 4.6 Ga, which is assumed to be the time when the first solids formed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       around the then-young Sun. The Hadean, then, was the period of time between the formation of these early rocks in space, and the eventual solidification of the Earth's crust, some 700 million years later. This time would include the accretion of the planets from the disk and the slow cooling of the Earth into a solid body as the gravitational potential energy of accretion was released.
      </p>
      <p>
       Later calculations showed that the rate of collapse and cooling depends on the size of the rocky body. Scaling this rate to an object of Earth mass suggested very rapid cooling, requiring only 100 million years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The difference between measurement and theory presented a conundrum at the time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The LHB offers a potential explanation for this anomaly. Under this model, the rocks dating to 3.8 Ga solidified only after much of the crust was destroyed by the LHB. Collectively, the
       <a href="/wiki/Acasta_Gneiss" title="Acasta Gneiss">
        Acasta Gneiss
       </a>
       in the North American cratonic shield and the gneisses within the
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Hills" title="Jack Hills">
        Jack Hills
       </a>
       portion of the Narryer Gneiss Terrane in Western Australia are the oldest continental fragments on Earth, yet they appear to post-date the LHB. The oldest mineral yet dated on Earth, a zircon from Jack Hills, predates this event, but it may simply be a fragment of crust left over from the LHB, contained within a much younger (~3.8 Ga old) rock.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2008)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Jack Hills zircon led to something of a revolution in our understanding of the Hadean eon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Older references generally show that Hadean Earth had a molten surface with prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
        volcanos
       </a>
       . The name "Hadean" itself refers to the "hellish" conditions assumed on Earth for the time, from the Greek
       <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">
        Hades
       </a>
       . Zircon dating suggested, albeit controversially, that the Hadean surface was solid, temperate, and covered by acidic oceans. This picture derives from the presence of particular isotopic ratios that suggest the action of water-based chemistry at some time before the formation of the oldest rocks (see
       <a href="/wiki/Cool_early_Earth" title="Cool early Earth">
        Cool early Earth
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Of particular interest, Manfred Schidlowski argued in 1979 that the carbon isotopic ratios of some sedimentary rocks found in
       <a href="/wiki/Greenland" title="Greenland">
        Greenland
       </a>
       were a relic of organic matter. There was much debate over the precise dating of the rocks, with Schidlowski suggesting they were about 3.8 Ga old, and others suggesting a more "modest" 3.6 Ga. In either case it was a very short time for
       <a href="/wiki/Abiogenesis" title="Abiogenesis">
        abiogenesis
       </a>
       to have taken place, and if Schidlowski was correct, arguably too short a time. The
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       and the "re-melting" of the crust that it suggests provides a timeline under which this would be possible;
       <a href="/wiki/Life" title="Life">
        life
       </a>
       either formed immediately after the
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       , or more likely survived it, having arisen earlier during the
       <a href="/wiki/Hadean" title="Hadean">
        Hadean
       </a>
       . Recent studies suggest that the rocks Schidlowski found are indeed from the older end of the possible age range at about 3.85 Ga, suggesting the latter possibility is the most likely answer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AB-20021014_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AB-20021014-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       More recent studies have found no evidence for the isotopically light carbon ratios that were the basis for the original claims.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       More recently, a similar study of Jack Hills rocks shows traces of the same sort of potential organic indicators. Thorsten Geisler of the Institute for Mineralogy at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_M%C3%BCnster" title="University of Münster">
        University of Münster
       </a>
       studied traces of carbon trapped in small pieces of diamond and graphite within zircons dating to 4.25 Ga. The ratio of carbon-12 to carbon-13 was unusually high, normally a sign of "processing" by life.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NS-20080702_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NS-20080702-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Three-dimensional computer models developed in May 2009 by a team at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Colorado_at_Boulder" title="University of Colorado at Boulder">
        University of Colorado at Boulder
       </a>
       postulate that much of Earth's crust, and the microbes living in it, could have survived the bombardment. Their models suggest that although the surface of the Earth would have been sterilized,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydrothermal_vents" title="Hydrothermal vents">
        hydrothermal vents
       </a>
       below the Earth's surface could have incubated life by providing a sanctuary for
       <a href="/wiki/Thermophile" title="Thermophile">
        heat-loving microbes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RN-20090520_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RN-20090520-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In April 2014, scientists reported finding evidence of the largest terrestrial meteor
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_event" title="Impact event">
        impact event
       </a>
       to date near the
       <a href="/wiki/Barberton_Greenstone_Belt#Barberton_greenstone_belt" title="Barberton Greenstone Belt">
        Barberton Greenstone Belt
       </a>
       . They estimated the impact occurred about 3.26 billion years ago and that the impactor was approximately 37 to 58 kilometers (23 to 36 miles) wide. The crater from this event, if it still exists, has not yet been found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_causes">
        Possible causes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Possible causes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Giant-planet_migration">
        Giant-planet migration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Giant-planet migration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/Nice_model" title="Nice model">
        Nice model
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:502px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lhborbits.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="454" data-file-width="1360" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Lhborbits.png/500px-Lhborbits.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Lhborbits.png/750px-Lhborbits.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Lhborbits.png/1000px-Lhborbits.png 2x" width="500"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lhborbits.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Simulation showing outer planets and planetesimal belt: a) Early configuration, before Jupiter (green) and Saturn (orange) reach 2:1 resonance b) Scattering of planetesimals into the inner Solar System after the orbital shift of Neptune (dark blue) and Uranus (light blue) c) After ejection of planetesimals by planets.
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gomes05_26-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Gomes05-26">
           [26]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A series of simulations by Gomes
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       start with a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       where the giant planets are in a tight orbital configuration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This configuration is in itself stable, but assuming a rich
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        trans-Neptunian belt
       </a>
       , stray transneptunians interacted with these planets, causing them to migrate slowly during a time of several hundred million years. Jupiter is predicted to migrate inward, whereas the other planets go outwards. By this migration, the Solar System became catastrophically unstable when Jupiter and Saturn reached a 2:1
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       , causing the outer Solar System to reconfigure rapidly to a wide jovian system. As these planets migrated, resonances would be "swept" through the asteroid belt and Kuiper belt. These resonances would increase the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       of the objects, allowing them to enter the inner Solar System and impact with the terrestrial planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-taylor1_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-taylor1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Nice model has undergone some evolution since its initial publication. In recent works the giant planets begin in a multi-resonant configurations consistent with numerical models of the early Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morbidelli_etal_2007_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morbidelli_etal_2007-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the resonant configuration is destabilized by interactions with the trans-Neptunian belt
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Levison_2011_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Levison_2011-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a series of gravitational encounters occur. Among these are an encounter between one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_giant" title="Ice giant">
        ice giants
       </a>
       and Saturn propelling the ice giant inward onto a Jupiter-crossing orbit followed by an encounter with Jupiter which drives the ice giant outward. Referred to as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jumping-Jupiter_Scenario" title="Jumping-Jupiter Scenario">
        jumping-Jupiter scenario
       </a>
       , these encounters quickly increase the separation between Jupiter and Saturn which has been found to be necessary to avoid excessively altering the eccentricity of the inner planets
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brasser_2009_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brasser_2009-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the inclination distribution of the asteroids.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morbidelli_etal_2010_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Morbidelli_etal_2010-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This scenario also significantly reduces the fraction of asteroids removed from the main asteroid belt leaving a now nearly depleted
       <a href="/wiki/E-belt_asteroids" title="E-belt asteroids">
        inner band
       </a>
       of asteroids as the primary source of the impactors of the LHB.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bottke-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In numerical simulations the ice giant is often ejected following its encounter with Jupiter leading some to propose that the Solar System began with
       <a href="/wiki/Five-planet_Nice_model" title="Five-planet Nice model">
        five giant planets
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny_2011_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny_2011-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recent works, however, have found that impacts from this inner asteroid belt would be insufficient to explain the formation of ancient impact spherule beds and the lunar basins,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnson_etal_2016_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Johnson_etal_2016-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that the asteroid belt was probably not the source of the Late Heavy Bombardment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny_2016_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny_2016-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Late_Uranus.2FNeptune_formation">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Late_Uranus/Neptune_formation">
        Late Uranus/Neptune formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Late Uranus/Neptune formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       According to one
       <a href="/wiki/Planetesimal" title="Planetesimal">
        planetesimal
       </a>
       simulation of the establishment of the planetary system, the outermost planets Uranus and Neptune formed very slowly, over a period of several billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Harold Levison and his team have also suggested that the relatively low density of material in the outer Solar System during planet formation would have greatly slowed their accretion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This "late appearance" of these planets has therefore been suggested as a different reason for the LHB. However, recent calculations of gas-flows combined with planetesimal runaway growth in the outer Solar System imply that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jovian_planets" title="Jovian planets">
        Jovian planets
       </a>
       formed extremely rapidly, on the order of 10 My, which does not support this explanation for the LHB.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Planet_V_hypothesis">
        Planet V hypothesis
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Planet V hypothesis">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Planet_V" title="Planet V">
        Planet V
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Planet V hypothesis posits that a fifth
       <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_planet" title="Terrestrial planet">
        terrestrial planet
       </a>
       created the
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       when its meta-stable orbit entered the inner asteroid belt. The hypothetical fifth terrestrial planet, Planet V, had a mass less than half of Mars and originally orbited between Mars and the asteroid belt. Planet V's orbit became unstable due to perturbations from the other inner planets causing it to intersect the inner asteroid belt. After close encounters with Planet V, many asteroids entered Earth-crossing orbits producing the
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       . Planet V was ultimately lost, likely plunging into the Sun. In numerical simulations, an uneven distribution of asteroids, with the asteroids heavily concentrated toward the inner asteroid belt, has been shown to be necessary to produce the LHB via this mechanism.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brasser_2011_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brasser_2011-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An alternate version of this hypothesis in which the lunar impactors are debris resulting from Planet V impacting Mars, forming the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Borealis_Basin" title="Borealis Basin">
        Borealis Basin
       </a>
       , has been proposed to explain a low number of giant lunar basins relative to craters and a lack of evidence of cometary impactors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minton_etal_2015_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Minton_etal_2015-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Minton_Richardson_Fassett_2015_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Minton_Richardson_Fassett_2015-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Disruption_of_Mars-crossing_asteroid">
        Disruption of Mars-crossing asteroid
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Disruption of Mars-crossing asteroid">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A hypothesis proposed by Matija Ćuk posits that the last few basin-forming impacts were the result of the collisional disruption of a large Mars-crossing asteroid. This
       <a href="/wiki/4_Vesta" title="4 Vesta">
        Vesta
       </a>
       -sized asteroid was a remnant of a population which initially was much larger than the current main asteroid belt. Most of the pre-Imbrium impacts would have been due to these Mars-crossing objects, with the early bombardment extending until 4.1 billion years ago. A lull in basin-forming impacts then followed during which the lunar magnetic field decayed. Then roughly 3.9 billion years ago a catastrophic impact disrupted the Vesta-sized asteroid radically increasing the population of Mars-crossing objects. Many of these objects then evolved onto Earth-crossing orbits producing a spike in the lunar impact rate during which the last few lunar impact basins are formed. Ćuk points to the weak or absent residual magnetism of the last few basins and a change in the size-frequency distribution of craters which formed during this late bombardment as evidence supporting this hypothesis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_1012_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_1012-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The timing
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_etal_2010_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_etal_2010-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhatra_2011_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Malhatra_2011-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_etal_2011_44-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_etal_2011-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fasset_2012_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fasset_2012-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the cause
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchi_2012_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchi_2012-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of the change in the size-frequency distribution of craters is controversial.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_potential_sources">
        Other potential sources
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Late_Heavy_Bombardment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Other potential sources">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A number of other possible sources of the
       <i>
        Late Heavy Bombardment
       </i>
       have been investigated. Among these are additional Earth satellites orbiting independently or as lunar trojans, planetesimals left over from the formations of the terrestrial planets, Earth or Venus co-orbitals, and the breakup of a large main belt asteroid. Additional Earth satellites on independent orbits were shown to be quickly captured into resonances during the Moon's early tidally-driven orbital expansion and were lost or destroyed within in a few million years
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_2008_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_2008-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar trojans were found to be destabilized within 100 million years by a solar resonance when the Moon reached 27 Earth radii.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_Gladman_2009_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_Gladman_2009-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Planetesimals left over from the formation of the terrestrial planets were shown to be depleted too rapidly due to collisions and ejections to form the last lunar basins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bottke_etal_2007_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bottke_etal_2007-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The long term stability of primordial Earth or Venus co-orbitals (trojans or objects with horseshoe orbits) in conjunction with the lack of current observations indicate that they were unlikely to have been common enough to contribute to the LHB.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cuk_etal_2012_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cuk_etal_2012-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Producing the LHB from the collisional disruption of a main belt asteroid was found to require at minimum a 1000 – 1500 km parent body with the most favorable initial conditions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ito_Malhotra_2006_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ito_Malhotra_2006-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Debris produced by collisions among inner planets, now lost, has also been proposed as a source of the LHB.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volk_Gladman_2015_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Volk_Gladman_2015-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
