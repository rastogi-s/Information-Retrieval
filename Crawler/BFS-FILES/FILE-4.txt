      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurred on 11 August 1999 with an
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_of_eclipse" title="Magnitude of eclipse">
        eclipse magnitude
       </a>
       of 1.029. A
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       occurs when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       passes between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , thereby totally or partly obscuring the image of the Sun for a viewer on Earth. A total solar eclipse occurs when the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_diameter" title="Apparent diameter">
        apparent diameter
       </a>
       is larger than the Sun's, blocking all direct sunlight, turning day into darkness. Totality occurs in a narrow path across Earth's surface, with the partial solar eclipse visible over a surrounding region thousands of kilometres wide. The path of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       's shadow began in the
       <a href="/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean" title="Atlantic Ocean">
        Atlantic Ocean
       </a>
       and, before noon, was traversing the southern
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Belgium" title="Belgium">
        Belgium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Luxembourg" title="Luxembourg">
        Luxembourg
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Germany" title="Germany">
        Germany
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Austria" title="Austria">
        Austria
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Slovenia" title="Slovenia">
        Slovenia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Croatia" title="Croatia">
        Croatia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hungary" title="Hungary">
        Hungary
       </a>
       , and northern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FR_Yugoslavia" title="FR Yugoslavia">
        FR Yugoslavia
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Vojvodina" title="Vojvodina">
        Vojvodina
       </a>
       ). The eclipse's maximum was at 11:03 UTC at
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999&amp;params=45.1_N_24.3_E_">
         <span class="geo-nondefault">
          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           <span class="latitude">
            45°06′N
           </span>
           <span class="longitude">
            24°18′E
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          ﻿ / ﻿
         </span>
         <span class="geo-default">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           45.1°N 24.3°E
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
           ﻿ /
           <span class="geo">
            45.1; 24.3
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Romania" title="Romania">
        Romania
       </a>
       (next to a town called
       <a href="/wiki/Ocnele_Mari" title="Ocnele Mari">
        Ocnele Mari
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/R%C3%A2mnicu_V%C3%A2lcea" title="Râmnicu Vâlcea">
        Râmnicu Vâlcea
       </a>
       ); and it continued across
       <a href="/wiki/Bulgaria" title="Bulgaria">
        Bulgaria
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Sea" title="Black Sea">
        Black Sea
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
        Turkey
       </a>
       , northeastern tip of
       <a href="/wiki/Syria" title="Syria">
        Syria
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/Iraq" title="Iraq">
        Iraq
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
        Iran
       </a>
       , southern
       <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
        Pakistan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Srikakulam" title="Srikakulam">
        Srikakulam
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       and ended in the
       <a href="/wiki/Bay_of_Bengal" title="Bay of Bengal">
        Bay of Bengal
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It was the first total eclipse visible from Europe since
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_July_22,_1990" title="Solar eclipse of July 22, 1990">
        22 July 1990
       </a>
       , and the first visible in the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       since
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_June_29,_1927" title="Solar eclipse of June 29, 1927">
        29 June 1927
       </a>
       .
      </p>

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="179" data-file-width="1276" height="31" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg/220px-Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg/330px-Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg/440px-Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Film_eclipse_soleil_1999.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The eclipse as seen from France
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because of the high population densities in areas of the path, this was one of the most-viewed total solar eclipses in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_history" title="Human history">
        human history
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although some areas in the path of totality (mainly in Western Europe) offered impaired visibility due to adverse weather conditions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some of the organized eclipse-watching parties along the path of totality set up video projectors on which people could watch the Moon's shadow as it raced towards them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There was substantial coverage on International TV stations of the progress of the eclipse shadow. The Moon's shadow was also observed from the Russian
       <a href="/wiki/Mir" title="Mir">
        Mir
       </a>
       space station; during the eclipse, video from Mir was broadcast live on television.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
         BBC
        </a>
        concentrated its coverage efforts on the first landfall of the shadow across the western end of
        <a href="/wiki/Cornwall" title="Cornwall">
         Cornwall
        </a>
        (from
        <a href="/wiki/St_Ives,_Cornwall" title="St Ives, Cornwall">
         St Ives
        </a>
        to
        <a href="/wiki/Lizard_(village)" title="Lizard (village)">
         Lizard
        </a>
        ), which was packed with an extraordinary number of visitors, although Cornwall did not have nearly as many as expected leading to many specially organised events being left with very small attendance. The veteran amateur astronomer, broadcaster and eclipse-watcher
        <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore" title="Patrick Moore">
         Patrick Moore
        </a>
        was brought in to head a live programme, but the eclipse was clouded out.
        <a href="/wiki/BBC_One" title="BBC One">
         BBC One
        </a>
        also produced a special version of their
        <a href="/wiki/BBC_One_%27Balloon%27_idents" title="BBC One 'Balloon' idents">
         Balloon Idents
        </a>
        for the event. The BBC did not have a presence at
        <a href="/wiki/Goonhilly_Satellite_Earth_Station" title="Goonhilly Satellite Earth Station">
         Goonhilly
        </a>
        on the
        <a href="/wiki/The_Lizard" title="The Lizard">
         Lizard Peninsula
        </a>
        , one of the few places in Cornwall where the clouds parted just in time for the total eclipse to be visible.
       </li>
       <li>
        Some of the best viewing conditions were to be had mid-
        <a href="/wiki/English_Channel" title="English Channel">
         Channel
        </a>
        , where ferries were halted in calm conditions to obtain an excellent view. Hundreds of people who gathered on the island of
        <a href="/wiki/Alderney" title="Alderney">
         Alderney
        </a>
        also experienced the event. There was extensive cloud in
        <a href="/wiki/Perranporth" title="Perranporth">
         Perranporth
        </a>
        which parted just in time, allowing the very large crowd that filled the beach and hillsides to witness this spectacular event. The clouds' parting was as magical as the eclipse itself.
       </li>
       <li>
        A gathering of several thousand people at the airport in
        <a href="/wiki/Soissons" title="Soissons">
         Soissons
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">
         France
        </a>
        , which was on the path of totality, were denied all but a few fleeting glimpses of the eclipse through the overcast sky. The clouds cleared completely just a few minutes after the eclipse.
       </li>
       <li>
        In contrast, the overcast sky in
        <a href="/wiki/Amiens" title="Amiens">
         Amiens
        </a>
        , France, where thousands had gathered, cleared only minutes before the eclipse began.
       </li>
       <li>
        Further inland, viewing conditions were also perfect at
        <a href="/wiki/Vouziers" title="Vouziers">
         Vouziers
        </a>
        , a French country town gridlocked by Belgian cars from day-visitors. The patchy cloud covering cleared a short time before the shadow arrived. Some photos from Vouziers were used on the subsequent BBC
        <a href="/wiki/The_Sky_at_Night" title="The Sky at Night">
         Sky at Night
        </a>
        programme.
       </li>
       <li>
        The San Francisco
        <a href="/wiki/Exploratorium" title="Exploratorium">
         Exploratorium
        </a>
        featured a live webcast from a crowded town square in
        <a href="/wiki/Amasya" title="Amasya">
         Amasya
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">
         Turkey
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Doordarshan" title="Doordarshan">
         Doordarshan
        </a>
        , the national TV channel in India, broadcast live coverage from
        <a href="/wiki/Srikakulam" title="Srikakulam">
         Srikakulam
        </a>
        , hosted by the TV personality
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monoranjan_Bhattacharya" title="Monoranjan Bhattacharya">
         Mona Bhattacharya
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        A
        <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Air_Force" title="Bulgarian Air Force">
         Bulgarian Air Force
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Mikoyan-Gurevich_MiG-21" title="Mikoyan-Gurevich MiG-21">
         MiG-21
        </a>
        two-seater was used by the
        <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Bulgarian Academy of Sciences">
         Bulgarian Academy of Sciences
        </a>
        to study the solar
        <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
         corona
        </a>
        . The MiG-21, flying at 1600–1700 km/h (M=1,4-1,5) at an altitude of 13,000 m, was able to stay in the Moon's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
         umbra
        </a>
        for 6 min. The photographer, an air force pilot, used two film cameras, both fitted with 200 mm lenses and
        <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
         infrared
        </a>
        filters, and one
        <a href="/wiki/Digital8" title="Digital8">
         Digital8
        </a>
        video camera.
       </li>
       <li>
        Hungary's most popular tourist destination,
        <a href="/wiki/Lake_Balaton" title="Lake Balaton">
         Lake Balaton
        </a>
        and its surrounding area, fell into the path of the eclipse entirely, which made the area even more popular for that day. The motorway leading there was so crowded, many people had to watch the eclipse while caught in a traffic jam.
       </li>
       <li>
        One French and two British
        <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">
         Concordes
        </a>
        briefly followed the eclipse with tourists on board.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">
         BBC
        </a>
        was filming one of its episodes for its TV series
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Airport_(TV_series)" title="Airport (TV series)">
          Airport
         </a>
        </i>
        that day and, during the show, resident press officers Russell Clisby and Steve Meller took photographs of the eclipse at
        <a href="/wiki/Heathrow_Airport" title="Heathrow Airport">
         Heathrow Airport
        </a>
        , as well as
        <a href="/wiki/Aeroflot" title="Aeroflot">
         Aeroflot
        </a>
        supervisor
        <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Spake" title="Jeremy Spake">
         Jeremy Spake
        </a>
        witnessing the eclipse on a special charter flight.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Radio_Television_of_Serbia" title="Radio Television of Serbia">
         RTS
        </a>
        , the national public broadcaster of
        <a href="/wiki/Serbia" title="Serbia">
         Serbia
        </a>
        , urged people to remain inside, citing dangers to public health. This caused the streets of all Serbian cities, towns and villages to be entirely deserted during the eclipse, with many opting to watch it on TV instead.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/The_BMJ" title="The BMJ">
         The BMJ
        </a>
        a month after the eclipse reported only 14 cases of eye damage from improper viewing of the eclipse, a number lower than initially feared. In one of the most serious cases the patient had looked at the sun without eye protection for twenty minutes, but overall the public health campaign had succeeded.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_Public_Health_Assessment_5-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-UK_Public_Health_Assessment-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_145">
        Saros 145
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_eclipse_of_August_11,_1999&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Saros 145">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This solar eclipse is a part of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Saros_145" title="Solar Saros 145">
        Saros cycle 145
       </a>
       , repeating every 18 years, 11 days, 8 hours, containing 77 events. The series started with a partial solar eclipse on January 4, 1639, and reached a first annular eclipse on June 6, 1891. It was a hybrid event on June 17, 1909, and total eclipses from June 29, 1927, through September 9, 2648. The series ends at member 77 as a partial eclipse on April 17, 3009. The longest eclipse will occur on June 25, 2522, with a maximum duration of totality of 7 minutes, 12 seconds.

        <a href="#cite_note-8">

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_series">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
