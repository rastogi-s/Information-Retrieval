      <p>
       A
       <b>
        radiation zone
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        radiative zone
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        radiative region
       </b>
       is a layer of a star's interior where energy is primarily transported toward the exterior by means of
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiative diffusion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_conduction" title="Thermal conduction">
        thermal conduction
       </a>
       , rather than by
       <a href="/wiki/Convection" title="Convection">
        convection
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_norton2010_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ryan_norton2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Energy travels through the radiation zone in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic radiation
       </a>
       as
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photons
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Matter in a radiation zone is so dense that photons can travel only a short distance before they are absorbed or scattered by another particle, gradually shifting to longer wavelength as they do so. For this reason, it takes an average of 171,000 years for
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
        gamma rays
       </a>
       from the core of the Sun to leave the radiation zone. Over this range, the temperature of the plasma drops from 15 million K near the core down to 1.5 million K at the base of the convection zone.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elkins_tanton2006_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-elkins_tanton2006-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <i>

       <i>

       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">

       </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Main_sequence_stars">
        Main sequence stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Main sequence stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For
       <a href="/wiki/Main_sequence" title="Main sequence">
        main sequence
       </a>
       stars—those stars that are generating energy through the
       <a href="/wiki/Thermonuclear_fusion" title="Thermonuclear fusion">
        thermonuclear fusion
       </a>
       of hydrogen at the core, the presence and location of radiative regions depends on the star's mass. Main sequence stars below about 0.3
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_mass" title="Solar mass">
        solar masses
       </a>
       are entirely convective, meaning they do not have a radiative zone. From 0.3 to 1.2 solar masses, the region around the stellar core is a radiation zone, separated from the overlying convection zone by the
       <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">
        tachocline
       </a>
       . The radius of the radiative zone increases
       <a href="/wiki/Monotonic_function" title="Monotonic function">
        monotonically
       </a>
       with mass, with stars around 1.2 solar masses being almost entirely radiative. Above 1.2 solar masses, the core region becomes a convection zone and the overlying region is a radiation zone, with the amount of mass within the convective zone increasing with the mass of the star.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-padmanabhan2001_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-padmanabhan2001-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sun">
        The Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Radiation_zone&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In the Sun, the region between the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_core" title="Solar core">
        solar core
       </a>
       at 0.2 of the Sun's radius and the outer
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection zone
       </a>
       at 0.71 of the Sun's radius is referred to as the radiation zone, although the core is also a radiative region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ryan_norton2010_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ryan_norton2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_zone" title="Convection zone">
        convection zone
       </a>
       and the radiation zone are divided by the
       <a href="/wiki/Tachocline" title="Tachocline">
        tachocline
       </a>
       , another part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
