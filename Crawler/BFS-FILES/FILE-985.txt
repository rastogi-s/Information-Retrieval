      <p>
       A
       <b>
        cyclotron
       </b>
       is a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_accelerator" title="Particle accelerator">
        particle accelerator
       </a>
       invented by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ernest_O._Lawrence" title="Ernest O. Lawrence">
        Ernest O. Lawrence
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Patent1948384_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Patent1948384-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lawrence-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in 1934 in which
       <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle" title="Charged particle">
        charged particles
       </a>
       accelerate outwards from the centre along a spiral path.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nave_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nave-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Close_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Close-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The particles are held to a spiral trajectory by a static magnetic field and accelerated by a rapidly varying (
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
        radio frequency
       </a>
       ) electric field. Lawrence was awarded the 1939
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nobel_prize_in_physics" title="Nobel prize in physics">
        Nobel prize in physics
       </a>
       for this invention.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Close_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Close-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Cyclotrons were the most powerful particle accelerator technology until the 1950s when they were superseded by the
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">
        synchrotron
       </a>
       , and are still used to produce particle beams in physics and
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_medicine" title="Nuclear medicine">
        nuclear medicine
       </a>
       . The largest single-magnet cyclotron was the 4.67 m (184 in)
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrocyclotron" title="Synchrocyclotron">
        synchrocyclotron
       </a>
       built between 1940 and 1946 by Lawrence at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" title="University of California at Berkeley">
        University of California at Berkeley
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Close_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Close-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which could accelerate protons to 730
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MeV" title="MeV">
        MeV
       </a>
       . The largest cyclotron is the 17.1 m (56 ft) multimagnet
       <a href="/wiki/TRIUMF" title="TRIUMF">
        TRIUMF
       </a>
       accelerator at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia" title="University of British Columbia">
        University of British Columbia
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vancouver,_British_Columbia" title="Vancouver, British Columbia">
        Vancouver, British Columbia
       </a>
       which can produce 500 MeV protons.
      </p>
      <p>
       Over 1200 cyclotrons are used in nuclear medicine worldwide for the production of radionuclides.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The cyclotron was conceived in Germany in the 1920s. At
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aachen_University" title="Aachen University">
        Aachen University
       </a>
       in 1926, the cyclotron was proposed by a co-student of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rolf_Wider&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rolf Wider (page does not exist)">
        Rolf Wider
       </a>
       , who rejected the idea as too complicated to construct.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1927,
       <a href="/wiki/Max_Steenbeck" title="Max Steenbeck">
        Max Steenbeck
       </a>
       developed the concept of the cyclotron at Siemens, but a misunderstanding prevented him from publishing and building the apparatus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first cyclotron patent was filed by Hungarian physicist
       <a href="/wiki/Leo_Szilard" title="Leo Szilard">
        Leo Szilard
       </a>
       in 1929, while working at
       <a href="/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin" title="Humboldt University of Berlin">
        Humboldt University of Berlin
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first cyclotron was developed and patented
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lawrence-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       by Ernest Lawrence in 1932 at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Berkeley" title="University of California, Berkeley">
        University of California, Berkeley
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Physics_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Physics-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He used large electromagnets recycled from obsolete
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poulsen_arc" title="Poulsen arc">
        Poulsen arc
       </a>
       radio transmitters provided by the
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Telegraph_Company" title="Federal Telegraph Company">
        Federal Telegraph Company
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A graduate student,
       <a href="/wiki/M._Stanley_Livingston" title="M. Stanley Livingston">
        M. Stanley Livingston
       </a>
       , did much of the work of translating the idea into working hardware.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lawrence read an article about the concept of a drift tube
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linac" title="Linac">
        linac
       </a>
       by Rolf Widerøe,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who had also been working along similar lines with the
       <a href="/wiki/Betatron" title="Betatron">
        betatron

       concept. At the

      <p>
       A cyclotron accelerates a
       <a href="/wiki/Charged_particle_beam" title="Charged particle beam">
        charged particle beam
       </a>
       using a
       <a href="/wiki/High_frequency" title="High frequency">
        high frequency
       </a>
       alternating
       <a href="/wiki/Voltage" title="Voltage">
        voltage
       </a>
       which is applied between two hollow "D"-shaped sheet metal electrodes called "dees" inside a vacuum chamber.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singh_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Singh-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The dees are placed face to face with a narrow gap between them, creating a cylindrical space within them for the particles to move. The particles are injected into the center of this space. The dees are located between the poles of a large
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnet" title="Electromagnet">
        electromagnet
       </a>
       which applies a static
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       <i>
        B
       </i>
       perpendicular to the electrode plane. The magnetic field causes the particles' path to bend in a circle due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lorentz_force" title="Lorentz force">
        Lorentz force
       </a>
       perpendicular to their direction of motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       If the particles' speeds were constant, they would travel in a circular path within the dees under the influence of the magnetic field. However a
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
        radio frequency
       </a>
       (RF) alternating voltage of several thousand
       <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">
        volts
       </a>
       is applied between the dees. The frequency is set so that the particles make one circuit during a single cycle of the voltage. To achieve this, the frequency must match the particle's
       <a href="/wiki/Cyclotron_resonance" title="Cyclotron resonance">
        cyclotron resonance
       </a>
       frequency
      </p>

      <p>
       where
       <i>
        B
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       strength,
       <i>
        q
       </i>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_charge" title="Electric charge">
        electric charge
       </a>
       of the particle, and
       <i>
        m
       </i>
       is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Relativistic_mass" title="Relativistic mass">
        relativistic mass
       </a>
       of the charged particle. Each time after the particles pass to the other dee electrode the polarity of the RF voltage reverses. Therefore, each time the particles cross the gap from one dee electrode to the other, the
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_field" title="Electric field">
        electric field
       </a>
       is in the correct direction to accelerate them. The particles' increasing speed due to these pushes causes them to move in a larger radius circle with each rotation, so the particles move in a
       <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">
        spiral
       </a>
       path outward from the center to the rim of the dees. When they reach the rim a small voltage on a metal plate deflects the beam so it exits the dees through a small gap between them, and hits a target located at the exit point at the rim of the chamber, or leaves the cyclotron through an evacuated beam tube to hit a remote target. Various materials may be used for the target, and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactions" title="Nuclear reactions">
        nuclear reactions
       </a>
       due to the collisions will create secondary particles which may be guided outside of the cyclotron and into instruments for analysis.
      </p>
      <p>
       The cyclotron was the first "cyclical" accelerator. The advantage of the cyclotron design over the existing "electrostatic" accelerators of the time such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cockcroft-Walton_accelerator" title="Cockcroft-Walton accelerator">
        Cockcroft-Walton accelerator
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Van_de_Graaff_generator" title="Van de Graaff generator">
        Van de Graaff generator
       </a>
       , was that in these machines the particles were only accelerated once by the voltage, so the particles' energy was equal to the accelerating voltage on the machine, which was limited by air breakdown to a few million volts. In the cyclotron, in contrast, the particles encounter the accelerating voltage many times during their spiral path, and so are accelerated many times,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lawrence_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lawrence-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       so the output energy can be many times the accelerating voltage.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Isochronous_cyclotron">
        Isochronous cyclotron
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Isochronous cyclotron">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       An alternative to the synchrocyclotron is the
       <i>
        isochronous cyclotron
       </i>
       , which has a magnetic field that increases with radius, rather than with time. Isochronous cyclotrons are capable of producing much greater beam current than synchrocyclotrons, but require azimuthal variations in the field strength to provide a
       <a href="/wiki/Strong_focusing" title="Strong focusing">
        strong focusing
       </a>
       effect and keep the particles captured in their spiral trajectory. For this reason, an isochronous cyclotron is also called an "AVF (azimuthal varying field) cyclotron".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sylee014_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sylee014-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This solution for focusing the particle beam was proposed by

      <p>
       The transverse de-focusing effect of this radial field gradient is compensated by ridges on the magnet faces which vary the field azimuthally as well. This allows particles to be accelerated continuously, on every period of the
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_frequency" title="Radio frequency">
        radio frequency
       </a>
       (RF), rather than in bursts as in most other accelerator types. This principle that alternating field gradients have a net focusing effect is called
       <a href="/wiki/Strong_focusing" title="Strong focusing">
        strong focusing
       </a>
       . It was obscurely known theoretically long before it was put into practice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lhthomas_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lhthomas-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <a href="/wiki/TRIUMF" title="TRIUMF">
        TRIUMF
       </a>
       cyclotron mentioned below is the largest with an outer orbit radius of 7.9 metres, extracting protons at up to 510 MeV, which is 3/4 of the speed of light. The
       <a href="/wiki/Paul_Scherrer_Institute" title="Paul Scherrer Institute">
        PSI
       </a>
       cyclotron reaches higher energy but is smaller because of using a higher magnetic field.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">
        Usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For several decades, cyclotrons were the best source of high-energy beams for
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_physics" title="Nuclear physics">
        nuclear physics
       </a>
       experiments; several cyclotrons are still in use for this type of research. The results enable the calculation of various properties, such as the mean spacing between atoms and the creation of various collision products. Subsequent chemical and particle analysis of the target material may give insight into
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_transmutation" title="Nuclear transmutation">
        nuclear transmutation
       </a>
       of the elements used in the target.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cyclotrons can be used in
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_therapy" title="Particle therapy">
        particle therapy
       </a>
       to treat
       <a href="/wiki/Cancer" title="Cancer">
        cancer
       </a>
       . Ion beams from cyclotrons can be used, as in
       <a href="/wiki/Proton_therapy" title="Proton therapy">
        proton therapy
       </a>
       , to penetrate the body and kill tumors by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiation_poisoning" title="Radiation poisoning">
        radiation damage
       </a>
       , while minimizing damage to healthy tissue along their path. Cyclotron beams can be used to bombard other atoms to produce short-lived
       <a href="/wiki/Positron" title="Positron">
        positron
       </a>
       -emitting isotopes suitable for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PET_imaging" title="PET imaging">
        PET imaging
       </a>
       . More recently some cyclotrons currently installed at hospitals for radio isotopes production have been retrofitted to enable them to produce
       <a href="/wiki/Technetium-99m" title="Technetium-99m">
        technetium-99m
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Technetium-99m is a diagnostic isotope in short supply due to difficulties at Canada's
       <a href="/wiki/Chalk_River_Laboratories" title="Chalk River Laboratories">
        Chalk River
       </a>
       facility.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages_and_limitations">
        Advantages and limitations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">

        </a>

      <p>
       The cyclotron was an improvement over the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_accelerator" title="Linear accelerator">
        linear accelerators
       </a>
       (
       <i>
        linac
       </i>
       s) that were available when it was invented, being more cost- and space-effective due to the iterated interaction of the particles with the accelerating field. In the 1920s, it was not possible to generate the high power, high-frequency radio waves which are used in modern linacs (generated by
       <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">
        klystrons
       </a>
       ). As such, impractically long linac structures were required for higher-energy particles. The compactness of the cyclotron reduces other costs as well, such as foundations, radiation shielding, and the enclosing building. Cyclotrons have a single electrical driver, which saves both money and power. Furthermore, cyclotrons are able to produce a continuous stream of particles at the target, so the average power passed from a particle beam into a target is relatively high.
      </p>

      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Spiral" title="Spiral">
        spiral
       </a>
       path of the cyclotron beam can only "sync up" with klystron-type (constant frequency) voltage sources if the accelerated particles are approximately obeying
       <a href="/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion" title="Newton's laws of motion">
        Newton's laws of motion
       </a>
       . If the particles become fast enough that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Special_Relativity" title="Special Relativity">
        relativistic
       </a>
       effects become important, the beam becomes out of phase with the oscillating electric field, and cannot receive any additional acceleration. The classical cyclotron is therefore only capable of accelerating particles up to a few percent of the speed of light. To accommodate increased mass the magnetic field may be modified by appropriately shaping the pole pieces as in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Isochronous_cyclotron" title="Isochronous cyclotron">
        isochronous cyclotrons
       </a>
       , operating in a pulsed mode and changing the frequency applied to the dees as in the
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrocyclotron" title="Synchrocyclotron">
        synchrocyclotrons
       </a>
       , either of which is limited by the diminishing cost effectiveness of making larger machines. Cost limitations have been overcome by employing the more complex
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">
        synchrotron
       </a>
       or modern,
       <a href="/wiki/Klystron" title="Klystron">
        klystron
       </a>
       -driven
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Linear_accelerator" title="Linear accelerator">
        linear accelerators
       </a>
       , both of which have the advantage of scalability, offering more power within an improved cost structure as the machines are made larger.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_examples">
        Notable examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Notable examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One of the world's largest cyclotrons is at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RIKEN" title="RIKEN">
        RIKEN
       </a>
       laboratory in Japan. Called the SRC, for Superconducting Ring Cyclotron, it has six separated superconducting sectors, and is 19 m in diameter and 8 m high. Built to accelerate heavy ions, its maximum magnetic field is 3.8
       <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">
        T
       </a>
       , yielding a bending ability of 8 T·m. The total weight of the cyclotron is 8,300 t. The Riken magnetic field covers from 3.5 m radius to 5.5 m with the maximum beam radius of about 5 m (200 in). It has accelerated uranium ions to 345 MeV per atomic mass unit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/TRIUMF" title="TRIUMF">
        TRIUMF
       </a>
       , Canada's national laboratory for nuclear and particle physics, houses the world's largest cyclotron.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 18 m diameter, 4,000 t main magnet produces a field of 0.46 T while a 23 MHz 94
       <a href="/wiki/Volt" title="Volt">
        kV
       </a>
       electric field is used to accelerate the 300 μA beam.The TRIUMF field goes from 0 to 813 cm (0 to 320 in) radius with the maximum beam radius of 790 cm (310 in). Its large size is partly a result of using negative hydrogen ions rather than protons; this requires a lower magnetic field to reduce EM stripping of the loosely bound electrons. The advantage is that extraction is simpler; multi-energy, multi-beams can be extracted by inserting thin carbon stripping foils at appropriate radii. TRIUMF is run by a consortium of eighteen Canadian universities and is located at the University of British Columbia.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_technologies">
        Related technologies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Related technologies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The spiraling of electrons in a cylindrical vacuum chamber within a transverse magnetic field is also employed in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetron" title="Magnetron">
        magnetron
       </a>
       , a device for producing high frequency radio waves (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microwaves" title="Microwaves">
        microwaves
       </a>
       ). The
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrotron" title="Synchrotron">
        synchrotron
       </a>
       moves the particles through a path of constant radius, allowing it to be made as a pipe and so of much larger radius than is practical with the cyclotron and
       <a href="/wiki/Synchrocyclotron" title="Synchrocyclotron">
        synchrocyclotron
       </a>
       . The larger radius allows the use of numerous magnets, each of which imparts angular momentum and so allows particles of higher velocity (mass) to be kept within the bounds of the evacuated pipe. The magnetic field strength of each of the bending magnets is increased as the particles gain energy in order to keep the bending angle constant.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Facilities">
        Facilities
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Cyclotron&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Facilities">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://user88.lbl.gov/" rel="nofollow">
         The 88-Inch Cyclotron
        </a>
        at
        <a href="/wiki/Lawrence_Berkeley_National_Laboratory" title="Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory">
         Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.cyclotron.nl/__5_3_2_2" rel="nofollow">
         The first Cyclotron in Amsterdam, Netherlands (1964)
        </a>
        , at the site of the Free University
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.nscl.msu.edu/" rel="nofollow">
         National Superconducting Cyclotron Laboratory
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Michigan_State_University" title="Michigan State University">
         Michigan State University
        </a>
        —Home of coupled K500 and K1200 cyclotrons; the K500, the first superconducting cyclotron, and the K1200, formerly the most powerful in the world.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.physics.rutgers.edu/cyclotron/" rel="nofollow">
         Rutgers Cyclotron
        </a>
        —Students at
        <a href="/wiki/Rutgers_University" title="Rutgers University">
         Rutgers University
        </a>
        built a 30 cm (12 in) 1 MeV cyclotron as an undergraduate project, which is now used for a senior-level undergraduate and a graduate lab course.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="external text" href="http://www.rarf.riken.jp/Eng/" rel="nofollow">
         RIKEN Nishina Center for Accelerator-based Science
        </a>
        —Home of the most powerful cyclotron in the world
       </li>
      </ul>
