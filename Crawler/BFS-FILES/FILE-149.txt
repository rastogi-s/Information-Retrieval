      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , a
       <b>
        transit
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        astronomical transit
       </b>
       is the phenomenon of at least one
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Celestial_body" title="Celestial body">
        celestial body
       </a>
       appearing to move across the face of another celestial body, hiding a small part of it, as seen by an observer at some particular vantage point.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
        Definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="369" height="229" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif/220px-Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif/330px-Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Jupiter-io-transit_feb_10_2009.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>

        </div>

      </div>
      <p>
       The word "transit" refers to cases where the nearer object
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apparent_size" title="Apparent size">
        appears
       </a>
       considerably smaller than the more distant object. Cases where the nearer object appears larger and completely hides the more distant object are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        <i>
         occultations
        </i>
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       One example of a transit involves the motion of a
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       between a
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        terrestrial
       </a>
       observer and the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       . This can happen only with
       <a href="/wiki/Inferior_and_superior_planets" title="Inferior and superior planets">
        inferior planets
       </a>
       , namely
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Mercury" title="Transit of Mercury">
        transit of Mercury
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       ). However, as seen from outer planets such as
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Earth_from_Mars" title="Transit of Earth from Mars">
        Earth itself transits the Sun
       </a>
       on occasion.
      </p>

         is
         <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
          occulted
         </a>
         by the
         <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">
          rings of Saturn
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The term can also be used to describe the motion of a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       across its parent planet, for instance one of the Galilean satellites (
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       ) across
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       , as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A transit requires three bodies to be lined up in a single line. More rare are cases where four bodies are lined up. The one closest to the present occurred on 27 June 1586, when Mercury transited the Sun as seen from Venus at the same time as a transit of Mercury from Saturn and a transit of Venus from Saturn.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In recent years the discovery of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extrasolar_planet" title="Extrasolar planet">
        extrasolar planets
       </a>
       has excited interest in the possibility of detecting their transits across their own
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stellar
       </a>
       primaries.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/HD_209458b" title="HD 209458b">
        HD 209458b
       </a>
       is the first such transiting planet to be discovered.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mutual_planetary_transits">
        Mutual planetary transits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mutual planetary transits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_transits_and_occultations" title="Planetary transits and occultations">
        Planetary transits and occultations
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       In rare cases, one planet can pass in front of another. If the nearer planet appears smaller than the more distant one, the event is called a
       <i>
        mutual planetary transit
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Contacts">
        Contacts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Contacts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="640" height="82" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif/150px-PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif/225px-PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif/300px-PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA18389-MarsCuriosityRover-MercuryTransitsSun-20140603.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
          Mercury
         </a>
         transiting the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         as viewed by the
         <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
          <i>
           Curiosity
          </i>
          rover
         </a>
         on
         <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
          Mars
         </a>
         (June 3, 2014).
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140610_1-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140610-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       During a transit there are four "contacts", when the
       <a href="/wiki/Circumference" title="Circumference">
        circumference
       </a>
       of the small circle (small body disk) touches the circumference of the large circle (large body disk)
       <a href="/wiki/Tangent" title="Tangent">
        at a single point
       </a>
       . Historically, measuring the precise time of each point of contact was one of the most accurate ways to determine the positions of astronomical bodies. The contacts happen in the following order:
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Astronomical_Transit.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="500" height="75" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Astronomical_Transit.gif/150px-Astronomical_Transit.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Astronomical_Transit.gif/225px-Astronomical_Transit.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Astronomical_Transit.gif/300px-Astronomical_Transit.gif 2x" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Astronomical_Transit.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artistic animation of a planet transitting its star.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <b>
         First contact
        </b>
        : the smaller body is entirely outside the larger body, moving inward ("exterior ingress")
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Second contact
        </b>
        : the smaller body is entirely inside the larger body, moving further inward ("interior ingress")
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Third contact
        </b>
        : the smaller body is entirely inside the larger body, moving outward ("interior egress")
       </li>
       <li>
        <b>
         Fourth contact
        </b>
        : the smaller body is entirely outside the larger body, moving outward ("exterior egress")
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLsafety_2-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-UCLsafety-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       A fifth named point is that of greatest transit, when the apparent centers of the two bodies are nearest to each other, halfway through the transit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UCLsafety_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-UCLsafety-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_observations">
        Notable observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_(astronomy)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Notable observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       No missions were planned to coincide with the transit of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       visible from
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       on 11 May 1984 and the Viking missions had been terminated a year previously. Consequently, the next opportunity to observe such an alignment will be in 2084.
      </p>
      <p>
       On December 21, 2012, the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        Cassini–Huygens
       </a>
       probe, in orbit around
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       , observed the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       transiting the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On 3 June 2014, the Mars rover
       <a href="/wiki/Curiosity_(rover)" title="Curiosity (rover)">
        <i>
         Curiosity
        </i>
       </a>
       observed the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       transiting the Sun, marking the first time a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_transit" title="Planetary transit">
        planetary transit
       </a>
       has been observed from a celestial body besides Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20140610_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20140610-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
