   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    The Moon is made of green cheese
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

        </div>

      <p>
       <b>
        "The Moon is made of Blue cheese"
       </b>
       is a statement referring to a fanciful belief that the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       is composed of
       <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">
        cheese
       </a>
       . In its original formulation as a
       <a href="/wiki/Proverb" title="Proverb">
        proverb
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Metaphor" title="Metaphor">
        metaphor
       </a>
       for credulity with roots in fable, this refers to the perception of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
        simpleton
       </a>
       who sees a reflection of the Moon in water and mistakes it for a round cheese wheel. It is widespread as a
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(folkloristics)" title="Morphology (folkloristics)">
        folkloric motif
       </a>
       among many of the world's cultures, and the notion has also found its way into children's
       <a href="/wiki/Folklore" title="Folklore">
        folklore
       </a>
       and modern popular culture.
      </p>
      <p>
       The phrase "
       <a href="/wiki/Blue_cheese" title="Blue cheese">
        blue cheese
       </a>
       " in this proverb simply refers to a young cheese (sometimes "
       <a href="/wiki/Cream_cheese" title="Cream cheese">
        cream cheese
       </a>
       " is used), though modern people may interpret the color reference literally.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There was never an actual historical popular belief that the Moon is made of blue cheese (
       <i>
        cf.
       </i>
       , the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myth_of_the_Flat_Earth" title="Myth of the Flat Earth">
        myth of the Flat Earth
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [A]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was typically used as an example of extreme
       <a href="/wiki/Credulity" title="Credulity">
        credulity
       </a>
       , a meaning that was clear and commonly understood as early as 1638.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fable">
        Fable
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Fable">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There exists a family of stories in
       <a href="/wiki/Comparative_mythology" title="Comparative mythology">
        comparative mythology
       </a>
       in diverse countries that concern a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
        simpleton
       </a>
       who sees a reflection of the Moon and mistakes it for a round cheese:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ... the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Servian_mythology" title="Servian mythology">
         Servian tale
        </a>
        where the fox leads the wolf to believe the moon reflection in the water is a cheese and the wolf bursts in the attempt to drink up the water to get at the cheese; the
        <a href="/wiki/Zulu_mythology" title="Zulu mythology">
         Zulu tale
        </a>
        of the hyena that drops the bone to go after the moon reflection in the water; the
        <a href="/wiki/Occitan_literature" title="Occitan literature">
         Gascon tale
        </a>
        of the peasant watering his
        <a href="/wiki/Donkey" title="Donkey">
         ass
        </a>
        on a moonlight night. A cloud obscures the moon, and the peasant, thinking the ass has drunk the moon, kills the beast to recover the moon; the
        <a href="/wiki/Turkish_folklore" title="Turkish folklore">
         Turkish tale
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Nasreddin" title="Nasreddin">
         Khoja Nasru-'d-Din
        </a>
        who thinks the moon has fallen into the well and gets a rope and chain with which to pull it out. In his efforts the rope breaks, and he falls back, but seeing the moon in the sky, praises Allah that the moon is safe; the
        <a href="/wiki/Scottish_mythology" title="Scottish mythology">
         Scotch tale
        </a>
        of the wolf fishing with his tail for the moon reflection;
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         G. H. McKnight
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_5-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-5">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_Wolf_and_the_Fox_story_type">
        <i>
         The Wolf and the Fox
        </i>
        story type
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Wolf and the Fox story type">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This
       <a href="/wiki/Morphology_(folkloristics)" title="Morphology (folkloristics)">
        folkloric motif
       </a>
       is first recorded in literature during the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Middle_Ages" title="High Middle Ages">
        High Middle Ages
       </a>
       by the French rabbi
       <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
        Rashi
       </a>
       with a
       <a href="/wiki/Mashal_(allegory)" title="Mashal (allegory)">
        Rabbinic parable
       </a>
       in his commentary weaving together three Biblical quotations given in the main text (including one on "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Fox_and_the_Grapes" title="The Fox and the Grapes">
        sour grapes
       </a>
       ") into a reconstruction of some of the Talmudic
       <a href="/wiki/Rabbi_Meir" title="Rabbi Meir">
        Rabbi Meir
       </a>
       's supposed three hundred
       <a href="/wiki/Foxes_in_popular_culture" title="Foxes in popular culture">
        fox fables
       </a>
       ("משלות שועלים", in later works "משלי שועלים"), in the tractate
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sanhedrin_(tractate)" title="Sanhedrin (tractate)">
         Sanhedrin
        </a>
       </i>
       :
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hebrew_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hebrew-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        A fox once craftily induced a wolf to go and join the Jews in their
        <a href="/wiki/Shabbat" title="Shabbat">
         Sabbath
        </a>
        preparations and share in their festivities. On his appearing in their midst the Jews fell upon him with sticks and beat him. He therefore came back determined to kill the fox. But the latter pleaded: 'It is no fault of mine that you were beaten, but they have a grudge against your father who once helped them in preparing their banquet and then consumed all the choice bits.' 'And was I beaten for the wrong done by my father?' cried the indignant wolf. 'Yes,' replied the fox, '
        <i>
         the fathers have eaten sour grapes and the children's teeth are set on edge
        </i>
        . However,' he continued, 'come with me and I will supply you with abundant food. He led him to a well which had a beam across it from either end of which hung a rope with a bucket attached. The fox entered the upper bucket and descended into the well whilst the lower one was drawn up. 'Where are you going?' asked the wolf. The fox, pointing to the cheese-like reflection of the moon, replied: 'Here is plenty of meat and cheese; get into the other bucket and come down at once.' The wolf did so, and as he descended, the fox was drawn up. 'And how am I to get out?' demanded the wolf. 'Ah' said the fox '
        <i>
         the righteous is delivered out of trouble and the wicked cometh in his stead
        </i>
        . Is it not written,
        <i>
         Just balances, just weights
        </i>
        '?
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <a href="/wiki/Rashi" title="Rashi">
          Rashi
         </a>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
          <a href="#cite_note-7">
           [6]
          </a>
         </sup>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
          <a href="#cite_note-8">
           [7]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Rashi as the first literary reference may reflect the well-known
       <a href="/wiki/Beast_fable" title="Beast fable">
        beast fable
       </a>
       tradition of
       <a href="/wiki/French_folklore" title="French folklore">
        French folklore
       </a>
       or a more obscure such tradition in
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_mythology" title="Jewish mythology">
        Jewish folklore
       </a>
       (see also the tradition in
       <a href="/wiki/Berechiah_ha-Nakdan" title="Berechiah ha-Nakdan">
        Berechiah ha-Nakdan
       </a>
       ); the near-contemporary Iraqi rabbi
       <a href="/wiki/Hai_Gaon" title="Hai Gaon">
        Hai Gaon
       </a>
       also reconstructed this Rabbi Meir tale, sharing some elements of Rashi's story, but with a lion caught in a
       <a href="/wiki/Trapping_pit" title="Trapping pit">
        trapping pit
       </a>
       rather than a wolf in a well — however, Rashi may have actively "adapted contemporary [French] folklore to the [T]almudic passage", as was
       <a href="/wiki/Homiletics" title="Homiletics">
        homiletically
       </a>
       practiced in different Jewish communities.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETeitelbaum1999[https://books.google.com/books?id=LbZPv-2LO6YC&amp;pg=PA260_260–263]_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETeitelbaum1999[https://books.google.com/books?id=LbZPv-2LO6YC&amp;pg=PA260_260–263]-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Though the tale itself is probably of non-Jewish European origin, Rashi's form and elements are likely closer to the original in oral folklore than the somewhat later variation recorded featuring
       <a href="/wiki/Reynard" title="Reynard">
        Reynard
       </a>
       . Rashi's version already includes the fox, the wolf, the
       <a href="/wiki/Water_well" title="Water well">
        well
       </a>
       and the Moon that are seen in later versions.
       <a href="/wiki/Petrus_Alphonsi" title="Petrus Alphonsi">
        Petrus Alphonsi
       </a>
       , a Spanish Jewish convert to Christianity, popularized this tale in Europe in his collection
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Disciplina_Clericalis#The_Oxen_Which_the_Countryman_had_Promised_to_the_Wolf,_and_the_Judgment_of_the_Fox" title="Disciplina Clericalis">
         Disciplina Clericalis
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="383" data-file-width="539" height="156" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg/220px-Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg/330px-Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg/440px-Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Le_loup_et_le_renard_Chauveau.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The wolf is tempted by the Moon's reflection.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The variation featuring
       <a href="/wiki/Reynard" title="Reynard">
        Reynard
       </a>
       the Fox appeared soon after Petrus Alphonsi in the French classic
       <i>
        Le Roman de Renart
       </i>
       (as "Renart et Ysengrin dans le puits" in Branch IV); the Moon/cheese element is absent (it is replaced by a promise of Paradise at the bottom of the well), but such a version is alluded to in another part of the collection. This was the first Reynard tale to be adapted into English (as the Middle English "þe Vox and þe Wolf"), preceding Chaucer's "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Nun%27s_Priest%27s_Tale" title="The Nun's Priest's Tale">
        The Nun's Priest's Tale
       </a>
       " and the much later work of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Caxton" title="William Caxton">
        William Caxton
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McKnight_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-McKnight-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Later still, the Middle Scots
       <a href="/wiki/The_Fox,_the_Wolf_and_the_Husbandman" title="The Fox, the Wolf and the Husbandman">
        The Fox, the Wolf and the Husbandman
       </a>
       does include the Moon/cheese element. La Fontaine includes the story in the French classic compilation
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/La_Fontaine%27s_Fables" title="La Fontaine's Fables">
         Fables
        </a>
       </i>
       ("Le Loup et le Renard" in Book XI). The German tale of
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wolf_and_the_Fox" title="The Wolf and the Fox">
        The Wolf and the Fox
       </a>
       in Grimm replaces the well with a well-stocked cellar, where a newly satiated wolf is trapped and subject to the farmer's revenge, being now too overstuffed to escape through the exit.
      </p>

      <p>
       One of the facets of this morphology is grouped as "The Wolf Dives into the Water for Reflected Cheese" (Type 34) of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aarne%E2%80%93Thompson_classification_system" title="Aarne–Thompson classification system">
        Aarne–Thompson classification
       </a>
       of folktales, where the Moon's reflection is mistaken for cheese, in the section devoted to tales of
       <a href="/wiki/Foxes_in_popular_culture" title="Foxes in popular culture">
        The Clever Fox
       </a>
       . It can also be grouped as "The Moon in the Well" (Type 1335A), in the section devoted to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
        Stories about a Fool
       </a>
       , referring to stories where the simpleton believes the Moon itself is a tangible object in the water.
      </p>
      <p>
       The use of the words "The moon is made of green cheese" in a passive sentence creates a dilemma of syntax, depending upon whether it is considered to be a "phrase" and its larger context.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAnderson2006[https://books.google.com/books?id=X1SVzsc8mbEC&amp;pg=PA206_206]_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAnderson2006[https://books.google.com/books?id=X1SVzsc8mbEC&amp;pg=PA206_206]-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proverb">
        Proverb
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Proverb">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       "The Moon is made of green cheese" was one of the most popular proverbs in 16th and 17th-century English literature,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAppersonManser2003392_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAppersonManser2003392-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and it was also in use after this time. It likely originated in this formulation in 1546, when
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/John_Heywood" title="John Heywood">
         The Proverbs of John Heywood
        </a>
       </i>
       claimed "the moon is made of a greene cheese."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [B]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A common variation at that time was "to make one believe the Moon is made of green cheese" (
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       , to hoax).
      </p>
      <p>
       In French, there is the proverb "
       <i>
        Il veut prendre la lune avec les dents
       </i>
       " ("He wants to take the moon with his teeth"), alluded to in
       <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Rabelais" title="François Rabelais">
        Rabelais
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The characterization is also common in stories of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simpleton_(folklore)" title="Simpleton (folklore)">
        gothamites
       </a>
       , including the
       <a href="/wiki/Moonrakers" title="Moonrakers">
        Moonrakers
       </a>
       of Wiltshire, who were said to have taken advantage of this trope, and the assumption of their own naivete, to hide their smuggling activities from government officials.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Childlore_and_popular_culture">
        Childlore and popular culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Childlore and popular culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A 1902 survey of
       <a href="/wiki/Childlore" title="Childlore">
        childlore
       </a>
       by psychologist
       <a href="/wiki/G._Stanley_Hall" title="G. Stanley Hall">
        G. Stanley Hall
       </a>
       in the United States found that though most young children were unsure of the Moon's composition, that it was made of cheese was the single most common explanation:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Careful inquiry and reminiscence concerning the substance of the moon show that eighteen children [of 423], averaging five years, thought it made of cheese. Sometime the mice eat it horseshoe-shaped, or that it could be fed by throwing cheese up so clouds could catch it; or it was green because the man in the moon fed on green grass; its spots were mould; it was really green but looked yellow, because wrapped in yellow cheese cloth; it was cheese mixed with wax or with melted lava, which might be edible; there were many rats, mice and skippers there; it grew big from a starry speck of light by eating cheese.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Before that time, and since, the idea of the Moon actually being made of cheese has appeared as a humorous conceit in much of children's popular culture with astronomical themes (cf. the
       <a href="/wiki/Man_in_the_Moon" title="Man in the Moon">
        Man in the Moon
       </a>
       ), and in adult references to it.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">
        Popular culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The phrase is the title of a book, and appears on at least 21 of its 306 pages.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEComstock1929_14-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEComstock1929-14">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/A_Trip_to_the_Moon_(attraction)" title="A Trip to the Moon (attraction)">
          A Trip to the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        was an early Coney Island
        <a href="/wiki/Dark_ride" title="Dark ride">
         dark ride
        </a>
        where, after their "trip" to the Moon's surface and its cities, samples of green cheese were given out to visitors.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/John_Maynard_Keynes" title="John Maynard Keynes">
         John Maynard Keynes
        </a>
        's principal work "
        <a href="/wiki/The_General_Theory_of_Employment,_Interest_and_Money" title="The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money">
         The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money
        </a>
        " one finds in Chapter XVII a section "Unemployment develops, that is to say, because people want the moon; [..] There is no remedy but to persuade the public that green cheese is practically the same thing and to have a green cheese factory (i.e. a central bank) under public control."
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Milky_Way_(1940_film)" title="The Milky Way (1940 film)">
          The Milky Way
         </a>
        </i>
        is an Academy Award-winning animated cartoon short subject. As "three little kittens who lost their mittens" explore a dreamland, space is made up entirely of dairy products (e.g. the
        <a href="/wiki/Milky_Way" title="Milky Way">
         Milky Way
        </a>
        is made of milk and the Moon is made of cheese).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropes_15-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-tropes-15">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        At the end of the 1967
        <a href="/wiki/Tom_and_Jerry" title="Tom and Jerry">
         Tom and Jerry
        </a>
        short,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/O-Solar_Meow" title="O-Solar Meow">
          O-Solar Meow
         </a>
        </i>
        , Jerry is blasted to the Moon, where he is seen stuffing himself with its large quantities of cheese.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/DuckTales_(video_game)" title="DuckTales (video game)">
          DuckTales
         </a>
        </i>
        and
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/DuckTales:_Remastered" title="DuckTales: Remastered">
          DuckTales: Remastered
         </a>
        </i>
        by
        <a href="/wiki/Capcom" title="Capcom">
         Capcom
        </a>
        has Scrooge obtain a chunk of the "Green Cheese of Longevity" from the Moon. Fenton also briefly mentions the fable.
       </li>
       <li>
        In Nick Park's short animated film
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/A_Grand_Day_Out" title="A Grand Day Out">
          A Grand Day Out
         </a>
        </i>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Wallace_and_Gromit" title="Wallace and Gromit">
         Wallace and Gromit
        </a>
        build a lunar rocket to go on a cheese-centered holiday.
        <ul>
         <li>
          Wallace: "Everybody knows the moon's made of cheese."
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
           <a href="#cite_note-16">
            [14]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the short tale by Kenneth Lans, "A 'Rounders' Story about the 'Green Cheese' Moon", the story revolves around the green cheese made by giant spiders which is transported to the moon by leaf-cutting ants.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropes_15-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-tropes-15">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The myth has spawned an
        <a href="/wiki/Apocrypha" title="Apocrypha">
         apocryphal
        </a>
        recipe for the preparation of "Moon cheese" in
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MouseHunt_(web_game)" title="MouseHunt (web game)">
         MouseHunt
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the children's educational show,
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Electric_Company_(1971_TV_series)" title="The Electric Company (1971 TV series)">
          The Electric Company
         </a>
        </i>
        , there is a sketch in which Fargo North is an astronaut in space who receives orders to proceed to the Moon. He protests that is impossible since the Moon is made of green cheese and his exasperated partner reminds him that he was told otherwise in training.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropes_15-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-tropes-15">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In another skit, a character forcefully states that the moon is not made of green cheese, but of
        <i>
         cream
        </i>
        cheese.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_humor_and_epistemology">
        Scientific humor and epistemology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Moon_is_made_of_green_cheese&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Scientific humor and epistemology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/British_television_Apollo_11_coverage" title="British television Apollo 11 coverage">
         British television Apollo 11 coverage
        </a>
        had interludes entitled "But What If It's Made of Cheese."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT-2384_18-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-RT-2384-18">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        On
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/April_Fool%27s_Day" title="April Fool's Day">
         April 1
        </a>
        , 2002,
        <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
         NASA
        </a>
        "proved" that the moon was made of green cheese with an expiration (or "
        <a href="/wiki/Shelf_life" title="Shelf life">
         sell by
        </a>
        ") date
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">
          [C]
         </a>
        </sup>
        using doctored pictures purportedly from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hubble_telescope" title="Hubble telescope">
         Hubble telescope
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
         <a href="#cite_note-20">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        At the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_writer" title="Science writer">
         Science Writers'
        </a>
        conference,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theoretical_physicist" title="Theoretical physicist">
         theoretical physicist
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Sean_M._Carroll" title="Sean M. Carroll">
         Sean M. Carroll
        </a>
        explained why there was no need to "sample the moon to know it's not made of cheese." He said the hypothesis is "
        <a href="/wiki/Absurdity" title="Absurdity">
         absurd
        </a>
        ", failing against our knowledge of the universe and, "This is not a proof, there is no metaphysical proof, like you can proof a statement in logic or math that the moon is not made of green cheese. But science nevertheless passes judgments on claims based on how well they fit in with the rest of our theoretical understanding."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mirsky_21-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Mirsky-21">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
         <a href="#cite_note-22">
          [D]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Notwithstanding this uncontrovertible argument, the harmonic signature of moon rock—the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismic_velocity" title="Seismic velocity">
         seismic velocity
        </a>
        at which
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shockwave" title="Shockwave">
         shockwaves
        </a>
        travel—is said to be closer to cheese than to any rock on earth.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
         <a href="#cite_note-23">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Early versions of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Google_Moon" title="Google Moon">
         Google Moon
        </a>
        used a
        <a href="/wiki/Swiss_cheese" title="Swiss cheese">
         Swiss cheese
        </a>
        pattern for the closest zoom levels before high-resolution images became available.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tropes_15-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-tropes-15">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
         <a href="#cite_note-24">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Dennis Lindley used the myth to help explain the necessity of
        <a href="/wiki/Cromwell%27s_rule" title="Cromwell's rule">
         Cromwell's rule
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Bayesian_probability" title="Bayesian probability">
         Bayesian probability
        </a>
        : "In other words, if a decision-maker thinks something cannot be true and interprets this to mean it has zero probability, he will never be influenced by any data, which is surely absurd. So leave a little probability for the moon being made of green cheese; it can be as small as 1 in a million, but have it there since otherwise an army of astronauts returning with samples of the said cheese will leave you unmoved."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELindley1991104_25-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELindley1991104-25">
          [21]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The hypothesis that "the Moon is made of green cheese" is said to be testable by
        <a href="/wiki/Cluster_analysis" title="Cluster analysis">
         cluster analysis
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERomesburg2004[https://books.google.com/books?id=ZuIPv7OKm10C&amp;pg=PA46_46]_26-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERomesburg2004[https://books.google.com/books?id=ZuIPv7OKm10C&amp;pg=PA46_46]-26">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
