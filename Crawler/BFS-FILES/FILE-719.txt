      <p>
       <b>
        Solar observation
       </b>
       is the scientific endeavor of studying the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       and its behavior and relation to the Earth and the remainder of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . Deliberate solar observation began thousands of years ago. That initial era of direct observation gave way to telescopes in the 1600s followed by satellites in the twentieth century.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prehistory">
        Prehistory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prehistory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Stratigraphy" title="Stratigraphy">
        Stratigraphic
       </a>
       data suggest that solar cycles have occurred for hundreds of millions of years, if not longer; measuring
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Varves" title="Varves">
        varves
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Precambrian" title="Precambrian">
        precambrian
       </a>
       sedimentary rock has revealed repeating peaks in layer thickness corresponding to the cycle. It is possible that the early atmosphere on Earth was more sensitive to solar irradiation than today, so that greater glacial melting (and thicker sediment deposits) could have occurred during years with greater sunspot activity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would presume annual layering; however, alternative explanations (diurnal) have also been proposed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Analysis of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tree_rings" title="Tree rings">
        tree rings
       </a>
       revealed a detailed picture of past solar cycles:
       <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">
        Dendrochronologically dated
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon-14" title="Carbon-14">
        radiocarbon
       </a>
       concentrations have allowed for a reconstruction of sunspot activity covering 11,400 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid15510145_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pmid15510145-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Early_observations">
        Early observations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early observations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Solar activity and related events have been regularly recorded since the time of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Babylonians" title="Babylonians">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       . In the 8th century BC,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1223BC_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1223BC-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       they described solar eclipses and possibly predicted them from numerological rules. The earliest extant report of sunspots dates back to the Chinese
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Book_of_Changes" title="Book of Changes">
         Book of Changes
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <abbr title="circa">
        c.
       </abbr>
       <span style="white-space:nowrap;">
        800 BC
       </span>
       . The phrases used in the book translate to "A dou is seen in the Sun" and "A mei is seen in the Sun", where
       <i>
        dou
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        mei
       </i>
       would be darkening or obscuration (based on the context). Observations were regularly noted by Chinese and Korean astronomers at the behest of the emperors, rather than independently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_1223BC_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_1223BC-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first clear mention of a sunspot in Western literature, around 300 BC, was by the
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        ancient Greek
       </a>
       scholar
       <a href="/wiki/Theophrastus" title="Theophrastus">
        Theophrastus
       </a>
       , student of
       <a href="/wiki/Plato" title="Plato">
        Plato
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       and successor to the latter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 17 March AD 807
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">
        Benedictine
       </a>
       monk Adelmus observed a large sunspot that was visible for eight days; however, Adelmus incorrectly concluded he was observing a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_(astronomy)" title="Transit (astronomy)">
        transit
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest surviving record of deliberate sunspot observation dates from 364 BC, based on comments by
       <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Chinese_astronomy" title="Timeline of Chinese astronomy">
        Chinese astronomer
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Gan_De" title="Gan De">
        Gan De
       </a>
       in a
       <a href="/wiki/Star_catalogue" title="Star catalogue">
        star catalogue
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By 28 BC, Chinese astronomers were regularly recording sunspot observations in official imperial records.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       A large sunspot was observed at the time of
       <a href="/wiki/Charlemagne" title="Charlemagne">
        Charlemagne
       </a>
       's death in AD 813.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunspot activity in 1129 was described by
       <a href="/wiki/John_of_Worcester" title="John of Worcester">
        John of Worcester
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Averroes" title="Averroes">
        Averroes
       </a>
       provided a description of sunspots later in the 12th century;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       however, these observations were also misinterpreted as planetary transits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first unambiguous mention of the solar corona was by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Leo_Diaconus" title="Leo Diaconus">
        Leo Diaconus
       </a>
       , a Byzantine historian. He wrote of the 22 December 968 total eclipse, which he experienced in Constantinople (modern-day Istanbul, Turkey):
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_0_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_0-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        at the fourth hour of the day ... darkness covered the earth and all the brightest stars shone forth. And it was possible to see the disk of the Sun, dull and unlit, and a dim and feeble glow like a narrow band shining in a circle around the edge of the disk.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Leo Diaconus
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_0_13-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_0-13">
           [13]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="17th_and_18th_centuries">
        17th and 18th centuries
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: 17th and 18th centuries">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_in_Samuel_Dunn_Wall_Map_of_the_World.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="230" data-file-width="200" height="230" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f7/Sun_in_Samuel_Dunn_Wall_Map_of_the_World.jpg" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sun_in_Samuel_Dunn_Wall_Map_of_the_World.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sunspots in
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1794_Samuel_Dunn_Map" title="1794 Samuel Dunn Map">
          1794 Samuel Dunn Map
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno" title="Giordano Bruno">
        Giordano Bruno
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Kepler" title="Johannes Kepler">
        Johannes Kepler
       </a>
       suggested the idea that the sun rotated on its axis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunspots were first observed telescopically in late 1610 by English astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Harriot" title="Thomas Harriot">
        Thomas Harriot
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Frisians" title="Frisians">
        Frisian
       </a>
       astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Fabricius" title="Johannes Fabricius">
        Johannes
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/David_Fabricius" title="David Fabricius">
        David Fabricius
       </a>
       , who published a description in June 1611.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solargreat_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-solargreat-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Fabricius' used
       <a href="/wiki/Camera_obscura" title="Camera obscura">
        camera obscura
       </a>
       telescopy to get a better view of the solar disk.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Galileo" title="Galileo">
        Galileo
       </a>
       had been showing sunspots to astronomers in Rome while
       <a href="/wiki/Christoph_Scheiner" title="Christoph Scheiner">
        Christoph Scheiner
       </a>
       had probably been observing the spots using an improved
       <a href="/wiki/Helioscope" title="Helioscope">
        helioscope
       </a>
       of his own design. Galileo and Scheiner, neither of whom knew of the Fabricius' work, futilely vied for the credit that eventually accrued to the father and son. In 1613, in
       <a href="/wiki/Letters_on_Sunspots" title="Letters on Sunspots">
        Letters on Sunspots
       </a>
       , Galileo refuted Scheiner's 1612 claim that sunspots were planets inside Mercury's orbit, showing that sunspots were surface features.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-solargreat_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-solargreat-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_02_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_02-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the physical aspects of sunspots were not identified until the 20th century, observations continued. Study was hampered during the 17th century due to the low number of sunspots during what is now recognized as an extended period of low solar activity, known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">
        Maunder Minimum
       </a>
       . By the 19th century, then-sufficient sunspot records allowed researchers to infer periodic cycles in sunspot activity. In 1845,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry" title="Joseph Henry">
        Henry
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Alexander_(astronomer)" title="Stephen Alexander (astronomer)">
        Alexander
       </a>
       observed the Sun with a
       <a href="/wiki/Thermopile" title="Thermopile">
        thermopile
       </a>
       and determined that sunspots emitted less radiation than surrounding areas. The emission of higher than average amounts of radiation later were observed from the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Facula" title="Facula">
        faculae
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sunspots had some importance in the debate over the nature of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . They showed that the Sun rotated, and their comings and goings showed that the Sun changed, contrary to Aristotle, who had taught that all celestial bodies were perfect, unchanging spheres.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspots were rarely recorded between 1650 and 1699. Later analysis revealed the problem to a reduced number of sunspots, rather than observational lapses. Building upon
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Sp%C3%B6rer" title="Gustav Spörer">
        Gustav Spörer
       </a>
       's work,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edward_Maunder" title="Edward Maunder">
        Edward Maunder
       </a>
       suggested that the Sun had changed from a period in which sunspots all but disappeared to a renewal of sunspot cycles starting in about 1700. Adding to this understanding of the absence of solar cycles were observations of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_aurora" title="Polar aurora">
        aurorae
       </a>
       , which were absent at the same time. The lack of a solar
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       was also noted prior to 1715. The period of low sunspot activity from 1645 to 1717 later became known as the "
       <a href="/wiki/Maunder_Minimum" title="Maunder Minimum">
        Maunder Minimum
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observers such as
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">
        Johannes Hevelius
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Picard" title="Jean Picard">
        Jean Picard
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jean_Dominique_Cassini" title="Jean Dominique Cassini">
        Jean Dominique Cassini
       </a>
       confirmed this change.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_02_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_02-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="19th_century">
        19th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: 19th century">
         edit

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_spectroscopy">
        Solar spectroscopy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Solar spectroscopy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       After the detection of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Infra-red_radiation" title="Infra-red radiation">
        infra-red radiation
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       in 1800 and of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation" title="Ultraviolet radiation">
        Ultraviolet radiation
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Johann_Wilhelm_Ritter" title="Johann Wilhelm Ritter">
        Johann Wilhelm Ritter
       </a>
       , solar spectrometry began in 1817 when
       <a href="/wiki/William_Hyde_Wollaston" title="William Hyde Wollaston">
        William Hyde Wollaston
       </a>
       noticed that dark lines appeared in the solar spectrum when viewed through a glass
       <a href="/wiki/Prism" title="Prism">
        prism
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer" title="Joseph von Fraunhofer">
        Joseph von Fraunhofer
       </a>
       later independently discovered the lines and they were named
       <a href="/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines" title="Fraunhofer lines">
        Fraunhofer lines
       </a>
       after him. Other physicists discerned that properties of the solar atmosphere could be determined from them. Notable scientists to advance spectroscopy were
       <a href="/wiki/David_Brewster" title="David Brewster">
        David Brewster
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff" title="Gustav Kirchhoff">
        Gustav Kirchhoff
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Wilhelm_Bunsen" title="Robert Wilhelm Bunsen">
        Robert Wilhelm Bunsen
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Anders_Jonas_%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Anders Jonas Ångström">
        Anders Jonas Ångström
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18007_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18007-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_cycle">
        Solar cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Solar cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:396px;max-width:396px">
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:202px;max-width:202px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SHSchwabe.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="274" data-file-width="283" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/SHSchwabe.jpg/200px-SHSchwabe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/43/SHSchwabe.jpg 1.5x" width="200"/>
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          Samuel Heinrich Schwabe (1789–1875). German astronomer, discovered the solar cycle through extended observations of sunspots
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:190px;max-width:190px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:RudolfWolf.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="366" data-file-width="357" height="193" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg/188px-RudolfWolf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg/282px-RudolfWolf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/67/RudolfWolf.jpg 2x" width="188"/>
          </a>
         </div>
         <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left">
          Rudolf Wolf (1816–1893), Swiss astronomer, carried out historical reconstruction of solar activity back to the seventeenth century
         </div>
        </div>
        <div style="clear:left">
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:392px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_Numbers.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="425" data-file-width="1020" height="163" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sunspot_Numbers.png/390px-Sunspot_Numbers.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sunspot_Numbers.png/585px-Sunspot_Numbers.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Sunspot_Numbers.png/780px-Sunspot_Numbers.png 2x" width="390"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_Numbers.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         400 year history of
         <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
          sunspot numbers
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The cyclic variation of the number of sunspots was first observed by
       <a href="/wiki/Heinrich_Schwabe" title="Heinrich Schwabe">
        Samuel Heinrich Schwabe
       </a>
       between 1826 and 1843.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Rudolf_Wolf" title="Rudolf Wolf">
        Rudolf Wolf
       </a>
       studied the historical record in an attempt to establish a history of solar variations. His data extended only to 1755. He also established in 1848 a relative sunspot number formulation to compare the work of different astronomers using varying equipment and methodologies, now known as the
       <a href="/wiki/Wolf_number" title="Wolf number">
        Wolf (or Zürich) sunspot number
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Sp%C3%B6rer" title="Gustav Spörer">
        Gustav Spörer
       </a>
       later suggested a 70-year period before 1716 in which sunspots were rarely observed as the reason for Wolf's inability to extend the cycles into the 17th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       Also in 1848,
       <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Henry" title="Joseph Henry">
        Joseph Henry
       </a>
       projected an image of the Sun onto a screen and determined that sunspots were cooler than the surrounding surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Around 1852, Edward Sabine, Wolf, Jean-Alfred Gautier and Johann von Lamont independently found a link between the solar cycle and geomagnetic activity, sparking the first research into interactions between the Sun and the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18006_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18006-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the second half of the nineteenth century
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">
        Richard Carrington
       </a>
       and Spörer independently noted the migration of sunspot activity towards the solar equator as the cycle progresses. This pattern is best visualized in the form of the so-called butterfly diagram, first constructed by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Walter_Maunder" title="Edward Walter Maunder">
        Edward Walter Maunder
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annie_Scott_Dill_Maunder" title="Annie Scott Dill Maunder">
        Annie Scott Dill Maunder
       </a>
       in the early twentieth century (see graph). Images of the Sun are divided into latitudinal strips, and the monthly-averaged fractional surface of sunspots calculated. This is plotted vertically as a color-coded bar, and the process is repeated month after month to produce a time-series diagram.
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:602px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1280" data-file-width="2150" height="357" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/600px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/900px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9e/Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif/1200px-Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif 2x" width="600"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Sunspot_butterfly_graph.gif" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          The sunspot butterfly diagram. This modern version is constructed (and regularly updated) by the solar group at NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Half a century later, the father-and-son team of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harold_Babcock" title="Harold Babcock">
        Harold
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Horace_Babcock" title="Horace Babcock">
        Horace Babcock
       </a>
       showed that the solar surface is magnetized even outside of sunspots; that this weaker magnetic field is to first order a
       <a href="/wiki/Dipole" title="Dipole">
        dipole
       </a>
       ; and that this dipole undergoes polarity reversals with the same period as the sunspot cycle (see graph below). These observations established that the solar cycle is a spatiotemporal magnetic process unfolding over the Sun as a whole.
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:702px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Synoptic-solmag.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="600" data-file-width="1300" height="323" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg/700px-Synoptic-solmag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg/1050px-Synoptic-solmag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/3/33/Synoptic-solmag.jpg 2x" width="700"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Synoptic-solmag.jpg" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Time vs. solar latitude diagram of the radial component of the solar magnetic field, averaged over successive solar rotation. The "butterfly" signature of sunspots is clearly visible at low latitudes. Diagram constructed (and regularly updated) by the solar group at NASA Marshall Space Flight Center.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Photography">
        Photography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Photography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The sun was photographed for the first time, on 2 April 1845, by French physicists
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louis_Fizeau" title="Louis Fizeau">
        Louis Fizeau
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Foucault" title="Léon Foucault">
        Léon Foucault
       </a>
       . Sunspots, as well as the
       <a href="/wiki/Limb_darkening" title="Limb darkening">
        limb darkening
       </a>
       effect, are visible in their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daguerrotypes" title="Daguerrotypes">
        daguerrotypes
       </a>
       . Photography assisted in the study of solar prominences,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_granulation" title="Solar granulation">
        granulation
       </a>
       and spectroscopy. Charles A. Young first captured a prominence in 1870. Solar eclipses were also photographed, with the most useful early images taken in 1851 by Berkowski in 1860 by De la Rue's team in Spain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18006_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18006-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">
        Rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Early estimates of the Sun's rotation period varied between 25 and 28 days. The cause was determined independently in 1858 by Richard C. Carrington and
       <a href="/wiki/Gustav_Sp%C3%B6rer" title="Gustav Spörer">
        Spörer
       </a>
       . They discovered that the latitude with the most sunspots decreases from 40° to 5° during each cycle, and that at higher latitudes sunspots rotate more slowly. The sun's rotation was thus shown to vary by latitude and that its outer layer must be fluid. In 1871
       <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Carl_Vogel" title="Hermann Carl Vogel">
        Hermann Vogel
       </a>
       , and shortly thereafter by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Augustus_Young" title="Charles Augustus Young">
        Charles Young
       </a>
       confirmed this spectroscopically.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nils_D%C3%BAner" title="Nils Dúner">
        Nils Dúner
       </a>
       's spectroscopic observation in the 1880s showed a 30% difference between the sun's faster equatorial regions and its slower polar regions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18006_24-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18006-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Space_weather">
        Space weather
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Space weather">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first modern, and clearly described, accounts of a solar flare and coronal mass ejection occurred in 1859 and 1860 respectively. On 1 September 1859, Richard C. Carrington, while observing sunspots, saw patches of increasingly bright light within a group of sunspots, which then dimmed and moved across that area within a few minutes. This event, also reported by R. Hodgson, is a description of a solar flare. The widely viewed total solar eclipse on 18 July 1860 resulted in many drawings, depicting an anomalous feature that corresponds with modern CME observations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18007_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18007-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For many centuries, the earthly effects of solar variation were noticed but not understood. E.g., displays of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
        auroral
       </a>
       light have long been observed at high latitudes, but were not linked to the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1724,
       <a href="/wiki/George_Graham_(clockmaker)" title="George Graham (clockmaker)">
        George Graham
       </a>
       reported that the needle of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_compass" title="Magnetic compass">
        magnetic compass
       </a>
       was regularly deflected from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_north" title="Magnetic north">
        magnetic north
       </a>
       over the course of each day. This effect was eventually attributed to overhead electric currents flowing in the ionosphere and magnetosphere by
       <a href="/wiki/Balfour_Stewart" title="Balfour Stewart">
        Balfour Stewart
       </a>
       in 1882, and confirmed by
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Schuster" title="Arthur Schuster">
        Arthur Schuster
       </a>
       in 1889 from analysis of magnetic observatory data.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1852, astronomer and British major general
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Sabine" title="Edward Sabine">
        Edward Sabine
       </a>
       showed that the probability of the occurrence of magnetic storms on Earth was correlated with the number of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sunspots" title="Sunspots">
        sunspots
       </a>
       , thus demonstrating a novel solar-terrestrial interaction. In 1859, a great
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_storm_of_1859" title="Solar storm of 1859">
        magnetic storm
       </a>
       caused brilliant auroral displays and disrupted global
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telegraph" title="Telegraph">
        telegraph
       </a>
       operations.
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Christopher_Carrington" title="Richard Christopher Carrington">
        Richard Carrington
       </a>
       correctly connected the storm with a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flare
       </a>
       that he had observed the day before in the vicinity of a large sunspot group—thus demonstrating that specific solar events could affect the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Kristian_Birkeland" title="Kristian Birkeland">
        Kristian Birkeland
       </a>
       explained the physics of aurora by creating artificial aurora in his laboratory and predicted the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="20th_century">
        20th century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: 20th century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observatories">
        Observatories
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Observatories">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Early in the 20th century, interest in astrophysics grew in America, and multiple observatories were built.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_King_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-history_King-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         320
        </span>
       </sup>
       Solar telescopes (and thus, solar observatories), were installed at
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">
        Mount Wilson Observatory
       </a>
       in California in 1904,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-history_King_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-history_King-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        :
        <span>
         324
        </span>
       </sup>
       and in the 1930s at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/McMath-Hulbert_Observatory" title="McMath-Hulbert Observatory">
        McMath-Hulbert Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Interest also grew in other parts of the world, with the establishment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Kodaikanal_Solar_Observatory" title="Kodaikanal Solar Observatory">
        Kodaikanal Solar Observatory
       </a>
       in India at the turn of the century,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Einstein_Tower" title="Einstein Tower">
        Einsteinturm
       </a>
       in Germany in 1924,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the Solar Tower Telescope at the National Observatory of Japan in 1930.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Around 1900, researchers began to explore connections between solar variations and Earth's weather.
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory">
        Smithsonian Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       (SAO) assigned
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Greeley_Abbot" title="Charles Greeley Abbot">
        Abbot
       </a>
       and his team to detect changes in the radiation of the Sun. They began by inventing instruments to measure solar radiation. Later, when Abbot was SAO head, they established a solar station at
       <a href="/wiki/Calama,_Chile" title="Calama, Chile">
        Calama, Chile
       </a>
       to complement its data from
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory" title="Mount Wilson Observatory">
        Mount Wilson Observatory
       </a>
       . He detected 27 harmonic periods within the 273-month
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hale_cycle" title="Hale cycle">
        Hale cycles
       </a>
       , including 7, 13, and 39-month patterns. He looked for connections to weather by means such as matching opposing solar trends during a month to opposing urban temperature and precipitation trends. With the advent of
       <a href="/wiki/Dendrochronology" title="Dendrochronology">
        dendrochronology
       </a>
       , scientists such as Glock attempted to connect variation in tree growth to periodic solar variations and infer long-term secular variability in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_constant" title="Solar constant">
        solar constant
       </a>
       from similar variations in millennial-scale chronologies.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-frits_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-frits-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Coronagraph">
        Coronagraph
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Coronagraph">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Until the 1930s, little progress was made on understanding the Sun's corona, as it could only be viewed during infrequent total solar eclipses. Bernard Lyot's 1931 invention of the
       <a href="/wiki/Coronagraph" title="Coronagraph">
        Coronagraph
       </a>
       – a telescope with an attachment to block out the direct light of the solar disk – allowed the corona to be studied in full daylight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HAO_timeline_18007_21-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-HAO_timeline_18007-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectroheliograph">
        Spectroheliograph
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Spectroheliograph">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       American astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/George_Ellery_Hale" title="George Ellery Hale">
        George Ellery Hale
       </a>
       , as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MIT" title="MIT">
        MIT
       </a>
       undergraduate, invented the
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroheliograph" title="Spectroheliograph">
        spectroheliograph
       </a>
       , with which he made the discovery of solar
       <a href="/wiki/Vortex" title="Vortex">
        vortices
       </a>
       . In 1908, Hale used a modified spectroheliograph to show that the spectra of hydrogen exhibited the
       <a href="/wiki/Zeeman_effect" title="Zeeman effect">
        Zeeman effect
       </a>
       whenever the area of view passed over a sunspot on the solar disc. This was the first indication that sunspots were basically magnetic phenomena, which appeared in opposite polarity pairs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-doi10.1086/141602_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-doi10.1086/141602-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hale's subsequent work demonstrated a strong tendency for east-west alignment of magnetic polarities in sunspots, with mirror symmetry across the solar equator; and that the magnetic polarity for sunspots in each hemisphere switched orientation from one solar cycle to the next.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This systematic property of sunspot magnetic fields is now commonly referred to as the "Hale–Nicholson law",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or in many cases simply "Hale's laws".
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_radio_bursts">
        Solar radio bursts
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Solar radio bursts">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The introduction of radio revealed periods of extreme static or noise. Severe
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_jamming" title="Radar jamming">
        radar jamming
       </a>
       during a large solar event in 1942 led to the discovery of solar radio bursts (radio waves that cover a broad waveband created by a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellites">
        Satellites
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Satellites">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Many satellites in Earth orbit or in the heliosphere have deployed solar telescopes and instruments of various kinds for
       <i>
        in situ
       </i>
       measurements of particles and fields.
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       , a notable large solar observational facility, grew out if the impetus of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Geophysical_Year" title="International Geophysical Year">
        International Geophysical Year
       </a>
       campaign and the facilities of
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       . Other spacecraft, in an incomplete list, have included the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbiting_Solar_Observatory" title="Orbiting Solar Observatory">
        OSO
       </a>
       series, the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission" title="Solar Maximum Mission">
        Solar Maximum Mission
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Yohkoh" title="Yohkoh">
        Yohkoh
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_and_Heliospheric_Observatory" title="Solar and Heliospheric Observatory">
        SOHO
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Advanced_Composition_Explorer" title="Advanced Composition Explorer">
        ACE
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
        TRACE
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Dynamics_Observatory" title="Solar Dynamics Observatory">
        SDO
       </a>
       among many others; still other spacecraft (such as
       <a href="/wiki/MESSENGER" title="MESSENGER">
        MESSENGER
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fermi_Gamma-ray_Space_Telescope" title="Fermi Gamma-ray Space Telescope">
        Fermi
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/NuSTAR" title="NuSTAR">
        NuSTAR
       </a>
       ) have contributed solar measurements by individual instruments.
      </p>
      <p>
       Modulation of solar bolometric radiation by magnetically active regions, and more subtle effects, was confirmed by satellite measurements of the total solar irradiance (TSI) by the ACRIM1 experiment on the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_Maximum_Mission" title="Solar Maximum Mission">
        Solar Maximum Mission
       </a>
       (launched in 1980).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Willson81_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Willson81-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The modulations were later confirmed in the results of the ERB experiment launched on the
       <a href="/wiki/Nimbus_program" title="Nimbus program">
        Nimbus 7
       </a>
       satellite in 1978.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Satellite observation was continued by
       <a href="/wiki/ACRIMSAT" title="ACRIMSAT">
        ACRIM-3
       </a>
       and other satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACRIM_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ACRIM-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Measurement_proxies">
        Measurement proxies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Measurement proxies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Direct irradiance measurements have been available during the last three cycles and are a composite of multiple observing satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ACRIM_36-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ACRIM-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the correlation between irradiance measurements and other proxies of solar activity make it reasonable to estimate solar activity for earlier cycles. Most important among these proxies is the record of sunspot observations that has been recorded since ~1610. Solar radio emissions at 10.7 cm wavelength provide another proxy that can be measured from the ground, since the atmosphere is transparent to such radiation.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other proxy data – such as the abundance of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmogenic" title="Cosmogenic">
        cosmogenic
       </a>
       isotopes – have been used to infer solar magnetic activity and thus likely brightness.
      </p>
      <p>
       Total solar irradiance has been claimed to vary in ways that are not predicted by sunspot changes or radio emissions. These shifts may be the result of inaccurate satellite calibration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
        <a href="#cite_note-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A long-term trend may exist in solar irradiance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
        <a href="#cite_note-40">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_developments">
        Other developments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Other developments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Sun was, until the 1990s, the only star whose surface had been resolved.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BURNS_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BURNS-41">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other major achievements included understanding of:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ground-based_Solar_Research_42-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ground-based_Solar_Research-42">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        X-ray-emitting loops
       </li>
       <li>
        Corona and solar wind
       </li>
       <li>
        Variance of solar brightness with level of activity and verification of this effect in other solar-type stars
       </li>
       <li>
        The intense
        <a href="/wiki/Fibril" title="Fibril">
         Fibril
        </a>
        state of the magnetic fields at the visible surface of a star like the sun
       </li>
       <li>
        The presence of magnetic fields of 0.5×10
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        to 1×10
        <sup>
         5
        </sup>
        gauss at the base of the conductive zone, presumably in some fibril form, inferred from the dynamics of rising azimuthal flux bundles.
       </li>
       <li>
        Low-level
        <a href="/wiki/Electron_neutrino" title="Electron neutrino">
         Electron neutrino
        </a>
        emission from the Sun's core.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ground-based_Solar_Research_42-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-Ground-based_Solar_Research-42">
          [42]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="21st_century">
        21st century
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Solar_observation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: 21st century">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most powerful flare observed by satellite instrumentation began on 4 November 2003 at 19:29 UTC, and saturated instruments for 11 minutes. Region 486 has been estimated to have produced an X-ray flux of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare#Classification_of_flares" title="Solar flare">
        X28
       </a>
       . Holographic and visual observations indicate significant activity continued on the far side of the Sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sunspot and infrared spectral line measurements made in the latter part of the first decade of the 2000s suggested that sunspot activity may again be disappearing, possibly leading to a new minimum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-test_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-test-43">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       From 2007 to 2009, sunspot levels were far below average. In 2008, the Sun was spot-free 73 percent of the time, extreme even for a solar minimum. Only 1913 was more pronounced, with no sunspots for 85 percent of that year. The Sun continued to languish through mid-December 2009, when the largest group of sunspots to emerge for several years appeared. Even then, sunspot levels remained well below those of recent cycles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Predictions3_strip.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="500" height="110" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Predictions3_strip.jpg/220px-Predictions3_strip.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Predictions3_strip.jpg/330px-Predictions3_strip.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/Predictions3_strip.jpg/440px-Predictions3_strip.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Predictions3_strip.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Nasa's 2006 prediction. At 2010/2011, the sunspot count was expected to be at its maximum, but in reality in 2010 it was still at its minimum.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In 2006, NASA predicted that the next sunspot maximum would reach between 150 and 200 around the year 2011 (30–50% stronger than cycle 23), followed by a weak maximum at around 2022.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
        <a href="#cite_note-45">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
        <a href="#cite_note-46">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Instead, the sunspot cycle in 2010 was still at its minimum, when it should have been near its maximum, demonstrating its unusual weakness.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [47]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cycle 24's minimum occurred around December 2008 and the next maximum was predicted to reach a sunspot number of 90 around May 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
        <a href="#cite_note-48">
         [48]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The monthly mean sunspot number in the northern solar hemisphere peaked in November 2011, while the southern hemisphere appears to have peaked in February 2014, reaching a peak monthly mean of 102. Subsequent months declined to around 70 (June 2014).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
        <a href="#cite_note-49">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In October 2014, sunspot AR 12192 became the largest observed since 1990.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The flare that erupted from this sunspot was classified as an X3.1-class solar storm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [51]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Independent scientists of the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Solar_Observatory" title="National Solar Observatory">
        National Solar Observatory
       </a>
       (NSO) and the
       <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Research_Laboratory" title="Air Force Research Laboratory">
        Air Force Research Laboratory
       </a>
       (AFRL) predicted in 2011 that Cycle 25 would be greatly reduced or might not happen at all.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52">
        <a href="#cite_note-52">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
