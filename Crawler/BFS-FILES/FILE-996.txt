      <p>
       A
       <b>
        metric prefix
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Unit_prefix" title="Unit prefix">
        unit prefix
       </a>
       that precedes a basic unit of measure to indicate a
       <a href="/wiki/Multiple_(mathematics)" title="Multiple (mathematics)">
        multiple
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics)" title="Fraction (mathematics)">
        fraction
       </a>
       of the unit. While all metric prefixes in common use today are
       <a href="/wiki/Decimal" title="Decimal">
        decadic
       </a>
       , historically there have been a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_number" title="Binary number">
        binary
       </a>
       metric prefixes as well.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fr_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fr-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Each prefix has a unique symbol that is prepended to the unit symbol. The prefix
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kilo-" title="Kilo-">
         kilo-
        </a>
       </i>
       , for example, may be added to
       <i>
        gram
       </i>
       to indicate
       <i>
        multiplication
       </i>
       by one thousand: one kilogram is equal to one thousand grams. The prefix
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Milli-" title="Milli-">
         milli-
        </a>
       </i>
       , likewise, may be added to
       <i>
        metre
       </i>
       to indicate
       <i>
        division
       </i>
       by one thousand; one millimetre is equal to one thousandth of a metre.
      </p>
      <p>
       Decimal multiplicative prefixes have been a feature of all forms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">
        metric system
       </a>
       , with six dating back to the system's introduction in the 1790s. Metric prefixes have even been prepended to non-metric units. The
       <b>
        SI prefixes
       </b>
       are standardized for use in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI) by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Bureau_of_Weights_and_Measures" title="International Bureau of Weights and Measures">
        International Bureau of Weights and Measures
       </a>
       (BIPM) in resolutions dating from 1960 to 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since 2009, they have formed part of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities" title="International System of Quantities">
        International System of Quantities
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       Each prefix name has a symbol that is used in combination with the symbols for units of measure. For example, the symbol for
       <i>
        kilo-
       </i>
       is 'k', and is used to produce 'km', 'kg', and 'kW', which are the SI symbols for kilometre, kilogram, and kilowatt, respectively. Where Greek letters are unavailable, the symbol for micro 'µ' is commonly replaced by 'u'.
      </p>
      <p>
       Prefixes corresponding to an integer power of one thousand are generally preferred. Hence
       <i>
        100 m
       </i>
       is preferred over
       <i>
        1 hm
       </i>
       (hectometre) or
       <i>
        10 dam
       </i>
       (decametres). The prefixes hecto, deca, deci, and centi are commonly used for everyday purposes, and the centimetre (cm) is especially common. However, some modern building codes require that the millimetre be used in preference to the centimetre, because "use of centimetres leads to extensive usage of decimal points and confusion".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Prefixes may not be used in combination. This also applies to
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       , for which the
       <a href="/wiki/SI_base_unit" title="SI base unit">
        SI base unit
       </a>
       (kilogram) already contains a prefix. For example, milligram (mg) is used instead of microkilogram (µkg).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Application_to_units_of_measurement">
        Application to units of measurement
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Application to units of measurement">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The use of prefixes can be traced back to the introduction of the metric system in the 1790s, long before the 1960 introduction of the SI. The prefixes, including those introduced after 1960, are used with any metric unit, whether officially included in the SI or not (e.g., millidynes and milligauss). Metric prefixes may also be used with non-metric units.
      </p>
      <p>
       The choice of prefixes with a given unit is usually dictated by convenience of use. Unit prefixes for amounts that are much larger or smaller than those actually encountered are seldom used.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metric_units">
        Metric units
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Metric units">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass">
        Mass
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mass">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In use, the
       <a href="/wiki/Kilogram" title="Kilogram">
        kilogram
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Gram" title="Gram">
        gram
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Milligram" title="Milligram">
        milligram
       </a>
       , microgram, and smaller are fairly common. However, megagram (and gigagram, teragram, etc.) are rarely used;
       <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">
        tonnes
       </a>
       (and kilotonnes, megatonnes, etc – although these units generally are not used as a measure of
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       <i>
        per se
       </i>
       , but rather
       <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">
        TNT energy equivalent of a mass
       </a>
       ) or
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_notation" title="Scientific notation">
        scientific notation
       </a>
       are used instead. Megagram is occasionally used to disambiguate the metric tonne from the various non-metric tons. An exception is pollution emission rates, which are typically on the order of Tg/yr. Sometimes, only one element or compound is denoted for an emission, such as Tg C/yr or Tg N/yr.
      </p>
      <p>
       Alone among SI units, the base unit of mass, the kilogram, already includes a prefix. The prefixes consequently do not indicate corresponding multipliers of the base unit in the case of mass; for example, a megagram is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7003100000000000000♠
        </span>
        1
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       kg, whereas
       <i>
        mega-
       </i>
       indicates a multiplier of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         6
        </sup>
       </span>
       .
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Volume">
        Volume
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Volume">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The litre (equal to a cubic decimetre), millilitre (equal to a cubic centimetre), microlitre, and smaller are common. In Europe, the centilitre is often used for packaged products (such as wine) and the decilitre less frequently. (The latter two items include prefixes corresponding to an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exponent" title="Exponent">
        exponent
       </a>
       that is not divisible by three.)
      </p>
      <p>
       Larger volumes are usually denoted in kilolitres, megalitres or gigalitres, or else in cubic metres (1 cubic metre = 1 kilolitre) or cubic kilometres (1 cubic kilometre = 1 teralitre). For scientific purposes, the cubic metre is usually used.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Length">
        Length
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Length">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The kilometre, metre, centimetre, millimetre, and smaller are common. (However, the decimetre is rarely used.) The micrometre is often referred to by the non-SI term
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Micrometre#SI_standardization" title="Micrometre">
         micron
        </a>
       </i>
       . In some fields, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Chemistry" title="Chemistry">
        chemistry
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m" title="Ångström">
        ångström
       </a>
       (equal to 0.1 nm) historically competed with the nanometre. The
       <a href="/wiki/Femtometre" title="Femtometre">
        femtometre
       </a>
       , used mainly in particle physics, is sometimes called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fermi_(unit)" title="Fermi (unit)">
        fermi
       </a>
       . For large scales, megametre, gigametre, and larger are rarely used. Instead, non-metric units are used, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_unit" title="Astronomical unit">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Light_year" title="Light year">
        light years
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Parsec" title="Parsec">
        parsecs
       </a>
       ; the astronomical unit is mentioned in the SI standards as an accepted non-SI unit.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Time_and_angles">
        Time and angles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Time and angles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       The second, millisecond, microsecond, and shorter are common. The kilosecond and megasecond also have some use, though for these and longer times one usually uses either scientific notation or minutes, hours, and so on.
      </p>
      <p>
       Official policies about the use of these prefixes vary slightly between the Bureau International des Poids et Mesures (BIPM) and the American
       <a href="/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology" title="National Institute of Standards and Technology">
        National Institute of Standards and Technology
       </a>
       (NIST); and some of the policies of both bodies are at variance with everyday practice. For instance, the NIST advises that "to avoid confusion, prefix symbols (and prefixes) are not used with the time-related unit symbols (names) min (minute), h (hour), d (day); nor with the angle-related symbols (names) ° (degree),
       <b>
        ′
       </b>
       (minute), and
       <b>
        ″
       </b>
       (second)."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The BIPM’s position on the use of SI prefixes with units of time larger than the second is the same as that of the NIST, but their position with regard to angles differs: they state "However astronomers use milliarcsecond, which they denote mas, and microarcsecond, µas, which they use as units for measuring very small angles."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The SI unit of angle is the
       <a href="/wiki/Radian" title="Radian">
        radian
       </a>
       , but, as mentioned above, degrees, minutes and seconds see some scientific use.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Temperature">
        Temperature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Temperature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       Official policy also varies from common practice for the degree Celsius (°C). NIST states:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Prefix symbols may be used with the unit symbol °C and prefixes may be used with the unit name 'degree Celsius'. For example, 12 m°C (12 millidegrees Celsius) is acceptable." In practice, it is more common for prefixes to be used with the
       <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
        kelvin
       </a>
       when it is desirable to denote extremely large or small absolute temperatures or temperature differences. Thus, temperatures of star interiors may be given in units of MK (megakelvins), and molecular cooling may be described in mK (millikelvins).
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Energy">
        Energy
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Energy">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In use the
       <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">
        joule
       </a>
       and kilojoule are common, with larger multiples seen in limited contexts. In addition, the
       <a href="/wiki/Kilowatt_hour" title="Kilowatt hour">
        kilowatt hour
       </a>
       , a composite unit formed from the kilowatt and hour, is often used for electrical energy; other multiples can be formed by modifying the prefix of watt (e.g. terawatt hour).
      </p>
      <p>
       There exist a number of definitions for the non-SI unit, the
       <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">
        calorie
       </a>
       . There are gram calories and kilogram calories. One kilogram calorie, which equals one thousand gram calories, often appears capitalized and without a prefix (i.e. 'Cal') when referring to "
       <a href="/wiki/Calorie" title="Calorie">
        dietary calories
       </a>
       " in food.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is common to apply metric prefixes to the gram calorie, but not to the kilogram calorie: thus, 1 kcal = 1000 cal = 1 Cal.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Non-metric_units">
        Non-metric units
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Non-metric units">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Metric prefixes are widely used outside the system of
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_system" title="Metric system">
        metric units
       </a>
       . Common examples include the
       <a href="/wiki/Megabyte" title="Megabyte">
        megabyte
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Decibel" title="Decibel">
        decibel
       </a>
       . Metric prefixes rarely appear with
       <a href="/wiki/Imperial_units" title="Imperial units">
        imperial
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_customary_units" title="United States customary units">
        US
       </a>
       units except in some special cases (e.g., microinch, kilofoot,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kilopound" title="Kilopound">
        kilopound
       </a>
       or 'kip'). They are also used with other specialized units used in particular fields (e.g.,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaelectronvolt" title="Megaelectronvolt">
        megaelectronvolt
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigaparsec" title="Gigaparsec">
        gigaparsec
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millibarn" title="Millibarn">
        millibarn
       </a>
       ). They are also occasionally used with currency units (e.g., gigadollar), mainly by people who are familiar with the prefixes from scientific usage. In geology and paleontology, the
       <a href="/wiki/Year#Symbols" title="Year">
        year
       </a>
       , with symbol a (from the Latin
       <i>
        annus
       </i>
       ), is commonly used with metric prefixes:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kiloannus" title="Kiloannus">
        ka
       </a>
       , Ma, and Ga.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Obsolete_metric_prefixes">
        <span id="myria">
        </span>
        <span id="myrio">
        </span>
        <span id="hebdo">
        </span>
        <span id="micri">
        </span>
        <span id="double">
        </span>
        <span id="demi">
        </span>
        <span id="Obsolete">
        </span>
        Obsolete metric prefixes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Obsolete metric prefixes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some of the prefixes formerly used in the metric system have fallen into disuse and were not adopted into the SI.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewster_1830_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brewster_1830-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brewster_1832_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brewster_1832-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The decimal prefix
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Myria-" title="Myria-">
         myria-
        </a>
       </i>
       (sometimes also written as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Myrio-" title="Myrio-">
         myrio-
        </a>
       </i>
       ) (ten thousand) as well as the
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_prefix" title="Binary prefix">
        binary prefixes
       </a>
       <i>
        double-
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        demi-
       </i>
       , denoting a factor of 2 and
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         1
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         2
        </span>
       </span>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/One_half" title="One half">
        one half
       </a>
       ), respectively, were parts of the original metric system adopted by France in 1795.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fr_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-fr-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These were not retained when the SI prefixes were internationally adopted by the 11th
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conf%C3%A9rence_g%C3%A9n%C3%A9rale_des_poids_et_mesures" title="Conférence générale des poids et mesures">
        CGPM conference
       </a>
       in 1960.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other metric prefixes used historically include
       <a href="/wiki/Hebdo-" title="Hebdo-">
        hebdo-
       </a>
       (10
       <sup>
        7
       </sup>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Micri-" title="Micri-">
        micri-
       </a>
       (10
       <sup>
        −14
       </sup>
       ).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Double_prefixes">
        <span id="dimi">
        </span>
        <span id="Double_prefix">
        </span>
        Double prefixes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Double prefixes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Double prefixes have been used in the past, such as
       <i>
        micromillimetres
       </i>
       or "millimicrons" (now
       <a href="/wiki/Nanometre" title="Nanometre">
        nanometres
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        micromicrofarads
       </i>
       (now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Picofarad" title="Picofarad">
        picofarads
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        kilomegatons
       </i>
       (now
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigaton" title="Gigaton">
        gigatons
       </a>
       ),
       <i>
        hectokilometres
       </i>
       (now 100
       <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
        kilometres
       </a>
       ) and the derived adjective
       <i>
        hectokilometric
       </i>
       (typically used for qualifying the fuel consumption measures).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rowlett_2008_Millimicro_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rowlett_2008_Millimicro-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These were disallowed with the introduction of the SI.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other obsolete double prefixes included "decimilli-" (10
       <sup>
        −4
       </sup>
       ), which was contracted to "dimi-"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and standardized in France up to 1961.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id=".22Hella.22_prefix_proposal">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"Hella"_prefix_proposal'>
        "Hella" prefix proposal
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title='Edit section: "Hella" prefix proposal'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 2010,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Davis" title="UC Davis">
        UC Davis
       </a>
       student Austin Sendek started a petition to designate "
       <a href="/wiki/Hella" title="Hella">
        hella
       </a>
       " as the SI prefix for one octillion (10
       <sup>
        27
       </sup>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The petition gathered over 60,000 supporters by circulating through
       <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">
        Facebook
       </a>
       and receiving a significant amount of media coverage.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although the Consultative Committee for Units considered the proposal, it was rejected. However,
       <i>
        hella
       </i>
       has been adopted by certain websites, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Google" title="Google">
        Google
       </a>
       Calculator
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Wolfram_Alpha" title="Wolfram Alpha">
        Wolfram Alpha
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="X.2C_W_and_V">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="X,_W_and_V">
        X, W and V
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: X, W and V">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Brian C. Lacki
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       follows Z and Y with the adopted prefixes X, W and V to mean
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7027100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         27
        </sup>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7030100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         30
        </sup>
       </span>
       and
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7033099999999999999♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         33
        </sup>
       </span>
       respectively, thus continuing the inverse alphabetical order.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_symbols_and_abbreviations">
        Similar symbols and abbreviations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Similar symbols and abbreviations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In written English, the symbol
       <i>
        K
       </i>
       is often used informally to indicate a multiple of thousand in many contexts. For example, one may talk of a
       <i>
        40K salary
       </i>
       (
       <span style="white-space:nowrap">
        40
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em">
         000
        </span>
       </span>
       ), or call the
       <a href="/wiki/Year_2000_problem" title="Year 2000 problem">
        Year 2000 problem
       </a>
       the
       <i>
        Y2K problem
       </i>
       . In these cases, an uppercase K is often used with an implied unit (although it could then be confused with the symbol for the kelvin temperature unit if the context is unclear). This informal postfix is read or spoken as "thousand" or "grand", or just "k", but never "kilo" (despite that being the origin of the letter).
      </p>
      <p>
       The financial and general news media mostly use m/M, b/B and t/T as abbreviations for million, billion (10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       ) and trillion (10
       <sup>
        12
       </sup>
       ) for large quantities, typically currency
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and population.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Medicine" title="Medicine">
        medical
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Automotive_industry" title="Automotive industry">
        automotive
       </a>
       fields in the United States use the abbreviations "cc" or "ccm" for cubic centimetres. 1
       <a href="/wiki/Cubic_centimetre" title="Cubic centimetre">
        cubic centimetre
       </a>
       is equivalent to 1
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millilitre" title="Millilitre">
        millilitre
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For nearly a century, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical" title="Electrical">
        electrical
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Construction_industry" title="Construction industry">
        construction industry
       </a>
       used the abbreviation "MCM" to designate a "thousand
       <a href="/wiki/Circular_mil" title="Circular mil">
        circular mils
       </a>
       " in specifying thicknesses of large
       <a href="/wiki/Electric_power_transmission" title="Electric power transmission">
        electrical cables
       </a>
       . Since the mid-1990s, "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kcmil" title="Kcmil">
        kcmil
       </a>
       " has been adopted as the "official" designation of a thousand circular mils, but the designation "MCM" still remains in wide use. A similar system is used in natural gas sales in the United States: m (or M) for thousands and mm (or MM) for millions of
       <a href="/wiki/British_thermal_unit" title="British thermal unit">
        British thermal units
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Therm" title="Therm">
        therms
       </a>
       , and in the oil industry,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where 'MMbbl' is the symbol for 'millions of barrels'. This usage of the capital letter M for 'thousand' is from
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">
        Roman numerals
       </a>
       , in which M means 1,000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Binary_prefixes">
        Binary prefixes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Metric_prefix&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Binary prefixes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In some fields of information technology, it has been common to designate non-decimal multiples based on powers of 1024, rather than 1000, for some SI prefixes (kilo, mega, giga), contrary to the definitions in the
       <a href="/wiki/International_System_of_Units" title="International System of Units">
        International System of Units
       </a>
       (SI). This practice has been sanctioned by some industry associations, including
       <a href="/wiki/JEDEC" title="JEDEC">
        JEDEC
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Electrotechnical_Commission" title="International Electrotechnical Commission">
        International Electrotechnical Commission
       </a>
       (IEC) standardized the system of
       <a href="/wiki/Binary_prefix" title="Binary prefix">
        binary prefixes
       </a>
       (kibi, mebi, gibi, etc.) for this purpose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [Note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
