      <p>
       The
       <b>
        heliospheric current sheet
       </b>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is the surface within the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       where the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">
        polarity
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       changes from north to south. This field extends throughout the Sun's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_plane" title="Equatorial plane">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The shape of the
       <a href="/wiki/Current_sheet" title="Current sheet">
        current sheet
       </a>
       results from the influence of the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Rotating_magnetic_field" title="Rotating magnetic field">
        rotating magnetic field
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_medium" title="Interplanetary medium">
        interplanetary medium
       </a>
       (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Wind" title="Solar Wind">
        Solar Wind
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A small
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrical_current" title="Electrical current">
        electrical current
       </a>
       flows within the sheet, about 10
       <sup>
        −10
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">
        A
       </a>
       /m². The thickness of the current sheet is about 10,000 km near the orbit of the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The underlying magnetic field is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_magnetic_field" title="Interplanetary magnetic field">
        interplanetary magnetic field
       </a>
       , and the resulting electric current forms part of the heliospheric current circuit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israelevich2001_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-israelevich2001-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The heliospheric current sheet is also sometimes called the
       <i>
        interplanetary current sheet
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Characteristics">
        Characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliospheric_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Ballerina.27s_skirt_shape">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ballerina's_skirt_shape">
        Ballerina's skirt shape
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliospheric_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ballerina's skirt shape">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Parker_spiral.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="268" data-file-width="360" height="223" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Parker_spiral.gif/300px-Parker_spiral.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c4/Parker_spiral.gif 1.5x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Parker_spiral.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Parker Spiral
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       As the Sun rotates, its magnetic field twists into a Parker spiral, a form of an
       <a href="/wiki/Archimedean_spiral" title="Archimedean spiral">
        Archimedean spiral
       </a>
       , as it extends through the solar system. This phenomenon is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">
        Eugene Parker
       </a>
       's work:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parker1958_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-parker1958-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       he predicted the solar wind and many of its associated phenomena in the 1950s. The spiral nature of the heliospheric magnetic field had been noted earlier by
       <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">
        Hannes Alfvén
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       based on the structure of comet tails.
      </p>
      <p>
       The influence of this spiral-shaped magnetic field on the
       <a href="/wiki/Interplanetary_medium" title="Interplanetary medium">
        interplanetary medium
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       ) creates the largest structure in the Solar System, the heliospheric current sheet. Parker's spiral magnetic field was divided in two by a
       <a href="/wiki/Current_sheet" title="Current sheet">
        current sheet
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       a mathematical model first developed in the early 1970s by Schatten. It warps into a wavy spiral shape that has been likened to a ballerina's skirt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rosenberg1969_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rosenberg1969-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilcox1980_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wilcox1980-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The waviness of the current sheet is because of the magnetic field dipole axis' tilt angle to the solar rotation axis and variations from an ideal dipole field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Unlike the familiar shape of the field from a bar
       <a href="/wiki/Magnet" title="Magnet">
        magnet
       </a>
       , the Sun's extended field is twisted into an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arithmetic_spiral" title="Arithmetic spiral">
        arithmetic spiral
       </a>
       by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic" title="Magnetohydrodynamic">
        magnetohydrodynamic
       </a>
       influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       travels outward from the Sun at a uniform rate, but an individual jet of solar wind from a particular feature on the Sun's surface rotates with the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">
        solar rotation
       </a>
       , making a spiral pattern in space. Unlike the jet from a sprinkler, the solar wind is tied to the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        MHD
       </a>
       effects, so that magnetic field lines are tied to the material in the jet and take on an arithmetic spiral shape. The cause of the ballerina spiral shape has sometimes been called the "garden sprinkler effect" or "garden hose effect",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Louise_K._Harra_2004_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Louise_K._Harra_2004-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-trs-new.jpl.nasa.gov_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-trs-new.jpl.nasa.gov-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       because it is likened to a
       <a href="/wiki/Irrigation_sprinkler" title="Irrigation sprinkler">
        lawn sprinkler
       </a>
       with nozzle that moves up and down while it spins. The stream of water represents the solar wind.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Parker spiral shape of the solar wind changes the shape of the Sun's magnetic field in the outer solar system: beyond about 10-20
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_units" title="Astronomical units">
        astronomical units
       </a>
       from the Sun, the magnetic field is nearly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Toroid_(geometry)" title="Toroid (geometry)">
        toroidal
       </a>
       (pointed around the equator of the Sun) rather than
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Poloidal" title="Poloidal">
        poloidal
       </a>
       (pointed from the North to the South pole, as in a bar magnet) or
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/radial" title="wikt:radial">
        radial
       </a>
       (pointed outward or inward, as might be expected from the flow of the solar wind if the Sun were not rotating). The spiral shape also greatly amplifies the strength of the solar magnetic field in the outer solar system.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Parker spiral may be responsible for the differential
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_rotation" title="Solar rotation">
        solar rotation
       </a>
       , in which the Sun's poles rotate more slowly (about a 35-day rotation period) than the equator (about a 27-day rotation period). The solar wind is guided by the Sun's magnetic field and hence largely emanates from the polar regions of the Sun; the induced spiral shape of the field causes a drag torque on the poles due to the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tension_force" title="Magnetic tension force">
        magnetic tension
       </a>
       force.
      </p>
      <p>
       During
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_maximum" title="Solar maximum">
        solar maximum
       </a>
       the entire magnetic field of the sun flips, thus alternating the polarity of the field every solar cycle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasaSolarmag_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasaSolarmag-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Magnetic_field">
        Magnetic field
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliospheric_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Magnetic field">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The heliospheric current sheet rotates along with the Sun with a period of about 25 days, during which time the peaks and troughs of the skirt pass through the Earth's magnetosphere, interacting with it. Near the surface of the Sun, the magnetic field produced by the radial electric current in the sheet is of the order of 5×10
       <sup>
        −6
       </sup>
       T.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israelevich2001_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-israelevich2001-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The magnetic field at the surface of the Sun is about 10
       <sup>
        −4
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Tesla_(unit)" title="Tesla (unit)">
        teslas
       </a>
       . If the form of the field were a
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_dipole" title="Magnetic dipole">
        magnetic dipole
       </a>
       , the strength would decrease with the cube of the distance, resulting in about 10
       <sup>
        −11
       </sup>
       teslas at the Earth's orbit. The heliospheric current sheet results in higher order multipole components so that the actual magnetic field at the Earth due to the Sun is 100 times greater.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Electric_current">
        Electric current
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliospheric_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Electric current">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The electric current in the heliospheric current sheet has a radial component (directed inward) as well as an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Azimuthal" title="Azimuthal">
        azimuthal
       </a>
       component, the radial circuit being closed by outward currents aligned with the Sun's magnetic field in the solar polar regions. The radial current in the circuit is on the order of 3×10
       <sup>
        9
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Ampere" title="Ampere">
        amperes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-israelevich2001_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-israelevich2001-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a comparison with other astrophysical electric currents, the
       <a href="/wiki/Birkeland_current" title="Birkeland current">
        Birkeland currents
       </a>
       that supply the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
        aurora
       </a>
       are about a thousand times weaker at a million amperes. The maximum current density in the sheet is on the order of 10
       <sup>
        −10
       </sup>
       A/m² (10
       <sup>
        −4
       </sup>
       A/km²).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Heliospheric_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The heliospheric current sheet was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/John_M._Wilcox" title="John M. Wilcox">
        John M. Wilcox
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Norman_F._Ness" title="Norman F. Ness">
        Norman F. Ness
       </a>
       , who published their finding in 1965.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wilcox1965_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wilcox1965-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Hannes_Alfv%C3%A9n" title="Hannes Alfvén">
        Hannes Alfvén
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Per_Carlqvist" title="Per Carlqvist">
        Per Carlqvist
       </a>
       speculate on the existence of a
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Galactic_current_sheet&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Galactic current sheet (page does not exist)">
        galactic current sheet
       </a>
       , a counterpart of the heliospheric current sheet, with an estimated galactic current of 10
       <sup>
        17
       </sup>
       to 10
       <sup>
        19
       </sup>
       amperes, that might flow in the plane of symmetry of the galaxy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-alfven1978_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-alfven1978-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
