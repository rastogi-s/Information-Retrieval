   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Transient lunar phenomenon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        transient lunar phenomenon
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        TLP
       </b>
       ) or
       <b>
        lunar transient phenomenon
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        LTP
       </b>
       ) is a short-lived light, color, or change in appearance on the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Claims of short-lived lunar phenomena go back at least 1,000 years, with some having been observed independently by multiple witnesses or reputable scientists. Nevertheless, the majority of transient lunar phenomenon reports are
       <a href="/wiki/Reproducibility" title="Reproducibility">
        irreproducible
       </a>
       and do not possess adequate
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Experimental_control" title="Experimental control">
        control experiments
       </a>
       that could be used to distinguish among alternative
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypotheses" title="Hypotheses">
        hypotheses
       </a>
       to explain their origins.
      </p>
      <p>
       Most lunar scientists will acknowledge that transient events such as
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impact_craters" title="Impact craters">
        impact cratering
       </a>
       do occur over
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_geologic_timescale" title="Lunar geologic timescale">
        geologic time
       </a>
       : the controversy lies in the frequency of such events.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term was created by
       <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Moore" title="Patrick Moore">
        Patrick Moore
       </a>
       in his co-authorship of NASA Technical Report R-277
       <i>
        Chronological Catalog of Reported Lunar Events
       </i>
       , published in 1968.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description_of_events">
        Description of events
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description of events">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Reports of transient lunar phenomena range from foggy patches to permanent changes of the lunar landscape. Cameron
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c1_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-c1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       classifies these as (1) gaseous, involving mists and other forms of obscuration, (2) reddish colorations, (3) green, blue or violet colorations, (4) brightenings, and (5) darkening. Two extensive catalogs of transient lunar phenomena exist,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-c1_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-c1-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with the most recent tallying 2,254 events going back to the 6th century. Of the most reliable of these events, at least one-third come from the vicinity of the
       <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater)" title="Aristarchus (crater)">
        Aristarchus plateau
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A few of the more famous historical accounts of transient phenomena include the following:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        On June 18, 1178, five or more monks from
        <a href="/wiki/Canterbury" title="Canterbury">
         Canterbury
        </a>
        reported an upheaval on the Moon shortly after sunset. "There was a bright new moon, and as usual in that phase its horns were tilted toward the east; and suddenly the upper horn split in two. From the midpoint of this division a flaming torch sprang up, spewing out, over a considerable distance, fire, hot coals, and sparks. Meanwhile the body of the moon which was below writhed, as it were, in anxiety, and, to put it in the words of those who reported it to me and saw it with their own eyes, the moon throbbed like a wounded snake. Afterwards it resumed its proper state. This phenomenon was repeated a dozen times or more, the flame assuming various twisting shapes at random and then returning to normal. Then after these transformations the moon from horn to horn, that is along its whole length, took on a blackish appearance." This description appears outlandish perhaps due to lacking public understanding of astronomical phenomena.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In 1976, Jack Hartung proposed that this described the formation of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giordano_Bruno_crater" title="Giordano Bruno crater">
         Giordano Bruno crater
        </a>
        . However, more recent studies suggest that it appears very unlikely the 1178 event was related to the formation of Crater Giordano Bruno, or was even a true transient lunar phenomenon at all. The millions of tons of lunar debris ejected from an impact large enough to leave a 22-km-wide crater would have resulted in an unprecedentedly intense, week-long
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor_storm" title="Meteor storm">
         meteor storm
        </a>
        on Earth. No accounts of such a memorable storm have been found in any known historical records, including several astronomical archives from around the world.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        In light of this, it is suspected that the group of monks (the event's only known witnesses) saw the atmospheric explosion of a directly oncoming meteor in chance alignment, from their specific vantage point, with the far more distant moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        During the night of April 19, 1787, the British astronomer Sir
        <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
         William Herschel
        </a>
        noticed three red glowing spots on the dark part of the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        He informed King
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/George_III" title="George III">
         George III
        </a>
        and other astronomers of his observations. Herschel attributed the phenomena to erupting volcanoes and perceived the luminosity of the brightest of the three as greater than the brightness of a comet that had been discovered on April 10. His observations were made while an
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_borealis" title="Aurora borealis">
         aurora borealis
        </a>
        (northern lights) rippled above
        <a href="/wiki/Padua" title="Padua">
         Padua
        </a>
        , Italy.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Aurora activity that far south from the Arctic Circle was very rare. Padua's display and Herschel's observations had happened a few days before the number of
        <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
         sunspots
        </a>
        had peaked in May 1787.
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1866, the experienced lunar observer and mapmaker
        <a href="/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Julius_Schmidt" title="Johann Friedrich Julius Schmidt">
         J. F. Julius Schmidt
        </a>
        claimed that the
        <a href="/wiki/Linn%C3%A9_(crater)" title="Linné (crater)">
         Linné crater
        </a>
        had changed its appearance. Based on drawings made earlier by
        <a href="/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter" title="Johann Hieronymus Schröter">
         J. H. Schröter
        </a>
        , as well as personal observations and drawings made between 1841 and 1843, he stated that the crater "at the time of oblique illumination
        <i>
         cannot at all be seen
        </i>
        "
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
         <a href="#cite_note-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        (his emphasis), whereas at high illumination, it was visible as a bright spot. Based on repeat observations, he further stated that "Linné can never be seen under any illumination as a crater of the normal type" and that "a local change has taken place". Today, Linné is visible as a normal young impact crater with a diameter of about 1.5 miles (2.4 km).
       </li>
       <li>
        On November 2, 1958, the Russian astronomer
        <a href="/wiki/Nikolai_Aleksandrovich_Kozyrev" title="Nikolai Aleksandrovich Kozyrev">
         Nikolai A. Kozyrev
        </a>
        observed an apparent half-hour "eruption" that took place on the central peak of
        <a href="/wiki/Alphonsus_(crater)" title="Alphonsus (crater)">
         Alphonsus crater
        </a>
        using a 48-inch (122-cm) reflector telescope equipped with a
        <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
         spectrometer
        </a>
        . During this time, the obtained spectra showed evidence for bright gaseous emission bands due to the molecules C
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
        and C
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
        While exposing his second spectrogram, he noticed "a marked increase in the brightness of the central region and an unusual white colour." Then, "all of a sudden the brightness started to decrease" and the resulting spectrum was normal.
       </li>
       <li>
        On October 29, 1963, two Aeronautical Chart and Information Center cartographers, James Clarke Greenacre and Edward M. Barr,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        at the
        <a href="/wiki/Lowell_Observatory" title="Lowell Observatory">
         Lowell Observatory
        </a>
        , Flagstaff, Arizona, manually recorded very bright red, orange, and pink colour phenomena on the southwest side of Cobra Head; a hill southeast of the lunar valley
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vallis_Schr%C3%B6teri" title="Vallis Schröteri">
         Vallis Schröteri
        </a>
        ; and the southwest interior rim of the
        <a href="/wiki/Aristarchus_(crater)" title="Aristarchus (crater)">
         Aristarchus crater
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This event sparked a major change in attitude towards TLP reports. According to
        <a href="/wiki/Willy_Ley" title="Willy Ley">
         Willy Ley
        </a>
        : "The first reaction in professional circles was, naturally, surprise, and hard on the heels of the surprise there followed an apologetic attitude, the apologies being directed at a long-dead great astronomer, Sir William Herschel."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        A notation by Winifred Sawtell Cameron states (1978, Event Serial No. 778): "This and their November observations started the modern interest and observing the Moon."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        The credibility of their findings stemmed from Greenacre's exemplary reputation as an impeccable cartographer, rather than from any photographic evidence.
       </li>
       <li>
        On the night of November 1–2, 1963, a few days after Greenacre's event, at the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_du_Pic-du-Midi" title="Observatoire du Pic-du-Midi">
         Observatoire du Pic-du-Midi
        </a>
        in the French Pyrenees,
        <a href="/wiki/Zden%C4%9Bk_Kopal" title="Zdeněk Kopal">
         Zdeněk Kopal
        </a>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        and Thomas Rackham
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
        made the first photographs of a "wide area lunar luminescence".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kopal_18-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-kopal-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>
        His article in
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Scientific_American" title="Scientific American">
          Scientific American
         </a>
        </i>
        transformed it into one of the most widely publicized TLP events.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">
          [19]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Kopal, like others, had argued that
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_Energetic_Particles" title="Solar Energetic Particles">
         Solar Energetic Particles
        </a>
        could be the cause of such a phenomenon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
         <a href="#cite_note-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        During the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
         Apollo 11
        </a>
        mission in 1969, Houston radioed to Apollo 11: "We've got an observation you can make if you have some time up there. There's been some lunar transient events reported in the vicinity of Aristarchus." Astronomers in
        <a href="/wiki/Bochum" title="Bochum">
         Bochum
        </a>
        , West Germany, had observed a bright glow on the lunar surface—the same sort of eerie luminescence that has intrigued Moon watchers for centuries. The report was passed on to Houston and thence to the astronauts. Almost immediately, Michael Collins reported back: "Hey, Houston, I'm looking north up toward Aristarchus now, and there's an area that is considerably more illuminated than the surrounding area. It seems to have a slight amount of fluorescence."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
         <a href="#cite_note-21">
          [21]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In 1992, Audouin Dollfus of the
        <a href="/wiki/Paris_Observatory" title="Paris Observatory">
         Observatoire de Paris
        </a>
        reported anomalous features on the floor of
        <a href="/wiki/Langrenus_(crater)" title="Langrenus (crater)">
         Langrenus crater
        </a>
        using a one-meter (3.2-foot) telescope. While observations on the night of December 29, 1992, were normal, unusually high
        <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
         albedo
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Polarization_(waves)" title="Polarization (waves)">
         polarization
        </a>
        features were recorded the following night that did not change in appearance over the six minutes of data collection.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
         <a href="#cite_note-22">
          [22]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Observations three days later showed a similar, but smaller, anomaly in the same vicinity. While the viewing conditions for this region were close to specular, it was argued that the amplitude of the observations were not consistent with a
        <a href="/wiki/Specular_reflection" title="Specular reflection">
         specular reflection
        </a>
        of sunlight. The favored hypothesis was that this was the consequence of light scattering from clouds of airborne particles resulting from a release of gas. The fractured floor of this crater was cited as a possible source of the gas.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanations">
        Explanations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Explanations for the transient lunar phenomena fall in four classes: outgassing, impact events, electrostatic phenomena, and unfavorable observation conditions.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Outgassing">
        Outgassing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Outgassing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Some TLPs may be caused by gas escaping from underground cavities. These gaseous events are purported to display a distinctive reddish hue, while others have appeared as white clouds or an indistinct haze. The majority of TLPs appear to be associated with floor-fractured craters, the edges of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       , or in other locations linked by geologists with volcanic activity. However, these are some of the most common targets when viewing the Moon, and this correlation could be an observational bias.
      </p>
      <p>
       In support of the outgassing hypothesis, data from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Alpha_particle" title="Alpha particle">
        alpha particle
       </a>
       spectrometer indicate the recent outgassing of
       <a href="/wiki/Radon" title="Radon">
        radon
       </a>
       to the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In particular, results show that radon gas was emanating from the vicinity of the craters
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aristarchus_crater" title="Aristarchus crater">
        Aristarchus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler_(lunar_crater)" title="Kepler (lunar crater)">
        Kepler
       </a>
       during the time of this two-year mission. These observations could be explained by the slow and visually imperceptible diffusion of gas to the surface, or by discrete explosive events. In support of explosive outgassing, it has been suggested that a roughly 3 km- (1.9 mi-) diameter region of the lunar surface was "recently" modified by a gas release event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The age of this feature is believed to be about 1 million years old, suggesting that such large phenomena occur only infrequently.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Impact_events">
        Impact events
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Impact events">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Impact events are continually occurring on the lunar surface. The most common events are those associated with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometeorites" title="Micrometeorites">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       , as might be encountered during meteor showers. Impact flashes from such events have been detected from multiple and simultaneous Earth-based observations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lim_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-lim-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tables of impacts recorded by video cameras exist for years since 2005 many of which are associated with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor_showers" title="Meteor showers">
        meteor showers
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Furthermore, impact clouds were detected following the crash of ESA's
       <a href="/wiki/SMART-1" title="SMART-1">
        SMART-1
       </a>
       spacecraft,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       India's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_Impact_Probe" title="Moon Impact Probe">
        Moon Impact Probe
       </a>
       and NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/LCROSS" title="LCROSS">
        LCROSS
       </a>
       . Impact events leave a visible scar on the surface, and these could be detected by analyzing before and after photos of sufficiently high resolution. No impact craters formed between the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine
       </a>
       (global resolution 100 metre, selected areas 7-20 metre) and SMART-1 (resolution 50 metre) missions have been identified.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Electrostatic_phenomena">
        Electrostatic phenomena
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Electrostatic phenomena">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:152px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seeing_Moon.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="150" data-file-width="150" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/ef/Seeing_Moon.gif" width="150"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seeing_Moon.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Eight individual frames taken from a video of the lunar crater Clavius showing the effect of the Earth's atmosphere on astronomical images
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       It has been suggested that effects related to either
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrostatic" title="Electrostatic">
        electrostatic
       </a>
       charging or discharging might be able to account for some of the transient lunar phenomena. One possibility is that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Electrodynamic" title="Electrodynamic">
        electrodynamic
       </a>
       effects related to the fracturing of near-surface materials could charge any gases that might be present, such as implanted
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radiogenic" title="Radiogenic">
        radiogenic
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daughter_products" title="Daughter products">
        daughter products
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If this were to occur at the surface, the subsequent discharge from this gas might be able to give rise to phenomena visible from Earth. Alternatively, it has been proposed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Triboelectric_effect" title="Triboelectric effect">
        triboelectric
       </a>
       charging of particles within a gas-borne dust cloud could give rise to electrostatic discharges visible from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Finally, electrostatic levitation of dust near the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_terminator" title="Lunar terminator">
        terminator
       </a>
       could potentially give rise to some form of phenomenon visible from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Unfavourable_observation_conditions">
        Unfavourable observation conditions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Unfavourable observation conditions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       It is possible that many transient phenomena might not be associated with the Moon itself but could be a result of unfavourable observing conditions or phenomena associated with the Earth. For instance, some reported transient phenomena are for objects near the resolution of the employed telescopes. The Earth's atmosphere can give rise to significant temporal distortions that could be confused with actual lunar phenomena (an effect known as
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">
        astronomical seeing
       </a>
       ). Other non-lunar explanations include the viewing of Earth-orbiting satellites and meteors or observational error.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lim_28-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-lim-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Debated_status_of_TLPs">
        Debated status of TLPs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transient_lunar_phenomenon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Debated status of TLPs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The most significant problem that faces reports of transient lunar phenomena is that the vast majority of these were made either by a single observer or at a single location on Earth (or both). The multitude of reports for transient phenomena occurring at the same
       <i>
        place
       </i>
       on the Moon could be used as evidence supporting their existence. However, in the absence of eyewitness reports from multiple observers at multiple locations on Earth for the same
       <i>
        event
       </i>
       , these must be regarded with caution. As discussed above, an equally plausible hypothesis for some of these events is that they are caused by the terrestrial atmosphere. If an event were to be observed at two different places on Earth at the same time, this could be used as evidence against an atmospheric origin.
      </p>
      <p>
       One attempt to overcome the above problems with transient phenomena reports was made during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine mission
       </a>
       by a network of amateur astronomers. Several events were reported, of which four of these were photographed both beforehand and afterward by the spacecraft. However, careful analysis of these images shows no discernible differences at these sites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This does not necessarily imply that these reports were a result of observational error, as it is possible that outgassing events on the lunar surface might not leave a visible marker, but neither is it encouraging for the hypothesis that these were authentic lunar phenomena.
      </p>
      <p>
       Observations are currently being coordinated by the
       <a href="/wiki/Association_of_Lunar_and_Planetary_Observers" title="Association of Lunar and Planetary Observers">
        Association of Lunar and Planetary Observers
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/British_Astronomical_Association" title="British Astronomical Association">
        British Astronomical Association
       </a>
       to re-observe sites where transient lunar phenomena were reported in the past. By documenting the appearance of these features under the same illumination and
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       conditions, it is possible to judge whether some reports were simply due to a misinterpretation of what the observer regarded as an abnormality. Furthermore, with digital images, it is possible to simulate atmospheric spectral
       <a href="/wiki/Dispersion_(optics)" title="Dispersion (optics)">
        dispersion
       </a>
       , astronomical seeing blur and light scattering by our atmosphere to determine if these phenomena could explain some of the original TLP reports.
      </p>
