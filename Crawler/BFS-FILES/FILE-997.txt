      <p>
       <b>
        Magnetic reconnection
       </b>
       is a physical process in highly conducting
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasmas
       </a>
       in which the magnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Topology" title="Topology">
        topology
       </a>
       is rearranged and magnetic energy is converted to
       <a href="/wiki/Kinetic_energy" title="Kinetic energy">
        kinetic energy
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Thermal_energy" title="Thermal energy">
        thermal energy
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_acceleration" title="Particle acceleration">
        particle acceleration
       </a>
       . Magnetic reconnection occurs on timescales intermediate between slow resistive diffusion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       and fast
       <a href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_wave" title="Alfvén wave">
        Alfvénic
       </a>
       timescales.
      </p>
      <p>
       The qualitative description of the reconnection process is such that magnetic field lines from different
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_domain" title="Magnetic domain">
        magnetic domains
       </a>
       (defined by the field line connectivity) are spliced to one another, changing their patterns of connectivity with respect to the sources. It is a violation of an approximate conservation law in plasma physics, called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n%27s_Theorem" title="Alfvén's Theorem">
        Alfvén's Theorem
       </a>
       , and can concentrate mechanical or magnetic energy in both space and time.
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        Solar flares
       </a>
       , the largest explosions in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , may involve the reconnection of large systems of magnetic flux on the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , releasing, in minutes, energy that has been stored in the magnetic field over a period of hours to days. Magnetic reconnection in
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       is one of the mechanisms responsible for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy)" title="Aurora (astronomy)">
        aurora
       </a>
       , and it is important to the science of controlled
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        nuclear fusion
       </a>
       because it is one mechanism preventing
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetic_confinement" title="Magnetic confinement">
        magnetic confinement
       </a>
       of the fusion fuel.
      </p>
      <p>
       In an electrically conductive
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       , magnetic field lines are grouped into 'domains'— bundles of field lines that connect from a particular place to another particular place, and that are topologically distinct from other field lines nearby. This topology is approximately preserved even when the magnetic field itself is strongly distorted by the presence of variable currents or motion of magnetic sources, because effects that might otherwise change the magnetic topology instead induce
       <a href="/wiki/Eddy_current" title="Eddy current">
        eddy currents
       </a>
       in the plasma; the eddy currents have the effect of canceling out the topological change.
      </p>
      <p>
       In two dimensions, the most common type of magnetic reconnection is
       <b>
        separator reconnection
       </b>
       , in which four separate magnetic domains exchange magnetic field lines. Domains in a magnetic plasma are separated by
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Separatrix_(Math)" title="Separatrix (Math)">
         separatrix
        </a>
        surfaces
       </i>
       : curved surfaces in space that divide different bundles of flux. Field lines on one side of the separatrix all terminate at a particular magnetic pole, while field lines on the other side all terminate at a different pole of similar sign. Since each field line generally begins at a north magnetic pole and ends at a south magnetic pole, the most general way of dividing simple flux systems involves four domains separated by two separatrices: one separatrix surface divides the flux into two bundles, each of which shares a south pole, and the other separatrix surface divides the flux into two bundles, each of which shares a north pole. The intersection of the separatrices forms a
       <i>
        separator
       </i>
       , a single line that is at the boundary of the four separate domains. In separator reconnection, field lines enter the separator from two of the domains, and are spliced one to the other, exiting the separator in the other two domains (see the figure).
      </p>
      <p>
       According to simple resistive
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics" title="Magnetohydrodynamics">
        magnetohydrodynamics
       </a>
       (MHD) theory, reconnection happens because the plasma's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resistivity" title="Resistivity">
        electrical resistivity
       </a>
       near the boundary layer opposes the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_(electricity)" title="Current (electricity)">
        currents
       </a>
       necessary to sustain the change in the magnetic field. The need for such a current can be seen from one of
       <a href="/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations" title="Maxwell's equations">
        Maxwell's equations
       </a>
       ,
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

      <p>
       The resistivity of the current layer allows
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_flux" title="Magnetic flux">
        magnetic flux
       </a>
       from either side to diffuse through the current layer, cancelling outflux from the other side of the boundary. When this happens, the plasma is pulled out by
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_tension_force" title="Magnetic tension force">
        magnetic tension
       </a>
       along the direction of the magnetic field lines. The resulting drop in pressure pulls more plasma and magnetic flux into the central region, yielding a self-sustaining process.
      </p>
      <p>
       A current problem in plasma
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       is that observed reconnection happens much faster than predicted by MHD in high
       <a href="/wiki/Lundquist_number" title="Lundquist number">
        Lundquist number
       </a>
       plasmas: solar flares, for example, proceed 13-14 orders of magnitude faster than a naive calculation would suggest, and several orders of magnitude faster than current theoretical models that include turbulence and kinetic effects. One possible mechanism to explain the discrepancy is that the electromagnetic
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       in the boundary layer is sufficiently strong to scatter electrons, raising the plasma's local resistivity. This would allow the magnetic flux to diffuse faster.
      </p>

      <h3>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Solar_atmosphere">
        Solar atmosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_reconnection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Solar atmosphere">
         edit

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Magnetic reconnection occurs during
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection" title="Coronal mass ejection">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       , and many other events in the solar atmosphere. The observational evidence for solar flares includes observations of inflows/outflows, downflowing loops, and changes in the magnetic topology. In the past, observations of the solar atmosphere were done using remote imaging; consequently, the magnetic fields were inferred or extrapolated rather than observed directly. However, the first direct observations of solar magnetic reconnection were gathered in 2012 (and released in 2013) by the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Resolution_Coronal_Imager" title="High Resolution Coronal Imager">
        High Resolution Coronal Imager
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       </a>

       </a>
       and in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetotail" title="Magnetotail">
        magnetotail
       </a>
       .
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cluster_mission" title="Cluster mission">
        Cluster
       </a>
       is a four-spacecraft mission, with the four spacecraft in a tetrahedron arrangement, to separate spatial from temporal changes as the suite flies through space. Cluster has now also unambiguously discovered 'reverse reconnection' near the polar cusps. 'Dayside reconnection' allows interconnection of the Earth's magnetic field with that of the Sun (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Interplanetary_Magnetic_Field" title="Interplanetary Magnetic Field">
        Interplanetary Magnetic Field
       </a>
       ), allowing particle and energy entry into the Earth's vicinity. Tail reconnection allows release of energy stored in the Earth's magnetic tail, injecting particles deep into the magnetosphere, causing auroral
       <a href="/wiki/Substorm" title="Substorm">
        substorms
       </a>
       . 'Reverse reconnection' is reconnection of Earth's tail magnetic fields with northward Interplanetary Magnetic Fields, causing sunward convection in the Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Ionosphere" title="Ionosphere">
        ionosphere
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetospheric_Multiscale_Mission" title="Magnetospheric Multiscale Mission">
        Magnetospheric Multiscale Mission
       </a>
       , launched on 13 March 2015, is improving on Cluster results by having a tighter constellation of spacecraft, allowing finer spatial measurements and finer time detail. In this way the behavior of the electrical currents in the electron diffusion region will be better understood.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 26 February 2008,
       <a href="/wiki/THEMIS" title="THEMIS">
        THEMIS
       </a>
       probes were able to determine, for the first time, the triggering event for the onset of magnetospheric substorms.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Two of the five probes, positioned approximately one third the distance to the Moon, measured events suggesting a magnetic reconnection event 96 seconds prior to Auroral intensification.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Dr. Vassilis Angelopoulos of the University of California, Los Angeles, who is the principal investigator for the THEMIS mission, claimed, "Our data show clearly and for the first time that magnetic reconnection is the trigger.".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Laboratory_plasma_experiments">
        Laboratory plasma experiments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Magnetic_reconnection&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Laboratory plasma experiments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The process of magnetic reconnection has been studied in detail by dedicated laboratory experiments, such as the Magnetic Reconnection Experiment (MRX) at Princeton Plasma Physics Laboratory (PPPL). Experiments such as these have confirmed many aspects of magnetic reconnection, including the Sweet-Parker model in regimes where this model is applicable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The confinement of plasma in devices such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tokamak" title="Tokamak">
        tokamaks
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Spherical_tokamak" title="Spherical tokamak">
        spherical tokamaks
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Reversed_field_pinch" title="Reversed field pinch">
        reversed field pinches
       </a>
       requires the presence of closed magnetic flux surfaces. By changing the magnetic topology, magnetic reconnection degrades confinement by disrupting these closed flux surfaces, allowing the hot central plasma to mix with cooler plasma closer to the wall.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
