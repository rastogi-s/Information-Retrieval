         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
          transits
         </a>
         the
         <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
          Sun
         </a>
         , as viewed by the Mars
         <a href="/wiki/Opportunity_(rover)" title="Opportunity (rover)">
          <i>

         </a>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        transit of Deimos
       </b>
       across the Sun as seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       occurs when
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       passes directly between the Sun and a point on the surface of Mars, obscuring a small part of the Sun's disc for an observer on Mars. During a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_transit" title="Astronomical transit">
        transit
       </a>
       , Deimos can be seen from Mars as a small dark spot rapidly moving across the Sun's face.
      </p>
      <p>
       The event could also be referred to as a partial
       <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">
        eclipse
       </a>
       of the Sun by Deimos. However, since the
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        angular diameter
       </a>
       of Deimos is only about 1/10 of the angular diameter of the Sun as seen from Mars, it is more natural to refer to it as a transit. The angular diameter of Deimos is only 2½ times the angular diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Venus" title="Venus">
        Venus
       </a>
       as seen from Earth during a
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Venus" title="Transit of Venus">
        transit of Venus
       </a>
       from Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transit">
        Transit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Transit_of_Deimos_from_Mars&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Transit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A transit of Deimos from Mars lasts a maximum of about two minutes, due to its relatively rapid
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       of about 30.3 hours.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because they orbit Mars in low-inclination equatorial orbits, the shadows of
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       or Deimos projected onto the surface of Mars exhibit a seasonal variation in latitude. At any given geographical location on the surface of Mars, there are two intervals in a Martian year when the shadows of Phobos or Deimos are passing through its latitude. During each such interval, no or one transit of Deimos can be seen by observers at that geographical location (compared to about half a dozen
       <a href="/wiki/Transit_of_Phobos_from_Mars" title="Transit of Phobos from Mars">
        transits of Phobos
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       The shadow always falls on the "winter hemisphere", except when it crosses the equator during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vernal_equinox" title="Vernal equinox">
        vernal equinox
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumnal_equinox" title="Autumnal equinox">
        autumnal equinox
       </a>
       . Thus transits of Deimos happen during Martian autumn and winter in the northern hemisphere and the southern hemisphere, roughly symmetrically around the
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       . Close to the equator they happen around the autumnal equinox and the vernal equinox; farther from the equator they happen closer to the winter solstice.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because it orbits relatively close to Mars, Deimos cannot be seen north of 82.7°N or south of 82.7°S; such latitudes will obviously not see transits either.
      </p>
      <p>
       On 4 March 2004, a transit was photographed by Mars Rover
       <i>
        Opportunity
       </i>
       , while on 13 March 2004, a transit was photographed by Mars Rover
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MER-A" title="MER-A">
         Spirit
        </a>
       </i>
       . In the captions below, the first row shows Earth time
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       and the second row shows
       <a href="/wiki/Timekeeping_on_Mars" title="Timekeeping on Mars">
        Martian local solar time
       </a>
       .
      </p>
