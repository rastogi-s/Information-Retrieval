   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Nanoflares
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">

          </a>
         </div>
         "This false-color temperature map shows
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_active_region" title="Solar active region">

         </a>
         AR10923, observed close to center of the sun's disk. Blue regions indicate plasma near 10 million degrees
         <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
          K
         </a>

         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
          <a href="#cite_note-1">
           [1]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A nanoflare is a very small episodic heating event which happens in the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       , the external
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere" title="Atmosphere">
        atmosphere
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The hypothesis of "microflares" as a possible explanation of the coronal heating was first suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Gold" title="Thomas Gold">
        Thomas Gold
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and then later developed by
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Parker" title="Eugene Parker">
        Eugene Parker
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Parker a nanoflare arises from an event of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       which converts the energy stored in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">
        solar magnetic field
       </a>
       into the motion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       . The plasma motion (thought as fluid motion) occurs at length-scales so small that it is soon dumped by the
       <a href="/wiki/Turbulence" title="Turbulence">
        turbulence
       </a>
       and then by the
       <a href="/wiki/Viscosity" title="Viscosity">
        viscosity
       </a>
       . In such a way the
       <a href="/wiki/Energy" title="Energy">
        energy
       </a>
       is quickly converted into
       <a href="/wiki/Heat" title="Heat">
        heat
       </a>
       , and conducted by the free electrons along the magnetic field lines closer to the place where the nanoflare switches on. In order to heat a region of very high
       <a href="/wiki/X-ray" title="X-ray">
        X-ray
       </a>
       emission, over an area 1" x 1", a nanoflare of 10
       <sup>
        17
       </sup>
       J should happen every 20 seconds, and 1000 nanoflares per second should occur in a large active region of 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       x 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       km
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       . On the basis of this theory, the emission coming from a big flare could be caused by a series of micro-nanoflares, not observable individually.
      </p>
      <p>
       The nanoflare model has long suffered from a lack of observational evidence. Simulations predict that nanoflares produce a faint, hot (~10 MK) component of the emission measure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Unfortunately, current instruments, such as the Extreme-Ultraviolet Imaging Spectrometer on board
       <a href="/wiki/Hinode" title="Hinode">
        Hinode
       </a>
       , are not adequately sensitive to the range in which this faint emission occurs, making a confident detection impossible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recent evidence from the EUNIS sounding rocket has provided some spectral evidence for non-flaring plasma at temperatures near 9 MK in active region cores.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nanoflares_and_coronal_activity">
        Nanoflares and coronal activity
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoflares&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Nanoflares and coronal activity">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_flare_(TRACE).gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="823" data-file-width="821" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif/220px-Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif/330px-Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif/440px-Solar_flare_%28TRACE%29.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_flare_(TRACE).gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Typical flaring
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
          coronal loops
         </a>
         observed by
         <a href="/wiki/TRACE" title="TRACE">
          TRACE
         </a>
         in the EUV rays
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Telescopic observations suggest that the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">
        solar magnetic field
       </a>
       , which theoretically is "frozen" into the gas of the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       , expands into roughly semicircular structures in the corona. These
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       , which can be seen in the EUV and X-ray images (see the figure on the left), often confine very hot plasmas, with emissions characteristic of temperature of a one to a few million degrees.
      </p>
      <p>
       Many flux tubes are relatively stable as seen in soft X-ray images, emitting at steady rate. However flickerings, brightenings, small explosions, bright points, flares and mass eruptions are observed very frequently, especially in
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        active regions
       </a>
       . These macroscopic signs of solar activity are considered by astrophysicists as the phenomenology related to events of relaxation of stressed magnetic fields, during which part of the energy they have stored is released ultimately into particle kinetic energy (heating); this could be via current dissipation,
       <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">
        Joule effect
       </a>
       , or any of several non-thermal plasma effects.
      </p>
      <p>
       Theoretical work often appeals to the concept of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       to explain these outbursts. Rather than a single large-scale episode of such a process, though, modern thinking suggests that a multitude of small-scale versions reconnection, cascading together, might be a better description. The theory of nanoflares then supposes that these events of magnetic reconnection, occurring at nearly the same time on small length-scales wherever in the corona, are very numerous, each providing an imperceptibly small fraction of the total energy required in a macroscopic event. These nanoflares might themselves resemble very tiny flares, close one to each other, both in time and in space, effectively heating the corona and underlying many of the phenomena of solar magnetic activity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Episodic heating often observed in
       <a href="/wiki/Sunspot" title="Sunspot">
        active regions
       </a>
       , including major events such as
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        flares
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejections" title="Coronal mass ejections">
        coronal mass ejections
       </a>
       could be provoked by cascade effects, similar to those described by the mathematical theories of catastrophes. In the hypothesis that the solar corona is in a state of
       <a href="/wiki/Self-organized_criticality" title="Self-organized criticality">
        self-organized criticality
       </a>
       , the stressing of the magnetic field should be enhanced until a small perturbation switches on many small instabilities, happening together as it occurs in avalanches.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the experimental results often cited in supporting the nanoflare theory is the fact that the distribution of the number of flares observed in the hard X-rays is a function of their energy, following a power law with negative spectral index. A sufficiently large power-law index would allow the smallest events to dominate the total energy. In the energy range of normal flares, the index has a value of approximately -1.8
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Actually, though, a negative spectral index larger than 2 is required in order to maintain the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_corona" title="Solar corona">
        solar corona
       </a>
       via the nanoflare hypothesis .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nanoflares_and_coronal_heating">
        Nanoflares and coronal heating
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Nanoflares&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Nanoflares and coronal heating">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="913" data-file-width="1008" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg/220px-Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg/330px-Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg/440px-Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Magnetic_Field_Lines.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar Magnetic Field Lines
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The problem of coronal heating is still unsolved, although research is ongoing and other evidence of nanoflares has been found in the solar corona. The amount of energy stored in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_magnetic_field" title="Solar magnetic field">
        solar magnetic field
       </a>
       can account for the coronal heating necessary to maintain the plasma at this temperature and to balance
       <a href="/wiki/Coronal_radiative_losses" title="Coronal radiative losses">
        coronal radiative losses
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The radiation is not the only mechanism of energy loss in the corona: since the
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       is highly ionized and the magnetic field is well organized, the thermal conduction is a competitive process. The energy losses due to the thermal conduction are of the same order of coronal radiative losses. The energy released in the corona which is not radiated externally is conducted back towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       along the arcs. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       where the temperature is about 10
       <sup>
        4
       </sup>
       -10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       K, radiative losses are too high to be balanced by any form of mechanical heating .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The very high temperature gradient observed in this range of temperatures increases the conductive flux in order to supply for the irradiated power. In other words, the transition region is so steep (the temperature increases from 10kK to 1MK in a distance of the order of 100 km) because the thermal conduction from the superior hotter atmosphere must balance the high radiative losses, as indicated to the numerous
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emission_lines" title="Emission lines">
        emission lines
       </a>
       , which are formed from ionized atoms (oxygen, carbon, iron and so on).
      </p>
      <p>
       The solar convection can supply the required heating, but in a way not yet known in detail. Actually, it is still unclear how this energy is transmitted from the chromosphere(where it could be absorbed or reflected), and then dissipated into the corona instead of dispersing into the solar wind. Furthermore, where does it occur exactly ? : in the low
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       or mainly in the higher corona, where the magnetic field lines open into the space
       <a href="/wiki/Heliosphere" title="Heliosphere">
        heliosphere
       </a>
       , driving the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       into the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_system" title="Solar system">
        solar system
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The importance of the magnetic field is recognized by all the scientists: there is a strict correspondence between the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stellar_active_region&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stellar active region (page does not exist)">
        active regions
       </a>
       , where the irradiated flux is higher (especially in the X-rays), and the regions of intense magnetic field.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Poletto_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Poletto-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The problem of coronal heating is complicated by the fact that different coronal features require very different amounts of energy. It is difficult to believe that very dynamic and energetic phenomena such as flares and coronal mass ejections share the same source of energy with stable structures covering very large areas on the Sun: if nanoflares would have heated the whole corona, then they should be distributed so uniformly so as to look like a steady heating. Flares themselves – and microflares, which when studied in detail seem to have the same physics – are highly intermittent in space and time, and would not therefore be relevant to any requirement for continuous heating. On the other hand, in order to explain very rapid and energetic phenomena such as solar flares, the magnetic field should be structured on distances of the order of the metre.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg/220px-Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg/330px-Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg/440px-Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Solar_Flare_and_Coronal_Mass_Ejection_2010-02-12.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Solar Flare and Coronal Mass Ejection (
         <a href="/wiki/STEREO" title="STEREO">
          STEREO
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alfv%C3%A9n_waves" title="Alfvén waves">
        Alfvén waves
       </a>
       generated by convective motions in the
       <a href="/wiki/Photosphere" title="Photosphere">
        photosphere
       </a>
       can go through the
       <a href="/wiki/Chromosphere" title="Chromosphere">
        chromosphere
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_transition_region" title="Solar transition region">
        transition region
       </a>
       , carrying an energy flux comparable to that required to sustain the
       <a href="/wiki/Corona" title="Corona">
        corona
       </a>
       . Anyway, wavetrain periods observed in the high chromosphere and in the lower transition region are of the order of 3-5 min. These times are longer than the time taken by Alfvén waves to cross a typical coronal loop. This means that most of the dissipative mechanisms may provide enough energy only at distances further from the solar corona. More probably, the Alfvén waves are responsible for the acceleration of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_holes" title="Coronal holes">
        coronal holes
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The theory initially developed by Parker of micro-nanoflares is one of those explaining the heating of the corona as the dissipation of electric currents generated by a spontaneous relaxation of the magnetic field towards a configuration of lower energy. The magnetic energy is thus transformed into
       <a href="/wiki/Joule_heating" title="Joule heating">
        Joule heating
       </a>
       . The braiding of the field lines of the coronal magnetic flux tubes provokes events of
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       with a consequent change of the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_field" title="Magnetic field">
        magnetic field
       </a>
       at small length-scales without a simultaneous alteration of the magnetic field lines at large length-scales. In this way it can be explained why
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coronal_loops" title="Coronal loops">
        coronal loops
       </a>
       are stable and so hot at the same time.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Ohmic dissipation by currents could be a valid alternative to explain the coronal activity. For many years the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection" title="Magnetic reconnection">
        magnetic reconnection
       </a>
       has been invoked as the main power source of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_flares" title="Solar flares">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . However this heating mechanism is not very efficient in large
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Current_sheets" title="Current sheets">
        current sheets
       </a>
       , while more energy is released in turbulent regimes when nanoflares happen at much smaller scale-lengths, where non-linear effects are not negligible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rappazzo_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rappazzo-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
