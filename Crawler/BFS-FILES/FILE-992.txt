      <p>
       In various scientific contexts, a
       <b>
        scale height
       </b>
       is a distance over which a quantity decreases by a factor of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant)" title="E (mathematical constant)">
         e
        </a>
       </i>
       (approximately 2.72, the base of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Natural_logarithms" title="Natural logarithms">
        natural logarithms
       </a>
       ). It is usually denoted by the capital letter
       <i>
        H
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scale_height_used_in_a_simple_atmospheric_pressure_model">
        Scale height used in a simple atmospheric pressure model
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Scale_height&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Scale height used in a simple atmospheric pressure model">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For planetary atmospheres,
       <b>
        scale height
       </b>
       is the increase in altitude for which the
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure" title="Atmospheric pressure">
        atmospheric pressure
       </a>
       decreases by a factor of
       <i>
        e
       </i>
       . The scale height remains constant for a particular temperature. It can be calculated by
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AMS_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AMS-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wolfram_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wolfram-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle H={\frac {kT}{Mg}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">

               H
              </mi>
              <mo>

              </mo>
              <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
               <mfrac>
                <mrow>
                 <mi>

                 </mi>

                <mrow>
                 <mi>
                  M
                 </mi>
                 <mi>
                  g
                 </mi>
                </mrow>
               </mfrac>
              </mrow>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle H={\frac {kT}{Mg}}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="H={\frac  {kT}{Mg}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cf2bd672168ad521f4672bfe85d771ad9e5f330" style="vertical-align: -2.338ex; width:9.556ex; height:5.843ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       or equivalently
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle H={\frac {RT}{mg}}}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">

              </mo>

             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle H={\frac {RT}{mg}}}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="H={\frac  {RT}{mg}}" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df0aea8da18833eeb2391222c879e3e284f7e4b0" style="vertical-align: -2.338ex; width:9.398ex; height:5.676ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       where:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         k
        </i>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Boltzmann_constant" title="Boltzmann constant">
         Boltzmann constant
        </a>
        = 1.38 x 10
        <sup>
         −23
        </sup>
        J·K
        <sup>
         −1
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         R
        </i>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Gas_constant" title="Gas constant">
         Gas constant
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        = mean atmospheric
        <a href="/wiki/Temperature" title="Temperature">
         temperature
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Kelvin" title="Kelvin">
         kelvins
        </a>
        = 250 K
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacob1999_3-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Jacob1999-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
        for Earth
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         M
        </i>
        = mean mass of a molecule (units kg)
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         m
        </i>
        = mean molecular mass of one atmospheric particle = 0.029 kg/mol for Earth
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         g
        </i>
        =
        <a href="/wiki/Acceleration" title="Acceleration">
         acceleration
        </a>
        due to
        <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
         gravity
        </a>
        on planetary surface (m/s²)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The pressure (force per unit area) at a given altitude is a result of the weight of the overlying atmosphere. If at a height of
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       the atmosphere has
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       <i>
        ρ
       </i>
       and pressure
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       , then moving upwards at an infinitesimally small height
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       will decrease the pressure by amount
       <i>
        dP
       </i>
       , equal to the weight of a layer of atmosphere of thickness
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Thus:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

              </mo>

               <!-- − -->

              </mi>

            </mrow>

             {\displaystyle {\frac {dP}{dz}}=-g\rho }

           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="{\frac  {dP}{dz}}=-g\rho " aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3921fd595a4be1ccfdfec84a071b7b583dd94c6" style="vertical-align: -2.005ex; width:11.022ex; height:5.509ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       where
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       is the acceleration due to gravity. For small
       <i>
        dz
       </i>
       it is possible to assume
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       to be constant; the minus sign indicates that as the height increases the pressure decreases. Therefore, using the
       <a href="/wiki/Equation_of_state" title="Equation of state">
        equation of state
       </a>
       for an
       <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">
        ideal gas
       </a>
       of mean molecular mass
       <i>
        M
       </i>
       at temperature
       <i>
        T,
       </i>
       the density can be expressed as
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">

              </mo>

               <mfrac>
                <mrow>
                 <mi>
                  M
                 </mi>
                 <mi>
                  P
                 </mi>
                </mrow>
                <mrow>
                 <mi>
                  k
                 </mi>
                 <mi>
                  T

                </mrow>

           </semantics>

         </span>

      </dl>
      <p>
       which will not change unless the temperature does. Integrating the above and assuming where
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       is the pressure at height
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       = 0 (pressure at
       <a href="/wiki/Sea_level" title="Sea level">
        sea level
       </a>
       ) the pressure at height
       <i>
        z
       </i>
       can be written as:
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <span class="mwe-math-element">
         <span class="mwe-math-mathml-inline mwe-math-mathml-a11y" style="display: none;">
          <math alttext="{\displaystyle P=P_{0}\exp \left(-{\frac {z}{H}}\right)}" xmlns="http://www.w3.org/1998/Math/MathML">
           <semantics>
            <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
             <mstyle displaystyle="true" scriptlevel="0">
              <mi>
               P
              </mi>
              <mo>
               =
              </mo>
              <msub>
               <mi>
                P
               </mi>
               <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                <mn>
                 0
                </mn>
               </mrow>
              </msub>
              <mi>
               exp
              </mi>
              <mo>
               ⁡
               <!-- ⁡ -->
              </mo>
              <mrow>
               <mo>
                (
               </mo>
               <mrow>
                <mo>
                 −
                 <!-- − -->
                </mo>
                <mrow class="MJX-TeXAtom-ORD">
                 <mfrac>
                  <mi>
                   z
                  </mi>
                  <mi>
                   H
                  </mi>
                 </mfrac>
                </mrow>
               </mrow>
               <mo>
                )
               </mo>
              </mrow>
             </mstyle>
            </mrow>
            <annotation encoding="application/x-tex">
             {\displaystyle P=P_{0}\exp \left(-{\frac {z}{H}}\right)}
            </annotation>
           </semantics>
          </math>
         </span>
         <img alt="P=P_{0}\exp \left(-{\frac  {z}{H}}\right)" aria-hidden="true" class="mwe-math-fallback-image-inline" src="https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f90b203b4b02628336bfaea604991cfdbdcb3c0d" style="vertical-align: -1.838ex; width:18.813ex; height:4.843ex;"/>
        </span>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       This translates as the pressure
       <a href="/wiki/Exponential_decay" title="Exponential decay">
        decreasing exponentially
       </a>
       with height.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iapetus_1_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iapetus_1-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </a>
       , the pressure at sea level
       <i>
        P
       </i>
       <sub>
        0
       </sub>
       averages about 1.01×10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       Pa, the mean molecular mass of dry air is 28.964
       <a href="/wiki/Unified_atomic_mass_unit" title="Unified atomic mass unit">
        u
       </a>
       and hence 28.964 × 1.660×10
       <sup>
        −27
       </sup>
       = 4.808×10
       <sup>
        −26
       </sup>
       kg, and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Standard_gravity" title="Standard gravity">
         g
        </a>
       </i>
       = 9.81 m/s². As a function of temperature the scale height of Earth's atmosphere is therefore 1.38/(4.808×9.81)×10
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       = 29.26 m/deg. This yields the following scale heights for representative air temperatures.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        = 290 K,
        <i>
         H
        </i>
        = 8500 m
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        = 273 K,
        <i>
         H
        </i>
        = 8000 m
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        = 260 K,
        <i>
         H
        </i>
        = 7610 m
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <i>
         T
        </i>
        = 210 K,
        <i>
         H
        </i>
        = 6000 m
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       These figures should be compared with the temperature and density of Earth's atmosphere plotted at
       <a href="/wiki/NRLMSISE-00" title="NRLMSISE-00">
        NRLMSISE-00
       </a>
       , which shows the air density dropping from 1200 g/m
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       at sea level to 0.5
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       = .125 g/m
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       at 70 km, a factor of 9600, indicating an average scale height of 70/ln(9600) = 7.64 km, consistent with the indicated average air temperature over that range of close to 260 K.
      </p>
      <p>
       Note:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Density is related to pressure by the
        <a href="/wiki/Ideal_gas" title="Ideal gas">
         ideal gas
        </a>
        laws. Therefore—with some departures caused by varying temperature—density will also decrease exponentially with height from a sea level value of
        <i>
         ρ
        </i>
        <sub>
         0
        </sub>
        roughly equal to 1.2 kg m
        <sup>
         −3
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        At heights over 100 km, molecular
        <a href="/wiki/Diffusion" title="Diffusion">
         diffusion
        </a>
        means that each molecular atomic species has its own scale height.
       </li>
      </ul>
