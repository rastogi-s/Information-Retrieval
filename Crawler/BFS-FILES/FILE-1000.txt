      <p>
       A
       <b>
        sounding rocket
       </b>
       , sometimes called a
       <b>
        research rocket
       </b>
       , is an instrument-carrying
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
        rocket
       </a>
       designed to take measurements and perform scientific experiments during its
       <a href="/wiki/Sub-orbital_spaceflight" title="Sub-orbital spaceflight">
        sub-orbital
       </a>
       flight. The rockets are used to carry instruments from 50 to 1,500 kilometres (31 to 932 mi)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       above the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       ; the altitude generally between
       <a href="/wiki/Weather_balloon" title="Weather balloon">
        weather balloons
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       (the maximum altitude for
       <a href="/wiki/Balloon" title="Balloon">
        balloons
       </a>
       is about 40 kilometres (25 mi) and the minimum for
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite" title="Satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       is approximately 120 kilometres (75 mi)).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-overview-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Certain sounding rockets, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Black_Brant_(rocket)" title="Black Brant (rocket)">
        Black Brant X and XII
       </a>
       , have an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       between 1,000 and 1,500 kilometres (620 and 930 mi); the maximum apogee of their class. Sounding rockets often use military surplus rocket motors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-what-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       routinely flies the Terrier Mk 70 boosted
       <a href="/wiki/Improved_Orion" title="Improved Orion">
        Improved Orion
       </a>
       lifting 270–450-kilogram (600–990 lb) payloads into the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/exoatmospheric" title="wiktionary:exoatmospheric">
        exoatmospheric
       </a>
       region between 100 and 200 kilometres (62 and 124 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Etymology">
        Etymology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sounding_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Etymology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The origin of the term comes from nautical vocabulary
       <i>
        to sound
       </i>
       , which is to throw a weighted line from a ship into the water to measure the water's depth. The term itself has its etymological roots in the Portuguese, Italian, Spanish, and French words for
       <i>
        probe
       </i>
       , which are "sonda" and "sonde", respectively.
       <i>
        Sounding
       </i>
       in the rocket context is equivalent to
       <i>
        taking a measurement
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-what-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Design">
        Design
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sounding_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4928" data-file-width="3264" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg/170px-SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg/255px-SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg/340px-SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg 2x" width="170"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:SoundingRocketSamplePayload-02.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Sample payloads for sounding rockets
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The basic elements of sounding rocket are a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solid-fuel_rocket" title="Solid-fuel rocket">
        solid-fuel rocket
       </a>
       motor and a science
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">
        payload
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-what-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Larger, higher altitude rockets have 2 to 3
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rocket_staging" title="Rocket staging">
        stages
       </a>
       to increase efficiency and payload capability. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freefall" title="Freefall">
        freefall
       </a>
       part of the flight is an
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptic trajectory
       </a>
       with vertical
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        major axis
       </a>
       allowing the payload to appear to hover near its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-overview-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The average flight time is less than 30 minutes, usually between five and 20 minutes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-overview-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rocket consumes its fuel on the first stage of the rising part of the flight, then separates and falls away, leaving the payload to complete the arc and return to the ground under a
       <a href="/wiki/Parachute" title="Parachute">
        parachute
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-what-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Advantages">
        Advantages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sounding_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Advantages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sounding rockets are advantageous for some research because of their low cost,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-overview-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       short lead time (sometimes less than six months)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-what_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-what-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and their ability to conduct research in areas inaccessible to either balloons or satellites. They are also used as test beds for equipment that will be used in more expensive and risky
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_spaceflight" title="Orbital spaceflight">
        orbital spaceflight
       </a>
       missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-overview_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-overview-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The smaller size of a sounding rocket also makes launching from temporary sites possible allowing for field studies at remote locations, and even in the middle of the ocean, if fired from a ship.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Research_applications">
        Research applications
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sounding_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Research applications">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sounding rockets are commonly used for:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Research in
        <a href="/wiki/Aeronomy" title="Aeronomy">
         aeronomy
        </a>
        , which requires this tool for
        <i>
         in situ
        </i>
        measurements in the upper atmosphere
       </li>
       <li>
        Ultraviolet and
        <a href="/wiki/X-ray_astronomy" title="X-ray astronomy">
         X-ray astronomy
        </a>
        , which require being above the bulk of the Earth's atmosphere
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">
         Microgravity
        </a>
        research, which benefits from a few minutes of
        <a href="/wiki/Weightlessness" title="Weightlessness">
         weightlessness
        </a>
        on rockets launched to altitudes of a few hundred kilometers
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Operators_and_programs">
        Operators and programs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sounding_rocket&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Operators and programs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Andøya Space Center in Norway operates two sounding rocket launch sites, one at Andøya and one at Svalbard. Has launched sounding rockets since 1962.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Poker_Flat_Research_Range" title="Poker Flat Research Range">
         Poker Flat Research Range
        </a>
        is owned by the
        <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alaska_Fairbanks" title="University of Alaska Fairbanks">
         University of Alaska Fairbanks
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The British
        <a href="/wiki/Skylark_(rocket)" title="Skylark (rocket)">
         Skylark rocket
        </a>
        was first designed in 1955 and was used for 441 launches before it was terminated in 2005
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ISRO" title="ISRO">
         ISRO
        </a>
        's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/VSSC" title="VSSC">
         VSSC
        </a>
        developed the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rohini_sounding_rocket_series" title="Rohini sounding rocket series">
         <i>
          Rohini
         </i>
        </a>
        sounding rockets series starting in 1967 that reached altitudes of 500 km
       </li>
       <li>
        The Australian Space Research Institute (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ASRI" title="ASRI">
         ASRI
        </a>
        ) operates a Small Sounding Rocket Program (SSRP) for launching payloads (mostly educational) to altitudes of about 7 km
       </li>
       <li>
        Indian Institute of Space Science and Technology (IIST) launched a Sounding Rocket (Vyom) in May, 2012, which reached an altitude of 15 km. Vyom Mk-II is in its conceptual design stage with an objective to reach 70 km altitude with 20 kg payload capacity.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/University_of_Queensland" title="University of Queensland">
         University of Queensland
        </a>
        operates Terrier-Orion sounding rockets (capable of reaching altitudes in excess of 300 km) as part of their
        <a href="/wiki/HyShot" title="HyShot">
         HyShot
        </a>
        hypersonics research
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Iranian_Space_Agency" title="Iranian Space Agency">
         Iranian Space Agency
        </a>
        operated its first sounding rocket in February 2007
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/UP_Aerospace" title="UP Aerospace">
         UP Aerospace
        </a>
        operates the
        <a href="/wiki/SpaceLoft_XL" title="SpaceLoft XL">
         SpaceLoft XL
        </a>
        sounding rocket that can reach altitudes of 225 km
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/TEXUS" title="TEXUS">
         TEXUS
        </a>
        and MiniTEXUS, German rocket programmes at
        <a href="/wiki/Esrange" title="Esrange">
         Esrange
        </a>
        for
        <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">
         DLR
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">
         ESA
        </a>
        microgravity research programmes
       </li>
       <li>
        Astrium operates missions with sounding rockets on a commercial basis, as prime contractor to ESA or the German Aerospace Centre (DLR).
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Maser_(rocket)" title="Maser (rocket)">
         MASER
        </a>
        , Swedish rocket programme at
        <a href="/wiki/Esrange" title="Esrange">
         Esrange
        </a>
        for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">
         ESA
        </a>
        microgravity research programmes
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Maxus_(rocket)" title="Maxus (rocket)">
         MAXUS
        </a>
        , German-Swedish rocket programme at
        <a href="/wiki/Esrange" title="Esrange">
         Esrange
        </a>
        for
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">
         ESA
        </a>
        microgravity research programmes
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Rexus_and_Bexus" title="Rexus and Bexus">
         REXUS
        </a>
        , German-Swedish rocket programme at
        <a href="/wiki/Esrange" title="Esrange">
         Esrange
        </a>
        for
        <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">
         DLR
        </a>
        and
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">
         ESA
        </a>
        student experiment programmes
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NASA_Sounding_Rocket_Program&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NASA Sounding Rocket Program (page does not exist)">
         NASA Sounding Rocket Program
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/JAXA" title="JAXA">
         JAXA
        </a>
        operates the sounding rockets
        <a href="/wiki/S-Series_(rocket_family)" title="S-Series (rocket family)">
         S-Series
        </a>
        : S-310 / S-520 / SS-520.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
         New Zealand
        </a>
        company
        <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Lab" title="Rocket Lab">
         Rocket Lab
        </a>
        is developing the highly adaptable Atea series of sounding rockets to carry 5–70 kg payloads to altitudes of 250 km or greater
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Meteor_(rocket)" title="Meteor (rocket)">
         Meteor
        </a>
        rockets were built in
        <a href="/wiki/Poland" title="Poland">
         Poland
        </a>
        between 1963 and 1974.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Kartika_I" title="Kartika I">
         Kartika I
        </a>
        rocket was built and launched in
        <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
         Indonesia
        </a>
        by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LAPAN" title="LAPAN">
         LAPAN
        </a>
        on 1964, becoming the second sounding rocket in Asia after Japan.
       </li>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
         Soviet Union
        </a>
        developed an extensive program using rockets such as the
        <a href="/wiki/M-100_(rocket)" title="M-100 (rocket)">
         M-100
        </a>
        , the most used ever; its successor by its successor state,
        <a href="/wiki/Russia" title="Russia">
         Russia
        </a>
        , is the
        <a href="/wiki/MR-20" title="MR-20">
         MR-20
        </a>
        and later the MR-30.
       </li>
       <li>
        Brazil has been launching its own sounding rockets since 1965. The largest and most current family of rocket are the Sonda rockets, which are the R&amp;D basis for Brazil's soon-to-be-launched VLS satellite launcher.
       </li>
      </ul>
