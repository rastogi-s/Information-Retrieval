   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunisolar calendar
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    </div>

    </div>

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunisolar calendar
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Calendar" title="Calendar">
        calendar
       </a>
       in many
       <a href="/wiki/Culture" title="Culture">
        cultures
       </a>
       whose date indicates both the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_phase" title="Moon phase">
        moon phase
       </a>
       and the time of the solar
       <a href="/wiki/Year" title="Year">
        year
       </a>
       . If the solar year is defined as a
       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
        tropical year
       </a>
       , then a lunisolar calendar will give an indication of the
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        season
       </a>
       ; if it is taken as a
       <a href="/wiki/Sidereal_year" title="Sidereal year">
        sidereal year
       </a>
       , then the calendar will predict the
       <a href="/wiki/Constellation" title="Constellation">
        constellation
       </a>
       near which the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       may occur. As with all calendars which divide the year into months there is an additional requirement that the year have a whole number of months. In this case ordinary years consist of twelve months but every second or third year is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Embolismic_month" title="Embolismic month">
        embolismic year
       </a>
       , which adds a thirteenth
       <a href="/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping)" title="Intercalation (timekeeping)">
        intercalary
       </a>
       , embolismic, or leap month.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">
        Examples
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Examples">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jain_calendar" title="Jain calendar">
        Jain
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Buddhist_calendar" title="Buddhist calendar">
        Buddhist
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu_calendar" title="Hindu calendar">
        Hindu
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Kurdish_calendar" title="Kurdish calendar">
        Kurdish
       </a>
       as well as the traditional
       <a href="/wiki/Tibetan_calendar" title="Tibetan calendar">
        Tibetan
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Vietnamese_calendar" title="Vietnamese calendar">
        Vietnamese
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mongolian_calendar" title="Mongolian calendar">
        Mongolian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Korean_calendar" title="Korean calendar">
        Korean
       </a>
       calendars (in the
       <a href="/wiki/East_Asian_cultural_sphere" title="East Asian cultural sphere">
        east Asian cultural sphere
       </a>
       ), plus the ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hellenic_calendars" title="Hellenic calendars">
        Hellenic
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Coligny_calendar" title="Coligny calendar">
        Coligny
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_calendar" title="Babylonian calendar">
        Babylonian calendars
       </a>
       are all lunisolar. Also, some of the ancient
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar#Pre-Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        pre-Islamic
       </a>
       calendars in
       <a href="/wiki/South_Arabia" title="South Arabia">
        south Arabia
       </a>
       followed a lunisolar system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EI2-Tarikh_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EI2-Tarikh-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Chinese, Coligny and Hebrew
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       lunisolar calendars track more or less the tropical year whereas the Buddhist and Hindu lunisolar calendars track the sidereal year. Therefore, the first three give an idea of the seasons whereas the last two give an idea of the position among the constellations of the full moon. The Tibetan calendar was influenced by both the Chinese and Hindu calendars. The
       <a href="/wiki/Germanic_calendar" title="Germanic calendar">
        Germanic peoples also used a lunisolar calendar
       </a>
       before their conversion to Christianity.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">
        lunar
       </a>
       , but not a lunisolar calendar because its date is not related to the sun. The civil versions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">
        Julian
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendars
       </a>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_calendar" title="Solar calendar">
        solar
       </a>
       , because their dates do not indicate the moon phase — however, both the Gregorian and Julian calendars include undated lunar calendars that allow them to
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        calculate
       </a>
       the Christian celebration of
       <a href="/wiki/Easter" title="Easter">
        Easter
       </a>
       , so both are lunisolar calendars in that respect.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Determining_leap_months">
        Determining leap months
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Determining leap months">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A rough idea of the frequency of the intercalary or leap month in all lunisolar calendars can be obtained by the following calculation, using approximate lengths of months and years in days:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Year: 365.25, Month: 29.53
       </li>
       <li>
        365.25/(12 × 29.53) = 1.0307
       </li>
       <li>
        1/0.0307 = 32.57 common months between leap months
       </li>
       <li>
        32.57/12 = 2.7 common years between leap years
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Intercalation of leap months is frequently controlled by the "epact", which is the difference between the lunar and solar years (approximately 11 days). The Metonic cycle, used in the Hebrew calendar and the Julian and Gregorian ecclesiastical calendars, adds seven months every nineteen years. The classic Metonic cycle can be reproduced by assigning an initial epact value of 1 to the last year of the cycle and incrementing by 11 each year. Between the last year of one cycle and the first year of the next the increment is 12. This adjustment, the
       <i>
        saltus lunae
       </i>
       , causes the epacts to repeat every 19 years. When the epact goes above 29 an intercalary month is added and 30 is subtracted. The intercalary years are numbers 3, 6, 8, 11, 14, 17 and 19. Both the Hebrew calendar and the Julian calendar use this sequence.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Buddhist and Hebrew calendars restrict the leap month to a single month of the year; the number of common months between leap months is, therefore, usually 36, but occasionally only 24 months. Because the Chinese and Hindu lunisolar calendars allow the leap month to occur after or before (respectively) any month but use the true motion of the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        sun
       </a>
       , their leap months do not usually occur within a couple of months of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       , when the apparent speed of the sun along the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       is fastest (now about 3 January). This increases the usual number of common months between leap months to roughly 34 months when a doublet of common years occurs, while reducing the number to about 29 months when only a common singleton occurs.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="With_uncounted_time">
        With uncounted time
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: With uncounted time">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       An alternative way of dealing with the fact that a solar year does not contain an integer number of months is by including uncounted time in the year that does not belong to any month. Some
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coast_Salish_peoples" title="Coast Salish peoples">
        Coast Salish peoples
       </a>
       used a calendar of this kind. For instance, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sts%27Ailes" title="Sts'Ailes">
        Chehalis
       </a>
       began their count of lunar months from the arrival of spawning
       <a href="/wiki/Chinook_salmon" title="Chinook salmon">
        chinook salmon
       </a>
       (in Gregorian calendar October), and counted 10 months, leaving an uncounted period until the next chinook
       <a href="/wiki/Salmon_run" title="Salmon run">
        salmon run
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Gregorian_lunisolar_calendar">
        Gregorian lunisolar calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunisolar_calendar&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Gregorian lunisolar calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Gregorian calendar has a lunisolar calendar, which is used to determine the date of Easter. The rules are in the
       <a href="/wiki/Computus" title="Computus">
        Computus
       </a>
       .
      </p>

          </span>
          <span class="reference-text">
           The modern Hebrew calendar, since it is based on rules rather than observations, does not exactly track the tropical year, and in fact the average Hebrew year of ~365.2468 days is intermediate between the tropical year (~365.2422 days) and the sidereal year (~365.2564 days).
          </span>
         </li>
