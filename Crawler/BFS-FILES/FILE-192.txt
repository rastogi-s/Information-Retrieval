      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Physical Review
        </b>
       </i>
       is an American
       <a href="/wiki/Peer_review" title="Peer review">
        peer-reviewed
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_journal" title="Scientific journal">
        scientific journal
       </a>
       established in 1893 by
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Leamington_Nichols" title="Edward Leamington Nichols">
        Edward Nichols
       </a>
       . It publishes
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Original_research" title="Original research">
        original research
       </a>
       as well as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scientific_review" title="Scientific review">
        scientific
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Literature_review" title="Literature review">
        literature reviews
       </a>
       on all aspects of
       <a href="/wiki/Physics" title="Physics">
        physics
       </a>
       . It is published by the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">
        American Physical Society
       </a>
       (APS). The journal is in its third series, and is split in several sub-journals each covering a particular field of physics. It has a sister journal,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Letters" title="Physical Review Letters">
         Physical Review Letters
        </a>
       </i>
       , which publishes shorter articles of broader interest.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Physical_Review&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        Physical Review
       </i>
       commenced publication in July 1893, organized by
       <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">
        Cornell University
       </a>
       professor
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Leamington_Nichols" title="Edward Leamington Nichols">
        Edward Nichols
       </a>
       and helped by the new president of Cornell,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._Gould_Schurman&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. Gould Schurman (page does not exist)">
        J. Gould Schurman
       </a>
       . The journal was managed and edited at Cornell in upstate
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
        New York
       </a>
       from 1893 to 1913 by Nichols,
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Merritt" title="Ernest Merritt">
        Ernest Merritt
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Frederick_Bedell&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Frederick Bedell (page does not exist)">
        Frederick Bedell
       </a>
       . The 33 volumes published during this time constitute
       <i>
        Physical Review Series I
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/American_Physical_Society" title="American Physical Society">
        American Physical Society
       </a>
       (APS), founded in 1899, took over its publication in 1913 and started
       <i>
        Physical Review Series II
       </i>
       . The journal remained at Cornell under
       <a href="/wiki/Editor-in-chief" title="Editor-in-chief">
        editor-in-chief
       </a>
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=G._S._Fulcher&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="G. S. Fulcher (page does not exist)">
        G. S. Fulcher
       </a>
       from 1913 to 1926, before relocating to the location of editor
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Torrence_Tate,_Sr." title="John Torrence Tate, Sr.">
        John Torrence Tate, Sr.
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [nb 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Minnesota" title="University of Minnesota">
        University of Minnesota
       </a>
       . In 1929, the APS started publishing
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Reviews_of_Modern_Physics" title="Reviews of Modern Physics">
         Reviews of Modern Physics
        </a>
       </i>
       , a venue for longer review articles.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the
       <a href="/wiki/Great_Depression" title="Great Depression">
        Great Depression
       </a>
       , wealthy scientist
       <a href="/wiki/Alfred_Lee_Loomis" title="Alfred Lee Loomis">
        Alfred Loomis
       </a>
       anonymously paid the journal's fees for authors who could not afford them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       After Tate's death in 1950, the journals were managed on an interim basis still in Minnesota by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=E._L._Hill&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="E. L. Hill (page does not exist)">
        E. L. Hill
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J._William_Buchta&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="J. William Buchta (page does not exist)">
        J. William Buchta
       </a>
       until
       <a href="/wiki/Samuel_Goudsmit" title="Samuel Goudsmit">
        Samuel Goudsmit
       </a>
       and Simon Pasternack were appointed and the editorial office moved to
       <a href="/wiki/Brookhaven_National_Laboratory" title="Brookhaven National Laboratory">
        Brookhaven National Laboratory
       </a>
       on Eastern
       <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">
        Long Island
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_(state)" title="New York (state)">
        New York
       </a>
       . In July 1958, the sister journal
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Letters" title="Physical Review Letters">
         Physical Review Letters
        </a>
       </i>
       was introduced to publish short articles of particularly broad interest, initially edited by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=George_L._Trigg&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="George L. Trigg (page does not exist)">
        George L. Trigg
       </a>
       , who remained as editor until 1988.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1970,
       <i>
        Physical Review
       </i>
       split into sub-journals
       <i>
        Physical Review A
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        B
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        C
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        D
       </i>
       . A fifth member of the family,
       <i>
        Physical Review E
       </i>
       , was introduced in 1993 to a large part to accommodate the huge amount of new research in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nonlinear_dynamics" title="Nonlinear dynamics">
        nonlinear dynamics
       </a>
       . Combined, these constitute
       <i>
        Physical Review Series III
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The editorial office moved in 1980 to its present location across the expressway from Brookhaven National Laboratory. Goudsmit retired in 1974 and Pasternack in the mid-1970s. Past Editors in Chief include
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=David_Lazarus_(physicist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="David Lazarus (physicist) (page does not exist)">
        David Lazarus
       </a>
       (1980–1990;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_Illinois_at_Urbana-Champaign" title="University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign">
        University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Benjamin_Bederson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Benjamin Bederson (page does not exist)">
        Benjamin Bederson
       </a>
       (1990–1996;
       <a href="/wiki/New_York_University" title="New York University">
        New York University
       </a>
       ),
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Martin_Blume&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Martin Blume (page does not exist)">
        Martin Blume
       </a>
       (1996–2007; Brookhaven National Laboratory), and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gene_Sprouse" title="Gene Sprouse">
        Gene Sprouse
       </a>
       (2007–2015;
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SUNY_Stony_Brook" title="SUNY Stony Brook">
        SUNY Stony Brook
       </a>
       ). The current Editor in Chief is
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Michael_Thoennessen&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Michael Thoennessen (page does not exist)">
        Michael Thoennessen
       </a>
       , whose term began in September 2017.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       To celebrate the hundredth anniversary of the journal, a memoir was published jointly by the APS and AIP.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1998, the first issue of
       <i>
        Physical Review Special Topics: Accelerators and Beams
       </i>
       was published, and in 2005,
       <i>
        Physical Review Special Topics: Physics Education Research
       </i>
       was launched. In January 2016 the names of both journals were changed to remove "Special Topics".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-special-topics-change_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-special-topics-change-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Physical Review
       </i>
       also started an online magazine,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Physical_Review_Focus" title="Physical Review Focus">
         Physical Review Focus
        </a>
       </i>
       , in 1998 to explain and provide historical context for selected articles from
       <i>
        Physical Review
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        Physical Review Letters
       </i>
       . This was merged into
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physics_(American_Physical_Society_journal)" title="Physics (American Physical Society journal)">
         Physics
        </a>
       </i>
       in 2011. The Special Topics journals are
       <a href="/wiki/Open_access" title="Open access">
        open access
       </a>
       ;
       <i>
        Physics Education Research
       </i>
       requires page charges from the authors, but
       <i>
        Accelerators and Beams
       </i>
       does not. Though not fully open access,
       <i>
        Physical Review Letters
       </i>
       also requires an author page charge, although this is voluntary. The other journals require such a charge only if manuscripts are not prepared in one of the preferred formats.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Authors can pay extra charges to make their papers open access.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-oa_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-oa-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such papers are published under the terms of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Creative_Commons_Attribution_3.0_License" title="Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 License">
        Creative Commons Attribution 3.0 License
       </a>
       (CC-BY).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Physical Review Letters
       </i>
       celebrated their 50th birthday in 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The APS has a
       <a href="/wiki/Copyright" title="Copyright">
        copyright
       </a>
       policy to permit the author to reuse parts of the published article in a derivative or new work, including on
       <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia" title="Wikipedia">
        Wikipedia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The APS has an online publication entitled
       <i>
        Physics
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       aiming to help physicists and physics students to learn about new developments outside of their own subfield. This now includes the general-interest articles that appeared as
       <i>
        Physical Review Focus
       </i>
       . It also publishes
       <i>
        Physical Review X
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       an online-only gold
       <a href="/wiki/Open_access_journal" title="Open access journal">
        open access journal
       </a>
       . It is a peer-reviewed journal that publishes, as timely as possible, original research papers from all areas of pure, applied, and interdisciplinary physics. In 2014
       <i>
        Physical Review Applied
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       began publishing research across all aspects of experimental and theoretical applications of physics, including their interactions with other sciences, engineering, and industry. In 2016 the APS launched
       <i>
        Physical Review Fluids
       </i>
       "to include additional areas of
       <a href="/wiki/Fluid_dynamics" title="Fluid dynamics">
        fluid dynamics
       </a>
       research",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and in 2017 it launched
       <i>
        Physical Review Materials
       </i>
       "to fill a gap" in the coverage of materials research.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
