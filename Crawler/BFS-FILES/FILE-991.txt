      <p>
       <b>
        Seismology
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ; from
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       σεισμός (
       <i>
        seismós
       </i>
       ) meaning "earthquake" and -λογία (
       <i>
        -logía
       </i>
       ) meaning "study of") is the scientific study of
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        earthquakes
       </a>
       and the propagation of
       <a href="/wiki/Linear_elasticity#Elastic_wave" title="Linear elasticity">
        elastic waves
       </a>
       through the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       or through other planet-like bodies. The field also includes studies of
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_environmental_effects" title="Earthquake environmental effects">
        earthquake environmental effects
       </a>
       such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tsunamis" title="Tsunamis">
        tsunamis
       </a>
       as well as diverse
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_source" title="Seismic source">
        seismic sources
       </a>
       such as volcanic, tectonic, oceanic, atmospheric, and artificial processes such as explosions. A related field that uses
       <a href="/wiki/Geology" title="Geology">
        geology
       </a>
       to infer information regarding past earthquakes is
       <a href="/wiki/Paleoseismology" title="Paleoseismology">
        paleoseismology
       </a>
       . A recording of earth motion as a function of time is called a
       <a href="/wiki/Seismogram" title="Seismogram">
        seismogram
       </a>
       . A seismologist is a scientist who does research in seismology.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <table class="plainlinks metadata ambox ambox-content ambox-Refimprove" role="presentation">
       <tr>
        <td class="mbox-image">
         <div style="width:52px">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg">

      <p>
       Scholarly interest in earthquakes can be traced back to antiquity. Early speculations on the natural causes of earthquakes were included in the writings of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thales" title="Thales">
        Thales
       </a>
       of Miletus (c. 585 BCE),
       <a href="/wiki/Anaximenes_of_Miletus" title="Anaximenes of Miletus">
        Anaximenes
       </a>
       of Miletus (c. 550 BCE),
       <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">
        Aristotle
       </a>
       (c. 340 BCE) and
       <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">
        Zhang Heng
       </a>
       (132 CE).
      </p>
      <p>
       In 132 CE,
       <a href="/wiki/Zhang_Heng" title="Zhang Heng">
        Zhang Heng
       </a>
       of China's
       <a href="/wiki/Han_dynasty" title="Han dynasty">
        Han dynasty
       </a>
       designed the first known seismoscope.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1664,
       <a href="/wiki/Athanasius_Kircher" title="Athanasius Kircher">
        Athanasius Kircher
       </a>
       argued that earthquakes were caused by the movement of fire within a system of channels inside the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1703,
       <a href="/wiki/Martin_Lister" title="Martin Lister">
        Martin Lister
       </a>
       (1638 to 1712) and
       <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Lemery" title="Nicolas Lemery">
        Nicolas Lemery
       </a>
       (1645 to 1715) proposed that earthquakes were caused by chemical explosions within the earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">
        Lisbon earthquake of 1755
       </a>
       , coinciding with the general flowering of science in Europe, set in motion intensified scientific attempts to understand the behaviour and causation of earthquakes. The earliest responses include work by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Bevis" title="John Bevis">
        John Bevis
       </a>
       (1757) and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Michell" title="John Michell">
        John Michell
       </a>
       (1761). Michell determined that earthquakes originate within the Earth and were waves of movement caused by "shifting masses of rock miles below the surface."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From 1857,
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Mallet" title="Robert Mallet">
        Robert Mallet
       </a>
       laid the foundation of instrumental seismology and carried out seismological experiments using explosives. He is also responsible for coining the word "seismology".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1897,
       <a href="/wiki/Emil_Wiechert" title="Emil Wiechert">
        Emil Wiechert
       </a>
       's theoretical calculations led him to conclude that the
       <a href="/wiki/Structure_of_the_Earth" title="Structure of the Earth">
        Earth's interior
       </a>
       consists of a mantle of silicates, surrounding a core of iron.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1906
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dixon_Oldham" title="Richard Dixon Oldham">
        Richard Dixon Oldham
       </a>
       identified the separate arrival of P-waves, S-waves and surface waves on seismograms and found the first clear evidence that the Earth has a central core.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1910, after studying the April
       <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">
        1906 San Francisco earthquake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Fielding_Reid" title="Harry Fielding Reid">
        Harry Fielding Reid
       </a>
       put forward the "
       <a href="/wiki/Elastic-rebound_theory" title="Elastic-rebound theory">
        elastic rebound theory
       </a>
       " which remains the foundation for modern tectonic studies. The development of this theory depended on the considerable progress of earlier independent streams of work on the behaviour of elastic materials and in mathematics.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1926,
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Jeffreys" title="Harold Jeffreys">
        Harold Jeffreys
       </a>
       was the first to claim, based on his study of earthquake waves, that below the mantle, the core of the Earth is liquid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1937,
       <a href="/wiki/Inge_Lehmann" title="Inge Lehmann">
        Inge Lehmann
       </a>
       determined that within the earth's liquid outer core there is a solid
       <i>
        inner
       </i>
       core.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By the 1960s, earth science had developed to the point where a comprehensive theory of the causation of seismic events had come together in the now well-established theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        plate tectonics
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Types_of_seismic_wave">
        Types of seismic wave
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Types of seismic wave">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave" title="Seismic wave">
        Seismic wave
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:322px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seismogram.gif">
         <img alt="Three lines with frequent vertical excursions." class="thumbimage" data-file-height="420" data-file-width="619" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Seismogram.gif/320px-Seismogram.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Seismogram.gif/480px-Seismogram.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a6/Seismogram.gif 2x" width="320"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seismogram.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Seismogram records showing the three components of ground motion. The red line marks the first arrival of P-waves; the green line, the later arrival of S-waves.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Seismic waves are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elastic_waves" title="Elastic waves">
        elastic waves
       </a>
       that propagate in solid or fluid materials. They can be divided into
       <i>
        body waves
       </i>
       that travel through the interior of the materials;
       <i>
        surface waves
       </i>
       that travel along surfaces or interfaces between materials; and
       <i>
        normal modes
       </i>
       , a form of standing wave.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Body_waves">
        Body waves
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Body waves">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are two types of body waves, Pressure waves or Primary waves (
       <a href="/wiki/P-wave" title="P-wave">
        P-waves
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Shearing_(physics)" title="Shearing (physics)">
        Shear
       </a>
       or Secondary waves (
       <a href="/wiki/S-wave" title="S-wave">
        S-waves
       </a>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/P-wave" title="P-wave">
        P-waves
       </a>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Longitudinal_wave" title="Longitudinal wave">
        longitudinal waves
       </a>
       that involve
       <a href="/wiki/Compression_(geology)" title="Compression (geology)">
        compression
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rarefaction" title="Rarefaction">
        expansion
       </a>
       in the direction that the wave is moving and are always the first waves to appear on a
       <a href="/wiki/Seismogram" title="Seismogram">
        seismogram
       </a>
       as they are the fastest moving waves through solids.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S-waves" title="S-waves">
        S-waves
       </a>
       are
       <a href="/wiki/Transverse_wave" title="Transverse wave">
        transverse waves
       </a>
       that move perpendicular to the direction of propagation. S-waves are slower than P-waves. Therefore, they appear later than P-waves on a seismogram. Fluids cannot support perpendicular motion, so S-waves only travel in solids.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gubbins1990_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gubbins1990-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_waves">
        Surface waves
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface waves">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The two main surface wave types are Rayleigh waves, which have some compressional motion, and
       <a href="/wiki/Love_wave" title="Love wave">
        Love waves
       </a>
       , which do not. Rayleigh waves result from the interaction of vertically polarized P- and S-waves that satisfy the boundary conditions on the surface. Love waves can exist in the presence of a subsurface layer, and are only formed by horizontally polarized S-waves. Surface waves travel more slowly than P-waves and S-waves; however, because they are guided by the Earth's surface and their energy is thus trapped near the surface, they can be much stronger than body waves, and can be the largest signals on earthquake seismograms. Surface waves are strongly excited when their source is close to the surface, as in a shallow earthquake or a near surface explosion.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gubbins1990_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gubbins1990-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Normal_modes">
        Normal modes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Normal modes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_wave#Free_oscillations_of_the_Earth" title="Seismic wave">
        Free oscillations of the Earth
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Both body and surface waves are traveling waves; however, large earthquakes can also make the Earth "ring" like a bell. This ringing is a mixture of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_modes" title="Normal modes">
        normal modes
       </a>
       with discrete frequencies and periods of an hour or shorter. Motion caused by a large earthquake can be observed for up to a month after the event.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gubbins1990_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gubbins1990-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first observations of normal modes were made in the 1960s as the advent of higher fidelity instruments coincided with two of the largest earthquakes of the 20th century – the
       <a href="/wiki/1960_Valdivia_earthquake" title="1960 Valdivia earthquake">
        1960 Valdivia earthquake
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/1964_Alaska_earthquake" title="1964 Alaska earthquake">
        1964 Alaska earthquake
       </a>
       . Since then, the normal modes of the Earth have given us some of the strongest constraints on the deep structure of the Earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquakes">
        Earthquakes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Earthquakes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main articles:
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        Earthquake
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lists_of_earthquakes" title="Lists of earthquakes">
        Lists of earthquakes
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       One of the first attempts at the scientific study of earthquakes followed the
       <a href="/wiki/1755_Lisbon_earthquake" title="1755 Lisbon earthquake">
        1755 Lisbon earthquake
       </a>
       . Other notable earthquakes that spurred major advancements in the science of seismology include the
       <a href="/wiki/1857_Basilicata_earthquake" title="1857 Basilicata earthquake">
        1857 Basilicata earthquake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/1906_San_Francisco_earthquake" title="1906 San Francisco earthquake">
        1906 San Francisco earthquake
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/1964_Alaska_earthquake" title="1964 Alaska earthquake">
        1964 Alaska earthquake
       </a>
       , the 2004
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sumatra-Andaman_earthquake" title="Sumatra-Andaman earthquake">
        Sumatra-Andaman earthquake
       </a>
       , and the 2011
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tohoku_earthquake" title="Tohoku earthquake">
        Great East Japan earthquake
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Controlled_seismic_sources">
        Controlled seismic sources
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Controlled seismic sources">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Reflection_seismology" title="Reflection seismology">
        Reflection seismology
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Seismic waves produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Explosion" title="Explosion">
        explosions
       </a>
       or vibrating controlled sources are one of the primary methods of
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_geophysics" title="Exploration geophysics">
        underground exploration in geophysics
       </a>
       (in addition to many different
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation" title="Electromagnetic radiation">
        electromagnetic
       </a>
       methods such as
       <a href="/wiki/Induced_polarization" title="Induced polarization">
        induced polarization
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetotellurics" title="Magnetotellurics">
        magnetotellurics
       </a>
       ). Controlled-source seismology has been used to map
       <a href="/wiki/Salt_dome" title="Salt dome">
        salt domes
       </a>
       , anticlines and other geologic traps in
       <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">
        petroleum
       </a>
       -bearing
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rocks
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_(geology)" title="Fault (geology)">
        faults
       </a>
       , rock types, and long-buried giant
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor" title="Meteor">
        meteor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       . For example, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chicxulub_Crater" title="Chicxulub Crater">
        Chicxulub Crater
       </a>
       , which was caused by an impact that has been
       <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event">
        implicated in the extinction
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">
        dinosaurs
       </a>
       , was localized to Central America by analyzing ejecta in the
       <a href="/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_boundary" title="Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary">
        Cretaceous–Paleogene boundary
       </a>
       , and then physically proven to exist using seismic maps from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oil_exploration" title="Oil exploration">
        oil exploration
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schulte2010_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schulte2010-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Detection_of_seismic_waves">
        Detection of seismic waves
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Detection of seismic waves">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Seismometer-iceland.JPG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Seismometer-iceland.JPG/220px-Seismometer-iceland.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Seismometer-iceland.JPG/330px-Seismometer-iceland.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/Seismometer-iceland.JPG/440px-Seismometer-iceland.JPG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Seismometer-iceland.JPG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Installation for a temporary seismic station, north Iceland highland.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">
        Seismometers
       </a>
       are sensors that detect and record the motion of the Earth arising from elastic waves. Seismometers may be deployed at the Earth's surface, in shallow vaults, in boreholes, or
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean-bottom_seismometer" title="Ocean-bottom seismometer">
        underwater
       </a>
       . A complete instrument package that records seismic signals is called a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seismograph" title="Seismograph">
        seismograph
       </a>
       . Networks of seismographs continuously record ground motions around the world to facilitate the monitoring and analysis of global earthquakes and other sources of seismic activity. Rapid location of earthquakes makes
       <a href="/wiki/Tsunami" title="Tsunami">
        tsunami
       </a>
       warnings possible because seismic waves travel considerably faster than tsunami waves. Seismometers also record signals from non-earthquake sources ranging from explosions (nuclear and chemical), to local noise from wind
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or anthropogenic activities, to incessant signals generated at the ocean floor and coasts induced by ocean waves (the global
       <a href="/wiki/Microseism" title="Microseism">
        microseism
       </a>
       ), to
       <a href="/wiki/Cryosphere" title="Cryosphere">
        cryospheric
       </a>
       events associated with large icebergs and glaciers. Above-ocean meteor strikes with energies as high as 4.2 × 10
       <sup>
        13
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Joule" title="Joule">
        J
       </a>
       (equivalent to that released by an explosion of ten kilotons of TNT) have been recorded by seismographs, as have a number of industrial accidents and terrorist bombs and events (a field of study referred to as
       <a href="/wiki/Forensic_seismology" title="Forensic seismology">
        forensic seismology
       </a>
       ). A major long-term motivation for the global seismographic monitoring has been for the detection and study of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_testing" title="Nuclear testing">
        nuclear testing
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Mapping_the_earth.27s_interior">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mapping_the_earth's_interior">
        Mapping the earth's interior
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Mapping the earth's interior">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_interior" title="Earth's interior">
        Earth's interior
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:282px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earthquake_wave_paths.svg">
         <img alt="Diagram with concentric shells and curved paths" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="250" data-file-width="300" height="233" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Earthquake_wave_paths.svg/280px-Earthquake_wave_paths.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Earthquake_wave_paths.svg/420px-Earthquake_wave_paths.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Earthquake_wave_paths.svg/560px-Earthquake_wave_paths.svg.png 2x" width="280"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earthquake_wave_paths.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Seismic velocities and boundaries in the interior of the
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         sampled by seismic waves
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because seismic waves commonly propagate efficiently as they interact with the internal structure of the Earth, they provide high-resolution noninvasive methods for studying the planet's interior. One of the earliest important discoveries (suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Dixon_Oldham" title="Richard Dixon Oldham">
        Richard Dixon Oldham
       </a>
       in 1906 and definitively shown by
       <a href="/wiki/Harold_Jeffreys" title="Harold Jeffreys">
        Harold Jeffreys
       </a>
       in 1926) was that the
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_core" title="Outer core">
        outer core
       </a>
       of the earth is liquid. Since S-waves do not pass through liquids, the liquid core causes a "shadow" on the side of the planet opposite the earthquake where no direct S-waves are observed. In addition, P-waves travel much slower through the outer core than the mantle.
      </p>
      <p>
       Processing readings from many seismometers using
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_tomography" title="Seismic tomography">
        seismic tomography
       </a>
       , seismologists have mapped the mantle of the earth to a resolution of several hundred kilometers. This has enabled scientists to identify
       <a href="/wiki/Convection_cell" title="Convection cell">
        convection cells
       </a>
       and other large-scale features such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Large_low-shear-velocity_provinces" title="Large low-shear-velocity provinces">
        large low-shear-velocity provinces
       </a>
       near the
       <a href="/wiki/Core%E2%80%93mantle_boundary" title="Core–mantle boundary">
        core–mantle boundary
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Earthquake_prediction">
        Earthquake prediction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Earthquake prediction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_prediction" title="Earthquake prediction">
        Earthquake prediction
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Forecasting a probable timing, location, magnitude and other important features of a forthcoming seismic event is called
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_prediction" title="Earthquake prediction">
        earthquake prediction
       </a>
       . Various attempts have been made by seismologists and others to create effective systems for precise earthquake predictions, including the
       <a href="/wiki/VAN_method" title="VAN method">
        VAN method
       </a>
       . Most seismologists do not believe that a system to provide timely warnings for individual earthquakes has yet been developed, and many believe that such a system would be unlikely to give useful warning of impending seismic events. However, more general forecasts routinely predict
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_hazard" title="Seismic hazard">
        seismic hazard
       </a>
       . Such forecasts estimate the probability of an earthquake of a particular size affecting a particular location within a particular time-span, and they are routinely used in
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_engineering" title="Earthquake engineering">
        earthquake engineering
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Public controversy over earthquake prediction erupted after Italian authorities
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indicted" title="Indicted">
        indicted
       </a>
       six seismologists and one government official for
       <a href="/wiki/Manslaughter" title="Manslaughter">
        manslaughter
       </a>
       in connection with
       <a href="/wiki/2009_L%27Aquila_earthquake" title="2009 L'Aquila earthquake">
        a magnitude 6.3 earthquake in L'Aquila, Italy on April 5, 2009
       </a>
       . The indictment has been widely perceived
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (October 2011)">
           by whom?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       as an indictment for failing to predict the earthquake and has drawn condemnation from the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science" title="American Association for the Advancement of Science">
        American Association for the Advancement of Science
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/American_Geophysical_Union" title="American Geophysical Union">
        American Geophysical Union
       </a>
       . The indictment claims that, at a special meeting in L'Aquila the week before the earthquake occurred, scientists and officials were more interested in pacifying the population than providing adequate information about earthquake risk and preparedness.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_seismology">
        Engineering seismology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Seismology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Engineering seismology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Engineering seismology is the study and application of seismology for engineering purposes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It generally applied to the branch of seismology that deals with the assessment of the
       <a href="/wiki/Seismic_hazard" title="Seismic hazard">
        seismic hazard
       </a>
       of a site or region for the purposes of
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake_engineering" title="Earthquake engineering">
        earthquake engineering
       </a>
       . It is, therefore, a link between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_science" title="Earth science">
        earth science
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Civil_engineering" title="Civil engineering">
        civil engineering
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are two principal components of engineering seismology. Firstly, studying earthquake history (e.g. historical
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and instrumental catalogs
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       of seismicity) and
       <a href="/wiki/Tectonics" title="Tectonics">
        tectonics
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       to assess the
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        earthquakes
       </a>
       that could occur in a region and their characteristics and frequency of occurrence. Secondly, studying strong ground motions generated by earthquakes to assess the expected shaking from future earthquakes with similar characteristics. These strong ground motions could either be observations from
       <a href="/wiki/Accelerometer" title="Accelerometer">
        accelerometers
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Seismometer" title="Seismometer">
        seismometers
       </a>
       or those simulated by computers using various techniques
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , which are then often used to develop ground motion prediction equations
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (or ground-motion models)
       <a class="external autonumber" href="http://www.gmpe.org.uk/" rel="nofollow">
        [1]
       </a>
       .
      </p>
