   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar Flag Assembly
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2365" data-file-width="2349" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg/220px-Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg/330px-Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg/440px-Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Buzz_salutes_the_U.S._Flag.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
          Buzz Aldrin
         </a>
         salutes the first American flag erected on the Moon, July 21, 1969
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar Flag Assembly
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        LFA
       </b>
       ) was a kit containing a
       <a href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_United_States" title="Flag of the United States">
        flag of the United States
       </a>
       designed to be planted by astronauts on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       . Six such flag assemblies were planted on the Moon. The
       <a href="/wiki/Nylon" title="Nylon">
        nylon
       </a>
       flags were hung on telescoping staffs and horizontal bars constructed of one-inch
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anodized_aluminum" title="Anodized aluminum">
        anodized aluminum
       </a>
       tubes. The flags were carried on the outside of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Apollo Lunar Module
       </a>
       , most of them on the descent ladder inside a thermally insulated tubular case to protect them from exhaust gas temperatures calculated to reach 2,000 °F (1,090 °C). The assembly was designed and supervised by
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kinzler" title="Jack Kinzler">
        Jack Kinzler
       </a>
       , head of technical services at the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Manned Spacecraft Center
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">
        Houston
       </a>
       , Texas. Six of the flags were ordered from a government supply catalog and measured 3 by 5 feet (0.91 by 1.52 m); the last one planted on the Moon was the slightly larger, 6-foot (1.8 m)-wide flag which had hung in the Mission Operations Control Room in the Manned Spacecraft Center for most of the Apollo program.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Political_background">
        Political background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flag_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Political background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In January 1969, President
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Richard_M._Nixon" title="Richard M. Nixon">
        Richard M. Nixon
       </a>
       set an international tone for the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       in his inaugural address
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        As we explore the reaches of space, let us go to the new worlds together -- not as new worlds to be conquered, but as a new adventure to be shared.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       This inspired an idea, discussed within
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       , of having the astronauts plant a
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       flag on the first landing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELow1969_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELow1969-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Acting NASA Administrator
       <a href="/wiki/Thomas_O._Paine" title="Thomas O. Paine">
        Thomas O. Paine
       </a>
       appointed a Committee on Symbolic Activities for the First Lunar Landing on February 25, which he instructed to select symbolic activities that would not jeopardize crew safety or interfere with mission objectives; that would "signalize [sic] the first lunar landing as an historic forward step of all mankind that has been accomplished by the United States" and that would not give the impression that the United States was "taking possession of the moon" in violation of the
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty" title="Outer Space Treaty">
        Outer Space Treaty
       </a>
       of 1967. The committee considered several options, including leaving the UN flag, a United States flag, a set of miniature flags of all nations, and another commemorative marker on the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJaffe1969_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaffe1969-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShapley1969_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShapley1969-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPaine1969_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPaine1969-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The committee recommended leaving the U.S. flag along with a commemorative plaque bearing the inscription: "Here men from the planet Earth first set foot upon the Moon July 1969, A.D. We came in peace for all mankind." The memorial would be attached to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       descent stage and left on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Some Americans anticipated possible controversy over planting the United States flag on the Moon, since territorial claims to any extraterrestrial body were prohibited by the Outer Space Treaty.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But since it was made clear the United States had no intention of making a
       <a href="/wiki/Territorial_dispute" title="Territorial dispute">
        territorial claim
       </a>
       to the Moon, no serious controversy materialized. In fact, the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">
        United States Congress
       </a>
       passed a bill in November 1969, which was signed into law by President Nixon, stating:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The flag of the United States, and no other flag, shall be implanted or otherwise placed on the surface of the moon, or on the surface of any planet, by members of the crew of any spacecraft ... as part of any mission ... the funds for which are provided entirely by the Government of the United States. ... this act is intended as a symbolic gesture of national pride in achievement and is not to be construed as a declaration of national appropriation by claim of sovereignty.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993_2-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Design">
        Design
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flag_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Design">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="437" data-file-width="600" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif/220px-Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif/330px-Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif/440px-Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Flag_Assembly.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The first Lunar Flag Assembly, carried on
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
          Apollo 11
         </a>
         , prior to packing and mounting on the
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
          Apollo Lunar Module
         </a>
         ladder
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       About three months prior to the July 1969 Apollo 11 mission,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Gilruth" title="Robert Gilruth">
        Robert Gilruth
       </a>
       , Director of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Manned Spacecraft Center
       </a>
       (MSC) and a member of the Committee on Symbolic Activities, still needed to select someone to design the flag assembly. He asked
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Kinzler" title="Jack Kinzler">
        Jack Kinzler
       </a>
       , head of technical services at MSC and also known as "Mr. Fix It" for the task. Inspired by the memory of his mother hanging curtains during his childhood, Kinzler came up with the idea of inserting a horizontal pole through a hemmed pocket in the top of the flag to support it and make it appear to fly on the airless Moon as it would in the wind on Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moon_Flag_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moon_Flag-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He worked out the details over several days, assisted by Deputy Division Chief Dave McCraw. Kinzler also suggested, designed, and oversaw the creation of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_plaques" title="Lunar plaques">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       affixed to all the Apollo Lunar Modules.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moon_Flag_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moon_Flag-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Though the flag itself was a simple, government supply 3-by-5-foot (0.91 by 1.52 m) nylon flag altered only by sewing the top hem, its packaging, tolerance of environmental conditions, and means of deployment presented minor engineering challenges. The horizontal and vertical poles were each made of one-inch aluminum tubes in two telescoping parts, anodized with a gold color. Due to the limits of the astronauts' spacesuits, the total height of the flagpole was limited to their 28-inch (71 cm) minimum and 66-inch (170 cm) maximum reach. The flag cost $5.50 and the tubing cost $75.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AS12-47-6983_(21668759581).jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="4175" data-file-width="4175" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg/220px-AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg/330px-AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg/440px-AS12-47-6983_%2821668759581%29.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AS12-47-6983_(21668759581).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Apollo 12 flag with faulty latch mechanism
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The flag assembly was stored immediately behind the left side of the LM ladder. Due to the exhaust from the descent engine, the temperatures were calculated to be 250 °F (121 °C) for most of the landing, however the temperatures would increase to 2,000 °F (1,090 °C) during the final 13 seconds at touchdown. To insulate the flag from these extreme temperatures, it had to be packed inside a dual-walled protective shroud consisting of a stainless steel outer case separated from an aluminum layer by Thermoflex insulation, with several layers of Kapton thermal insulation foil between the inner case and the flag. The insulation limited the temperature to which the flag was subjected to 180 °F (82 °C). The shroud was estimated to cost several hundred dollars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPlatoff1993-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The complete package needed to be as light as possible so as not to cut into the lunar payload, and weighed 9 pounds 7 ounces (4.3 kg).
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Detail_of_Apollo_AS12-47-6897.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The Apollo 12 crew could not get the horizontal pole to latch at the proper 90° angle.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The crew of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">
        Charles P. "Pete" Conrad
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Alan_Bean" title="Alan Bean">
        Alan Bean
       </a>
       , had trouble with the latch mechanism which was supposed to keep the supporting pole horizontal, so the flag they deployed drooped at an angle. In response to this, the assembly was improved to include a double-latch locking mechanism for subsequent missions.
      </p>
      <p>
       The flags deployed on the last three landing flights were carried in the
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Modular_Equipment_Stowage_Assembly&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Modular Equipment Stowage Assembly (page does not exist)">
        Modular Equipment Stowage Assembly
       </a>
       (MESA, an equipment drawer which opened from the side of the Lunar Module) rather than on the ladder. This eliminated the need for the thermal protection shroud.
      </p>
      <p>
       The flag which had hung in the Mission Operations Control Room (MOCR) in the
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">
        Mission Control Center
       </a>
       through all the prior Apollo landings was flown to the Moon for the final mission,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       . This flag measured 20% wider and higher than the others, therefore it required a 6-foot (1.8 m) long horizontal pole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Eugene Cernan and Harrison Schmitt carried a second, identical flag to the Moon and back, and presented it to flight controller
       <a href="/wiki/Gene_Kranz" title="Gene Kranz">
        Gene Kranz
       </a>
       after the flight to replace the one left on the Moon.
      </p>

      <p>
       Buzz Aldrin reported that the Apollo 11 flag, placed about 27 feet (8.2 m) from the centerline of the
       <i>
        Eagle
       </i>
       landing craft, was blown over by the blast of the rocket exhaust during takeoff.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SixFlags_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SixFlags-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because of this, care was taken by subsequent crews to place the flags at greater distances from the Lunar Module.
      </p>
      <p>
       The landing on
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">
        Apollo 13
       </a>
       was aborted due to a major spacecraft malfunction encountered before reaching the Moon. The flag carried on that flight was destroyed with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       <i>
        Aquarius
       </i>
       when it reentered the Earth's atmosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       A review of photographs taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       (LRO) indicates that flags placed during the Apollo 12, Apollo 16, and Apollo 17 missions were still standing as of 2012.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SixFlags_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SixFlags-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some experts theorize that the colors of some flags may have turned white due to sunlight and space radiation, or that the fabric might have disintegrated entirely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
