      <p>
       The
       <b>
        lunar maria
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">
            ɑːr
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       (singular:
       <b>
        mare
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ɑːr/: 'ar' in 'far'">
            ɑːr
           </span>
           <span title="/eɪ/: 'a' in 'face'">
            eɪ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       )
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are large, dark,
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basaltic
       </a>
       plains on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , formed by ancient
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic" title="Volcanic">
        volcanic
       </a>
       eruptions. They were dubbed
       <i>
        maria
       </i>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       for "seas", by early astronomers who mistook them for actual
       <a href="/wiki/Sea" title="Sea">
        seas
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are less
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        reflective
       </a>
       than the "highlands" as a result of their iron-rich compositions, and hence appear dark to the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        naked eye
       </a>
       . The maria cover about 16% of the lunar surface, mostly on the
       <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
        side visible from Earth
       </a>
       . The few maria on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side" title="Far side">
        far side
       </a>
       are much smaller, residing mostly in very large craters. The traditional nomenclature for the Moon also includes one
       <i>
        oceanus
       </i>
       (ocean), as well as features with the names
       <i>
        lacus
       </i>
       (lake),
       <i>
        palus
       </i>
       (marsh), and
       <i>
        sinus
       </i>
       (bay). The last three are smaller than maria, but have the same nature and characteristics.
      </p>
      <p>
       The names of maria refer to sea features (Humorum, Imbrium, Insularum, Nubium, Spumans, Undarum, Vaporum, Procellarum, Frigoris), sea attributes (Australe, Orientale, Cognitum, Marginis), or states of mind (Crisium, Ingenii, Serenitatis, Tranquillitatis).
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Humboldtianum" title="Mare Humboldtianum">
        Mare Humboldtianum
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Smythii" title="Mare Smythii">
        Mare Smythii
       </a>
       were established before the final nomenclature, that of states of mind, was accepted, and do not follow this pattern.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       When
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Moscoviense" title="Mare Moscoviense">
        Mare Moscoviense
       </a>
       was discovered by the
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       , and the name was proposed by the Soviet Union, it was only accepted by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       with the justification that Moscow is the state of mind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ages">
        Ages
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_mare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ages">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The ages of the mare basalts have been determined both by direct
       <a href="/wiki/Radiometric_dating" title="Radiometric dating">
        radiometric dating
       </a>
       and by the technique of
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_counting" title="Crater counting">
        crater counting
       </a>
       . The radiometric ages range from about 3.16 to 4.2 Ga,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas the youngest ages determined from crater counting are about 1.2 Ga (1 Ga = 1 billion years old).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nevertheless, the majority of mare basalts appear to have erupted between about 3 and 3.5 Ga. The few basaltic eruptions that occurred on the far side are old, whereas the youngest flows are found within
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       on the nearside. While many of the basalts either erupted within, or flowed into, low-lying impact basins, the largest expanse of volcanic units, Oceanus Procellarum, does not correspond to any known impact basin.
      </p>

      <p>
       There are many common misconceptions concerning the spatial distribution of mare basalts.
      </p>
      <ol>
       <li>
        Since many mare basalts fill low-lying impact basins, it was once assumed that the impact event itself somehow caused the volcanic eruption. [Note: current data in fact may not preclude this, although the timing and length of mare volcanism in a number of basins cast some doubt on it. Initial mare volcanism generally seems to have begun within 100 million years of basin formation.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-7">
          [7]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Although these authors felt that 100 million years was sufficiently long that a correlation between impact and volcanism seemed unlikely, there are problems with this argument.
        <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
         [
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
           <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2015)">
            citation needed
           </span>
          </a>
         </i>
         ]
        </sup>
        The authors also point out that the oldest and deepest basalts in each basin are likely buried and inaccessible, leading to a sampling bias.]
       </li>
       <li>
        It is sometimes suggested that the gravity field of the Earth might preferentially allow eruptions to occur on the
        <a href="/wiki/Near_side_of_the_Moon" title="Near side of the Moon">
         near side
        </a>
        , but not on the
        <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
         far side
        </a>
        . However, in a reference frame rotating with the Moon, the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Centrifugal_acceleration" title="Centrifugal acceleration">
         centrifugal acceleration
        </a>
        the Moon is experiencing is exactly equal and opposite to the gravitational acceleration of the Earth. There is thus no net force directed towards the Earth. The Earth tides do act to deform the shape of the Moon, but this shape is that of an elongated ellipsoid with high points at both the sub- and anti-Earth points. As an analogy, one should remember that there are two high tides per day on Earth, and not one.
       </li>
       <li>
        Since mare basaltic magmas are denser than upper crustal
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anorthositic" title="Anorthositic">
         anorthositic
        </a>
        materials, basaltic eruptions might be favored at locations of low elevation where the crust is thin. However, the
        <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
         far side
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pole-Aitken_basin" title="South Pole-Aitken basin">
         South Pole-Aitken basin
        </a>
        contains the lowest elevations of the Moon and yet is only sparingly filled by basaltic lavas. In addition, the crustal thickness beneath this basin is predicted to be much smaller than beneath
        <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
         Oceanus Procellarum
        </a>
        . While the thickness of the crust might modulate the quantity of basaltic lavas that ultimately reach the surface, crustal thickness by itself cannot be the sole factor controlling the distribution of mare basalts.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
         <a href="#cite_note-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        It is commonly suggested that there is some form of link between the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronous_rotation" title="Synchronous rotation">
         synchronous rotation
        </a>
        of the Moon about the Earth, and the mare basalts. However, gravitational torques that result in tidal despinning only arise from the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moments_of_inertia" title="Moments of inertia">
         moments of inertia
        </a>
        of the body (these are directly relatable to the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spherical_harmonic" title="Spherical harmonic">
         spherical harmonic
        </a>
        degree-2 terms of the gravity field), and the mare basalts hardly contribute to this (see also
        <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
         tidal locking
        </a>
        ). (Hemispheric structures correspond to spherical harmonic degree 1, and do not contribute to the moments of inertia.) Furthermore, tidal despinning is predicted to have occurred quickly (in the order of tens of millions of years), whereas the majority of mare basalts erupted about one billion years later.
       </li>
      </ol>

      <p>
       The reason that the mare basalts are predominantly located on the near-side hemisphere of the Moon is still being debated by the scientific community. Based on data obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       mission, it appears that a large proportion of the Moon's inventory of heat producing elements (in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/KREEP" title="KREEP">
        KREEP
       </a>
       ) is located within the regions of
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Imbrium basin
       </a>
       , a unique geochemical province now referred to as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_terranes" title="Lunar terranes">
        Procellarum KREEP Terrane
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While the enhancement in heat production within the Procellarum KREEP Terrane is most certainly related to the longevity and intensity of volcanism found there, the mechanism by which KREEP became concentrated within this region is not agreed upon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2011, planetary scientists Erik Ahsphaug and Martin Jutzi published a study proposing that the uneven distribution of maria occurred because the moon collided with another, smaller moon of the Earth, a few million years after its formation. In this low-impact collision, the smaller moon was crushed into the surface of the moon, thickening the moon's crust on one side. Subsequent impacts broke through the crust and released lava only where the crust was thin, leading to the observed uneven distribution of maria.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition">
        Composition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_mare&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Using terrestrial classification schemes, all mare basalts are classified as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tholeiitic" title="Tholeiitic">
        tholeiitic
       </a>
       , but specific subclassifications have been invented to further describe the population of lunar basalts. Mare basalts are generally grouped into three series based on their major element chemistry:
       <i>
        high-Ti basalts
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        low-Ti basalts
       </i>
       , and
       <i>
        very-low-Ti (VLT) basalts
       </i>
       . While these groups were once thought to be distinct based on the Apollo samples, global remote sensing data from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine mission
       </a>
       now shows that there is a continuum of titanium concentrations between these end members, and that the high-titanium concentrations are the least abundant. TiO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       abundances can reach up to 15 wt.% for mare basalts, whereas most terrestrial basalts have abundances much less than 4 wt.%. A special group of lunar basalts is the KREEP basalts, which are abnormally rich in
       <a href="/wiki/Potassium" title="Potassium">
        potassium
       </a>
       (K),
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare_earth_elements" title="Rare earth elements">
        rare earth elements
       </a>
       (REE), and
       <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">
        phosphorus
       </a>
       (P). A major difference between terrestrial and lunar basalts is the near-total absence of water in any form in the lunar basalts. Lunar basalts do not contain hydrogen-bearing minerals like the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amphiboles" title="Amphiboles">
        amphiboles
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Phyllosilicates" title="Phyllosilicates">
        phyllosilicates
       </a>
       that are common in terrestrial basalts due to alteration or metamorphism.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
