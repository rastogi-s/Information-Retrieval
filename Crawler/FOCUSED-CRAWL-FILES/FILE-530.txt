      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Netherlands lunar sample displays
       </b>
       are two
       <a href="/wiki/Commemorative_plaque" title="Commemorative plaque">
        commemorative plaques
       </a>
       consisting of small fragments of moon specimens brought back with the Apollo 11 and Apollo 17 Moon missions and given to the people of the
       <a href="/wiki/Netherlands" title="Netherlands">
        Netherlands
       </a>
       by United States President
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">
        Richard Nixon
       </a>
       as goodwill gifts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Netherlands_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_11">
        Apollo 11
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Netherlands_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Apollo 11">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 11 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 11 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Netherlands Apollo 11 lunar sample display commemorative podium style plaque consists of four "moon rock" rice size particle specimens that were collected by Apollo 11 astronauts
       <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
        Neil Armstrong
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       in 1969 and a small Netherland country flag that was taken to the Moon and back.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The four "Moon rocks" weigh about 0.05 grams in total. They are encased in a clear plastic button the size of a coin which is mounted to a wooden board approximately a foot square on a small
       <a href="/wiki/Podium" title="Podium">
        podium
       </a>
       pedestal display. The small podium plaque display also has mounted on it a small
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flag_of_the_Kingdom_of_the_Netherlands" title="Flag of the Kingdom of the Netherlands">
        Netherlands flag
       </a>
       that had been taken to the Moon and back on Apollo 11, which lies directly below the "goodwill moon rocks". The small podium plaque display was given to the people of the country of Netherlands as a gift by United States President Richard Nixon. Similar lunar sample displays were also distributed to all the states of the United States and all the countries of the world.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_17">
        Apollo 17
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Netherlands_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Apollo 17">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17_lunar_sample_display" title="Apollo 17 lunar sample display">
        Apollo 17 lunar sample display
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="578" data-file-width="558" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg/220px-Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg/330px-Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg/440px-Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Netherlands_Apollo_17_display.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <center>
          Netherlands Apollo 17 display
         </center>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Netherlands Apollo 17 lunar sample display commemorative style plaque (10 by 14 inches) consists of one "moon rock" particle specimen that was cut from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_basalt_70017" title="Lunar basalt 70017">
        lunar basalt 70017
       </a>
       and a Netherlands country flag. The
       <a href="/wiki/Basalt" title="Basalt">
        basalt
       </a>
       70017 was collected by Apollo 17 astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       on the Moon in 1972. Once lunar basalt 70017 was brought back to Earth from the Moon, the basalt Moon rock was cut up into small fragments of approximately 1 gram. The specimen was enveloped in a plastic ball and mounted on the wooden plaque along with the Netherlands country flag which had been taken to the moon and back by the crew of Apollo 17. The plaque was then distributed in 1973 by President Richard Nixon to the country of Netherlands as he did that year to 134 other countries worldwide (the same as for the Apollo 11 plaque gifts). This was done as a goodwill gesture to promote peace and harmony.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Netherlands_lunar_sample_displays&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1314" data-file-width="2468" height="117" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg/220px-Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg/330px-Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg/440px-Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rijksmuseum_Amsterdam.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <center>
          Rijksmuseum
         </center>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       While other "goodwill moon rocks" commemorative plaque displays were reported lost or missing by several recipient countries,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       both the Netherlands Apollo 11 and Apollo 17 "goodwill moon rocks" commemorative plaque displays are in the country and accounted for.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterling_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sterling-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in 2009 the
       <a href="/wiki/Rijksmuseum" title="Rijksmuseum">
        Rijksmuseum
       </a>
       of the Netherlands declared that the "goodwill moon rock" it had received in 1992 from the estate of
       <a href="/wiki/Prime_Minister_of_the_Netherlands" title="Prime Minister of the Netherlands">
        Netherlands Prime Minister
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Willem_Drees" title="Willem Drees">
        Willem Drees
       </a>
       was bogus. This "moon rock," which had been exhibited for more than a decade on a gold-colored piece of cardboard, weighs 89 grams (3.1 ounces) – far larger than the 1 gram piece of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_basalt_70017" title="Lunar basalt 70017">
        lunar basalt 70017
       </a>
       affixed to the Netherlands Apollo 17 lunar sample display. Testing of the Rijksmuseum "moon rock" in 2009 showed it to be a piece of
       <a href="/wiki/Petrified_wood" title="Petrified wood">
        petrified wood
       </a>
       , likely from
       <a href="/wiki/Arizona" title="Arizona">
        Arizona
       </a>
       . The Rijksmuseum had insured this "moon rock" for
       <a href="/wiki/Guilder" title="Guilder">
        ƒ
       </a>
       100,000 (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Euros" title="Euros">
        €
       </a>
       50,000 [$85,000] in 2012 currency) upon its receipt.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterling_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sterling-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockcollector_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rockcollector-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topic_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-topic-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fakebbc_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fakebbc-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fakeabc_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fakeabc-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An investigation showed that United States Ambassador
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._William_Middendorf_II" title="J. William Middendorf II">
        J. William Middendorf II
       </a>
       had presented Drees with the "moon rock" on October 9, 1969. The Apollo 11 astronauts were visiting the Netherlands at that time on a goodwill tour. Drees' grandson speculates that his grandfather formed the mistaken impression that the "moon rock" he received was from the Apollo 11 mission. When Drees' "moon rock" was received by the Rijksmuseum in 1992, the museum phoned
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       to verify its provenance and was told over the phone, without seeing the piece, that it was "possible" it was a Moon rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterling_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sterling-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
         USA Today
        </a>
       </i>
       says the discovery of a bogus "moon rock" at the Rijksmuseum should serve as a wake-up call for all the countries of the world and all the states of the United States that received the Apollo 11 and 17 lunar plaque displays from the Nixon administration to locate the displays and fully secure them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sterling_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sterling-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rockcollector_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-rockcollector-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-topic_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-topic-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fakebbc_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-fakebbc-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       According to Moon rock researcher
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_Pearlman" title="Robert Pearlman">
        Robert Pearlman
       </a>
       , both the Netherlands Apollo 11 and Apollo 17 lunar sample displays are in the National Museum of the History of Science and Medicine in
       <a href="/wiki/Leiden" title="Leiden">
        Leiden
       </a>
       , Netherlands.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_11_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_11-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-moon_rocks_Apollo_17_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-moon_rocks_Apollo_17-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
