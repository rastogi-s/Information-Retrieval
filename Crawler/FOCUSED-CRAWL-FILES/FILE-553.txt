      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Sex on the Moon: The Amazing Story Behind the Most Audacious Heist in History
        </b>
       </i>
       is a book by
       <a href="/wiki/Ben_Mezrich" title="Ben Mezrich">
        Ben Mezrich
       </a>
       , author of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times_Best_Seller" title="New York Times Best Seller">
        New York Times Best Seller
       </a>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Bringing_Down_the_House_(book)" title="Bringing Down the House (book)">
         Bringing Down the House
        </a>
       </i>
       and of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Accidental_Billionaires" title="The Accidental Billionaires">
         The Accidental Billionaires
        </a>
       </i>
       . It retells the
       <a href="/wiki/Stolen_and_missing_moon_rocks" title="Stolen and missing moon rocks">
        theft and attempted sale of lunar samples
       </a>
       plus a
       <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">
        Martian meteorite
       </a>
       from a vault at NASA's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center
       </a>
       by a
       <a href="/wiki/Cooperative_education" title="Cooperative education">
        cooperative education
       </a>
       student assisted by another co-op, an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Intern" title="Intern">
        intern
       </a>
       , plus an acquaintance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The book received mainly negative reviews, criticized for its
       <a href="/wiki/Purple_prose" title="Purple prose">
        purple prose
       </a>
       and overly-sympathetic discussion of Roberts.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Summary">
        Summary
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sex_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Summary">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The story follows Thad Roberts, a
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Utah" title="University of Utah">
        University of Utah
       </a>
       student and high-achieving NASA co-op in
       <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">
        Houston
       </a>
       , dreaming of doing great things such as becoming an astronaut; his love interest Rebecca;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FBI-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and his accomplices Sandra
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FBI-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and Gordon McWhorter. The group stole lunar samples from
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_buildings_in_the_Johnson_Space_Center" title="List of buildings in the Johnson Space Center">
        Building 31
       </a>
       , had sex on a bed covered by the precious stones, were arrested by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/FBI" title="FBI">
        FBI
       </a>
       during a
       <a href="/wiki/Sting_operation" title="Sting operation">
        sting operation
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orlando" title="Orlando">
        Orlando
       </a>
       , and sentenced.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FBI_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FBI-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The book also describes Thad's release from jail.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">
        Reception
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sex_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Reception">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In a review in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Globe_and_Mail" title="The Globe and Mail">
         The Globe and Mail
        </a>
       </i>
       , the book is described as "in the pulp-non-fiction genre, crafted with colour-saturated prose and hyperbolic plot points that have its screenplay in view", but was criticized for failing to identify or explore why Roberts undertook the theft in the first place, "other than sex".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       criticized Mezrich's writing style for being excessively elaborate and dramatic, and for both the book and Roberts repeating the themes and style of his previous works, most notably the biography of
       <a href="/wiki/Facebook" title="Facebook">
        Facebook
       </a>
       founder
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_Zuckerberg" title="Mark Zuckerberg">
        Mark Zuckerberg
       </a>
       ,
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Accidental_Billionaires" title="The Accidental Billionaires">
         The Accidental Billionaires
        </a>
       </i>
       . The writing of
       <i>
        Sex on the Moon
       </i>
       was described as "cookie-cutter" and Roberts having a motivation of "a desire to be cool and attract hot babes". The review also criticized the book for highlighting the drama of the events without noting that Roberts' actions resulted in the destruction of 30 years’ worth of research notes regarding the moon rocks by Roberts' Nasa mentor, Everett K. Gibson.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review in
       <a href="/wiki/The_A.V._Club" title="The A.V. Club">
        The A.V. Club
       </a>
       also compared the book to
       <i>
        The Accidental Billionaires
       </i>
       and Mezrich's portrayal of Roberts to his portrayal of Zuckerberg, stating that the novel-like approach to a nonfiction book was jarring at times, but had the advantage of providing a strong characterization of the central player, Roberts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
         USA Today
        </a>
       </i>
       gave the book two out of four stars and stated that "Mezrich has a credibility gap that shines through writing that's overwrought, overstated, over-everything." The review criticized Mezrich's prose for being excessively flowery, and for recreating dialogue a decade after the conversations took place, which allowed for a more compelling narrative but raised serious questions about the book's resulting accuracy.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USAToday-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review for
       <a href="/wiki/CNN" title="CNN">
        CNN
       </a>
       also noted Mezrich's repeating choice of writing about "young geniuses, some with questionable ethics".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review written for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Daily_Beast" title="The Daily Beast">
         The Daily Beast
        </a>
       </i>
       describes the choice to write from Roberts' perspective as a "narrative pitfall" given he appears to be "somewhat delusional", making it hard to differentiate fact from fiction, and notes that Mezrich takes frequent "creative liberties". The review also criticized Mezrich for being overly symapthetic to Roberts and his rationalizations for his actions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A review for
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Boston_(magazine)" title="Boston (magazine)">
         Boston
        </a>
       </i>
       describes the book as containing "impressively hackneyed writing", but a fast read with many interesting details, though it does not deal with the moral dilemmas raised by Roberts' actions. The characterization of Roberts is seen as the main flaw, who is described as "off-putting" and unsympathetic with "glib rationalization" for his actions, ultimately coming off as a "sad sociopath".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Kirkus_Reviews" title="Kirkus Reviews">
         Kirkus Reviews
        </a>
       </i>
       describes the lead-up to the theft as the most interesting part of the book, but the prose as "overheated", and states that Mezrich fails in his efforts to portray Roberts, ultimately a "small-time crook", as a hero.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Film">
        Film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sex_on_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2011, the book was
       <a href="/wiki/Option_(filmmaking)" title="Option (filmmaking)">
        optioned
       </a>
       by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Pictures_Entertainment" title="Sony Pictures Entertainment">
        Sony Pictures Entertainment
       </a>
       , who also produced
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Social_Network" title="The Social Network">
         The Social Network
        </a>
       </i>
       based on Mezrich's biography of Mark Zuckerberg.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-USAToday-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
