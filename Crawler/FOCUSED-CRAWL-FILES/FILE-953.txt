      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caliban_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Caliban follows a distant orbit, more than 10 times further from Uranus than the furthest regular moon
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGladman_Nicholson_et_al.1998-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbit is retrograde, moderately
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       and slightly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentric
       </a>
       . The
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_elements" title="Orbital elements">
        orbital parameters
       </a>
       suggest that it may belong to the same dynamic cluster as
       <a href="/wiki/Stephano_(moon)" title="Stephano (moon)">
        Stephano
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Francisco_(moon)" title="Francisco (moon)">
        Francisco
       </a>
       , suggesting common origin.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The diagram illustrates the orbital parameters of the retrograde
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       of Uranus (in polar co-ordinates) with the eccentricity of the orbits represented by the segments extending from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        pericentre
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apocentre
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caliban_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Its diameter is estimated at 72 km (assuming
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.04)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JPL-SSD-sat_phys_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-JPL-SSD-sat_phys-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       making it the second largest irregular satellite of Uranus, half the size of
       <a href="/wiki/Sycorax_(moon)" title="Sycorax (moon)">
        Sycorax
       </a>
       , the biggest irregular satellite of Uranus.
      </p>
      <p>
       Somewhat inconsistent reports put Caliban in
       <i>
        light-red
       </i>
       category (
       <span class="nowrap">
        B–V = 0.83
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        V–R = 0.52
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTERettigWalshConsolmagno2001_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTERettigWalshConsolmagno2001-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="nowrap">
        B–V = 0.84 ± 0.03
       </span>
       <span class="nowrap">
        V–R = 0.57 ± 0.03
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), redder than
       <a href="/wiki/Himalia_(moon)" title="Himalia (moon)">
        Himalia
       </a>
       but still less red than most
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       objects. Caliban may be slightly redder than Sycorax.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maris_2001_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maris_2001-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It also absorbs light at 0.7 μm, and one group of astronomers think this may be a result of liquid water that modified the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schmude_2008_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schmude_2008-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Light_curve" title="Light curve">
        light curve
       </a>
       suggests the rotation period of Caliban is about 2.7h.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Maris_2001_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Maris_2001-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Caliban_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Caliban is hypothesized to be a captured object: it did not form in the accretionary disk that existed around Uranus just after its formation. The exact capture mechanism is not known, but capturing a moon requires the
       <a href="/wiki/Dissipation" title="Dissipation">
        dissipation
       </a>
       of energy. The possible capture processes include: gas drag in the
       <a href="/wiki/Protoplanetary_disk" title="Protoplanetary disk">
        protoplanetary disk
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/N-body_problem" title="N-body problem">
        many body interactions
       </a>
       and the capture during the fast growth of the Uranus' mass (so-called "pull-down").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESheppardJewittKleyna2005-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGravHolmanFraser2004-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
