    Blood moon prophecy
   </h1>

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        blood moon prophecy
       </b>
       were a series of
       <a href="/wiki/Apocalypticism" title="Apocalypticism">
        apocalyptic beliefs
       </a>
       promoted by Christian ministers
       <a href="/wiki/John_Hagee" title="John Hagee">
        John Hagee
       </a>
       and Mark Biltz, which state that a
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrad_(astronomy)" title="Tetrad (astronomy)">
        tetrad
       </a>
       (a series of four consecutive
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipses
       </a>
       —coinciding on
       <a href="/wiki/Jewish_holidays" title="Jewish holidays">
        Jewish holidays
       </a>
       —with six full moons in between, and no intervening partial lunar eclipses) which began with the
       <a href="/wiki/April_2014_lunar_eclipse" title="April 2014 lunar eclipse">
        April 2014 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       is a sign of the beginning of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/End_times" title="End times">
        end times
       </a>
       as described in the Bible in the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Joel" title="Book of Joel">
        Book of Joel
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">
        Acts
       </a>
       2:20 and
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">
        Revelation
       </a>
       6:12. The tetrad ended with the
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse on September 27-28, 2015
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_moon_prophecy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On April 15, 2014, there was a total lunar eclipse which was the first of four consecutive total eclipses in a series, known as a tetrad; a second one took place on October 8, 2014, the third one on April 4, 2015, and the remaining one took place on September 27, 2015. It is one of eight tetrads to take place during the 21st century AD.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthSky_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EarthSky-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As with most lunar eclipses, the moon appeared red during the April 15, 2014, eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USAToday-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNS_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RNS-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The red color is caused by
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       of sunlight through the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere" title="Earth's atmosphere">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </a>
       , the same effect that causes sunsets to appear red.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-USAToday-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hagee also connects the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_March_20,_2015" title="Solar eclipse of March 20, 2015">
        solar eclipse of March 20, 2015
       </a>
       , in the middle of the sequence.
      </p>

      <p>
       The idea of a "blood moon" serving as an omen of the coming of the end times comes from the
       <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Joel" title="Book of Joel">
        Book of Joel
       </a>
       , where it is written "the sun will turn into darkness, and the moon into blood, before the great and terrible day of the Lord comes."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This phrase is again mentioned by
       <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">
        Saint Peter
       </a>
       during
       <a href="/wiki/Pentecost" title="Pentecost">
        Pentecost
       </a>
       , as recorded in
       <a href="/wiki/Acts_of_the_Apostles" title="Acts of the Apostles">
        Acts
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although Peter says that the date of Pentecost, not some future date, was the fulfillment of
       <a href="/wiki/Joel_(prophet)" title="Joel (prophet)">
        Joel
       </a>
       's prophecy. The blood moon also appears in the Book of Revelation chapter 6 verses 11 - 13,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where verse 12 says " And I beheld when he had opened the sixth seal, and, lo, there was a great earthquake; and the sun became black as sackcloth of hair, and the moon became as blood".
      </p>
      <p>
       Around 2008, Biltz began predicting that the
       <a href="/wiki/Second_Coming" title="Second Coming">
        Second Coming
       </a>
       of Jesus would occur in the fall of 2015 with the seven years of the great tribulation beginning in the fall of 2008. He said he had "discovered" an astronomical pattern that predicted the next tetrad would coincide with the end times. When the prediction failed, he pulled the article from his website, but continued to teach on the "significance" of the tetrad.
      </p>
      <p>
       Hagee would later seize on Biltz' prediction to write
       <i>
        Four Blood Moons
       </i>
       , which would become a best seller, spending more than 150 days in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Amazon.com" title="Amazon.com">
        Amazon.com
       </a>
       's top 150 by April 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNS_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-RNS-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For the week ending March 30, 2014, it was the ninth best selling paperback, according to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Publishers_Weekly" title="Publishers Weekly">
         Publishers Weekly
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By mid-April, Hagee's book had hit No. 4 on
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">
         The New York Times
        </a>
       </i>
       best-seller list in the advice category.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNS_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-RNS-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Hagee's book (and subsequent sermon series at his home congregation, Cornerstone Church) did not proclaim that any specific "end times" event would occur (as did Biltz in his original prophecy), but claimed that every prior tetrad of the last 500 years coincided with events in Jewish and Israeli history that were originally tragic, yet followed by triumph.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Media_attention_and_critics">
        Media attention and critics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blood_moon_prophecy&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Media attention and critics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Hagee and Biltz's speculations gained mainstream media attention in publications such as
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/USA_Today" title="USA Today">
         USA Today
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Washington_Post" title="The Washington Post">
         The Washington Post
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USAToday_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-USAToday-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RNS_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-RNS-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Earth &amp; Sky
       </i>
       reported receiving "a number of inquiries about Blood Moon", prompting a response.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthSky_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-EarthSky-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Samantha Blake of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Christian_Today" title="Christian Today">
         Christian Today
        </a>
       </i>
       wrote that only a "small group of Christians" saw the eclipse as significant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Writing for
       <i>
        Earth &amp; Sky
       </i>
       Bruce McClure and
       <a href="/wiki/Deborah_Byrd" title="Deborah Byrd">
        Deborah Byrd
       </a>
       point out that the referenced verse also says the "sun will be turned into darkness", an apparent reference to a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        <i>
         solar
        </i>
        eclipse
       </a>
       . They note that since the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_Calendar" title="Jewish Calendar">
        Jewish Calendar
       </a>
       is lunar, one sixth of all eclipses will occur during Passover or
       <a href="/wiki/Sukkot" title="Sukkot">
        Sukkot
       </a>
       . Furthermore, there have been 62
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrad_(astronomy)" title="Tetrad (astronomy)">
        tetrads
       </a>
       since the first century AD, though only eight of them have coincided with both feasts. Additionally, three of the four eclipses in the tetrad were not even visible in the biblical homeland of
       <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">
        Israel
       </a>
       , casting further doubt on Hagee and Biltz's interpretation; even then, only the very end of the last eclipse was visible in Israel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EarthSky_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-EarthSky-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Writing for
       <a href="/wiki/Space.com" title="Space.com">
        Space.com
       </a>
       , Geoff Gaherty said he was saddened that
       <span style="padding-right:.2em;">
        "
       </span>
       'prophets of doom' ... view these life-enriching events as portents of disaster" and said the eclipse was "hardly something to be concerned about".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In January 2014, Mike Moore, the then General Secretary of
       <a href="/wiki/Christian_Witness_to_Israel" title="Christian Witness to Israel">
        Christian Witness to Israel
       </a>
       , wrote a lengthy article dismissing the claims of Biltz and Hagee. Moore's view was that no significance can be drawn from the eclipses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
