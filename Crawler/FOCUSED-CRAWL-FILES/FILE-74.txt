   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Claimed moons of Earth
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       Claims of the existence of
       <b>
        other moons of Earth
       </b>
       —that is, of one or more
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       other than the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       that
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       —have existed for some time. Several candidates have been proposed, but none have been confirmed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klotz_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-klotz-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since the 19th century, scientists have made genuine searches for more moons, but the possibility has also been the subject of a number of dubious non-scientific speculations as well as a number of likely
       <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">
        hoaxes
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Drye-wordpress-dubious_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Drye-wordpress-dubious-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the Moon is Earth's only natural satellite, there are a number of
       <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">
        near-Earth objects
       </a>
       (NEOs) with orbits that are in
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       with Earth. These have been called, inaccurately but provocatively, "second", "third" or "other" moons of Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2016_HO3" title="2016 HO3">
         2016 HO
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           3
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       discovered on 27 April 2016, is possibly the most stable
       <a href="/wiki/Quasi-satellite" title="Quasi-satellite">
        quasi-satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA-20160615_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-20160615-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As it orbits the Sun,
       <span class="nowrap">
        2016 HO
        <sub>
         3
        </sub>
       </span>
       appears to circle around
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       as well. It is too distant to be a true
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of Earth, but is the best and most stable example of a quasi-satellite, a type of
       <a href="/wiki/Near-Earth_object" title="Near-Earth object">
        near-Earth object
       </a>
       . They appear to orbit a point other than Earth itself, such as the orbital path of the NEO
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/3753_Cruithne" title="3753 Cruithne">
        3753 Cruithne
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Earth_trojan" title="Earth trojan">
        Earth trojans
       </a>
       , such as
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2010_TK7" title="2010 TK7">
         2010 TK
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           7
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       , are NEOs that orbit the Sun (not Earth) on the same orbital path as Earth, and appear to lead or follow Earth along the same orbital path.
      </p>
      <p>
       Other
       <a href="/wiki/Small_Solar_System_body" title="Small Solar System body">
        small natural objects in orbit around the Sun
       </a>
       may enter orbit around Earth for a short amount of time, becoming temporary natural satellites. To date
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Claimed_moons_of_Earth&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , the only confirmed example has been
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2006_RH120" title="2006 RH120">
         2006 RH
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           120
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       in Earth orbit during 2006 and 2007, though further instances are already predicted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klotz_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-klotz-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claimed_moons_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Petit.27s_moon">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Petit's_moon">
        Petit's moon
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claimed_moons_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Petit's moon">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The first major claim of another moon of Earth was made by French astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Petit_(astronomer)" title="Frédéric Petit (astronomer)">
        Frédéric Petit
       </a>
       , director of the
       <a href="/wiki/Toulouse_Observatory" title="Toulouse Observatory">
        Toulouse Observatory
       </a>
       , who in 1846 announced that he had discovered a second moon in an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" title="Elliptical orbit">
        elliptical orbit
       </a>
       around Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was claimed to have also been reported by Lebon and Dassier at
       <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">
        Toulouse
       </a>
       , and by Larivière at Artenac Observatory, during the early evening of March 21, 1846.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Petit proposed that this second moon had an elliptical orbit, a period of 2 hours 44 minutes, with 3,570 km (2,220 mi)
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apogee
       </a>
       and 11.4 km (7.1 mi)
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        perigee
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This claim was soon dismissed by his peers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wandering_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-wandering-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 11.4 km (37,000 ft) perigee is similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/Altitude#Altitude_in_aviation_and_in_spaceflight" title="Altitude">
        cruising altitude
       </a>
       of most modern airliners, and within Earth's atmosphere. Petit published another paper on his 1846 observations in 1861, basing the second moon's existence on perturbations in movements of the actual Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This second moon hypothesis was not confirmed either.
      </p>
      <p>
       Petit's proposed moon became a plot point in
       <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
        Jules Verne
       </a>
       's 1870 science fiction novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
         Around the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley195707_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley195707-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Waltemath.27s_moons">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Waltemath's_moons">
        Waltemath's moons
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claimed_moons_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Waltemath's moons">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1898
       <a href="/wiki/Hamburg" title="Hamburg">
        Hamburg
       </a>
       scientist Dr.
       <a href="/wiki/Georg_Waltemath" title="Georg Waltemath">
        Georg Waltemath
       </a>
       announced that he had located a system of tiny moons orbiting Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He had begun his search for secondary moons based on the hypothesis that something was gravitationally affecting the Moon's orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PO_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PO-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Waltemath described one of the proposed moons as being 1,030,000 km (640,000 mi) from Earth, with a diameter of 700 km (430 mi), a 119-day
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       , and a 177-day
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_period" title="Synodic period">
        synodic period
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He also said it did not reflect enough sunlight to be observed without a telescope, unless viewed at certain times, and made several predictions of its next appearances.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PO_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-PO-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Sometimes, it shines at night like the sun but only for an hour or so."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PO_10-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-PO-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cambridge1_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cambridge1-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       E. Stone Wiggins, a Canadian weather expert, ascribed the cold spring of 1907 to the effect of a second moon, which he said he had first seen in 1882 and had publicized the find in 1884 in the New York Tribune when he put it forward as probable cause of an anomalous solar eclipse of May of that year. He said it was also probably the "green crescent moon" seen in New Zealand and later in North America in 1886, for periods of less than a half-hour each time. He said this was the "second moon" seen by Waltemath in 1898. Wiggins hypothesized that the second moon had a high carbon atmosphere but could be seen occasionally by its reflected light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The existence of these objects put forward by Waltemath (and Wiggins) was discredited after the absence of corroborating observation by other members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_community" title="Scientific community">
        scientific community
       </a>
       . Especially problematic was a failed prediction that they would be seen in February 1898.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">

       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>

       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Popular_Astronomy_(US_magazine)" title="Popular Astronomy (US magazine)">
         Popular Astronomy
        </a>
       </i>
       resulted in increased searches for small natural satellites by amateur astronomers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Pickering had also proposed the Moon itself had broken off from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickering_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pickering-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In early 1954 the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">
        United States Army
       </a>
       's Office of Ordnance Research commissioned
       <a href="/wiki/Clyde_Tombaugh" title="Clyde Tombaugh">
        Clyde Tombaugh
       </a>
       , discoverer of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       , to search for near-Earth asteroids. The Army issued a public statement to explain the rationale for this survey.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Donald_Keyhoe" title="Donald Keyhoe">
        Donald Keyhoe
       </a>
       , who was later director of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Investigations_Committee_on_Aerial_Phenomena" title="National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena">
        National Investigations Committee on Aerial Phenomena
       </a>
       (NICAP), a UFO research group, said that his
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">
        Pentagon
       </a>
       source had told him that the actual reason for the quickly initiated search was that two near-Earth objects had been picked up on new long-range
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       in mid-1953. In May 1954, Keyhoe asserted that the search had been successful, and either one or two objects had been found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">
        The Pentagon
       </a>
       , a general who heard the news reportedly asked whether the satellites were natural or artificial. Tombaugh denied the alleged discovery in a letter to
       <a href="/wiki/Willy_Ley" title="Willy Ley">
        Willy Ley
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley195707_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley195707-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the October 1955 issue of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Popular_Mechanics" title="Popular Mechanics">
         Popular Mechanics
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine reported:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        Professor Tombaugh is closemouthed about his results. He won't say whether or not any small natural satellites have been discovered. He does say, however, that newspaper reports of 18 months ago announcing the discovery of natural satellites at 400 and 600 miles out are not correct. He adds that there is no connection between the search program and the reports of so-called flying saucers.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stimson_20-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Stimson-20">
          [20]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       At a meteor conference in Los Angeles in 1957, Tombaugh reiterated that his four-year search for natural satellites had been unsuccessful.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1959, he issued a final report stating that nothing had been found in his search.

       Although no other moons of Earth have been found to date, there are various types of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-Earth_objects" title="Near-Earth objects">
        near-Earth objects
       </a>
       in 1:1 resonance with it, which are known as
       <a href="/wiki/Quasi-satellite" title="Quasi-satellite">
        quasi-satellites
       </a>
       . Quasi-satellites orbit the Sun from the same distance as a planet, rather than the planet itself. Their orbits are unstable, and will fall into other resonances or be kicked into other orbits over thousands of years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Quasi-satellites of Earth include
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2010_SO16" title="2010 SO16">
         2010 SO
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           16
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(164207)_2004_GU9" title="(164207) 2004 GU9">
         (164207) 2004 GU
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           9
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(277810)_2006_FV35" title="(277810) 2006 FV35">

         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">

         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">

          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2014_OL339" title="2014 OL339">
         2014 OL
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">

          </span>
         </span>

       The key difference between a satellite and a quasi-satellite is that the orbit of a satellite of Earth fundamentally depends on the gravity of the Earth–Moon system, whereas the orbit of a quasi-satellite would negligibly change if Earth and the Moon were suddenly removed because a quasi-satellite is orbiting the Sun on an Earth-like orbit in the vicinity of Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granvik2011_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Granvik2011-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>

          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       is roughly 300 metres across. Like quasi-satellites, it orbits the Sun in a 1:1 resonance with Earth, rather than Earth itself.

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Temporary_satellites">
        Temporary satellites
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Claimed_moons_of_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Temporary satellites">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On 14 September 2006, an object estimated at 5 meters in diameter was discovered in near-polar orbit around Earth. Originally thought to be a third-stage Saturn
       <a href="/wiki/S-IVB" title="S-IVB">
        S-IVB
       </a>
       booster from
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       , it was later determined to be an
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       and designated as
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/2006_RH120" title="2006 RH120">
         2006 RH
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           120
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       . The asteroid re-entered Solar orbit after 13 months and is expected to return to Earth orbit in 21 years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-astronomy_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-astronomy-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Computer models by astrophysicists Mikael Granvik, Jeremie Vaubaillon, and Robert Jedicke suggest that these "temporary satellites" should be quite common; and that "At any given time, there should be at least one natural Earth satellite of 1-meter diameter orbiting the Earth."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cornell_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cornell-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such objects would remain in orbit for ten months on average, before returning to solar orbit once more, and so would make relatively easy targets for manned space exploration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cornell_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-cornell-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       "Mini-moons" were further examined in a study published in the March issue of
       <i>
        Icarus
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-minimoons_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-minimoons-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The earliest known mention
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Granvik2011_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Granvik2011-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in the scientific literature of a temporarily captured orbiter is by
       <a href="/wiki/Clarence_Chant" title="Clarence Chant">
        Clarence Chant
       </a>
       about the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteor_procession_of_February_9,_1913" title="Meteor procession of February 9, 1913">
        Meteor procession of February 9, 1913
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "It would seem that the bodies had been traveling through space, probably in an orbit about the sun, and that on coming near the earth they were promptly captured by it and caused to move about it as a satellite."
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chant1913_31-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Chant1913-31">
          [31]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       And later in 1916,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Frederick_Denning" title="William Frederick Denning">
        William Frederick Denning
       </a>
       surmised:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/1913_Great_Meteor_Procession" title="1913 Great Meteor Procession">
         The large meteors which passed over Northern America on February 9, 1913
        </a>
        , presented some unique features. The length of their observed flight was about 2600 miles [4,200 km], and they must have been moving in paths concentric, or nearly concentric, with the earth's surface, so that they temporarily formed new terrestrial satellites.”
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denning1916_32-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-Denning1916-32">
          [32]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It has been proposed that NASA search for temporary natural satellites, and use them for a sample return mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-klotz_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-klotz-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In April 2015, an object was discovered orbiting the Earth, and initially designated
       <span class="nowrap">
        2015 HP
        <sub>
         116
        </sub>
       </span>
       , but more detailed investigation quickly showed the object to be the
       <a href="/wiki/Gaia_(spacecraft)" title="Gaia (spacecraft)">
        Gaia spacecraft
       </a>
       , and the object's discovery soon was retracted.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On October 3, 2015, a small object, temporarily designated
       <a href="/wiki/WT1190F" title="WT1190F">
        WT1190F
       </a>
       , was found to be orbiting the Earth every ~23 days, and had been orbiting since at least late 2009. It impacted the Earth on November 13, 2015 at 06:18:34.3 (±1.3 seconds) UTC.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On February 8, 2016 a 0.5-meter object was discovered orbiting the Earth with a period of 5 days and given the temporary designation XC83E0D, and most likely lost. The object was later identified with the lost artificial satellite SR-11A, or possibly its companion SR-11B, which were launched in 1976 and lost in 1979.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On April 8, 2016, an object, named S509356, was discovered with an orbital period of 3.58 days. Although it has the typical area-to-mass ratio (m^2/kg) of satellites, it has a color typical of
       <a href="/wiki/S-type_asteroid" title="S-type asteroid">
        S-type asteroids
       </a>
       . It has not yet been identified with any known artificial satellites, because there are no known satellites currently orbiting Earth in between 2.99 and 3.79 days.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On December 8, 2017, the object YX205B9 was discovered with an orbital period of 21 days, on an eccentric orbit taking it from slightly beyond the geocentric satellite ring to almost twice the distance of the Moon. While its orbit remains poor, its absolute magnitude (25.1) makes an artificial satellite unlikely.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2017)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>

      <ul>
       <li>
        The writer
        <a href="/wiki/Jules_Verne" title="Jules Verne">
         Jules Verne
        </a>
        learned of Petit's 1861 proposal and made use of the idea in his 1865 novel,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
          Around the Moon
         </a>
        </i>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wandering_6-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-wandering-6">
          [6]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This fictional moon was not, however, exactly based on the Toulouse observations or Petit's proposal at a technical level, and so the orbit suggested by Verne was mathematically incorrect.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schlyter_5-7">
         <a href="#cite_note-Schlyter-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        Petit died in 1865, and so was not alive to offer a response to Verne's fictional moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
         <a href="#cite_note-35">
          [35]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Seun Ayoade's Sci Fi Adventure
        <i>
         Double Bill
        </i>
        has a twin mooned parallel earth.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Cameron" title="Eleanor Cameron">
         Eleanor Cameron
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         Mushroom Planet
        </i>
        novels for children (starting with the 1954
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Wonderful_Flight_to_the_Mushroom_Planet" title="The Wonderful Flight to the Mushroom Planet">
          The Wonderful Flight to the Mushroom Planet
         </a>
        </i>
        ) are set on a tiny, habitable second moon called Basidium in an invisible orbit 50,000 miles (80,000 km) from Earth. There is an even smaller moon called Lepton orbiting at only 1,000 miles (1,600 km).
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
         <a href="#cite_note-36">
          [36]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The 1956
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tom_Swift,_Jr." title="Tom Swift, Jr.">
         Tom Swift, Jr.
        </a>
        juvenile novel,
        <i>
         Tom Swift on the Phantom Satellite
        </i>
        , features a new moon entering Earth orbit at 50,000 miles (80,000 km) altitude.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
         <a href="#cite_note-37">
          [37]
         </a>
        </sup>
        A 1963 sequel,
        <i>
         Tom Swift and the Asteroid Pirates
        </i>
        , has the moon Nestria, also called Little Luna, which was originally an asteroid and was moved into Earth orbit at 50,000 miles (80,000 km) altitude. It was claimed for the United States and a research base was established there by Swift Enterprises.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
         <a href="#cite_note-38">
          [38]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Samuel_R._Delany" title="Samuel R. Delany">
         Samuel R. Delany
        </a>
        's 1975 novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Dhalgren" title="Dhalgren">
          Dhalgren
         </a>
        </i>
        features an Earth that mysteriously acquires a second moon named George.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39">
         <a href="#cite_note-39">
          [39]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Haruki_Murakami" title="Haruki Murakami">
         Haruki Murakami
        </a>
        's 2011 novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/1Q84" title="1Q84">
          1Q84
         </a>
        </i>
        , a second moon, irregularly shaped and green in color, is visible to some characters in the story.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
         <a href="#cite_note-40">
          [40]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
