      <p>
       <b>
        Pan
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          PAN
         </span>
        </i>
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el">
        Πάν
       </span>
       ) is the innermost
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moons_of_Saturn_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moons_of_Saturn-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It is a small,
       <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">
        walnut
       </a>
       -shaped moon approximately 35
       <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
        kilometres
       </a>
       across and 23 km wide that orbits within the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encke_Gap" title="Encke Gap">
        Encke Gap
       </a>
       in Saturn's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Ring" title="A Ring">
        A Ring
       </a>
       . Pan is a
       <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_moon" title="Shepherd moon">
        ring shepherd
       </a>
       and is responsible for keeping the Encke Gap free of ring particles.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Mark_R._Showalter" title="Mark R. Showalter">
        Mark R. Showalter
       </a>
       in 1990 from analysis of old
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       probe photos and received the provisional designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1981 S 13
        </span>
       </b>
       because the discovery images dated back to 1981.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-iauc5052_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-iauc5052-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prediction_and_discovery">
        Prediction and discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prediction and discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The existence of a moon in the Encke Gap was first predicted by Jeffrey N. Cuzzi and Jeffrey D. Scargle in 1985, based on wavy edges of the gap which indicated a gravitational disturbance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1986 Showalter
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       inferred its orbit and mass by modeling its gravitational wake. They arrived at a very precise prediction of 133,603 ± 10 km for the semi-major axis and a mass of 5–10
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −12
       </sup>
       Saturn masses, and inferred that there was only a single moon within the Encke gap.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter1986_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter1986-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The actual semi-major axis differs by 19 km and the actual mass is 8.6
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        −12
       </sup>
       of Saturn's.
      </p>
      <p>
       The moon was later found within 1° of the predicted position. The search was undertaken by considering all
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       images and using a computer calculation to predict whether the moon would be visible under sufficiently favorable conditions in each one. Every qualifying Voyager 2 image with resolution better than ~50 km/pixel shows Pan clearly. In all, it appears in eleven
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       images.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter1990_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter1990-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Showalter1991_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Showalter1991-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The moon was named on 16 September 1991,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       after the mythological
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pan_(mythology)" title="Pan (mythology)">
        Pan
       </a>
       , who was (among other things) the god of shepherds. This is a reference to Pan's role as a
       <a href="/wiki/Shepherd_moon" title="Shepherd moon">
        shepherd moon
       </a>
       . It is also designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn XVIII
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gazetteer_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gazetteer-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of Pan's orbit causes its distance from Saturn to vary by ~4 km. Its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       , which would cause it to move up and down, is not distinguishable from zero with present data. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Encke_Gap" title="Encke Gap">
        Encke Gap
       </a>
       , within which Pan orbits, is about 325 km wide.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Geography">
        Geography
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Geography">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pan_(moon)_edge-on.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="219" data-file-width="245" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Pan_%28moon%29_edge-on.jpg/220px-Pan_%28moon%29_edge-on.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/aa/Pan_%28moon%29_edge-on.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pan_(moon)_edge-on.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Pan, photographed by
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
           Cassini
          </a>
         </i>
         on March 7, 2017. The thin
         <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_ridge" title="Equatorial ridge">
          equatorial ridge
         </a>
         is clearly visible.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       scientists have described Pan as "
       <a href="/wiki/Walnut" title="Walnut">
        walnut
       </a>
       -shaped"
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       owing to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equatorial_ridge" title="Equatorial ridge">
        equatorial ridge
       </a>
       , similar to that on
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_(moon)" title="Atlas (moon)">
        Atlas
       </a>
       , that is visible in images. The ridge is due to ring material that Pan has swept up from the Encke gap. It has also been referred to by journalists as a space
       <a href="/wiki/Empanada" title="Empanada">
        empanada
       </a>
       , a form of stuffed bread or pastry, as well as a ravioli.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space_empanada_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space_empanada-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Pandean_ringlet">
        Pandean ringlet
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Pan_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Pandean ringlet">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Encke Gap contains a ringlet that is coincident with Pan's orbit, indicating that Pan maintains the particles in
       <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
        horseshoe orbits
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A second ringlet is periodically disrupted by Pan, similarly to how the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F_Ring" title="F Ring">
        F Ring
       </a>
       is disturbed by
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus_(moon)" title="Prometheus (moon)">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
