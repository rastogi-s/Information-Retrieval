       <b>
        Aegaeon
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/dʒ/: 'j' in 'jam'">
            dʒ
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="/ən/: 'on' in 'button'">
            ən
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         ee-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          JEE
         </span>
         -ən
        </i>
       </a>
       ; or as Greek
       <i>
        Αιγαίων
       </i>
       ), also
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn LIII
        </span>
       </b>
       (provisional designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/2008 S 1
        </span>
       </b>
       ), is a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It is thought to be similarly smooth as
       <a href="/wiki/Methone_(moon)" title="Methone (moon)">
        Methone
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smooth_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smooth-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It orbits between
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       within Saturn's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#G_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        G Ring
       </a>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">
        Discovery and naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aegaeon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Images of Aegaeon were taken by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       on 15 August 2008, and its discovery was announced on 3 March 2009 by
       <a href="/wiki/Carolyn_Porco" title="Carolyn Porco">
        Carolyn Porco
       </a>
       of the Cassini Imaging Science Team using the provisional designation
       <span class="nowrap">
        S/2008 S 1
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUCircular_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUCircular-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Aegaeon was named after Ægæon, one of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hekatonkheires" title="Hekatonkheires">
        hekatonkheires
       </a>
       , on 5 May 2009.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aegaeon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Aegaeon_(2008_S1).jpg">
         <img alt="Aegaeon (2008 S1).jpg" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="695" data-file-width="1132" height="123" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg/200px-Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg/300px-Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg/400px-Aegaeon_%282008_S1%29.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Aegaeon_(2008_S1).jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Aegaeon orbits within the bright segment of Saturn's
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#G_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        G Ring
       </a>
       , and is probably a major source of the ring.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Debris knocked off Aegaeon forms a bright arc near the inner edge, which in turn spreads to form the rest of the ring. Aegaeon orbits in a 7:6
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        corotation eccentricity resonance
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icarus_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Icarus-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which causes an approximately 4-year oscillation of about 4 km in its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       , and a corresponding oscillation of a few degrees in its
       <a href="/wiki/Mean_longitude" title="Mean longitude">
        mean longitude
       </a>
       . It orbits Saturn at an average distance of 167,500 km in 0.80812 days, at an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       of 0.001° to Saturn's equator, with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.0002.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUCircular_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUCircular-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div style="clear:left;">
      </div>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aegaeon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Aegaeon is the smallest known moon of Saturn and has a highly elongated shape, measuring 1.4x0.5x0.4 km in size.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Icarus2_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Icarus2-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Measurements of its mass, based on its interaction with the dust particles that make up the G ring arc the moon is embedded in, suggest a density similar to that of water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Aegaeon has the lowest albedo, below 0.15, of any Saturnian moon inward of Titan.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This might be due to either darker meteoric material making up the dust in the G ring or due to Aegaeon having been disrupted, stripping away its ice-rich surface and leaving the rocky inner core behind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Aegaeon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       spacecraft has performed four flybys of Aegaeon closer than 20,000 km, though only one has occurred since it was discovered in 2008. The closest of these pre-discovery encounters took place on 5 September 2005 at a distance of 8,517 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PSCassini_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PSCassini-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       An encounter on 27 January 2010 at a distance 13,306 km allowed
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       to acquire its highest resolution images of Aegaeon to-date.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DPS_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-DPS-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 19 December 2015,
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       was unable to acquire any images from a planned close flyby.
      </p>
