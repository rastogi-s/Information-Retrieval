      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_orbit">
        Discovery and orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=174567_Varda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       174567 Varda was discovered in March 2006, using imagery dated from June 21, 2003 by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jeffrey_A._Larsen" title="Jeffrey A. Larsen">
        Jeffrey A. Larsen
       </a>
       with the
       <a href="/wiki/Spacewatch" title="Spacewatch">
        Spacewatch
       </a>
       telescope as part of a United States Naval Academy Trident Scholar project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Larsen2007_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Larsen2007-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is currently 47.5 AU from the Sun,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDys_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-AstDys-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and will come to
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        perihelion
       </a>
       around November 2096.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Horizons-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been observed 68 times over 14
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(planets)" title="Opposition (planets)">
        oppositions
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">
        precovery
       </a>
       images back to 1980.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jpldata_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-jpldata-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=174567_Varda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Names for Varda and its moon were announced on 16 January 2014.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Varda" title="Varda">
         Varda
        </a>
       </i>
       is the queen of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Valar" title="Valar">
        Valar
       </a>
       , creator of the stars, and principal goddess of the elves in J. R. R. Tolkien's
       <a href="/wiki/Tolkien%27s_legendarium" title="Tolkien's legendarium">
        fictional mythology
       </a>
       .
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Ilmar%C3%AB" title="Ilmarë">
         Ilmarë
        </a>
       </i>
       is a chief of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maiar" title="Maiar">
        Maiar
       </a>
       and Varda's handmaiden.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Varda_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-Varda-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite">
        Satellite
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=174567_Varda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Satellite">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Varda has at least one satellite,
       <b>
        Ilmarë
       </b>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       (stress on the first syllable, Quenya:
       <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)">
        [ˈilmarɛ]
       </span>
       ), or
       <b>
        Varda I
       </b>
       , which was discovered in an image obtained by the Hubble Space Telescope on 26 April 2009, and reported in 2011. It is estimated to be about 320–360 km in diameter (about 50% that of its primary). As of 2015 two mirror orbital solutions are possible with slightly different parameters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool-10_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool-10-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy-2015_7-6">

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_properties">
        Physical properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=174567_Varda&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Physical properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The estimated combined size of the Varda–Ilmarë system is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005792000000000000♠
        </span>
        792
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +91
          <br/>
          −84
         </span>
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       . The size of the primary is estimated at
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005705000000000000♠
        </span>
        705
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +81
          <br/>
          −75
         </span>
        </span>
        km
       </span>
       . The total mass of the system is about 2.66
       <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
        ×
       </span>
       10
       <sup>
        <span style="white-space:nowrap">
         20
        </span>
       </sup>
       kg. The density of both the primary and the satellite is estimated at about 1.24 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       assuming that they have equal density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool-10_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool-10-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy-2015_7-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy-2015-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The surfaces of both the primary and the satellite appear to be red in the visible and near-infrared parts of the spectrum (spectral class IR), with Ilmarë being slightly redder than Varda. The spectrum of the system does not show water absorption but shows evidence of
       <a href="/wiki/Methanol" title="Methanol">
        methanol
       </a>
       ice. The rotation period of Varda is estimated at 5.61 hours.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy-2015_7-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy-2015-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
