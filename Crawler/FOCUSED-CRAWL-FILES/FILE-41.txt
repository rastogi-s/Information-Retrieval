   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar Laser Ranging experiment
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

      <p>
       The ongoing
       <b>
        Lunar Laser Ranging Experiment
       </b>
       measures the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        distance
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       using
       <a href="/wiki/Lidar" title="Lidar">
        laser ranging
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
        Lasers
       </a>
       on Earth are aimed at
       <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">
        retroreflectors
       </a>
       planted on the Moon during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        11
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        14
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        15
       </a>
       ) and the two
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_programme" title="Lunokhod programme">
        Lunokhod
       </a>
       missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lunakhod_2_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lunakhod_2-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The time for the reflected light to return is measured.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">

        </a>

      <p>
       The first successful tests were carried out in 1962 when a team from the
       <a href="/wiki/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology" title="Massachusetts Institute of Technology">
        Massachusetts Institute of Technology
       </a>
       succeeded in observing laser pulses reflected from the Moon's surface using a laser with a millisecond pulse length.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Similar measurements were obtained later the same year by a Soviet team at the
       <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Astrophysical_Observatory" title="Crimean Astrophysical Observatory">
        Crimean Astrophysical Observatory
       </a>
       using a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Q-switched" title="Q-switched">
        Q-switched
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ruby_laser" title="Ruby laser">
        ruby laser
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Greater accuracy was achieved following the installation of a
       <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">
        retroreflector
       </a>
       array on July 21, 1969, by the crew of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       , and two more retroreflector arrays left by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       missions have also contributed to the experiment. Successful lunar laser range measurements to the retroreflectors were first reported by the 3.1 m telescope at
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Air_Force_Cambridge_Research_Laboratories" title="Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratories">
        Air Force Cambridge Research Laboratories
       </a>
       Lunar Ranging Observatory in Arizona, the
       <a href="/wiki/Pic_du_Midi_de_Bigorre" title="Pic du Midi de Bigorre">
        Pic du Midi Observatory
       </a>
       in France, the Tokyo
       <a href="/wiki/National_Astronomical_Observatory_of_Japan" title="National Astronomical Observatory of Japan">
        Astronomical Observatory
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">
        McDonald Observatory
       </a>
       in Texas.
      </p>
      <p>
       The unmanned Soviet
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">
         Lunokhod 1
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
         Lunokhod 2
        </a>
       </i>
       rovers carried smaller arrays. Reflected signals were initially received from
       <i>
        Lunokhod 1
       </i>
       , but no return signals were detected after 1971 until a team from University of California rediscovered the array in April 2010 using images from
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Lunokhod 2's
       </i>
       array continues to return signals to Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jwjd1_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-jwjd1-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Lunokhod arrays suffer from decreased performance in direct sunlight—a factor considered in reflector placement during the Apollo missions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Apollo 15 array is three times the size of the arrays left by the two earlier Apollo missions. Its size made it the target of three-quarters of the sample measurements taken in the first 25 years of the experiment. Improvements in technology since then have resulted in greater use of the smaller arrays, by sites such as the
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur_Observatory" title="Côte d'Azur Observatory">
        Côte d'Azur Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Grasse" title="Grasse">
        Grasse
       </a>
       , France; and the
       <a href="/wiki/Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation" title="Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser-ranging Operation">
        Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser-ranging Operation
       </a>
       (APOLLO) at the
       <a href="/wiki/Apache_Point_Observatory" title="Apache Point Observatory">
        Apache Point Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Details">
        Details
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Details">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The distance to the Moon is calculated
       <i>
        approximately
       </i>
       using this equation:
      </p>
      <p>
       <span class="texhtml">
        distance = (speed of light × time taken for light to reflect) / 2
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       In actuality, the round-trip time of about 2.5 seconds is affected by the location of the Moon in the sky, the relative motion of Earth and the Moon, Earth's rotation,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_libration" title="Lunar libration">
        lunar libration
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Weather" title="Weather">
        weather
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">
        polar motion
       </a>
       , propagation delay through
       <a href="/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth" title="Atmosphere of Earth">
        Earth's atmosphere
       </a>
       , the motion of the observing station due to
       <a href="/wiki/Plate_tectonics" title="Plate tectonics">
        crustal motion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       , velocity of light in various parts of air and
       <a href="/wiki/Theory_of_relativity" title="Theory of relativity">
        relativistic effects
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Nonetheless, the Earth–Moon distance has been measured with increasing accuracy for more than 35 years. The distance continually changes for a number of reasons, but averages 385,000.6 km (239,228.3 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-millimeter_challenge_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-millimeter_challenge-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       At the Moon's surface, the beam is about 6.5 kilometers (4.0 mi) wide
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ApolloLaser_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ApolloLaser-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and scientists liken the task of aiming the beam to using a rifle to hit a moving
       <a href="/wiki/Dime_(United_States_coin)" title="Dime (United States coin)">
        dime
       </a>
       3 kilometers (1.9 mi) away. The reflected light is too weak to see with the human eye. Out of 10
       <sup>
        17
       </sup>
       photons aimed at the reflector, only one is received back on Earth every few seconds, even under good conditions. They can be identified as originating from the laser because the laser is highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Monochromatic" title="Monochromatic">
        monochromatic
       </a>
       . This is one of the most precise distance measurements ever made, and is equivalent in accuracy to determining the distance between Los Angeles and New York to 0.25 mm (0.0098 in).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2002
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , work is progressing on increasing the accuracy of the Earth–Moon measurements to near millimeter accuracy, though the performance of the reflectors continues to degrade with age.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UniverseToday_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-UniverseToday-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Lunar laser ranging measurement data is available from the Paris Observatory Lunar Analysis Center,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and the active stations. Some of the findings of this
       <a href="/wiki/Long-term_experiment" title="Long-term experiment">
        long-term experiment
       </a>
       are:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The Moon is spiraling away from Earth at a rate of 3.8 cm per year.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ApolloLaser_9-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-ApolloLaser-9">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
        This rate has been described as anomalously high.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Moon probably has a liquid core of about 20% of the Moon's radius.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jwjd1_5-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-jwjd1-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The universal force of
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
         gravity
        </a>
        is very stable. The experiments have constrained the change in
        <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
         Newton's
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">
         gravitational constant
        </a>
        <i>
         G
        </i>
        to a factor of (2±7)×10
        <sup>
         −13
        </sup>
        per year.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The likelihood of any "
        <a href="/wiki/Nordtvedt_effect" title="Nordtvedt effect">
         Nordtvedt effect
        </a>
        " (a differential acceleration of the Moon and Earth towards the Sun caused by their different degrees of compactness) has been ruled out to high precision,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
        strongly supporting the validity of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Strong_equivalence_principle" title="Strong equivalence principle">
         Strong Equivalence Principle.
        </a>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
         Einstein's
        </a>
        theory of gravity (the
        <a href="/wiki/General_relativity" title="General relativity">
         general theory of relativity
        </a>
        ) predicts the Moon's
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_orbit" title="Planetary orbit">
         orbit
        </a>
        to within the accuracy of the laser ranging measurements.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-jwjd1_5-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-jwjd1-5">
          [5]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Gauge freedom plays a major role in a correct physical interpretation of the relativistic effects in the Earth-Moon system observed with LLR technique
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
