      <p>
       <b>
        "The Man Who Sold the Moon"
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Novella" title="Novella">
        novella
       </a>
       by American author
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       , written in 1949 and published in 1951. A part of his
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Future_History_(novel)" title="Future History (novel)">
         Future History
        </a>
       </i>
       and prequel to "
       <a href="/wiki/Requiem_(short_story)" title="Requiem (short story)">
        Requiem
       </a>
       ", it covers events around a fictional first Moon landing in 1978 and the schemes of
       <a href="/wiki/Delos_D._Harriman" title="Delos D. Harriman">
        Delos D. Harriman
       </a>
       , a businessman who is determined to personally reach and control
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Moon" title="The Moon">
        the Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
        Plot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D._D._Harriman" title="D. D. Harriman">
        Delos David "D. D." Harriman
       </a>
       , "the last of the
       <a href="/wiki/Robber_baron_(industrialist)" title="Robber baron (industrialist)">
        Robber Barons
       </a>
       ", is obsessed with being the first to travel to—and possess—the moon. He asks his business partner, George Strong, and other tycoons to invest in the venture. Most dismiss Harriman's plans as foolhardy: Nuclear rocket fuel is scarce as the
       <a href="/wiki/Blowups_Happen" title="Blowups Happen">
        space station that produces it
       </a>
       blew up, also destroying the only existing spaceship. The necessary technology for a chemical-fueled rocket stretches the boundaries of current engineering. The endeavor is both incredibly costly and of uncertain profitability. One skeptic offers to sell "all of my interest in the Moon...for fifty cents"; Harriman accepts and tries to buy the other associates' interests as well. Strong and two others agree to back his plans.
      </p>
      <p>
       The technical problems are solvable with money and talent. To solve the tougher financial problems, Harriman exploits commercial and political rivalries. He implies to the Moka-Coka company, for example, that rival soft drink maker 6+ plans to turn the Moon into
       <a href="/wiki/Space_advertising" title="Space advertising">
        a massive billboard
       </a>
       , using a rocket to scatter black dust on the surface in patterns. To an anti-Communist associate, he suggests that the Russians may print the
       <a href="/wiki/Hammer_and_sickle" title="Hammer and sickle">
        hammer and sickle
       </a>
       across the face of the Moon if
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Soviet_Moonshot" title="Soviet Moonshot">
        they get to it first
       </a>
       . To a
       <a href="/wiki/Television_network" title="Television network">
        television network
       </a>
       , he offers the Moon as a reliable and
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_Communications_Commission" title="Federal Communications Commission">
        uncensorable
       </a>
       broadcasting station.
      </p>
      <p>
       Harriman seeks to avoid government ownership of the Moon. As it passes directly overhead only in a narrow band north and south of the equator, he uses a legal principle that states that
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuius_est_solum_eius_est_usque_ad_coelum_et_ad_inferos" title="Cuius est solum eius est usque ad coelum et ad inferos">
        property rights extend to infinity above a land parcel
       </a>
       . On that basis, Mexico, Central and parts of South America, and other countries in those latitudes around the world, have a claim on the Moon. The United States also has a claim due to
       <a href="/wiki/Florida" title="Florida">
        Florida
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Texas" title="Texas">
        Texas
       </a>
       . By arranging for many countries to assert their rights Harriman persuades the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       to, as a compromise, assign management of the Moon to his company.
      </p>
      <p>
       Money remains the main difficulty. Harriman liquidates his assets, risks bankruptcy, damages his marriage, and raises funds in numerous legitimate and semi-legitimate ways; "I", he says, "would cheat, lie, steal, beg, bribe—do
       <i>
        anything
       </i>
       to accomplish what we have accomplished". Children donate money for a promise of all contributors' names engraved on a plaque left on the Moon. The names, however, will be microscopic in size. Harriman sells land and
       <a href="/wiki/Naming_rights" title="Naming rights">
        naming rights
       </a>
       to craters, and plans to sell
       <a href="/wiki/Cover_(philately)" title="Cover (philately)">
        postal covers
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Cancellation_(mail)" title="Cancellation (mail)">
        canceled
       </a>
       on the Moon to collectors. He starts rumors that diamonds exist in moondust, intending to secretly place gems in the rocket to convince people that the rumors are true. Harriman will strenuously deny that the diamonds are from the Moon, being merely part of a scientific experiment; he expects people not to believe him, but he will not be guilty of actual fraud.
      </p>
      <p>
       Harriman wants to be on the first flight of the
       <i>
        Pioneer
       </i>
       but the ship only has room for one pilot, Leslie LeCroix. The
       <a href="/wiki/Multistage_rocket" title="Multistage rocket">
        multistage rocket
       </a>
       launches from
       <a href="/wiki/Peterson_Air_Force_Base" title="Peterson Air Force Base">
        Peterson Field
       </a>
       , near
       <a href="/wiki/Colorado_Springs,_Colorado" title="Colorado Springs, Colorado">
        Colorado Springs, Colorado
       </a>
       , lands on the Moon, and returns to Earth. Harriman is the first to open the rocket's hatch; the canceled postal covers were left behind to save weight and he needs to get them aboard surreptitiously. While doing so, he asks LeCroix for the "lunar" diamonds. The pilot complies, then produces real lunar diamonds as well.
      </p>
      <p>
       As Harriman predicted, once the first flight succeeds, many seek to invest in his venture to make more flights using a
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_driver" title="Mass driver">
        catapult launcher
       </a>
       built on
       <a href="/wiki/Pikes_Peak" title="Pikes Peak">
        Pikes Peak
       </a>
       . The next flight will begin a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_colony" title="Lunar colony">
        lunar colony
       </a>
       . Harriman intends to be on the ship, but the majority owners of the venture object to his presence on the flight; he is too valuable to the company to risk in space. The rocket leaves without Harriman, who "looks as
       <a href="/wiki/Moses" title="Moses">
        Moses
       </a>
       must have looked, when he gazed out over the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Promised_land" title="Promised land">
        promised land
       </a>
       ."
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_works">
        Related works
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related works">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon_(short_story_collection)" title="The Man Who Sold the Moon (short story collection)">
          The Man Who Sold the Moon
         </a>
        </b>
       </i>
       is also the title of two collections of Heinlein's short stories. Both collections include
       <a href="/wiki/Let_There_Be_Light_(Heinlein_short_story)" title="Let There Be Light (Heinlein short story)">
        "Let There Be Light"
       </a>
       , "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Roads_Must_Roll" title="The Roads Must Roll">
        The Roads Must Roll
       </a>
       ", and "Requiem"; the first also includes "
       <a href="/wiki/Life-Line" title="Life-Line">
        Life-Line
       </a>
       " and "
       <a href="/wiki/Blowups_Happen" title="Blowups Happen">
        Blowups Happen
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the science fiction film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Destination_Moon_(film)" title="Destination Moon (film)">
         Destination Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       is generally described as being based on Heinlein's novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Rocket_Ship_Galileo" title="Rocket Ship Galileo">
         Rocket Ship Galileo
        </a>
       </i>
       , the story in fact bears a much closer resemblance to
       <i>
        The Man Who Sold the Moon
       </i>
       , whose copyright date shows that it was written in 1949, although it wasn't published until 1951, the year after
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       was released. However, the technology of
       <i>
        The Man Who Sold the Moon
       </i>
       is very different: its rocket is multi-staged, while
       <i>
        Destination Moon
       </i>
       uses a
       <a href="/wiki/Single-stage-to-orbit" title="Single-stage-to-orbit">
        single-stage-to-orbit
       </a>
       spaceship that takes off and lands vertically, both on Earth and the Moon, which is practically impossible using only chemical fuels. (Dialogue in the film makes it very clear that the spaceship is nuclear powered.)
      </p>
      <p>
       The novella also inspired David Bowie's song "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Man_Who_Sold_the_World" title="The Man Who Sold the World">
        The Man Who Sold the World
       </a>
       ", in both its title and its central themes.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">

        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2016)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Influence_on_other_Heinlein_works">
        Influence on other Heinlein works
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Man_Who_Sold_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Influence on other Heinlein works">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Harriman appears in "
       <a href="/wiki/Requiem_(short_story)" title="Requiem (short story)">
        Requiem
       </a>
       " as an old man who has still not been able to go to the Moon. It was published in 1940, 11 years before
       <i>
        The Man Who Sold the Moon
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The name "Harriman" reappears in many
       <i>
        Future History
       </i>
       stories as the name of various businesses and foundations, indicating that Harriman's impact on that timeline is significant. The name is also used in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Variable_Star" title="Variable Star">
         Variable Star
        </a>
       </i>
       , a novel outlined by Heinlein but written by
       <a href="/wiki/Spider_Robinson" title="Spider Robinson">
        Spider Robinson
       </a>
       following Heinlein's death; the novel diverges from the Future History.
      </p>
