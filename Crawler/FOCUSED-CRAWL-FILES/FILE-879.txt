      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         Rabbit's Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Avant-garde" title="Avant-garde">
        avant-garde
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">
        short film
       </a>
       by American filmmaker
       <a href="/wiki/Kenneth_Anger" title="Kenneth Anger">
        Kenneth Anger
       </a>
       . Filmed in 1950,
       <i>
        Rabbit's Moon
       </i>
       was not completed (nor did it see release) until 1972. Anger re-released the film in 1979, sped up and with a different soundtrack.
      </p>
      <p>
       Filmed under a blue filter and set within a wooded glade during the night, the plot revolves around a clown,
       <a href="/wiki/Pierrot" title="Pierrot">
        Pierrot
       </a>
       , his longing for the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       (in which a rabbit lives – a concept found in both Japanese folklore and
       <a href="/wiki/Aztec_mythology" title="Aztec mythology">
        Aztec
       </a>
       mythology), and his futile attempts to jump up and catch it. Subsequently, another clown (
       <a href="/wiki/Harlequin" title="Harlequin">
        Harlequin
       </a>
       ) appears and teases Pierrot, showing him
       <a href="/wiki/Columbina" title="Columbina">
        Columbina
       </a>
       , with whom he appears to fall in love.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Production">
        Production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbit%27s_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The sets were borrowed from French filmmaker
       <a href="/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Melville" title="Jean-Pierre Melville">
        Jean-Pierre Melville
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Music">
        Music
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rabbit%27s_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Music">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The 1972 version of
       <i>
        Rabbit's Moon
       </i>
       features a soundtrack consisting of 1950s and '60s
       <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">
        pop
       </a>
       : "There's a Moon Out Tonight" by
       <a href="/wiki/The_Capris" title="The Capris">
        The Capris
       </a>
       , "
       <a href="/wiki/Oh,_What_a_Night_(The_Dells_song)" title="Oh, What a Night (The Dells song)">
        Oh, What a Night
       </a>
       " by
       <a href="/wiki/The_Dells" title="The Dells">
        The Dells
       </a>
       , "
       <a href="/wiki/Bye_Bye_Baby_(Mary_Wells_song)" title="Bye Bye Baby (Mary Wells song)">
        Bye Bye Baby
       </a>
       " by
       <a href="/wiki/Mary_Wells" title="Mary Wells">
        Mary Wells
       </a>
       , "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/I_Only_Have_Eyes_For_You" title="I Only Have Eyes For You">
        I Only Have Eyes For You
       </a>
       " by
       <a href="/wiki/The_Flamingos" title="The Flamingos">
        The Flamingos
       </a>
       and "
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tears_On_My_Pillow" title="Tears On My Pillow">
        Tears On My Pillow
       </a>
       " by
       <a href="/wiki/The_El_Dorados" title="The El Dorados">
        The El Dorados
       </a>
       . The 1979 version features only a loop of A Raincoat's "It Came In The Night" as its soundtrack.
      </p>
