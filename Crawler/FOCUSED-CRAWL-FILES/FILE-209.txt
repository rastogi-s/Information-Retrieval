      <p>
       The
       <b>
        December 2010 lunar eclipse
       </b>
       occurred from 5:27 to 11:06
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        UTC
       </a>
       on December 21, 2010, coinciding with the date of the
       <a href="/wiki/December_solstice" title="December solstice">
        December solstice
       </a>
       . It was visible in its entirety as a total
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/North_America" title="North America">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_America" title="South America">
        South America
       </a>
       , Iceland, Ireland, Britain and northern Scandinavia.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
        Occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=December_2010_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The eclipse of December 2010 was the first total lunar eclipse in almost three years, since the
       <a href="/wiki/February_2008_lunar_eclipse" title="February 2008 lunar eclipse">
        February 2008 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It is the second of two lunar eclipses in 2010. The first was a
       <a href="/wiki/June_2010_lunar_eclipse" title="June 2010 lunar eclipse">
        partial lunar eclipse on June 26, 2010
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The eclipse was the first total
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       to occur on the day of the Northern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Solstice" title="Winter Solstice">
        Winter Solstice
       </a>
       (Southern
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Summer_Solstice" title="Summer Solstice">
        Summer Solstice
       </a>
       ) since
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_17th-century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 17th-century lunar eclipses">
        1638
       </a>
       , and only the second in the
       <a href="/wiki/Common_Era" title="Common Era">
        Common Era
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_eclipses">
        Related eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=December_2010_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Related eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       This eclipse occurred at the descending node of the moon's orbit. Lunar eclipses are always paired with a solar eclipse either 2 weeks before or after at
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       in the opposite node. In this case, it was followed by a partial
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       at the ascending node on
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_January_4,_2011" title="Solar eclipse of January 4, 2011">
        January 4, 2011
       </a>
       , visible from Europe, northern Africa, and western Asia.
      </p>
      <p>
       The following two lunar eclipses were also total, occurring on
       <a href="/wiki/June_2011_lunar_eclipse" title="June 2011 lunar eclipse">
        June 15, 2011
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/December_2011_lunar_eclipse" title="December 2011 lunar eclipse">
        December 10, 2011
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        See
        <a href="#Lunar_year_series">
         This lunar year cycle
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The next December solstice total lunar eclipse, as a
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        Metonic twin
       </a>
       eclipse, will be
       <a href="/wiki/December_2029_lunar_eclipse" title="December 2029 lunar eclipse">
        December 20, 2029
       </a>
       (19 years later), one day before solstice.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        See
        <a href="#Metonic_cycles_(19_years)">
         This 19 year Metonic cycle
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)" title="Saros (astronomy)">
        saros
       </a>
       series lasts for many centuries and has a similar event every 18 years and 11 days. This eclipse is the 18th of 26 total lunar eclipses in lunar saros series 125. The previous occurrence was on
       <a href="/wiki/December_1992_lunar_eclipse" title="December 1992 lunar eclipse">
        December 9, 1992
       </a>
       , and the next will occur on
       <a href="/wiki/December_2028_lunar_eclipse" title="December 2028 lunar eclipse">
        December 31, 2028
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        See
        <a href="#Saros_series">
         This saros
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <p>
       In North America, the eclipse was visible in its entirety on December 21, 2010, from 12:27 a.m. to 6:06 a.m.
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">
        Eastern Standard Time
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Standard_Time" title="Central Standard Time">
        Central Standard Time
       </a>
       zone and west, the eclipse began the night of December 20.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Observers along South America's east coast missed the late stages of the eclipse because they occurred after moon-set.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citystatetimes1_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-citystatetimes1-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Likewise much of Europe and Africa experienced moon-set while the eclipse was in progress. In Europe, only those observers in northern Scandinavia (including Iceland), Ireland and Britain could observe the entire event. For observers in eastern Asia the moon rose in eclipse. The eclipse was not visible from southern and eastern Africa, the Middle East or South Asia. In Japan and northeastern Asia, the eclipse's end was visible, with the moon rising at sunset. In the
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       it was observable as a partial lunar eclipse just after sunset.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-citystatetimes1_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-citystatetimes1-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Predictions suggested that the total eclipse may appear unusually orange or red, as a result of the eruption of
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Merapi" title="Mount Merapi">
        Mount Merapi
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       on October 26.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span id="Metonic_cycles_.2819_years.29">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Metonic_cycles_(19_years)">
        Metonic cycles (19 years)
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=December_2010_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Metonic cycles (19 years)">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        Metonic cycle
       </a>
       repeats nearly exactly every 19 years and represents a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">
        Saros cycle
       </a>
       plus one lunar year. Because it occurs on the same calendar date, the earth's shadow will be in nearly the same location relative to the background stars.
      </p>

      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Tritos" title="Tritos">
        tritos
       </a>
       series repeats 31 days short of 11 years at alternating nodes. Sequential events have incremental
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saros_cycle" title="Saros cycle">
        Saros cycle
       </a>
       indices.
      </p>
      <p>
       This series produces 20 total eclipses between April 24, 1967 and August 1, 2167, only being partial on November 19, 2021.

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Saros_series">
        Saros series
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=December_2010_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Saros series">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)" title="Saros (astronomy)">
        saros
       </a>
       series 125, repeating every 18 years and 11 days, has 26 total lunar eclipses. The first was on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_18th_century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 18th century lunar eclipses">
        June 17, 1704
       </a>
       and the last will be on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_22nd_century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 22nd century lunar eclipses">
        March 19, 2155
       </a>
       . The longest totality occurrence of this series (7th) was on
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_19th_century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 19th century lunar eclipses">
        August 22, 1812
       </a>
       when totality lasted one hour and 42 minutes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
