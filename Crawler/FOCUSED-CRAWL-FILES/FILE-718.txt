   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Maya moon goddess
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       The traditional Mayas generally assume the moon to be female, and the moon's phases are accordingly conceived as the stages of a woman's life. The
       <b>
        Maya moon goddess
       </b>
       wields great influence in many areas. Being in the image of a woman, she is associated with sexuality and procreation, fertility and growth, not only of human beings, but also of the vegetation and the crops. Since growth can also cause all sorts of ailments, the moon goddess is also a goddess of disease. Everywhere in
       <a href="/wiki/Mesoamerica" title="Mesoamerica">
        Mesoamerica
       </a>
       , including the Mayan area, she is specifically associated with water, be it wells, rainfall, or the rainy season. In the codices, she has a terrestrial counterpart in
       <a href="/wiki/Goddess_I" title="Goddess I">
        goddess I
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_mythology">
        Lunar mythology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_moon_goddess&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lunar mythology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The sources for Maya lunar mythology are almost entirely contemporaneous, with the exception of the
       <a href="/wiki/Popol_Vuh" title="Popol Vuh">
        Popol Vuh
       </a>
       . A division can be made according to the moon's kinship roles.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Moon as a male sibling: celestial power
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In the Popol Vuh (16th century), the
       <a href="/wiki/Maya_Hero_Twins" title="Maya Hero Twins">
        Maya Hero Twins
       </a>
       are finally transformed into sun and moon, implying the recognition of a male moon, in a departure from the main Maya tradition. However, the Popol Vuh hardly belongs to lunar mythology, and becoming sun and moon may well be a metonym for acquiring dominance over the sky and thus, metaphorically, political predominance.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Moon as a wife: origin of menstruation
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       True lunar mythology is first and foremost represented by the
       <a href="/wiki/Q%27eqchi%27_people" title="Q'eqchi' people">
        Q'eqchi'
       </a>
       myth of Sun and Moon first studied by Eric Thompson.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It makes the Moon Goddess (Po) the daughter of the Earth God, or 'Mountain-Valley'. She is wooed and finally captured by Sun. They sleep together. When this is discovered and the couple flees, the angry father reacts by having his daughter destroyed. In all likelihood, this patriarchal punishment of a basic infraction of the rules of alliance represents the origin of menstruation, the 'evil blood' of a disobedient daughter colouring the water of sea and lake red, or sinking into the earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The menstrual blood is stored in thirteen jars. In the jars, it is first transformed into creatures such as snakes and insects, a transformation leading up to the origin of poison and the diseases caused by it. However, some jars also hold medicinal plants. The thirteenth jar is the lunar jar: On being opened, the Moon is reborn from it. The creation of her vagina on instigation of, or directly by, her husband represents the origin of human procreation. Subsequent episodes make the Moon Goddess cohabit with Sun's elder brother, Cloud, and with the devil in the shape of a king vulture, thus connecting her to rainfall and black sorcery.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <i>
         Moon as a (grand)mother: the rabbit in the moon
        </i>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       Among the Mayas of Chiapas and the Northwestern Highlands of Guatemala, Moon is not Sun's wife, but his mother or grandmother, while Sun is a young boy harassed by his elder brethren. Only in this mythology do we find the origin of the lunar rabbit, either as one of the elder brethren transformed into wild animals and caught by his mother,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or as a creature responsible for the resurgence of the wild vegetation on Sun's maize field. In the latter case, the rabbit is caught by Sun, passed on to his mother, and again taken into the sky.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In Northwestern Guatemala, the rabbit in the moon is sometimes replaced by a deer in the moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_moon_goddess_in_the_Post-Classic_and_Classic_periods">
        The moon goddess in the Post-Classic and Classic periods
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_moon_goddess&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The moon goddess in the Post-Classic and Classic periods">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tmulti tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:388px;max-width:388px">
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:222px;max-width:222px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="565" data-file-width="488" height="255" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg/220px-Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg/330px-Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg/440px-Goddess_O_Ixchel.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="tsingle" style="float:left;margin:1px;width:162px;max-width:162px">
         <div class="thumbimage">
          <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg">
           <img alt="" data-file-height="1064" data-file-width="665" height="256" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg/160px-Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg/240px-Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg/320px-Maya_moon_goddess_from_Sacul_drinking_vessel.jpg 2x" width="160"/>
          </a>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div style="clear:left">
        </div>
        <div class="thumbcaption" style="clear:left;text-align:center;background-color:transparent">
         Moon Goddess with rabbit (left); Maize God with rabbit (right)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In the three Post-Classic codices, the Moon Goddess is underrepresented. Instead, one finds almanacs devoted to what appears to be her terrestrial counterpart, the
       <a href="/wiki/Goddess_I" title="Goddess I">
        Goddess I
       </a>
       ('White Woman'). In Classic Maya art, however, the Moon Goddess occurs frequently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       She is shown as a young woman holding her rabbit, and framed by the crescent of the waxing moon, which is her most important, identifying attribute. The Moon Goddess may also be sitting on a throne, alone (as in the Dresden codex), or behind god D (
       <a href="/wiki/Itzamna" title="Itzamna">
        Itzamna
       </a>
       ). Although, in oral tradition, the goddess is often treated as the consort of the Sun Deity, Classic iconography does not insist on this (see
       <a href="/wiki/Kinich_Ahau" title="Kinich Ahau">
        Kinich Ahau
       </a>
       ). The lunar rabbit (perhaps a
       <a href="/wiki/Trickster" title="Trickster">
        Trickster
       </a>
       character) has an important role to play in a poorly understood episode involving the Moon Goddess, the Twins, the
       <a href="/wiki/Maya_maize_god" title="Maya maize god">
        Maya maize god
       </a>
       , and the aged
       <a href="/wiki/God_L" title="God L">
        god L
       </a>
       . In some cases, the Moon Goddess is fused with the main Maya maize god, making it uncertain whether what we see is a Moon Goddess with a maize aspect (that is, a maize-bringing moon), or a Maize God with a lunar aspect or function.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Calendrical_functions">
        Calendrical functions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Maya_moon_goddess&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Calendrical functions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Moon Goddess is the patroness of the month of Ch'en 'Well'. ("Moon has gone to her well" is an expression referring to New Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) She is also the patroness of one of the Venus 'years'. Her importance is reflected by the eclipse tables of the Dresden Codex and by the Lunar Series of the
       <a href="/wiki/Long_Count" title="Long Count">
        Long Count
       </a>
       . Glyph C of the Lunar Series (indicating sequences of six lunations for purposes of eclipse prediction)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       connects her to other deities, such as the death god (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/God_A" title="God A">
        God A
       </a>
       ), the Jaguar God of the Underworld, and, perhaps, the Maize God.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
