      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbits
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       direction and completes one
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        revolution
       </a>
       relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Fixed_stars" title="Fixed stars">
        stars
       </a>
       in about 27.323 days (a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sidereal_month" title="Sidereal month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       ) and one revolution relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       in about 29.530 days (a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       ). Earth and the Moon orbit about their
       <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
        barycentre
       </a>
       (common center of mass), which lies about 4,600 km (2,900 mi) from Earth's center (about
       <span class="sfrac nowrap" style="display:inline-block; vertical-align:-0.5em; font-size:85%; text-align:center;">
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em;">
         3
        </span>
        <span class="visualhide">
         /
        </span>
        <span style="display:block; line-height:1em; margin:0 0.1em; border-top:1px solid;">
         4
        </span>
       </span>
       of the radius of Earth). On average, the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        distance to the Moon
       </a>
       is about 385,000 km (239,000 mi) from Earth's center, which corresponds to about 60 Earth radii. With a mean
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Orbital_velocity" title="Orbital velocity">
        orbital velocity
       </a>
       of 1.022 km/s (2,290 mph),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the Moon covers a distance approximately its diameter, or about
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        half a degree
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_sphere" title="Celestial sphere">
        celestial sphere
       </a>
       , each hour. The Moon differs from most
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       of other
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planets
       </a>
       in that its orbit is close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       plane instead of that of its primary (in this case, the Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equatorial_plane" title="Equatorial plane">
        equatorial plane
       </a>
       ). The Moon's orbital plane is
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       by about 5.1° to the ecliptic plane, whereas the Moon's equatorial plane is
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        tilted
       </a>
       by only 1.5°.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">
        Properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The properties of the orbit described in this section are approximations. The Moon's orbit around Earth has many irregularities (
       <a href="/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy)" title="Perturbation (astronomy)">
        perturbations
       </a>
       ), the study of which (
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory" title="Lunar theory">
        lunar theory
       </a>
       ) has a long history.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MG-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_perigee_apogee.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Comparison of the Moon's apparent size at lunar
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
          perigee
         </a>
         –
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
          apogee

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elliptic_shape">
        Elliptic shape
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Elliptic shape">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The orbit of the Moon is distinctly elliptical, with an average
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of 0.0549. The non-circular form of the lunar orbit causes variations in the Moon's angular speed and apparent size as it moves towards and away from an observer on Earth. The mean angular movement relative to an imaginary observer at the barycentre is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001131760000000000♠
        </span>
        13.176
       </span>
       ° per day to the east (
       <a href="/wiki/Julian_day" title="Julian day">
        Julian day
       </a>
       2000).
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Elongation">
        Elongation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Elongation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Elongation_(astronomy)" title="Elongation (astronomy)">
        elongation
       </a>
       is its angular distance east of the Sun at any time. At new moon, it is zero and the Moon is said to be in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       . At full moon, the elongation is 180° and it is said to be in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy)" title="Opposition (astronomy)">
        opposition
       </a>
       . In both cases, the Moon is in
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       , that is, the Sun, Moon and Earth are nearly aligned. When elongation is either 90° or 270°, the Moon is said to be in
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrature_(astronomy)" title="Quadrature (astronomy)">
        quadrature
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Rotation">
        Rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_apsidal_precession.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1257" data-file-width="1446" height="191" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Moon_apsidal_precession.png/220px-Moon_apsidal_precession.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Moon_apsidal_precession.png/330px-Moon_apsidal_precession.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Moon_apsidal_precession.png/440px-Moon_apsidal_precession.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_apsidal_precession.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
          Apsidal precession
         </a>
         —the Moon's orbit rotates once every 8.85 years.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Earth-Moon.PNG">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="545" data-file-width="860" height="139" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth-Moon.PNG/220px-Earth-Moon.PNG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth-Moon.PNG/330px-Earth-Moon.PNG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/43/Earth-Moon.PNG/440px-Earth-Moon.PNG 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Earth-Moon.PNG" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
          Orbital inclination
         </a>
         —the Moon's orbit is inclined by 5.14° to the
         <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
          ecliptic
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_perturbation.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="583" data-file-width="625" height="205" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Lunar_perturbation.jpg/220px-Lunar_perturbation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Lunar_perturbation.jpg/330px-Lunar_perturbation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Lunar_perturbation.jpg/440px-Lunar_perturbation.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_perturbation.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Earth's lunar orbit perturbations
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The orientation of the orbit is not fixed in space, but rotates over time. This orbital precession is also called
       <a href="/wiki/Apsidal_precession" title="Apsidal precession">
        apsidal precession
       </a>
       and is the rotation of the Moon's orbit within the orbital plane, i.e. the axes of the ellipse change direction. The Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Major_axis" title="Major axis">
        major axis
       </a>
       – the longest diameter of the orbit, joining its nearest and farthest points, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
        apogee
       </a>
       , respectively – makes one complete revolution every 8.85 Earth years, or 3,232.6054 days, as it rotates slowly in the same direction as the Moon itself (direct motion). The Moon's apsidal precession is distinct from, and should not be confused with its
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_precession" title="Axial precession">
        axial precession
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       For the moon to make a complete rotation on its axis takes approximately 27.3 days. It takes the moon about this same time to orbit the earth; this is why we always see the same side of the moon (see
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inclination">
        Inclination
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Inclination">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The mean inclination of the lunar orbit to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Plane_of_the_ecliptic" title="Plane of the ecliptic">
        ecliptic plane
       </a>
       is 5.145°. The rotational axis of the Moon is also not perpendicular to its orbital plane, so the lunar equator is not in the plane of its orbit, but is inclined to it by a constant value of 6.688° (this is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity" title="Obliquity">
        obliquity
       </a>
       ). This does not mean that, as a result of the precession of the Moon's orbital plane, the angle between the lunar equator and the ecliptic would vary between the sum (11.833°) and difference (1.543°) of these two angles, but, as was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Jacques_Cassini" title="Jacques Cassini">
        Jacques Cassini
       </a>
       in 1722, the rotational axis of the Moon precesses with the same rate as its orbital plane, but is 180° out of phase
       <i>
        (see
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini%27s_Laws" title="Cassini's Laws">
         Cassini's Laws
        </a>
        )
       </i>
       . Therefore, the angle between the ecliptic and the lunar equator is always 1.543°, even though the rotational axis of the Moon is not fixed with respect to the stars.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of the inclination of the moon's orbit, the moon is above the horizon at the
       <a href="/wiki/North_Pole" title="North Pole">
        North
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole" title="South Pole">
        South Pole
       </a>
       for almost two weeks every month, even though the sun is below the horizon for six months at a time. The period from moonrise to moonrise at the poles is quite close to the sidereal period, or 27.3 days. When the sun is the furthest below the horizon (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Winter_Solstice" title="Winter Solstice">
        winter solstice
       </a>
       ), the moon will be full when it is at its highest point.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nodes">
        Nodes
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Nodes">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_node" title="Lunar node">
        Lunar node
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The nodes are points at which the Moon's orbit crosses the ecliptic. The Moon crosses the same node every 27.2122 days, an interval called the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Draconic_month" title="Lunar month">
         draconic
        </a>
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        draconitic month
       </i>
       . The line of nodes, the intersection between the two respective planes, has a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde motion
       </a>
       : for an observer on Earth, it rotates westward along the ecliptic with a period of 18.60 years or 19.3549° per year. When viewed from the celestial north, the nodes move clockwise around Earth, opposite to Earth's own spin and its revolution around the Sun. Lunar and solar eclipses can occur when the nodes align with the Sun, roughly every 173.3 days. Lunar orbit inclination also determines eclipses; shadows cross when nodes coincide with full and new moon when the Sun, Earth, and Moon align in three dimensions.
      </p>
      <p>
       In effect, this means that the "
       <a href="/wiki/Tropical_year" title="Tropical year">
        tropical year
       </a>
       " on the moon is only 347 days long. This is called the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Draconic_year" title="Draconic year">
        draconic year
       </a>
       or eclipse year. The "seasons" on the moon fit into this period. For about half of this draconic year, the sun is north of the lunar equator (but at most 1.543°), and for the other half, it is south of the lunar equator. Obviously, the effect of these seasons is minor compared to the difference between lunar night and lunar day. At the lunar poles, instead of usual lunar days and nights of about 15 Earth days, the sun will be "up" for 173 days as it will be "down"; polar sunrise and sunset takes 18 days each year. "Up" here means that the centre of the sun is above the horizon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Lunar polar sunrises and sunsets occur around the time of eclipses (solar or lunar). For example, at the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_March_9,_2016" title="Solar eclipse of March 9, 2016">
        Solar eclipse of March 9, 2016
       </a>
       , the moon was near its descending node, and the sun was near the point in the sky where the equator of the moon crosses the ecliptic. When the sun reaches that point, the centre of the sun sets at the lunar north pole and rises at the lunar south pole.
      </p>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_standstill">
        Lunar standstill
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Lunar standstill">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_standstill" title="Lunar standstill">
        Lunar standstill
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Every 18.6 years, the angle between the Moon's orbit and Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       reaches a maximum of 28°36′, the sum of Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        equatorial tilt
       </a>
       (23°27′) and the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        orbital inclination
       </a>
       (5°09′) to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       . This is called
       <i>
        major
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_standstill" title="Lunar standstill">
         lunar standstill
        </a>
       </i>
       . Around this time, the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Declination" title="Declination">
        declination
       </a>
       will vary from −28°36′ to +28°36′. Conversely, 9.3 years later, the angle between the Moon's orbit and Earth's equator reaches its minimum of 18°20′. This is called a
       <i>
        minor lunar standstill
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       When the inclination of the Moon's orbit to the Earth's equator is at its minimum of 18°20′, the centre of the Moon's disk will be above the
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       every day from latitudes less than 71°40' (90° − 18°20') north or south. When the inclination is at its maximum of 28°36', the centre of the Moon's disk will be above the horizon every day only from latitudes less than 61°24' (90° − 28°36') north or south.
      </p>
      <p>
       At
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_regions_of_Earth" title="Polar regions of Earth">
        higher latitudes
       </a>
       , there will be a period of at least one day each month when the Moon does not rise, but there will also be a period of at least one day each month when the Moon does not set. This is similar to the
       <a href="/wiki/Season" title="Season">
        seasonal
       </a>
       behaviour of the Sun, but with a period of 27.3 days instead of 365 days. Note that a point on the Moon can actually be visible when it is about 34
       <a href="/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc" title="Minute and second of arc">
        arc minutes
       </a>
       below the horizon, due to
       <a href="/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction" title="Atmospheric refraction">
        atmospheric refraction
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_observations_and_measurements">
        History of observations and measurements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: History of observations and measurements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       About
       <a href="/wiki/1000s_BC_(decade)" title="1000s BC (decade)">
        1000 BC
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Babylon" title="Babylon">
        Babylonians
       </a>
       were the first human civilization known to have kept a consistent record of lunar observations. Clay tablets from that period, which have been found over the territory of present-day Iraq, are inscribed with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cuneiform" title="Cuneiform">
        cuneiform
       </a>
       writing recording the times and dates of moonrises and moonsets, the stars that the Moon passed close by, and the time differences between rising and setting of both the Sun and the Moon around the time of the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy" title="Babylonian astronomy">
        Babylonian astronomy
       </a>
       discovered the three main periods of the Moon's motion and used
       <a href="/wiki/Data_analysis" title="Data analysis">
        data analysis
       </a>
       to build lunar calendars that extended well into the future.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MG-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This use of detailed, systematic observations to make predictions based on experimental data may be classified as the first
       <a href="/wiki/Scientific_method" title="Scientific method">
        scientific study
       </a>
       in human history. However, the Babylonians seem to have lacked any geometrical or physical interpretation of their data, and they could not predict future lunar eclipses (although "warnings" were issued before likely eclipse times).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greece" title="Ancient Greece">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       astronomers were the first to introduce and analyze
       <a href="/wiki/Mathematical_model" title="Mathematical model">
        mathematical models
       </a>
       of the motion of objects in the sky.
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       described lunar motion by using a well-defined geometric model of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Epicycle" title="Epicycle">
        epicycles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Evection" title="Evection">
        evection
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MG-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">
        Sir Isaac Newton
       </a>
       was the first to develop a complete theory of motion, mechanics. The sheer wealth of humanity's observations of the lunar motion was the main testbed of his theory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MG_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MG-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_periods">
        Lunar periods
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <p>
       There are several different periods associated with the lunar orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Sidereal_month" title="Lunar month">
        sidereal month
       </a>
       is the time it takes to make one complete orbit around Earth with respect to the fixed stars. It is about 27.32 days. The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Synodic_month" title="Lunar month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       is the time it takes the Moon to reach the same visual
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        phase
       </a>
       . This varies notably throughout the year,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AA_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AA-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but averages around 29.53 days. The synodic period is longer than the sidereal period because the Earth–Moon system moves in its orbit around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       during each sidereal month, hence a longer period is required to achieve a similar alignment of Earth, the Sun, and the Moon. The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Anomalistic_month" title="Lunar month">
        anomalistic month
       </a>
       is the time between perigees and is about 27.55 days. The Earth–Moon separation determines the strength of the lunar tide raising force.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Draconic_month" title="Lunar month">
        draconic month
       </a>
       is the time from
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_node" title="Orbital node">
        ascending node
       </a>
       to ascending node. The time between two successive passes of the same ecliptic longitude is called the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month#Tropical_month" title="Lunar month">
        tropical month
       </a>
       . The latter three periods are slightly different from the sidereal month.
      </p>
      <p>
       The average length of a
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        calendar month
       </a>
       (a twelfth of a year) is about 30.4 days. This is not a lunar period, though the calendar month is historically related to the visible lunar phase.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tidal_evolution">
        Tidal evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Tidal evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        Tide
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        Tidal acceleration
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt#Long_term" title="Axial tilt">
        Axial tilt § Long term
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gravitation" title="Gravitation">
        gravitational
       </a>
       attraction that the Moon exerts on Earth is the cause of
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       in the sea; the Sun has a lesser tidal influence. If Earth had a global ocean of uniform depth, the Moon would act to deform both the solid Earth (by a small amount) and the ocean in the shape of an ellipsoid with the high points roughly beneath the Moon and on the opposite side of Earth. However, because of the presence of the continents, Earth's much faster rotation and varying
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean" title="Ocean">
        ocean
       </a>
       depths, this simplistic visualisation does not happen. Although the tidal flow period is generally synchronized to the Moon's orbit around Earth, its relative timing varies greatly. In some places on Earth, there is only one high tide per day, whereas others such as
       <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">
        Southampton
       </a>
       have four, though this is somewhat rare.
      </p>
      <p>
       The notional tidal bulges are carried ahead of the Earth–Moon orientation by the continents as a result of Earth's rotation. The eccentric mass of each bulge exerts a small amount of gravitational attraction on the Moon, with the bulge on the side of Earth closest to the Moon pulling in a direction slightly forward along the Moon's orbit (because Earth's rotation has carried the bulge forward). The bulge on the side furthest from the Moon has the opposite effect, but because the gravitational attraction varies inversely with the square of distance, the effect is stronger for the near-side bulge. As a result, some of Earth's angular (or rotational) momentum is gradually being transferred to the rotation of the Earth–Moon pair around their mutual centre of mass, called the barycentre. This slightly faster rotation causes the Earth–Moon distance to increase at approximately 38 millimetres per year.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conservation_of_angular_momentum" title="Conservation of angular momentum">
        Conservation of angular momentum
       </a>
       means that Earth's axial rotation is gradually slowing, and because of this its day lengthens by approximately 23 microseconds every year (excluding
       <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound#Effects" title="Post-glacial rebound">
        glacial rebound
       </a>
       ). Both figures are valid only for the current configuration of the continents.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhythmites" title="Rhythmites">
        Tidal rhythmites
       </a>
       from 620 million years ago show that, over hundreds of millions of years, the Moon receded at an average rate of 22 mm (0.87 in) per year and the day lengthened at an average rate of 12 microseconds per year, both about half of their current values. See
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal acceleration
       </a>
       for a more detailed description and references.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon is gradually receding from Earth into a higher orbit, and calculations suggest that this would continue for about 50 billion years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By that time, Earth and the Moon would be in a mutual spin–orbit resonance or
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       , in which the Moon will orbit Earth in about 47 days (currently 27 days), and both the Moon and Earth would rotate around their axes in the same time, always facing each other with the same side. This has already happened to the Moon—the same side always faces Earth—and is also slowly happening to the Earth. However, the slowdown of Earth's rotation is not occurring fast enough for the rotation to lengthen to a month before other effects change the situation: approximately 2.3 billion years from now, the increase of the Sun's
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       will have caused Earth's oceans to evaporate,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       removing the bulk of the tidal friction and acceleration.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Libration">
        Libration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Libration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        Libration
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="334" data-file-width="356" height="206" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif/220px-Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c0/Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif/330px-Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c0/Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_libration_with_phase2.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Animation of the Moon as it cycles through its phases. The apparent wobbling of the Moon is known as
         <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
          libration
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Moon is in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synchronous_rotation" title="Synchronous rotation">
        synchronous rotation
       </a>
       , meaning that it keeps the same face toward Earth at all times. This synchronous rotation is only true on average, because the Moon's orbit has a definite eccentricity. As a result, the angular velocity of the Moon varies as it orbits Earth and hence is not always equal to the Moon's rotational velocity. When the Moon is at its perigee, its rotation is slower than its orbital motion, and this allows us to see up to eight degrees of longitude of its eastern (right)
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side
       </a>
       . Conversely, when the Moon reaches its apogee, its rotation is faster than its orbital motion and this reveals eight degrees of longitude of its western (left) far side. This is referred to as
       <i>
        longitudinal libration
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the lunar orbit is also inclined to Earth's ecliptic plane by 5.1°, the rotational axis of the Moon seems to rotate towards and away from Earth during one complete orbit. This is referred to as
       <i>
        latitudinal libration
       </i>
       , which allows one to see almost 7° of latitude beyond the pole on the far side. Finally, because the Moon is only about 60 Earth radii away from Earth's centre of mass, an observer at the equator who observes the Moon throughout the night moves laterally by one Earth diameter. This gives rise to a
       <i>
        diurnal libration
       </i>
       , which allows one to view an additional one degree's worth of lunar longitude. For the same reason, observers at both of Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Geographical_pole" title="Geographical pole">
        geographical poles
       </a>
       would be able to see one additional degree's worth of libration in latitude.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Path_of_Earth_and_Moon_around_Sun">
        Path of Earth and Moon around Sun
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Orbit_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Path of Earth and Moon around Sun">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="floatleft">
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Sun_earth_moon.svg">
        <img alt="Sun earth moon.svg" class="thumbborder" data-file-height="205" data-file-width="205" height="70" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Sun_earth_moon.svg/70px-Sun_earth_moon.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Sun_earth_moon.svg/105px-Sun_earth_moon.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Sun_earth_moon.svg/140px-Sun_earth_moon.svg.png 2x" width="70"/>
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_trajectory1.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="392" data-file-width="800" height="108" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Moon_trajectory1.svg/220px-Moon_trajectory1.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Moon_trajectory1.svg/330px-Moon_trajectory1.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Moon_trajectory1.svg/440px-Moon_trajectory1.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_trajectory1.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Section of the Earth–Moon system's trajectory around the Sun (to scale)
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapes3_18-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-shapes3-18">
           [18]
          </a>
         </sup>
         <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column">
          <span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid #00289E; background-color: #6B90FF; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">
          </span>
          Orbital path of the Earth
         </div>
         <div class="legend" style="-webkit-column-break-inside: avoid;page-break-inside: avoid;break-inside: avoid-column">
          <span class="legend-color" style="display:inline-block; width:1.5em; height:1.5em; margin:1px 0; border:1px solid #555; background-color: #bfbfbf; color:black; font-size:100%; text-align:center;">
          </span>
          Orbital path of the Moon
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       When viewed from the north
       <a href="/wiki/Celestial_pole" title="Celestial pole">
        celestial pole
       </a>
       (i.e., from the approximate direction of the star
       <a href="/wiki/Polaris" title="Polaris">
        Polaris
       </a>
       ) the Moon orbits Earth
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anticlockwise" title="Anticlockwise">
        anticlockwise
       </a>
       and Earth orbits the Sun anticlockwise, and the Moon and Earth rotate on their own axes anticlockwise.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Right-hand_rule#Direction_associated_with_a_rotation" title="Right-hand rule">
        right-hand rule
       </a>
       can be used to indicate the direction of the angular velocity. If the thumb of the right hand points to the north celestial pole, its fingers curl in the direction that the Moon orbits Earth, Earth orbits the Sun, and the Moon and Earth rotate on their own axes.
      </p>
      <p>
       In representations of the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , it is common to draw the trajectory of Earth from the point of view of the Sun, and the trajectory of the Moon from the point of view of Earth. This could give the impression that the Moon orbits Earth in such a way that sometimes it goes backwards when viewed from the Sun's perspective. Because the orbital velocity of the Moon around Earth (1 km/s) is small compared to the orbital velocity of Earth about the Sun (30 km/s), this never happens. There are no rearward loops in the Moon's solar orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       Considering the Earth–Moon system as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Binary_planet" title="Binary planet">
        binary planet
       </a>
       , its centre of gravity is within Earth, about 4,624 km (2,873 mi) or 72.6% of the Earth's radius from the centre of the Earth. This centre of gravity remains on the line between the centres of the Earth and Moon as the Earth completes its diurnal rotation. The path of the Earth–Moon system in its solar orbit is defined as the movement of this mutual centre of gravity around the sun. Consequently, Earth's centre veers inside and outside the solar orbital path during each synodic month as the Moon moves in its orbit around the common centre of gravity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convex_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-convex-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Sun's gravitational effect on the Moon is more than twice that of Earth's on the Moon; consequently, the Moon's trajectory is always convex
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convex_19-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-convex-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (as seen when looking Sunward at the entire Sun–Earth–Moon system from a great distance outside Earth–Moon solar orbit), and is nowhere concave (from the same perspective) or looped.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapes3_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-shapes3-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-convex_19-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-convex-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       That is, the region enclosed by the Moon's orbit of the Sun is a
       <a href="/wiki/Convex_set" title="Convex set">
        convex set
       </a>
       .
      </p>
