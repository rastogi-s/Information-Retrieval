   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Moon Impact Probe
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Moon Impact Probe
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        MIP
       </b>
       ) developed by the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation" title="Indian Space Research Organisation">
        Indian Space Research Organisation (ISRO)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">
        India
       </a>
       's national space agency, was a
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        lunar
       </a>
       probe that was released by ISRO's
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_the_Moon" title="Exploration of the Moon">
        lunar remote sensing
       </a>
       orbiter which in turn was launched, on 22 October 2008, aboard a modified version of ISRO's
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle" title="Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle">
        Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle
       </a>
       . It discovered the presence of water on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Water_found_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Water_found-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_first_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-India_first-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Moon Impact Probe separated from the moon-orbiting Chandrayaan-1 on 14 November 2008, 20:06 and crashed, as planned, into the lunar south pole after a controlled descent. The MIP struck the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shackleton_Crater" title="Shackleton Crater">
        Shackleton Crater
       </a>
       at 20:31 on 14 November 2008 releasing underground debris that could be analysed by the orbiter for presence of water/ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandrayaan_over_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chandrayaan_over-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With this mission, India became the fourth entity to reach the lunar surface. Other entities to have done so are the former
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Space_Agency" title="European Space Agency">
        European Space Agency
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Japan" title="Japan">
        Japan
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/China" title="China">
        China
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOI_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TOI-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span id="Kalam.27s_vision">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Kalam's_vision">
        Kalam's vision
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Kalam's vision">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The probe was a product of former
       <a href="/wiki/President_of_India" title="President of India">
        President
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abdul_Kalam" title="Abdul Kalam">
        Abdul Kalam
       </a>
       's vision who felt that since the Chandrayaan orbiter was already going so near to the moon, the mission would have more scientific relevance if the probe was included. He believed that the moon could not be left to a few countries and that India should not be left behind
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Times of India also reported a political motive by former president Abdul Kalam as "He believed that if this was done, India "could always stake" a claim to a portion of the Moon."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TOI_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-TOI-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the same time, The Indian Express reported Kalam as saying "no nation can claim the moon as its own. The resources of the moon should be a common property and that is one of the aims of the moon mission."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C4WDefault-984988_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-C4WDefault-984988-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_objectives">
        Mission objectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mission objectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The main objectives of the MIP were to demonstrate the technologies for reaching a specified location on the Moon, qualifying technologies required for any future soft landing missions, and scientific exploration of the moon from close range just prior to the impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET1_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ET1-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The probe was designed to collide with the lunar surface and eject underground soil which could then be analysed by instruments on the orbiting Chandrayaan for the presence of water ice, and organic and other materials.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Configuration_and_payloads">
        Configuration and payloads
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Configuration and payloads">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The MIP carried three instruments:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radar_Altimeter" title="Radar Altimeter">
         Radar Altimeter
        </a>
        – which measured the altitude of the probe during descent and provided information on qualifying technologies for future landing missions. The operating frequency band was 4.3
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GHz" title="GHz">
         GHz
        </a>
        ± 100
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/MHz" title="MHz">
         MHz
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Video_cameras" title="Video cameras">
         Video Imaging System
        </a>
        – acquired close range images of the surface of the Moon during descent and before impact. The video imaging system consisted of an
        <a href="/wiki/Analog_signal" title="Analog signal">
         analog
        </a>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CCD_camera" title="CCD camera">
         CCD camera
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mass_Spectrometer_based_payload_CHACE&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mass Spectrometer based payload CHACE (page does not exist)">
         Mass Spectrometer based payload CHACE
        </a>
        – measured trace constituents of the lunar atmosphere during descent. This instrument was a
        <a href="/wiki/Quadrupole_mass_analyzer" title="Quadrupole mass analyzer">
         quadrupole mass spectrometer
        </a>
        with a mass resolution of 0.5
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atomic_mass_unit" title="Atomic mass unit">
         amu
        </a>
        and sensitivities to partial pressures on the order of 1.3×10
        <sup>
         −11
        </sup>
        <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
         pascals
        </a>
        .
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The probe used aluminium sandwich structure onto which the payloads were mounted. The probe attached to the mother spacecraft with a ball lock separation system which jettisoned the unit from the Chandrayaan. A solid propellent de-orbit mortar nudged the craft into lunar orbit, while spin thrusters stabilized the orientation enabling imaging system to capture the descent profile.
      </p>
      <p>
       The probe's external surface had four oval-shaped anodised aluminium plates measuring 120mm X 180 mm
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DH_flag_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-DH_flag-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on which the image of the Indian flag was depicted, complete with the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Emblem_of_India" title="Emblem of India">
        Emblem of India
       </a>
       and the words "
       <a href="/wiki/Satyameva_Jayate" title="Satyameva Jayate">
        Satyameva Jayate
       </a>
       ".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DH_flag_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-DH_flag-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These plates were attached to each one of the four vertical sides of the probe. As per ISRO specifications these plates had to endure a temperature range from −50 degrees
       <a href="/wiki/Celsius" title="Celsius">
        celsius
       </a>
       and as high as more than 150 degrees celsius.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DH_flag_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-DH_flag-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_chronology">
        Mission chronology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission chronology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       India launched the
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       spacecraft using a modified version of the
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_Satellite_Launch_Vehicle" title="Polar Satellite Launch Vehicle">
        PSLV C11
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ISRO-Mission_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ISRO-Mission-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hindu_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hindu-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       on 22 October 2008 from
       <a href="/wiki/Satish_Dhawan_Space_Centre" title="Satish Dhawan Space Centre">
        Satish Dhawan Space Centre
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Sriharikota" title="Sriharikota">
        Sriharikota
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Andhra_Pradesh" title="Andhra Pradesh">
        Andhra Pradesh
       </a>
       at 06:22
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Standard_Time" title="Indian Standard Time">
        IST
       </a>
       (00:52
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar probe separated from Chandrayaaan-1, in a 102 km circular polar orbit around the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , at 20:06
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_Standard_Time" title="Indian Standard Time">
        IST
       </a>
       on 14 November 2008.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chandrayaan_over_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chandrayaan_over-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After separation, it first fired its spin up rockets and then its retro rocket to lower itself to an orbit intersecting the lunar surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Express1_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Express1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While descending, the MIP continuously sent information back to the Chandrayaan-1 orbiter which in turn beamed the information back to earth. After a 25-minute controlled descent, it struck the Moon at 20:31 IST, 14 November 2008 at a speed of 1.69 kilometers per second (approximately 6100 km/h or 3800 miles per hour).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Express1_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Express1-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The crash destroyed the probe. It crashed into
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shackleton_Crater" title="Shackleton Crater">
        Shackleton Crater
       </a>
       , at the lunar south pole,
       <span class="plainlinks nourlexpansion">
        <a class="external text" href="//tools.wmflabs.org/geohack/geohack.php?pagename=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;params=89_S_30_W_globe:Moon">
         <span class="geo-default">
          <span class="geo-dms" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           <span class="latitude">
            89°S
           </span>
           <span class="longitude">
            30°W
           </span>
          </span>
         </span>
         <span class="geo-multi-punct">
          ﻿ / ﻿
         </span>
         <span class="geo-nondefault">
          <span class="geo-dec" title="Maps, aerial photos, and other data for this location">
           89°S 30°W
          </span>
          <span style="display:none">
           ﻿ /
           <span class="geo">
            -89; -30

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_of_water">
        Discovery of water
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moon_Impact_Probe&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Discovery of water">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On 25 September 2009, ISRO announced that the MIP had discovered water on the moon just before impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Water_found_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Water_found-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This announcement was made after the discovery of water was announced on September 24, 2009 by
       <a href="/wiki/Science_(journal)" title="Science (journal)">
        Science
       </a>
       magazine by the NASA payload
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_Mineralogy_Mapper" title="Moon Mineralogy Mapper">
        Moon Mineralogy Mapper
       </a>
       carried on board Chandrayaan-1.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       MIP discovered water on moon before NASA's Moon Mineralogy Mapper. The announcement of this discovery was not made until NASA confirmed it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-India_first_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-India_first-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This gave the answer to the millennia-old question whether there is water on or in Earth’s moon when it led to the discovery of water in its vapour phase by the CHACE (CHandra’s Altitudinal Composition Explorer) payload on board the Moon Impact Probe (MIP) and complementarily in its solid phase by the Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3) payload on board the main orbiter in the Chandrayaan I mission. This ‘discovery-class-of-finding’ by CHACE was achieved by direct in situ measurement of the lunar atmosphere during the descend journey of the MIP to the Lunar South Pole, while M3 discovered water in ice form by remote sensing techniques. As water cannot retain its liquid phase in the lunar environment because of its own vapour pressure and the ultra-high vacuum prevailing there, it can be found in solid (ice) and gaseous (vapour) phases. While the Moon Mineralogy Mapper (M3), a payload by NASA, on board Chandrayaan I lunar orbiter has detected, by mapping almost 97% of the lunar surface using remote sensing techniques, the presence of water in ice form in higher latitudes especially in the polar caps, the CHACE payload in the lunar impactor (MIP) has directly detected water in its gaseous form along 14 degree E meridian from 45 degree N to 90 degree S latitude, with a latitudinal resolution of around 0.10 and altitudinal resolution of ~ 250 m from 98 km altitude till impact .
      </p>
