      <p>
       <b>
        Polydeuces
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        Saturn XXXIV
       </b>
       (34), is a small
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       that is co-orbital with the moon
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       and librates around its trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian point
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       ). Its diameter is estimated to be 2–3 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2010_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thomas2010-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is pronounced
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/dj/: 'd' in 'dew'">
            dj
           </span>
           <span title="/uː/: 'oo' in 'goose'">
            uː
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'z' in 'zoom'">
            z
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          POL
         </span>
         -i-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          DEW
         </span>
         -seez
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el">
        Πολυδεύκης
       </span>
       . Dione's other co-orbital moon is
       <a href="/wiki/Helene_(moon)" title="Helene (moon)">
        Helene
       </a>
       , which is bigger and located at the leading L4 point.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Polydeuces was discovered by the
       <a class="external text" href="http://ciclops.org/team/iss_team.php" rel="nofollow">
        Cassini Imaging Team
       </a>
       on October 24, 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       in images taken on October 21, 2004,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPorco_Baker_et_al._2005-7">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8432_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8432-8">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/2004 S 5
       </b>
       . Subsequent searches of earlier Cassini imaging showed it in images as far back as April 9, 2004.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Of the four known
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_moon" title="Trojan moon">
        Lagrangian co-orbitals in the Saturn system
       </a>
       ('trojan moon'), Polydeuces
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        wanders
       </a>
       the farthest from its Lagrangian point: its distance behind Dione varies from 33.9° to 91.4° with a period of 790.931 days (for comparison,
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         5
        </sub>
       </a>
       trails Dione by 60°).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpitale_Jacobson_et_al.2006-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Polydeuces's libration is large enough that it takes on some qualities of a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tadpole_orbit" title="Tadpole orbit">
        tadpole orbit
       </a>
       , as evidenced by the clear asymmetry between excursions towards and away from Dione. In the course of one such cycle, Polydeuces's orbital radius also varies by about ± 7660 km with respect to Dione's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurray_Cooper_et_al._2005-6">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The name Polydeuces was approved by the IAU Working Group on Planetary System Nomenclature on January 21, 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8471-9">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castor_and_Polydeuces" title="Castor and Polydeuces">
        Polydeuces
       </a>
       is another name for Pollux, twin brother of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Castor_and_Polydeuces" title="Castor and Polydeuces">
        Castor
       </a>
       , son of
       <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">
        Zeus
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Leda_(mythology)" title="Leda (mythology)">
        Leda
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to gravitational interactions with Dione, its surface may be in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       but not its interior, similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Methone_(moon)" title="Methone (moon)">
        Methone
       </a>
       being in hydrostatic equilibrium due to interactions with
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       .
      </p>
