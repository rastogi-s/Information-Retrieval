      <p>
       <b>
        Epimetheus
       </b>
       is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It is also known as
       <b>
        Saturn XI
       </b>
       . It is named after the mythological
       <a href="/wiki/Epimetheus_(mythology)" title="Epimetheus (mythology)">
        Epimetheus
       </a>
       , brother of
       <a href="/wiki/Prometheus" title="Prometheus">
        Prometheus
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Epimetheus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="465" height="228" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Epimetheus.jpg/220px-Epimetheus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Epimetheus.jpg/330px-Epimetheus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Epimetheus.jpg/440px-Epimetheus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Epimetheus
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Epimetheus occupies essentially the same orbit as the moon
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       . Astronomers assumed that there was only one body in that orbit (disbelieving that two moons could share nearly identical orbits without colliding
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), and accordingly had difficulty determining their orbital characteristics. Observations were photographic and spaced widely apart in time, so that while the presence of two objects was not obvious, the observations were difficult to reconcile with a reasonable orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPhys.org_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPhys.org-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Audouin_Dollfus" title="Audouin Dollfus">
        Audouin Dollfus
       </a>
       observed a moon on 15 December 1966,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1987_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1987-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which he proposed to be named "Janus".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1995_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1995-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On 18 December,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Walker_(astronomer)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Richard Walker (astronomer) (page does not exist)">
        Richard Walker
       </a>
       made a similar observation which is now credited as the discovery of Epimetheus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1991_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_1991-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, at the time, it was believed that there was only one moon, unofficially known as "Janus", in the given orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Twelve years later, in October 1978,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stephen_M._Larson" title="Stephen M. Larson">
        Stephen M. Larson
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=John_W._Fountain&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="John W. Fountain (page does not exist)">
        John W. Fountain
       </a>
       realised that the 1966 observations were best explained by two distinct objects (Janus and Epimetheus) sharing very similar orbits.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFountainLarson1978_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFountainLarson1978-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This was confirmed in 1980 by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
         Voyager 1
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELeverington2003454_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELeverington2003454-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and so Larson and Fountain officially share the discovery of Epimetheus with Walker.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A moon that was probably Epimetheus appeared in two
       <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">
        Pioneer 11
       </a>
       images and was designated
       <b>
        1979S1
       </b>
       , there is uncertainly though because the two images were not enough to allow a reliable orbit to be calculated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ulivi150_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ulivi150-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Epimetheus received its name in 1983.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythology_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mythology-12">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name Janus was approved by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       at the same time, although the name had been used informally since Dollfus proposed it shortly after the 1966 discovery.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Epimetheus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA08170_Epimetheus_and_Janus.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="220" data-file-width="300" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/PIA08170_Epimetheus_and_Janus.jpg/220px-PIA08170_Epimetheus_and_Janus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/27/PIA08170_Epimetheus_and_Janus.jpg 1.5x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA08170_Epimetheus_and_Janus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Epimetheus (lower left) and
         <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
          Janus
         </a>
         (right) seen on 20 March 2006, two months after swapping orbits. The two moons appear close only because of
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreshortening" title="Foreshortening">
          foreshortening
         </a>
         ; in reality, Janus is about 40,000 km farther from
         <i>
          Cassini
         </i>
         than Epimetheus.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="697" data-file-width="1029" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/250px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/375px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png/500px-Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Epimetheus-Janus_Orbit.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Rotating_reference_frame" title="Rotating reference frame">
          Rotating-frame
         </a>
         depiction of the
         <a href="/wiki/Horseshoe_orbit" title="Horseshoe orbit">
          horseshoe orbits
         </a>
         of Janus and Epimetheus
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Epimetheus's orbit is
       <a href="/wiki/Co-orbital_configuration" title="Co-orbital configuration">
        co-orbital
       </a>
       with that of
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       . Janus's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semimajor_axis" title="Semimajor axis">
        mean orbital radius
       </a>
       from Saturn is, as of 2006 (as shown by green color in the adjacent picture), only 50 km less than that of Epimetheus, a distance smaller than either moon's mean radius. In accordance with
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">
        Kepler's laws of planetary motion
       </a>
       , the closer
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       is completed more quickly. Because of the small difference it is completed in only about 30 seconds less. Each day, the inner moon is an additional 0.25° farther around Saturn than the outer moon. As the inner moon catches up to the outer moon, their mutual gravitational attraction increases the inner moon's momentum and decreases that of the outer moon. This added momentum means that the inner moon's distance from Saturn and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       are increased, and the outer moon's are decreased. The timing and magnitude of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">
        momentum exchange
       </a>
       is such that the moons effectively swap orbits, never approaching closer than about 10,000 km. At each encounter Janus's orbital radius changes by ~20 km and Epimetheus's by ~80 km: Janus's orbit is less affected because it is four times more massive than Epimetheus. The exchange takes place close to every four years; the last close approaches occurred in January 2006,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_The_Dancing_Moons_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_The_Dancing_Moons-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       2010 and 2014, and the next in 2018. This is the only such orbital configuration known in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEEl_Moutamid_et_al2015_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEl_Moutamid_et_al2015-14">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The orbital relationship between Janus and Epimetheus can be understood in terms of the
       <a href="/wiki/Three-body_problem#Circular" title="Three-body problem">
        circular restricted three-body problem
       </a>
       , as a case in which the two moons (the third body being Saturn) are similar in size to each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELlibre_and_Ollé_2011_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELlibre_and_Ollé_2011-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Epimetheus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are several Epimethean craters larger than 30 km in diameter, as well as both large and small ridges and grooves. The extensive cratering indicates that Epimetheus must be quite old. Janus and Epimetheus may have formed from a disruption of a single parent to form co-orbital satellites, but if this is the case the disruption must have happened early in the history of the satellite system. From its very low density and relatively high
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       , it seems likely that Epimetheus is a very porous icy body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is a lot of uncertainty in these values, however, and so this remains to be confirmed.
      </p>
      <p>
       The south pole shows what might be the remains of a large impact crater covering most of this face of the moon, and which could be responsible for the somewhat flattened shape of the southern part of Epimetheus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There appear to be two terrain types: darker, smoother areas, and brighter, slightly more yellowish, fractured terrain. One interpretation is that the darker material evidently moves down slopes, and probably has a lower ice content than the brighter material, which appears more like "bedrock". Nonetheless, materials in both terrains are likely to be rich in water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_Revealed_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Epimetheus_Revealed-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ring">
        Ring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Epimetheus_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Ring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Janus/Epimetheus_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        faint dust ring
       </a>
       is present around the region occupied by the orbits of Epimetheus and Janus, as revealed by images taken in forward-scattered light by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        Cassini spacecraft
       </a>
       in 2006. The ring has a radial extent of about 5000 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Moon-Made_Rings_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Moon-Made_Rings-17">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its source are particles blasted off their surfaces by meteoroid impacts, which then form a diffuse ring around their orbital paths.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWilliams_et_al._2011_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilliams_et_al._2011-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Creating_New_Rings_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJPL/NASA:_Creating_New_Rings-19">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
