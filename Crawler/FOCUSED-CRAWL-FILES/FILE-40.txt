      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar meteorite
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Meteorite" title="Meteorite">
        meteorite
       </a>
       that is known to have originated on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . A meteorite hitting the Moon is normally classified as a
       <a href="/wiki/Transient_lunar_phenomenon" title="Transient lunar phenomenon">
        transient lunar phenomenon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In January 1982,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Schutt" title="John Schutt">
        John Schutt
       </a>
       , leading an expedition in
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       for the
       <a href="/wiki/ANSMET" title="ANSMET">
        ANSMET
       </a>
       program, found a meteorite that he recognized to be unusual. Shortly thereafter, the meteorite now called
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allan_Hills_81005" title="Allan Hills 81005">
        Allan Hills 81005
       </a>
       was sent to Washington, DC, where
       <a href="/wiki/Smithsonian_Institution" title="Smithsonian Institution">
        Smithsonian Institution
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geochemist" title="Geochemist">
        geochemist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Brian_Harold_Mason" title="Brian Harold Mason">
        Brian Mason
       </a>
       recognized that the sample was unlike any other known meteorite and resembled some rocks brought back from the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Several years later, Japanese scientists
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (November 2010)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       recognized that they had also collected a lunar meteorite,
       <a href="/wiki/Yamato_791197" title="Yamato 791197">
        Yamato 791197
       </a>
       , during the 1979 field season in
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       . As of August 2017
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , about 306 lunar meteorites have been discovered,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MeteorBulletin_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MeteorBulletin-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       perhaps representing more than 30 separate meteorite falls (i.e., many of the stones are "paired" fragments of the same
       <a href="/wiki/Meteoroid" title="Meteoroid">
        meteoroid
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The total mass is more than 190 kilograms (420 lb).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       All lunar meteorites have been found in deserts; most have been found in
       <a href="/wiki/Antarctica" title="Antarctica">
        Antarctica
       </a>
       , northern
       <a href="/wiki/Africa" title="Africa">
        Africa
       </a>
       , and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sultanate_of_Oman" title="Sultanate of Oman">
        Sultanate of Oman
       </a>
       . None have yet been found in North America, South America, or Europe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar origin is established by comparing the mineralogy, the chemical composition, and the isotopic composition between meteorites and samples from the Moon collected by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       missions.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transfer_to_Earth">
        Transfer to Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Transfer to Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most lunar meteorites are launched from the Moon by impacts making lunar craters of a few kilometers in diameter or less.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No source crater of lunar meteorites has been positively identified, although there is speculation that the highly anomalous lunar meteorite
       <a href="/wiki/Sayh_al_Uhaymir_169" title="Sayh al Uhaymir 169">
        Sayh al Uhaymir 169
       </a>
       derives from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lalande_(crater)" title="Lalande (crater)">
        Lalande
       </a>
       impact crater on the lunar nearside.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cosmic-ray exposure history established with noble-gas measurements have shown that all lunar meteorites were ejected from the Moon in the past 20 million years. Most left the Moon in the past 100,000 years. After leaving the Moon, most lunar meteoroids go into orbit around Earth and eventually succumb to Earth's gravity. Some meteoroids ejected from the Moon get launched into orbits around the Sun. These meteoroids remain in space longer, but eventually intersect the Earth's orbit and land.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_relevance">
        Scientific relevance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific relevance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       All six of the Apollo missions on which samples were collected landed in the central nearside of the Moon, an area that has subsequently been shown to be geochemically anomalous by the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       mission. In contrast, the numerous lunar meteorites are random samples of the Moon and consequently provide a more representative sampling of the lunar surface than the Apollo samples. Half the lunar meteorites, for example, likely sample material from the farside of the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       At the time the first meteorite from the Moon was discovered in 1982, there was speculation that some other unusual meteorites that had been found previously
       <a href="/wiki/Martian_meteorite" title="Martian meteorite">
        originated from Mars
       </a>
       . The positive identification of lunar meteorites on Earth supported the hypothesis that meteoroid impacts on Mars could eject rocks from that planet. There are also speculations about the possibility of finding "Earth meteorites" on the surface of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would be very interesting because in this case stones from Earth older than 3.9 billion years, which are destroyed on Earth by various geological processes, may have survived on the Moon. Thus some scientists propose new missions to the Moon to search for ancient rocks from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_history">
        Observation history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Observation history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Today, about one in every thousand newly discovered meteorites is a lunar meteorite, whereas the vast majority of meteorites are from the asteroid belt. In the early 19th century most scientists believed that all meteorites falling towards the Earth were from the Moon. Although today supported only by a minority of researchers, there are also theories that
       <a href="/wiki/Tektite" title="Tektite">
        tektites
       </a>
       are from the Moon and should therefore also be regarded as lunar meteorites. However, most scientists regard such theories as outdated.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (July 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Private_ownership">
        Private ownership
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_meteorite&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Private ownership">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2114" data-file-width="2966" height="157" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg/220px-Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg/330px-Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg/440px-Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_meteorite_NWA_4483_piece.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A privately owned 11 milligrams (0.17 gr) (2.2 by 2.0 millimetres (0.087 in × 0.079 in)) piece of the lunar meteorite
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_West_Africa" title="North West Africa">
          NWA
         </a>
         4483
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lunar meteorites collected in Africa and Oman are, for all practical purposes, the only source of moon rocks available for private ownership. This is because all rocks collected during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       moon-landing program are property of the United States government or of other nations to which the U.S. conveyed them as gifts. Similarly, all lunar meteorites collected by the U.S. and Japanese Antarctic programs are, by treaty, held by those governments for research and education purposes only. Although there is no U.S. law specifically against the ownership of Apollo moonrocks, none has ever been (or is likely to ever be) given or sold by the U.S. government to private citizens. Even in the cases of plaques containing genuine Apollo moonrocks given in 2004 to astronauts and
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">
        Walter Cronkite
       </a>
       , NASA retained ownership of the rocks themselves.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Most of the moonrocks collected by the
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_16" title="Luna 16">
        Luna 16
       </a>
       probe are also unavailable for private ownership, although three tiny samples were sold at auction for $442,500 in 1993.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
