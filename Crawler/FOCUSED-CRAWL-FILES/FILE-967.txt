        Neptune

        Despoina

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Poseidon" title="Poseidon">

       </a>

       <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">
        Demeter
       </a>

      <p>
       Despina was discovered in late July 1989 from the images taken by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        probe
       </a>
       . It was given the temporary designation
       <b>
        S/1989 N 3
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_4824_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_4824-8">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery was announced (IAUC 4824) on August 2, 1989, but the text only talks of "10 frames taken over 5 days", giving a discovery date of sometime before July 28. The name was given on 16 September 1991.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC_5347_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC_5347-9">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Despina is irregularly shaped and shows no sign of any geological modification. It is likely that it is a rubble pile re-accreted from fragments of Neptune's original satellites, which were smashed up by perturbations from
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       soon after that moon's capture into a very eccentric initial orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BanfieldMurray1992_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BanfieldMurray1992-10">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Despina's orbit lies close to but outside of the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Thalassa_(moon)" title="Thalassa (moon)">
        Thalassa
       </a>
       and just inside the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Neptune" title="Rings of Neptune">
        Le Verrier ring
       </a>
       . As it is also below Neptune's
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       radius, it is slowly spiralling inward due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_deceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal deceleration
       </a>
       and may eventually impact Neptune's atmosphere, or break up into a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        planetary ring
       </a>
       upon passing its
       <a href="/wiki/Roche_limit" title="Roche limit">
        Roche limit
       </a>
       due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_forces" title="Tidal forces">
        tidal stretching
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The diameter of Despina is approximately 150 km (90 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
