   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Moon landings in fiction
   </h1>

      <p>
       Because of its extreme difficulty and otherworldly nature, a successful Moon landing is viewed as being among humanity's greatest achievements.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A Moon landing combines three essential elements:
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        science
       </a>
       , a description of "what" to do that conforms to the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Physical_laws" title="Physical laws">
        physical laws
       </a>
       of the universe;
       <a href="/wiki/Technology" title="Technology">
        technology
       </a>
       , a means of "how" to do it using
       <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">
        engineering
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Machines" title="Machines">
        machines
       </a>
       ; and finally
       <a href="/wiki/Imagination" title="Imagination">
        imagination
       </a>
       , the human compulsion of "why" to do it.
      </p>
      <p>
       Particularly in the United States, works of fiction helped to create the will to go the moon by creating a narrative that allowed people to feel good about themselves and their country in the face of the turbulent events of the 1950s and 1960s, when the
       <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
        Cold War
       </a>
       was at its height.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There was Soviet interest in science fiction literature and cinema, too, during the first half of the 20th Century—but it tended to focus on Mars landings rather than Moon landings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Key examples include the novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Red_Star_(novel)" title="Red Star (novel)">
         Red Star
        </a>
       </i>
       (1908) by
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Bogdanov" title="Alexander Bogdanov">
        Alexander Bogdanov
       </a>
       and the film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Aelita" title="Aelita">
         Aelita
        </a>
       </i>
       (1924) by
       <a href="/wiki/Yakov_Protazanov" title="Yakov Protazanov">
        Yakov Protazanov
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Well before the technology and knowledge of physical laws became sufficiently advanced to allow manned space-flight, going to the Moon was the dream of many people. With the passage of time, the idea of a Moon landing became a common
       <a href="/wiki/Trope_(literature)" title="Trope (literature)">
        trope
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Literature" title="Literature">
        literature
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Film" title="Film">
        cinema
       </a>
       . Below is a partial list of notable "Moon landings" depicted in works of art.
      </p>
