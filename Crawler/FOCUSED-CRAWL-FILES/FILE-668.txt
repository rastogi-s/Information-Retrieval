      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       , the
       <b>
        new moon
       </b>
       is the first
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_phase" title="Lunar phase">
        lunar phase
       </a>
       , when the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       have the same
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptic longitude
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At this phase, the lunar disk is not visible to the
       <a href="/wiki/Naked_eye" title="Naked eye">
        unaided eye
       </a>
       , except when
       <a href="/wiki/Silhouette" title="Silhouette">
        silhouetted
       </a>
       during a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Daylight" title="Daylight">
        Daylight
       </a>
       outshines the
       <a href="/wiki/Earthlight_(astronomy)" title="Earthlight (astronomy)">
        earthlight
       </a>
       that dimly illuminates the dark side of the new Moon. The actual phase is usually a very thin crescent because the Moon rarely passes directly in front of the Sun, except in a solar eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The original meaning of the term
       <i>
        new moon
       </i>
       , sometimes still used in non-astronomical contexts, was the first visible crescent of the Moon, after
       <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy)" title="Conjunction (astronomy)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       with the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This crescent Moon is briefly visible when low above the western
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       shortly after
       <a href="/wiki/Sunset" title="Sunset">
        sunset
       </a>
       and before moonset.
      </p>
      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunation
       </b>
       or
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synodic_month" title="Synodic month">
        synodic month
       </a>
       is the average time from one new moon to the next. In the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J2000.0" title="J2000.0">
        J2000.0
       </a>
       epoch, the average length of a lunation is 29.530588
       <a href="/wiki/Day" title="Day">
        days
       </a>
       (or 29 days, 12 hours, 44 minutes, and 2.8 seconds). However, the length of any one synodic month can vary from 29.26 to 29.80 days due to the
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Perturbation" title="Perturbation">
        perturbing
       </a>
       effects of the Sun's gravity on the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric orbit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_calendar" title="Lunar calendar">
        lunar calendar
       </a>
       , each
       <a href="/wiki/Month" title="Month">
        month
       </a>
       corresponds to a lunation. Each lunar cycle can be assigned a unique lunation number to identify it.
      </p>

      <p>
       The length of a lunation is about 29.53 days. Its precise duration is linked to many phenomena in nature, such as the variation between
       <a href="/wiki/Tide#Range_variation:_springs_and_neaps" title="Tide">
        spring and neap tides
       </a>
       (the extreme highest and lowest tides, respectively). An approximate formula to compute the mean moments of new moon (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       between Sun and Moon) for successive months is:

      <p>
       Periodic perturbations change the time of true conjunction from these mean values. For all new moons between 1601 and 2401, the maximum difference is 0.592 days = 14h13m in either direction. The duration of a lunation (
       <i>
        i.e.
       </i>
       the time from new moon to the next new moon) varies in this period between 29.272 and 29.833 days, i.e. −0.259d = 6h12m shorter, or +0.302d = 7h15m longer than average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This range is smaller than the difference between mean and true conjunction, because during one lunation the periodic terms cannot all change to their maximum opposite value.
      </p>
      <p>
       See the article on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Full_moon_cycle" title="Full moon cycle">
        full moon cycle
       </a>
       for a fairly simple method to compute the moment of new moon more accurately.
      </p>
      <p>
       The long-term error of the formula is approximately: 1 cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       seconds in TT, and 11 cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       seconds in UT (
       <i>
        cy
       </i>
       is centuries since 2000; see section
       <i>
        Explanation of the formulae
       </i>
       for details.)
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Explanation_of_the_formula">
        Explanation of the formula
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Explanation of the formula">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The moment of mean conjunction can easily be computed from an expression for the mean ecliptical longitude of the Moon minus the mean ecliptical longitude of the Sun (Delauney parameter
       <i>
        D
       </i>
       ).
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Meeus" title="Jean Meeus">
        Jean Meeus
       </a>
       gave formulae to compute this in his
       <i>
        Astronomical Formulae for Calculators
       </i>
       based on the ephemerides of Brown and Newcomb (ca. 1900); and in his 1st edition of
       <i>
        Astronomical Algorithms
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       based on the ELP2000-85
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (the 2nd edition uses ELP2000-82 with improved expressions from Chapront
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       in 1998). These are now outdated: Chapront
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       (2002)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       published improved parameters. Also Meeus's formula uses a fractional variable to allow computation of the four main phases, and uses a second variable for the secular terms. For the convenience of the reader, the formula given above is based on Chapront's latest parameters and expressed with a single integer variable, and the following additional terms have been added:
      </p>
      <p>
       constant term:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Like Meeus, apply the constant terms of the
        <a href="/wiki/Aberration_of_light" title="Aberration of light">
         aberration of light
        </a>
        for the Sun's motion and
        <a href="/wiki/Light-time_correction" title="Light-time correction">
         light-time correction
        </a>
        for the Moon
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
         <a href="#cite_note-10">
          [note 2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        to obtain the apparent difference in ecliptical longitudes:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        Sun: +20.496"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [9]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Moon: −0.704"
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [10]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
       <dd>
        Correction in conjunction: −0.000451 days
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [note 3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For UT: at 1 January 2000,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Delta_T" title="Delta T">
         ΔT
        </a>
        (=
        <a href="/wiki/Terrestrial_Time" title="Terrestrial Time">
         TT
        </a>
        −
        <a href="/wiki/Universal_Time" title="Universal Time">
         UT
        </a>
        ) was +63.83 s;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [note 4]
         </a>
        </sup>
        hence the correction for the clock time UT = TT − ΔT of the conjunction is:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        −0.000739 days.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       quadratic term:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In ELP2000–85 (see Chapront
        <i>
         et alii
        </i>
        1988),
        <i>
         D
        </i>
        has a quadratic term of −5.8681"T
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ; expressed in lunations N, this yields a correction of +87.403
        <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         –12
        </sup>
        N
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [note 5]
         </a>
        </sup>
        days to the time of conjunction. The term includes a
        <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration" title="Tidal acceleration">
         tidal contribution
        </a>
        of 0.5×(−23.8946 "/cy
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        ). The most current estimate from Lunar Laser Ranging for the acceleration is (see Chapront
        <i>
         et alii
        </i>
        2002): (−25.858 ±0.003)"/cy
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        . Therefore, the new quadratic term of
        <i>
         D
        </i>
        is = -6.8498"T
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">

         </a>

        <i>

        <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −12
        </sup>
        N
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        days to the time of conjunction; the quadratic term now is:
       </li>
      </ul>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        +102.026
        <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −12
        </sup>
        N
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        days.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <ul>
       <li>
        For UT: analysis of historical observations shows that ΔT has a long-term increase of +31 s/cy
        <sup>
         2

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">

       </li>

      <dl>
       <dd>
        −235
        <span style="margin:0 .15em 0 .25em">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −12
        </sup>
        N
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
        days.
       </dd>

      <p>
       The theoretical tidal contribution to ΔT is about +42 s/cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the smaller observed value is thought to be mostly due to changes in the shape of the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the discrepancy is not fully explained, uncertainty of our prediction of UT (rotation angle of the Earth) may be as large as the difference between these values: 11 s/cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       . The error in the position of the Moon itself is only maybe 0.5"/cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [note 8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       or (because the apparent mean angular velocity of the Moon is about 0.5"/s), 1 s/cy
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       in the time of conjunction with the Sun.

      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Lunation Number
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        Lunation Cycle
       </i>
       is a number given to each
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/lunation" title="wiktionary:lunation">
        lunation
       </a>
       beginning from a certain one in history. Several conventions are in use.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The most commonly used is the
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_William_Brown" title="Ernest William Brown">
        Brown
       </a>
       Lunation Number (BLN), which defines lunation 1 as beginning at the first new moon of 1923, the year when
       <a href="/wiki/Ernest_William_Brown" title="Ernest William Brown">
        Ernest William Brown's
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory" title="Lunar theory">
        lunar theory
       </a>
       was introduced in the major national astronomical almanacs. Lunation 1 occurred at approximately 02:41
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       , January 17, 1923. New moons occur on

       </span>
       , with the given uncertainty due to varying torques from the sun.
      </p>
      <p>
       Another increasingly popular lunation number (simply called the Lunation Number), introduced by
       <a href="/wiki/Jean_Meeus" title="Jean Meeus">
        Jean Meeus
       </a>
       , defines lunation 0 as beginning on the first new moon of 2000 (this occurred at approximately 18:14
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       , January 6, 2000). The formula relating this Lunation Number with the Brown Lunation Number is: BLN = LN + 953.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Goldstine Lunation Number refers to the lunation numbering used by
       <a href="/wiki/Herman_Goldstine" title="Herman Goldstine">
        Herman Goldstine
       </a>
       in his 1973 book
       <i>
        New and Full Moons: 1001 B.C. to A.D. 1651
       </i>
       , with lunation 0 beginning on January 11, 1001 BC, and can be calculated using GLN = LN + 37105.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Hebrew Lunation Number is the count of lunations in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hebrew_Calendar" title="Hebrew Calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       with lunation 1 beginning on October 7, 3761 BC. It can be calculated using HLN = LN + 71234. The Islamic Lunation Number is the count of lunations in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Islamic_Calendar" title="Islamic Calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       with lunation 1 as beginning on July 16, 622. It can be calculated using ILN = LN + 17038. The Thai Lunation Number is called "มาสเกณฑ์" (Maasa-Kendha), defines lunation 0 as beginning of the SouthEast-Asian Calendar on Sunday March 22, 638 (Julian Calendar). It can be calculated using TLN = LN + 16843.
      </p>

      <p>
       In non-astronomical contexts,
       <i>
        new moon
       </i>
       refers to the first visible
       <a href="/wiki/Crescent" title="Crescent">
        crescent
       </a>
       of the Moon, after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conjunction_(astronomy_and_astrology)" title="Conjunction (astronomy and astrology)">
        conjunction
       </a>
       with the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This takes place over the western
       <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
        horizon
       </a>
       in a brief period between sunset and moonset, and therefore the precise time and even the date of the appearance of the new moon by this definition will be influenced by the geographical location of the observer. The astronomical new moon, sometimes known as the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dark_moon" title="Dark moon">
         dark moon
        </a>
       </i>
       to avoid confusion, occurs by definition at the moment of conjunction in
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinate_system" title="Ecliptic coordinate system">
        ecliptical longitude
       </a>
       with the Sun, when the Moon is invisible from the Earth. This moment is unique and does not depend on location, and in certain circumstances it coincides with a
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipse" title="Solar eclipse">
        solar eclipse
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the above meaning
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       , the first crescent marks the beginning of the month in the
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       , and in some
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar calendars
       </a>
       such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       . In the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese calendar
       </a>
       , the beginning of the month is marked by the dark moon. The new moon is also important in
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrology
       </a>
       , as is the
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Hindu_calendar">
        Hindu calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Hindu calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The new moon is significant in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hindu" title="Hindu">
        Hindu
       </a>
       calendar. People
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch">
          <span title="The material near this tag possibly uses too-vague attribution or weasel words. (May 2015)">
           who?
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       generally wait for the new moon to begin projects, as the waxing period of the moon is considered to be favorable for new work.
      </p>
      <p>
       There are fifteen moon dates for each of the waxing and waning periods. These fifteen dates divided evenly into five categories:
       <i>
        Nanda
       </i>
       ,
       <i>
        Bhadra',
       </i>
       Jaya
       <i>
        ,
       </i>
       Rikta
       <i>
        , and
       </i>
       Purna
       <i>
        , which are cycled through in that order.
       </i>
      </p>
      <p>
       Nanda dates are considered to be favorable for auspicious works; Bhadra dates for works related with community, social, family, friends; and Jaya dates for dealing with conflict. Rikta dates are considered beneficial only for works related to cruelty. Purna dates are considered to be favorable for all work.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first day of the Lunar Hindu calendar starts the day after the new moon (
       <a href="/wiki/Amavasya" title="Amavasya">
        Amavasya
       </a>
       ), which is considered a powerful force for good or evil. The Hindu epic
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mahabharatha" title="Mahabharatha">
        Mahabharatha
       </a>
       states that the
       <a href="/wiki/Kurukshetra_War" title="Kurukshetra War">
        Kurukshetra War
       </a>
       started this day, which was also a Tuesday (Mangalvaar, day of the week named after Mars).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Islamic_calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Islamic calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       has retained an observational definition of the new moon, marking the new month when the first crescent moon is actually seen, and making it impossible to be certain in advance of when a specific month will begin (in particular, the exact date on which
       <a href="/wiki/Ramadan_(calendar_month)" title="Ramadan (calendar month)">
        Ramadan
       </a>
       will begin is not known in advance). In
       <a href="/wiki/Saudi_Arabia" title="Saudi Arabia">
        Saudi Arabia
       </a>
       , the new King Abdullah Centre for Crescent Observations and Astronomy in Makkah has a clock for addressing this as an international scientific project.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Pakistan" title="Pakistan">
        Pakistan
       </a>
       , there is a "Central Ruet-e-Hilal Committee" whose head is Mufti Muneeb-ur-Rehman, assisted by 150 observatories of the
       <a href="/wiki/Pakistan_Meteorological_Department" title="Pakistan Meteorological Department">
        Pakistan Meteorological Department
       </a>
       , which announces the sighting of the new moon. Since its creation in 1974, the status of the Central Ruet-e-Hilal Committee has been controversial as it refused the "Witnesses" (Shahadats) from other sects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Iran" title="Iran">
        Iran
       </a>
       a special committee receives observations of every new moon to determine the beginning of each month. This committee uses one hundred groups of observers.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An attempt to unify Muslims on a scientifically calculated worldwide calendar was adopted by both the
       <a href="/wiki/Fiqh_Council_of_North_America" title="Fiqh Council of North America">
        Fiqh Council of North America
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Council_for_Fatwa_and_Research" title="European Council for Fatwa and Research">
        European Council for Fatwa and Research
       </a>
       in 2007. The new calculation requires that conjunction occur before sunset in Mecca, Saudi Arabia, and that moonset on the following day must take place after sunset. These can be precisely calculated and therefore a unified calendar is imminent if it becomes adopted worldwide.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Chinese_calendar">
        Chinese calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Chinese calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The new moon is the beginning of the month in the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese calendar
       </a>
       . Some Buddhist Chinese keep a vegetarian diet on the new moon and full moon each month.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Jewish_calendar">
        Jewish calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Jewish calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Chodesh" title="Rosh Chodesh">
        Rosh Chodesh
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The new moon signifies the start of every Jewish month, and is considered an important date and
       <a href="/wiki/Rosh_Chodesh" title="Rosh Chodesh">
        minor holiday
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_calendar" title="Hebrew calendar">
        Hebrew calendar
       </a>
       . The modern form of the calendar is a rule-based
       <a href="/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar" title="Lunisolar calendar">
        lunisolar calendar
       </a>
       , akin to the
       <a href="/wiki/Chinese_calendar" title="Chinese calendar">
        Chinese calendar
       </a>
       , measuring months defined in lunar cycles as well as years measured in solar cycles, and distinct from the purely lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Islamic_calendar" title="Islamic calendar">
        Islamic calendar
       </a>
       and the predominently solar
       <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">
        Gregorian calendar
       </a>
       . The Jewish calendar is calculated based on mathematical rules designed to ensure that festivals are observed in their traditional season. Passover always falls in the springtime.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This fixed lunisolar calendar follows rules introduced by
       <a href="/wiki/Hillel_II" title="Hillel II">
        Hillel II
       </a>
       and refined until the ninth century This calculation makes use of a mean lunation length used by
       <a href="/wiki/Ptolemy" title="Ptolemy">
        Ptolemy
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_theory#Babylon" title="Lunar theory">
        handed down from Babylonians
       </a>
       , which is still very accurate: ca. 29.530594 days vs. a present value (see
       <a href="#Determining_new_moons:_an_approximate_formula">
        below
       </a>
       ) of 29.530589 days. This difference of only 0.0000005, or five millionths of a day, adds up to about only four hours since Babylonian times.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Hebrew_Roots" title="Hebrew Roots">
        messianic
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pentecostal" title="Pentecostal">
        Pentecostal
       </a>
       group, the
       <a href="/wiki/Agricultural_People%27s_Front_of_Peru" title="Agricultural People's Front of Peru">
        New Israelites of Peru
       </a>
       , keeps the new moon as a Sabbath of rest. As an evangelical church, it follows the Bible's teachings that God sanctified the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seventh-day_Sabbath" title="Seventh-day Sabbath">
        seventh-day Sabbath
       </a>
       , and the new moons in addition to it. See Ezekiel 46:1, 3. No work may be done from dusk until dusk, and the services run for 11 hours, although a large number spend 24 hours within the gates of the temples, sleeping and singing praises throughout the night.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span id="Bah.C3.A1.27.C3.AD_calendar">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bahá'í_calendar">
        Bahá'í calendar
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=New_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Bahá'í calendar">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Faith" title="Bahá'í Faith">
        Bahá'í Faith
       </a>
       , effective from 2015 onwards, the "
       <a href="/wiki/Twin_Holy_Birthdays" title="Twin Holy Birthdays">
        Twin Holy Birthdays
       </a>
       ", referring to two successive holy days in the
       <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_calendar" title="Bahá'í calendar">
        Bahá'í calendar
       </a>
       (the birth of the
       <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A1b" title="Báb">
        Báb
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Birth_of_Bah%C3%A1%27u%27ll%C3%A1h" title="Birth of Bahá'u'lláh">
        birth of Bahá'u'lláh
       </a>
       ), will be observed on the first and the second day following the occurrence of the eighth new moon after
       <a href="/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%27%C3%AD_Naw-R%C3%BAz" title="Bahá'í Naw-Rúz">
        Naw-Rúz
       </a>
       (Bahá'í New Year), as determined in advance by astronomical tables using
       <a href="/wiki/Tehran" title="Tehran">
        Tehran
       </a>
       as the point of reference.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This will result in the observance of the Twin Birthdays moving, year to year, from mid-October to mid-November according to the Gregorian calendar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Clarifications_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Clarifications-30">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

          <span class="reference-text">
           <i>
            Annual aberration
           </i>
           is the ratio of Earth's orbital velocity (around 30 km/s) to the speed of light (about 300,000 km/s), which shifts the Sun's apparent position relative to the celestial sphere toward the west by about 1/10,000 radian. Light-time correction for the Moon is the distance it moves during the time it takes its light to reach Earth divided by the Earth-Moon distance, yielding an angle in radians by which its apparent position lags behind its computed geometric position. Light-time correction for the Sun is negligible because it is almost motionless relative to the
           <a href="/wiki/Barycenter" title="Barycenter">
            barycenter
           </a>
           (center-of-mass) of the solar system during the 8.3 minutes that light travels between Sun and Earth. The aberration of light for the Moon is also negligible (the center of the Earth moves too slowly around the Earth-Moon barycenter (0.002 km/s); and the so-called diurnal aberration, caused by the motion of an observer on the surface of the rotating Earth (0.5 km/s at the equator) can be neglected. Although aberration and light-time are often combined as
           <i>
            planetary aberration
           </i>
           , Meeus separated them (
           <i>
            op.cit.
           </i>
           p.210).
