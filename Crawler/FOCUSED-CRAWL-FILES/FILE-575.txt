    Manfred Memorial Moon Mission
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       <b>
        Manfred Memorial Moon Mission
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        4M
       </b>
       ) was the first commercial mission to the Moon. It was led by
       <a href="/wiki/Luxspace" title="Luxspace">
        LuxSpace
       </a>
       , a child company of German
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/OHB_System" title="OHB System">
        OHB System
       </a>
       , in honor of OHB Systems founder, professor
       <a href="/wiki/Manfred_Fuchs" title="Manfred Fuchs">
        Manfred Fuchs
       </a>
       , who died in 2014, and was carried on the Chinese
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5-T1" title="Chang'e 5-T1">
        Chang'e 5-T1
       </a>
       test spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceflightnowFirst_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spaceflightnowFirst-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.com-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Moon flyby took place on 28 October 2014, after which the spacecraft entered the elliptical earth orbit and continued transmission until 11 November 2014, exceeding its designed lifetime by four times.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoonLuxspace_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoonLuxspace-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saft_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Saft-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">
        Spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manfred_Memorial_Moon_Mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="quotebox pullquote" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; padding: 10px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
       <div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
        <div>
         REMEMBER THE SPACE PIONEER MANFRED FUCHS.
         <br/>
         REST IN PEACE MANFRED FUCHS.
         <br/>
         DAMIT ALLE MENSCHEN AUF DERWELT IN FRIEDEN ZUSAMMEN LEBEN.
         <br/>
         LA LI LU NUR DER MANN IM MOND SCHAUTZU WENN DIE KLEINEN KINDER SCHLAFEN UND SO SCHLAF AUCH DU.
         <br/>
         DO NOT GO WHERE THE PATH MAY LEAD GO INSTEAD WHERE THERE IS NO PATH AND LEAVE
         <br/>
         A TRAIL.
         <br/>
         ALL LIFE IS AN EXPERIMENT. THE MORE EXPERIMENTS YOU MAKE THE BETTER.
        </div>
       </div>
       <cite style="display: block; text-align: left;">
        Part of the message sent by the 4M
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-1">
         <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
          [4]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </cite>
      </div>
      <p>
       The briefcase-sized spacecraft weighing 14 kg was built by the Luxemburg company
       <a href="/wiki/Luxspace" title="Luxspace">
        LuxSpace
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spaceflightnowFirst_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-spaceflightnowFirst-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its primary power source consisted of 28 non-rechargeable
       <a href="/wiki/Saft_Groupe_S.A." title="Saft Groupe S.A.">
        Saft
       </a>
       LSH20 HTS lithium cells, which provided 4.5W power for the payload electronic to perform the primary objectives of the mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saft_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Saft-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The secondary power source included 4 Saft MPS
       <a href="/wiki/Lithium-ion_battery" title="Lithium-ion battery">
        lithium-ion batteries
       </a>
       recharged by a 2x8 grid of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">
        solar panels
       </a>
       , allowing the mission to extend its operational lifetime. Unlike the primary power source, however, it was dependent on the spacecraft altitude and rotation, determining the availability of sunlight to recharge the batteries. The onboard computer was an FM430.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MissionLuxspace_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MissionLuxspace-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.comFirstToLaunch_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.comFirstToLaunch-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The payload was divided between two experiments: a ham radio and a radiation experiment. The radio payload consisted of a quarter-wave
       <a href="/wiki/Monopole_antenna" title="Monopole antenna">
        monopole antenna
       </a>
       supported by an
       <a href="/wiki/Quadrature_modulation" title="Quadrature modulation">
        I/Q modulator
       </a>
       , and an
       <a href="/wiki/RF_power_amplifier" title="RF power amplifier">
        RF power amplifier
       </a>
       providing 1.5W power.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MissionLuxspace_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MissionLuxspace-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C5spaceflight101_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-C5spaceflight101-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The radiation experiment consisted of a radiation
       <a href="/wiki/Dosimeter" title="Dosimeter">
        dosimeter
       </a>
       provided by the Spanish company
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=IC-M%C3%A1laga&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="IC-Málaga (page does not exist)">
        iC-Málaga
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.com-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As 4M remained attached to the
       <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3C" title="Long March 3C">
        Long March 3C
       </a>
       /G2 upper stage through the mission, its design was adapted for heavy
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_interference" title="Electromagnetic interference">
        electromagnetic interference
       </a>
       from the nearby 1 kW
       <a href="/wiki/S_band" title="S band">
        S band
       </a>
       transmitter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Radio_experiment">
        Radio experiment
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manfred_Memorial_Moon_Mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Radio experiment">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The spacecraft's onboard antenna transmitted up to 2500, 13-character digital messages at 145.980 MHz using digital mode
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JT65B" title="JT65B">
        JT65B
       </a>
       , with additional
       <a href="/wiki/Signal_tone" title="Signal tone">
        tone transmissions
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MissionLuxspace_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MissionLuxspace-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       LuxSpace created a contest with prizes for
       <a href="/wiki/Amateur_radio_operator" title="Amateur radio operator">
        amateur radio operators
       </a>
       to receive these transmissions and send results back to the company.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The radio experiment was activated 77.8 minutes after the launch, and the first radio signal was received in
       <a href="/wiki/Brazil" title="Brazil">
        Brazil
       </a>
       at 19:18 UTC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C5spaceflight101_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-C5spaceflight101-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The team expected 10 participants to join the contest, but more than 60 people took part. Scoring was based on the number of messages received. The final list of participants, who would receive certificates, along with their scores, was announced on 18 November 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">
        Mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manfred_Memorial_Moon_Mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The spacecraft was launched attached to the third stage of
       <a href="/wiki/Long_March_3C" title="Long March 3C">
        Long March 3C
       </a>
       /G2 on 23 October 2014 at 18:00 UTC along with
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_5-T1" title="Chang'e 5-T1">
        Chang'e 5-T1
       </a>
       . It remained attached to the third stage on its way to the Moon, making its closest flyby a day after Chang'e 5-T1.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C5spaceflight101_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-C5spaceflight101-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The radiation experiment, which measured
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_Ionizing_Dose" title="Total Ionizing Dose">
        Total Ionizing Dose
       </a>
       every 5 minutes, showed a significant increase in radiation doses while crossing
       <a href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt" title="Van Allen radiation belt">
        Van Allen radiation belts
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Moon flyby took place during the night of 28 October 2014.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoonLuxspace_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoonLuxspace-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       During the return, 215 hours into the mission, the radiation experiment stopped working for unknown reasons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The spacecraft entered a highly elliptical
       <a href="/wiki/Geocentric_orbit" title="Geocentric orbit">
        geocentric orbit
       </a>
       with a period of 14 days and remained in space.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saft_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Saft-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-C5spaceflight101_8-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-C5spaceflight101-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The last transmission from the spacecraft was received on 11 November 2014, 01:35 UTC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EndMissionLuxspace_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-EndMissionLuxspace-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Honorifics">
        Honorifics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Manfred_Memorial_Moon_Mission&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Honorifics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On 13 May 1998
       <a href="/wiki/PAS-22" title="PAS-22">
        PAS-22
       </a>
       , at the time called HGS-1, a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geostationary" title="Geostationary">
        geostationary
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Communications_satellite" title="Communications satellite">
        communications satellite
       </a>
       made a moon fly by in a recovery attempt after a partially failed launch that left it on an unusable, highly elliptical orbit. Using
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_assist" title="Gravity assist">
        gravity assist
       </a>
       manoeuvres satellite was successfully recovered on 17 June. Unlike 4M mission HGS-1 wasn't designed as a moon mission, and made a flyby only in a recovery attempt, however according to Hughes Global Services, satellite operator at the time, it claimed the title of the first commercial mission to the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-hughes_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-hughes-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
