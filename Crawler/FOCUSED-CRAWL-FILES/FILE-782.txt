      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Jupiter Icy Moons Orbiter
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        JIMO
       </b>
       ) was a proposed
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       designed to explore the icy
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       . The main target was
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       , where an ocean of liquid water may harbor alien life.
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       , which are now thought to have liquid, salty oceans beneath their icy surfaces, were also targets of interest for the probe.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="The_JIMO_spacecraft">
        The JIMO spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter_Icy_Moons_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: The JIMO spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Prometheus1.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="645" data-file-width="1125" height="126" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Prometheus1.jpg/220px-Prometheus1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Prometheus1.jpg/330px-Prometheus1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Prometheus1.jpg/440px-Prometheus1.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Prometheus1.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Forward view of JIMO.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       JIMO was to have a large number of revolutionary features. Throughout its main voyage to the Jupiter moons, it was to be propelled by an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_propulsion" title="Ion propulsion">
        ion propulsion
       </a>
       system via either the
       <a href="/wiki/High_Power_Electric_Propulsion" title="High Power Electric Propulsion">
        High Power Electric Propulsion
       </a>
       or NEXIS engine, and powered by a small
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_reactor" title="Nuclear reactor">
        fission reactor
       </a>
       . A
       <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">
        Brayton
       </a>
       power conversion system would convert reactor heat into electricity. Providing a thousand times the electrical output of conventional
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_cell" title="Solar cell">
        solar
       </a>
       - or
       <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">
        radioisotope thermoelectric generator
       </a>
       (RTG)-based power systems, the reactor was expected to open up opportunities like flying a full scale ice-penetrating
       <a href="/wiki/Radar" title="Radar">
        radar
       </a>
       system and providing a strong, high-bandwidth
       <a href="/wiki/Data" title="Data">
        data
       </a>
       transmitter.
      </p>
      <p>
       Using electric propulsion (8 ion engines, plus
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hall_effect_thruster" title="Hall effect thruster">
        Hall thrusters
       </a>
       of varying sizes) would make it possible to go into and leave orbits around the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Moons of Jupiter">
        moons of Jupiter
       </a>
       , creating more thorough observation and mapping windows than exist for current spacecraft, which must make short fly-by maneuvers because of limited fuel for maneuvering.
      </p>
      <p>
       The design called for the reactor to be positioned in the tip of the spacecraft behind a strong
       <a href="/wiki/Particle_radiation" title="Particle radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       shield protecting sensitive spacecraft equipment. The reactor would only be powered up once the probe was well out of Earth orbit, so that the amount of
       <a href="/wiki/Radionuclide" title="Radionuclide">
        radionuclides
       </a>
       that must be launched into orbit is minimized. This configuration is thought to be less risky than the
       <a href="/wiki/Radioisotope_thermoelectric_generator" title="Radioisotope thermoelectric generator">
        RTGs
       </a>
       used on previous missions to the outer
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Europa Lander Mission
       </i>
       proposed to include on JIMO a small nuclear-powered Europa lander. It would travel with the orbiter, which would also function as a communication relay to Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-elm_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-elm-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It would investigate Europa's
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_habitability" title="Planetary habitability">
        habitability
       </a>
       and assess its
       <a href="/wiki/Astrobiology" title="Astrobiology">
        astrobiological
       </a>
       potential by confirming the existence and determining the characteristics of water within and below Europa's icy shell.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lander_2012_report_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lander_2012_report-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">
        Northrop Grumman
       </a>
       was selected on September 20, 2004 for a $400 million preliminary design contract, beating
       <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Martin" title="Lockheed Martin">
        Lockheed Martin
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boeing_Integrated_Defense_Systems" title="Boeing Integrated Defense Systems">
        Boeing IDS
       </a>
       . The contract was to have run through to 2008. Separate contracts, covering construction and individual instruments, were to be awarded at a later date.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Nuclear_propulsion">
        Nuclear propulsion
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter_Icy_Moons_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Nuclear propulsion">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The JIMO mission was proposed to include a
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_electric_rocket" title="Nuclear electric rocket">
        nuclear electric propulsion
       </a>
       system for the long-duration mission, with power provided by a small 200 kWe
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fission_power_system" title="Fission power system">
        fission power system
       </a>
       . The nuclear propulsion program was conducted from 2003 to 2005 by the Naval Reactors branch of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_DOE" title="US DOE">
        DOE
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa/doe2010_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa/doe2010-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The proposed system design was a
       <a href="/wiki/Gas-cooled_reactor" title="Gas-cooled reactor">
        gas-cooled reactor
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Brayton_cycle" title="Brayton cycle">
        Brayton
       </a>
       power conversion to generate a peak output of 200 kilowatts of power over the long life of the JIMO mission.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa/doe2010_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa/doe2010-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission_profile">
        Mission profile
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter_Icy_Moons_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Mission profile">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:352px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="661" data-file-width="983" height="235" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg/350px-JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg/525px-JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg/700px-JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg 2x" width="350"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:JIMO_Europa_Lander_Mission.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Europa Lander Mission concept circa 2005 (
         <a class="external text" href="http://trs-new.jpl.nasa.gov/dspace/bitstream/2014/37545/1/05-0403.pdf" rel="nofollow">
          NASA
         </a>
         ).
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Three launches were planned for May 2015 to LEO in order to assemble the two transfer stages and the probe. Transfer stages were designed to launch the probe on its trajectory to Jupiter during the launch window extending from late October 2015 to mid-January 2016.
      </p>
      <p>
       During the first month of flight, the probe's main structures would be deployed, the nuclear reactor activated, and the thrusters tested. Interplanetary flight would have lasted until April 2021 (the ion engines were supposed to work two thirds of the time).
      </p>
      <p>
       Once the probe was in the influence area of Jupiter, the navigation would become more complex and difficult. The probe would have to use gravity assist maneuvers to enter orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       The probe would have studied
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       and then
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       for three months each, and finally
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       for one month (studies of
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       were also planned when the orbital conditions would have been favorable).
      </p>
      <p>
       At the end of the mission in September 2025, the vehicle would have been parked in a stable orbit around Europa.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cancellation">
        Cancellation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter_Icy_Moons_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Cancellation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Due to a shift in priorities at
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       that favored manned space missions, the project lost funding in 2005, effectively cancelling the JIMO mission. Among other issues, the proposed nuclear technology was deemed too ambitious, as was the multiple-launch and in-orbit assembly mission architecture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berger_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Berger-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Engineers at the Jet Propulsion Laboratory with JIMO were laid off or reassigned during the spring and summer of 2005.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As a result of the budget changes, NASA is instead considering a demonstration mission to a target closer to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       to test out the reactor and heat rejection systems. The spacecraft would possibly be scaled down from its original size as well.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2007)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       When it was cancelled, the JIMO mission was in an early planning stage and launch wasn't expected before 2017. It was to be the first proposed mission of NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Prometheus" title="Project Prometheus">
        Project Prometheus
       </a>
       , a program for developing
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fission" title="Nuclear fission">
        nuclear fission
       </a>
       into a means of
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft_propulsion" title="Spacecraft propulsion">
        spacecraft propulsion
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Subsequent_proposed_replacements">
        Subsequent proposed replacements
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter_Icy_Moons_Orbiter&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Subsequent proposed replacements">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       After JIMO, NASA and ESA planned a joint mission to Jupiter's moons, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Europa_Jupiter_System_Mission" title="Europa Jupiter System Mission">
        Europa Jupiter System Mission
       </a>
       . This mission was also cancelled in 2011.
      </p>
      <p>
       ESA has since continued to work separately on that design and on 2 May 2012 selected the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter_Icy_Moon_Explorer" title="Jupiter Icy Moon Explorer">
        Jupiter Icy Moon Explorer
       </a>
       (JUICE) mission over two other ESA missions for funding. The JUICE mission will study the Jupiter moons Europa, Callisto and Ganymede and be launched as an ESA L-class mission in 2022 on an
       <a href="/wiki/Ariane_5" title="Ariane 5">
        Ariane 5
       </a>
       carrier rocket.
      </p>
