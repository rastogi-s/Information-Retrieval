      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       ,
       <b>
        lunar orbit
       </b>
       (also known as a
       <b>
        selenocentric orbit
       </b>
       ) is the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       of an object around the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       As used in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Space_program" title="Space program">
        space program
       </a>
       , this refers not to the orbit of the Moon about the
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       , but to orbits by various manned or unmanned spacecraft around the Moon. The altitude at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">
        apoapsis
       </a>
       (point farthest from the surface) for a lunar orbit is known as
       <b>
        apolune
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        apocynthion
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        aposelene
       </b>
       , while the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       (point closest to the surface) is known as
       <b>
        perilune
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        pericynthion
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        periselene
       </b>
       , from names or epithets of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_goddess" title="Moon goddess">
        moon goddess
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <b>
        Low Lunar orbit
       </b>
       (LLO)—orbits below 100 kilometres (62 mi) altitude—are of particular interest in exploration of the moon, but suffer from gravitational perturbation effects that make most unstable, and leave only a few orbital inclinations possible for indefinite
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">
         frozen orbits
        </a>
       </i>
       , useful for long-term stays in LLO.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20061106_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20061106-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Robotic_spacecraft">
        Robotic spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Robotic spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       sent the first spacecraft to the vicinity of the Moon, the robotic vehicle
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_1" title="Luna 1">
        Luna 1
       </a>
       , on January 4, 1959.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mark_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mark-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It passed within 6,000 kilometres (3,200 nmi; 3,700 mi) of the Moon's surface, but did not achieve lunar orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mark_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-mark-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       , launched on October 4, 1959, was the first robotic spacecraft to complete a
       <a href="/wiki/Circumlunar_trajectory" title="Circumlunar trajectory">
        circumlunar
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">
        free return trajectory
       </a>
       , still not a lunar orbit, but a figure-8 trajectory which swung around the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       and returned to the Earth. This craft provided the first pictures of the far side of the Lunar surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mark_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-mark-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_10" title="Luna 10">
        Luna 10
       </a>
       became the first spacecraft to actually orbit the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       in April 1966.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyers_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-beyers-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It studied
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeoroid
       </a>
       flux, and lunar environment until May 30, 1966.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-beyers_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-beyers-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       spacecraft to orbit the Moon was
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_1" title="Lunar Orbiter 1">
        Lunar Orbiter 1
       </a>
       on August 14, 1966.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbiter_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-orbiter-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The first orbit was an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elliptical_orbit" title="Elliptical orbit">
        elliptical orbit
       </a>
       , with an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apolune" title="Apolune">
        apolune
       </a>
       of 1,008 nautical miles (1,867 km; 1,160 mi) and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perilune" title="Perilune">
        perilune
       </a>
       of 102.1 nautical miles (189.1 km; 117.5 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Then the orbit was circularized at around 170 nautical miles (310 km; 200 mi) to obtain suitable imagery. Five such spacecraft were launched over a period of thirteen months, all of which successfully mapped the Moon, primarily for the purpose of finding suitable
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       landing sites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbiter_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-orbiter-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The most recent was the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Atmosphere_and_Dust_Environment_Explorer" title="Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer">
        Lunar Atmosphere and Dust Environment Explorer
       </a>
       (LADEE), which became a ballistic impact experiment in 2014.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Human_crewed_spacecraft">
        Human crewed spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Human crewed spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">
        Command/Service Module
       </a>
       (CSM) remained in a lunar parking orbit while the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        Lunar Module
       </a>
       (LM) landed. The combined CSM/LM would first enter an elliptical orbit, nominally 170 nautical miles (310 km; 200 mi) by 60 nautical miles (110 km; 69 mi), which was then changed to a circular parking orbit of about 60 nautical miles (110 km; 69 mi). Orbital periods vary according to the sum of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apoapsis" title="Apoapsis">
        apoapsis
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       , and for the CSM were about two hours. The LM began its landing sequence with a Descent Orbit Insertion (DOI) burn to lower their
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       to about 50,000 feet (15 km; 8.2 nmi), chosen to avoid hitting
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_mountains_on_the_Moon" title="List of mountains on the Moon">
        lunar mountains
       </a>
       reaching heights of 20,000 feet (6.1 km; 3.3 nmi). After the second landing mission, the procedure was changed on
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       to save more of the LM fuel for its powered descent, by using the CSM's fuel to perform the DOI burn, and later raising its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Periapsis" title="Periapsis">
        periapsis
       </a>
       back to a circular orbit after the LM had made its landing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a11fj_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-a11fj-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Perturbation_effects">
        Perturbation effects
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_orbit&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Perturbation effects">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Gravitational anomalies slightly distorting the orbits of some Lunar Orbiters led to the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Mass_concentration_(astronomy)" title="Mass concentration (astronomy)">
        mass concentrations
       </a>
       (dubbed
       <b>
        mascons
       </b>
       ), beneath the lunar surface caused by large impacting bodies at some remote time in the past.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20061106_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20061106-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These anomalies are significant enough to cause a lunar orbit to change significantly over the course of several days. The
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       first manned landing mission employed the first attempt to correct for the perturbation effect (the frozen orbits were not known at that time). The parking orbit was "circularized" at 66 nautical miles (122 km; 76 mi) by 54 nautical miles (100 km; 62 mi), which was expected to become the nominal circular 60 nautical miles (110 km; 69 mi) when the LM made its return
       <a href="/wiki/Space_rendezvous" title="Space rendezvous">
        rendezvous
       </a>
       with the CSM. But the effect was overestimated by a factor of two; at rendezvous the orbit was calculated to be 63.2 nautical miles (117.0 km; 72.7 mi) by 56.8 nautical miles (105.2 km; 65.4 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Apollo_11_Mission_Report_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Apollo_11_Mission_Report-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Study of the mascons' effect on lunar spacecraft led to the discovery in 2001 of "
       <a href="/wiki/Frozen_orbit" title="Frozen orbit">
        frozen orbits
       </a>
       " occurring at four
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        orbital inclinations
       </a>
       : 27°, 50°, 76°, and 86°, in which a spacecraft can stay in a low orbit indefinitely.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nasa20061106_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-nasa20061106-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       subsatellite
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PFS-1" title="PFS-1">
        PFS-1
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        Apollo 16
       </a>
       subsatellite
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/PFS-2" title="PFS-2">
        PFS-2
       </a>
       , both small satellites released from the Apollo
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module#Service_Module_(SM)" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">
        Service Module
       </a>
       , contributed to this discovery. PFS-1 ended up in a long-lasting orbit, at 28 degrees
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       , and successfully completed its mission after one and a half years. PFS-2 was placed in a particularly unstable orbital inclination of 11 degrees, and lasted only 35 days in orbit before crashing into the lunar surface.
