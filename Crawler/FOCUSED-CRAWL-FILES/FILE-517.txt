      <p>
       <b>
        Kepler
       </b>
       (Latin
       <i>
        Keplerus
       </i>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       that lies between the
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       to the west and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Insularum" title="Mare Insularum">
        Mare Insularum
       </a>
       in the east. To the north is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Bessarion_(crater)" title="Bessarion (crater)">
        Bessarion
       </a>
       , to the east-northeast is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Milichius_(crater)" title="Milichius (crater)">
        Milichius
       </a>
       and southeast is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Encke_(crater)" title="Encke (crater)">
        Encke
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Description">
        Description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Kepler_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Kepler is most notable for the prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        ray system
       </a>
       that covers the surrounding
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        mare
       </a>
       . The rays extend for well over 300 kilometers, overlapping the rays from other craters. Kepler has a small
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/rampart" title="wikt:rampart">
        rampart
       </a>
       of ejecta surrounding the exterior of its high rim. The outer wall is not quite circular, and possesses a slightly
       <a href="/wiki/Polygon" title="Polygon">
        polygonal
       </a>
       form. The interior walls of Kepler are slumped and slightly
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/terrace" title="wiktionary:terrace">
        terraced
       </a>
       , descending to an uneven floor and a minor central rise.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the rays from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tycho_(crater)" title="Tycho (crater)">
        Tycho
       </a>
       , when extended across the
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       , intersects this crater. This was a factor in the choice of the crater's name when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giovanni_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Riccioli">
        Giovanni Riccioli
       </a>
       was creating his system of
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        lunar nomenclature
       </a>
       , as Kepler used the observations of
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">
        Tycho Brahe
       </a>
       while devising his three laws of planetary motion. On Riccioli's maps, this crater was named
       <i>
        Keplerus
       </i>
       , and the surrounding skirt of higher
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       terrain was named
       <i>
        Insulara Ventorum
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Due to its prominent rays, Kepler is mapped as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Copernican_period" title="Copernican period">
        Copernican System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
