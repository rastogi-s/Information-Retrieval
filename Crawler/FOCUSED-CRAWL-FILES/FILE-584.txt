      <p>
       The
       <b>
        International Lunar Network
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        ILN
       </b>
       is a proposed network of a series of landed stations of the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and the other
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_space_agencies" title="List of space agencies">
        space-faring countries
       </a>
       on the lunar surface in the 2010s. Each of these stations will act as a
       <a href="/wiki/Node_(networking)" title="Node (networking)">
        node
       </a>
       in a lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Geophysics" title="Geophysics">
        geophysical
       </a>
       network. Ultimately this network could comprise 8-10 or more nodes operating simultaneously. In the ILN concept, each node will have a minimum of two core capabilities. These capabilities include seismic sensing, heat flow sensing, and laser
       <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">
        retroreflectors
       </a>
       , and will be specific to each station. Because some nodes are planned to be located on the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration" title="National Aeronautics and Space Administration">
        NASA
       </a>
       will study a lunar communications relay satellite capability as a part of its contribution to this project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Individual nodes launched by different space agencies can and likely will carry additional, unique experiments to study local or global lunar science. Such experiments might include atmospheric and dust instruments, plasma physics investigations, astronomical instruments, electromagnetic profiling of lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       and crust, local geochemistry, and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/In-situ_resource_utilization" title="In-situ resource utilization">
        in-situ resource utilization
       </a>
       demonstrations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=International_Lunar_Network&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On July 24, 2008 a meeting of the space agencies of
       <a href="/wiki/Canadian_Space_Agency" title="Canadian Space Agency">
        Canada
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">
        France
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/German_Aerospace_Center" title="German Aerospace Center">
        Germany
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organization" title="Indian Space Research Organization">
        India
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Italian_Space_Agency" title="Italian Space Agency">
        Italy
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japan_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japan Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japan
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Korea_Aerospace_Research_Institute" title="Korea Aerospace Research Institute">
        Republic of Korea
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/British_National_Space_Centre" title="British National Space Centre">
        United Kingdom
       </a>
       , and the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       was held at NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_and_Planetary_Institute" title="Lunar and Planetary Institute">
        Lunar Science Institute
       </a>
       , located at the
       <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">
        Ames Research Center
       </a>
       . During the meeting, the representatives of the nine space agencies discussed about the cooperation on ILN and agreed on a statement of intent as a first step in planning. NASA’s
       <a href="/wiki/Science_Mission_Directorate" title="Science Mission Directorate">
        Science Mission Directorate
       </a>
       (SMD) and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Exploration_Systems_Mission_Directorate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Exploration Systems Mission Directorate (page does not exist)">
        Exploration Systems Mission Directorate
       </a>
       (ESMD) have agreed to provide two pairs of nodes (landed stations) for this network.
      </p>
      <p>
       The first two nodes were to be launched in 2013 and 2014, with the second pair being launched some time between 2016 and 2017. However, the new planning date for launch of the first node is March 01, 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA1_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA1-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The landers are being developed under the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Precursor_Robotic_Program" title="Lunar Precursor Robotic Program">
        Lunar Precursor Robotic Program
       </a>
       at NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       in Huntsville, Alabama.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA2_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA2-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
