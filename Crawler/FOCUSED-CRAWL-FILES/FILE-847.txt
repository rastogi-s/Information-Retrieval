   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Icy moon
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

     <a href="#mw-head">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       <b>
        Icy moons
       </b>
       are a class of
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       with surfaces composed mostly of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       . An icy moon may harbor an ocean underneath the surface, and possibly include a rocky core of silicate or metallic rocks. It is thought that they may be composed of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice_II" title="Ice II">
        ice II
       </a>
       or other
       <a href="/wiki/Polymorphism_(materials_science)" title="Polymorphism (materials science)">
        polymorph
       </a>
       of water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ice_II_structure_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ice_II_structure-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The prime example of this class of object is
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Icy moons warmed by
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tides
       </a>
       may be the most common type of object to have liquid
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       and thus the type of object most likely to have
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>
       -based
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
        life
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy_dispute#Disputed_statement" title="Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute">
          <span title="The material near this tag is possibly inaccurate or nonfactual. (February 2012)">
           dubious
          </span>
         </a>
         <span class="metadata">
          –
          <a href="/wiki/Talk:Icy_moon#Dubious" title="Talk:Icy moon">
           discuss
          </a>
         </span>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Some icy moons exhibit
       <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">
        cryovolcanism
       </a>
       , as well as geysers. The best studied example is
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn's
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbits">
        Orbits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Icy_moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Most known large icy moons belong to
       <a href="/wiki/Giant_planet" title="Giant planet">
        giant planets
       </a>
       , whose orbits lie beyond the Solar System's
       <a href="/wiki/Frost_line_(astrophysics)" title="Frost line (astrophysics)">
        frost line
       </a>
       ; the remainder (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Dysnomia_(moon)" title="Dysnomia (moon)">
        Dysnomia
       </a>
       ) formed around dwarf planets such as
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       , typically in large impacts not unlike the impact thought to have formed Earth's moon. In the case of icy gas giant satellites, an additional requirement is that a moon did not form in the inner region of a proto-satellite disk, which is too warm for ices to condense.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       is thought to contain 8% ice and water by mass with the remainder rock.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-arxiv0812_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-arxiv0812-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jupiter's outer two
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       contain more ice since they formed further from the hot proto-Jupiter.
      </p>
      <p>
       Saturn's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       looks and behaves more like Earth than any other body in the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0915_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU0915-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Titan is known to have stable pools of liquid on the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU0915_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU0915-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
