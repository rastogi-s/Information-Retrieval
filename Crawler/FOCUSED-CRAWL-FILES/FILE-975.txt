      <p>
       <b>
        Remus
       </b>
       is the inner and smaller moon of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Main-belt" title="Main-belt">
        main-belt
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/87_Sylvia" title="87 Sylvia">
        87 Sylvia
       </a>
       . It follows an almost-circular close-to-equatorial orbit around the parent asteroid. In this respect it is similar to the other moon
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus_(moon)" title="Romulus (moon)">
        Romulus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Remus was discovered several years after Romulus on images taken starting on August 9, 2004, and announced on August 10, 2005. It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Franck_Marchis" title="Franck Marchis">
        Franck Marchis
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Berkeley" title="UC Berkeley">
        UC Berkeley
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pascal_Descamps&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pascal Descamps (page does not exist)">
        Pascal Descamps
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Daniel_Hestroffer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Daniel Hestroffer (page does not exist)">
        Daniel Hestroffer
       </a>
       , and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=J%C3%A9r%C3%B4me_Berthier&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jérôme Berthier (page does not exist)">
        Jérôme Berthier
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Observatoire_de_Paris" title="Observatoire de Paris">
        Observatoire de Paris
       </a>
       , France, using the Yepun telescope of the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">
        European Southern Observatory
       </a>
       (ESO) in
       <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">
        Chile
       </a>
       . Marchis, the project leader, was waiting for the completion of the image acquisition programme before starting to process the data. Just as he was set to go on vacation in March 2005, Descamps sent him a brief note entitled "87 Sylvia est triple ?" pointing out that he could see two moonlets on several images of Sylvia. The entire team then focused quickly on analysis of the data, wrote a paper, submitted an abstract to the August meeting in Rio de Janeiro and submitted a naming proposal to the IAU.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its full designation is
       <b>
        (87) Sylvia II Remus
       </b>
       ; before receiving its name, it was known as
       <b>
        S/2004 (87) 1
       </b>
       . The moon is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Romulus_and_Remus" title="Romulus and Remus">
        Remus
       </a>
       , twin of the mythological founder of
       <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">
        Rome
       </a>
       , one of the children of
       <a href="/wiki/Rhea_Silvia" title="Rhea Silvia">
        Rhea Silvia
       </a>
       raised by a wolf.
      </p>
      <p>
       87 Sylvia has a low density, which indicates that it is probably a
       <a href="/wiki/Rubble_pile" title="Rubble pile">
        rubble pile
       </a>
       asteroid formed when debris from a collision between its parent body and another asteroid re-accreted gravitationally. Thus it is likely that both Remus and Romulus are smaller rubble piles which accreted in orbit around the main body from debris of the same collision. In this case their albedo and density are expected to be similar to Sylvia's.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis05_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis05-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Remus' orbit is expected to be quite stable: it lies far inside Sylvia's
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       (about 1/100 of Sylvia's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hill_radius" title="Hill radius">
        Hill radius
       </a>
       ), but also far outside the
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous orbit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Marchis05_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Marchis05-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       From Remus' surface, Sylvia appears huge, taking up an angular region roughly 30°×18° across, while Romulus' apparent size varies between 1.6° and 0.5° across.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>
