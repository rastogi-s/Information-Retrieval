      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's extensive system of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jupiter%27s_natural_satellites" title="Jupiter's natural satellites">
        natural satellites
       </a>
       – in particular the four large
       <a href="/wiki/Galilean_moons" title="Galilean moons">
        Galilean moons
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       ) – has been a common
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
        science fiction
       </a>
       setting.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Satellite_system">
        Satellite system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter%27s_moons_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Satellite system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some references in fiction focus on more than one moon, or do not refer to a specific moon.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">
        Literature
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter%27s_moons_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Literature">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Seetee_Ship" title="Seetee Ship">
          Seetee Ship
         </a>
        </i>
        (1949) and
        <i>
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Seetee_Shock&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Seetee Shock (page does not exist)">
          Seetee Shock
         </a>
        </i>
        (1950) by
        <a href="/wiki/Jack_Williamson" title="Jack Williamson">
         Jack Williamson
        </a>
        , the Jovian moons are colonised by the
        <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
         Soviet Union
        </a>
        , which transfers its government there after the United States builds a nuclear base on the
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         Moon
        </a>
        , which enables the Americans to dominate the whole of Earth. The Jovian Soviet is one of the main powers contending for control of the mineral wealth of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Asteroid_Belt" title="Asteroid Belt">
         Asteroid Belt
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
         Robert A. Heinlein
        </a>
        's young adult novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Farmer_in_the_Sky" title="Farmer in the Sky">
          Farmer in the Sky
         </a>
        </i>
        (1950) is set on Ganymede.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
         Arthur C. Clarke
        </a>
        's short story,
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_Five" title="Jupiter Five">
          Jupiter Five
         </a>
        </i>
        (1953), has most of the action in the Jupiter system. Clarke also concentrates heavily on the system in his
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Space_Odyssey" title="Space Odyssey">
          Space Odyssey
         </a>
        </i>
        series.
       </li>
       <li>
        The novel
        <i>
         The Runaway Robot
        </i>
        (1965) by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lester_Del_Rey" title="Lester Del Rey">
         Lester Del Rey
        </a>
        is set primarily on Ganymede.
       </li>
       <li>
        "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Moons_of_Jupiter_(short_story)" title="The Moons of Jupiter (short story)">
         The Moons of Jupiter
        </a>
        " (1978) by
        <a href="/wiki/Alice_Munro" title="Alice Munro">
         Alice Munro
        </a>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Io">
        Io
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Io,_moon_of_Jupiter,_NASA.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="391" data-file-width="379" height="227" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Io%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/220px-Io%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Io%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/330px-Io%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/14/Io%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Io,_moon_of_Jupiter,_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Io
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Io_(moon)" title="Io (moon)">
        Io
       </a>
       is the closest of the Galilean satellites to Jupiter. It is almost the same size as the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Because of its position, it is subject to constant
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal flexing
       </a>
       and heavy
       <a href="/wiki/Radiation" title="Radiation">
        radiation
       </a>
       from Jupiter's magnetic field. Since 1979, it has also been known for its abundant
       <a href="/wiki/Volcanism" title="Volcanism">
        volcanism
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Europa-moon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="913" data-file-width="913" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/220px-Europa-moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/330px-Europa-moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/54/Europa-moon.jpg/440px-Europa-moon.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

         Europa
        </div>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       is the smallest of the four Galilean satellites and the second closest to Jupiter. It is theorized to have an ocean of liquid water underneath its icy surface; the thickness of the ice is much debated. The probable presence of the water ocean has made it a favored location for modern fictional speculation about
       <a href="/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life" title="Extraterrestrial life">
        extraterrestrial life
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ganymede">
        Ganymede
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter%27s_moons_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Ganymede">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ganymede,_moon_of_Jupiter,_NASA.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="550" data-file-width="539" height="224" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/220px-Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/330px-Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/440px-Ganymede%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ganymede,_moon_of_Jupiter,_NASA.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Ganymede
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       is the third of the Galilean moons from Jupiter. It is the largest moon in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       , bigger than the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       (though less massive), almost 52% larger than the
       <a href="/wiki/Diameter" title="Diameter">
        diameter
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and with twice its
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       . It is 77% the diameter of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . Ganymede's size made it a popular location for early science fiction authors looking for locations beyond Mars that might be inhabitable by humans. In reality, Ganymede is a cold, icy, cratered world with a vanishingly thin atmosphere.
      </p>

      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Callisto,_moon_of_Jupiter,_NASA.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="508" data-file-width="510" height="219" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/220px-Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/330px-Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg/440px-Callisto%2C_moon_of_Jupiter%2C_NASA.jpg 2x" width="220"/>

         Callisto
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       is the outermost of the Galilean satellites. It is a large moon, only slightly smaller than the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       . It is cold, icy, and heavily cratered, with a very tenuous atmosphere. Despite its size and early discovery, it has not been featured in fiction as much as the other Galilean satellites.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Amalthea">
        Amalthea
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter%27s_moons_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=34" title="Edit section: Amalthea">

        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Amalthea_PIA02532.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="410" data-file-width="860" height="105" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Amalthea_PIA02532.png/220px-Amalthea_PIA02532.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Amalthea_PIA02532.png/330px-Amalthea_PIA02532.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Amalthea_PIA02532.png/440px-Amalthea_PIA02532.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Amalthea_PIA02532.png" title="Enlarge">

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Amalthea_(moon)" title="Amalthea (moon)">
        Amalthea
       </a>
       , or Jupiter V, is the third moon from Jupiter and the largest of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellites_of_Jupiter" title="Inner satellites of Jupiter">
        inner satellites of Jupiter
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/James_Blish" title="James Blish">
         James Blish
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Cities_in_Flight" title="Cities in Flight">
          Cities in Flight
         </a>
        </i>
        series begins with the story
        <i>
         They Shall Have Stars
        </i>
        (1956), where a base has been established on Jupiter V. This base is the remote operations centre for the Bridge Project on Jupiter proper.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
         Arthur C. Clarke
        </a>
        universe
        <ul>
         <li>
          The short story
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Jupiter_Five" title="Jupiter Five">
            Jupiter Five
           </a>
          </i>
          (1951) is set on Amalthea; its plot depends on the moon's weak gravity, and explores what might happen if an astronaut were thrown from its surface. Clarke continued referring to Amalthea as
          <i>
           Jupiter V
          </i>
          in his later works.
         </li>
         <li>
          In an early draft of
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(novel)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (novel)">
            2001: A Space Odyssey
           </a>
          </i>
          novel, the predecessor of the
          <a href="/wiki/Monolith_(Space_Odyssey)" title="Monolith (Space Odyssey)">
           giant monolith
          </a>
          is located on the surface of Amalthea (according to Clarke in
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_Worlds_of_2001" title="The Lost Worlds of 2001">
            The Lost Worlds of 2001
           </a>
          </i>
          [1972]).
         </li>
         <li>
          In
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/A_Meeting_with_Medusa" title="A Meeting with Medusa">
            A Meeting with Medusa
           </a>
          </i>
          (1971), space vessels use Amalthea as a natural particle/radiation shield.
         </li>
         <li>
          <a href="/wiki/Paul_Preuss_(author)" title="Paul Preuss (author)">
           Paul Preuss
          </a>
          '
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Venus_Prime" title="Venus Prime">
            Venus Prime
           </a>
          </i>
          series book five,
          <i>
           The Diamond Moon
          </i>
          (1990), and six,
          <i>
           The Shining Ones
          </i>
          (1991), deal with the exploration of Amalthea.
         </li>
        </ul>
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Dan_Simmons" title="Dan Simmons">
         Dan Simmons
        </a>
        's
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Olympos_(novel)" title="Olympos (novel)">
          Olympos
         </a>
        </i>
        , the moravec Retrograde Sinopessen is a member of the Five Moons Consortium from Amalthea.
       </li>
       <li>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/The_Way_to_Amalthea" title="The Way to Amalthea">
          The Way to Amalthea
         </a>
        </i>
        is a sci-fi story set on Amaltheia written by
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boris_and_Arkady_Strugatsky" title="Boris and Arkady Strugatsky">
         Boris and Arkady Strugatsky
        </a>
        in 1959 (published in Russian in 1960, in English translation in 1963).

       <li>

        <a href="/wiki/Shoot_%27em_up" title="Shoot 'em up">

        </a>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Sol-Feace" title="Sol-Feace">
          Sol-Feace
         </a>
        </i>
        , the strongest line of the villain's defense and sixth level is on Amalthea.
       </li>
       <li>

        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Astro_Boy_(1980_TV_series)" title="Astro Boy (1980 TV series)">
          Astro Boy
         </a>
        </i>
        , Amalthea is destroyed by the illegal test-firing of an
        <a href="/wiki/Antiproton" title="Antiproton">
         antiproton
        </a>

       </li>
       <li>
        In the X-Entertainment
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Advent_Calendar" title="Advent Calendar">
          Advent Calendar
         </a>
        </i>
        , the villains Hare Winningham and Hssxxllo Usall were imprisoned on Amalthea.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pasiphae_(moon)" title="Pasiphae (moon)">
        Pasiphae
       </a>
       (Jupiter VIII) is used as a setting in
       <a href="/wiki/John_Varley_(author)" title="John Varley (author)">
        John Varley's
       </a>
       novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Ophiuchi_Hotline" title="The Ophiuchi Hotline">
         The Ophiuchi Hotline
        </a>
       </i>
       , though it is referred to by its pre-1975 name,
       <i>
        Poseidon
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sinope">
        Sinope
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Jupiter%27s_moons_in_fiction&amp;action=edit&amp;section=36" title="Edit section: Sinope">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sinope_(moon)" title="Sinope (moon)">
        Sinope
       </a>
       , or Jupiter IX, is a small irregular satellite of Jupiter. From the time of its discovery in 1914 until the discovery of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaclite_(moon)" title="Megaclite (moon)">
        Megaclite
       </a>
       in 2000, it was the outermost of Jupiter's known moons. It is still the most distant Jovian moon to have a diameter of more than 10 km.
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        In
        <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Asimov" title="Isaac Asimov">
         Isaac Asimov
        </a>
        's 1957 novel
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Lucky_Starr_and_the_Moons_of_Jupiter" title="Lucky Starr and the Moons of Jupiter">
          Lucky Starr and the Moons of Jupiter
         </a>
        </i>
        , an experimental ship design is located on "Jupiter Nine". Asimov erroneously calls the moon "Adrastea", although in 1957 it had no official name and had been unofficially dubbed "Hades", while "Adrastea" was unofficially used for Jupiter XII (now called
        <a href="/wiki/Ananke_(moon)" title="Ananke (moon)">
         Ananke
        </a>
        ). Asimov's confusion may have arisen from the fact that, of the moons known in the 1950s, Jupiter IX was the twelfth most distant from Jupiter, and Jupiter XII was the ninth. To add to the confusion,
        <a href="/wiki/Adrastea_(moon)" title="Adrastea (moon)">
         Adrastea
        </a>
        is now used as the name of an inner satellite of Jupiter that was not discovered until 1979.
       </li>
       <li>
        In
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Exosquad" title="Exosquad">
          Exosquad
         </a>
        </i>
        (1993–1995), Sinope was the location of the Neosapiens' top-secret super-weapon,
        <i>
         Fusion Pulse Cannon
        </i>
        . After the Cannon has been destroyed by the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exofleet" title="Exofleet">
         Terrans
        </a>
        , Sinope was blown to
        <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
         asteroids
        </a>
        by the explosion and, thus, ceased to exist.
       </li>
      </ul>
