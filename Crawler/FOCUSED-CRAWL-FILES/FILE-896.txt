          /

        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          HEL
         </span>
         -ə-nee
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pronunciation_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-pronunciation-5">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el">
        Ἑλένη
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Pierre_Laques&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Pierre Laques (page does not exist)">
        Pierre Laques
       </a>
       and
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Jean_Lecacheux&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Jean Lecacheux (page does not exist)">
        Jean Lecacheux
       </a>
       in 1980 from ground-based observations at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pic_du_Midi_Observatory" title="Pic du Midi Observatory">
        Pic du Midi Observatory
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTELecacheux1980_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTELecacheux1980-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         S/1980 S 6
        </span>
        .
       </b>

        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3496-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1988 it was officially named after
       <a href="/wiki/Helen_of_Troy" title="Helen of Troy">
        Helen of Troy
       </a>
       , who was the granddaughter of
       <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">
        Cronus
       </a>
       (Saturn) in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_4609_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_4609-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Helene is also designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Saturn XII
        </span>
       </b>
       (12), which it was given in 1982, and
       <b>
        Dione B
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mythology_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-mythology-8">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
       because it is co-orbital with
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       and located in its leading
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point" title="Lagrangian point">
        Lagrangian point
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L4_and_L5" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         4
        </sub>
       </a>
       ). It is one of four known
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trojan_moon" title="Trojan moon">
        trojan moons
       </a>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Helene_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Helene was initially observed from Earth in 1980,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3496_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_3496-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager
        </a>
       </i>
       flybys of Saturn in the early 1980s allowed much closer views. The
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini–Huygens
        </a>
       </i>
       mission, which went into orbit around Saturn in 2004, provided still better views, and allowed more in-depth analysis of Helene, including views of the surface under different lighting conditions. Some of the closest images of Helene to date are from the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       spacecraft's 1800 km flyby on March 3, 2010, and another very successful imaging sequence occurred in June 2011. There have been many other approaches over the course of the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       mission.
      </p>
