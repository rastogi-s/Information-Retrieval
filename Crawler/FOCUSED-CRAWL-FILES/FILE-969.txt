      <p>
       <b>
        Halimede
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'h' in 'hi'">
            h
           </span>
           <span title="/æ/: 'a' in 'bad'">
            æ
           </span>
           <span title="'l' in 'lie'">
            l
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'m' in 'my'">
            m
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          HAL
         </span>
         -i-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          MEE
         </span>
         -dee
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <i>
        Αλιμήδη
       </i>
       ), or
       <b>
        Neptune IX
       </b>
       , is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_J._Holman" title="Matthew J. Holman">
        Matthew J. Holman
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_J._Kavelaars" title="John J. Kavelaars">
        John J. Kavelaars
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tommy_Grav" title="Tommy Grav">
        Tommy Grav
       </a>
       , Wesley C. Fraser and
       <a href="/wiki/Dan_Milisavljevic" title="Dan Milisavljevic">
        Dan Milisavljevic
       </a>
       on August 14, 2002.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Halimede has the second most
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentric
       </a>
       and third most
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       orbit around Neptune.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Williams2008-nssdc_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Williams2008-nssdc-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is illustrated on the diagram in relation to other irregular satellites of Neptune. The satellites above the horizontal axis are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prograde_and_retrograde_motion" title="Prograde and retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       , the satellites beneath it are retrograde. The yellow segments extend from the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        pericentre
       </a>
       to the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        apocentre
       </a>
       , showing the eccentricity.
      </p>
      <p>
       Halimede is about 62 kilometers in diameter (assuming an
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of 0.04)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewittKleyna2006_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewittKleyna2006-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and appears neutral (grey) in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Visible_light" title="Visible light">
        visible light
       </a>
       . Given the very similar colour of the satellite to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Nereid_(moon)" title="Nereid (moon)">
        Nereid
       </a>
       together with the high probability (41%
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-HolmanKavelaarsGrav2004-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) of collision in the past lifespan of the Solar System, it has been suggested that the satellite could be a fragment of Nereid.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GravHolmanFraser2004_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GravHolmanFraser2004-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Halimede, like many of the outer satellites of Neptune, is named after one of the Nereids, the fifty daughters of Nereus and Doris. Before the announcement of its name on February 3, 2007 (IAUC 8802), Halimede was known by the provisional designation S/2002 N 1.
      </p>
