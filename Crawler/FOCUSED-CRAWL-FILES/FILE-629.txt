      <p>
       <b>
        Lunarcrete
       </b>
       , also known as "
       <b>
        mooncrete
       </b>
       ", an idea first proposed by Larry A. Beyer of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Pittsburgh" title="University of Pittsburgh">
        University of Pittsburgh
       </a>
       in 1985, is a hypothetical aggregate building material, similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Concrete" title="Concrete">
        concrete
       </a>
       , formed from lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       , that would reduce the construction costs of building on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GFH_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GFH-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Ingredients">
        Ingredients
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunarcrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Ingredients">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Only comparatively small amounts of moon rock have been transported to Earth, so in 1988 researchers at the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_North_Dakota" title="University of North Dakota">
        University of North Dakota
       </a>
       proposed simulating the construction of such a material by using
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lignite_coal" title="Lignite coal">
        lignite coal
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Coal_ash" title="Coal ash">
        ash
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GFH_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GFH-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other researchers have used the subsequently developed
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_regolith_simulant" title="Lunar regolith simulant">
        lunar regolith simulant
       </a>
       materials, such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/JSC-1" title="JSC-1">
        JSC-1
       </a>
       (developed in 1994 and as used by Toutanji et al.).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some small-scale testing, with actual regolith, has been performed in laboratories, however.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruess_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ruess-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The basic ingredients for lunarcrete would be the same as those for terrestrial concrete: aggregate, water, and
       <a href="/wiki/Cement" title="Cement">
        cement
       </a>
       . In the case of lunarcrete, the aggregate would be lunar regolith. The cement would be manufactured by
       <a href="/wiki/Beneficiation" title="Beneficiation">
        beneficiating
       </a>
       lunar rock that had a high calcium content. Water would either be supplied from off the moon, or by combining oxygen with hydrogen produced from
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_soil" title="Lunar soil">
        lunar soil
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruess_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ruess-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lin et al. used 40g of the lunar regolith samples obtained by
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        Apollo 16
       </a>
       to produce lunarcrete in 1986.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lunarcrete was cured by using steam on a dry aggregate/cement mixture. Lin proposed that the water for such steam could be produced by mixing hydrogen with lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Ilmenite" title="Ilmenite">
        ilmenite
       </a>
       at 800 °C, to produce
       <a href="/wiki/Titanium_oxide" title="Titanium oxide">
        titanium oxide
       </a>
       , iron, and water. It was capable of withstanding compressive pressures of 75 MPa, and lost only 20% of that strength after repeated exposure to vacuum.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2008, Houssam Toutanji, of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Alabama_in_Huntsville" title="University of Alabama in Huntsville">
        University of Alabama in Huntsville
       </a>
       , and Richard Grugel, of the
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       , used a lunar soil simulant to determine whether lunarcrete could be made without water, using
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        sulfur
       </a>
       (obtainable from lunar dust) as the binding agent. The process to create this
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_concrete" title="Sulfur concrete">
        sulfur concrete
       </a>
       required heating the sulfur to 130–140 °C. After exposure to 50 cycles of temperature changes, from -27 °C to room temperature, the simulant lunarcrete was found to be capable of withstanding compressive pressures of 17MPa, which Toutanji and Grugel believed could be raised to 20MPa if the material were reinforced with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silica" title="Silica">
        silica
       </a>
       (also obtainable from lunar dust).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Casting_and_production">
        Casting and production
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunarcrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Casting and production">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There would need to be significant infrastructure in place before industrial scale production of lunarcrete could be possible.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruess_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ruess-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The casting of lunarcrete would require a pressurized environment, because attempting to cast in a vacuum would simply result in the water
       <a href="/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition)" title="Sublimation (phase transition)">
        sublimating
       </a>
       , and the lunarcrete failing to harden. Two solutions to this problem have been proposed: premixing the aggregate and the cement and then using a steam injection process to add the water, or the use of a pressurized concrete fabrication plant that produces pre-cast concrete blocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruess_2-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ruess-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunarcrete shares the same lack of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tensile_strength" title="Tensile strength">
        tensile strength
       </a>
       as terrestrial concrete. One suggested lunar equivalent tensioning material for creating
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-stressed_concrete" title="Pre-stressed concrete">
        pre-stressed concrete
       </a>
       is lunar glass, also formed from regolith, much as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibreglass" title="Fibreglass">
        fibreglass
       </a>
       is already sometimes used as a terrestrial concrete reinforcement material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ruess_2-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ruess-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another tensioning material, suggested by David Bennett, is
       <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">
        Kevlar
       </a>
       , imported from Earth (which would be cheaper, in terms of mass, to import from Earth than conventional steel).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Sulfur_based_.22Waterless_Concrete.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Sulfur_based_"Waterless_Concrete"'>
        Sulfur based "Waterless Concrete"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunarcrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title='Edit section: Sulfur based "Waterless Concrete"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This proposal is based on the observation that water is likely to be a precious commodity on the Moon. Also sulfur gains strength in a very short time and doesn't need any period of cooling, unlike hydraulic cement. This would reduce the time that human astronauts would need to be exposed to the surface lunar environment.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Sulfur is present on the moon in the form of the mineral troilite, (FeS)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casanova_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Casanova-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and could be reduced to obtain sulfur. It also doesn't require the ultra high temperatures needed for extraction of cementitious components (e.g.
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthosites
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur_concrete" title="Sulfur concrete">
        "Sulfur "concrete"
       </a>
       is an established construction material. Strictly speaking it isn't a concrete as there is little by way of chemical reaction. Instead the sulfur acts as a thermoplastic material binding with a non reactive substrate. Cement and water are not required. The concrete doesn't have to be cured, instead it is simply heated to above the melting point of sulfur, 140 °C, and after cooling it reaches high strength immediately.
      </p>
      <p>
       The best mixture for tensile and compressive strength is 65% JSC-1 lunar regolith simulant and 35% sulfur, with an average compressive strength of 33.8 MPa and tensile strength of 3.7 MPa. Addition of 2% metal fiber increase the compressive strength to 43.0 MPa
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Addition of silica also increases the strength of the concrete.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       This sulfur concrete could be of especial value for dust minimization, for instance to create a launching pad for rockets leaving the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casanova_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Casanova-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id="Issues_for_.22Sulfur_Concrete.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='Issues_for_"Sulfur_Concrete"'>
        Issues for "Sulfur Concrete"
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunarcrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title='Edit section: Issues for "Sulfur Concrete"'>
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       It provides less protection from cosmic radiation, so walls would need to be thicker than concrete walls (the water in concrete is an especially good absorber of cosmic radiation).
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Sulfur" title="Sulfur">
        Sulfur
       </a>
       melts at 115.2 °C, and lunar temperatures in high latitudes can reach 123 Celsius at midday. In addition, the temperature changes could change the volume of the sulfur concrete due to polymorphic transitions in the sulfur.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Casanova_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Casanova-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (see
       <a href="/wiki/Allotropes_of_sulfur" title="Allotropes of sulfur">
        Allotropes of sulfur
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       So unprotected sulfur concrete on the Moon, if directly exposed to the surface temperatures, would need to be limited to higher latitudes or shaded locations with maximum temperatures less than 96 °C and monthly variations not exceeding 114 °C.
      </p>
      <p>
       The material would degrade through repeated temperature cycles, but the effects are likely to be less extreme on the Moon due to the slowness of the monthly temperature cycle. The outer few millimeters may be damaged through sputtering from impact of high energy particles from the solar wind and solar flares. This may however be easy to repair, by reheating or recoating the surface layers in order to sinter away cracks and heal the damage.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Use">
        Use
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunarcrete&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Use">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       David Bennett, of the British Cement Association, argues that lunarcrete has the following advantages as a construction material for lunar bases:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Lunarcrete production would require less energy than lunar production of
        <a href="/wiki/Steel" title="Steel">
         steel
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
         aluminium
        </a>
        , or
        <a href="/wiki/Brick" title="Brick">
         brick
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        It is unaffected by temperature variations of +120 °C to −150 °C.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-4">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        It will absorb
        <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
         gamma rays
        </a>
        .
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-5">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Material integrity is not affected by prolonged exposure to vacuum. Although free water will evaporate from the material, the water that is chemically bound as a result of the curing process will not.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-6">
         <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
          [8]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       He observes, however, that lunarcrete is not an airtight material, and to make it airtight would require the application of an
       <a href="/wiki/Epoxy" title="Epoxy">
        epoxy
       </a>
       coating to the interior of any lunarcrete structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Bennett suggests that hypothetical lunar buildings made of lunarcrete would most likely use a low-grade concrete block for interior compartments and rooms, and a high-grade
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dense_silica_particle_cement&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dense silica particle cement (page does not exist)">
        dense silica particle cement
       </a>
       -based concrete for exterior skins.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bennett_8-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bennett-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
