      <p>
       <b>
        Moonrakers
       </b>
       is the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquial" title="Colloquial">
        colloquial
       </a>
       name for people from
       <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire" title="Wiltshire">
        Wiltshire
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/County" title="County">
        county
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/West_Country" title="West Country">
        West Country
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
        England
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legend">
        Legend
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonrakers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Legend">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This name refers to a folk story set in the time when
       <a href="/wiki/Smuggling" title="Smuggling">
        smuggling
       </a>
       was a significant industry in rural England, with Wiltshire lying on the smugglers' secret routes between the south coast and customers in the centre of the country.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The story goes that some local people had hidden
       <a href="/wiki/Contraband" title="Contraband">
        contraband
       </a>
       barrels of French
       <a href="/wiki/Brandy" title="Brandy">
        brandy
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/Customs_officer" title="Customs officer">
        customs officers
       </a>
       in a village pond. While trying to retrieve it at night, they were caught by the
       <a href="/wiki/HM_Customs_and_Excise" title="HM Customs and Excise">
        revenue
       </a>
       men, but explained themselves by pointing to the moon's reflection and saying they were trying to rake in a round
       <a href="/wiki/Cheese" title="Cheese">
        cheese
       </a>
       . The revenue men, thinking they were simple
       <a href="/wiki/Yokel" title="Yokel">
        yokels
       </a>
       , laughed at them and went on their way. But, as the story goes, it was the moonrakers who had the last laugh. In the words of Wiltshire shepherd William Little who recounted the story to writer
       <a href="/wiki/John_Yonge_Akerman" title="John Yonge Akerman">
        John Yonge Akerman
       </a>
       : “ Zo the excizeman ’as ax’d ’n the question ’ad his grin at ’n,…but they’d a good laugh at ’ee when ’em got whoame the stuff.”
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin">
        Origin
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Moonrakers&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origin">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The story dates to before 1787, when the Moonrakers tale appeared in
       <a href="/wiki/Francis_Grose" title="Francis Grose">
        Francis Grose
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Provincial Glossary
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Research by
       <a href="/wiki/Wiltshire_Council" title="Wiltshire Council">
        Wiltshire Council
       </a>
       's Community History Project shows that a claim can be made for the Crammer, a pond at Southbroom,
       <a href="/wiki/Devizes" title="Devizes">
        Devizes
       </a>
       , as the original location for the tale.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCC_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-WCC-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other accounts naming the village of
       <a href="/wiki/Bishops_Cannings" title="Bishops Cannings">
        Bishops Cannings
       </a>
       2½ miles to the north-east of Devizes, which has no pond, are explained by a change in the parish boundaries in 1835, which transferred the Crammer from that parish into the town.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-wgh_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-wgh-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WCC_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-WCC-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, many other places in the county have laid claim to the story.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
