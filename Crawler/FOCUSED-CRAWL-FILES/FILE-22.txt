    Supermoon
   </h1>

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        supermoon
       </b>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full moon
       </a>
       or a
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       that approximately coincides with the
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        closest
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        distance
       </a>
       that the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       reaches to
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       in its
       <a href="/wiki/Elliptic_orbit" title="Elliptic orbit">
        elliptic orbit
       </a>
       , resulting in a slightly larger-than-usual
       <a href="/wiki/Angular_diameter" title="Angular diameter">
        apparent size
       </a>
       of the lunar disk as seen from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT-20140907_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140907-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The technical name is the
       <b>
        perigee
        <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
         syzygy
        </a>
       </b>
       of the Earth–Moon–Sun system or more simply full (or new) Moon at perigee.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [a]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The term
       <i>
        supermoon
       </i>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrological
       </a>
       in origin, and has no precise
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomical
       </a>
       definition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discover_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Discover-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The real association of the Moon with both
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        oceanic
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustal_tide" title="Crustal tide">
        crustal tides
       </a>
       has led to claims that the supermoon phenomenon may be associated with increased risk of events like
       <a href="/wiki/Earthquake" title="Earthquake">
        earthquakes
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Volcanic_eruption" title="Volcanic eruption">
        volcanic eruptions
       </a>
       , but no such link has been found.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The opposite phenomenon, an
       <b>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apogee" title="Apogee">
         apogee
        </a>
        <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
         syzygy
        </a>
       </b>
       , has been called a
       <b>
        micromoon
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though this term is not as widespread as
       <i>
        supermoon
       </i>
       . Also, some do not consider new moons near perigee as supermoons.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Occurrence">
        Occurrence
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supermoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Occurrence">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Of the possible 12-13 full or new Moons each year, 3-4 usually may be classed as a supermoon as commonly defined.
      </p>
      <p>
       The most recent supermoon occurrence was on January 31, 2018, and the next one will be on December 22, 2018.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The one on November 14, 2016 was the closest supermoon since January 26, 1948, and will not be surpassed until November 25, 2034.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The closest supermoon of the
       <a href="/wiki/21st_century" title="21st century">
        century
       </a>
       will occur on December 6, 2052.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:802px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="328" data-file-width="1337" height="196" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png/800px-Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png/1200px-Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/24/Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png 2x" width="800"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Moon_distance_with_full_%26_new.png" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          Supermoons will be the marked points nearest the bottom of the graph.
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The oscillating nature of the distance of the full and new moons is due to the difference between the synodic and anomalistic
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_month" title="Lunar month">
        months
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Beat_(acoustics)" title="Beat (acoustics)">
        beat
       </a>
       period between the two is roughly 14 synodic months.
      </p>
      <p>
       Occasionally, a supermoon coincides with a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Total_lunar_eclipse" title="Total lunar eclipse">
        total lunar eclipse
       </a>
       . The most recent occurrence of this was in
       <a href="/wiki/January_2018_lunar_eclipse" title="January 2018 lunar eclipse">
        January 2018
       </a>
       , and the next will be in
       <a href="/wiki/January_2019_lunar_eclipse" title="January 2019 lunar eclipse">
        January 2019
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Appearance">
        Appearance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supermoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Appearance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3120" data-file-width="3808" height="180" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg/220px-Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg/330px-Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg/440px-Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Eclipse_and_Super_blue_blood_moon_31.01.2018_DSCN9664.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/January_2018_lunar_eclipse" title="January 2018 lunar eclipse">
          Supermoon lunar eclipse of January 2018
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       A full moon at perigee appears roughly 14% larger in diameter than at apogee.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many observers insist that the moon looks bigger to them. This is likely due to observations shortly after sunset when Moon is near the horizon and the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_illusion" title="Moon illusion">
        moon illusion
       </a>
       is at its most apparent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       While the moon's surface
       <a href="/wiki/Luminance" title="Luminance">
        luminance
       </a>
       remains the same, because it is closer to the earth the
       <a href="/wiki/Illuminance" title="Illuminance">
        illuminance
       </a>
       is about 30% brighter than at its farthest point, or apogee. This is due to the inverse square law of light which changes the amount of light received on earth in inverse proportion to the distance from the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PhillipsNASA2011_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-PhillipsNASA2011-12">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While a typical summer full moon at temperate latitudes provides only about 0.05-0.1
       <a href="/wiki/Lux" title="Lux">
        lux
       </a>
       , a supermoon directly overhead in the tropics could provide up to 0.36 lux.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definitions">
        Definitions
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supermoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Definitions">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The name
       <i>
        supermoon
       </i>
       was coined by
       <a href="/wiki/Astrology" title="Astrology">
        astrologer
       </a>
       Richard Nolle in 1979, in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Dell_Horoscope" title="Dell Horoscope">
         Dell Horoscope
        </a>
       </i>
       magazine arbitrarily defined as:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        ... a new or full moon which occurs with the Moon at or near (within 90% of) its closest approach to Earth in a given orbit (
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
         perigee
        </a>
        ). In short, Earth, Moon and Sun are all in a line, with Moon in its nearest approach to Earth.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         Richard Nolle
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-HolleSupermoon_14-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-HolleSupermoon-14">
           [13]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Nolle told
       <i>
        Atlantic
       </i>
       magazine that he created the term because “‘perigee syzygy’ is something that the average sixth-grader can’t handle, and that’s the average reading capability of the American adult,”
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though literacy tests in the United States consistently place the mean level at 9th grade or higher.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       He came up with the name while reading “Strategic Role Of Perigean Spring Tides in Nautical History and Coastal Flooding” published in 1976 by NOAA
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrology" title="Hydrology">
        Hydrologist
       </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">

        </a>

       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Discover_3-1">

      <p>
       In practice, there is no official or even consistent definition of how near perigee the full Moon must occur to receive the supermoon label, and new moons rarely receive a supermoon label.
       <a href="/wiki/Sky_%26_Telescope" title="Sky &amp; Telescope">
        Sky and Telescope
       </a>
       magazine refers to full Moon which comes within 223,000 miles (359,000 km), TimeandDate.com prefers a definition of 360,000 kilometres (220,000 mi). EarthSky uses Nolle's definition comparing their calculations to tables published by Nolle in 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The term
       <i>
        perigee-syzygy
       </i>
       or
       <i>
        perigee full/new moon
       </i>
       is preferred in the scientific community.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-23">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Perigee is the point at which the Moon is closest in its orbit to the Earth, and
       <a href="/wiki/Syzygy_(astronomy)" title="Syzygy (astronomy)">
        syzygy
       </a>
       is when the Earth, the Moon and the Sun are aligned, which happens at every
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new moon
       </a>
       . Astrophysicist
       <a href="/wiki/Fred_Espenak" title="Fred Espenak">
        Fred Espenak
       </a>
       uses Nolle's definition but preferring the label of
       <i>
        full Moon at perigee
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:0-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Wood used the definition of a full or new moon occurring within 24 hours of perigee and also used the label
       <i>
        perigee-syzygy
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Wood also coined the less used term
       <i>
        proxigee
       </i>
       where perigee and the full or new moon are separated by 10 hours or less.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-:1-18">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Effects_on_Earth">
        Effects on Earth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Supermoon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Effects on Earth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Nolle also claimed that the moon causes "geophysical stress" during the time of a supermoon. There has also been media speculation that natural disasters, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/2011_T%C5%8Dhoku_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami">
        2011 Tōhoku earthquake and tsunami
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/2004_Indian_Ocean_earthquake_and_tsunami" title="2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami">
        2004 Indian Ocean earthquake and tsunami
       </a>
       , are causally linked with the 1–2 week period surrounding a supermoon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A large, 7.5 magnitude
       <a href="/wiki/2016_Kaikoura_earthquake" title="2016 Kaikoura earthquake">
        earthquake centred 15 km north-east of Culverden, New Zealand
       </a>
       at 00:03
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/NZDT" title="NZDT">
        NZDT
       </a>
       on November 14, 2016, also coincided with a supermoon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These claims have been refuted by scientists.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Scientists have confirmed that the combined effect of the Sun and Moon on the Earth's oceans, the
       <a href="/wiki/Tide" title="Tide">
        tide
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plait2008_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plait2008-32">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Tide#Range_variation:_springs_and_neaps" title="Tide">
        greatest
       </a>
       when the Moon is either
       <a href="/wiki/New_moon" title="New moon">
        new
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Full_moon" title="Full moon">
        full
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TideAnimation_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-TideAnimation-33">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and that during lunar perigee, the tidal force is somewhat stronger,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MoonConnection_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MoonConnection-34">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       resulting in
       <a href="/wiki/Perigean_spring_tide" title="Perigean spring tide">
        perigean spring tides
       </a>
       . However, even at its most powerful, this force is still relatively weak,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plait2011_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plait2011-35">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       causing tidal differences of inches at most.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
