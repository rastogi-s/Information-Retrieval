      <p>
       "
       <b>
        Blue Moon of Kentucky
       </b>
       " is a
       <a href="/wiki/Waltz" title="Waltz">
        waltz
       </a>
       written in 1946 by
       <a href="/wiki/Bluegrass_music" title="Bluegrass music">
        bluegrass
       </a>
       musician
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Monroe" title="Bill Monroe">
        Bill Monroe
       </a>
       and recorded by his band, the Blue Grass Boys. The song has since been recorded by many artists, including
       <a href="/wiki/Elvis_Presley" title="Elvis Presley">
        Elvis Presley
       </a>
       . In 2003, the song was chosen to be added to the United States Library of Congress
       <a href="/wiki/National_Recording_Registry" title="National Recording Registry">
        National Recording Registry
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_of_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       "Blue Moon" is the official bluegrass song of
       <a href="/wiki/Kentucky" title="Kentucky">
        Kentucky
       </a>
       . In 2002, Monroe's version was one of 50 recordings chosen that year by the
       <a href="/wiki/Library_of_Congress" title="Library of Congress">
        Library of Congress
       </a>
       to be added to the
       <a href="/wiki/National_Recording_Registry" title="National Recording Registry">
        National Recording Registry
       </a>
       . In 2003,
       <a href="/wiki/CMT_(U.S._TV_channel)" title="CMT (U.S. TV channel)">
        CMT
       </a>
       ranked "Blue Moon" number 11 in its list of 100 Greatest Songs in Country Music.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Bill_Monroe">
        Bill Monroe
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_of_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Bill Monroe">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Bill Monroe wrote the song in 1946, recording it for
       <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">
        Columbia Records
       </a>
       on September 16. It was released in early 1947.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time, the Bluegrass Boys included vocalist and guitarist
       <a href="/wiki/Lester_Flatt" title="Lester Flatt">
        Lester Flatt
       </a>
       and banjoist
       <a href="/wiki/Earl_Scruggs" title="Earl Scruggs">
        Earl Scruggs
       </a>
       , who later formed their own bluegrass band, the
       <a href="/wiki/Foggy_Mountain_Boys" title="Foggy Mountain Boys">
        Foggy Mountain Boys
       </a>
       . Both Flatt and Scruggs performed on the recording, although Bill Monroe supplied the vocals on this song.
      </p>
      <p>
       The song, described as a "bluegrass waltz", had become a United States wide hit by 1947
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naylor_39_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Naylor_39-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and also became enormously popular with other bluegrass, country, and early
       <a href="/wiki/Rockabilly" title="Rockabilly">
        rockabilly
       </a>
       acts. The song was revered at the
       <a href="/wiki/Grand_Ole_Opry" title="Grand Ole Opry">
        Grand Ole Opry
       </a>
       and others;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Naylor_39_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Naylor_39-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Carl_Perkins" title="Carl Perkins">
        Carl Perkins
       </a>
       played an uptempo version of this song in his early live performances.
      </p>

      <p>
       The search for another song to release along with "
       <a href="/wiki/That%27s_All_Right" title="That's All Right">
        That's All Right
       </a>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Sun_Records" title="Sun Records">
        Sun Records
       </a>
       in July 1954 led to "Blue Moon of Kentucky" via
       <a href="/wiki/Bill_Black" title="Bill Black">
        Bill Black
       </a>
       . According to
       <a href="/wiki/Scotty_Moore" title="Scotty Moore">
        Scotty Moore
       </a>
       :
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        We all of us knew we needed something...and things seemed hopeless after a while. Bill is the one who came up with "Blue Moon of Kentucky"...We're taking a little break and he starts beating on the bass and singing "Blue Moon of Kentucky", mocking Bill Monroe, singing the high falsetto voice. Elvis joins in with him, starts playing and singing along with him.
       </p>
       <div class="templatequotecite">
        —
        <cite>
         <i>
          The Blue Moon Boys - The Story of Elvis Presley's Band
         </i>
         ,
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
          <a href="#cite_note-4">
           [4]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </cite>
       </div>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       Presley, Moore, and Black, with the encouragement of Sam Phillips, transformed Monroe's slow waltz, in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3/4_time" title="3/4 time">
        3/4 time
       </a>
       , into an upbeat, blues-flavored tune in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/4/4_time" title="4/4 time">
        4/4 time
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       After an early rendition of the song, Sun Records owner
       <a href="/wiki/Sam_Phillips" title="Sam Phillips">
        Sam Phillips
       </a>
       exclaimed, "BOY, that's fine, that's fine. That's a POP song now!."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As with all of the Presley records issued by Sun, the artists were listed and stylized as "ELVIS PRESLEY SCOTTY and BILL".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The same night that Dewey Phillips first played the flip side of this first release of Presley's music on WHBQ, "That's All Right", Sleepy Eye John at WHHM loosed "Blue Moon of Kentucky". Bob Neal of WMPS played the record, too. The pop jockeys, entranced by something new, began slipping "That's All Right" and "Blue Moon of Kentucky" in among the easy-listening pop of
       <a href="/wiki/Teresa_Brewer" title="Teresa Brewer">
        Teresa Brewer
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Nat_King_Cole" title="Nat King Cole">
        Nat Cole
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Tony_Bennett" title="Tony Bennett">
        Tony Bennett
       </a>
       , and others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scottymoore.net_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-scottymoore.net-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       With Presley's version of Monroe's song consistently rated higher, both sides began to chart across the Southern United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Billboard
       </i>
       has the song listed only in Memphis, and as number six with "That's All Right" at number 7 on October 9 in the C&amp;W Territorial Best Sellers.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       By October 23, "Blue Moon" was in the top 10 in Memphis, Nashville, and New Orleans, with "That's All Right" absent from the listings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Fellow Sun Records artist
       <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Feathers" title="Charlie Feathers">
        Charlie Feathers
       </a>
       has often claimed that he came up with the arrangement of the song used by Presley. While others sources claimed that it was Presley who arranged the song.
      </p>
      <p>
       The song was later used in a scene of the 2005 TV miniseries
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elvis_(TV_series)" title="Elvis (TV series)">
         Elvis
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Other_recordings">
        Other recordings
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Blue_Moon_of_Kentucky&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Other recordings">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       In 1954,
       <a href="/wiki/The_Stanley_Brothers" title="The Stanley Brothers">
        the Stanley Brothers
       </a>
       recorded a version of the song using Presley's 4/4 arrangement with bluegrass instrumentation, neatly bridging the stylistic gap between Monroe and Presley's approaches. Bill Monroe subsequently re-recorded and performed the song using a mixture of the two styles, starting the song in its original 3/4 time arrangement, then launching into an uptempo 4/4 rendition.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Patsy_Cline" title="Patsy Cline">
        Patsy Cline
       </a>
       recorded "Blue Moon" in 1963.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones1999_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jones1999-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1968,
       <a href="/wiki/Al_Kooper" title="Al Kooper">
        Al Kooper
       </a>
       recorded a version for his debut solo album
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/I_Stand_Alone_(Al_Kooper_album)" title="I Stand Alone (Al Kooper album)">
         I Stand Alone
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2003, the Psychobilly/Rockabilly band "Zombie Ghost Train covered the song on their album Monster Formal Wear
      </p>
