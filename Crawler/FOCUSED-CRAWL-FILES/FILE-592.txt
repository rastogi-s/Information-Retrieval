   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Planetary Observer program
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">

    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Planetary Observer program
       </b>
       is a cancelled
       <a href="/wiki/Space_exploration" title="Space exploration">
        space exploration
       </a>
       program designed by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       to provide cheaper planetary orbiters by using Earth-orbiting satellite components and technology, using solar panels for power, and a common spacecraft bus platform for all Planetary Observer-class probes. Only one spacecraft of this class was eventually constructed—the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mars_Observer" title="Mars Observer">
         Mars Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_Observer_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       After the flagship multibillion-dollar missions of the 1970s, in the 1980s NASA was looking for a new, more affordable direction for the 1990s and beyond. Two projects were conceived by NASA's Solar System Exploration Committee in 1983, the Planetary Observer program and
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mariner_Mark_II" title="Mariner Mark II">
         Mariner Mark II
        </a>
       </i>
       . The Observer program, starting with
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Mars_Observer" title="Mars Observer">
         Mars Observer
        </a>
       </i>
       , was envisioned as a series of low-cost missions to the inner Solar System, based on commercial Earth satellites. The Mariner Mark II, on the other hand, was to be a series of large spacecraft for the exploration of the outer Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first Planetary Observer spacecraft to be approved was
       <i>
        Mars Observer
       </i>
       , in 1985.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Lunar Observer
       </i>
       (LO), proposed for a 1997 launch,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       would have been sent into a long-term lunar orbit at 60 miles above the Moon's poles. The Mercury Observer (MO) was also proposed for a 1997 launch. However, Congressionally imposed reductions to FY 1992-93 funding requirements forced NASA to terminate the Planetary Observer program, with just
       <i>
        Mars Observer
       </i>
       funded.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mars_Observer">
        <i>
         Mars Observer
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_Observer_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Mars Observer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Observer" title="Mars Observer">
        Mars Observer
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       <i>
        Mars Observer
       </i>
       was an unmanned spacecraft designed to study the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geoscience" title="Geoscience">
        geoscience
       </a>
       and climate of
       <a href="/wiki/Mars" title="Mars">
        Mars
       </a>
       . The first of the proposed Observer series of planetary missions, it was launched by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       on September 25, 1992. Three days before
       <i>
        Mars Observer
       </i>
       was scheduled to enter the orbit of Mars, contact with the spacecraft was lost. Attempts to re-establish communication with the spacecraft were unsuccessful.
      </p>
      <p>
       A few photographs were indeed taken, and these were the bulk of data collected.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Lunar_Observer">
        <i>
         Lunar Observer
        </i>
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Planetary_Observer_program&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Lunar Observer">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <i>
        Lunar Observer
       </i>
       program was started with an estimated budget of US$500–700 million. The proposed orbit was 70 km (43 mi) above the surface. The
       <i>
        Lunar Observer
       </i>
       spacecraft garnered some attention from the Soviet Union, and there was a suggestion that they might cooperate with NASA to field some instruments for it.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        Lunar Observer
       </i>
       was proposed for FY1991 at US$188 million by President George H.W. Bush.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
