      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Martian Moons Exploration
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        MMX
       </b>
       ) is a robotic space probe set for launch in 2024 to bring back the first samples from Mars' largest moon
       <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
        Phobos
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homepage_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Homepage-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Developed by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Japanese_Aerospace_Exploration_Agency" title="Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency">
        Japanese Aerospace Exploration Agency
       </a>
       (JAXA) and announced in 9 June 2015, MMX will land and collect samples from Phobos once or twice, along with conducting
       <a href="/wiki/Deimos_(moon)" title="Deimos (moon)">
        Deimos
       </a>
       flyby observations and monitoring Mars' climate.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The mission aims to provide key information to help determine whether the Martian moons are captured asteroids or the result of a larger body hitting Mars.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview">
        Overview
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martian_Moons_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Overview">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_colour_2008.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3300" data-file-width="3374" height="215" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Phobos_colour_2008.jpg/220px-Phobos_colour_2008.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Phobos_colour_2008.jpg/330px-Phobos_colour_2008.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Phobos_colour_2008.jpg/440px-Phobos_colour_2008.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phobos_colour_2008.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Phobos_(moon)" title="Phobos (moon)">
          Phobos
         </a>
         , the largest moon of Mars
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The spacecraft will enter orbit around Mars, then transfer to Phobos,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_Clark_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-S_Clark-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and land once or twice and gather sand-like regolith particles using a simple
       <a href="/wiki/Pneumatics" title="Pneumatics">
        pneumatic
       </a>
       system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNES_col_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CNES_col-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The lander mission aims to retrieve a minimum 10 g (0.35 oz) of samples.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sample_mass_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-sample_mass-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujimoto20170111_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-fujimoto20170111-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The spacecraft will then take off from Phobos and make several flybys of the smaller moon Deimos before sending the Sample Return Capsule back to Earth, arriving in July 2029.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_Clark_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-S_Clark-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homepage_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Homepage-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission leader is Yasuhiro Kawakatsu.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborations">
        Collaborations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martian_Moons_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Collaborations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ESA" title="ESA">
        ESA
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">
        CNES
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       are also participating in the project, and will provide scientific instruments.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The U.S. will contribute a neutron and
       <a href="/wiki/Gamma-ray_spectrometer" title="Gamma-ray spectrometer">
        gamma-ray spectrometer
       </a>
       called MEGANE (an acronym, which also means "eyeglasses" in Japanese, for Mars-moon Exploration with GAmma rays and NEutrons),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S_Clark_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-S_Clark-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and France (
       <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">
        CNES
       </a>
       ) the Near IR Spectrometer (NIRS4/MacrOmega).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujimoto20170111_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-fujimoto20170111-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       France is also contributing expertise in flight dynamics to plan the mission's orbiting and landing manoeuvres.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNES_col_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-CNES_col-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Development and testing of key components, including the sampler, is ongoing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As of 2017, MMX is scheduled to be launched in 2024, and will return to Earth five years later.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-fujimoto20170111_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-fujimoto20170111-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_payload">
        Scientific payload
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martian_Moons_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific payload">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The scientific payload consists on Japanese and international contributions. They will be powered by
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">
        solar arrays
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Homepage_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Homepage-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Gamma_ray" title="Gamma ray">
         Gamma ray
        </a>
        and Neutron Spectrometer (MEGANE) - developed by NASA, USA
       </li>
       <li>
        Wide Angle Multiband Camera (WAM)
       </li>
       <li>
        Near-Infrared Spectrometer (MacrOmega) - developed by
        <a href="/wiki/CNES" title="CNES">
         CNES
        </a>
        , France.
       </li>
       <li>
        Telescopic Camera (TL)
       </li>
       <li>
        Light Detection and Ranging (LIDAR)
       </li>
       <li>
        Circum-Martian Dust Monitor (CMDM)
       </li>
       <li>
        Mass Spectrum Analyzer (MSA)
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       The CNES team is also conducting a study of the feasibility of including a small rover.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CNES_col_7-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-CNES_col-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Sampling">
        Sampling
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Martian_Moons_Exploration&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Sampling">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       For sample collection, the mission opted to use an air gun to puff pressurised gas, pushing about 10 grams of soil into the sample container.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is a similar principle to the one
       <a href="/wiki/OSIRIS-REx" title="OSIRIS-REx">
        OSIRIS-REx
