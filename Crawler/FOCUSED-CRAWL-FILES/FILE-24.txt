      <p>
       The
       <b>
        far side of the Moon
       </b>
       , sometimes figuratively known as the
       <b>
        dark side of the moon
       </b>
       , is the
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere#Hemisphere" title="Sphere">
        hemisphere
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       that always faces away from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       . The far side's terrain is rugged, with a multitude of
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact craters
       </a>
       and relatively few flat
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       . It has one of the
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_largest_craters_in_the_Solar_System" title="List of largest craters in the Solar System">
        largest craters in the Solar System
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin" title="South Pole–Aitken basin">
        South Pole–Aitken basin
       </a>
       . Although both sides of the moon experience two weeks of sunlight followed by two weeks of night, the far side is sometimes called the "dark side of the Moon," with "dark" meaning "unseen" rather than lack of light.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Sigurdsson_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Sigurdsson-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-O'Conner_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-O'Conner-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Messer_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Messer-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Falin_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Falin-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       About 18% of the far side is occasionally visible from Earth due to
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       . The remaining 82% remained unobserved until 1959, when the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        space probe
       </a>
       photographed it. The
       <a href="/wiki/Russian_Academy_of_Sciences" title="Russian Academy of Sciences">
        Soviet Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       published the first
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas" title="Atlas">
        atlas
       </a>
       of the far side in 1960. In 1968, the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       mission's
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       were the first humans to view this region directly when they orbited the Moon. To date, no human being has ever stood on the surface of the far side of the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        Astronomers
       </a>
       have suggested installing a large
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">
        radio telescope
       </a>
       on the far side, where the Moon would shield it from possible radio interference from Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Definition">
        Definition
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_side_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Definition">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Tidal forces from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       have slowed down the Moon's rotation to the point where the same side is always facing the Earth--a phenomenon called
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal locking
       </a>
       . The other face, most of which is never visible from the Earth, is therefore called the "far side of the Moon". Over time some parts of the far side can be seen due to
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In total 59 percent of the Moon's surface is visible from Earth at one time or another. Useful observation of the parts of the far side of the Moon occasionally visible from Earth is difficult because of the low viewing angle from Earth (they cannot be observed "full on").
      </p>
      <p>
       The idiomatic phrase "dark side of the Moon" does not refer to "dark" as in the absence of light, but rather "dark" as in unknown: until humans were able to send spacecraft around the Moon, this area had never been seen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Sigurdsson_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Sigurdsson-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-O'Conner_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-O'Conner-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Messer_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Messer-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Etymology-Falin_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Etymology-Falin-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       While many misconstrue this to think that the "dark side" receives little to no sunlight, in reality, both the near and far sides receive (on average) almost equal amounts of light directly from the Sun. However, the near side also receives sunlight reflected from the Earth, known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earthshine" title="Earthshine">
        earthshine
       </a>
       . Earthshine does not reach the area of the far side which cannot be seen from Earth. Only during a full moon (as viewed from Earth) is the whole far side of the Moon dark. The word "dark" has expanded to also refer to the fact that communication with spacecraft can be blocked while on the far side of the Moon, during Apollo space missions for example.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dark_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-dark-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The two hemispheres have distinctly different appearances, with the near side covered in multiple, large
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        <i>
         maria
        </i>
       </a>
       (Latin for 'seas,' since the earliest astronomers incorrectly thought that these plains were seas of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        lunar water
       </a>
       ). The far side has a battered, densely cratered appearance with few maria. Only 1% of the surface of the far side is covered by maria,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley196604-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       compared to 31.2% on the near side. One commonly accepted explanation for this difference is related to a higher concentration of heat-producing elements on the near-side hemisphere, as has been demonstrated by geochemical maps obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       gamma-ray spectrometer. While other factors such as surface elevation and crustal thickness could also affect where basalts erupt, these do not explain why the farside
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin" title="South Pole–Aitken basin">
        South Pole–Aitken basin
       </a>
       (which contains the lowest elevations of the Moon and possesses a thin crust) was not as volcanically active as
       <a href="/wiki/Oceanus_Procellarum" title="Oceanus Procellarum">
        Oceanus Procellarum
       </a>
       on the near side.
      </p>
      <p>
       It has also been proposed that the differences between the two hemispheres may have been caused by a collision with a smaller companion moon that also originated from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis" title="Giant impact hypothesis">
        Theia collision
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In this model the impact led to an accretionary pile rather than a crater, contributing a hemispheric layer of extent and thickness that may be consistent with the dimensions of the farside highlands.
      </p>
      <p>
       The far side has more visible craters. This was thought to be a result of the effects of lunar lava flows, which cover and obscure craters, rather than a shielding effect from the Earth. NASA calculates that the Earth obscures only about 4 square degrees out of 41,000 square degrees of the sky as seen from the Moon. "This makes the Earth negligible as a shield for the Moon. . . . It is likely that each side of the Moon has received equal numbers of impacts, but the resurfacing by lava results in fewer craters visible on the near side than the far side, even though the [sic] both sides have received the same number of impacts."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Newer research suggests that the reason the side of the moon facing Earth has fewer impact craters is heat from Earth at the time when the Moon was formed. The lunar crust consists primarily of
       <a href="/wiki/Plagioclase" title="Plagioclase">
        plagioclases
       </a>
       formed when
       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
        aluminium
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Calcium" title="Calcium">
        calcium
       </a>
       condensed and combined with
       <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">
        silicates
       </a>
       in the mantle. The cooler far side experienced condensation of these elements sooner and so formed a thicker crust; meteoroid impacts on the near side would sometimes penetrate the thinner crust here and release basaltic lava that created the maria, but would rarely do so on the far side.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_side_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Luna_3_moon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="384" data-file-width="500" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Luna_3_moon.jpg/220px-Luna_3_moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Luna_3_moon.jpg/330px-Luna_3_moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Luna_3_moon.jpg/440px-Luna_3_moon.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Luna_3_moon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The first image returned by Luna 3 showed the far side of the Moon
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Until the late 1950s, little was known about the far side of the Moon.
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        Librations
       </a>
       of the Moon periodically allowed limited glimpses of features near the lunar limb on the far side. These features, however, were seen from a low angle, hindering useful observation. (It proved difficult to distinguish a crater from a mountain range.) The remaining 82% of the surface on the far side remained unknown, and its properties were subject to much speculation.
      </p>
      <p>
       An example of a far side feature that can be seen through libration is the
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Orientale" title="Mare Orientale">
        Mare Orientale
       </a>
       , which is a prominent impact basin spanning almost 1,000 kilometres (600 mi), yet this was not even named as a feature until 1906, by
       <a href="/wiki/Julius_Heinrich_Franz" title="Julius Heinrich Franz">
        Julius Franz
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        Der Mond
       </i>
       . The true nature of the basin was discovered in the 1960s when rectified images were projected onto a globe. The basin was photographed in fine detail by
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_4" title="Lunar Orbiter 4">
        Lunar Orbiter 4
       </a>
       in 1967.
      </p>
      <p>
       Before space exploration began, astronomers did not expect that the far side would be different from the side visible to Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley196604-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On October 7, 1959, the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       probe
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_3" title="Luna 3">
        Luna 3
       </a>
       took the first photographs of the lunar far side, eighteen of them resolvable,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_9-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley196604-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       covering one-third of the surface invisible from the Earth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSE_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-GSE-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The images were analysed, and the first atlas of the far side of the Moon was published by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR_Academy_of_Sciences" title="USSR Academy of Sciences">
        USSR Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       on November 6, 1960.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It included a catalog of 500 distinguished features of the landscape.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A year later the first globe (1:13 600 000
       <a href="/wiki/Scale_(map)" title="Scale (map)">
        scale
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAI_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SAI-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) containing lunar features invisible from the Earth was released in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/USSR" title="USSR">
        USSR
       </a>
       , based on images from Luna 3.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On July 20, 1965 another Soviet probe,
       <a href="/wiki/Zond_3" title="Zond 3">
        Zond 3
       </a>
       , transmitted 25 pictures of very good quality of the lunar far side,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with much better resolution than those from Luna 3. In particular, they revealed chains of craters, hundreds of kilometers in length,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSE_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-GSE-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but, unexpectedly, no mare plains like those visible from Earth with the naked eye.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ley196604_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-ley196604-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1967 the second part of the "Atlas of the Far Side of the Moon" was published in
       <a href="/wiki/Moscow" title="Moscow">
        Moscow
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       based on data from Zond 3, with the catalog now including 4,000 newly discovered features of the lunar far side landscape.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GSE_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-GSE-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the same year the first "Complete Map of the Moon" (1:5 000 000 scale
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAI_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SAI-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) and updated complete globe (1:10 000 000 scale), featuring 95 percent of the lunar surface,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SAI_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SAI-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       were released in the Soviet Union.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As many prominent landscape features of the far side were discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet
       </a>
       space probes, Soviet scientists selected names for them. This caused some controversy, and the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       , leaving many of those names intact, later assumed the role of naming lunar features on this hemisphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       On April 26, 1962, NASA's
       <a href="/wiki/Ranger_4" title="Ranger 4">
        Ranger 4
       </a>
       space probe became the first spacecraft to impact the far side of the Moon, although it failed to return any scientific data before impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The first truly comprehensive and detailed mapping survey of the far side was undertaken by the American unmanned
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_program" title="Lunar Orbiter program">
        Lunar Orbiter program
       </a>
       launched by NASA from 1966 to 1967. Most of the coverage of the far side was provided by the final probe in the series,
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_5" title="Lunar Orbiter 5">
        Lunar Orbiter 5
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       The far side was first seen directly by
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        human
       </a>
       eyes during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       mission in 1968.
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        Astronaut
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">
        William Anders
       </a>
       described the view:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        The backside looks like a sand pile my kids have played in for some time. It's all beat up, no definition, just a lot of bumps and holes.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       It has been seen by all crew members of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_10" title="Apollo 10">
        Apollo 10
       </a>
       through
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       missions since that time, and photographed by multiple lunar probes. Spacecraft passing behind the Moon were out of direct
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       communication with the Earth, and had to wait until the orbit allowed transmission. During the Apollo missions, the main engine of the Service Module was fired when the vessel was behind the Moon, producing some tense moments in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyndon_B._Johnson_Space_Center" title="Lyndon B. Johnson Space Center">
        Mission Control
       </a>
       before the craft reappeared.
      </p>
      <p>
       Geologist-astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (December 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       who became the last to step onto the Moon, had aggressively lobbied for his landing site to be on the far side of the Moon, targeting the lava-filled crater
       <a href="/wiki/Tsiolkovskiy_(crater)" title="Tsiolkovskiy (crater)">
        Tsiolkovskiy
       </a>
       . Schmitt's ambitious proposal included a special communications satellite based on the existing TIROS satellites to be launched into a
       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">
        Farquhar–Lissajous halo orbit
       </a>
       around the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2" title="Lagrange point">
        L2 point
       </a>
       so as to maintain line-of-sight contact with the astronauts during their powered descent and lunar surface operations. NASA administrators rejected these plans on the grounds of added risk and lack of funding.
      </p>
      <p>
       The China National Space Administration plans for its
       <a href="/wiki/Chang%27e_4" title="Chang'e 4">
        Chang'e 4
       </a>
       mission to achieve the first landing on the lunar far side. In May 2015, Chief lunar exploration engineer Wu Weiren told China Central Television, "We are currently discussing the next moon landing site for Chang’e 4 (...) Our next move probably will see some spacecraft land on the far side of the moon."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In September 2015, the Xinhua news agency confirmed that Chang'e 4 would attempt a far side landing before 2020, equipped with a low frequency radio spectrograph and geological research tools.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Meanwhile, the plans have assumed concrete form, with a planned launch in late 2018 and several landing sites proposed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Potential">
        Potential
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_side_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Potential">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1035" data-file-width="918" height="248" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg/220px-Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg/330px-Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/49/Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg/440px-Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Craters_of_the_Far_Side_of_the_Moon.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Some of the features of the geography are labeled in this image.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Because the far side of the Moon is shielded from radio transmissions from the Earth, it is considered a good location for placing
       <a href="/wiki/Radio_telescope" title="Radio telescope">
        radio telescopes
       </a>
       for use by
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomers
       </a>
       . Small, bowl-shaped craters provide a natural formation for a stationary
       <a href="/wiki/Telescope" title="Telescope">
        telescope
       </a>
       similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Arecibo_Observatory" title="Arecibo Observatory">
        Arecibo
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Puerto_Rico" title="Puerto Rico">
        Puerto Rico
       </a>
       . For much larger-scale telescopes, the 100-kilometer (62 mi) diameter
       <a href="/wiki/Daedalus_(crater)" title="Daedalus (crater)">
        crater Daedalus
       </a>
       is situated near the center of the far side, and the 3 km (2 mi)-high rim would help to block stray communications from orbiting satellites. Another potential candidate for a radio telescope is the
       <a href="/wiki/Saha_(crater)" title="Saha (crater)">
        Saha crater
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cnn_stenger_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-cnn_stenger-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Before deploying radio telescopes to the far side, several problems must be overcome. The fine lunar dust can contaminate equipment, vehicles, and space suits. The conducting materials used for the radio dishes must also be carefully shielded against the effects of
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       . Finally the area around the telescopes must be protected against contamination by other radio sources.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lagrange_point" title="Lagrange point">
        Lagrange point
       </a>
       of the Earth–Moon system is located about 62,800 km (39,000 mi) above the far side, which has also been proposed as a location for a future radio telescope which would perform a
       <a href="/wiki/Lissajous_orbit" title="Lissajous orbit">
        Lissajous orbit
       </a>
       about the Lagrangian point.
      </p>
      <p>
       One of the
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       missions to the Moon under study would send a sample-return lander to the
       <a href="/wiki/South_Pole%E2%80%93Aitken_basin" title="South Pole–Aitken basin">
        South Pole–Aitken basin
       </a>
       , the location of a major impact event that created a formation nearly 2,400 kilometers (1,491 mi) across. The force of this impact has created a deep penetration into the lunar surface, and a sample returned from this site could be analyzed for information concerning the interior of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
        <a href="#cite_note-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because the near side is partly shielded from the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       by the Earth, the far side
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        maria
       </a>
       are expected to have the highest concentration of
       <a href="/wiki/Helium-3" title="Helium-3">
        helium-3
       </a>
       on the surface of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This
       <a href="/wiki/Isotope" title="Isotope">
        isotope
       </a>
       is relatively rare on the Earth, but has good potential for use as a fuel in
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_fusion" title="Nuclear fusion">
        fusion
       </a>
       reactors. Proponents of lunar settlement have cited the presence of this material as a reason for developing a Moon base.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jack_Schmitt's_Moon_base_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jack_Schmitt's_Moon_base-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Alleged_UFO_sightings_and_conspiracies">
        Alleged UFO sightings and conspiracies
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Far_side_of_the_Moon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alleged UFO sightings and conspiracies">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Some conspiracy theorists, notably
       <a href="/wiki/Milton_William_Cooper" title="Milton William Cooper">
        Milton William Cooper
       </a>
       , have alleged that some Apollo astronauts had seen
       <a href="/wiki/Unidentified_flying_object" title="Unidentified flying object">
        UFOs
       </a>
       on the far side of the Moon but were told to keep quiet about them.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
        <a href="#cite_note-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some have allegedly reported seeing an alien base (code named "Luna") and even encountered aliens who told them to stay off the Moon. Some photographs circulated on the Internet purport to show a large "castle" on the Moon. NASA states that these claims are hoaxes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
        <a href="#cite_note-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The late
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secretary_of_Defense" title="Secretary of Defense">
        Secretary of Defense
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_McNamara" title="Robert McNamara">
        Robert McNamara
       </a>
       stated that several unspecified officials ("Chiefs") within
       <a href="/wiki/The_Pentagon" title="The Pentagon">
        The Pentagon
       </a>
       were opposed to a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nuclear_Test_Ban_Treaty" title="Nuclear Test Ban Treaty">
        Nuclear Test Ban Treaty
       </a>
       between the
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        United States
       </a>
       and the
       <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">
        Soviet Union
       </a>
       , on the premise that the Soviets would continue
       <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_weapons_testing" title="Nuclear weapons testing">
        nuclear weapons testing
       </a>
       on the far side of the Moon, far from the observations of American observers. McNamara considered this premise "...absurd" and that “…[they were] out of [their] minds”, but he believed that it was an example of the state of mind of some Pentagon officials during the
       <a href="/wiki/Cold_War" title="Cold War">
        Cold War
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ironically, it was later revealed that the Pentagon had their own plan to detonate a nuclear weapon on the moon as part of the experiment
       <a href="/wiki/Project_A119" title="Project A119">
        Project A119
       </a>
       . The project was created not only to help in answering some of the mysteries in planetary
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomy" title="Astronomy">
        astronomy
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astrogeology" title="Astrogeology">
        astrogeology
       </a>
       , but also as a
       <a href="/wiki/Show_of_force" title="Show of force">
        show of force
       </a>
       intended to boost domestic confidence in the astro-capabilities of the United States, a boost that was needed after the Soviet Union took an early lead in the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Race" title="Space Race">
        Space Race
       </a>
       and who were thought by some to be working on a similar project.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
