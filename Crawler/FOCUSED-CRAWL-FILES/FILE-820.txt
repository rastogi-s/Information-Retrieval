      <p>
       <b>
        Sinope
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         si-
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          NOH
         </span>
         -pee
        </i>
       </a>
       ;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_language" title="Greek language">
        Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="el" xml:lang="el">
        Σινώπη
       </span>
       ) is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Seth_Barnes_Nicholson" title="Seth Barnes Nicholson">
        Seth Barnes Nicholson
       </a>
       at
       <a href="/wiki/Lick_Observatory" title="Lick Observatory">
        Lick Observatory
       </a>
       in 1914,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-discovery_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-discovery-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Sinope_(mythology)" title="Sinope (mythology)">
        Sinope
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Sinope did not receive its present name until 1975;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nicholson1938_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nicholson1938-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       before then, it was simply known as
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Jupiter IX
        </span>
       </b>
       . It was sometimes called "
       <a href="/wiki/Hades" title="Hades">
        Hades
       </a>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gaposchkin_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Gaposchkin-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       between 1955 and 1975.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sinope was the outermost known
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       until the discovery of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Megaclite_(moon)" title="Megaclite (moon)">
        Megaclite
       </a>
       in 2000. The most distant moon of Jupiter now known is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/S/2003_J_2" title="S/2003 J 2">
         S/2003 J 2
        </a>
       </span>
       .
      </p>

        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Sinope orbits Jupiter on a high eccentricity and high inclination retrograde orbit. The orbital elements are as of January 2000.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_2000_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_2000-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are continuously changing due to
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        solar
       </a>
       and planetary perturbations. It is often believed to belong to the
       <a href="/wiki/Pasiphae_group" title="Pasiphae group">
        Pasiphae group
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewitt2003_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewitt2003-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, given its mean inclination and different colour, Sinope could be also an independent object, captured independently, unrelated to the collision and break-up at the origin of the group.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The diagram illustrates Sinope's orbital elements in relation to other satellites of the group.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sinope is also known to be in a
       <a href="/wiki/Secular_resonance" title="Secular resonance">
        secular resonance
       </a>
       with Jupiter, similar to Pasiphae. However, Sinope can drop out of this resonance and has periods of both resonant and non resonant behaviour in time scales of 10
       <sup>
        7
       </sup>
       years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nesvorny2004_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Nesvorny2004-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Sinope_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Sinope has an estimated diameter of 38 km (assuming an albedo of 0.04)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SheppardJewitt2003_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-SheppardJewitt2003-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sinope is red (
       <a href="/wiki/Color_index" title="Color index">
        colour indices
       </a>
       B−V=0.84, R−V=0.46),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2003_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2003-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       unlike Pasiphae, which is grey.
      </p>
      <p>
       Sinope's
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum" title="Electromagnetic spectrum">
        spectrum
       </a>
       is similar to those of
       <a href="/wiki/D-type_asteroid" title="D-type asteroid">
        D-type asteroids
       </a>
       but different from that of Pasiphae.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grav2004_11-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grav2004-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These dissimilarities of the physical parameters suggest a different origin from the core members of the group.
      </p>
