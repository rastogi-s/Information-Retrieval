      <p>
       A total
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        lunar eclipse
       </a>
       took place on January 31, 2018. The moon was close to its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perigee" title="Perigee">
        perigee
       </a>
       on January 30 and so may be described as a "
       <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
        supermoon
       </a>
       ". The previous supermoon lunar eclipse was in
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        September 2015
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earthsky_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-earthsky-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       As it was also a
       <a href="/wiki/Blue_moon" title="Blue moon">
        blue moon
       </a>
       , the second full moon in the calendar month of January 2018, it was referred to as a "
       <b>
        super blue blood moon
       </b>
       "; the term "blood" referred to the typical color of the moon during the lunar eclipse.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This coincidence last occurred on
       <a href="/wiki/December_1982_lunar_eclipse" title="December 1982 lunar eclipse">
        December 30, 1982
       </a>
       for the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_hemisphere" title="Eastern hemisphere">
        eastern hemisphere
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and otherwise before that on
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_19th-century_lunar_eclipses" title="List of 19th-century lunar eclipses">
        March 31, 1866
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_Mathewson_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.com_Mathewson-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The next occurrence will be on
       <a href="/wiki/January_2037_lunar_eclipse" title="January 2037 lunar eclipse">
        January 31, 2037
       </a>
       , one
       <a href="/wiki/Metonic_cycle" title="Metonic cycle">
        metonic cycle
       </a>
       forward (19 years).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        Lunar eclipse
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes within Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       (shadow). As the eclipse begins, Earth's shadow first darkens the Moon slightly. Then, the shadow begins to "cover" part of the Moon, turning it a dark red-brown color (typically – the color can vary based on atmospheric conditions). The Moon appears to be reddish because of
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       (the same effect that causes sunsets to appear reddish) and the refraction of that light by Earth's atmosphere into its umbra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The following simulation shows the approximate appearance of the Moon passing through Earth's shadow. The northern portion of the Moon is closest to the center of the shadow, making it darkest and reddest in appearance.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif">
        <img alt="Animation January 18 2018 lunar eclipse appearance.gif" data-file-height="481" data-file-width="882" height="262" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif/480px-Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif/720px-Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/e/e1/Animation_January_18_2018_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif 2x" width="480"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span id=".22Super_blue_blood_moon.22">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id='"Super_blue_blood_moon"'>
        "Super blue blood moon"
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This was a "
       <a href="/wiki/Supermoon" title="Supermoon">
        supermoon
       </a>
       ", as the Moon was near to its closest distance to earth in its elliptical orbit, making it 7% larger in diameter or 14% larger in area, than an average full moon. The previous supermoon lunar eclipse was the
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        September 2015 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-earthsky_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-earthsky-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The full moon of 31 January 2018 was the second full moon that calendar month (in most time zones), making it, under one definition of the term, a "
       <a href="/wiki/Blue_moon" title="Blue moon">
        blue moon
       </a>
       ".
      </p>
      <p>
       Additionally referencing the orange or red "blood" colors that occur during a lunar eclipse, media sources described the event as a "super blue blood Moon".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_sbbm_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-BBC_sbbm-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Visibility">
        Visibility
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Pacific_Ocean" title="Pacific Ocean">
        Pacific Ocean
       </a>
       was turned toward the Moon at the time of the eclipse.
       <a href="/wiki/Central_Asia" title="Central Asia">
        Central
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eastern_Asia" title="Eastern Asia">
        eastern Asia
       </a>
       (including most of
       <a href="/wiki/Siberia" title="Siberia">
        Siberia
       </a>
       ),
       <a href="/wiki/Philippines" title="Philippines">
        Philippines
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Indonesia" title="Indonesia">
        Indonesia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/New_Zealand" title="New Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </a>
       and most of
       <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
        Australia
       </a>
       got a good view of this moon show in the evening sky. For
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Asia" title="Western Asia">
        Western Asia
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Indian_subcontinent" title="Indian subcontinent">
        Indian subcontinent
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">
        Middle East
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Europe" title="Eastern Europe">
        Eastern Europe
       </a>
       , the eclipse was underway as the moon rose.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_Rao_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.com_Rao-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Along the U.S.
       <a href="/wiki/West_Coast_of_the_United_States" title="West Coast of the United States">
        West Coast
       </a>
       , the total phase began at 4:51 a.m. PST. The further east, the closer the start of the partial phases coincided with moonset. Along the U.S.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atlantic_Seaboard" title="Atlantic Seaboard">
        Atlantic Seaboard
       </a>
       , for instance, the Moon had only just begun to enter the darkest part of Earth's shadow, the umbra, at 6:48 a.m. EST when it disappeared from view below the west-northwest horizon. The duration of the total phase was 77 minutes, with the Moon tracking through the southern part of the Earth's shadow. During totality, the Moon's lower limb appeared brighter than the dark upper limb.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Space.com_Rao_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Space.com_Rao-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
