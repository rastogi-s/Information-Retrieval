      <p>
       <b>
        Miranda
       </b>
       , also designated
       <b>
        Uranus V
       </b>
       , is the smallest and innermost of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       's five round
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellites
       </a>
       . It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">
        Gerard Kuiper
       </a>
       on 16 February 1948 at
       <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">
        McDonald Observatory
       </a>
       , and named after
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miranda_(Shakespeare)" title="Miranda (Shakespeare)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       from
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's play
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Like the other large
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        moons of Uranus
       </a>
       , Miranda orbits close to its planet's equatorial plane. Because Uranus orbits the Sun on its side, Miranda's orbit is perpendicular to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       and shares Uranus's extreme seasonal cycle.
      </p>
      <p>
       At just 470 km in diameter, Miranda is one of the smallest closely observed objects in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       that might be in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       (spherical under its own gravity). The only close-up images of Miranda are from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       probe, which made observations of Miranda during its Uranus flyby in January 1986. During the flyby, Miranda's southern hemisphere pointed towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , so only that part was studied. There are no active plans at present to return to study the moon in more detail, although various concepts such as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranus_orbiter_and_probe" title="Uranus orbiter and probe">
        Uranus orbiter and probe
       </a>
       have been proposed from time to time.
      </p>
      <p>
       Like all of Uranus' moons, Miranda probably formed from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        accretion disc
       </a>
       that surrounded the planet shortly after its formation, and, like other large moons, it is likely
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">
        differentiated
       </a>
       , with an inner core of rock surrounded by a
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       of ice. Miranda has one of the most extreme and varied topographies of any object in the Solar System, including
       <a href="/wiki/Verona_Rupes" title="Verona Rupes">
        Verona Rupes
       </a>
       , a 5- to 10-kilometer-high scarp, and chevron-shaped
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">
        tectonic
       </a>
       features called
       <i>
        coronae
       </i>
       . The origin and evolution of this varied geology, the most of any Uranian satellite, are still not fully understood, and multiple hypotheses exist regarding Miranda's evolution.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">
        Discovery and name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Miranda was discovered on 16 February 1948 by planetary astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Gerard_Kuiper" title="Gerard Kuiper">
        Gerard Kuiper
       </a>
       using the McDonald Observatory's 82-inch (2,080 mm)
       <a href="/wiki/Otto_Struve_Telescope" title="Otto Struve Telescope">
        Otto Struve Telescope
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Otto_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Otto-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its motion around Uranus was confirmed on 1 March 1948.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_June_1949_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_June_1949-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the first satellite of Uranus discovered in nearly 100 years. Kuiper elected to name the object "Miranda" after the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Miranda_(Shakespeare)" title="Miranda (Shakespeare)">
        character
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       , because the four previously discovered moons of Uranus,
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       , had all been named after characters of Shakespeare or
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       . However, the previous moons had been named specifically after fairies,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-namesof_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-namesof-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       whereas Miranda was a human. Subsequently, discovered satellites of Uranus were named after characters from Shakespeare and Pope, whether fairies or not.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Of Uranus's five round satellites, Miranda orbits closest to it, at roughly 129,000 km from the surface; about a quarter again as far as its most distant
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Uranus" title="Rings of Uranus">
        ring
       </a>
       . Its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       is 34 hours, and, like that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , is synchronous with its
       <a href="/wiki/Rotation_period" title="Rotation period">
        rotation period
       </a>
       , which means it always shows the same face to Uranus, a condition known as
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidal lock
       </a>
       . Miranda's orbital
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       (4.34°) is unusually high for a body so close to its planet, and roughly ten times that of the other major Uranian satellites. The reason for this is still uncertain; there are no
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mean-motion_resonance" title="Mean-motion resonance">
        mean-motion resonances
       </a>
       between the moons that could explain it, leading to the hypothesis that the moons occasionally pass through
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Secondary_resonance&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Secondary resonance (page does not exist)">
        secondary resonances
       </a>
       , which at some point in the past led to Miranda being locked for a time into a 3:1 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       , before chaotic behaviour induced by the secondary resonances moved it out of it again.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-umbriel_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-umbriel-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the Uranian system, due to the planet's lesser degree of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblateness
       </a>
       , and the larger relative size of its satellites, escape from a mean-motion resonance is much easier than for satellites of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra_Dermott_1990_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Malhotra_Dermott_1990-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Miranda_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_scarp.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1002" data-file-width="1016" height="197" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/200px-Miranda_scarp.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/300px-Miranda_scarp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/Miranda_scarp.jpg/400px-Miranda_scarp.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_scarp.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Close-up of
         <a href="/wiki/Verona_Rupes" title="Verona Rupes">
          Verona Rupes
         </a>
         , a large fault scarp on Miranda possibly 5 km (3.1 mi) high,
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PIA00044_11-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-PIA00044-11">
           [11]
          </a>
         </sup>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_12-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-12">
           [12]
          </a>
         </sup>
         taken by
         <i>
          Voyager 2
         </i>
         in January 1986
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       At 1.2 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       , Miranda is the least dense of Uranus's round satellites. That density suggests a composition of more than 60% water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-water_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-water-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Miranda's surface may be mostly water ice, with the low-density body also probably containing
       <a href="/wiki/Silicate" title="Silicate">
        silicate
       </a>
       rock and
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        organic compounds
       </a>
       in its interior.
      </p>
      <p>
       Miranda's surface has patchwork regions of broken terrain indicating intense geological activity in Miranda's past, and is criss-crossed by huge canyons. It also has the largest known
       <a href="/wiki/Cliff" title="Cliff">
        cliff
       </a>
       in the Solar System, Verona Rupes, which has a height of over 5 km (3.1 mi). Some of Miranda's terrain is possibly less than 100 million years old based on crater counts, which suggests that Miranda may still be geologically active today.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-usra_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-usra-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       There are three giant 'racetrack'-like grooved structures called
       <a href="/wiki/Corona_(planetary_geology)" title="Corona (planetary geology)">
        coronae
       </a>
       in the southern hemisphere, each at least 200 km (120 mi) wide and up to 20 km (12 mi) deep, named Arden, Elsinore and Inverness after locations in Shakespeare's plays, may have formed via
       <a href="/wiki/Rift" title="Rift">
        extensional
       </a>
       processes at the tops of
       <a href="/wiki/Diapir" title="Diapir">
        diapirs
       </a>
       , or upwellings of warm ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pappalardo_Reynolds_et_al._1997-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-space-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-teachastronomy_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-teachastronomy-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is believed through computer modelling that Miranda may have an additional corona on the unimaged hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The ridges probably represent extensional tilt blocks. The canyons probably represent
       <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
        graben
       </a>
       formed by
       <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">
        extensional faulting
       </a>
       . Other features may be due to
       <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">
        cryovolcanic
       </a>
       eruptions of icy magma. The diapirs may have changed the density distribution within Miranda, which could have caused Miranda to reorient itself,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pappalardo_Greeley_1993_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pappalardo_Greeley_1993-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       similar to a process believed to have occurred at
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       's geologically active moon
       <a href="/wiki/Enceladus" title="Enceladus">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Miranda's past geological activity is believed to have been driven by
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">
        tidal heating
       </a>
       during the time when it was in orbital resonance with Umbriel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The resonance would have increased
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       ; resulting tidal friction due to time-varying
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       from Uranus would have caused warming of Miranda's interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1989-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Malhotra_Dermott_1990_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Malhotra_Dermott_1990-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Miranda may have also once been in a 5:3 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       , which would have also contributed to its internal heating. However, the maximum heating attributable to the resonance with Umbriel was likely about three times greater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       An earlier theory, proposed shortly after the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       flyby, was that a previous incarnation of Miranda was shattered by a massive impact, with the fragments reassembling and denser ones subsequently sinking to produce the current strange pattern.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chaikin_2001_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chaikin_2001-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Miranda_eclipse.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Approaching the 7 December 2007
         <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">

         </a>
         Miranda produced brief solar
         <a href="/wiki/Eclipse" title="Eclipse">

         </a>
         over the center of Uranus.
        </div>

      <p>
       Miranda's apparent magnitude is +16.6, making it invisible to many amateur telescopes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-michigan_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-michigan-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Virtually all known information regarding its geology and geography was obtained during the
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_flyby" title="Planetary flyby">
        flyby
       </a>
       of Uranus made by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1986, The closest approach of
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       to Miranda was 29,000 km (18,000 mi)—significantly less than the distances to all other Uranian moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_1987_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stone_1987-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
