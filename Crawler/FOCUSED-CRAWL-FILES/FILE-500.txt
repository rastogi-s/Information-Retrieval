      <p>
       <b>
        Tycho
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/aɪ/: 'i' in 'tide'">
            aɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       ) is a prominent
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_craters" title="Lunar craters">
        lunar
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       located in the southern lunar highlands, named after the Danish astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">
        Tycho Brahe
       </a>
       (1546–1601).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       To the south of Tycho is the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Street_(crater)" title="Street (crater)">
        Street
       </a>
       , to the east is
       <a href="/wiki/Pictet_(crater)" title="Pictet (crater)">
        Pictet
       </a>
       , and to the north-northeast is
       <a href="/wiki/Sasserides_(crater)" title="Sasserides (crater)">
        Sasserides
       </a>
       . The surface around Tycho is replete with craters of various sizes, many overlapping still older craters. Some of the smaller craters are secondary craters formed from larger chunks of
       <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
        ejecta
       </a>
       from Tycho.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Age_and_description">
        Age and description
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tycho_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Age and description">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Tycho is a relatively young crater, with an estimated age of 108 million years (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Annum" title="Annum">
        Ma
       </a>
       ), based on analysis of samples of the crater ray recovered during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
        Apollo 16
       </a>
       mission. This age initially suggested that the impactor may have been a member of the
       <a href="/wiki/Baptistina_family" title="Baptistina family">
        Baptistina family
       </a>
       of asteroids, but as the composition of the impactor is unknown this remained conjecture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, this possibility was ruled out by the
       <a href="/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer" title="Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer">
        Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer
       </a>
       in 2011, as it was discovered that the Baptistina family was produced much later than expected, having formed approximately 80 million years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The crater is sharply defined, unlike older craters that have been degraded by subsequent impacts. The interior has a high
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo_feature" title="Albedo feature">
        albedo
       </a>
       that is prominent when the Sun is overhead, and the crater is surrounded by a distinctive
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        ray system
       </a>
       forming long spokes that reach as long as 1,500 kilometers. Sections of these rays can be observed even when Tycho is illuminated only by
       <a href="/wiki/Planetshine" title="Planetshine">
        earthlight
       </a>
       . Due to its prominent rays, Tycho is mapped as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Copernican_period" title="Copernican period">
        Copernican System
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tycho_Crater.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="531" data-file-width="800" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tycho_Crater.jpg/250px-Tycho_Crater.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tycho_Crater.jpg/375px-Tycho_Crater.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Tycho_Crater.jpg/500px-Tycho_Crater.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tycho_Crater.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The large
         <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
          ray system
         </a>
         centered on Tycho
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The ramparts beyond the rim have a lower albedo than the interior for a distance of over a hundred kilometers, and are free of the ray markings that lie beyond. This darker rim may have been formed from minerals excavated during the impact.
      </p>
      <p>
       Its inner wall is slumped and
       <a href="/wiki/Terrace_(geology)" title="Terrace (geology)">
        terraced
       </a>
       , sloping down to a rough but nearly flat floor exhibiting small, knobby domes. The floor displays signs of past volcanism, most likely from rock melt caused by the impact. Detailed
       <a href="/wiki/Astrophotography" title="Astrophotography">
        photographs
       </a>
       of the floor show that it is covered in a criss-crossing array of cracks and small hills. The central peaks rise 1.6 kilometers (0.99 mi) above the floor, and a lesser peak stands just to the northeast of the primary
       <a href="/wiki/Massif" title="Massif">
        massif
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Infrared observations of the lunar surface during an eclipse have demonstrated that Tycho cools at a slower rate than other parts of the surface, making the crater a "hot spot". This effect is caused by the difference in materials that cover the crater.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="480" data-file-width="625" height="192" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg/250px-Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg/375px-Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg/500px-Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tycho_Crater_Panorama.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Panoramic view of the lunar surface taken by
         <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_7" title="Surveyor 7">
          Surveyor 7
         </a>
         , which landed about 29 km (18 mi) from the rim of Tycho
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The rim of this crater was chosen as the target of the
       <a href="/wiki/Surveyor_7" title="Surveyor 7">
        Surveyor 7
       </a>
       mission. The robotic spacecraft safely touched down north of the crater in January 1968. The craft performed chemical measurements of the surface, finding a composition different from the maria. From this, one of the main components of the highlands was theorized to be
       <a href="/wiki/Anorthosite" title="Anorthosite">
        anorthosite
       </a>
       , an
       <a href="/wiki/Aluminium" title="Aluminium">
        aluminium
       </a>
       -rich mineral. The crater was also imaged in great detail by
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Orbiter_5" title="Lunar Orbiter 5">
        Lunar Orbiter 5
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       From the 1950s through the 1990s, NASA aerodynamicist Dean Chapman and others advanced the lunar origin theory of
       <a href="/wiki/Tektite" title="Tektite">
        tektites
       </a>
       . Chapman used complex orbital computer models and extensive wind tunnel tests to support the theory that the so-called Australasian tektites originated from the Rosse ejecta ray of Tycho. Until the Rosse ray is sampled, a lunar origin for these tektites cannot be ruled out.
      </p>
      <p>
       This crater was drawn on lunar maps as early as 1645, when
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antonius_Maria_Schyrleus_de_Rheita" title="Antonius Maria Schyrleus de Rheita">
        Antonius Maria Schyrleus de Rheita
       </a>
       depicted the bright ray system.
      </p>
      <p>
       During
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_eclipses_on_the_Moon" title="Solar eclipses on the Moon">
        solar eclipses
       </a>
       , especially with
       <a href="/wiki/Total_eclipses_on_the_Moon" title="Total eclipses on the Moon">
        long totality
       </a>
       , the crater temperature remains high, indicating that their temperature is higher than in the surrounding area and the distant dark mare. The first was taken in mid-infrared from Earth orbit during a
       <a href="/wiki/September_1996_lunar_eclipse" title="September 1996 lunar eclipse">
        September 1996 lunar eclipse
       </a>
       by the
       <a href="/wiki/Midcourse_Space_Experiment" title="Midcourse Space Experiment">
        Midcourse Space Experiment
       </a>
       (MSX) satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Names">
        Names
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tycho_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Names">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Tycho is named after the Danish astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Tycho_Brahe" title="Tycho Brahe">
        Tycho Brahe
       </a>
       . Like many of the craters on the Moon's near side, it was given its name by the
       <a href="/wiki/Society_of_Jesus" title="Society of Jesus">
        Jesuit
       </a>
       astronomer
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Riccioli" title="Giovanni Battista Riccioli">
        Giovanni Riccioli
       </a>
       , whose 1651 nomenclature system has become standardized.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhitaker200361_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitaker200361-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Earlier lunar cartographers had given the feature different names.
       <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi" title="Pierre Gassendi">
        Pierre Gassendi
       </a>
       named it Umbilicus Lunaris ('the
       <a href="/wiki/Navel" title="Navel">
        navel
       </a>
       of the Moon').
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhitaker200333_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitaker200333-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_van_Langren" title="Michael van Langren">
        Michael van Langren
       </a>
       's 1645 map calls it "Vladislai IV" after
       <a href="/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_IV_Vasa" title="Władysław IV Vasa">
        Władysław IV Vasa
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_Poland" title="King of Poland">
        King of Poland
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWhitaker2003198_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhitaker2003198-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       And
       <a href="/wiki/Johannes_Hevelius" title="Johannes Hevelius">
        Johannes Hevelius
       </a>
       named it 'Mons Sinai' after
       <a href="/wiki/Mount_Sinai" title="Mount Sinai">
        Mount Sinai
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fictional_references">
        Fictional references
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Tycho_(lunar_crater)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Fictional references">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There is a chapter entitled "Tycho" in Jules Verne's
       <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
        Around the Moon
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Around_the_Moon" title="Around the Moon">
        Autour de la Lune
       </a>
       , 1870) which describes the crater and its ray system.
      </p>
      <p>
       Tycho was the location of the
       <a href="/wiki/Monolith_(Space_Odyssey)#Tycho_Magnetic_Anomaly-1" title="Monolith (Space Odyssey)">
        Tycho Magnetic Anomaly
       </a>
       (TMA-1), and subsequent excavation of an alien monolith, in
       <a href="/wiki/2001:_A_Space_Odyssey_(novel)" title="2001: A Space Odyssey (novel)">
        <i>
         2001: A Space Odyssey
        </i>
       </a>
       , the seminal science-fiction film by
       <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">
        Stanley Kubrick
       </a>
       and book by
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
        Arthur C. Clarke
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It also serves as the location of "Tycho City" in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Trek:_First_Contact" title="Star Trek: First Contact">
         Star Trek: First Contact
        </a>
       </i>
       ; a lunar metropolis by the 24th century.
      </p>
      <p>
       In the film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Can%27t_Buy_Me_Love_(film)" title="Can't Buy Me Love (film)">
         Can't Buy Me Love
        </a>
       </i>
       , Cindy notices Tycho while looking through a telescope on her final "contractual" date with Ronny in the Airplane Graveyard.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       's book
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Moon_Is_a_Harsh_Mistress" title="The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress">
         The Moon Is a Harsh Mistress
        </a>
       </i>
       , Tycho is the location of the lunar habitat "Tycho Under".
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Williamson" title="Jack Williamson">
        Jack Williamson
       </a>
       's novel
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Terraforming_Earth&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Terraforming Earth (page does not exist)">
         Terraforming Earth
        </a>
       </i>
       , the crater is utilized for "Tycho Base", a self-sustaining, robot controlled installation aimed at restoring life to the (dead) planet Earth after an asteroid sterilizes the biosphere.
      </p>
      <p>
       In Heinlein's short story
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Blowups_Happen" title="Blowups Happen">
         Blowups Happen
        </a>
       </i>
       , a character hypothesizes that Tycho may have been the location of a sentient race's main atomic power plant, in a past time when the Moon was still habitable—and that the plant exploded, causing the craters, the rays spreading from Tycho, and the death of all life on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clifford_Simak" title="Clifford Simak">
        Clifford Simak
       </a>
       set a novelette
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Trouble_with_Tycho&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Trouble with Tycho (page does not exist)">
         The Trouble with Tycho
        </a>
       </i>
       , at the lunar crater. He also postulated that the crater's rays were composed of volcanic glass (
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tektites" title="Tektites">
        tektites
       </a>
       ) akin to a theory postulated by NASA researchers Dean Chapman and John O'Keefe in the 1970s.
      </p>
      <p>
       Crater Tycho figures prominently in the
       <a href="/wiki/Matthew_Looney" title="Matthew Looney">
        Matthew Looney
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Maria_Looney" title="Maria Looney">
        Maria Looney
       </a>
       series of children's books set on the Moon, authored by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jerome_Beatty" title="Jerome Beatty">
        Jerome Beatty
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roger_Macbride_Allen" title="Roger Macbride Allen">
        Roger Macbride Allen
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        Hunted Earth
       </i>
       series of novels, the Naked Purples own a former penal colony in or around Tycho crater known as "Tycho Purple Penal" (see
       <a href="/wiki/The_Ring_of_Charon" title="The Ring of Charon">
        The Ring of Charon
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
