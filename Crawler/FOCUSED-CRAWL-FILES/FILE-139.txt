       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">

           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">

           </span>

         </a>
        </span>

       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">

       <a href="/wiki/Ceres_(dwarf_planet)" title="Ceres (dwarf planet)">
        Ceres
       </a>
       , for which precise measurements have confirmed a shape consistent with
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">

       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">

       </a>
       .

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:132px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Cassini.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="588" data-file-width="423" height="181" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Giovanni_Cassini.jpg/130px-Giovanni_Cassini.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Giovanni_Cassini.jpg/195px-Giovanni_Cassini.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/Giovanni_Cassini.jpg/260px-Giovanni_Cassini.jpg 2x" width="130"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Giovanni_Cassini.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">
          Giovanni Domenico Cassini
         </a>
         , discoverer of Rhea in 1672
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Rhea was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Giovanni_Domenico_Cassini" title="Giovanni Domenico Cassini">
        Giovanni Domenico Cassini
       </a>
       on 23 December 1672.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com.Rhea_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com.Rhea-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the third moon discovered around Saturn and the second by him.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com.Rhea_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com.Rhea-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Rhea is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titan
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Rhea_(mythology)" title="Rhea (mythology)">
        Rhea
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , the "mother of the gods". It is also designated
       <b>
        Saturn V
       </b>
       (being the fifth major moon going outward from the planet).
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Cassini named the four moons he discovered (
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       , Rhea and
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       )
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Sidera_Lodoicea" title="Sidera Lodoicea">
         Sidera Lodoicea
        </a>
       </i>
       (the stars of Louis) to honor King
       <a href="/wiki/Louis_XIV_of_France" title="Louis XIV of France">
        Louis XIV
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com.Rhea_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com.Rhea-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Astronomers fell into the habit of referring to them and
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       as
       <i>
        Saturn I
       </i>
       through
       <i>
        Saturn V
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com.Rhea_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com.Rhea-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Once
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       were discovered, in 1789, the numbering scheme was extended to
       <i>
        Saturn VII
       </i>
       , and then to
       <i>
        Saturn VIII
       </i>
       with the discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       in 1848, before the numbering scheme was frozen to prevent further confusion.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2015)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Rhea was not named until 1847, when
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       (son of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       , discoverer of the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       , and two other moons of Saturn,
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(moon)" title="Mimas (moon)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Enceladus_(moon)" title="Enceladus (moon)">
        Enceladus
       </a>
       ) suggested in
       <i>
        Results of Astronomical Observations made at the Cape of Good Hope
       </i>
       that the names of the Titans, sisters and brothers of Cronos (Saturn, in Roman mythology), be used.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell1848_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell1848-10">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-space.com.Rhea_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-space.com.Rhea-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span id="Size.2C_mass.2C_and_internal_structure">
       </span>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Size,_mass,_and_internal_structure">
        Size, mass, and internal structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Size, mass, and internal structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhea,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2026" data-file-width="3000" height="169" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/250px-Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/375px-Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg/500px-Rhea%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhea,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Size comparison of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         , and Rhea
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Rhea is an icy body with a density of about 1.236 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       . This low
       <a href="/wiki/Density" title="Density">
        density
       </a>
       indicates that it is made of ~25% rock (density ~3.25 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ) and ~75% water ice (density ~0.93 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ). Although Rhea is the ninth-largest moon, it is only the tenth-most-massive moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [b]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Earlier it was assumed that Rhea had a rocky core in the center.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2003_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2003-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, measurements taken during a close flyby by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
       </a>
       orbiter in 2005 (see below) cast this into doubt. In a paper published in 2007 it was claimed that the axial dimensionless
       <a href="/wiki/Moment_of_inertia" title="Moment of inertia">
        moment of inertia
       </a>
       coefficient was 0.4.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [c]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson_2007_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson_2007-13">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such a value indicated that Rhea had an almost homogeneous interior (with some compression of ice in the center) while the existence of a rocky core would imply a moment of inertia of about 0.34.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2003_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2003-12">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the same year another paper claimed the moment of inertia was about 0.37.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [d]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rhea being either partially or fully differentiated would be consistent with the observations of the Cassini probe.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Iess2007_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Iess2007-15">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A year later yet another paper claimed that the moon may not be in hydrostatic equilibrium meaning that the moment of inertia can not be determined from the gravity data alone.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MacKenzie2008_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MacKenzie2008-17">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 2008 an author of the first paper tried to reconcile these three disparate results. He concluded that there is a systematic error in the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       radio Doppler data used in the analysis, but after restricting the analysis to a subset of data obtained closest to the moon, he arrived at his old result that Rhea was in hydrostatic equilibrium and had the moment inertia of about 0.4, again implying a homogeneous interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Anderson2008_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Anderson2008-18">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Triaxial_ellipsoid" title="Triaxial ellipsoid">
        triaxial
       </a>
       shape of Rhea is consistent with a homogeneous body in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       rotating at Rhea's angular velocity.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2007_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thomas2007-19">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Models suggest that Rhea could be capable of sustaining an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Subsurface_ocean" title="Subsurface ocean">
        internal liquid-water ocean
       </a>
       through heating by
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive decay
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features">
        Surface features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Surface features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_geological_features_on_Rhea" title="List of geological features on Rhea">
        List of geological features on Rhea
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Rhea's features resemble those of
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       , with dissimilar leading and trailing hemispheres, suggesting similar composition and histories. The temperature on Rhea is 99 K (−174 °C) in direct sunlight and between 73 K (−200 °C) and 53 K (−220 °C) in the shade.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1020" data-file-width="1020" height="250" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg/250px-PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg/375px-PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg/500px-PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA18310-SaturnMoon-Rhea-20150210.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Surface features on Rhea well defined due to the lighting.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Rhea has a rather typical heavily
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        cratered
       </a>
       surface,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreSchenk_2004_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MooreSchenk_2004-21">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       with the exceptions of a few large Dione-type chasmata or fractures (
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)#The_ice_cliffs_(formerly_'wispy_terrain')" title="Dione (moon)">
        wispy terrain
       </a>
       ) on the trailing hemisphere (the side facing away from the direction of motion along Rhea's orbit)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2008_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2008-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a very faint "line" of material at Rhea's equator that may have been deposited by material deorbiting from its rings.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schenk2009_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Schenk2009-23">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rhea has two very large impact basins on its anti-Cronian hemisphere (facing away from Saturn), which are about 400 and 500 km across.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2008_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2008-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The more northerly and less degraded of the two, called
       <a href="/wiki/Tirawa_(crater)" title="Tirawa (crater)">
        Tirawa
       </a>
       , is roughly comparable to the basin Odysseus on
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MooreSchenk_2004_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MooreSchenk_2004-21">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There is a 48 km-diameter impact crater at 112°W that is prominent because of an extended system of bright
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        rays
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2008_22-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2008-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This crater, called
       <a href="/wiki/Inktomi_(crater)" title="Inktomi (crater)">
        Inktomi
       </a>
       , is nicknamed "The Splat", and may be one of the youngest craters on the inner moons of Saturn.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2008_22-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2008-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No evidence of any endogenic activity has been discovered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wagner2008_22-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Wagner2008-22">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rhea_at_approximately_2,348_miles_(3,778_kilometers)_away.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="870" data-file-width="1023" height="170" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg/200px-Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg/300px-Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg/400px-Rhea_at_approximately_2%2C348_miles_%283%2C778_kilometers%29_away.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Rhea_at_approximately_2,348_miles_(3,778_kilometers)_away.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Rhea's surface
         <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">
          [
          <i>
           <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources">
            <span title="Statement needs to be more specific about the content to which it refers. (April 2015)">
             specify
            </span>
           </a>
          </i>
          ]
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Its surface can be divided into two geologically different areas based on
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        crater
       </a>
       density; the first area contains craters which are larger than 40 km in diameter, whereas the second area, in parts of the polar and equatorial regions, has only craters under that size. This suggests that a major resurfacing event occurred some time during its formation. The leading hemisphere is heavily cratered and uniformly bright. As on
       <a href="/wiki/Callisto_(moon)" title="Callisto (moon)">
        Callisto
       </a>
       , the craters lack the high relief features seen on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_(planet)" title="Mercury (planet)">
        Mercury
       </a>
       . It has been theorized that these cratered plains are as many as four billion years old on average.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the trailing hemisphere there is a network of bright swaths on a dark background and few visible craters. It had been thought that these bright areas might be material ejected from ice
       <a href="/wiki/Volcano" title="Volcano">
        volcanoes
       </a>
       early in Rhea's history when its interior was still liquid. However, observations of Dione, which has an even darker trailing hemisphere and similar but more prominent bright streaks, show that the streaks are actually ice cliffs resulting from extensive fracturing of the moon's surface.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (February 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       The extensive dark areas are thought to be deposited
       <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">
        tholins
       </a>
       , which are a mix of complex
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        organic compounds
       </a>
       generated on the ice by
       <a href="/wiki/Pyrolysis" title="Pyrolysis">
        pyrolysis
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Radiolysis" title="Radiolysis">
        radiolysis
       </a>
       of simple compounds containing carbon, nitrogen and hydrogen.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cruikshank_2005_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cruikshank_2005-25">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The January 17, 2006 distant flyby by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
       </a>
       spacecraft yielded images of the wispy hemisphere at better resolution and a lower Sun angle than previous observations. Images from this and subsequent flybys showed that Rhea's streaks in fact are tectonically formed ice cliffs (
       <i>
        chasmata
       </i>
       ) similar to those of Dione.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The moons of Saturn are thought to have formed through
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        co-accretion
       </a>
       , a similar process to that believed to have formed the planets in the Solar System. As the young giant planets formed, they were surrounded by discs of material that gradually coalesced into moons. However, a proposed model for the formation of
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(moon)" title="Titan (moon)">
        Titan
       </a>
       may also shine a new light on the origin of Rhea and
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       . In this model, Titan was formed in a series of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Giant_impact" title="Giant impact">
        giant impacts
       </a>
       between pre-existing moons, and Rhea and Iapetus are thought to have formed from part of the debris of these collisions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Atmosphere">
        Atmosphere
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Atmosphere">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       On November 27, 2010,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       announced the discovery of a tenuous atmosphere—an
       <a href="/wiki/Exosphere" title="Exosphere">
        exosphere
       </a>
       . It consists of oxygen and carbon dioxide in proportion of roughly 5 to 2. The surface density of the exosphere is from 10
       <sup>
        5
       </sup>
       to 10
       <sup>
        6
       </sup>
       molecules in a cubic centimeter depending on local temperature. The main source of oxygen is
       <a href="/wiki/Radiolysis" title="Radiolysis">
        radiolysis
       </a>
       of water ice at the surface by ions supplied by the
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Saturn" title="Magnetosphere of Saturn">
        magnetosphere of Saturn
       </a>
       . The source of the carbon dioxide is less clear, but it may be related to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oxidation" title="Oxidation">
        oxidation
       </a>
       of the organics present in ice or to
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       of the moon's interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-atmosphere_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-atmosphere-27">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Teolis2010_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Teolis2010-28">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_ring_system">
        Possible ring system
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Possible ring system">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Rhea" title="Rings of Rhea">
        Rings of Rhea
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       On March 6, 2008,
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       announced that Rhea may have a tenuous ring system. This would mark the first discovery of rings about a moon. The rings' existence was inferred by observed changes in the flow of electrons trapped by Saturn's magnetic field as
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       passed by Rhea.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rings_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-rings-29">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jones2008_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jones2008-30">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LakdawallaE_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LakdawallaE-31">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Dust and debris could extend out to Rhea's
       <a href="/wiki/Hill_sphere" title="Hill sphere">
        Hill sphere
       </a>
       , but were thought to be denser nearer the moon, with three narrow rings of higher density. The case for a ring was strengthened by the subsequent finding of the presence of a set of small ultraviolet-bright spots distributed along Rhea's equator (interpreted as the impact points of deorbiting ring material).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lakdawalla2_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lakdawalla2-32">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, when
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       made targeted observations of the putative ring plane from several angles, no evidence of ring material was found, suggesting that another explanation for the earlier observations is needed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tiscareno2010_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tiscareno2010-33">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-norings_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-norings-34">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Rhea_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The first images of Rhea were obtained by
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_program" title="Voyager program">
         Voyager 1 &amp; 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft in 1980–1981.
      </p>
      <p>
       There were five close targeted fly-bys by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       orbiter: at a distance of 500 km on November 26, 2005, at a distance of 5,750 km on August 30, 2007, at a distance of 100 km on March 2, 2010 and at 69 km flyby on January 11, 2011
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CookJ2011_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CookJ2011-35">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and a last flyby at 992 km on March 9, 2013.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LastFlybyRhea_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LastFlybyRhea-36">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Rhea has been also imaged many times from long to moderate distances by the orbiter.
      </p>
