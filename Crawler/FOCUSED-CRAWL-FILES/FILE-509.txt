      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite
       </b>
       (
       <b>
        LCROSS
       </b>
       ) was a
       <a href="/wiki/Robotic_spacecraft" title="Robotic spacecraft">
        robotic spacecraft
       </a>
       operated by
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       . The mission was conceived as a low-cost means of determining the nature of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrogen" title="Hydrogen">
        hydrogen
       </a>
       detected at the polar regions of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Launched immediately after discovery of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        lunar water
       </a>
       by
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the main LCROSS mission objective was to further explore the presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Water" title="Water">
        water
       </a>
       ice in a permanently shadowed crater near a lunar polar region.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was successful in confirming water in the southern lunar crater
       <a href="/wiki/Cabeus_(crater)" title="Cabeus (crater)">
        Cabeus
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectralResult_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spectralResult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was launched together with the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       (LRO) on June 18, 2009, as part of the shared
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Precursor_Robotic_Program" title="Lunar Precursor Robotic Program">
        Lunar Precursor Robotic Program
       </a>
       , the first
       <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">
        American
       </a>
       mission to the Moon in over ten years. Together, LCROSS and LRO form the vanguard of NASA's return to the Moon, and are expected to influence
       <a href="/wiki/Federal_government_of_the_United_States" title="Federal government of the United States">
        United States government
       </a>
       decisions on whether or not to
       <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">
        colonize the Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       LCROSS was designed to collect and relay data from the impact and debris plume resulting from the launch vehicle's spent
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">
        Centaur
       </a>
       upper stage (and data collecting Shepherding Spacecraft) striking the crater
       <a href="/wiki/Cabeus_(crater)" title="Cabeus (crater)">
        Cabeus
       </a>
       near the south pole of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Centaur had nominal impact mass of 2,305 kg (5,081 lb), and an impact velocity of about 9,000 km/h (5,600 mph),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moonstar_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moonstar-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       releasing the kinetic
       <a href="/wiki/TNT_equivalent" title="TNT equivalent">
        energy equivalent
       </a>
       of detonating approximately 2 tons of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trinitrotoluene" title="Trinitrotoluene">
        TNT
       </a>
       (8.86
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gigajoule" title="Gigajoule">
        GJ
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       LCROSS suffered a malfunction on August 22, depleting half of its fuel and leaving very little fuel margin in the spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.Clark_10-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-S.Clark-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Centaur impacted successfully on October 9, 2009, at 11:31
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       . The Shepherding Spacecraft descended through Centaur's ejectate plume, collected and relayed data, impacting six minutes later at 11:37 UTC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Contrary to media reports at the time, neither the impact nor its dust cloud could be seen from Earth, using the naked eye or telescopes.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

         </a>
        </li>
       </ul>
      </div>
      <p>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mission">
        Mission
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Mission">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="477" data-file-width="715" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/220px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/330px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f1/LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg/440px-LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur_Impact_Flash.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The flash from the LCROSS Centaur impact.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       LCROSS was a fast-track, low-cost companion mission to the LRO. The LCROSS
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Payload_(air_and_space_craft)" title="Payload (air and space craft)">
        payload
       </a>
       was added after NASA moved the LRO from the
       <a href="/wiki/Delta_II" title="Delta II">
        Delta II
       </a>
       to a larger launch vehicle. It was chosen from 19 other proposals.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       LCROSS's mission was dedicated to late American broadcaster
       <a href="/wiki/Walter_Cronkite" title="Walter Cronkite">
        Walter Cronkite
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moonstar_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moonstar-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       LCROSS launched with the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        LRO
       </a>
       aboard an
       <a href="/wiki/Atlas_V" title="Atlas V">
        Atlas V
       </a>
       rocket from
       <a href="/wiki/Cape_Canaveral,_Florida" title="Cape Canaveral, Florida">
        Cape Canaveral, Florida
       </a>
       , on June 18, 2009, at 21:32
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       (17:32
       <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Time_Zone" title="Eastern Time Zone">
        EDT
       </a>
       ). On June 23, four and a half days after launch, LCROSS and its attached
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(rocket_stage)" title="Centaur (rocket stage)">
        Centaur booster rocket
       </a>
       successfully completed a lunar swingby and entered into
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_orbit" title="Polar orbit">
        polar Earth orbit
       </a>
       with a period of 37 days, positioning LCROSS for impact on a lunar pole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Early in the morning on August 22, 2009, LCROSS ground controllers discovered an anomaly caused by a sensor problem, which had resulted in the spacecraft burning through 140 kilograms (309 pounds) of fuel, more than half of the fuel remaining at the time. According to Dan Andrews, the LCROSS project manager, "Our estimates now are if we pretty much baseline the mission, meaning just accomplish the things that we have to [do] to get the job done with full mission success, we're still in the black on propellant, but not by a lot."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-S.Clark_10-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-S.Clark-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="300" height="257" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg/220px-LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg/330px-LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg/440px-LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Trajectory_Side_View.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The LCROSS trajectory
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2048" data-file-width="2048" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/LCROSS_Centaur.jpg/220px-LCROSS_Centaur.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/LCROSS_Centaur.jpg/330px-LCROSS_Centaur.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fc/LCROSS_Centaur.jpg/440px-LCROSS_Centaur.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_Centaur.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         An illustration of the LCROSS Centaur rocket stage and shepherding spacecraft as they approach impact with the lunar south pole on October 9, 2009.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Lunar impacts, after approximately three orbits, occurred on October 9, 2009, with the Centaur crashing into the Moon at 11:31
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UTC" title="UTC">
        UTC
       </a>
       and the Shepherding Spacecraft following a few minutes later.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AP_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-AP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission team initially announced that Cabeus A would be the target crater for the LCROSS dual impacts,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observation_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-observation-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but later refined the target to be the larger, main Cabeus crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On its final approach to the Moon, the Shepherding Spacecraft and Centaur separated October 9, 2009, at 01:50 UTC.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The Centaur upper stage acted as a heavy impactor to create a debris plume that rose above the lunar surface. Following four minutes after impact of the Centaur upper stage, the Shepherding Spacecraft flew through this debris plume, collecting and relaying data back to Earth before it struck the lunar surface to produce a second debris plume. The impact velocity was projected to be 9,000 km/h (5,600 mph) or 2.5 km/second.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Centaur impact was expected to excavate more than 350
       <a href="/wiki/Tonne" title="Tonne">
        metric tons
       </a>
       (390
       <a href="/wiki/Short_ton" title="Short ton">
        short tons
       </a>
       ) of lunar material and create a crater about 20 m (65 ft) in diameter to a depth of about 4 m (13 ft). The Shepherding Spacecraft impact was projected to excavate an estimated 150 metric tons (170 short tons) and create a crater 14 m (46 ft) in diameter to a depth of about 2 m (6 ft). Most of the material in the Centaur debris plume was expected to remain at (lunar) altitudes below 10 km (6 mi).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressKit_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-PressKit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       It was hoped that
       <a href="/wiki/Spectroscopy" title="Spectroscopy">
        spectral analysis
       </a>
       of the resulting impact plume would help to confirm preliminary findings by the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Prospector" title="Lunar Prospector">
        Lunar Prospector
       </a>
       missions which hinted that there may be
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_ice" title="Lunar ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       in the permanently shadowed regions. Mission scientists expected that the Centaur impact plume would be visible through amateur-class telescopes with apertures as small as 25 to 30 cm (10 to 12 inches).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-observation_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-observation-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But no plume was observed by such amateur telescopes. Even world class telescopes such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hale_telescope" title="Hale telescope">
        Hale telescope
       </a>
       , equipped with adaptive optics, did not detect the plume. The plume may have still occurred but at a small scale not detectable from Earth. Both impacts were also monitored by Earth-based observatories and by orbital assets, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Whether or not LCROSS would find water had been stated to be influential in whether or not the United States government pursues creating a
       <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">
        Moon base
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chosun_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Chosun-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On November 13, 2009, NASA confirmed that water was detected after the Centaur impacted the crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectralResult_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-spectralResult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spacecraft">
        Spacecraft
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Spacecraft">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="743" data-file-width="1011" height="162" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png/220px-Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png/330px-Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png/440px-Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Exploded_view_of_LCROSS_spacecraft.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         LCROSS spacecraft (
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Exploded_view" title="Exploded view">
          exploded view
         </a>
         )
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The LCROSS mission took advantage of the structural capabilities of the
       <a href="/wiki/Evolved_Expendable_Launch_Vehicle" title="Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle">
        Evolved Expendable Launch Vehicle
       </a>
       (EELV)
       <a href="/wiki/EELV_Secondary_Payload_Adapter" title="EELV Secondary Payload Adapter">
        Secondary Payload Adapter (ESPA)
       </a>
       ring
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       used to attach LRO to the Centaur upper-stage rocket. Mounted on the outside of the ESPA were six panels that hold the spacecraft's science payload, command and control systems, communications equipment, batteries, and solar panels. A small
       <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant_rocket" title="Monopropellant rocket">
        monopropellant propulsion system
       </a>
       was mounted inside of the ring. Also attached were two
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/S_Band" title="S Band">
        S Band
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Omnidirectional_antenna" title="Omnidirectional antenna">
        omnidirectional antennas
       </a>
       and two medium-gain antennas. The mission's strict schedule, mass, and budget constraints posed difficult challenges to engineering teams from NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Ames_Research_Center" title="Ames Research Center">
        Ames Research Center
       </a>
       (ARC) and
       <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">
        Northrop Grumman
       </a>
       . Their creative thinking led to a unique use of the ESPA ring and innovative sourcing of other spacecraft components. Usually, the ESPA ring is used as a platform to hold six small deployable satellites; for LCROSS, it became the backbone of the satellite, a first for the ring. LCROSS also took advantage of commercially available instruments and used many of the already-flight-verified components used on
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        LRO
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (March 2010)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="482" data-file-width="720" height="147" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg/220px-LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg/330px-LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg/440px-LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LCROSS_LRO_being_prepared_for_fairing_installation.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         LRO (top, silver) and LCROSS (bottom, gold) prepared for fairing
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       LCROSS is managed by NASA's ARC and was built by
       <a href="/wiki/Northrop_Grumman" title="Northrop Grumman">
        Northrop Grumman
       </a>
       . The LCROSS preliminary design review was completed on September 8, 2006. The LCROSS mission passed its Mission Confirmation Review on February 2, 2007,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and its Critical Design Review on February 22, 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After assembly and testing at Ames, the instrument payload, provided by Ecliptic Enterprises Corporation,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       was shipped to Northrop Grumman on January 14, 2008, for integration with the spacecraft.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
        <a href="#cite_note-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       LCROSS passed its review on February 12, 2009.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Instruments">
        Instruments
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Instruments">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The LCROSS science instrument payload, provided by NASA's ARC, consisted of a total of nine instruments: one visible, two near infrared, and two mid-infrared cameras; one visible and two near-infrared spectrometers; and a photometer. A data handling unit (DHU) collected the information from each instrument for transmission back to LCROSS Mission Control. Because of the schedule and budget constraints, LCROSS took advantage of rugged, commercially available components. The individual instruments went through a rigorous testing cycle that simulated launch and flight conditions, identifying design weaknesses and necessary modifications for use in space, at which point the manufacturers were allowed to modify their designs.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PressKit_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-PressKit-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Results">
        Results
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Results">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The impact was not as visually prominent as had been anticipated. Project manager Dan Andrews believed that this was due to pre-crash simulations that exaggerated the plume's prominence.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Because of data bandwidth issues, the exposures were kept short, which made the plume difficult to see in the images in the visible spectra. This resulted in the need for image processing to increase clarity. The infrared camera also captured a thermal signature of the booster's impact.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26">
        <a href="#cite_note-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Presence_of_water">
        Presence of water
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Presence of water">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 13 November 2009, NASA reported that multiple lines of evidence show water was present in both the high-angle vapor plume and the ejecta curtain created by the LCROSS Centaur impact. As of November 2009
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=LCROSS&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       , the concentration and distribution of water and other substances required more analysis.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectralResult_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-spectralResult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additional confirmation came from an emission in the ultraviolet spectrum that was attributed to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hydroxyl" title="Hydroxyl">
        hydroxyl
       </a>
       fragments, a product from the break-up of water by sunlight.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spectralResult_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-spectralResult-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Analysis of the spectra indicate that a reasonable estimate of the concentration of water in the frozen regolith is on the order of one percent.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Evidence from other missions suggests that this may have been a relatively dry spot, as thick deposits of relatively pure ice appear do present themselves in other craters.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
        <a href="#cite_note-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A later, more definitive, analysis found the concentration of water to be "5.6 ± 2.9% by mass."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Colaprete2010_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Colaprete2010-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
