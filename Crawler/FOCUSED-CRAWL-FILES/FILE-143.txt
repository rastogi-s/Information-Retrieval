      <p>
       <b>
        Ariel
       </b>
       is the fourth-largest of the 27 known
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        moons of Uranus
       </a>
       . Ariel orbits and rotates in the equatorial plane of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       , which is almost perpendicular to the orbit of Uranus and so has an extreme seasonal cycle.
      </p>
      <p>
       It was discovered in October 1851 by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       and named for a character in two different pieces of literature. As of 2017, much of the detailed knowledge of Ariel derives from a single
       <a href="/wiki/Space_probe" title="Space probe">
        flyby
       </a>
       of Uranus performed by the spacecraft
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       in 1986, which managed to image around 35% of the moon's surface. There are no active plans at present to return to study the moon in more detail, although various concepts such as a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Uranus_orbiter_and_probe" title="Uranus orbiter and probe">
        Uranus orbiter and probe
       </a>
       have been proposed from time to time.
      </p>
      <p>
       After
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       , Ariel is the second-smallest of Uranus' five major rounded satellites and the second-closest to its
       <a href="/wiki/Planet" title="Planet">
        planet
       </a>
       . Among the smallest of the Solar System's 19 known spherical moons (it ranks 14th among them in diameter), it is believed to be composed of roughly equal parts ice and rocky material. Its mass is approximately equal in magnitude to Earth's
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrosphere" title="Hydrosphere">
        hydrosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Like all of Uranus' moons, Ariel probably formed from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        accretion disc
       </a>
       that surrounded the planet shortly after its formation, and, like other large moons, it is likely
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">
        differentiated
       </a>
       , with an inner core of rock surrounded by a
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       of ice. Ariel has a complex surface consisting of extensive cratered terrain cross-cut by a system of
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_scarp" title="Fault scarp">
        scarps
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">
        canyons
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Ridge" title="Ridge">
        ridges
       </a>
       . The surface shows signs of more recent geological activity than other Uranian moons, most likely due to
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_heating" title="Tidal heating">
        tidal heating
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_name">
        Discovery and name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Discovered on 24 October 1851 by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       , it is named for a
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(The_Tempest)" title="Ariel (The Tempest)">
        sky spirit
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Both Ariel and the slightly larger Uranian satellite
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       were discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       on 24 October 1851.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1851_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1851-15">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell,_letter_1851_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell,_letter_1851-16">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       , who discovered Uranus's two largest moons
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon_(moon)" title="Oberon (moon)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       in 1787, claimed to have observed four additional moons,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1798_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1798-17">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       this was never confirmed and those four objects are now thought to be spurious.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Struve_1848_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Struve_1848-18">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Holden_1874_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Holden_1874-19">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1874_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1874-20">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       All of Uranus's moons are named after characters from the works of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       . The names of all four satellites of Uranus then known were suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John Herschel
       </a>
       in 1852 at the request of Lassell.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1852_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1852-21">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel is named after the leading
       <a href="/wiki/Sylph" title="Sylph">
        sylph
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Rape_of_the_Lock" title="The Rape of the Lock">
         The Rape of the Lock
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Harrington_2011_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Harrington_2011-22">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also the name of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ariel_(Shakespeare)" title="Ariel (Shakespeare)">
        the spirit who serves Prospero
       </a>
       in Shakespeare's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Tempest" title="The Tempest">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_1949_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_1949-23">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon is also designated
       <b>
        Uranus I
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell,_letter_1851_16-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell,_letter_1851-16">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Among Uranus's
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus" title="Moons of Uranus">
        five major moons
       </a>
       , Ariel is the second closest to the planet, orbiting at the distance of about 190,000 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [f]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbit has a small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       and is
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclined
       </a>
       very little relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       of Uranus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbit_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-orbit-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
        orbital period
       </a>
       is around 2.5 Earth days, coincident with its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_period" title="Rotational period">
        rotational period
       </a>
       . This means that one side of the moon always faces the planet; a condition known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidal_lock" title="Tidal lock">
        tidal lock
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel's orbit lies completely inside the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Uranus" title="Magnetosphere of Uranus">
        Uranian magnetosphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The trailing
       <a href="/wiki/Sphere#Hemisphere" title="Sphere">
        hemispheres
       </a>
       (those facing away from their directions of orbit) of airless satellites orbiting inside a magnetosphere like Ariel are struck by magnetospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Plasma_(physics)" title="Plasma (physics)">
        plasma
       </a>
       co-rotating with the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986-26">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This bombardment may lead to the darkening of the trailing hemispheres observed for all Uranian moons except Oberon (see below).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel also captures magnetospheric charged particles, producing a pronounced dip in energetic particle count near the moon's orbit observed by
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       in 1986.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Krimigis_Armstrong_et_al._1986_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Krimigis_Armstrong_et_al._1986-27">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Ariel, like Uranus, orbits the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus#Axial_tilt" title="Uranus">
        almost on its side
       </a>
       relative to its rotation, its northern and southern hemispheres face either directly towards or directly away from the Sun at the
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstices
       </a>
       . This means it is subject to an extreme seasonal cycle; just as Earth's poles see
       <a href="/wiki/Polar_night" title="Polar night">
        permanent night
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Midnight_sun" title="Midnight sun">
        daylight
       </a>
       around the solstices, so Ariel's poles see permanent night or daylight for half a Uranian year (42 Earth years), with the Sun rising close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zenith
       </a>
       over one of the poles at each solstice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       flyby coincided with the 1986 southern summer solstice, when nearly the entire northern hemisphere was dark. Once every 42 years, when Uranus has an
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       and its equatorial plane intersects the Earth, mutual
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       of Uranus's moons become possible. A number of such events occurred in 2007–2008, including an occultation of Ariel by Umbriel on 19 August 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-occultations_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-occultations-28">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Currently Ariel is not involved in any
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with other Uranian satellites. In the past, however, it may have been in a 5:3 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       , which could have been partially responsible for the heating of that moon (although the maximum heating attributable to a former 1:3 resonance of Umbriel with Miranda was likely about three times greater).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_Wisdom_1990-29">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel may have once been locked in the 4:1 resonance with Titania, from which it later escaped.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_1990_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_1990-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Escape from a mean motion resonance is much easier for the moons of Uranus than for those of
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       , due to Uranus's lesser degree of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oblate_spheroid" title="Oblate spheroid">
        oblateness
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_1990_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_1990-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This resonance, which was likely encountered about 3.8 billion years ago, would have increased Ariel's
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       , resulting in tidal friction due to time-varying
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
        tidal forces
       </a>
       from Uranus. This would have caused warming of the moon's interior by as much as 20
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvins" title="Kelvins">
        K
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_1990_30-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_1990-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_internal_structure">
        Composition and internal structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition and internal structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="729" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/250px-Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/375px-Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/500px-Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ariel_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Size comparison of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         , and Ariel.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Ariel is the fourth largest of the Uranian moons, and may have the third greatest
       <a href="/wiki/Mass" title="Mass">
        mass
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32">
        <a href="#cite_note-32">
         [g]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moon's density is 1.66 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992-7">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which indicates that it consists of roughly equal parts
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       and a dense non-ice component.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter could consist of
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbonaceous
       </a>
       material including heavy
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        organic compounds
       </a>
       known as
       <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">
        tholins
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The presence of water ice is supported by
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic" title="Spectroscopic">
        spectroscopic
       </a>
       observations, which have revealed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">
        crystalline
       </a>
       water ice on the surface of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Water ice
       <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">
        absorption bands
       </a>
       are stronger on Ariel's leading hemisphere than on its trailing hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cause of this asymmetry is not known, but it may be related to bombardment by charged particles from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Uranus" title="Magnetosphere of Uranus">
        Uranus's magnetosphere
       </a>
       , which is stronger on the trailing hemisphere (due to the plasma's co-rotation).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The energetic particles tend to
       <a href="/wiki/Sputtering" title="Sputtering">
        sputter
       </a>
       water ice, decompose
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       trapped in ice as
       <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">
        clathrate hydrate
       </a>
       and darken other organics, leaving a dark, carbon-rich
       <a href="/wiki/Residue_(chemistry)" title="Residue (chemistry)">
        residue
       </a>
       behind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Except for water, the only other compound identified on the surface of Ariel by
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">
        infrared spectroscopy
       </a>
       is
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_dioxide" title="Carbon dioxide">
        carbon dioxide
       </a>
       (CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ), which is concentrated mainly on its trailing hemisphere. Ariel shows the strongest spectroscopic evidence for CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       of any Uranian satellite,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and was the first Uranian satellite on which this compound was discovered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The origin of the carbon dioxide is not completely clear. It might be produced locally from
       <a href="/wiki/Carbonate" title="Carbonate">
        carbonates
       </a>
       or organic materials under the influence of the energetic charged particles coming from Uranus's magnetosphere or solar
       <a href="/wiki/Ultraviolet" title="Ultraviolet">
        ultraviolet
       </a>
       radiation. This hypothesis would explain the asymmetry in its distribution, as the trailing hemisphere is subject to a more intense magnetospheric influence than the leading hemisphere. Another possible source is the
       <a href="/wiki/Outgassing" title="Outgassing">
        outgassing
       </a>
       of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Primordial_element" title="Primordial element">
        primordial
       </a>
       CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       trapped by water ice in Ariel's interior. The escape of CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       from the interior may be related to past geological activity on this moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_12-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Given its size, rock/ice composition and the possible presence of salt or
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       in solution to lower the freezing point of water, Ariel's interior may be
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_differentiation" title="Planetary differentiation">
        differentiated
       </a>
       into a rocky
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       surrounded by an icy
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If this is the case, the radius of the core (372 km) is about 64% of the radius of the moon, and its mass is around 56% of the moon's mass—the parameters are dictated by the moon's composition. The pressure in the center of Ariel is about 0.3
       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
        GPa
       </a>
       (3
       <a href="/wiki/Bar_(unit)" title="Bar (unit)">
        kbar
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The current state of the icy mantle is unclear, although the existence of a subsurface ocean is considered unlikely by some,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but possible by others.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sci_Am_2017_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sci_Am_2017-34">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Albedo_and_color">
        Albedo and color
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Albedo and color">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Ariel is the most reflective of Uranus's moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its surface shows an
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_surge" title="Opposition surge">
        opposition surge
       </a>
       : the reflectivity decreases from 53% at a phase angle of 0° (
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        geometrical albedo
       </a>
       ) to 35% at an angle of about 1°. The
       <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">
        Bond albedo
       </a>
       of Ariel is about 23%—the highest among Uranian satellites.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The surface of Ariel is generally neutral in color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There may be an asymmetry between the leading and trailing hemispheres;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-36">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the latter appears to be redder than the former by 2%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [h]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel's surface generally does not demonstrate any correlation between albedo and geology on the one hand and color on the other hand. For instance, canyons have the same color as the cratered terrain. However, bright impact deposits around some fresh craters are slightly bluer in color.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_35-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_36-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-36">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       There are also some slightly blue spots, which do not correspond to any known surface features.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_36-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-36">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features">
        Surface features
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Surface features">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       See also:
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_geological_features_on_Ariel" title="List of geological features on Ariel">
        List of geological features on Ariel
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The observed surface of Ariel can be divided into three terrain types: cratered terrain, ridged terrain, and plains.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The main surface features are
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Impact_craters" title="Impact craters">
        impact craters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Canyon" title="Canyon">
        canyons
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_scarp" title="Fault scarp">
        fault scarps
       </a>
       , ridges, and
       <a href="/wiki/Trough_(geology)" title="Trough (geology)">
        troughs
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg">
         <img alt="dark, angular features cut by smooth ravines into triangles, cast into high contrast by sunlight" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="512" data-file-width="512" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg/220px-Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg/330px-Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg/440px-Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ariel%27s_transecting_valleys.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
          Graben
         </a>
         (chasmata) near Ariel's
         <a href="/wiki/Terminator_(solar)" title="Terminator (solar)">
          terminator
         </a>
         . Their floors are covered by smooth material, possibly extruded from beneath via
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanism" title="Cryovolcanism">
          cryovolcanism
         </a>
         . Several are cut by
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sinuous" title="Sinuous">
          sinuous
         </a>
         central grooves, e.g. Sprite and Leprechaun valles above and below the triangular
         <a href="/wiki/Horst_(geology)" title="Horst (geology)">
          horst
         </a>
         near the bottom.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The cratered terrain, a rolling surface covered by numerous impact craters and centered on Ariel's south pole, is the moon's oldest and most geographically extensive
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_unit" title="Geological unit">
        geological unit
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is intersected by a network of scarps, canyons (graben), and narrow ridges mainly occurring in Ariel's mid-southern latitudes.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The canyons, known as
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chasmata" title="Chasmata">
         chasmata
        </a>
       </i>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schenk_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-schenk-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       probably represent
       <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
        graben
       </a>
       formed by
       <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">
        extensional faulting
       </a>
       , which resulted from global tensional stresses caused by the freezing of water (or aqueous ammonia) in the moon's interior (see below).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They are 15–50 km wide and trend mainly in an east- or northeasterly direction.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The floors of many canyons are convex; rising up by 1–2 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schenk_40-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-schenk-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Sometimes the floors are separated from the walls of canyons by grooves (troughs) about 1 km wide.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schenk_40-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-schenk-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The widest graben have grooves running along the crests of their convex floors, which are called
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Vallis_(planetary_geology)" title="Vallis (planetary geology)">
         valles
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The longest canyon is
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kachina_Chasma" title="Kachina Chasma">
        Kachina Chasma
       </a>
       , at over 620 km in length (the feature extends into the hemisphere of Ariel that
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       did not see illuminated).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_39-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stryk_2008_41-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stryk_2008-41">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The second main terrain type—ridged terrain—comprises bands of ridges and troughs hundreds of kilometers in extent. It bounds the cratered terrain and cuts it into polygons. Within each band, which can be up to 25 to 70 km wide, are individual ridges and troughs up to 200 km long and between 10 and 35 km apart. The bands of ridged terrain often form continuations of canyons, suggesting that they may be a modified form of the graben or the result of a different reaction of the crust to the same extensional stresses, such as brittle failure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Ariel_USGS.png">
         <img alt="a patch of observed surface is lit in light blue, against a blank disc representing the moon's entire diameter" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="347" data-file-width="347" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Ariel_USGS.png/220px-Ariel_USGS.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Ariel_USGS.png/330px-Ariel_USGS.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/Ariel_USGS.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Ariel_USGS.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         False-color map of Ariel. The prominent noncircular
         <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
          crater
         </a>
         below and left of center is
         <a href="/wiki/Yangoor_(crater)" title="Yangoor (crater)">
          Yangoor
         </a>
         . Part of it was erased during formation of ridged
         <a href="/wiki/Terrain" title="Terrain">
          terrain
         </a>
         via
         <a href="/wiki/Extensional_tectonics" title="Extensional tectonics">
          extensional tectonics
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The youngest terrain observed on Ariel are the plains: relatively low-lying smooth areas that must have formed over a long period of time, judging by their varying
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_counting" title="Crater counting">
        levels of cratering
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The plains are found on the floors of canyons and in a few irregular depressions in the middle of the cratered terrain.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the latter case they are separated from the cratered terrain by sharp boundaries, which in some cases have a lobate pattern.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The most likely origin for the plains is through volcanic processes; their linear vent geometry, resembling terrestrial
       <a href="/wiki/Shield_volcano" title="Shield volcano">
        shield volcanoes
       </a>
       , and distinct topographic margins suggest that the erupted liquid was very viscous, possibly a supercooled water/ammonia solution, with solid ice volcanism also a possibility.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schenk_40-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-schenk-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The thickness of these hypothetical cryolava flows is estimated at 1–3 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-schenk_40-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-schenk-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The canyons must therefore have formed at a time when endogenic resurfacing was still taking place on Ariel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A few of these areas appear to be less than 100 million years old, suggesting that Ariel may still be geologically active in spite of its relatively small size and lack of current tidal heating.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
        <a href="#cite_note-42">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Ariel appears to be fairly evenly cratered compared to other moons of Uranus;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the relative paucity of large craters
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [i]
        </a>
       </sup>
       suggests that its surface does not date to the Solar System's formation, which means that Ariel must have been completely resurfaced at some point of its history.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel's past geologic activity is believed to have been driven by
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_acceleration#Tidal_heating" title="Tidal acceleration">
        tidal heating
       </a>
       at a time when its orbit was more eccentric than currently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tittemore_1990_30-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tittemore_1990-30">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The largest crater observed on Ariel,
       <a href="/wiki/Yangoor_(crater)" title="Yangoor (crater)">
        Yangoor
       </a>
       , is only 78 km across,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS_39-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and shows signs of subsequent deformation. All large craters on Ariel have flat floors and central peaks, and few of the craters are surrounded by bright ejecta deposits. Many craters are polygonal, indicating that their appearance was influenced by the preexisting crustal structure. In the cratered plains there are a few large (about 100 km in diameter) light patches that may be degraded impact craters. If this is the case they would be similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Palimpsest_(planetary_astronomy)" title="Palimpsest (planetary astronomy)">
        palimpsests
       </a>
       on
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Ganymede_(moon)" title="Ganymede (moon)">
        Ganymede
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It has been suggested that a circular depression 245 km in diameter located at 10°S 30°E is a large, highly degraded impact structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution">
        Origin and evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Ariel is thought to have formed from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        accretion disc
       </a>
       or subnebula; a disc of gas and dust that either existed around Uranus for some time after its formation or was created by the giant impact that most likely gave Uranus its large
       <a href="/wiki/Axial_tilt" title="Axial tilt">
        obliquity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The precise composition of the subnebula is not known; however, the higher density of Uranian moons compared to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        moons of Saturn
       </a>
       indicates that it may have been relatively water-poor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
        <a href="#cite_note-47">
         [j]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Significant amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       may have been present in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       (CO) and
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        molecular nitrogen
       </a>
       (N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       ), instead of methane and
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_46-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moons that formed in such a subnebula would contain less water ice (with CO and N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       trapped as clathrate) and more rock, explaining the higher density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The accretion process probably lasted for several thousand years before the moon was fully formed.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_46-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Models suggest that impacts accompanying accretion caused heating of Ariel's outer layer, reaching a maximum temperature of around 195 K at a depth of about 31 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_48-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-48">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the end of formation, the subsurface layer cooled, while the interior of Ariel heated due to decay of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">
        radioactive elements
       </a>
       present in its rocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cooling near-surface layer contracted, while the interior expanded. This caused strong
       <a href="/wiki/Stress_(mechanics)" title="Stress (mechanics)">
        extensional stresses
       </a>
       in the moon's crust reaching estimates of 30
       <a href="/wiki/Pascal_(unit)" title="Pascal (unit)">
        MPa
       </a>
       , which may have led to cracking.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillier_Squyres_1991_49-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hillier_Squyres_1991-49">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some present-day scarps and canyons may be a result of this process,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which lasted for about 200 million years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillier_Squyres_1991_49-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hillier_Squyres_1991-49">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The initial
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        accretional heating
       </a>
       together with continued decay of radioactive elements and likely tidal heating may have led to melting of the ice if an
       <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">
        antifreeze
       </a>
       like ammonia (in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">
        ammonia hydrate
       </a>
       ) or some
       <a href="/wiki/Salt_(chemistry)" title="Salt (chemistry)">
        salt
       </a>
       was present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_48-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-48">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The melting may have led to the separation of ice from rocks and formation of a rocky core surrounded by an icy mantle.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A layer of liquid water (ocean) rich in dissolved ammonia may have formed at the core–mantle boundary. The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic_temperature" title="Eutectic temperature">
        eutectic temperature
       </a>
       of this mixture is 176 K.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_33-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ocean, however, is likely to have frozen long ago. The freezing of the water likely led to the expansion of the interior, which may have been responsible for the formation of the canyons and obliteration of the ancient surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The liquids from the ocean may have been able to erupt to the surface, flooding floors of canyons in the process known as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanism" title="Cryovolcanism">
        cryovolcanism
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_48-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-48">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Thermal modeling of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Dione_(moon)" title="Dione (moon)">
        Dione
       </a>
       , which is similar to Ariel in size, density, and surface temperature, suggests that solid state convection could have lasted in Ariel's interior for billions of years, and that temperatures in excess of 173
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kelvins" title="Kelvins">
        K
       </a>
       (the melting point of aqueous ammonia) may have persisted near its surface for several hundred million years after formation, and near a billion years closer to the core.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_and_exploration">
        Observation and exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Observation and exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Uranus" title="Exploration of Uranus">
        Exploration of Uranus
       </a>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Arieluranus.jpg">
         <img alt="the planet Uranus is seen through the Hubble telescope, its atmosphere defined by bands of electric blue and green. Ariel appears as a white dot floating above it, casting a dark shadow below" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="400" data-file-width="400" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Arieluranus.jpg/220px-Arieluranus.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b9/Arieluranus.jpg/330px-Arieluranus.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b9/Arieluranus.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Arieluranus.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
          HST
         </a>
         image of Ariel transiting Uranus, complete with shadow
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of Ariel is 14.8;
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Arlot_Sicardy_2008_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Arlot_Sicardy_2008-14">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       similar to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       near
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       . However, while Pluto can be seen through a telescope of 30 cm
       <a href="/wiki/Aperture" title="Aperture">
        aperture
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Singapore_Science_Centre,_Pluto_50-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Singapore_Science_Centre,_Pluto-50">
         [40]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ariel, due to its proximity to Uranus's glare, is often not visible to telescopes of 40 cm aperture.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Sinnott_&amp;_Ashford_51-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Sinnott_&amp;_Ashford-51">
         [41]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The only close-up images of Ariel were obtained by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       probe, which photographed the moon during its flyby of Uranus in January 1986. The closest approach of
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       to Ariel was 127,000 km (79,000 mi)—significantly less than the distances to all other Uranian moons except Miranda.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_1987_52-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stone_1987-52">
         [42]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The best images of Ariel have a spatial resolution of about 2 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       They cover about 40% of the surface, but only 35% was photographed with the quality required for
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_mapping" title="Geological mapping">
        geological mapping
       </a>
       and crater counting.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987_38-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia_('Ariel')_1987-38">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the flyby the southern hemisphere of Ariel (like those of the other moons) was pointed towards the Sun, so the northern (dark) hemisphere could not be studied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_25-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-25">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No other spacecraft has ever visited the Uranian system, and no mission to Uranus and its moons is planned.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NASA_Solar_System_Exploration_2010_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-NASA_Solar_System_Exploration_2010-53">
         [43]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The possibility of sending the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini-Huygens" title="Cassini-Huygens">
        Cassini spacecraft
       </a>
       to Uranus was evaluated during its mission extension planning phase.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiker_54-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spiker-54">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It would have taken about twenty years to get to the Uranian system after departing Saturn, and these plans were scrapped in favour of remaining at Saturn and eventually destroying the spacecraft in Saturn's atmosphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spiker_54-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-spiker-54">
         [44]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Transits">
        Transits
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Ariel_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Transits">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       On 26 July 2006, the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       captured a rare transit made by Ariel on Uranus, which cast a shadow that could be seen on the Uranian cloud tops. Such events are rare and only occur around
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinoxes
       </a>
       , as the moon's orbital plane about Uranus is tilted 98° to Uranus's orbital plane about the Sun.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-transit_55-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-transit-55">
         [45]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Another transit, in 2008, was recorded by the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">
        European Southern Observatory
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-European_Southern_Observatory_2008_56-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-European_Southern_Observatory_2008-56">
         [46]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
