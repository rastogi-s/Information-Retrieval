      <p>
       The
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Topography" title="Topography">
         topography
        </a>
        of the
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         Moon
        </a>
       </b>
       has been measured by the methods of laser altimetry and stereo image analysis, most recently from data obtained during the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clementine_mission" title="Clementine mission">
        Clementine mission
       </a>
       . The most visible topographic feature is the giant far side
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/South_Pole-Aitken_basin" title="South Pole-Aitken basin">
        South Pole-Aitken basin
       </a>
       , which possesses the lowest
       <a href="/wiki/Elevation" title="Elevation">
        elevations
       </a>
       of the Moon. The highest elevations are found just to the north-east of this basin, and it has been suggested that this area might represent thick
       <a href="/wiki/Ejecta" title="Ejecta">
        ejecta
       </a>
       deposits that were emplaced during an oblique South Pole-Aitken basin impact event. Other large impact basins, such as the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_mare" title="Lunar mare">
        maria
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Imbrium" title="Mare Imbrium">
        Imbrium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis" title="Mare Serenitatis">
        Serenitatis
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Crisium" title="Mare Crisium">
        Crisium
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Smythii" title="Mare Smythii">
        Smythii
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Mare_Orientale" title="Mare Orientale">
        Orientale
       </a>
       , also possess regionally low elevations and elevated
       <a href="/wiki/Rim_(craters)" title="Rim (craters)">
        rims
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Another distinguishing feature of the Moon's shape is that the elevations are on average about 1.9
       <a href="/wiki/Kilometre" title="Kilometre">
        km
       </a>
       higher on the far side than the near side. If it is assumed that the
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       is in
       <a href="/wiki/Isostasy" title="Isostasy">
        isostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       , and that the density of the crust is everywhere the same, then the higher elevations would be associated with a thicker crust. Using gravity, topography and
       <a href="/wiki/Seismology" title="Seismology">
        seismic
       </a>
       data, the crust is thought to be on average about
       <span class="nowrap">
        50 ± 15 km
       </span>
       thick, with the far-side crust being on average thicker than the near side by about 15 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-W06_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-W06-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
