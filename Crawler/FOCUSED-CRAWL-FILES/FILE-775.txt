      <p>
       <b>
        Mooning
       </b>
       is the act of displaying one's bare
       <a href="/wiki/Buttocks" title="Buttocks">
        buttocks
       </a>
       by removing clothing, e.g., by lowering the backside of one's
       <a href="/wiki/Trousers" title="Trousers">
        trousers
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Underpants" title="Underpants">
        underpants
       </a>
       , usually bending over, whether also exposing the genitals or not. Mooning is used in the English-speaking world to express
       <a href="/wiki/Protest" title="Protest">
        protest
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Contempt" title="Contempt">
        scorn
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disrespect" title="Disrespect">
        disrespect
       </a>
       , or provocation, or can be done for
       <a href="/wiki/Shock_value" title="Shock value">
        shock value
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Fun" title="Fun">
        fun
       </a>
       , or as form of
       <a href="/wiki/Exhibitionism" title="Exhibitionism">
        exhibitionism
       </a>
       . Some jurisdictions regard mooning to be
       <a href="/wiki/Indecent_exposure" title="Indecent exposure">
        indecent exposure
       </a>
       , sometimes depending on the context.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Word_history">
        Word history
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Word history">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
         Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       has been a common shape metaphor for the
       <a href="/wiki/Buttocks" title="Buttocks">
        buttocks
       </a>
       in English since 1743, and the verb
       <i>
        to moon
       </i>
       has meant "to expose to (moon)light" since 1601.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As documented by McLaren, "'mooning', or exposing one's butt to shame an enemy ... had a long pedigree in peasant culture" throughout the
       <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">
        Middle Ages
       </a>
       , and in many nations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-McLaren_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-McLaren-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Formerly, "mooning" was slang for "wandering idly" and "romantically pining".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Although the practice of mooning was widespread by the 19th century, the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Oxford_English_Dictionary" title="Oxford English Dictionary">
         Oxford English Dictionary
        </a>
       </i>
       dates the use of "moon" and "mooning" to describe the act to student slang of the 1960s, when the gesture became increasingly popular among students at universities in the United States.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Slate_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Slate-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Legal_status">
        Legal status
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Legal status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The legal position related to mooning varies between
       <a href="/wiki/Jurisdiction_(area)" title="Jurisdiction (area)">
        jurisdictions
       </a>
       ; some consider it
       <a href="/wiki/Indecent_exposure" title="Indecent exposure">
        indecent exposure
       </a>
       , while others classify it as legal self-expression.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_various_countries_and_cultures">
        In various countries and cultures
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: In various countries and cultures">

      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Australia">
        Australia
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Australia">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <h4>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Victoria">
        Victoria
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Victoria">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h4>
      <p>
       In 2016, mooning in a public place in Victoria was made a criminal offence/something for which one could be punished.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="New_Zealand">
        New Zealand
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: New Zealand">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       <b>
        Whakapohane
       </b>
       is the
       <a href="/wiki/M%C4%81ori_people" title="Māori people">
        Māori
       </a>
       practice of baring one's buttocks with the intent to offend. It symbolises the birthing act and renders the recipient
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Tapu_(Polynesian_culture)#Noa" title="Tapu (Polynesian culture)">
         noa
        </a>
       </i>
       ("base").
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A modern example was that of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mihi_Kotukutuku_Stirling" title="Mihi Kotukutuku Stirling">
        Mihi Kotukutuku Stirling
       </a>
       . She stood on the
       <a href="/wiki/Marae" title="Marae">
        marae
       </a>
       (sacred area) and the chief of the
       <a href="/wiki/Te_Arawa" title="Te Arawa">
        Te Arawa
       </a>
       tribes,
       <a href="/wiki/Mita_Taupopoki" title="Mita Taupopoki">
        Mita Taupopoki
       </a>
       , objected telling her that she must get off
       <i>
        his
       </i>
       marae as she was a woman. She stood her ground and when he had finished his objections she defended her position.
      </p>
      <p>
       She said that she was descended from a prior-born ancestor than the chief. She was not on
       <i>
        his
       </i>
       marae; she was on
       <i>
        her
       </i>
       marae. She exposed her genitals, telling the chief that that was where he came from. Those assembled were asked to gainsay her speech but no one came forward. The Maori gesture of Whakapohane had countered the argument that was aimed at her.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Salmond2017_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Salmond2017-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">
        United States
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooning&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: United States">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mooning.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1179" data-file-width="1744" height="149" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Mooning.jpg/220px-Mooning.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Mooning.jpg/330px-Mooning.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Mooning.jpg/440px-Mooning.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mooning.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Students at
         <a href="/wiki/Stanford_University" title="Stanford University">
          Stanford University
         </a>
         conduct a "mass mooning" in May 1995. This demonstration was in protest of
         <a href="/wiki/Censorship_in_the_United_States" title="Censorship in the United States">
          censorship in the American media
         </a>
         .
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In January 2006, a
       <a href="/wiki/Maryland" title="Maryland">
        Maryland
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/State_court_(United_States)" title="State court (United States)">
        state
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Circuit_court" title="Circuit court">
        circuit court
       </a>
       determined that mooning is a form of artistic expression protected by the
       <a href="/wiki/First_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="First Amendment to the United States Constitution">
        First Amendment
       </a>
       as a form of
       <a href="/wiki/Freedom_of_speech" title="Freedom of speech">
        speech
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Post-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The court ruled that
       <a href="/wiki/Indecent_exposure" title="Indecent exposure">
        indecent exposure
       </a>
       relates only to exposure of the genitals, adding that even though mooning was a "disgusting" and "demeaning" act to engage in, and had taken place in the presence of a minor, "If exposure of half of the buttocks constituted indecent exposure, any woman wearing a
       <a href="/wiki/Thong_(clothing)" title="Thong (clothing)">
        thong
       </a>
       at the beach at
       <a href="/wiki/Ocean_City,_Maryland" title="Ocean City, Maryland">
        Ocean City
       </a>
       would be guilty."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Post_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Post-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Defense attorneys had cited a case from 1983 of a woman who was arrested after protesting in front of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court_building" title="United States Supreme Court building">
        U.S. Supreme Court building
       </a>
       wearing nothing but a cardboard sign that covered the front of her body. In that case, the
       <a href="/wiki/District_of_Columbia_Court_of_Appeals" title="District of Columbia Court of Appeals">
        District of Columbia Court of Appeals
       </a>
       had ruled that indecent exposure is limited to a person's genitalia. No review of the case by a higher court took place since prosecutors dropped the case after the ruling.
      </p>
      <p>
       In California, an appellate court found in December 2000 that mooning does not constitute indecent exposure (and, therefore, does not subject the defendant to
       <a href="/wiki/Sex_offender" title="Sex offender">
        sex offender
       </a>
       registration laws) unless it can be proven beyond reasonable doubt that the conduct was sexually motivated.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Notable_incidents">
        Notable incidents

      <ul>
       <li>
        In 80 AD,
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Flavius_Josephus" title="Flavius Josephus">
         Flavius Josephus
        </a>
        recorded the first known incident of mooning. Josephus recorded that in 66 AD, at around the beginning of the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/First_Roman%E2%80%93Jewish_War" title="First Roman–Jewish War">
         First Roman–Jewish War
        </a>
        , a soldier in the
        <a href="/wiki/Roman_army" title="Roman army">
         Roman army
        </a>
        mooned
        <a href="/wiki/Jews" title="Jews">
         Jewish
        </a>
        pilgrims at the
        <a href="/wiki/Second_Temple" title="Second Temple">
         Jewish Temple
        </a>
        in
        <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">
         Jerusalem
        </a>
        who had gathered for Passover, and "spake such words as you might expect upon such a posture" causing a riot in which youths threw stones at the soldiers, who then called in reinforcements—the pilgrims panicked, and the ensuing
        <a href="/wiki/Stampede" title="Stampede">
         stampede
        </a>
        resulted in the death of ten thousand Jews.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
         <a href="#cite_note-11">
          [11]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
         <a href="#cite_note-12">
          [12]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        In the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siege_of_Constantinople_(1204)" title="Siege of Constantinople (1204)">
         Siege of Constantinople in 1204
        </a>
        , the Greeks exposed their bare buttocks to the Crusaders after they repulsed them from the walls.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
         <a href="#cite_note-13">
          [13]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
         <a href="#cite_note-14">
          [14]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        During the
        <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Battle of Crécy">
         Battle of Crécy
        </a>
        in 1346, when king
        <a href="/wiki/Edward_III_of_England" title="Edward III of England">
         Edward III
        </a>
        of
        <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">
         England
        </a>
        took
        <a href="/wiki/Caen" title="Caen">
         Caen
        </a>
        on the way to
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cr%C3%A9cy" title="Crécy">
         Crécy
        </a>
        , several hundred
        <a href="/wiki/Normandy" title="Normandy">
         Norman
        </a>
        soldiers "exposed their backsides to the English
        <a href="/wiki/Archery" title="Archery">
         archers
        </a>
        and many of them paid a high price for doing so".
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">

         </a>

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">

        </sup>

       <li>
        At the
        <a href="/wiki/Siege_of_Nice" title="Siege of Nice">
         Siege of Nice
        </a>
        , in the summer of 1543, Catherine Ségurane, a common washerwoman, led the townspeople into battle. Legend has it that she took the lead in defending the city by standing before the invading forces and exposing her bare bottom.
       </li>
       <li>
        A number of early explorers of the Atlantic coastline noted that the
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Etchemin" title="Etchemin">
         Etchemin
        </a>
        tribe of Maine practiced this custom.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
         <a href="#cite_note-17">
          [17]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        Since 1979, The
        <i>
         Annual Mooning of Amtrak
        </i>
        has been an annual tradition in
        <a href="/wiki/Laguna_Niguel,_California" title="Laguna Niguel, California">
         Laguna Niguel
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="/wiki/California" title="California">
         California
        </a>
        on the second Saturday of July, where many people spend the day mooning passing
        <a href="/wiki/Amtrak" title="Amtrak">
         Amtrak
        </a>
        trains;
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
         <a href="#cite_note-18">
          [18]
         </a>
        </sup>

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
         <a href="#cite_note-19">

         Diana, Princess of Wales
        </a>

        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prince_Charles" title="Prince Charles">
