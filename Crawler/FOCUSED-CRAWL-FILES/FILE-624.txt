   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Lunar space elevator
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       A
       <b>
        lunar space elevator
       </b>
       or
       <b>
        lunar spacelift
       </b>
       is a proposed transportation system for moving a mechanical climbing vehicle up and down a ribbon-shaped tethered cable that is set between the surface of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       "at the bottom" and a docking port suspended tens of thousands of kilometers above in space at the top.
      </p>
      <p>
       It is similar in concept to the better known
       <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">
        Earth-based space elevator
       </a>
       idea, but since the Moon's surface gravity is much lower than the Earth's, the engineering requirements for constructing a lunar elevator system can be met using currently available materials and technology. For a lunar elevator, the cable or
       <a href="/wiki/Tether" title="Tether">
        tether
       </a>
       extends considerably farther out from the lunar surface into space than one that would be used in an Earth-based system. However, the main function of a space elevator system is the same in either case; both allow for a reusable, controlled means of transporting payloads of cargo, or possibly people, between a base station at the bottom of a
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity_well" title="Gravity well">
        gravity well
       </a>
       and a docking port in outer space.
      </p>
      <p>
       A lunar elevator could significantly reduce the costs and improve reliability of soft-landing equipment on the lunar surface. For example, it would permit the use of mass-efficient (high specific impulse), low thrust drives such as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ion_drive" title="Ion drive">
        ion drives
       </a>
       which otherwise cannot land on the Moon. Since the docking port would be connected to the cable in a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity" title="Microgravity">
        microgravity
       </a>
       environment, these and other drives can reach the cable from
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       (LEO) with minimal launched fuel from Earth. With conventional
       <a href="/wiki/Rocket" title="Rocket">
        rockets
       </a>
       , the fuel needed to reach the lunar surface from LEO is many times the landed mass, thus the elevator can reduce launch costs for payloads bound for the lunar surface by a similar factor.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Location">
        Location
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Location">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       There are two points in space where an elevator's docking port could maintain a stable, lunar-synchronous position: the Earth-Moon Lagrange points
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       is 56,000 km away from the Earth-facing side of the Moon, (at the lunar equator) and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       is 67,000 km from the center of the Moon's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Far_side_(Moon)" title="Far side (Moon)">
        far side
       </a>
       , in the exact opposite direction. At these points, the effect of the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Gravity" title="Gravity">
        gravity
       </a>
       and the effect of the
       <a href="/wiki/Centrifugal_force" title="Centrifugal force">
        centrifugal force
       </a>
       resulting from the elevator system's synchronous,
       <a href="/wiki/Rigid_body" title="Rigid body">
        rigid body
       </a>
       rotation cancel each other out. The gravitational stability of these Lagrange points are not permanent, (
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       are in unstable equilibrium along a straight line between Earth and Moon,) but so long as small inertial adjustments are made to account for minor gravitational perturbations, any object positioned there can remain stationary.
      </p>
      <p>
       Both of these positions are substantially farther up than the 36,000 km from Earth to geostationary orbit. Furthermore, the weight of the limb of the cable system extending down to the Moon would have to be balanced by the cable extending further up, and the Moon's slow rotation means the upper limb would have to be much longer than for an Earth-based system, or be topped by a much more massive counterweight. To suspend a kilogram of cable or payload just above the surface of the Moon would require 1,000 kg of counterweight, 26,000 km beyond
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       . (A smaller counterweight on a longer cable, e.g., 100 kg at a distance of 230,000 km — more than halfway to Earth — would have the same balancing effect.) Without the Earth's gravity to attract it, an
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       cable's lowest kilogram would require 1,000 kg of counterweight at a distance of 120,000 km from the Moon. The average Earth-Moon distance is 384,400 km.
      </p>
      <p>
       The anchor point of a space elevator is normally considered to be at the equator. However, there are several possible cases to be made for locating a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_base" title="Lunar base">
        lunar base
       </a>
       at one of the Moon's poles; a base on a
       <a href="/wiki/Peak_of_eternal_light" title="Peak of eternal light">
        peak of eternal light
       </a>
       could take advantage of near-continuous solar power, for example, or small quantities of water and other volatiles may be trapped in permanently shaded crater bottoms. A space elevator could be anchored near a lunar pole, though not directly at it. A tramway could be used to bring the cable the rest of the way to the pole, with the Moon's low gravity allowing much taller support towers and wider spans between them than would be possible on Earth.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Fabrication">
        Fabrication
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Fabrication">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Because of the Moon's lower gravity and lack of atmosphere, a lunar elevator would have less stringent requirements for the tensile strength of the material making up its cable than an Earth-tethered cable. An Earth-based elevator would require high strength-to-weight materials that are theoretically possible, but not yet fabricated in practice (e.g.,
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">
        carbon nanotubes
       </a>
       ). A lunar elevator, however, could be constructed using commercially available mass-produced high-strength para-aramid fibres (such as
       <a href="/wiki/Kevlar" title="Kevlar">
        Kevlar
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/M5_fiber" title="M5 fiber">
        M5
       </a>
       ) or
       <a href="/wiki/Ultra-high-molecular-weight_polyethylene#Fiber" title="Ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene">
        ultra-high-molecular-weight polyethylene fibre
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Compared to an Earth space elevator, there would be fewer geographic and political restrictions on the location of the surface connection. The connection point of a lunar elevator would not necessarily have to be directly under its center of gravity, and could even be near the poles, where evidence suggests there might be frozen water in deep craters that never see sunlight; if so, this might be collected and converted into rocket fuel.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cross-Section_Profile">
        Cross-Section Profile
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Cross-Section Profile">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Space elevator designs for Earth typically have a taper of the tether that provides a uniform stress profile rather than a uniform cross-section. Because the strength requirement of a lunar space elevator is much lower than that of an Earth space elevator, a uniform cross-section is possible for the lunar space elevator. The study done for NASA's Institute of Advanced Concepts states "Current composites have characteristic heights of a few hundred kilometers, which would require taper ratios of about 6 for Mars, 4 for the Moon, and about 6000 for the Earth. The mass of the Moon is small enough that a uniform cross-section lunar space elevator could be constructed, without any taper at all."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A uniform cross-section could make it possible for a lunar space elevator to be built in a double-tether pulley configuration. This configuration would greatly simplify repairs of a space elevator compared to a tapered elevator configuration. However a pulley configuration would require a strut at the counterweight hundreds of kilometers long to separate the up-tether from the down-tether and keep them from tangling. A pulley configuration might also allow the system capacity to be gradually expanded by stitching new tether material on at the Lagrange point as the tether rotated.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The idea of space elevators has been around since 1960 when
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Artsutanov" title="Yuri Artsutanov">
        Yuri Artsutanov
       </a>
       wrote a Sunday supplement to
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Pravda" title="Pravda">
         Pravda
        </a>
       </i>
       on how to build such a structure and the utility of geosynchronous orbit. His article however, was not known in the West.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       Then in 1966,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Isaacs" title="John Isaacs">
        John Isaacs
       </a>
       , a leader of a group of American Oceanographers at
       <a href="/wiki/Scripps_Institution_of_Oceanography" title="Scripps Institution of Oceanography">
        Scripps Institute
       </a>
       , published an article in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Science_(magazine)" title="Science (magazine)">
         Science
        </a>
       </i>
       about the concept of using thin wires hanging from a geostationary satellite. In that concept, the wires were to be thin (thin wires/tethers are now understood to be more susceptible to
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeoroid
       </a>
       damage). Like Artsutanov, Isaacs’ article also wasn’t well known to the aerospace community.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2012)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1972, James Cline submitted a paper to NASA describing a "mooncable" concept similar to a lunar elevator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       NASA responded negatively to the idea citing technical risk and lack of funds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1975,
       <a href="/wiki/Jerome_Pearson" title="Jerome Pearson">
        Jerome Pearson
       </a>
       independently came up with the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">
        Space elevator
       </a>
       concept and published it in
       <i>
        Acta Astronautica
       </i>
       . That made the aerospace community at large aware of the space elevator for the first time. His article inspired
       <a href="/wiki/Arthur_C._Clarke" title="Arthur C. Clarke">
        Sir Arthur Clarke
       </a>
       to write the novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Fountains_of_Paradise" title="The Fountains of Paradise">
         The Fountains of Paradise
        </a>
       </i>
       (published in 1979, almost simultaneously with
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Sheffield" title="Charles Sheffield">
        Charles Sheffield's
       </a>
       novel on the same topic,
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Web_Between_the_Worlds&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Web Between the Worlds (page does not exist)">
         The Web Between the Worlds
        </a>
       </i>
       ). In 1978 Pearson extended his theory to the moon and changed to using the Lagrangian points instead of having it in geostationary orbit.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1977, some papers of Soviet space pioneer
       <a href="/wiki/Friedrich_Zander" title="Friedrich Zander">
        Friedrich Zander
       </a>
       were posthumously published, revealing that he conceived of a lunar space tower in 1910
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2005 Jerome Pearson completed a study for NASA Institute of Advanced Concepts which showed the concept is technically feasible within the prevailing state of the art using existing commercially available materials.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In October 2011 on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/LiftPort" title="LiftPort">
        LiftPort
       </a>
       website Michael Laine announced that LiftPort is pursuing a Lunar space elevator as an interim goal before attempting a terrestrial elevator. At the 2011 Annual Meeting of the
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/leag/" rel="nofollow">
        <b>
         Lunar Exploration Analysis Group
        </b>
       </a>
       (LEAG), LiftPort CTO Marshall Eubanks presented a paper on the prototype Lunar Elevator co-authored by Laine.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In August 2012,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Liftport" title="Liftport">
        Liftport
       </a>
       announced that the project may actually start near 2020.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Materials">
        Materials
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Materials">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Unlike earth-anchored space elevators, the materials for lunar space elevators won’t require a lot of strength. Lunar elevators can be made with materials available today. Carbon nanotubes aren’t required to build the structure.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This would make it possible to build the elevator much sooner, since available carbon nanotube materials in sufficient quantities are still years away.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cain_2004_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cain_2004-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One material that has great potential is
       <a href="/wiki/M5_fiber" title="M5 fiber">
        M5 fiber
       </a>
       . This is a synthetic fiber that is lighter than Kevlar or Spectra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       According to Pearson, Levin, Oldson, and Wykes in their article The Lunar Space Elevator, an M5 ribbon 30 mm wide and 0.023 mm thick, would be able to support 2000 kg on the lunar surface (2005). It would also be able to hold 100 cargo vehicles, each with a mass of 580 kg, evenly spaced along the length of the elevator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Other materials that could be used are T1000G carbon fiber, Spectra 200,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dyneema" title="Dyneema">
        Dyneema
       </a>
       (used on the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/YES2" title="YES2">
        YES2
       </a>
       spacecraft), or
       <a href="/wiki/Zylon" title="Zylon">
        Zylon
       </a>
       . All of these materials have
       <a href="/wiki/Specific_strength" title="Specific strength">
        breaking lengths
       </a>
       of several hundred kilometers under 1
       <i>
        g
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       The materials will be used to manufacture the ribbon-shaped, tethered cable which will connect from the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L2" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         2
        </sub>
       </a>
       balance points to the surface of the moon. The climbing vehicles which will travel the length of these cables in a finished elevator system will not move very fast, thus simplifying some of the challenges of transferring cargo and maintaining structural integrity of the system. However, any small objects suspended in space for extended periods of time, like the tethered cables would be, are vulnerable to damage by
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeoroids
       </a>
       , so one possible method of improving their survivability would be to design a "multi-ribbon" system instead of just a single-tethered cable.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Such a system would have interconnections at regular intervals, so that if one section of ribbon is damaged, parallel sections could carry the load until robotic vehicles could arrive to replace the severed ribbon. The interconnections would be spaced about 100 km apart, which is small enough to allow a robotic climber to carry the mass of the replacement 100 km of ribbon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Climbing_vehicles">
        Climbing vehicles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Climbing vehicles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       One method of getting materials needed from the moon into orbit would be the use of robotic climbing vehicles.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These vehicles would consist of two large wheels pressing against the ribbons of the elevator to provide enough friction for lift.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The climbers could be set for horizontal or vertical ribbons.
      </p>
      <p>
       The wheels would be driven by electric motors, which would obtain their power from solar energy or beamed energy. The power required to climb the ribbon would depend upon the lunar gravity field, which drops off the first few percent of the distance to
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The power that a climber would require to traverse the ribbon drops in proportion to proximity to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       point. If a 540 kg climber traveled at a velocity of fifteen meters per second, by the time it was seven percent of the way to the
       <a href="/wiki/Lagrangian_point#L1" title="Lagrangian point">
        L
        <sub>
         1
        </sub>
       </a>
       point, the required power would drop to less than a hundred watts, versus 10 kilowatts at the surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       One problem with using a solar powered vehicle is the lack of sunlight during some parts of the trip. For half of every month, the solar arrays on the lower part of the ribbon would be in the shade.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One way to fix this problem would be to launch the vehicle at the base with a certain velocity then at the peak of the trajectory, attach it to the ribbon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Possible_uses">
        Possible uses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_space_elevator&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Possible uses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Materials from Earth may be sent into orbit and then down to the Moon to be used by lunar bases and installations.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Former
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._President" title="U.S. President">
        U.S. President
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/George_W._Bush" title="George W. Bush">
        George W. Bush
       </a>
       , in an address about his
       <a href="/wiki/Vision_for_Space_Exploration" title="Vision for Space Exploration">
        Vision for Space Exploration
       </a>
       , suggested that the Moon may serve as a cost-effective construction, launching and fueling
       <a href="/wiki/Colonization_of_the_Moon" title="Colonization of the Moon">
        site
       </a>
       for future space exploration missions. As President Bush noted,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Vision_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Vision-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        "(Lunar) soil contains raw materials that might be harvested and processed into rocket fuel or breathable air."
       </i>
       For example, the proposed
       <a href="/wiki/Ares_V" title="Ares V">
        Ares V
       </a>
       heavy-lift rocket system could cost-effectively
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LowCostMoon_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-LowCostMoon-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       deliver
       <a href="/wiki/Raw_material" title="Raw material">
        raw materials
       </a>
       from Earth to a
       <a href="/wiki/Space_dock" title="Space dock">
        docking station
       </a>
       , (connected to the lunar elevator as a counterweight,)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       where future spacecraft could be built and launched, while extracted lunar resources could be shipped up from a base on the Moon's surface, near the elevator's anchoring point. If the elevator was connected somehow to a lunar base built near the Moon's north pole, then workers could also mine the water ice which is known to exist there, providing an ample source of readily accessible water for the crew at the elevator's docking station.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Also, since the total energy needed for transit between the Moon and Mars is considerably less than for between Earth and Mars, this concept could lower some of the engineering obstacles to sending humans to Mars.
      </p>
      <p>
       The lunar elevator could also be used to transport supplies and materials from the surface of the moon into the Earth’s orbit and vice versa. According to Jerome Pearson, many of the Moon's material resources can be extracted and sent into Earth orbit more easily than if they were launched from the Earth's surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-13">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For example, lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Regolith" title="Regolith">
        regolith
       </a>
       itself could be used as massive material to shield space stations or crewed spacecraft on long missions from
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_flare" title="Solar flare">
        solar flares
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation" title="Van Allen radiation">
        Van Allen radiation
       </a>
       , and other kinds of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cosmic_radiation" title="Cosmic radiation">
        cosmic radiation
       </a>
       . The Moon's naturally occurring metals and minerals could be mined and used for construction. Lunar deposits of silicon, which could be used to build solar panels for massive
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Satellite_solar_power" title="Satellite solar power">
        satellite solar power
       </a>
       stations, seem particularly promising.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pearson_2005_1-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pearson_2005-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       One disadvantage of the lunar elevator is that the speed of the climbing vehicles may be too slow to efficiently serve as a human transportation system. In contrast to an Earth-based elevator, the longer distance from the docking station to the lunar surface would mean that any "elevator car" would need to be able to sustain a crew for several days, even weeks, before it reached its destination.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cain_2004_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Cain_2004-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
