             </a>
            </sup>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </div>
        </td>

      <p>
       <b>
        Oberon
       </b>
       , also designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Uranus IV
        </span>
       </b>
       , is the outermost major
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        moon
       </a>
       of the planet
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . It is the second-largest and second most massive of the Uranian moons, and the ninth most massive moon in the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_System" title="Solar System">
        Solar System
       </a>
       . Discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       in 1787, Oberon is named after the
       <a href="/wiki/Oberon" title="Oberon">
        mythical king of the fairies
       </a>
       who appears as a character in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shakespeare" title="Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       's
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">
         A Midsummer Night's Dream
        </a>
       </i>
       . Its orbit lies partially outside Uranus's
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       It is likely that Oberon formed from the
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_disk" title="Accretion disk">
        accretion disk
       </a>
       that surrounded Uranus just after the planet's formation. The moon consists of approximately equal amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Volatiles" title="Volatiles">
        ice
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       , and is probably differentiated into a rocky
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       and an icy
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       . A layer of liquid water may be present at the boundary between the mantle and the core. The surface of Oberon, which is dark and slightly red in color, appears to have been primarily shaped by asteroid and comet impacts. It is covered by numerous
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact craters
       </a>
       reaching 210 km in diameter. Oberon possesses a system of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Chasma" title="Chasma">
         chasmata
        </a>
       </i>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
        graben
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_scarp" title="Fault scarp">
        scarps
       </a>
       ) formed during crustal extension as a result of the expansion of its interior during its early evolution.
      </p>
      <p>
       The Uranian system has been studied up close only once: the spacecraft
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       took several images of Oberon in January 1986, allowing 40% of the moon's surface to be mapped.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_and_naming">
        Discovery and naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery and naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Oberon was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       on January 11, 1787; on the same day he discovered Uranus's largest moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1787_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1787-2">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1788_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1788-15">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He later reported the discoveries of four more satellites,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1798_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1798-16">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although they were subsequently revealed as spurious.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Struve_1848_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Struve_1848-17">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       For nearly fifty years following their discovery, Titania and Oberon would not be observed by any instrument other than William Herschel's,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel_1834_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel_1834-18">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although the moon can be seen from
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       with a present-day high-end amateur telescope.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Newton_Teece_1995_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Newton_Teece_1995-14">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       All of the moons of Uranus are named after characters created by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pope" title="Alexander Pope">
        Alexander Pope
       </a>
       . The name Oberon was derived from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Oberon_(Fairy_King)" title="Oberon (Fairy King)">
        Oberon
       </a>
       , the King of the Fairies in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/A_Midsummer_Night%27s_Dream" title="A Midsummer Night's Dream">
         A Midsummer Night's Dream
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kuiper_1949_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Kuiper_1949-19">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The names of all four satellites of Uranus then known were suggested by Herschel's son
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John
       </a>
       in 1852, at the request of
       <a href="/wiki/William_Lassell" title="William Lassell">
        William Lassell
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1852_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1852-20">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       who had discovered the other two moons,
       <a href="/wiki/Ariel_(moon)" title="Ariel (moon)">
        Ariel
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       , the year before.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1851_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1851-21">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The adjectival form of the name is
       <i>
        Oberonian,
       </i>
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˌ/: secondary stress follows">
            ˌ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɒ/: 'o' in 'body'">
            ɒ
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'r' in 'rye'">
            r
           </span>
           <span title="/oʊ/: 'o' in 'code'">
            oʊ
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'n' in 'nigh'">
            n
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Shakespeare_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Shakespeare-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Oberon was initially referred to as "the second satellite of Uranus", and in 1848 was given the designation
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Uranus II
        </span>
       </b>
       by William Lassell,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1848_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1848-22">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       although he sometimes used William Herschel's numbering (where Titania and Oberon are II and IV).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell_1850_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell_1850-23">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In 1851 Lassell eventually numbered all four known satellites in order of their distance from the planet by
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_numerals" title="Roman numerals">
        Roman numerals
       </a>
       , and since then Oberon has been designated
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         Uranus IV
        </span>
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lassell,_letter_1851_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Lassell,_letter_1851-24">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Oberon orbits Uranus at a distance of about 584,000 km, being the farthest from the planet among its five major moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-five_major_moons_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-five_major_moons-25">
         [e]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon's orbit has a small
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        orbital eccentricity
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_inclination" title="Orbital inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       relative to the
       <a href="/wiki/Equator" title="Equator">
        equator
       </a>
       of Uranus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-orbit_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-orbit-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its orbital period is around 13.5 days, coincident with its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotational_period" title="Rotational period">
        rotational period
       </a>
       . In other words, Oberon is a
       <a href="/wiki/Synchronous_orbit" title="Synchronous orbit">
        synchronous satellite
       </a>
       ,
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tidally_locked" title="Tidally locked">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       , with one face always pointing toward the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon spends a significant part of its orbit outside the Uranian
       <a href="/wiki/Magnetosphere" title="Magnetosphere">
        magnetosphere
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986-26">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       As a result, its surface is directly struck by the
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_wind" title="Solar wind">
        solar wind
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This is important, because the trailing hemispheres of satellites orbiting inside a magnetosphere are struck by the magnetospheric plasma, which co–rotates with the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986_26-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ness_Acuña_et_al._1986-26">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This bombardment may lead to the darkening of the trailing hemispheres, which is actually observed for all Uranian moons except Oberon (see below).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Because Uranus orbits the Sun almost on its side, and its moons orbit in the planet's equatorial plane, they (including Oberon) are subject to an extreme seasonal cycle. Both northern and southern
       <a href="/wiki/Poles_of_astronomical_bodies#Geographic_poles" title="Poles of astronomical bodies">
        poles
       </a>
       spend 42 years in a complete darkness, and another 42 years in continuous sunlight, with the sun rising close to the
       <a href="/wiki/Zenith" title="Zenith">
        zenith
       </a>
       over one of the poles at each
       <a href="/wiki/Solstice" title="Solstice">
        solstice
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       flyby coincided with the southern hemisphere's 1986 summer solstice, when nearly the entire northern hemisphere was in darkness. Once every 42 years, when Uranus has an
       <a href="/wiki/Equinox" title="Equinox">
        equinox
       </a>
       and its equatorial plane intersects the Earth, mutual
       <a href="/wiki/Occultation" title="Occultation">
        occultations
       </a>
       of Uranus's moons become possible. One such event, which lasted for about six minutes, was observed on May 4, 2007, when Oberon occulted Umbriel.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hidas_Christou_et_al._2008_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hidas_Christou_et_al._2008-27">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Composition_and_internal_structure">
        Composition and internal structure
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Composition and internal structure">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="729" height="168" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/250px-Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/375px-Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png/500px-Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png 2x" width="250"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oberon_Earth_Moon_Comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Size comparison of
         <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
          Earth
         </a>
         , the
         <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
          Moon
         </a>
         , and Oberon.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Oberon is the second largest and most massive of the Uranian moons after
       <a href="/wiki/Titania_(moon)" title="Titania (moon)">
        Titania
       </a>
       , and the ninth most massive moon in the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29">
        <a href="#cite_note-29">
         [f]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon's density of 1.63 g/cm³,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992_8-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jacobson_Campbell_et_al._1992-8">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is higher than the typical density of Saturn's satellites, indicates that it consists of roughly equal proportions of
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        water ice
       </a>
       and a dense non-ice component.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter could be made of
       <a href="/wiki/Rock_(geology)" title="Rock (geology)">
        rock
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbonaceous
       </a>
       material including heavy
       <a href="/wiki/Organic_compound" title="Organic compound">
        organic compounds
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The presence of water ice is supported by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spectroscopic" title="Spectroscopic">
        spectroscopic
       </a>
       observations, which have revealed
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">
        crystalline
       </a>
       water ice on the surface of the moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Water ice
       <a href="/wiki/Absorption_band" title="Absorption band">
        absorption bands
       </a>
       are stronger on Oberon's trailing hemisphere than on the leading hemisphere. This is the opposite of what is observed on other Uranian moons, where the leading hemisphere exhibits stronger water ice signatures.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cause of this asymmetry is not known, but it may be related to
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_gardening" title="Impact gardening">
        impact gardening
       </a>
       (the creation of soil via impacts) of the surface, which is stronger on the leading hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Meteorite_impact" title="Meteorite impact">
        Meteorite impacts
       </a>
       tend to sputter (knock out) ice from the surface, leaving dark non-ice material behind.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006_13-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Grundy_Young_et_al._2006-13">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The dark material itself may have formed as a result of radiation processing of
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Clathrate_hydrate" title="Clathrate hydrate">
        clathrates
       </a>
       or radiation darkening of other organic compounds.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_31-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-31">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Oberon may be differentiated into a rocky
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Core_(geology)" title="Core (geology)">
        core
       </a>
       surrounded by an icy
       <a href="/wiki/Mantle_(geology)" title="Mantle (geology)">
        mantle
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If this is the case, the radius of the core (480 km) is about 63% of the radius of the moon, and its mass is around 54% of the moon's mass—the proportions are dictated by the moon's composition. The pressure in the center of Oberon is about 0.5
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GPa" title="GPa">
        GPa
       </a>
       (5
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kbar" title="Kbar">
        kbar
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The current state of the icy mantle is unclear. If the ice contains enough
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       or other
       <a href="/wiki/Antifreeze" title="Antifreeze">
        antifreeze
       </a>
       , Oberon may possess a liquid
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ocean_layer" title="Ocean layer">
        ocean layer
       </a>
       at the core–mantle boundary. The thickness of this ocean, if it exists, is up to 40 km and its temperature is around 180 K.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the internal structure of Oberon depends heavily on its thermal history, which is poorly known at present.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Surface_features_and_geology">
        Surface features and geology
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Surface features and geology">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:268px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Oberon-NASA_names_en.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="266" data-file-width="266" height="266" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Oberon-NASA_names_en.png" width="266"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Oberon-NASA_names_en.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         A photo of Oberon. All named surface features are captioned.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Oberon is the second-darkest large moon of Uranus after
       <a href="/wiki/Umbriel_(moon)" title="Umbriel (moon)">
        Umbriel
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its surface shows a strong
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_surge" title="Opposition surge">
        opposition surge
       </a>
       : its reflectivity decreases from 31% at a phase angle of 0° (
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        geometrical albedo
       </a>
       ) to 22% at an angle of about 1°. Oberon has a low
       <a href="/wiki/Bond_albedo" title="Bond albedo">
        Bond albedo
       </a>
       of about 14%.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble_12-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Karkoschka_2001,_Hubble-12">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Its surface is generally red in color, except for fresh impact deposits, which are neutral or slightly blue.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990-32">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon is, in fact, the reddest among the major Uranian moons. Its trailing and leading hemispheres are asymmetrical: the latter is much redder than the former, because it contains more dark red material.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_31-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-31">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reddening of the surfaces is often a result of
       <a href="/wiki/Space_weathering" title="Space weathering">
        space weathering
       </a>
       caused by bombardment of the surface by charged particles and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Micrometeorites" title="Micrometeorites">
        micrometeorites
       </a>
       over the age of the Solar System.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bell_McCord_1991_31-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Bell_McCord_1991-31">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the color asymmetry of Oberon is more likely caused by accretion of a reddish material spiraling in from outer parts of the Uranian system, possibly from
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       , which would occur predominately on the leading hemisphere.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buratti_Mosher_1991_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buratti_Mosher_1991-33">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Scientists have recognized two classes of geological feature on Oberon:
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Chasma" title="Chasma">
        chasmata
       </a>
       ('canyons'—deep, elongated, steep-sided depressions
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS-Nomenclature_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS-Nomenclature-34">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which would probably be described as
       <a href="/wiki/Rift_valley" title="Rift valley">
        rift valleys
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Escarpment" title="Escarpment">
        escarpments
       </a>
       if on Earth).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon's surface is the most heavily cratered of all the Uranian moons, with a crater density approaching saturation—when the formation of new craters is balanced by destruction of old ones. This high number of craters indicates that Oberon has the most ancient surface among Uranus's moons.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The crater diameters range up to 206 kilometers for the largest known crater,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Hamlet_(crater)" title="Hamlet (crater)">
        Hamlet
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USGS:_Uranus:_Oberon:_Hamlet_36-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-USGS:_Uranus:_Oberon:_Hamlet-36">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Many large craters are surrounded by bright impact ejecta (
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_system" title="Ray system">
        rays
       </a>
       ) consisting of relatively fresh ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The largest craters, Hamlet, Othello and Macbeth, have floors made of a very dark material deposited after their formation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       A peak with a height of about 11 km was observed in some
       <i>
        Voyager
       </i>
       images near the south-eastern limb of Oberon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004-37">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which may be the central peak of a large impact basin with a diameter of about 375 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004_37-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Moore_Schenk_et_al._2004-37">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Oberon's surface is intersected by a system of canyons, which, however, are less widespread than those found on Titania.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The canyons' sides are probably
       <a href="/wiki/Fault_scarp" title="Fault scarp">
        scarps
       </a>
       produced by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Normal_fault" title="Normal fault">
        normal faults
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [g]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which can be either old or fresh: the latter
       <a href="/wiki/Transect" title="Transect">
        transect
       </a>
       the bright deposits of some large craters, indicating that they formed later.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft_1989_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft_1989-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The most prominent Oberonian canyon is
       <a href="/wiki/Mommur_Chasma" title="Mommur Chasma">
        Mommur Chasma
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mommur_40-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mommur-40">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The geology of Oberon was influenced by two competing forces:
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       formation and endogenic resurfacing.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft_1989_39-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft_1989-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The former acted over the moon's entire history and is primarily responsible for its present-day appearance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The latter processes were active for a period following the moon's formation. The endogenic processes were mainly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tectonic" title="Tectonic">
        tectonic
       </a>
       in nature and led to the formation of the canyons, which are actually giant cracks in the ice crust.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft_1989_39-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft_1989-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The canyons obliterated parts of the older surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft_1989_39-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft_1989-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cracking of the crust was caused by the expansion of Oberon by about 0.5%,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Croft_1989_39-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Croft_1989-39">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which occurred in two phases corresponding to the old and young canyons.
      </p>
      <p>
       The nature of the dark patches, which mainly occur on the leading hemisphere and inside craters, is not known. Some scientists hypothesized that they are of
       <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">
        cryovolcanic
       </a>
       origin (analogs of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_maria" title="Lunar maria">
        lunar maria
       </a>
       ),
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while others think that the impacts excavated dark material buried beneath the pure ice (
       <a href="/wiki/Crust_(geology)" title="Crust (geology)">
        crust
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990-32">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the latter case Oberon should be at least partially differentiated, with the ice crust lying atop the non-differentiated interior.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990_32-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Helfenstein_Hillier_et_al._1990-32">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Origin_and_evolution">
        Origin and evolution
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Origin and evolution">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Oberon is thought to have formed from an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Accretion_disc" title="Accretion disc">
        accretion disc
       </a>
       or subnebula: a disc of gas and dust that either existed around Uranus for some time after its formation or was created by the giant impact that most likely gave Uranus its large
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Obliquity" title="Obliquity">
        obliquity
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The precise composition of the subnebula is not known; however, the relatively high density of Oberon and other Uranian moons compared to the
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        moons of Saturn
       </a>
       indicates that it may have been relatively water-poor.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44">
        <a href="#cite_note-44">
         [h]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Significant amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbon
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Nitrogen" title="Nitrogen">
        nitrogen
       </a>
       may have been present in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">
        carbon monoxide
       </a>
       and N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       instead of methane and
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The moons that formed in such a subnebula would contain less water ice (with CO and N
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       trapped as clathrate) and more rock, explaining the higher density.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Oberon's accretion probably lasted for several thousand years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mousis_2004_43-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mousis_2004-43">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The impacts that accompanied accretion caused heating of the moon's outer layer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_45-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The maximum temperature of around 230 K was reached at the depth of about 60 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988_45-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Squyres_Reynolds_et_al._1988-45">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the end of formation, the subsurface layer cooled, while the interior of Oberon heated due to decay of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radioactivity" title="Radioactivity">
        radioactive elements
       </a>
       present in its rocks.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The cooling near-surface layer contracted, while the interior expanded. This caused strong
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extensional_stress" title="Extensional stress">
        extensional stresses
       </a>
       in the moon's crust leading to cracking. The present-day system of canyons may be a result of this process, which lasted for about 200 million years,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991_46-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       implying that any endogenous activity from this cause ceased billions of years ago.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The initial
       <a href="/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics)" title="Accretion (astrophysics)">
        accretional heating
       </a>
       together with continued decay of radioactive elements were probably strong enough to melt the ice
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991_46-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hillier_&amp;_Squyres_1991-46">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       if some antifreeze like ammonia (in the form of
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrate" title="Hydrate">
        ammonia hydrate
       </a>
       ) or some
       <a href="/wiki/Salt" title="Salt">
        salt
       </a>
       was present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further melting may have led to the separation of ice from rocks and formation of a rocky core surrounded by an icy mantle. A layer of liquid water ('ocean') rich in dissolved ammonia may have formed at the core–mantle boundary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eutectic_temperature" title="Eutectic temperature">
        eutectic temperature
       </a>
       of this mixture is 176 K.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006_30-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussmann_Sohl_et_al._2006-30">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If the temperature dropped below this value the ocean would have frozen by now. Freezing of the water would have led to expansion of the interior, which may have also contributed to the formation of canyon-like
       <a href="/wiki/Graben" title="Graben">
        graben
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Still, present knowledge of the evolution of Oberon is very limited.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Oberon_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_of_Uranus" title="Exploration of Uranus">
        Exploration of Uranus
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       So far the only close-up images of Oberon have been from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       probe, which photographed the moon during its flyby of Uranus in January 1986. Since the closest approach of
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       to Oberon was 470,600 km,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stone_1987_47-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stone_1987-47">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       the best images of this moon have spatial resolution of about 6 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The images cover about 40% of the surface, but only 25% of the surface was imaged with a resolution that allows
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geological_mapping" title="Geological mapping">
        geological mapping
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Plescia1987_35-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Plescia1987-35">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       At the time of the flyby the southern hemisphere of Oberon was pointed towards the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , so the dark northern hemisphere could not be studied.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986_11-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Smith_Soderblom_et_al._1986-11">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       No other spacecraft has ever visited the Uranian system, and no mission to this satellite is planned in the foreseeable future.
      </p>
