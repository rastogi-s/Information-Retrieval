      <p>
       <b>
        Phoebe
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'f' in 'find'">
            f
           </span>
           <span title="/iː/: 'ee' in 'fleece'">
            iː
           </span>
           <span title="'b' in 'buy'">
            b
           </span>
           <span title="/i/: 'y' in 'happy'">
            i
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          FEE
         </span>
         -bee
        </i>
       </a>
       ; Greek:
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language" title="Ancient Greek language">
        Ancient Greek
       </a>
       :
       <span lang="grc">
        Φοίβη
       </span>
       <i>
        Phoíbē
       </i>
       ) is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn" title="Moons of Saturn">
        satellite of Saturn
       </a>
       with a mean diameter of 213 km. It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">
        William Henry Pickering
       </a>
       on March 18, 1899
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtizLyfe/Charlie_Kovas_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-ArtizLyfe/Charlie_Kovas-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from photographic plates that had been taken starting on 16 August 1898 at the
       <a href="/wiki/Boyden_Observatory" title="Boyden Observatory">
        Boyden Observatory
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Arequipa" title="Arequipa">
        Arequipa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/DeLisle_Stewart" title="DeLisle Stewart">
        DeLisle Stewart
       </a>
       . It was the first satellite to be discovered photographically.
      </p>
      <p>
       Phoebe was the first target encountered upon the arrival of the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft in the Saturn system in 2004, and is thus unusually well-studied for an
       <a href="/wiki/Irregular_moon" title="Irregular moon">
        irregular satellite
       </a>
       of its size.
       <i>
        Cassini'
       </i>
       s trajectory to Saturn and time of arrival were specifically chosen to permit this flyby.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinez_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Martinez-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       After the encounter and its insertion into orbit,
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       did not go much beyond the orbit of
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Phoebe is roughly spherical and has a differentiated interior. It was spherical and hot early in its history and was battered out of roundness by repeated impacts. It is believed to be a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">
        centaur
       </a>
       that originated in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jewitt2007_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Jewitt2007-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Phoebe was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">
        William Henry Pickering
       </a>
       on 18 March 1899
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ArtizLyfe/Charlie_Kovas_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-ArtizLyfe/Charlie_Kovas-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       from photographic plates that had been taken starting on 16 August 1898 at the
       <a href="/wiki/Boyden_Observatory" title="Boyden Observatory">
        Boyden Observatory
       </a>
       near
       <a href="/wiki/Arequipa" title="Arequipa">
        Arequipa
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Peru" title="Peru">
        Peru
       </a>
       , by
       <a href="/wiki/DeLisle_Stewart" title="DeLisle Stewart">
        DeLisle Stewart
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickering1899_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pickering1899-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was the first satellite to be discovered photographically.
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Naming">
        Naming
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Naming">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Phoebe was named after
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(mythology)" title="Phoebe (mythology)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , a
       <a href="/wiki/Titan_(mythology)" title="Titan (mythology)">
        Titaness
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       that was associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Pickering1899_9-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Pickering1899-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is also designated Saturn IX in some scientific literature. The
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_nomenclature" title="Planetary nomenclature">
        nomenclature standards
       </a>
       have stated that features on Phoebe are to be named after characters in the Greek myth of
       <a href="/wiki/Jason" title="Jason">
        Jason and the Argonauts
       </a>
       . In
       <a href="/wiki/2005_in_science" title="2005 in science">
        2005
       </a>
       , the IAU officially named 24 craters
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (Acastus, Admetus, Amphion, Butes, Calais, Canthus, Clytius, Erginus, Euphemus, Eurydamas, Eurytion, Eurytus, Hylas, Idmon, Iphitus, Jason, Mopsus, Nauplius, Oileus, Peleus, Phlias, Talaus, Telamon, and Zetes).
      </p>
      <p>
       Toby Owen of the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_Hawaii" title="University of Hawaii">
        University of Hawaii
       </a>
       at Manoa, chairman of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       Outer Solar System Task Group said:
      </p>
      <blockquote class="templatequote">
       <p>
        We picked the legend of the Argonauts for Phoebe as it has some resonance with the exploration of the Saturn system by Cassini–Huygens. We can't say that our participating scientists include heroes like Hercules and Atalanta, but they do represent a wide, international spectrum of outstanding people who were willing to take the risk of joining this voyage to a distant realm in hopes of bringing back a grand prize.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit">
        Orbit
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbit">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Phoebe's orbit is
       <a href="/wiki/Retrograde_and_prograde_motion" title="Retrograde and prograde motion">
        retrograde
       </a>
       ; that is, it orbits Saturn opposite to Saturn's rotation. For more than 100 years, Phoebe was Saturn's outermost known moon, until the discovery of several smaller moons in 2000. Phoebe is almost 4 times more distant from Saturn than its nearest major neighbor (
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       ), and is substantially larger than any of the other moons orbiting planets at comparable distances.
      </p>
      <p>
       All of Saturn's
       <a href="/wiki/Regular_moon" title="Regular moon">
        regular moons
       </a>
       except Iapetus
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       very nearly in the plane of Saturn's equator. The outer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Irregular_satellite" title="Irregular satellite">
        irregular satellites
       </a>
       follow moderately to highly
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentric
       </a>
       orbits, and none are expected to rotate synchronously as all the inner moons of Saturn do (except for
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       ). See
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Saturn#Orbital_groups" title="Moons of Saturn">
        Saturn's satellites' families
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Phoebe_ring">
        Phoebe ring
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Phoebe ring">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2400" data-file-width="3000" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg/200px-Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg/300px-Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg/400px-Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saturn_largest_ring_Spitzer_telescope_20091006.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Artist's impression of the
         <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Phoebe_ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
          Phoebe ring
         </a>
         , which dwarfs the main rings
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Phoebe_ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Rings of Saturn § Phoebe ring
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       The Phoebe ring is one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn" title="Rings of Saturn">
        rings of Saturn
       </a>
       . This ring is tilted 27 degrees from Saturn's equatorial plane (and the other rings). It extends from at least 128 to 207
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Verbiscer_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Verbiscer-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       times the radius of Saturn; Phoebe orbits the planet at an average distance of 215 Saturn radii. The ring is about 20 times as thick as the diameter of the planet.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because the ring's particles are presumed to have originated from
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometeoroid" title="Micrometeoroid">
        micrometeoroid
       </a>
       impacts on Phoebe, they should share its
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion#Retrograde_orbit" title="Retrograde motion">
        retrograde orbit
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-sciencenews_large_ring_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-sciencenews_large_ring-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is opposite to the orbital motion of the next inner moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        Iapetus
       </a>
       . Inwardly migrating ring material would thus strike Iapetus's leading hemisphere, creating its
       <a href="/wiki/Iapetus_(moon)#Two-tone_coloration" title="Iapetus (moon)">
        two-tone coloration
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mason_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Mason-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Denk_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Denk-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spencer_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Spencer-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Although very large, the ring is virtually invisible—it was discovered using
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        infrared
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer Space Telescope
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="487" data-file-width="500" height="292" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg/300px-Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg/450px-Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/24/Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe_13-06-2004.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
           Cassini
          </a>
         </i>
         <span class="nowrap" style="padding-left:0.1em;">
          's
         </span>
         closeup of Phoebe from 13 June 2004; the crater
         <i>
          Euphemus
         </i>
         is at top center
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Cratersofphoebe.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1501" data-file-width="2750" height="120" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cratersofphoebe.jpg/220px-Cratersofphoebe.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cratersofphoebe.jpg/330px-Cratersofphoebe.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/Cratersofphoebe.jpg/440px-Cratersofphoebe.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Cratersofphoebe.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Named
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Craters_on_Phoebe" title="Craters on Phoebe">
          craters on Phoebe
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Phoebe is roughly spherical and has a diameter of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005213000000000000♠
        </span>
        213
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        1.4 km
       </span>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thomas2010_1-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Thomas2010-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (132 mi), approximately one-sixteenth of that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       . Phoebe rotates every nine hours 16 minutes and it completes a full orbit around Saturn in about 18 months. Its surface temperature is 75 K (−198.2 °C).
      </p>
      <p>
       Most of Saturn's inner moons have very bright surfaces, but Phoebe's
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       is very low (0.06), as dark as
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lampblack" title="Lampblack">
        lampblack
       </a>
       . The Phoebean surface is extremely heavily scarred, with craters up to 80 kilometres across, one of which has walls 16 kilometres high.
      </p>
      <p>
       Phoebe's dark coloring initially led to scientists surmising that it was a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       , as it resembled the common class of dark
       <a href="/wiki/C-type_asteroid" title="C-type asteroid">
        carbonaceous asteroids
       </a>
       . These are chemically very primitive and are thought to be composed of original solids that condensed out of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_nebula" title="Solar nebula">
        solar nebula
       </a>
       with little modification since then.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, images from
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       indicate that Phoebe's craters show a considerable variation in brightness, which indicate the presence of large quantities of ice below a relatively thin blanket of dark surface deposits some 300 to 500 metres (980 to 1,640 ft) thick. In addition, quantities of carbon dioxide have been detected on the surface, a finding that has never been replicated for an asteroid. It is estimated that Phoebe is about 50% rock, as opposed to the 35% or so that typifies Saturn's inner moons. For these reasons, scientists are coming to think that Phoebe is in fact a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Centaur_(minor_planet)" title="Centaur (minor planet)">
        centaur
       </a>
       , one of a number of icy
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetoid" title="Planetoid">
        planetoids
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       that
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       between
       <a href="/wiki/Jupiter" title="Jupiter">
        Jupiter
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
        <a href="#cite_note-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
        <a href="#cite_note-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Phoebe is the first such object to be imaged as anything other than a dot.
      </p>
      <p>
       Despite its small size, Phoebe is thought to have been a spherical body early in its history, with a differentiated interior, before solidifying and being battered into its current, slightly non-equilibrium shape.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetlike_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetlike-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Material displaced from Phoebe's surface by microscopic meteor impacts may be responsible for the dark areas on the surface of
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [note 1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Debris from the biggest impacts may be the origin of the other moons of Phoebe's group (the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Norse_group" title="Norse group">
        Norse group
       </a>
       )—all of which are less than 10 km in diameter.
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Formation">
        Formation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Formation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Phoebe formed in the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       within three million years after the
       <a href="/wiki/Formation_and_evolution_of_the_Solar_System" title="Formation and evolution of the Solar System">
        origin of the Solar System
       </a>
       . This was early enough that sufficient radioactive material was available to collapse it into a sphere and stay warm enough to have liquid water for tens of millions of years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-planetlike_22-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-planetlike-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Observation_and_exploration">
        Observation and exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Phoebe_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Observation and exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="685" data-file-width="828" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg/200px-Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg/300px-Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg/400px-Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg 2x" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Phoebe-2010Mar09.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Phoebe (with
         <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=NGC_4179&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="NGC 4179 (page does not exist)">
          NGC 4179
         </a>
         in the lower right corner) as imaged with a 24"
         <a href="/wiki/RC_Optical_Systems" title="RC Optical Systems">
          telescope
         </a>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Unlike Saturn's other moons, Phoebe was not favorably placed for the
       <i>
        Voyager
       </i>
       probes.
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       observed Phoebe for a few hours in September 1981. In the images, taken from a distance of 2.2 million kilometres at low phase angle, the size of Phoebe was approx. 11 pixels and showed bright spots on the otherwise dark surface.
      </p>
      <p>
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       passed 2,068 kilometres (1,285 mi) from Phoebe on 11 June 2004, returning many high-resolution images, which revealed a scarred surface. Because
       <i>
        Voyager 2
       </i>
       had not been able to produce any high quality images of Phoebe, obtaining them was a priority for the
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       mission
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Martinez_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Martinez-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and its flight path was deliberately designed to take it close by; otherwise,
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       would likely not have returned images much better than
       <i>
        Voyager'
       </i>
       s. Because of Phoebe's short rotation period of approximately 9 hours, 17 minutes,
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       was able to map virtually the entire surface of Phoebe. The close fly-by enabled the mass of Phoebe to be determined with an uncertainty of only 1 in 500.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24">
        <a href="#cite_note-24">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_culture">
        In culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

        </span>

         </a>

        <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">

        </a>

       </li>
