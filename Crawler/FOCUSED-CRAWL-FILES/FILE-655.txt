      <p>
       Thirty-two
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        astronauts
       </a>
       were assigned to fly in the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo manned lunar landing program
       </a>
       . Twenty-four of them left
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       's orbit and flew around the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       on nine missions. (Of the other manned missions,
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">
        Apollo 1
       </a>
       did not launch and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_7" title="Apollo 7">
        Apollo 7
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">
        Apollo 9
       </a>
       were
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       spacecraft testing missions). In addition, nine astronauts flew Apollo spacecraft in the Apollo Applications Programs
       <a href="/wiki/Skylab" title="Skylab">
        Skylab
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">
        Apollo–Soyuz Test Project
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Twelve of these astronauts walked on the Moon's surface, and six of those drove
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Roving_Vehicle" title="Lunar Roving Vehicle">
        Lunar Roving Vehicles
       </a>
       on the Moon. While three astronauts flew to the Moon twice, of which two landed, none landed on the Moon more than once. The nine Apollo missions to the Moon occurred between December 1968 and December 1972.
      </p>
      <p>
       Apart from these twenty-four people who visited the Moon, no human being has gone beyond
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       . They have, therefore, been farther from the Earth than anyone else. They are also the only people to have directly viewed the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_side_of_the_Moon" title="Far side of the Moon">
        far side of the Moon
       </a>
       . The twelve who walked on the Moon are the only people ever to have set foot on an
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_object" title="Astronomical object">
        astronomical object
       </a>
       other than the Earth.
      </p>
      <p>
       Of the twenty-four astronauts who flew to the Moon, two went on to command a Skylab mission, one commanded Apollo–Soyuz, one flew as commander for
       <a href="/wiki/Approach_and_Landing_Tests" title="Approach and Landing Tests">
        Approach and Landing Tests
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle" title="Space Shuttle">
        Space Shuttle
       </a>
       , and two went on to command orbital Space Shuttle missions. A total of twenty-four NASA astronauts from the Apollo era flew on the Space Shuttle.
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Prime_crew_members">
        Prime crew members
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Apollo_astronauts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Prime crew members">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       NASA's Director of Flight Crew Operations during the
       <a href="/wiki/Project_Gemini" title="Project Gemini">
        Gemini
       </a>
       and Apollo programs was
       <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">
        Donald K. "Deke" Slayton
       </a>
       , one of the original
       <a href="/wiki/Mercury_Seven" title="Mercury Seven">
        Mercury Seven
       </a>
       astronauts, who was medically grounded in September 1962 due to a minor cardiac arrythmia – paroxysmal atrial fibrillation. Slayton was responsible for making all Gemini and Apollo crew assignments. In March 1972, Slayton was restored to flight status, and flew on the 1975
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">
        Apollo–Soyuz Test Project
       </a>
       mission.
      </p>
      <p>
       The prime crew members selected for actual missions are here grouped by their NASA astronaut selection groups, and within each group in the order selected for flight.
      </p>

      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Gus_Grissom" title="Gus Grissom">
         Virgil I. "Gus" Grissom
        </a>
        – Pilot of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_4" title="Mercury-Redstone 4">
          Liberty Bell 7
         </a>
        </i>
        and commander of the first manned
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_3" title="Gemini 3">
         Gemini 3
        </a>
        , Grissom was selected in 1966 to command the first manned mission,
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_1" title="Apollo 1">
         Apollo 1
        </a>
        , a low Earth orbit test of the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">
         Apollo Command/Service Module
        </a>
        . This mission ended a month before its scheduled launch,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MarAprChron_1-0">
         <a href="#cite_note-MarAprChron-1">
          [1]
         </a>
        </sup>
        when a cabin fire on the launch pad killed Grissom and his entire crew—
        <a href="/wiki/Ed_White_(astronaut)" title="Ed White (astronaut)">
         Ed White
        </a>
        and
        <a href="/wiki/Roger_B._Chaffee" title="Roger B. Chaffee">
         Roger Chaffee
        </a>
        —on January 27, 1967. According to Slayton, Grissom would have been his choice to be the first to walk on the Moon.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
         <a href="#cite_note-2">
          [2]
         </a>
        </sup>
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
         <a href="#cite_note-3">
          [3]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Wally_Schirra" title="Wally Schirra">
         Walter M. Schirra Jr.
        </a>
        – Pilot of
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Atlas_8" title="Mercury-Atlas 8">
          Sigma 7
         </a>
        </i>
        and commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_6A" title="Gemini 6A">
         Gemini 6A
        </a>
        , was selected to command a second CSM test flight which was cancelled in late 1966, at which time he was reassigned as Grissom's backup. Twenty-one months after the Apollo 1 fire, Schirra commanded the first CSM test flight,
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_7" title="Apollo 7">
         Apollo 7
        </a>
        . He was the only member of the "Mercury Seven" to fly on all three programs.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">
         Alan B. Shepard Jr.
        </a>
        – America's first man in space on
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Mercury-Redstone_3" title="Mercury-Redstone 3">
          Freedom 7
         </a>
        </i>
        was originally selected to command Gemini 3, but was medically grounded for the duration of Gemini due to
        <a href="/wiki/M%C3%A9ni%C3%A8re%27s_disease" title="Ménière's disease">
         Ménière's disease
        </a>
        and assisted Slayton in Flight Operations. After corrective surgery, Shepard was restored to flight status and commanded
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
         Apollo 14
        </a>
        , the third successful Moon landing mission.
       </li>
      </ul>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="From_Astronaut_Group_2">

      <p>
       All of these astronauts flew on Gemini, and except for White, each commanded one Gemini and one Apollo mission:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Ed_White_(astronaut)" title="Ed White (astronaut)">
         Ed White
        </a>
        – Second-seat veteran of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_4" title="Gemini 4">
         Gemini 4
        </a>
        who made the United States' first
        <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">
         walk in space
        </a>
        , selected as Senior Pilot (second seat) on Apollo 1. White was killed in the Apollo 1 fire along with Grissom and Chaffee.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/James_McDivitt" title="James McDivitt">
         James A. McDivitt
        </a>
        – Commander of Gemini 4, selected in late 1966 to command the first Earth orbital flight test of the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
         Apollo Lunar Module
        </a>
        with the CSM. This mission flew in March 1969 as
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_9" title="Apollo 9">
         Apollo 9
        </a>
        . After his flight, McDivitt was promoted to Manager of Lunar Landing Operations, and in August 1969 was promoted to Manager of the Apollo Spacecraft Program.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Frank_Borman" title="Frank Borman">
         Frank F. Borman II
        </a>
        – Commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_7" title="Gemini 7">
         Gemini 7
        </a>
        , selected to command a higher Earth orbit test of the complete Apollo spacecraft. But when delays prevented the LM from being ready in time for its first flight in December 1968, Borman's mission was changed to the first lunar orbital flight of the CSM on
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
         Apollo 8
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">
         James A. Lovell Jr.
        </a>
        – Second-seat veteran of Gemini 7, and commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_12" title="Gemini 12">
         Gemini 12
        </a>
        , flew as Command Module Pilot (second seat) on Apollo 8. Lovell became the first to fly a second Apollo mission as commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">
         Apollo 13
        </a>
        , the third lunar landing attempt. This mission was unsuccessful, due to a Service Module electrical system failure caused by an oxygen tank explosion. Lovell and his crew managed to return to Earth safely.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Thomas_P._Stafford" title="Thomas P. Stafford">
         Thomas P. Stafford
        </a>
        – Second-seat veteran of Gemini 6A and commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_9A" title="Gemini 9A">
         Gemini 9A
        </a>
        , commanded a lunar orbital test of the Lunar Module on
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_10" title="Apollo 10">
         Apollo 10
        </a>
        . He also commanded the
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo%E2%80%93Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo–Soyuz Test Project">
         Apollo–Soyuz Test Project
        </a>
        mission.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">
         John W. Young
        </a>
        – Second-seat veteran of Gemini 3 and commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_10" title="Gemini 10">
         Gemini 10
        </a>
        , flew as Command Module Pilot on Apollo 10. Young later commanded the successful
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_16" title="Apollo 16">
         Apollo 16
        </a>
        lunar landing. He also commanded the first Space Shuttle flight,
        <a href="/wiki/STS-1" title="STS-1">
         STS-1
        </a>
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Columbia" title="Space Shuttle Columbia">
          Columbia
         </a>
        </i>
        , on April 12, 1981. He died January 5, 2018.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Neil_Armstrong" title="Neil Armstrong">
         Neil A. Armstrong
        </a>
        – Commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_8" title="Gemini 8">
         Gemini 8
        </a>
        , commanded
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
         Apollo 11
        </a>
        , becoming the first man to set foot on the Moon.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">
         Charles "Pete" Conrad Jr.
        </a>
        – Second-seat veteran of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_5" title="Gemini 5">
         Gemini 5
        </a>
        and commander of
        <a href="/wiki/Gemini_11" title="Gemini 11">
         Gemini 11
        </a>
        , commanded
        <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
         Apollo 12
        </a>
        , the second lunar landing. He went on to Command Skylab 2, successfully completing repairs to the spacecraft that saved it for this and two subsequent missions.
       </li>
      </ul>

      <p>
       In June 1965, NASA named a group of five scientist astronauts, the first group qualified by doctorate degrees rather than test or military fighter pilot experience.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Geologist Dr.
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison H. "Jack" Schmitt
       </a>
       participated heavily in the geological training of the lunar landing astronauts, as well as assisting in the analysis of returned samples and the preparation of mission reports. In 1970, he was selected as Lunar Module Pilot for the Apollo 15 backup crew, and prime crew on Apollo 18. When program cutbacks cancelled missions 18 through 20, NASA's lunar geological community insisted on having a geologist on the Moon, so Slayton reassigned Schmitt to
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       .
      </p>

       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">

       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing" title="Moon landing">
        lunar landings
       </a>
       took place between July 1969 and December 1972 as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       .

      <p>
       On each of the Apollo 17
       <a href="/wiki/Extravehicular_activity" title="Extravehicular activity">
        extravehicular activities (EVAs)
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Harrison_Schmitt" title="Harrison Schmitt">
        Harrison Schmitt
       </a>
       was the second person out of, and the first person back into, the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Module" title="Apollo Lunar Module">
        lunar module
       </a>
       . Schmitt is thus the 12th and last person to have stepped onto the Moon.
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">
        Eugene Cernan
       </a>
       , as the second person to enter the lunar module on the final EVA, was the last person to have walked on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Alan Shepard was the oldest person to walk on the Moon, at age 47 years and 80 days.
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_Duke" title="Charles Duke">
        Charlie Duke
       </a>
       was the youngest, at age 36 years and 201 days.
      </p>
      <p>
       Jim Lovell and Fred Haise were scheduled to walk on the Moon during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">
        Apollo 13
       </a>
       mission, but the lunar landing was aborted following a major malfunction en route to the Moon. Haise was again scheduled to walk on the Moon as commander of Apollo 19, but
       <a href="/wiki/Canceled_Apollo_missions#Follow-on_lunar_missions" title="Canceled Apollo missions">
        Apollo 18 and Apollo 19
       </a>
       were cancelled on September 2, 1970.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Joe_Engle" title="Joe Engle">
        Joe Engle
       </a>
       had trained on the backup crew for
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       to explore the Moon with Cernan, but Engle was replaced by Schmitt on the primary crew for
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_17" title="Apollo 17">
        Apollo 17
       </a>
       . Schmitt had previously been crewed with
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Command/Service_Module#Command_Module_.28CM.29" title="Apollo Command/Service Module">
        command module
       </a>
       pilot
       <a href="/wiki/Richard_F._Gordon_Jr." title="Richard F. Gordon Jr.">
        Dick Gordon
       </a>
       in anticipation of Apollo 18, but Schmitt replaced Engle on Apollo 17 after the cancellation of Apollo 18 and Apollo 19, leaving Gordon as the last Apollo astronaut to train extensively for lunar exploration without ever landing on the Moon.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apollo_astronauts_who_flew_to_the_Moon_without_landing">
        Apollo astronauts who flew to the Moon without landing
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=List_of_Apollo_astronauts&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Apollo astronauts who flew to the Moon without landing">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Besides the 12 people who have walked on the Moon, 12 more have flown to within 0.001
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_distance_(astronomy)" title="Lunar distance (astronomy)">
        lunar distance
       </a>
       of its surface. During each of the six missions with successful lunar landings, one astronaut remained in lunar orbit while the other two landed. In addition, the three-person crews of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_8" title="Apollo 8">
        Apollo 8
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_10" title="Apollo 10">
        Apollo 10
       </a>
       also entered lunar orbit, and the crew of
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_13" title="Apollo 13">
        Apollo 13
       </a>
       looped around the Moon on a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_return_trajectory" title="Free return trajectory">
        free-return trajectory
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       All nine manned missions to the Moon took place as part of the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo program
       </a>
       over a period of just under four years, from 21 December 1968 to 19 December 1972. The 24 people who have flown to the Moon are the only people who have traveled beyond
       <a href="/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit" title="Low Earth orbit">
        low Earth orbit
       </a>
       . Thirteen of them are still living as of January 2018
       <sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;">
        <a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_Apollo_astronauts&amp;action=edit">
         [update]
        </a>
       </sup>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Jim_Lovell" title="Jim Lovell">
        Jim Lovell
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/John_Young_(astronaut)" title="John Young (astronaut)">
        John Young
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/Eugene_Cernan" title="Eugene Cernan">
        Eugene Cernan
       </a>
       are the only three people to have flown to the Moon twice. Young and Cernan each set foot on it during their respective second lunar missions, while Lovell is the only person to have flown to the Moon twice without landing.
      </p>
      <p>
       During Cernan's first lunar mission on Apollo 10, he tied the present record set by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Anders" title="William Anders">
        Bill Anders
       </a>
       on Apollo 8 as the youngest person to fly to the Moon. Each was 35 years and 65 days old on his launch date and 35 years and 68 days old when he entered lunar orbit. The oldest person to fly to the Moon was
       <a href="/wiki/Alan_Shepard" title="Alan Shepard">
        Alan Shepard
       </a>
       , who walked on its surface during the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       mission. Shepard was 47 years and 74 days old on his launch date and 47 years and 78 days old when he entered lunar orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       Because of Apollo 13's free-return trajectory, Lovell,
       <a href="/wiki/Jack_Swigert" title="Jack Swigert">
        Jack Swigert
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Fred_Haise" title="Fred Haise">
        Fred Haise
       </a>
       flew higher above the Moon's
       <a href="/wiki/Selenographic_coordinates" title="Selenographic coordinates">
        180° meridian
       </a>
       (opposite Earth) than anyone else has flown (254 km/158 mi). Coincidentally, due to the Moon's distance from Earth at the time, they simultaneously set the present record for humans' greatest distance from Earth, reaching an altitude of 400,171 km (248,655 mi) above sea level at
       <a href="/wiki/Coordinated_Universal_Time" title="Coordinated Universal Time">
        0:21 UTC
       </a>
       on 15 April 1970.
