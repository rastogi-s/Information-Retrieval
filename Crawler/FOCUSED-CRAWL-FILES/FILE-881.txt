      <p>
       A
       <b>
        mooncalf
       </b>
       (or
       <b>
        moon-calf
       </b>
       ,
       <b>
        calvaluna
       </b>
       ) is a
       <a href="/wiki/Monstrous_birth" title="Monstrous birth">
        monstrous birth
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage" title="Miscarriage">
        abortive
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Fetus" title="Fetus">
        fetus
       </a>
       of a
       <a href="/wiki/Cattle" title="Cattle">
        cow
       </a>
       or other
       <a href="/wiki/Livestock" title="Livestock">
        farm animal
       </a>
       . The term was occasionally applied to an abortive
       <a href="/wiki/Human" title="Human">
        human
       </a>
       fetus.
      </p>
      <p>
       The term derives from the once widespread
       <a href="/wiki/Superstition" title="Superstition">
        superstition
       </a>
       , present in many
       <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">
        European
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Folk_medicine" title="Folk medicine">
        folk
       </a>
       traditions, that such malformed creatures were the product of the sinister influence of the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       on fetal development.
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modern_usage">
        Modern usage
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mooncalf&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Modern usage">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The term came to also refer to any monstrous or grotesque thing.
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        Shakespeare
       </a>
       , for instance, used the term to describe
       <a href="/wiki/Caliban" title="Caliban">
        Caliban
       </a>
       , the deformed servant of
       <a href="/wiki/Prospero" title="Prospero">
        Prospero
       </a>
       , in
       <i>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Tempest_(play)" title="The Tempest (play)">
         The Tempest
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       ' 1901 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
         The First Men in the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       , large creatures domesticated by the Selenites are referred to as "mooncalves."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mooncalf is used as a derogatory term indicating someone is a dullard, fool or otherwise not particularly bright or sharp. For example,
       <a href="/wiki/W._C._Fields" title="W. C. Fields">
        W. C. Fields
       </a>
       in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Bank_Dick" title="The Bank Dick">
         The Bank Dick
        </a>
       </i>
       (1940) advises his prospective son-in-law to avoid being a "mooncalf" by buying shares he has been conned into believing are worth much more than the proffered price.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mooncalf is also the name of a species of magical creatures in the world of the
       <a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter" title="Harry Potter">
        Harry Potter
       </a>
       series. It is described in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Beasts_and_Where_to_Find_Them" title="Fantastic Beasts and Where to Find Them">
         Fantastic Beasts and Where to Find Them
        </a>
       </i>
       as a shy, nocturnal creature with a smooth, pale grey body, bulging eyes and four spindly legs with large flat feet. Mooncalves perform dances in the moonlight, and are apparently responsible for
       <a href="/wiki/Crop_circle" title="Crop circle">
        crop circles
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       In the
       <a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Beasts_and_Where_to_Find_Them_(film)" title="Fantastic Beasts and Where to Find Them (film)">
        film of the same name
       </a>
       , Newt Scamander's collection of creatures in his suitcase includes a herd of Mooncalves.
      </p>
      <p>
       Wilfred Maxwell, narrator and protagonist of
       <a href="/wiki/Occult" title="Occult">
        occultist
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Dion_Fortune" title="Dion Fortune">
        Dion Fortune
       </a>
       ’s 1938 novel,
       <i>
        <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Sea_Priestess&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Sea Priestess (page does not exist)">
         The Sea Priestess
        </a>
       </i>
       , refers several times to a mentally handicapped character who falls into the sea and disappears as a "mooncalf".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Commodore refers to Enoch Thompson as a mooncalf in conversation with sheriff Lindsay in season 5 of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Boardwalk_Empire" title="Boardwalk Empire">
         Boardwalk Empire
        </a>
       </i>
       .
      </p>
