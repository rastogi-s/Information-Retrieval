      <p>
       <b>
        90482 Orcus
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="/ɔːr/: 'ar' in 'war'">
            ɔːr
           </span>
           <span title="'k' in 'kind'">
            k
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
           <span title="'s' in 'sigh'">
            s
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          OR
         </span>
         -kəs
        </i>
       </a>
       ), provisional designation
       <span class="texhtml mvar" style="font-style:italic; white-space: nowrap; font-size: 110%">
        2004 DW
       </span>
       , is a
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        trans-Neptunian object
       </a>
       from the
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       with a large
       <a href="/wiki/Minor-planet_moon" title="Minor-planet moon">
        moon
       </a>
       , Vanth. It was discovered on 17 February 2004, by American astronomers
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Michael Brown
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/California_Institute_of_Technology" title="California Institute of Technology">
        Caltech
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chad_Trujillo" title="Chad Trujillo">
        Chad Trujillo
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/Gemini_Observatory" title="Gemini Observatory">
        Gemini Observatory
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/David_L._Rabinowitz" title="David L. Rabinowitz">
        David Rabinowitz
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Yale_University" title="Yale University">
        Yale University
       </a>
       . It is probably a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Precovery" title="Precovery">
        Precovery
       </a>
       images taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Palomar_Observatory" title="Palomar Observatory">
        Palomar Observatory
       </a>
       as early as November 8, 1951 were later obtained from the
       <a href="/wiki/Digitized_Sky_Survey" title="Digitized Sky Survey">
        Digitized Sky Survey
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Orcus_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-Orcus-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Orcus is a
       <a href="/wiki/Plutino" title="Plutino">
        plutino
       </a>
       , a trans-Neptunian object that is locked in a 2:3 resonance with the ice giant
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       , making two revolutions around the Sun to every three of Neptune's. This is much like
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       , except that it is constrained to always be in the opposite
       <a href="/wiki/Phase_(waves)#Phase_difference" title="Phase (waves)">
        phase
       </a>
       of its orbit from Pluto: Orcus is at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aphelion" title="Aphelion">
        aphelion
       </a>
       when Pluto is at
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelion" title="Perihelion">
        perihelion
       </a>
       and vice versa. Moreover, the aphelion of Orcus's orbit points in nearly the opposite direction from Pluto's, although the eccentricities and inclinations are similar. Because of these similarities and contrasts, along with its large moon Vanth that recalls Pluto's large moon
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       , Orcus has been regarded as the
       <b>
        <span class="nowrap">
         anti-Pluto
        </span>
       </b>
       . This was a major consideration in selecting its name, as the deity
       <a href="/wiki/Orcus" title="Orcus">
        Orcus
       </a>
       was the
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_mythology" title="Etruscan mythology">
        Etruscan
       </a>
       equivalent of the
       <a href="/wiki/Roman_mythology" title="Roman mythology">
        Roman
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto_(mythology)" title="Pluto (mythology)">
        Pluto
       </a>
       , and later became an alternate name for Pluto.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The surface of Orcus is relatively bright with albedo reaching 30%, grey in color and water-rich. The ice is predominantly in crystalline form, which may be related to past
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryovolcanic" title="Cryovolcanic">
        cryovolcanic
       </a>
       activity. Other compounds like
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       may also be present. The existence of a satellite allowed astronomers to determine the mass of the system, which is approximately equal to that of the
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturnian
       </a>
       moon
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       . The ratio of masses of Orcus and Vanth is uncertain, possibly anywhere from 1:33 to 1:12. The diameter of Orcus is estimated to be 761 or 807 km and the diameter of Vanth 378 or 267 km respectively, depending on their relative albedos.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carry2011_8-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carry2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbit_and_rotation">
        Orbit and rotation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Orbit and rotation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="200" data-file-width="400" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg/300px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg/450px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg/600px-TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg.png 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:TheKuiperBelt_Orbits_Orcus2.svg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The orbits of Orcus (blue), Pluto (red) and Neptune (grey). Orcus and Pluto are shown in the April 2006 positions. The dates of their
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perihelia" title="Perihelia">
          perihelia
         </a>
         (q) and
         <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
          aphelia
         </a>
         (Q) are also marked.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Orcus is in
       <a href="/wiki/Plutino" title="Plutino">
        2:3 orbital resonance
       </a>
       with
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       having an orbital period of 247 years. Orcus's orbit is similar to
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       's (both have perihelia above the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ), but is oriented differently. Although at one point its orbit approaches that of
       <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
        Neptune
       </a>
       , the resonance between the two bodies means that Orcus itself is always a great distance away from Neptune (there is always an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Angular_separation" title="Angular separation">
        angular separation
       </a>
       of over 60 degrees between them). Over a 14,000-year period Orcus stays more than 18 AU from Neptune.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC2004-D15_16-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC2004-D15-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Because their mutual resonance with Neptune constrains Orcus and Pluto to remain in opposite phases of their otherwise very similar motions, Orcus is sometimes described as the "anti-Pluto".
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Orcus is currently 48.0 AU from the Sun
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AstDys_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-AstDys-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and will come to
       <a href="/wiki/Apsis" title="Apsis">
        aphelion
       </a>
       (farthest distance from the Sun) in 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Horizons_13-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Horizons-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Simulations by the
       <a href="/wiki/Deep_Ecliptic_Survey" title="Deep Ecliptic Survey">
        Deep Ecliptic Survey
       </a>
       (DES) show that over the next 10 million years Orcus can acquire a perihelion distance (qmin) as small as 27.8 AU.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buie_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Buie-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The rotation period of the primary is not known. Different photometric surveys have produced different results. Some show low amplitude variations with periods ranging from 7 to 21 hours, whereas others show no variability.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the value obtained by Ortiz
       <i>
        et al.
       </i>
       , about 10.5 hours, seems to be the most likely one.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2011_18-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2011-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The rotational poles of Orcus probably coincide with the orbital poles of its moon, Vanth. This means that Orcus is currently viewed pole-on, which could explain the near absence of any rotational modulation of its brightness.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2011_18-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2011-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If, however, the primary is tidally locked with the satellite, the rotational period will coincide with the 9.7-day orbital period of Vanth.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2011_18-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2011-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       This
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_planet" title="Minor planet">
        minor planet
       </a>
       was named after one of the Roman gods of the underworld,
       <a href="/wiki/Orcus" title="Orcus">
        Orcus
       </a>
       . While
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto_(mythology)" title="Pluto (mythology)">
        Pluto
       </a>
       was the ruler of the underworld, Orcus was a punisher of the damned. The approved naming citation was published by the
       <a href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Center" title="Minor Planet Center">
        Minor Planet Center
       </a>
       on 26 November 2004 (
       <small>
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minor_Planet_Circulars" title="Minor Planet Circulars">
         M.P.C.
        </a>
        53177
       </small>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MPC-Circulars-Archive_19-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-MPC-Circulars-Archive-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Under the guidelines of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       's (IAU)
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions" title="Astronomical naming conventions">
        naming conventions
       </a>
       , objects with a similar size and
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       to that of
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       are named after
       <a href="/wiki/Underworld" title="Underworld">
        underworld
       </a>
       deities. Accordingly, the discoverers suggested naming the object after
       <i>
        Orcus
       </i>
       , the Etruscan god of the underworld and punisher of broken oaths. He was portrayed in paintings in Etruscan tombs as a hairy, bearded giant. The name was also a private reference to the homonymous
       <a href="/wiki/Orcas_Island" title="Orcas Island">
        Orcas Island
       </a>
       , where Brown's wife Diane had lived as a child and that they visit frequently.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown16_20-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-brown16-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On March 30, 2005, Orcus's companion, Vanth was named after a
       <a href="/wiki/Vanth" title="Vanth">
        winged female demon
       </a>
       of the Etruscan underworld. She could be present at the moment of death, and frequently acted as a guide of the deceased to the underworld.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston90482_21-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Johnston90482-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">

         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Size_and_magnitude">
        Size and magnitude
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Size and magnitude">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">
        absolute magnitude
       </a>
       of Orcus is approximately 2.3
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_7-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       (comparable with the 2.6 of
       <a href="/wiki/Classical_Kuiper_belt_object" title="Classical Kuiper belt object">
        cubewano
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">
        50000 Quaoar
       </a>
       ). The detection of Orcus by the
       <a href="/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope" title="Spitzer Space Telescope">
        Spitzer Space Telescope
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Far_infrared" title="Far infrared">
        far infrared
       </a>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Stansberry_2007_22-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Stansberry_2007-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Herschel_Space_Telescope" title="Herschel Space Telescope">
        Herschel Space Telescope
       </a>
       in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Submillimeter" title="Submillimeter">
        submillimeter
       </a>
       constrains its diameter to
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005958400000000000♠
        </span>
        958.4
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        22.9 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_7-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Orcus appears to have an
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo#Astronomical_albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       of about 21% to 25%,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_7-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which may be typical of trans-Neptunian objects approaching the 1000 km diameter range.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tnoalbedo_23-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-tnoalbedo-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In the magnitude and size estimates reported above it was assumed that Orcus is a singular object. The presence of a relatively large satellite (Vanth, see below) may change them considerably. The absolute magnitude of Vanth is estimated at 4.88, which means that it is about 11 times fainter than Orcus itself.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       If the albedos of both bodies are the same at 0.23 then the size of Orcus is about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005917000000000000♠
        </span>
        917
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        25 km
       </span>
       , and the size of Vanth is about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7005276000000000000♠
        </span>
        276
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        17 km
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_7-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Orcus,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="490" data-file-width="729" height="148" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png/220px-Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png/330px-Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6e/Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png/440px-Orcus%2C_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Orcus,_Earth_%26_Moon_size_comparison.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Orcus compared to Earth and the Moon
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Mike Brown
       </a>
       's website lists Orcus as a
       <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_planet" title="Dwarf planet">
        dwarf planet
       </a>
       with "near certainty",
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown-dplist_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown-dplist-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Tancredi concludes that it is one,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tancredi2008_24-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tancredi2008-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and is massive enough to be considered one under the
       <a href="/wiki/IAU_definition_of_planet#Draft_proposal" title="IAU definition of planet">
        2006 draft proposal
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        IAU
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-draft2006_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU-draft2006-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the IAU has not formally recognized it as such.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUplanetnames_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUplanetnames-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27">
        <a href="#cite_note-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Mass_and_density">
        Mass and density
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Mass and density">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Since Orcus is known to be a binary system, the mass of the system has been estimated to be
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7020632000000000000♠
        </span>
        (6.32
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.05)
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         20
        </sup>
        kg
       </span>
       , or about 3.8% the mass of the most massive known dwarf planet,
       <a href="/wiki/Eris_(dwarf_planet)" title="Eris (dwarf planet)">
        Eris
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       How this mass is partitioned between Orcus and Vanth depends of their relative sizes. If Vanth's diameter is about one third that of Orcus, its mass is only 3% of the system mass. On the other hand, if the size of Vanth is about half that of the primary (see above), then its mass could be as high as 1/12 of the system mass, or about 8% of the mass of Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The density of the primary (and secondary assuming the same density) is about
       <span class="nowrap">
        1.5 g/cm
        <sup>
         3
        </sup>
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TNOsCool8_7-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-TNOsCool8-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Spectra_and_surface">
        Spectra and surface
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Spectra and surface">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="527" data-file-width="749" height="211" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif/300px-OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif/450px-OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif/600px-OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:OrcusandPlutoRotatingFrame.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The orbital resonances of Orcus and
         <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
          Pluto
         </a>
         in a
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rotating_frame" title="Rotating frame">
          rotating frame
         </a>
         with a period equal to
         <a href="/wiki/Neptune" title="Neptune">
          Neptune
         </a>
         's
         <a href="/wiki/Orbital_period" title="Orbital period">
          orbital period
         </a>
         . (Neptune is held stationary.)
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The first spectroscopic observations in 2004 showed that the visible spectrum of Orcus is flat (neutral in color) and featureless, whereas in the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Near-infrared" title="Near-infrared">
        near-infrared
       </a>
       there were moderately strong water absorption bands at 1.5 and 2.0 μm. Thus Orcus appeared to be different from other trans-Neptunian objects like
       <a href="/wiki/28978_Ixion" title="28978 Ixion">
        Ixion
       </a>
       with red visible and often featureless infrared spectra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Fornasier2004_28-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Fornasier2004-28">
         [28]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Further
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared" title="Infrared">
        Infrared
       </a>
       observations in 2004 by the
       <a href="/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory" title="European Southern Observatory">
        European Southern Observatory
       </a>
       and the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gemini_telescope" title="Gemini telescope">
        Gemini telescope
       </a>
       give results consistent with mixtures of water
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Carbon" title="Carbon">
        carbonaceous
       </a>
       compounds, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Tholin" title="Tholin">
        tholins
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deBergh2005_11-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-deBergh2005-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The water and methane ices can cover no more than 50% and 30% of the surface, respectively.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trujillo2005_29-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trujillo2005-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This means the proportion of ice on the surface is less than on
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       , but similar to that on
       <a href="/wiki/Triton_(moon)" title="Triton (moon)">
        Triton
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trujillo2005_29-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Trujillo2005-29">
         [29]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Later in 2008–2010 new infrared spectroscopic observations with a higher
       <a href="/wiki/Signal-to-noise_ratio" title="Signal-to-noise ratio">
        signal-to-noise ratio
       </a>
       revealed additional spectral features. Among them are a deep water ice absorption band at 1.65 μm, which is an evidence of the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crystalline" title="Crystalline">
        crystalline
       </a>
       water ice on the surface of Orcus, and a new absorption band at 2.22 μm. The origin of the latter feature is not completely clear. It can be caused either by
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonium" title="Ammonium">
        ammonium
       </a>
       dissolved in the water ice or by
       <a href="/wiki/Methane" title="Methane">
        methane
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Ethane" title="Ethane">
        ethane
       </a>
       ices.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Radiative_transfer" title="Radiative transfer">
        radiative transfer
       </a>
       modeling showed that a mixture of water ice, tholins (as a darkening agent), ethane ice and ammonium ion (NH
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       <sup>
        +
       </sup>
       ) provides the best match to the spectra, whereas a combination of water ice, tholins, methane ice and ammonia hydrate gives a slightly inferior result. On the other hand, a mixture of only ammonia hydrate, tholins and water ice failed to provide a satisfactory match.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       So, as of 2010, the only reliably identified compounds on the surface of Orcus are crystalline water ice and, possibly, dark tholins. A firm identification of ammonia, methane and other
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrocarbon" title="Hydrocarbon">
        hydrocarbons
       </a>
       requires better infrared spectra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Orcus straddles the edge for trans-Neptunian objects massive enough to retain volatiles such as methane on the surface.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The reflectance spectrum of Orcus shows the deepest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Water_absorption" title="Water absorption">
        water-ice absorption
       </a>
       bands of any Kuiper belt object (KBO) that is not associated with the
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea_family" title="Haumea family">
        Haumea collisional family
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The
       <a href="/wiki/Moons_of_Uranus#Large_moons" title="Moons of Uranus">
        large icy satellites of Uranus
       </a>
       have infrared spectra quite similar to that of Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Among other trans-Neptunian objects Pluto's moon
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       appears to be most similar to Orcus. It has a higher albedo but very similar visible and near-infrared spectra. Their densities are also similar and both have water ice rich surfaces.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">
        Quaoar
       </a>
       —a trans-Neptunian object of similar size—has strong water ice absorption features in its spectra but also has a very red color in the visible line implying the presence of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Ultrared_matter&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Ultrared matter (page does not exist)">
        ultrared matter
       </a>
       on its surface. Dwarf planet
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       and objects from its collisional family have much higher albedos and much deeper water absorption bands than Orcus. Finally,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <a href="/wiki/(208996)_2003_AZ84" title="(208996) 2003 AZ84">
         (208996) 2003 AZ
         <span style="position: relative; top: 0.3em;">
          <span style="font-size:80%;">
           84
          </span>
         </span>
        </a>
       </span>
       —another large object in 2:3 resonance with Neptune—has spectral properties very similar to Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Cryovolcanism">
        Cryovolcanism
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Cryovolcanism">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The presence of
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal" title="Crystal">
        crystalline
       </a>
       water
       <a href="/wiki/Ice" title="Ice">
        ice
       </a>
       , and possibly
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       ice may indicate that a renewal mechanism was active in the past on the surface of Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Ammonia so far has not been detected on any TNO or icy satellite of the outer planets other than
       <a href="/wiki/Miranda_(moon)" title="Miranda (moon)">
        Miranda
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 1.65
       <a href="/wiki/Micrometre" title="Micrometre">
        μm
       </a>
       band on Orcus is broad and deep (12%), as on
       <a href="/wiki/Charon_(moon)" title="Charon (moon)">
        Charon
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/50000_Quaoar" title="50000 Quaoar">
        Quaoar
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Haumea" title="Haumea">
        Haumea
       </a>
       , and icy satellites of giant planets.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       On the other hand, the crystalline water ice on the surfaces of TNOs should be completely
       <a href="/wiki/Amorphous_solid" title="Amorphous solid">
        amorphized
       </a>
       by the galactic and Solar radiation in about 10 million years.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barucci2008_9-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Barucci2008-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Some calculations indicate that
       <a href="/wiki/Cryovolcano" title="Cryovolcano">
        cryovolcanism
       </a>
       , which is considered one of the possible renewal mechanisms, may indeed be possible for TNOs larger than about 1000 km.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Orcus may have experienced at least one such episode in the past, which turned the amorphous water ice on its surface into crystalline. The preferred type of volcanism may have been explosive aqueous volcanism driven by an explosive dissolution of methane from water–ammonia melts.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Delsanti2010_17-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Delsanti2010-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Models of internal heating via
       <a href="/wiki/Radioactive_decay" title="Radioactive decay">
        radioactive decay
       </a>
       suggest that Orcus may be capable of sustaining an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_ocean" title="Internal ocean">
        internal ocean
       </a>
       of liquid water.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hussman2006_30-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Hussman2006-30">
         [30]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <p>
       Orcus has one known moon, Vanth (full designation
       <span class="nowrap">
        (90482) Orcus I Vanth
       </span>
       ). It was discovered by
       <a href="/wiki/Michael_E._Brown" title="Michael E. Brown">
        Mike Brown
       </a>
       and T.-A. Suer using discovery images taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       on November 13, 2005.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8812_32-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8812-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery was announced on 22 February 2007 in IAUC 8812.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-johnston90482_33-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-johnston90482-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Using observations with the Hubble Space Telescope from November 13, 2005, Mike Brown and T.A. Suer detected a natural satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAU-C8812_34-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAU-C8812-34">
         [34]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The discovery of a satellite of Orcus was reported in IAUC 8812 on 22 February 2007.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Johnston90482_21-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Johnston90482-21">
         [21]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The satellite was given the designation S/2005 (90482) 1 before later being named
       <i>
        Vanth
       </i>
       . It orbits Orcus in a nearly face-on circular orbit with an
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity" title="Orbital eccentricity">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       of about 0.007, and an orbital period of 9.54 days.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vanth orbits only
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7006903000000000000♠
        </span>
        9030
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        89 km
       </span>
       from Orcus and is too close to Orcus for ground-based spectroscopy to determine the surface composition of the satellite.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carry2011_8-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carry2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mike Brown suspects that like the Pluto–Charon system, Orcus and Vanth are
       <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
        tidally locked
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vanth does not resemble known collisional satellites because its spectrum is very different from that of its primary, and it may be a captured KBO.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Vanth could also have originated as a result of
       <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Rotational_fission&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rotational fission (page does not exist)">
        rotational fission
       </a>
       of the
       <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/primordial" title="wikt:primordial">
        primordial
       </a>
       Orcus, which would have rotated much faster than now.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ortiz2011_18-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Ortiz2011-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       On March 23, 2009, Brown asked readers of his weekly column to suggest possible names for the satellite, with the best one to be submitted to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU) on April 5.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name
       <a href="/wiki/Vanth" title="Vanth">
        Vanth
       </a>
       , after the
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_mythology" title="Etruscan mythology">
        Etruscan
       </a>
       goddess who guided the souls of the dead to the underworld, was eventually chosen from among a large pool of submissions.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown16_20-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-brown16-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This submission was assessed by the IAU's Committee for Small Body Nomenclature, which voted to approve it in March 2010, in accordance with the normal object
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_naming_conventions#Minor_planets" title="Astronomical naming conventions">
        naming procedures
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CSBN-MPnames_35-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-CSBN-MPnames-35">
         [35]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36">
        <a href="#cite_note-36">
         [36]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Properties">
        Properties
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Properties">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Vanth was found at 0.25
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arcsecond" title="Arcsecond">
        arcsec
       </a>
       from Orcus with
       <a href="/wiki/Absolute_magnitude" title="Absolute magnitude">
        magnitude
       </a>
       difference of
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000270000000000000♠
        </span>
        2.7
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        1.0
       </span>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IAUC8812_32-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-IAUC8812-32">
         [32]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Estimates made in 2009 by Mike Brown show that the
       <a href="/wiki/Apparent_magnitude" title="Apparent magnitude">
        apparent magnitude
       </a>
       of Vanth is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7001219700000000000♠
        </span>
        21.97
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.05
       </span>
       which is
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7000254000000000000♠
        </span>
        2.54
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ±
        </span>
        0.01
       </span>
       magnitudes fainter than Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Assuming equal albedos this would mean a diameter of 280 km, or 2.9 times smaller than the primary.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carry2011_8-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carry2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, the dissimilar colors of Orcus (neutral) and Vanth (
       <a href="/wiki/Trans-Neptunian_object#Colors" title="Trans-Neptunian object">
        red
       </a>
       ) suggest that Vanth could have an albedo a factor of two lower than Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Should Vanth have an albedo of only 0.12, Vanth could be as large as 380 km with Orcus being 760 km in diameter.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carry2011_8-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carry2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mass of Vanth also depends on its albedo and can vary from 3 to 9% of the total system mass.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Carry2011_8-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-Carry2011-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown2010_14-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown2010-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Recently Brown and Butler have estimated Vanth's diameter at 480 km ± 75 km, about 1/2 Orcus' diameter, using resolved ALMA images, making Orcus and Vanth a smaller analog of Pluto and Charon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brown_butler_2018_37-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Brown_butler_2018-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <i>
        Brown &amp; Butler
       </i>
       measure Vanth's albedo as only
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6998800000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.08
        <span style="margin-left:0.3em;">
         <span style="display:inline-block;margin-bottom:-0.3em;vertical-align:-0.4em;line-height:1.2em;font-size:85%;text-align:right;">
          +0.02
          <br/>
          −0.01
         </span>
        </span>
       </span>
       , almost 3 times darker than Orcus.
      </p>
      <p>
       The satellite does not resemble other known collisional satellites because its spectrum is very different from that of its primary. Vanth may thus be a captured
       <a href="/wiki/Kuiper_belt" title="Kuiper belt">
        Kuiper belt
       </a>
       object.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name_of_Vanth">
        Name of Vanth
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=90482_Orcus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Name of Vanth">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Upon discovery, Vanth was issued a provisional designation,
       <span class="nowrap">
        <b>
         S/2005 (90482) 1
        </b>
       </span>
       . On March 23, 2009, Brown asked readers of his weekly column to suggest possible names for the satellite, with the best one to be submitted to the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union" title="International Astronomical Union">
        International Astronomical Union
       </a>
       (IAU) on April 5.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MBP_15-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-MBP-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The name
       <a href="/wiki/Vanth" title="Vanth">
        Vanth
       </a>
       , the winged
       <a href="/wiki/Etruscan_mythology" title="Etruscan mythology">
        Etruscan
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Psychopomp" title="Psychopomp">
        psychopomp
       </a>
       who guides the souls of the dead to the underworld, was chosen from among a large pool of submissions. Vanth was the only suggestion that was purely Etruscan in origin. It was the most popular submission, first suggested by
       <a href="/wiki/Sonya_Taaffe" title="Sonya Taaffe">
        Sonya Taaffe
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown16_20-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-brown16-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This submission was assessed and approved by the IAU's Committee for Small Body Nomenclature in their March 30, 2010, circular.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Etruscan Vanth is frequently portrayed in the company of
       <a href="/wiki/Charun" title="Charun">
        Charun
       </a>
       (Charon), and so as the name of the moon of Orcus (nicknamed the "anti-Pluto" because resonance with Neptune keeps it on the opposite side of the Sun from Pluto), it is an allusion to the parallels between Orcus and
       <a href="/wiki/Pluto" title="Pluto">
        Pluto
       </a>
       . Brown quoted Taaffe as saying that if Vanth "accompanies dead souls from the moment of death to the underworld itself, then of course her face is turned always toward Orcus", a reference to the likely synchronous orbit of Vanth about Orcus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-brown16_20-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-brown16-20">
         [20]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
