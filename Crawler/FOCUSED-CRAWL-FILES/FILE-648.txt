      <p>
       The
       <b>
        examination of Apollo Moon photographs
       </b>
       is an endeavour undertaken by certain people engaged in the debate as to the merits of
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_landing_conspiracy_theories" title="Moon landing conspiracy theories">
        Moon landing conspiracy theories
       </a>
       , despite the abundance of
       <a href="/wiki/Third-party_evidence_for_Apollo_Moon_landings" title="Third-party evidence for Apollo Moon landings">
        third-party evidence for Apollo Moon landings
       </a>
       . A number of allegations and refutations with a variable degree of notability are put forward due to this examination.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Absence_of_stars">
        Absence of stars
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Absence of stars">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Conspiracy theorists (henceforth
       <i>
        <a class="extiw" href="https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/conspiracist" title="wikt:conspiracist">
         conspiracists
        </a>
       </i>
       ) frequently point out that there are no
       <a href="/wiki/Star" title="Star">
        stars
       </a>
       in the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_program" title="Apollo program">
        Apollo
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
        photographs
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Astronaut" title="Astronaut">
        Cosmonaut
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Yuri_Gagarin" title="Yuri Gagarin">
        Yuri Gagarin
       </a>
       , the first human to travel in
       <a href="/wiki/Outer_space" title="Outer space">
        outer space
       </a>
       in 1961, commented that the stars seen from his
       <a href="/wiki/Vostok_(spacecraft)" title="Vostok (spacecraft)">
        Vostok
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spacecraft
       </a>
       were "bright and clear cut."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Conspiracists contend that
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       chose not to put the stars into the
       <a href="/wiki/Photograph" title="Photograph">
        photos
       </a>
       because
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomers
       </a>
       would have been able to use them to determine whether the photos were taken from the Earth or the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       , by means of identifying them and comparing their celestial position and
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">
        parallax
       </a>
       to what would be expected for either observation site.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Inconsistent_color_and_angle_of_shadows_and_light">
        Inconsistent color and angle of shadows and light
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Inconsistent color and angle of shadows and light">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The shadows should be absolutely black and run parallel to each other.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Shadows on the Moon are complicated because there are several light sources: the Sun, and the Earth, as well as the astronauts and the Lunar Module.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
            <a href="#cite_note-11">
             [11]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Light from these sources is scattered by
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_dust" title="Lunar dust">
            lunar dust
           </a>
           in many different directions, including into shadows. Additionally, the Moon's surface is not flat and shadows falling into craters and hills appear longer, shorter and distorted from the simple expectations of the conspiracists. More significantly,
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perspective_(visual)" title="Perspective (visual)">
            perspective
           </a>
           effects come into play, particularly on rough or angled ground.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-terrain_12-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-terrain-12">
             [12]
            </a>
           </sup>
           This leads to non-parallel shadows even on objects which are extremely close to each other, and can be observed easily on Earth wherever fences or trees are found. And finally, the camera in use was fitted with a wide angle lens, which naturally resulted in subtle versions of
           <a href="/wiki/Fisheye_lens" title="Fisheye lens">
            fisheye lens
           </a>
           distortion.
          </i>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
           <a href="#cite_note-13">
            [13]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">

       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>

      <div class="thumb tright">

         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2369" data-file-width="2349" height="303" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/300px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/450px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg/600px-Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>

         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_AS11-40-5866.jpg" title="Enlarge">

      </div>
      <p>
       Conspiracists claim that the classic photo of Apollo 11 astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Buzz_Aldrin" title="Buzz Aldrin">
        Buzz Aldrin
       </a>
       on the Moon (
       <a href="/wiki/File:Aldrin_Apollo_11.jpg" title="File:Aldrin Apollo 11.jpg">
        AS11-40-5903
       </a>
       ) looks as if a huge spotlight was used at a close distance.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Jan Lundberg, an engineer at Hasselblad responsible for producing the photographic cameras used for the Apollo missions, stated: "
       <i>
        Yes, it seems like he is standing in a spotlight and I can't explain that. Umm, that escapes me why. So maybe you have to find Armstrong and ask him!
       </i>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           The illumination of the Aldrin photograph referred to by Lundberg is an artifact of altering the brightness and contrast of the image, and it is not found in all reproductions. This can be seen in the next section, where this photograph is shown in two versions.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           Lunar dust reflects light in a manner similar to street signs or wet grass – a significant amount of light is reflected back at the light source (the Sun in this case) instead of being scattered in all directions as Earth sand would do.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
            <a href="#cite_note-19">
             [19]
            </a>
           </sup>
           This can be observed on Earth, as it explains why the full Moon is much more than twice as bright as a half Moon.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
            <a href="#cite_note-20">
             [20]
            </a>
           </sup>
           This effect explains hot spots in photos that contain the photographer's own shadow.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           The sunlit portion of Armstrong's
           <a href="/wiki/Space_suit" title="Space suit">
            spacesuit
           </a>
           is in the correct place to provide the light for the hotspot in the photo of Aldrin on the ladder.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>
           The "hot spots" are discussed at the
           <a href="/wiki/Debunker" title="Debunker">
            debunking
           </a>
           <a href="/wiki/Website" title="Website">
            website
           </a>
           Moon Base Clavius (Clavius.org).
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bootspot_21-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-bootspot-21">
             [21]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       In an
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_12" title="Apollo 12">
        Apollo 12
       </a>
       voice recording during the mission's first EVA, astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Pete_Conrad" title="Pete Conrad">
        Pete Conrad
       </a>
       said: "
       <i>
        Boy, that Sun is bright. That's just like somebody shining a spotlight in your hand.
       </i>
       " [...] "
       <i>
        I'll tell you...You know, this Sun...It really is...It's just like somebody's got a super-bright spotlight.
       </i>
       "
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22">
        <a href="#cite_note-22">
         [22]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
        <a href="#cite_note-23">
         [23]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Conrad is only describing the brightness of the sunlight on the Moon.
          </i>

      <h3>

        a) In some photos, the crosshairs (

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apparently_identical_backgrounds_in_Apollo_15_photographs_taken_at_different_locations">
        Apparently identical backgrounds in Apollo 15 photographs taken at different locations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Apparently identical backgrounds in Apollo 15 photographs taken at different locations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       There are issues with Apollo 15 photos having identical backgrounds.
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2355" data-file-width="2340" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg/220px-Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg/330px-Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg/440px-Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_15_LM_on_surface.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         AS15-82-11057: View of
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
          Apollo 15
         </a>
         Lunar Module from the nearby
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_Lunar_Surface_Experiments_Package" title="Apollo Lunar Surface Experiments Package">
          ALSEP
         </a>
         site (Station 8) during the third
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Extra-vehicular_activity" title="Extra-vehicular activity">
          EVA
         </a>
         .
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as15ops_27-0">
          <a href="#cite_note-as15ops-27">
           [27]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div class="center">
       <div class="thumb tnone">
        <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
         <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:AS15-82-11082.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2355" data-file-width="2340" height="221" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/AS15-82-11082.jpg/220px-AS15-82-11082.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/AS15-82-11082.jpg/330px-AS15-82-11082.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ab/AS15-82-11082.jpg/440px-AS15-82-11082.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
         </a>
         <div class="thumbcaption">
          <div class="magnify">
           <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:AS15-82-11082.jpg" title="Enlarge">
           </a>
          </div>
          AS15-82-11082: Later photo with same hills in the background near
          <a href="/wiki/Mons_Hadley" title="Mons Hadley">
           Hadley Rille
          </a>
          (Station 9) during the third EVA, approximately 1.4 km west of the Lunar Module.
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-as15ops_27-1">
           <a href="#cite_note-as15ops-27">
            [27]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </div>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <div style="clear:both;">
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Detailed comparison of the backgrounds said to be identical in fact show significant changes in the relative positions of the hills that are consistent with the claimed locations that the images were taken from. Parallax effects clearly demonstrate that the images were taken from widely different locations around the landing sites.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Claims that the appearance of the background is identical while the foreground changes (for example, from a boulder strewn crater to the Lunar Module) are trivially explained when the images were taken from nearby locations, akin to seeing distant mountains appearing the same on Earth from locations that are hundreds of feet apart showing different foreground items.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Furthermore, as there is only an extremely tenuous atmosphere on the Moon, very distant objects will appear clearer and closer to the human eye. What appears as nearby hills in some photographs, are actually mountains several kilometers high and some 10–20 kilometers away. Changes in such very distant backgrounds are quite subtle, and can be mistaken for no change at all.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           As the Moon is also much smaller than the Earth, the
           <a href="/wiki/Horizon" title="Horizon">
            horizon
           </a>
           is significantly nearer in photographs than Earthbound observers are used to seeing (an eye 1.7 m above completely flat ground will see the horizon 4.7 km away on Earth, but only 2.4 km away on the Moon). This can lead to confusing interpretations of the images.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28">
            <a href="#cite_note-28">
             [28]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           One specific case is debunked in "Who Mourns For Apollo?" by Mike Bara.
          </i>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-whomourns_29-0">
           <a href="#cite_note-whomourns-29">
            [29]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <i>
             While it is true that there is no haze to assist in judging distance, the maximum distance to the horizon is much closer than on Earth, due to the smaller size of the Moon. This limits the scope for the same objects to appear in different shots taken at different locations.
            </i>
           </li>
          </ul>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <i>
             For a flat area of the Moon, the distance to horizon ≈ sqrt ( 2 * radius of Moon * height of observer )
             <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30">
              <a href="#cite_note-30">
               [30]
              </a>
             </sup>
            </i>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <i>
             The Moon's average radius is 1,080 miles, and assuming generously that the astronauts held the camera 5 feet, or 0.000947 miles above the surface (the cameras are shown in photographs as having no
             <a href="/wiki/Viewfinder" title="Viewfinder">
              viewfinder
             </a>
             , and the astronauts seem to hold them in the centre of their chest, so five feet is generous).
            </i>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <i>
             These figures give distance = sqrt (2 * 1080 * 0.000947) = 2 miles.
            </i>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <dl>
           <dd>
            <i>
             It seems that this would be far enough for terrain features to appear in shots taken from locations some distance apart, but perhaps not "miles apart." Without having specific information as to which shots and the terrain they are purported to contain, there is no definite answer.
            </i>
           </dd>
          </dl>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="High_number_of_photographs">
        High number of photographs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: High number of photographs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       When the number of photographs taken by astronauts during the EVAs of all Apollo missions is divided by the duration of all of the EVAs, one arrives at a figure of 1.19 photos per minute. That is one photo per 50 seconds. Discounting time spent on other activities results in one photo per 15 seconds for Apollo 11. This is even more remarkable because many places in the photographs are miles apart and would have taken considerable travel time to reach, especially in bulky pressure suits. Moreover, the cameras were equipped with neither a viewfinder nor automatic
       <a href="/wiki/Camera#Exposure_control" title="Camera">
        exposure control
       </a>
       , which means that taking good pictures would take considerably longer.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
        <a href="#cite_note-31">
         [31]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1181" data-file-width="1181" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf/page1-220px-Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf/page1-330px-Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf/page1-440px-Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Apollo_11_photo_map.pdf" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Map of photos taken during
         <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
          Apollo 11
         </a>
         moonwalk
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           The astronauts were well trained in photography before the mission. Because there was no weather to contend with and the bright sunlight allowed the use of small apertures, with consequent large
           <a href="/wiki/Depth_of_field" title="Depth of field">
            depth of field
           </a>
           , the equipment generally was kept at a single setting throughout the mission. All that was needed to take a picture was to open the shutter. Film winding was
           <a href="/wiki/Motor_drive" title="Motor drive">
            automatic
           </a>
           .
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           In these conditions it is possible to take two photographs per second at best. The camera was in a bracket mounted on the front of their spacesuit, so they looked straight ahead at what they wanted to photograph; no viewfinder was needed. Also, many of the photographs were
           <a href="/wiki/Anaglyph_3D" title="Anaglyph 3D">
            stereoscopic pairs
           </a>
           or sets of
           <a href="/wiki/Panoramic_photography" title="Panoramic photography">
            panoramic images
           </a>
           , taken immediately after each other. The
           <a href="/wiki/Lunar_and_Planetary_Institute" title="Lunar and Planetary Institute">
            Lunar and Planetary Institute
           </a>
           's Apollo Image Atlas shows that 70 mm magazine 40/S of Apollo 11 has 123 photos taken during the walk on the surface—less than one per minute.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a11atlas_32-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-a11atlas-32">
             [32]
            </a>
           </sup>
           In addition, by looking at the photographs in sequence, one can see that very often several of them were taken in rapid succession. Here is a
           <a class="external text" href="http://www.hq.nasa.gov/alsj/a11/images11.html#Mag40" rel="nofollow">
            list of Apollo 11 surface photos
           </a>
           (AS11-40-5850 to AS11-40-5970). As can be seen from the map, many photos were taken from a similar position.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Quality_of_the_photographs">
        Quality of the photographs
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Quality of the photographs">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Given the lack of time and viewfinders, the photos look much better than would be expected, with perfect focus and exposure, a charge made by
       <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Ren%C3%A9" title="Ralph René">
        Ralph René
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33">
        <a href="#cite_note-33">
         [33]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           The astronauts were trained in the use of their gear, and shots and poses were planned in advance as part of the mission. NASA selected only the best photographs for release to the public, and some of the photos were cropped to improve their composition. There are many badly exposed, badly focused and poorly composed images amongst the thousands of photos that were taken by the Apollo astronauts. All photos taken on the surface of the Moon by the astronauts can be seen at the
           <a class="external text" href="http://www.hq.nasa.gov/alsj/frame.html" rel="nofollow">
            Apollo Lunar Surface Journal
           </a>
           . Photos were taken on high-quality Hasselblad cameras with
           <a href="/wiki/Carl_Zeiss_AG" title="Carl Zeiss AG">
            Zeiss
           </a>
           lenses, using 70 mm
           <a href="/wiki/Medium_format_(film)" title="Medium format (film)">
            medium format
           </a>
           film.
          </i>
          <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34">
           <a href="#cite_note-34">
            [34]
           </a>
          </sup>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Artifacts_in_the_film">
        Artifacts in the film
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Artifacts in the film">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A resident of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Perth,_Western_Australia" title="Perth, Western Australia">
        Perth, Australia
       </a>
       , a woman named Una Ronald (a pseudonym created by the authors of the source
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
        <a href="#cite_note-37">
         [37]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ), said that for two or three seconds she saw a
       <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">
        Coca-Cola
       </a>
       bottle roll across the lower right quadrant of her television screen that was displaying the
       <a href="/wiki/Live_television" title="Live television">
        live broadcast
       </a>
       of the Apollo 11 EVA, and believed the rolling bottle was edited out of later playbacks of the footage. She also said that several letters appeared in
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_West_Australian" title="The West Australian">
         The West Australian
        </a>
       </i>
       discussing the Coca-Cola bottle incident within ten days of the lunar landing.
       <a href="/wiki/Western_Australia" title="Western Australia">
        Western Australia
       </a>
       was the only place in the world that received their feed live without delay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
        <a href="#cite_note-38">
         [38]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavius.org_39-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-clavius.org-39">
         [39]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           It is true that
           <a href="/wiki/Australia" title="Australia">
            Australian
           </a>
           viewers saw the footage first, as the downlink was to several
           <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Radio-telescope" title="Radio-telescope">
            radio-telescopes
           </a>
           in
           <a href="/wiki/New_South_Wales" title="New South Wales">
            New South Wales
           </a>
           , including
           <a href="/wiki/Parkes_Observatory" title="Parkes Observatory">
            Parkes Observatory
           </a>
           .
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkes.atnf.csiro.au_40-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-parkes.atnf.csiro.au-40">
             [40]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
          But
          <i>
           <b>
            the lead over Houston's transmission was only 6 seconds
           </b>
           . Not enough time to do a convincing superimposition of a bottle being kicked by an astronaut, and not enough time to convincingly remove a bottle kicked by an actor, even with today's technology, and even if the operator was prepared in advance.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Transmissions from the Moon required video signals of very different design than that of ordinary television, and were converted to standard video by pointing a camera at a video screen, a process known as
           <a href="/wiki/Kinescope" title="Kinescope">
            kinescope
           </a>
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkes.atnf.csiro.au_40-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-parkes.atnf.csiro.au-40">
             [40]
            </a>
           </sup>
           —similar to the predominant method of recording television in the day—to
           <a href="/wiki/16_mm_film" title="16 mm film">
            16 mm film
           </a>
           , not to
           <a href="/wiki/Videotape" title="Videotape">
            videotape
           </a>
           , which was expensive and cumbersome.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvt_41-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-tvt-41">
             [41]
            </a>
           </sup>
           The process is vulnerable to added reflections between the monitor glass and the camera lens. "
           <a href="/wiki/Ghosting_(television)" title="Ghosting (television)">
            Ghost
           </a>
           " mirror images of highlights appear throughout the recordings of the broadcast video and are undoubtedly a result of this process.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42">
            <a href="#cite_note-42">
             [42]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Such artifacts were noticed at the time by the operators, though some of them may have been introduced in the recording of the broadcast, rather than during the preparation for broadcast.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           Analysis shows an optical artifact fitting the description given. It is clearly caused by a reflection inside the kinescope conversion system. Its motion precisely mirrors Aldrin's in the shot.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavius.org_39-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-clavius.org-39">
             [39]
            </a>
           </sup>
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cokebottle_43-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-cokebottle-43">
             [43]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           An
           <a href="/wiki/Moving_Picture_Experts_Group" title="Moving Picture Experts Group">
            MPEG
           </a>
           video segment available directly from NASA, said to be of the exact footage in question,
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cokebottle_43-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-cokebottle-43">
             [43]
            </a>
           </sup>
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-a11v_1101342.mpg_44-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-a11v_1101342.mpg-44">
             [44]
            </a>
           </sup>
           does indeed show artifacts which correspond to ghosting occurring—although none
          </i>
          obviously
          <i>
           resemble any type of bottle. Indeed, with the quality of the recording available, spotting a stray bottle on the "set" is a hard task, even when told what to look for, where and when.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           A Clavius.org researcher, Peter Barrett,
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45">
            <a href="#cite_note-45">
             [note 1]
            </a>
           </sup>
           who examined archival copies of the editions of the paper surrounding this time, was unable to find any evidence of published discussions described by Ronald. Likewise, chief librarian Tracey Bennett for The West Australian confirmed that for at least two weeks following the Apollo 11 moonwalk, the paper published no accounts of a
           <a href="/wiki/Coca-Cola" title="Coca-Cola">
            Coke
           </a>
           bottle being seen in the broadcast.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavius.org_39-2">
            <a href="#cite_note-clavius.org-39">
             [39]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Ronald's claims have only been relayed by one source.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46">
            <a href="#cite_note-46">
             [45]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           According to the one source,
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
            <a href="#cite_note-47">
             [46]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Ronald distinctly mentioned that she had to stay up late to watch the Moon landing live. This may indicate that she is an invention of someone who is not from Australia, or is someone who has little knowledge of the Moon landing, as those who did watch the Moon landing live in Australia usually recall that it occurred in the middle of the Australian day.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavius.org_39-3">
            <a href="#cite_note-clavius.org-39">
             [39]
            </a>
           </sup>
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48">
            <a href="#cite_note-48">
             [47]
            </a>
           </sup>
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           It would be technically
          </i>
          incorrect
          <i>
           to say that Western Australia received the footage "before the rest of the world," since this discounts the remainder of Australia. So if that is what was claimed in the original source for the claim, then that is one more glossing-over of the specific details of the event, which does not count in their favour, and demonstrates an actual lack-of-familiarity with Australia.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           Parkes puts the time of the broadcast at 12:54 p.m., and Western Australia is two hours behind
           <a href="/wiki/Time_in_Australia" title="Time in Australia">
            Australian Eastern Standard Time
           </a>
           , so any live broadcast would have been received there at around 11 a.m. local time. (
           <a href="/wiki/Daylight_saving_time" title="Daylight saving time">
            Daylight saving time
           </a>
           is not active in the
           <a href="/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere" title="Southern Hemisphere">
            Southern Hemisphere
           </a>
           in July when the Moon landing took place, so the calculation is simplified.) So assuming Ronald did indeed watch her broadcast late at night, then logically the reason her footage differed from that seen by the rest of the world must have been that it had been doctored between the time of the live broadcast when most of the world failed to observe anything unusual, and her later viewing some kind of delayed broadcast
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clavius.org_39-4">
            <a href="#cite_note-clavius.org-39">
             [39]
            </a>
           </sup>
           (none is known to have taken place, but the possibility is hard to rule out).
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <dl>
         <dd>
          <i>
           However, it is not difficult to verify that videotape technology was not widely available in 1969, and was bulky, expensive and required specialist knowledge to operate.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvt_41-1">
            <a href="#cite_note-tvt-41">
             [41]
            </a>
           </sup>
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49">
            <a href="#cite_note-49">
             [48]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Film was still the predominant storage medium, even for professional archiving of television broadcasts.
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-parkes.atnf.csiro.au_40-2">
            <a href="#cite_note-parkes.atnf.csiro.au-40">
             [40]
            </a>
           </sup>
           Altering a videotape would require access to prized equipment, which would be unlikely to be available to the casual practical-joker, even if they had the ability to operate it so well.
          </i>
         </dd>
        </dl>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_of_a_clearly_altered_photograph">
        Publication of a clearly altered photograph

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Publication of a clearly altered photograph">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The 1994
       <a href="/wiki/Hardcover" title="Hardcover">
        hardcover
       </a>
       version of
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Moon_Shot" title="Moon Shot">
         Moon Shot
        </a>
       </i>
       by Apollo 14 astronaut Alan Shepard and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apollo-Soyuz_Test_Project" title="Apollo-Soyuz Test Project">
        Apollo-Soyuz Test Project
       </a>
       crewmember
       <a href="/wiki/Deke_Slayton" title="Deke Slayton">
        Deke Slayton
       </a>
       contains a photograph of Shepard playing
       <a href="/wiki/Golf" title="Golf">
        golf
       </a>
       on the Moon with another astronaut.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50">
        <a href="#cite_note-50">
         [49]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The picture is an obvious fake, there being no one else to take the shot of the two, and the artwork was poor (such as the grapefruit sized "golf ball"), and yet it was presented as if it were a real photo.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51">
        <a href="#cite_note-51">
         [50]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>

           <a href="/wiki/United_Kingdom" title="United Kingdom">

           <a href="/wiki/Television" title="Television">
            TV
           </a>
           monitor, which the editors of the book apparently felt were too grainy to present in a book's picture section.
          </i>
         </li>
         <li>
          <i>

           <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/resources/apollo/frame/?AS14-66-9276" rel="nofollow">

           <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/resources/apollo/frame/?AS14-66-9240" rel="nofollow">

           <a class="external text" href="http://www.lpi.usra.edu/resources/apollo/frame/?AS14-66-9241" rel="nofollow">

      <p>
       Shepard duffed the first ball and hit the second one fairly cleanly. From
       <a href="/wiki/Houston" title="Houston">
        Houston
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Christopher_C._Kraft_Jr._Mission_Control_Center" title="Christopher C. Kraft Jr. Mission Control Center">
        Mission Control
       </a>
       's
       <a href="/wiki/Flight_controller#Capsule_Communicator_(CAPCOM)" title="Flight controller">
        CAPCOM
       </a>
       , astronaut
       <a href="/wiki/Fred_Haise" title="Fred Haise">
        Fred Haise
       </a>
       , joked to Shepard,
       <i>
        "That looked like a slice to me, Al,"
       </i>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-golfshot_53-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-golfshot-53">
         [52]
        </a>
       </sup>
       yet a
       <a href="/wiki/Golf_stroke_mechanics" title="Golf stroke mechanics">
        slice
       </a>
       is caused by uneven airflow on the ball. This is inconsequential with an atmosphere as thin as that of the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54">
        <a href="#cite_note-54">
         [53]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <ul>
         <li>
          <i>
           The ball moved only two or three feet. Shepard also stated that the second ball went "miles and miles and miles" (off-camera of the television broadcast), which was clearly a joke, like the comment about the slice. Later, in an interview, Shepard said, "Then I thought, with the same clubhead speed, the ball's going to go at least six times as far. There's absolutely no drag, so if you do happen to spin it, it won't slice or hook 'cause there's no atmosphere to make it turn."
           <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-slice_55-0">
            <a href="#cite_note-slice-55">
             [54]
            </a>
           </sup>
           A slice comes from hitting the ball off the outer end of the club-head, versus hitting it square in the middle of the club-head, versus hooking it, which is hitting it off the inner end of the club-head. Shepard did, in effect, "slice" the ball at first, and as he notes, being in the virtually non-existent lunar atmosphere, the ball did not curve laterally as an earthbound slice would.
          </i>
         </li>
        </ul>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Apparent_air_resistance">
        Apparent air resistance
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Examination_of_Apollo_Moon_photographs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Apparent air resistance">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       With no air resistance, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Trajectory_of_a_projectile" title="Trajectory of a projectile">
        trajectory of a projectile
       </a>
       is a
       <a href="/wiki/Parabola" title="Parabola">
        parabola
       </a>
       . Apparent deviation of the dust particles from the ideal parabolic trajectory on the footage of the Apollo 16 lunar rover was attributed to the influence of air resistance, implying that the footage is fake. The argument would be plausible were the dust particles ejected with a constant velocity by a source moving with a constant speed. However, the wheels were ejecting the particles with a greatly varying speed, when the rover was moving over deep dust pockets.
