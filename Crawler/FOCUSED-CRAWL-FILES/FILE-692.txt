      <p>
       <i>
        <b>
         First Men in the Moon
        </b>
       </i>
       is a 1964 British
       <a href="/wiki/Technicolor" title="Technicolor">
        Technicolor
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">
        science fiction film
       </a>
       , produced by
       <a href="/wiki/Charles_H._Schneer" title="Charles H. Schneer">
        Charles H. Schneer
       </a>
       , directed by
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nathan_Juran" title="Nathan Juran">
        Nathan Juran
       </a>
       , that stars
       <a href="/wiki/Edward_Judd" title="Edward Judd">
        Edward Judd
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Martha_Hyer" title="Martha Hyer">
        Martha Hyer
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Jeffries" title="Lionel Jeffries">
        Lionel Jeffries
       </a>
       . It is an adaptation by science fiction scriptwriter
       <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Kneale" title="Nigel Kneale">
        Nigel Kneale
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       ' 1901 novel
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">
         The First Men in the Moon
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Ray_Harryhausen" title="Ray Harryhausen">
        Ray Harryhausen
       </a>
       provided the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stop-motion_animation" title="Stop-motion animation">
        stop-motion animation
       </a>
       effects, which include the Selenites, giant
       <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">
        caterpillar
       </a>
       -like "Moon
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cow" title="Cow">
        Cows
       </a>
       ", and the big-brained Prime Lunar.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-juran_3-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-juran-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">
        Plot
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=First_Men_in_the_Moon_(1964_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 1964, the
       <a href="/wiki/United_Nations" title="United Nations">
        United Nations
       </a>
       (UN) has launched a rocket flight to the Moon. A multi-national group of astronauts in the UN spacecraft land on the Moon, believing themselves to be the first lunar explorers. They discover a British Union Jack on the surface and a note naming Katherine Callender, claiming the Moon for
       <a href="/wiki/Queen_Victoria" title="Queen Victoria">
        Queen Victoria
       </a>
       . Attempting to trace Callender, UN authorities find she has died, but find her husband Arnold Bedford now an old man in an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nursing_home" title="Nursing home">
        old people's home
       </a>
       . The nursing home staff do not let him watch television reports of the expedition because, according to the matron, it "excites him", dismissing his claims to have been on the Moon as an insane delusion. The UN representatives question him about the Moon and he tells them his story. The rest of the film, as a
       <a href="/wiki/Flashback_(narrative)" title="Flashback (narrative)">
        flashback
       </a>
       , shows what Bedford and Professor Cavor did in the 1890s.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1899, Arnold Bedford and his fiancée Katherine Callender – known as Kate – meet an inventor, Joseph Cavor, who has invented
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavorite" title="Cavorite">
        Cavorite
       </a>
       , a substance that will let anything it is applied to or made of
       <a href="/wiki/Anti-gravity" title="Anti-gravity">
        deflect the force of gravity
       </a>
       and which he plans to use to travel to the Moon. Cavor has already built a spherical
       <a href="/wiki/Spacecraft" title="Spacecraft">
        spaceship
       </a>
       for this purpose, taking Arnold and (accidentally) Kate with him. While exploring the Moon, Bedford and Cavor fall down a vertical shaft and discover to their amazement an
       <a href="/wiki/Insectoid" title="Insectoid">
        insectoid
       </a>
       population, the Selenites, living beneath the surface. (Cavor coins this name for the creatures after the Greek goddess of the moon,
       <a href="/wiki/Selene" title="Selene">
        Selene
       </a>
       ). Bedford attacks a group of Selenites in fear -- killing several, despite Cavor's horrified protests. After escaping from the Selenites back to the surface, they discover that their ship, still containing Kate (who stayed behind because Cavor had brought only two spacesuits), has been dragged into their underground city.
      </p>
      <p>
       The two, following the drag trail, find and enter the city. The city holds a breathable atmosphere, so (unwisely) they remove and leave their spacesuit helmets. Upon finding the living quarters, they are attacked by a giant
       <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar" title="Caterpillar">
        caterpillar
       </a>
       -like "moon
       <a href="/wiki/Bull" title="Bull">
        bull
       </a>
       " which pursues them until the Selenites find out and are able to zap it to death with their
       <a href="/wiki/Tesla_coil" title="Tesla coil">
        Tesla coil
       </a>
       -like electrical stun ray gun. Cavor and Bedford see the city's
       <a href="/wiki/Power_station" title="Power station">
        power station
       </a>
       , powered by sunlight. In the end, they reach their ship underground. The Selenites quickly learn English and interrogate Cavor, who believes they wish to exchange
       <a href="/wiki/Science" title="Science">
        scientific
       </a>
       knowledge; this also leads up to Cavor having a discussion with the "Grand Lunar", the ruling entity of the Selenites. Bedford, however, upon entering the chamber just as the Grand Lunar voices his concerns over human aggressiveness, makes the assumption that Cavor, and presumably all humanity, is on trial, attempts to murder the Grand Lunar with an elephant gun -- failing due to Cavor's attempts to stop him. Running for their lives, Bedford manages to find the sphere and escape, but Cavor stays voluntarily on the Moon.
      </p>
      <p>
       Bedford, along with Kate (who only leaves the ship once, to help repair the damage caused by the Selenites), flies the ship up a vertical shaft, shattering the window cover at the top, and back to Earth. The aged Bedford concludes his story by mentioning that the ship came down in the sea off
       <a href="/wiki/Zanzibar" title="Zanzibar">
        Zanzibar
       </a>
       , and sank, but he and Kate managed to swim ashore. Cavor's ultimate fate remained unknown.
      </p>
      <p>
       Back in the present day, Bedford, the UN party and newspaper reporters watch on television the latest events on the Moon, where the UN astronauts have broken into the Selenite city and find it deserted and decaying. Moments later, the ruined city starts to crumble and collapse, forcing the landing crew to retreat hastily, and seconds later the city -- and all of its history -- is completely destroyed. Bedford realizes that the Selenites must have been killed off by Cavor's
       <a href="/wiki/Common_cold" title="Common cold">
        common cold
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/Virus" title="Virus">
        viruses
       </a>
       to which they had no
       <a href="/wiki/Immune_system" title="Immune system">
        immunity
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">

       <a href="/wiki/Spacesuits_in_fiction" title="Spacesuits in fiction">
        Spacesuits in fiction
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       Two types of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">
        spacesuits
       </a>
       are featured. During the events of the story, which take place in the 1890s,
       <a href="/wiki/Standard_diving_dress" title="Standard diving dress">
        standard diving dresses
       </a>
       , each fitted with a 1960s-type
       <a href="/wiki/Aqua-lung" title="Aqua-lung">
        aqualung
       </a>
       cylinder worn
       <a href="/wiki/Backpack" title="Backpack">
        backpack
       </a>
       instead of a
       <a href="/wiki/NASA" title="NASA">
        NASA
       </a>
       -type
       <a href="/wiki/Life_support" title="Life support">
        life support
       </a>
       backpack, and no lifeline or airline from outside, are used as spacesuits. The film's Victorian-era astronauts may be outfitted in diving suits with oxygen tanks, but their suits are neither pressurized nor heated or cooled. They do not wear protective gloves despite the vacuum of space and extreme cold (and heat) of the lunar surface. There are other technical issues confronting the movie maker: even with heating and cooling provided, using rubber-lined diving suits on the Moon is impractical. Even before the space age began, the 1948 science fiction short story, "Gentlemen, Be Seated!" by
       <a href="/wiki/Robert_A._Heinlein" title="Robert A. Heinlein">
        Robert A. Heinlein
       </a>
       , deftly describes the brittleness of rubber once it is exposed to the vacuum of space.
      </p>
      <p>
       Cavor and Bedford have no
       <a href="/wiki/Radio" title="Radio">
        radio
       </a>
       and must make their helmets touch each other to talk in the vacuum (although the filmmakers violate this rule several times). It is not clear whether the Selenites have radio. The
       <a href="/wiki/History_of_radio" title="History of radio">
        history of radio
       </a>
       was only just starting when the 1890s events were set.
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wireless_communication" title="Wireless communication">
        Wireless communication
       </a>
       from Cavor in the Moon appears in
       <a href="/wiki/H._G._Wells" title="H. G. Wells">
        H. G. Wells
       </a>
       's novel.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spacesuit" title="Spacesuit">
        spacesuit
       </a>
       worn by the UN Astronauts is actually the Windak high-altitude pressure suit,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-spacesuit_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-spacesuit-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       developed for the Royal Air Force (here each fitted with a 1960s-type aqualung cylinder worn backpack). These pressure suits would also be used in two
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Doctor_Who" title="Doctor Who">
         Doctor Who
        </a>
       </i>
       stories:
       <a href="/wiki/William_Hartnell" title="William Hartnell">
        William Hartnell's
       </a>
       final story "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Tenth_Planet" title="The Tenth Planet">
        The Tenth Planet
       </a>
       " and the
       <a href="/wiki/Patrick_Troughton" title="Patrick Troughton">
        Patrick Troughton
       </a>
       -Era "
       <a href="/wiki/The_Wheel_in_Space" title="The Wheel in Space">
        The Wheel in Space
       </a>
       ". They also appear in the original
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars" title="Star Wars">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       trilogy as the costumes for
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_Star_Wars_characters#B" title="List of Star Wars characters">
        Bossk
       </a>
       and Bo Shek.
      </p>
