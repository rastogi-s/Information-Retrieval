   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Themis (hypothetical moon)
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
    <div class="noprint" id="siteSub">
     From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia
    </div>
    <div id="contentSub">
    </div>
    <div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
     Jump to:
     <a href="#mw-head">
      navigation
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="#p-search">
      search
     </a>
    </div>
    <div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en">
     <div class="mw-parser-output">
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For other uses, see
       <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Themis_(disambiguation)" title="Themis (disambiguation)">
        Themis (disambiguation)
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <p>
       On April 28, 1905,
       <a href="/wiki/William_Henry_Pickering" title="William Henry Pickering">
        William H. Pickering
       </a>
       , who had seven years earlier discovered
       <a href="/wiki/Phoebe_(moon)" title="Phoebe (moon)">
        Phoebe
       </a>
       , announced the discovery of a tenth
       <a href="/wiki/Natural_satellite" title="Natural satellite">
        satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Saturn" title="Saturn">
        Saturn
       </a>
       , which he promptly named
       <b>
        <a href="/wiki/Themis" title="Themis">
         Themis
        </a>
       </b>
       . The photographic plates on which it supposedly appeared, thirteen in all, spanned a period between April 17 and July 8, 1904. However, no other astronomer has ever confirmed Pickering's claim.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pickering attempted to compute an orbit, which showed a fairly high
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inclination" title="Inclination">
        inclination
       </a>
       (39.1° to the
       <a href="/wiki/Ecliptic" title="Ecliptic">
        ecliptic
       </a>
       ), fairly large
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eccentricity_(orbit)" title="Eccentricity (orbit)">
        eccentricity
       </a>
       (0.23) and a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Semi-major_axis" title="Semi-major axis">
        semi-major axis
       </a>
       (1,457,000 kilometres (905,000 mi)) slightly less than that of
       <a href="/wiki/Hyperion_(moon)" title="Hyperion (moon)">
        Hyperion
       </a>
       . The period was supposedly 20.85 days, with
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Retrograde_motion" title="Retrograde motion">
        prograde
       </a>
       motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Pickering estimated the diameter at 61 kilometres (38 mi), but since he also gave 68 kilometres (42 mi) as the diameter of Phoebe, he was clearly overestimating the
       <a href="/wiki/Albedo" title="Albedo">
        albedo
       </a>
       ; using the modern figure for Phoebe gives Themis a diameter of 200 kilometres (120 mi).
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Themis_orbit.gif">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1143" data-file-width="906" height="278" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Themis_orbit.gif/220px-Themis_orbit.gif" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Themis_orbit.gif/330px-Themis_orbit.gif 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Themis_orbit.gif/440px-Themis_orbit.gif 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Themis_orbit.gif" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Two possible orbits for Themis as calculated by W. H. Pickering
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       Oddly, in April 1861,
       <a href="/wiki/Hermann_Goldschmidt" title="Hermann Goldschmidt">
        Hermann Goldschmidt
       </a>
       had also believed that he had discovered a new satellite of Saturn between Titan and Hyperion, which he called
       <a href="/wiki/Chiron_(hypothetical_moon)" title="Chiron (hypothetical moon)">
        Chiron
       </a>
       . Chiron also does not exist (however, the name was used much later for the
       <a href="/wiki/Comet" title="Comet">
        comet
       </a>
       /
       <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">
        asteroid
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/2060_Chiron" title="2060 Chiron">
        2060 Chiron
       </a>
       ).
      </p>
      <p>
       Pickering was awarded the
       <a href="/wiki/Lalande_Prize" title="Lalande Prize">
        Lalande Prize
       </a>
       of the
       <a href="/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences" title="French Academy of Sciences">
        French Academy of Sciences
       </a>
       in 1906 for his "discovery of the ninth and tenth satellites of Saturn".
      </p>
      <p>
       The actual tenth satellite of Saturn (in order of discovery) was
       <a href="/wiki/Janus_(moon)" title="Janus (moon)">
        Janus
       </a>
       , which was discovered in 1966 and confirmed in 1980. Its orbit is far from the supposed orbit of Themis.
      </p>
      <p>
       There is also an asteroid named
       <a href="/wiki/24_Themis" title="24 Themis">
        24 Themis
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Themis_in_fiction">
        Themis in fiction
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Themis_(hypothetical_moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Themis in fiction">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <ul>
       <li>
        Philip Latham (pen-name of
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_S._Richardson" title="Robert S. Richardson">
         Robert S. Richardson
        </a>
        ), in his novel
        <a href="/wiki/Missing_Men_of_Saturn" title="Missing Men of Saturn">
         Missing Men of Saturn
        </a>
        , has Themis collide with Titan, "getting rid of the little nuisance once and for all", according to the introduction.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/John_Varley_(author)" title="John Varley (author)">
         John Varley
        </a>
        's
        <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">
         science fiction
        </a>
        novel
        <i>
         <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titan_(John_Varley)" title="Titan (John Varley)">
          Titan
         </a>
        </i>
        is set aboard an expedition to Saturn. As they approach the planet and prepare to enter orbit, the astronomer onboard discovers a new moon. At first she believes she has recovered Pickering's lost moon, so she names it Themis.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Robert_Anton_Wilson" title="Robert Anton Wilson">
         Robert Anton Wilson's
        </a>
        novel
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger%27s_Cat_trilogy" title="Schrödinger's Cat trilogy">
         Schrödinger's Cat trilogy
        </a>
        makes frequent reference to Pickering's Moon as a satellite that revolves the "wrong way" around its primary.
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Nelson_S._Bond" title="Nelson S. Bond">
         Nelson S. Bond
        </a>
        , in his 1943 science-fiction story "
        <a href="/wiki/The_Remarkable_Exploits_of_Lancelot_Biggs,_Spaceman" title="The Remarkable Exploits of Lancelot Biggs, Spaceman">
         The Ordeal of Lancelot Biggs
        </a>
        ," explains that Themis periodically disappears when it is occulted by its own moon, an invisible body with "the peculiar property of being able to warp light waves around itself."
       </li>
      </ul>
