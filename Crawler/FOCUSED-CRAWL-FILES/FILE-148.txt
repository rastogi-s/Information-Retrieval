      <p>
       <b>
        Mimas
       </b>
       is a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Moon_of_Saturn" title="Moon of Saturn">
        moon of Saturn
       </a>
       which was discovered in 1789 by
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Herschel1790_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Herschel1790-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is named after
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(Giant)" title="Mimas (Giant)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       , a son of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">
        Gaia
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       , and is also designated
       <b>
        Saturn I
       </b>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       With a diameter of 396 kilometres (246 mi) it is the smallest
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Astronomical_body" title="Astronomical body">
        astronomical body
       </a>
       that is known to be rounded in shape because of
       <a href="/wiki/Self-gravitation" title="Self-gravitation">
        self-gravitation
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery">
        Discovery
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Discovery">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       Mimas was discovered by the
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomer" title="Astronomer">
        astronomer
       </a>
       <a href="/wiki/William_Herschel" title="William Herschel">
        William Herschel
       </a>
       on 17 September 1789. He recorded his discovery as follows: "The great light of my forty-foot [12 m] telescope was so useful that on the 17th of September, 1789, I remarked the seventh satellite, then situated at its greatest western elongation."
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Name">
        Name
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Name">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Mimas is named after one of the
       <a href="/wiki/Giants_(Greek_mythology)" title="Giants (Greek mythology)">
        Giants
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">
        Greek mythology
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Mimas_(Giant)" title="Mimas (Giant)">
        Mimas
       </a>
       . The names of all seven then-known satellites of Saturn, including Mimas, were suggested by William Herschel's son
       <a href="/wiki/John_Herschel" title="John Herschel">
        John
       </a>
       in his 1847 publication
       <i>
        Results of Astronomical Observations made at the Cape of Good Hope
       </i>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       He named them after Titans specifically because Saturn (the Roman equivalent of
       <a href="/wiki/Cronus" title="Cronus">
        Cronus
       </a>
       in Greek mythology), was the leader of the Titans and ruler of the world for some time. Mimas was a son of the Greek goddess
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gaia_(mythology)" title="Gaia (mythology)">
        Gaia
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Physical_characteristics">
        Physical characteristics
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Physical characteristics">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       For more details on this topic, see
       <a href="/wiki/List_of_geological_features_on_Mimas" title="List of geological features on Mimas">
        List of geological features on Mimas
       </a>
       .
      </div>
      <div class="thumb tleft">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:302px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1200" data-file-width="1200" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg/300px-PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg/450px-PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg/600px-PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg 2x" width="300"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:PIA06256_Mimas_full_view.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         <i>
          Cassini
         </i>
         view of Mimas's trailing hemisphere, showing craters up to 6 km deep and 1-km-deep chasmata (grooves). The large crater near center is Morgan; Arthur is close to the lower right limb. Pelion Chasma is faintly visible as a horizontal trough left of Arthur and below Morgan.
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       The surface area of Mimas is slightly less than the land area of
       <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">
        Spain
       </a>
       . The low density of Mimas, 1.15 g/cm
       <sup>
        3
       </sup>
       , indicates that it is composed mostly of water ice with only a small amount of rock. Due to the tidal forces acting on it, Mimas is noticeably
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prolate_spheroid" title="Prolate spheroid">
        prolate
       </a>
       ; its longest axis is about 10% longer than the shortest. The
       <a href="/wiki/Ellipsoid#Rotational_equilibrium" title="Ellipsoid">
        ellipsoidal
       </a>
       shape of Mimas is especially noticeable in some recent images from the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
         Cassini
        </a>
       </i>
       probe.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mimas's most distinctive feature is a giant
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        impact crater
       </a>
       130 km (81 mi) across, named
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_(Mimantean_crater)" title="Herschel (Mimantean crater)">
        Herschel
       </a>
       after the discoverer of Mimas. Herschel's diameter is almost a third of Mimas's own diameter; its walls are approximately 5 km (3 mi) high, parts of its floor measure 10 km (6 mi) deep, and its central peak rises 6 km (4 mi) above the crater floor. If there were a crater of an equivalent scale on
       <a href="/wiki/Earth" title="Earth">
        Earth
       </a>
       (in relative size) it would be over 4,000 km (2,500 mi) in diameter, wider than
       <a href="/wiki/Australia_(continent)" title="Australia (continent)">
        Australia
       </a>
       . The impact that made this crater must have nearly shattered Mimas: fractures can be seen on the opposite side of Mimas that may have been created by shock waves from the impact travelling through Mimas's body.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
        <a href="#cite_note-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Mimantean surface is saturated with smaller impact craters, but no others are anywhere near the size of Herschel. Although Mimas is heavily cratered, the cratering is not uniform. Most of the surface is covered with craters larger than 40 km (25 mi) in diameter, but in the south polar region, there are generally no craters larger than 20 km (12 mi) in diameter.
      </p>
      <p>
       Three types of geological features are officially recognized on Mimas:
       <a href="/wiki/Impact_crater" title="Impact crater">
        craters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Chasma" title="Chasma">
        chasmata
       </a>
       (chasms) and
       <a href="/wiki/Crater_chain" title="Crater chain">
        catenae
       </a>
       (crater chains).
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Orbital_resonances">
        Orbital resonances
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Orbital resonances">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       A number of features in
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturn%27s_rings" title="Saturn's rings">
        Saturn's rings
       </a>
       are related to
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        resonances
       </a>
       with Mimas. Mimas is responsible for clearing the material from the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cassini_Division" title="Cassini Division">
        Cassini Division
       </a>
       , the gap between Saturn's two widest rings, the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_Ring" title="A Ring">
        A Ring
       </a>
       and
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/B_Ring" title="B Ring">
        B Ring
       </a>
       . Particles in the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#Huygens_Gap" title="Rings of Saturn">
        Huygens Gap
       </a>
       at the inner edge of the Cassini division are in a 2:1
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        resonance
       </a>
       with Mimas. They orbit twice for each orbit of Mimas. The repeated pulls by Mimas on the Cassini division particles, always in the same direction in space, force them into new orbits outside the gap. The boundary between the C and B ring is in a 3:1 resonance with Mimas. Recently, the
       <a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Saturn#G_Ring" title="Rings of Saturn">
        G Ring
       </a>
       was found to be in a 7:6 co-rotation eccentricity resonance
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="margin-left:0.1em; white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify">
          <span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (November 2012)">
           clarification needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
       with Mimas; the ring's inner edge is about 15,000 km (9,300 mi) inside Mimas's orbit.
       <sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">
        [
        <i>
         <a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed">
          <span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2011)">
           citation needed
          </span>
         </a>
        </i>
        ]
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Mimas is also in a 2:1
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance#Mean-motion_resonances_in_the_Solar_System" title="Orbital resonance">
        mean-motion resonance
       </a>
       with the larger moon
       <a href="/wiki/Tethys_(moon)" title="Tethys (moon)">
        Tethys
       </a>
       , and in a 2:3 resonance with the outer
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/F_Ring" title="F Ring">
        F Ring
       </a>
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Planetary_ring" title="Planetary ring">
        shepherd
       </a>
       moonlet,
       <a href="/wiki/Pandora_(moon)" title="Pandora (moon)">
        Pandora
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Anomalous_libration">
        Anomalous libration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Anomalous libration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       In 2014, researchers noted that the
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        librational
       </a>
       motion of Mimas has a component that cannot be explained by its orbit alone, and concluded that it was due to either an interior that is not in
       <a href="/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium#Planetary_geology" title="Hydrostatic equilibrium">
        hydrostatic equilibrium
       </a>
       (an elongated
       <a href="/wiki/Planetary_core" title="Planetary core">
        core
       </a>
       ) or an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Internal_ocean" title="Internal ocean">
        internal ocean
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Tajeddine2014_13-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Tajeddine2014-13">
         [13]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in 2017 it was concluded that an ocean would lead to surface tidal stresses comparable to or greater than those on tectonically active
       <a href="/wiki/Europa_(moon)" title="Europa (moon)">
        Europa
       </a>
       . Thus, the lack of evidence for surface cracking or other tectonic activity on Mimas argues against the presence of such an ocean.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhoden2017_14-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rhoden2017-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Since formation of a core would likely have led to formation of an internal ocean in the past, probably leading to geologic activity, this explanation for the libration is also problematic. The presence of an asymmetric mass anomaly associated with the crater Herschel is another possible explanation for the libration.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Rhoden2017_14-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Rhoden2017-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Exploration">
        Exploration
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Exploration">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       <a href="/wiki/Pioneer_11" title="Pioneer 11">
        Pioneer 11
       </a>
       flew by Saturn in 1979, and its closest approach to Mimas was 104,263 km on September 1, 1979.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
        <a href="#cite_note-15">
         [15]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_1" title="Voyager 1">
        Voyager 1
       </a>
       flew by in 1980, and
       <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
        Voyager 2
       </a>
       in 1981.
      </p>
      <p>
       Mimas has been imaged several times by the
       <a href="/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens" title="Cassini–Huygens">
        <i>
         Cassini
        </i>
        orbiter
       </a>
       , which entered into orbit around Saturn in 2004. A close flyby occurred on February 13, 2010, when
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       passed by Mimas at 9,500 km (5,900 mi).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">
        In popular culture
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Mimas_(moon)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: In popular culture">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       When seen from certain angles, Mimas resembles the
       <a href="/wiki/Death_Star" title="Death Star">
        Death Star
       </a>
       , a fictional
       <a href="/wiki/Space_station" title="Space station">
        space station
       </a>
       and superweapon known from the 1977 film
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_(film)" title="Star Wars (film)">
         Star Wars
        </a>
       </i>
       .
       <a href="/wiki/Herschel_(Mimantean_crater)" title="Herschel (Mimantean crater)">
        Herschel
       </a>
       resembles the concave disc of the Death Star's "superlaser". This is coincidental, as the film was made nearly three years before Mimas was resolved well enough to see the crater.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
        <a href="#cite_note-16">
         [16]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       In 2010, NASA revealed a temperature map of Mimas, using images obtained by
       <i>
        Cassini
       </i>
       . The warmest regions, which are along one edge of Mimas, create a shape similar to the video game character
       <a href="/wiki/Pac-Man" title="Pac-Man">
        Pac-Man
       </a>
       , with Herschel Crater assuming the role of an "edible dot" or
       <a href="/wiki/Pac-Man_(TV_series)" title="Pac-Man (TV series)">
        "power pellet"
       </a>
       known from Pac-Man gameplay.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
