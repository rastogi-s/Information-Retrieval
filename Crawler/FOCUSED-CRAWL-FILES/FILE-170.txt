      <p>
       A
       <b>
        total
        <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
         lunar eclipse
        </a>
       </b>
       took place on October 8, 2014. It is the second of two total lunar eclipses in 2014, and the second in a
       <a href="/wiki/Tetrad_(astronomy)" title="Tetrad (astronomy)">
        <i>
         tetrad
        </i>
       </a>
       (four total lunar eclipses in series). Other eclipses in the tetrad are those of
       <a href="/wiki/April_2014_lunar_eclipse" title="April 2014 lunar eclipse">
        April 15, 2014
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/April_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="April 2015 lunar eclipse">
        April 4, 2015
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="/wiki/September_2015_lunar_eclipse" title="September 2015 lunar eclipse">
        September 28, 2015
       </a>
       .
      </p>

      <p>
       The eclipse was visible in its entirety over the Northern Pacific. Viewers in North America experienced the eclipse after midnight on Wednesday, October 8, and the eclipse was visible from the Western Pacific, Australia, Indonesia, Japan, and Eastern Asia after sunset on the evening of October 8. Many areas of North America experienced a
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse#Selenelion" title="Lunar eclipse">
        selenelion
       </a>
       , able to see both the sun and the eclipsed moon at the same time.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-selenelion_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-selenelion-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Background">
        Background
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=October_2014_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Background">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable" role="note">
       Main article:
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_eclipse" title="Lunar eclipse">
        Lunar eclipse
       </a>
      </div>
      <p>
       A lunar eclipse occurs when the Moon passes within Earth's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Umbra" title="Umbra">
        umbra
       </a>
       (shadow). As the eclipse begins, the Earth's shadow first darkens the Moon slightly. Then, the shadow begins to "cover" part of the Moon, turning it a dark red-brown color (typically - the color can vary based on atmospheric conditions). The Moon appears to be reddish because of
       <a href="/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering" title="Rayleigh scattering">
        Rayleigh scattering
       </a>
       (the same effect that causes sunsets to appear reddish) and the refraction of that light by the Earth's atmosphere into its umbra.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The following simulation shows the approximate appearance of the Moon passing through the earth's shadow. The Moon's brightness is exaggerated within the umbral shadow. The southern portion of the Moon was closest to the center of the shadow, making it darkest, and most red in appearance.
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Animation_October_8_2014_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif">
         <img alt="Animation October 8 2014 lunar eclipse appearance.gif" data-file-height="350" data-file-width="480" height="350" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/98/Animation_October_8_2014_lunar_eclipse_appearance.gif" width="480"/>
        </a>
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <p>
       The planet
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       was near
       <a href="/wiki/Opposition_(planets)" title="Opposition (planets)">
        opposition
       </a>
       (opposition on October 7
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       ) during the eclipse, just over 1° from the eclipsed Moon. Shining at
       <a href="/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy)" title="Magnitude (astronomy)">
        magnitude
       </a>
       5.7, Uranus should have been bright enough to identify in binoculars. Due to
       <a href="/wiki/Parallax" title="Parallax">
        parallax
       </a>
       , the position of Uranus relative to the Moon varied significantly depending on the viewing position on the surface of Earth.
      </p>

      <p>
       The timing of total lunar eclipses are determined by its contacts:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <dl>
       <dd>
        <b>
         P1
        </b>
        (First contact): Beginning of the penumbral eclipse. Earth's penumbra touches the Moon's outer limb.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U1
        </b>
        (Second contact): Beginning of the partial eclipse. Earth's umbra touches the Moon's outer limb.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U2
        </b>
        (Third contact): Beginning of the total eclipse. The Moon's surface is entirely within Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         Greatest eclipse
        </b>
        : The peak stage of the total eclipse. The Moon is at its closest to the center of Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U3
        </b>
        (Fourth contact): End of the total eclipse. The Moon's outer limb exits Earth's umbra.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         U4
        </b>
        (Fifth contact): End of the partial eclipse. Earth's umbra leaves the Moon's surface.
       </dd>
       <dd>
        <b>
         P4
        </b>
        (Sixth contact): End of the penumbral eclipse. Earth's penumbra no longer makes contact with the Moon.
       </dd>
      </dl>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Related_eclipses">
        Related eclipses
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=October_2014_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Related eclipses">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The eclipse is the one of four total lunar eclipses in a short-lived series at the descending node of the Moon's orbit.
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_year" title="Lunar year">
        lunar year
       </a>
       series repeats after 12
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunation" title="Lunation">
        lunations
       </a>
       , or 354 days (shifting back about 10 days in sequential years). Because of the date shift, Earth's shadow will be about 11° west in sequential events.
      </p>

      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Half-Saros_cycle">
        Half-Saros cycle
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=October_2014_lunar_eclipse&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Half-Saros cycle">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       A lunar eclipse will be preceded and followed by solar eclipses by 9 years and 5.5 days (a
       <a href="/wiki/Saros_(astronomy)#Relationship_between_lunar_and_solar_saros_.28sar.29" title="Saros (astronomy)">
        half saros
       </a>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This lunar eclipse is related to two annular solar eclipses of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_saros_134" title="Solar saros 134">
        solar saros 134
       </a>
       .
      </p>
