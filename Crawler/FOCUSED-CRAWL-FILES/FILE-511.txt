      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Lunar Flashlight
       </b>
       is a planned low-cost
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       lunar orbiter mission to explore, locate, and estimate size and composition of
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_water" title="Lunar water">
        water ice deposits on the Moon
       </a>
       for future exploitation by robots or humans.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laser_1-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Laser-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS_2016_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPS_2016-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-techport-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPMWall_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPMWall-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The spacecraft, of the 6U
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       format, was developed by a team from the
       <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
        Jet Propulsion Laboratory
       </a>
       (JPL), the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">
        University of California, Los Angeles
       </a>
       (UCLA), and NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_5-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-techport-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was selected in early 2015 by NASA's Advanced Exploration Systems (AES) for a launch in 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2019-slip_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-2019-slip-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>

      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History">
        History
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flashlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       NASA's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lunar_Crater_Observation_and_Sensing_Satellite" title="Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite">
        Lunar Crater Observation and Sensing Satellite
       </a>
       (LCROSS), the
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Reconnaissance_Orbiter" title="Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter">
        Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter
       </a>
       (LRO) and India's
       <a href="/wiki/Chandrayaan-1" title="Chandrayaan-1">
        Chandrayaan-1
       </a>
       lunar orbiters and other missions discovered in 2009 both water (H
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       O) and hydroxyl (—OH
       <sup>
        −
       </sup>
       ) deposits at high latitudes on the lunar surface, indicating the presence of trace amounts of
       <a href="/wiki/Adsorption" title="Adsorption">
        adsorbed
       </a>
       or bound water are present.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These missions suggest that there might be enough ice water at polar regions to be used by future landed missions,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RPMWall_7-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-RPMWall-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       but the distribution is difficult to reconcile with thermal maps.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Lunar prospecting missions are intended to pave the way toward incorporating use of space resources into mission architectures. NASA's planning for eventual
       <a href="/wiki/Human_mission_to_Mars" title="Human mission to Mars">
        human missions to Mars
       </a>
       depends on tapping the local natural resources to make oxygen and propellant for launching the return ship back to Earth, and a lunar precursor mission is a convenient location to test such
       <a href="/wiki/In_situ_resource_utilization" title="In situ resource utilization">
        in situ resource utilization
       </a>
       (ISRU) technology.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_2015_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_2015-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The mission concept was developed by a team from the
       <a href="/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory" title="Jet Propulsion Laboratory">
        Jet Propulsion Laboratory
       </a>
       (JPL), the
       <a href="/wiki/University_of_California,_Los_Angeles" title="University of California, Los Angeles">
        University of California, Los Angeles
       </a>
       (UCLA), and NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       and proposed to NASA's FY2014 Advanced Exporation Systems (AES) call.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_5-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-techport-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission was selected for funding in early 2015.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Misra_2016_9-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Misra_2016-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="380" data-file-width="970" height="86" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg/220px-Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg/330px-Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg/440px-Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lunar_Flashlight_satellite.jpg" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         The initial concept considered using a
         <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">
          solar sail
         </a>
         <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-3">
          <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
           [6]
          </a>
         </sup>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       In its original conception, the Lunar Flashlight spacecraft would have been a 6U
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       format or
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_bus" title="Satellite bus">
        bus
       </a>
       propelled by an 80 m
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_sail" title="Solar sail">
        solar sail
       </a>
       that would also function as reflector to illuminate some selected permanently shadowed areas on the Moon,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while an onboard
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">
        infrared spectrometer
       </a>
       measured the reflected spectrum diagnostic of surface compositional mix among rock/dust, regolith, water ice, CO
       <sub>
        2
       </sub>
       , methane ice (CH
       <sub>
        4
       </sub>
       ), and possibly
       <a href="/wiki/Ammonia" title="Ammonia">
        ammonia
       </a>
       ice (NH
       <sub>
        3
       </sub>
       ).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS_2016_4-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPS_2016-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The illuminated spot would have been about 400 m (1,300 ft) in diameter, reflected from an altitude of 20 km (12 mi).
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Overview_and_objectives">
        Overview and objectives
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flashlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Overview and objectives">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The goal of Lunar Flashlight is to determine the presence or absence of exposed water ice and its physical state, and map its concentration at the 1-2 kilometer scale within the permanently shadowed regions of the lunar south pole.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_5-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-techport-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-10">
         [10]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-11">
         [11]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The mission will be the first CubeSat to reach the Moon, and the first mission to use lasers to look for water ice.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laser_1-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-Laser-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Any polar volatile data collected by Lunar Flashlight could then ensure the most appropriate landing sites for a more expensive rover to perform
       <i>
        in situ
       </i>
       measurements and chemical analyses.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SSERVI_Flash2015_6-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-SSERVI_Flash2015-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The spacecraft will maneuver to its lunar polar orbit and use its near infrared
       <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
        lasers
       </a>
       to shine light into the shaded polar regions, while the on-board spectrometer measures surface reflection and composition.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laser_1-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Laser-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Barbara Cohen from the NASA
       <a href="/wiki/Marshall_Space_Flight_Center" title="Marshall Space Flight Center">
        Marshall Space Flight Center
       </a>
       is the Principal Investigator.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-techport_5-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-techport-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Scientific_payload">
        Scientific payload
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flashlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Scientific payload">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The proposed payload on this
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nanosatellite" title="Nanosatellite">
        nanosatellite
       </a>
       is an
       <a href="/wiki/Infrared_spectroscopy" title="Infrared spectroscopy">
        infrared spectrometer
       </a>
       , consisting of a lens, dichroic beam splitters and multiple single-element detectors. It occupies 2 of the 6 modules of the 6U
       <a href="/wiki/CubeSat" title="CubeSat">
        CubeSat
       </a>
       bus.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The ADACS (attitude control), command and data handling, and power systems will occupy 1.5U; the Iris telecom system will occupy 0.5U.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS_2016_4-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPS_2016-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The Lunar Flashlight payload is derived from a few predecessor systems, including JPL's INSPIRE (INterplanetary NanoSpacecraft in a Relevant Environment), MARCO (
       <a href="/wiki/Mars_Cube_One" title="Mars Cube One">
        Mars Cube One
       </a>
       ) and JPL's experience with
       <a href="/wiki/Spectrometer" title="Spectrometer">
        spectrometers
       </a>
       , including the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon_Mineralogy_Mapper" title="Moon Mineralogy Mapper">
        Moon Mineralogy Mapper
       </a>
       (M3).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Laser_1-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-Laser-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The 6U CubeSat bus will utilize mostly commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) components such as the
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lithium_ion_battery" title="Lithium ion battery">
        lithium ion batteries
       </a>
       , the
       <a href="/wiki/CPU_card" title="CPU card">
        CPU board
       </a>
       , HaWK
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Solar_panels" title="Solar panels">
        solar panels
       </a>
       produced by
       <a class="external text" href="http://www.mmadesignllc.com" rel="nofollow">
        MMA Design, LLC
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Star_tracker" title="Star tracker">
        star tracker
       </a>
       and 3-axis
       <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">
        reaction wheels
       </a>
       for
       <a href="/wiki/Attitude_control" title="Attitude control">
        attitude control
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The CPU is a 'Rad-Tol Dependable Multiprocessor'.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS_2016_4-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPS_2016-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       JPL will provide the miniaturized INSPIRE suite that provides timing, navigation and telecommunication on the
       <a href="/wiki/X_band" title="X band">
        X-band
       </a>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       which is to be monitored with the
       <a href="/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network" title="NASA Deep Space Network">
        NASA Deep Space Network
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LPS_2016_4-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-LPS_2016-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Launch">
        Launch
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flashlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Launch">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The spacecraft will be launched as a secondary payload on the first test flight of the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">
        Space Launch System
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Exploration_Mission_1" title="Exploration Mission 1">
        Exploration Mission 1
       </a>
       ) scheduled for 2019.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2019-slip_2-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-2019-slip-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Proposed_trajectory">
        Proposed trajectory
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lunar_Flashlight&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Proposed trajectory">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The Lunar Flashlight spacecraft will be ejected from the
       <a href="/wiki/Space_Launch_System" title="Space Launch System">
        Space Launch System
       </a>
       during its translunar flight, and will use a
       <a href="/wiki/Sun_sensor" title="Sun sensor">
        Sun sensor
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Solar_panels_on_spacecraft" title="Solar panels on spacecraft">
        solar panels
       </a>
       to power the 3-axes
       <a href="/wiki/Reaction_wheel" title="Reaction wheel">
        reaction wheels
       </a>
       . It also features a chemical
       <a href="/wiki/Monopropellant" title="Monopropellant">
        monopropellant
       </a>
       propulsion and orientation system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-propulsion_12-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-propulsion-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The propulsion system occupies 3U of volume including 2 kg of monopropellant.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-propulsion_12-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-propulsion-12">
         [12]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The concept is that it will then begin a trajectory toward a multiple lunar, and possibly an Earth swingby transfer; it will be captured into a lunar polar orbit in one or two months after launch, depending on the selected trajectory.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2013_proposal_3-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-2013_proposal-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
