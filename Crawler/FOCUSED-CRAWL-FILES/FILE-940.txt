      <p>
       <b>
        Perdita
       </b>
       (
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="IPA nopopups noexcerpt">
         <a href="/wiki/Help:IPA/English" title="Help:IPA/English">
          /
          <span style="border-bottom:1px dotted">
           <span title="/ˈ/: primary stress follows">
            ˈ
           </span>
           <span title="'p' in 'pie'">
            p
           </span>
           <span title="/ɜːr/: 'ur' in 'fur'">
            ɜːr
           </span>
           <span title="'d' in 'dye'">
            d
           </span>
           <span title="/ɪ/: 'i' in 'kit'">
            ɪ
           </span>
           <span title="'t' in 'tie'">
            t
           </span>
           <span title="/ə/: 'a' in 'about'">
            ə
           </span>
          </span>
          /
         </a>
        </span>
       </span>
       <a href="/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key" title="Help:Pronunciation respelling key">
        <i title="English pronunciation respelling">
         <span style="font-size:90%">
          PUR
         </span>
         -di-tə
        </i>
       </a>
       ) is an
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inner_satellite" title="Inner satellite">
        inner satellite
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Uranus" title="Uranus">
        Uranus
       </a>
       . Perdita's discovery was complicated. The first photographs of Perdita were taken by the
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/Voyager_2" title="Voyager 2">
         Voyager 2
        </a>
       </i>
       spacecraft in 1986, but it was not recognized from the photographs for more than a decade. In 1999, the moon was noticed by
       <a href="/wiki/Erich_Karkoschka" title="Erich Karkoschka">
        Erich Karkoschka
       </a>
       and reported.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001_1-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_7171_4-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_7171-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       But because no further pictures could be taken to confirm its existence, it was officially demoted in 2001.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEFoust2001_5-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoust2001-5">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, in 2003, pictures taken by the
       <a href="/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope" title="Hubble Space Telescope">
        Hubble Space Telescope
       </a>
       managed to pick up an object where Perdita was supposed to be, finally confirming its existence.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006_6-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8194_7-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_8194-7">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Following its discovery in 1999, it was given the temporary designation of
       <b>
        S/1986 U 10
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEIAUC_7171_4-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIAUC_7171-4">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It was named Perdita (
       <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">
        Latin
       </a>
       for 'lost') after
       <a href="/wiki/Perdita_(The_Winter%27s_Tale)" title="Perdita (The Winter's Tale)">
        the daughter
       </a>
       of
       <a href="/wiki/Leontes" title="Leontes">
        Leontes
       </a>
       and Hermione in
       <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">
        William Shakespeare
       </a>
       's play
       <i>
        <a href="/wiki/The_Winter%27s_Tale" title="The Winter's Tale">
         The Winter's Tale
        </a>
       </i>
       . The moon is also designated
       <b>
        Uranus XXV
       </b>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEUSGS:_Planet_and_Satellite_Names_and_Discoverers_8-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUSGS:_Planet_and_Satellite_Names_and_Discoverers-8">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The moon orbits between
       <a href="/wiki/Belinda_(moon)" title="Belinda (moon)">
        Belinda
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Puck_(moon)" title="Puck (moon)">
        Puck
       </a>
       . The above-mentioned Hubble measurements prove that Perdita does not follow a direct
       <a href="/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion" title="Kepler's laws of planetary motion">
        Keplerian
       </a>
       motion around Uranus. Instead, it is clearly caught in a 43:44
       <a href="/wiki/Orbital_resonance" title="Orbital resonance">
        orbital resonance
       </a>
       with the nearby moon
       <a href="/wiki/Belinda_(moon)" title="Belinda (moon)">
        Belinda
       </a>
       . It is also close to an 8:7 resonance with
       <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_(moon)" title="Rosalind (moon)">
        Rosalind
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001_1-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006_6-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       Perdita belongs to the Portia group of satellites, which also includes
       <a href="/wiki/Bianca_(moon)" title="Bianca (moon)">
        Bianca
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cressida_(moon)" title="Cressida (moon)">
        Cressida
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Desdemona_(moon)" title="Desdemona (moon)">
        Desdemona
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Portia_(moon)" title="Portia (moon)">
        Portia
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Juliet_(moon)" title="Juliet (moon)">
        Juliet
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Cupid_(moon)" title="Cupid (moon)">
        Cupid
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Rosalind_(moon)" title="Rosalind (moon)">
        Rosalind
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Belinda_(moon)" title="Belinda (moon)">
        Belinda
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001_3-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       These satellites have similar orbits and photometric properties.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001_3-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Little is known about Perdita apart from its orbit,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001_1-10">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006_6-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEShowalterLissauer2006-6">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
       radius of 15 km
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001_1-11">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Voyager2001-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       and geometric albedo of 0.08.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001_3-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKarkoschka,_Hubble2001-3">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
