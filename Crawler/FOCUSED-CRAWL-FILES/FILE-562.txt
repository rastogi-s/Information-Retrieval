   <h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">
    Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser-ranging Operation
   </h1>
   <div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">

      <p>
       The
       <b>
        Apache Point Observatory Lunar Laser-ranging Operation
       </b>
       , or
       <b>
        APOLLO
       </b>
       ,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-1">
         [1]
        </a>
       </sup>
       is a project at the
       <a href="/wiki/Apache_Point_Observatory" title="Apache Point Observatory">
        Apache Point Observatory
       </a>
       in
       <a href="/wiki/New_Mexico" title="New Mexico">
        New Mexico
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera_2-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matera-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
       It is an extension and advancement of previous
       <a href="/wiki/Lunar_Laser_Ranging_experiment" title="Lunar Laser Ranging experiment">
        Lunar Laser Ranging experiments
       </a>
       , which use
       <a href="/wiki/Retroreflector" title="Retroreflector">
        retroreflectors
       </a>
       on the
       <a href="/wiki/Moon" title="Moon">
        Moon
       </a>
       to track changes in lunar
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbital
       </a>
       distance and motion.
      </p>
      <p>
       Using telescopes on Earth, the reflectors on the Moon, and accurate timing of
       <a href="/wiki/Laser" title="Laser">
        laser
       </a>
       pulses, by the early 2000s scientists could measure and predict the
       <a href="/wiki/Orbit" title="Orbit">
        orbit
       </a>
       of the Moon to an accuracy of a few centimeters. This already impressive accuracy (the Moon is typically about 385,000 km away) provides the best known test of many aspects of our
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_gravity" title="Theory of gravity">
        theories of gravity
       </a>
       . APOLLO improves this even further, measuring the distance between the Moon to an accuracy of a few millimeters. Using this information, scientists will be able to further test various aspects of gravity: do the Earth and the Moon react the same to gravity despite their different compositions? Does the energy content of the Earth and the Moon react to gravity in the same way as
       <a href="/wiki/Albert_Einstein" title="Albert Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       predicts? In general, does Einstein's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Relativity" title="General Relativity">
        General Relativity
       </a>
       correctly predict the motion of the Moon, or are new theories required?
      </p>
      <p>
       The
       <a href="#The_collaboration">
        APOLLO collaboration
       </a>
       built their apparatus on the 3.5 meter telescope at Apache Point in southern New Mexico. By using a large telescope at a site with good atmospheric
       <a href="/wiki/Astronomical_seeing" title="Astronomical seeing">
        "seeing"
       </a>
       , the APOLLO collaboration gets much stronger reflections than any existing facilities. (Strong is a relative term here—APOLLO records approximately one returned laser
       <a href="/wiki/Photon" title="Photon">
        photon
       </a>
       per pulse, as opposed to the roughly 0.01 photon-per-pulse average experienced by previous LLR facilities.) The stronger return signal from APOLLO translates to much more accurate measurements.
      </p>
      <p>
      </p>

      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="History_and_motivation">
        History and motivation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History and motivation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       High precision Lunar Laser Ranging (LLR) started soon after the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_11" title="Apollo 11">
        Apollo 11
       </a>
       astronauts left the first retroreflector on the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-3">
         [3]
        </a>
       </sup>
       Additional reflectors were left by the
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_14" title="Apollo 14">
        Apollo 14
       </a>
       and
       <a href="/wiki/Apollo_15" title="Apollo 15">
        Apollo 15
       </a>
       astronauts, and two French-built reflector arrays were placed on the Moon by the Soviet
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_17" title="Luna 17">
        Luna 17
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_1" title="Lunokhod 1">
        Lunokhod 1
       </a>
       ) and
       <a href="/wiki/Luna_21" title="Luna 21">
        Luna 21
       </a>
       (
       <a href="/wiki/Lunokhod_2" title="Lunokhod 2">
        Lunokhod 2
       </a>
       ) lunar rover missions. Over the years since, many groups and experiments have used this technique to study the behavior of the Earth-Moon system, investigating gravitational and other effects.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4">
        <a href="#cite_note-4">
         [4]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5">
        <a href="#cite_note-5">
         [5]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       For the first few years, the distance between the observatory and the reflectors could be measured to about 25 cm accuracy. Improved techniques and equipment lead to accuracies of 12–16 cm until about 1984. Then
       <a href="/wiki/McDonald_Observatory" title="McDonald Observatory">
        McDonald Observatory
       </a>
       built a special purpose system (MLRS) just for ranging, and achieved roughly 3 cm accuracies mid-to-late 1980s. In the early 1990s a French LLR system at the
       <a href="/wiki/C%C3%B4te_d%27Azur_Observatory" title="Côte d'Azur Observatory">
        Observatoire de la Côte d’Azur
       </a>
       (OCA) started operation, with similar precision.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera_2-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matera-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The McDonald and OCA stations are collecting data that is about as good as possible, given the number of photons they collect back from the reflectors. Although minor improvements are certainly possible, getting significantly better data requires a larger telescope and a better site. This is the basic goal of the APOLLO collaboration.
      </p>
      <p>
       The APOLLO laser has been operational since October 2005, and routinely accomplishes millimeter level range accuracy between the Earth and the Moon.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
        <a href="#cite_note-6">
         [6]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Science_goals">
        Science goals
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Science goals">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       The goal of APOLLO is to push LLR into the millimeter range precision, which then translates directly into an order-of-magnitude improvement in the determination of fundamental physics parameters. Specifically, assuming improvements of a factor of ten over prior measurements,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
        <a href="#cite_note-7">
         [7]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8">
        <a href="#cite_note-8">
         [8]
        </a>
       </sup>
       APOLLO will test:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        the Weak
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equivalence_Principle" title="Equivalence Principle">
         Equivalence Principle
        </a>
        (WEP) to a part in
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7014100000000000000♠
         </span>
         10
         <sup>
          14
         </sup>
        </span>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        the Strong
        <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Equivalence_Principle" title="Equivalence Principle">
         Equivalence Principle
        </a>
        (SEP) to a few parts in
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7005100000000000000♠
         </span>
         10
         <sup>
          5
         </sup>
        </span>
        ,
       </li>
       <li>
        <a href="/wiki/Precession#Relativistic" title="Precession">
         de Sitter relativistic precession
        </a>
        to a few parts in
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7004100000000000000♠
         </span>
         10
         <sup>
          4
         </sup>
        </span>
        , and
       </li>
       <li>
        the time variation of the
        <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">
         Gravitational constant
        </a>
        G to a part in
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7013100000000000000♠
         </span>
         10
         <sup>
          13
         </sup>
        </span>
        per year.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Tests_of_the_Equivalence_Principles">
        Tests of the Equivalence Principles
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Tests of the Equivalence Principles">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       The Weak Equivalence Principle says that all objects fall the same way in a gravity field, no matter what they are made of. The Earth and the Moon have very different compositions—for example, the Earth has a
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Earth_core" title="Earth core">
        large iron core
       </a>
       , but the Moon does not. Furthermore, both are in orbit around the
       <a href="/wiki/Sun" title="Sun">
        Sun
       </a>
       , meaning they are both falling towards the Sun at all times, even as they revolve around each other. If the Earth and the Moon were affected differently by the gravity of the Sun, this would directly affect the orbit of the Moon around the Earth. But as closely as scientists can measure, the orbit of the Moon is just as predicted from assuming that gravity acts the same on each — to within 1 part in
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         7013100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         13
        </sup>
       </span>
       , the Earth and the Moon fall towards the Sun in exactly the same way, despite their different compositions. APOLLO will lead to even tighter limits.
      </p>
      <p>
       What about the Strong Equivalence Principle? According to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Einstein" title="Einstein">
        Einstein
       </a>
       's
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Relativity" title="General Relativity">
        General Relativity
       </a>
       , the mass of any object consists of two parts—the mass of the atoms themselves, plus the mass of
       <a href="/wiki/Binding_energy" title="Binding energy">
        the energy that holds the object together
       </a>
       . The question is whether the energy portion of mass behaves like the traditional part—does it contribute to measured gravity of the object? To the inertia? In General Relativity, the self energy affects both the gravity field and inertia, and does so equally. This is the Strong Equivalence Principle (SEP).
      </p>
      <p>
       Other modern theories, such as
       <a href="/wiki/String_theory" title="String theory">
        string theory
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="/wiki/Quintessence_(physics)" title="Quintessence (physics)">
        quintessence
       </a>
       , and various forms of
       <a href="/wiki/Quantum_gravity" title="Quantum gravity">
        quantum gravity
       </a>
       , almost all predict a violation of the Strong Equivalence Principle at some level. Additionally, many puzzling experimental results, such as
       <a href="/wiki/Galaxy_rotation_curve" title="Galaxy rotation curve">
        Galaxy rotation curves
       </a>
       that imply
       <a href="/wiki/Dark_matter" title="Dark matter">
        dark matter
       </a>
       or
       <a href="/wiki/Supernova_Cosmology_Project" title="Supernova Cosmology Project">
        supernova observations
       </a>
       that imply
       <a href="/wiki/Dark_energy" title="Dark energy">
        dark energy
       </a>
       , could also potentially be explained by alternative theories of gravity (see, for example,
       <a href="/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics" title="Modified Newtonian dynamics">
        MOND
       </a>
       ). Therefore, experimentalists believe it is important to make the most precise measurements of gravity possible, looking for any possible anomalies or confirming Einstein's predictions.
      </p>
      <p>
       Precise ranging to the Moon can test the SEP since the Earth and the Moon have a different fraction of their mass in the energy component. Precision measurements are needed since this component is very small—if m
       <sub>
        E
       </sub>
       is the self energy of the Earth — the energy needed to spread the atoms of the Earth out to infinity against the attraction of gravity — then the mass of the Earth is
       <i>
        decreased
       </i>
       by about m
       <sub>
        E
       </sub>
       /c
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       =
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6990459999999999999♠
        </span>
        4.6
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −10
        </sup>
       </span>
       of the Earth’s total mass. The self energy of the Moon is smaller yet, about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6989200000000000000♠
        </span>
        2
        <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">
         ×
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −11
        </sup>
       </span>
       of its mass. (The contribution for any object of laboratory size is negligible, about
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6973100000000000000♠
        </span>
        10
        <sup>
         −27

       </span>
       , so only measurements of planet-sized or bigger objects have any hope of seeing this effect.)
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
        <a href="#cite_note-9">
         [9]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       If the Moon just revolved around the Earth, there would be no way to tell what fraction of the Moon's or the Earth's gravity was caused by each form of mass, since only the total can be measured. However, the orbit of the Moon is also strongly affected by the gravity of the Sun—in essence, Earth and Moon are in
       <a href="/wiki/Free_fall" title="Free fall">
        free fall
       </a>
       around the Sun. If the energy portion of mass behaves differently from the conventional portion, then the Earth and the Moon will fall differently toward the Sun, and the orbit of the Moon around the Earth will be affected. For example, suppose the energy part of the mass does affect gravity, but does not affect inertia. Then:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p>

The calculation of the amplitude is complicated,

         </a>
        </sup>
        but a crude estimate may be derived by multiplying the Earth’s orbital radius of
        <span class="nowrap">
         <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
          7011150000000000000♠
         </span>
         1.5
         <span style="margin-left:0.25em;margin-right:0.15em;">

         </span>

        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera_2-2">

         </a>

       </p>

      <p>
       The signature of an EP violation is very simple, depending only on the distance of the Moon from the Sun. This repeats about every 29.5 days, somewhat longer than the time the Moon takes to go around the Earth once, which is 27.3 days. (This difference arises since the Earth moves along its orbit as the Moon goes around, so the Moon has to make a little more than one orbit to get back to the same position relative to the Sun.) This makes EP particularly easy to measure, since many confounding effects such as tides or weather will not repeat at 29.5 day intervals. Unfortunately, there is one effect—radiation pressure acting on the orbit of the Moon—that does repeat each 29.5 days. Fortunately, it is small, less than 4 mm, and fairly easy to model so it can be subtracted out.
      </p>
      <p>
       Finally, even if the experiments show no effect, there is a tiny theoretical loophole. The measurements show the sum of the WEP and SEP violations. If the experiments show no effect, the most natural explanation is that neither WEP or SEP are violated. But it is conceptually possible that both are violated, and by equal and opposite amounts. This would be an incredible coincidence since WEP and SEP depend on very different and arbitrary properties—the exact composition of the Earth and the Moon, and their self-energies. But this unlikely case cannot be completely ruled out until either other solar system bodies are measured to similar precision, or laboratory experiments reduce the bounds on WEP violations alone.
      </p>

      <p>

       <a href="/wiki/Gravitational_constant" title="Gravitational constant">
        Gravitational constant
       </a>
       , G, to about one part in
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">

        <sup>

        </sup>
       </span>
       per year. The
       <a href="/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space" title="Metric expansion of space">

       <span class="nowrap">

        <sup>

      </p>

       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">

      </p>
      <h2>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Principles_of_operation">
        Principles of operation
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Principles of operation">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <div class="thumb tright">
       <div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;">
        <a class="image" href="/wiki/File:LunarPhotons.png">
         <img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="360" data-file-width="491" height="161" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/LunarPhotons.png/220px-LunarPhotons.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/LunarPhotons.png/330px-LunarPhotons.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/LunarPhotons.png/440px-LunarPhotons.png 2x" width="220"/>
        </a>
        <div class="thumbcaption">
         <div class="magnify">
          <a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:LunarPhotons.png" title="Enlarge">
          </a>
         </div>
         Graph of returned photons
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
      <p>
       APOLLO is based on measuring the time-of-flight of a
       <a href="/wiki/Pulsed_laser" title="Pulsed laser">
        short-pulse laser
       </a>
       reflected from a distant target—in this case the retroreflector arrays on the Moon. Each burst of light lasts 100
       <a href="/wiki/Picosecond" title="Picosecond">
        picoseconds
       </a>
       (ps).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
        <a href="#cite_note-14">
         [14]
        </a>
       </sup>
       One millimeter in range corresponds to only 6.7 ps of round-trip travel time. However, the retroreflectors on the Moon introduce more than one millimeter of error themselves. They are not usually at an exact right angle to the incoming beam, so the different
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Corner_cube" title="Corner cube">
        corner cubes
       </a>
       of the retroreflectors are at different distances from the transmitter. This is because the Moon, although it keeps one face to the Earth, does not do so exactly—it wobbles from side to side and up and down, by as much as 10° in magnitude. (See
       <a href="/wiki/Libration" title="Libration">
        libration
       </a>
       .) These librations occur since the Moon rotates at constant speed, but has an elliptical and inclined orbit. This effect may seem small, but it is not only measurable, it forms the largest unknown in finding the range, since there is no way to tell which corner cube reflected each photon. The biggest array, the
       <span class="nowrap">
        <span class="sortkey" style="display:none">
         6999600000000000000♠
        </span>
        0.6 m
        <sup>
         2
        </sup>
       </span>
       Apollo 15 reflector, can have a corner-to-corner range spread of ≈ 1.2 sin(10°) m, or 210 mm, or about 1.4 ns of round-trip time. The root-mean-square (RMS) range spread is then about 400 ps. To determine the distance to the reflector to 1 mm precision, or 7 ps, by averaging, the measurement needs at least (400/7)
       <sup>
        2
       </sup>
       ≈ 3000 photons. This explains why a much larger system is needed to improve the existing measurements—the pre-APOLLO 2 cm RMS range precision required only about 10 photons, even at the worst-case orientation of the retroreflector array.
      </p>
      <p>
       APOLLO attacks this problem by using both a bigger telescope and better astronomical seeing. Both are considerably improved over existing systems. Compared to McDonald Observatory ranging station, the Apache Point telescope has a factor of 20 greater light-collecting area. There is also a big gain from better seeing—the APO site and telescope combined can often achieve one arcsecond seeing, compared to the ≈5 arcseconds typical for the previous McDonald Lunar Ranging Station (MLRS). The better seeing helps two ways—it both increases the laser beam intensity on the Moon and reduces the lunar background, since a smaller receiver field-of-view may be used, gathering light from a smaller spot on the Moon. Both effects scale as the inverse square of the seeing, so that the signal-to-noise ratio of the lunar return is inversely proportional to the fourth power of the seeing. APOLLO should therefore gain about 20 (from the bigger telescope) × 25 (for better seeing) = 500 × in return signal strength over MLRS, and additional factor of 25 in signal-to-noise (from fewer stray photons interfering with the desired ones). Likewise APOLLO should get a signal about 50 times stronger than the OCA LLR facility, which has a 1.5 m telescope and seeing of about 3 arcsec.
      </p>
      <p>
       The increased optical gain brings some problems due to the possibility of getting more than one returned photon per pulse. The most novel component of the APOLLO system is the integrated array of
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Single-Photon_Avalanche_Diode" title="Single-Photon Avalanche Diode">
        Single-Photon Avalanche Diodes
       </a>
       (SPADs) used in the detector. This technology is needed to deal with multiple photon returns within each pulse. Most single photon detectors suffer from "
       <a href="/wiki/Dead_time" title="Dead time">
        dead time
       </a>
       ": they cannot detect a photon if it arrives soon after another. This means that if more than one photon comes back in a single pulse, a conventional single-photon detector would only record the arrival time of the first photon. However, the important quantity is the centroid of the time of all returned photons (assuming the pulse and reflectors are symmetrical), so any system that can return multiple photons per pulse must record the arrival times of each photon. In APOLLO, the incoming photons are spread over an array of independent detectors, which reduces the chance that two or more photons hit any one of the detectors.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Matera_2-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-Matera-2">
         [2]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <h3>
       <span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling_station_locations">
        Modeling station locations
       </span>
       <span class="mw-editsection">
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Modeling station locations">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h3>
      <p>
       Any laser ranging station, APOLLO included, measures the transit time, and hence the distance, from the telescope to the reflector(s). But for lunar ranging science, what is really wanted is the distance between the
       <a href="/wiki/Center_of_mass" title="Center of mass">
        center of mass
       </a>
       of the Earth and the center of mass of the Moon. To do this, the positions of the telescope, and the reflectors, must be known to comparable precision (a few mm). Since both the telescope and the reflectors are stationary structures, it might seem they could be precisely measured, and then their position would be known thereafter. This assumption is not too bad for the Moon, which is a quiet environment. But for the Earth, the stations move quite a bit on this scale:
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        The
        <a href="/wiki/Polar_motion" title="Polar motion">
         Earth's polar axis moves
        </a>
        and the Earth's rotation is irregular. The polar axis moves due to various causes, some predictable (the Moon exerts a torque on Earth's tidal bulge) and some variable (rocks are rebounding from the last ice age, weather). Weather also affects the Earth's rotation, by moving large masses of water around. These effects, important to many other science projects as well, even have their own agency to keep track of them—the
        <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">
         International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service
        </a>
        .
       </li>
       <li>
        The stations move due to
        <a href="/wiki/Tidal_force" title="Tidal force">
         tides
        </a>
        . The Moon, since it is
        <a href="/wiki/Tidal_locking" title="Tidal locking">
         tidally locked
        </a>
        to the Earth, has relatively small and repeatable tides of about 10 cm. The solid Earth has larger tides, oscillating about 35 cm peak-to-peak, every 12 hours.
       </li>
       <li>
        The Earth's crust changes in response to long term fluctuations such as
        <a href="/wiki/Post-glacial_rebound" title="Post-glacial rebound">
         post-glacial rebound
        </a>
        and loading caused by sediment transport.
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
         <a href="#cite_note-15">
          [15]
         </a>
        </sup>
       </li>
       <li>
        The Earth's short-term weather can also affect the location of the telescope, primarily vertically. Various weather effects can load local regions of the Earth's crust, depressing the crust by a few millimeters. These effects come from the atmosphere (high pressure systems press on the Earth's surface), and the ocean (water piles up on the coast depressing the crust). Ground water fluctuations, caused by rain, can also affect the telescope location.
       </li>
       <li>
        The pressure of sunlight pushes the Moon's orbit slightly off center. This is a small effect, about 3.65 mm,
        <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
         <a href="#cite_note-16">
          [16]
         </a>
        </sup>
        but it is particularly important since it mimics the effect of an EP violation.
       </li>
       <li>
        Even
        <a href="/wiki/Continental_drift" title="Continental drift">
         continental drift
        </a>
        must be compensated for.
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       In addition, the Earth's atmosphere causes an additional delay, since the speed of light is
       <a href="/wiki/Speed_of_light#Interaction_with_transparent_materials" title="Speed of light">
        slightly slower through the atmosphere
       </a>
       . This amounts to about 1.6 meters when looking straight up at Apache Point. This delay is also affected by weather, primarily atmospheric pressure, which determines just how much air there is above the site.
      </p>
      <p>
       Since many of these effects are weather-related, and also affect the more common
       <a href="/wiki/Satellite_laser_ranging" title="Satellite laser ranging">
        satellite laser ranging
       </a>
       , ranging stations traditionally include weather stations, measuring local temperature, pressure, and relative humidity. APOLLO will measure all these, plus measure local gravity very precisely, using a precision
       <a href="/wiki/Gravimeter" title="Gravimeter">
        gravimeter
       </a>
       .
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
        <a href="#cite_note-17">
         [17]
        </a>
       </sup>
       This instrument is capable of sensing vertical displacements as small as 0.1 mm, by measuring the change in gravity as the observatory moves closer to or farther from the Earth's center.
      </p>
      <p>
       Using all these measurements, scientists try to model and predict the exact location of the telescope, and the delays through the atmosphere, so they can compensate for them. The tides are fairly predictable, and the Earth's rotation is measured by the
       <a href="/wiki/International_Earth_Rotation_and_Reference_Systems_Service" title="International Earth Rotation and Reference Systems Service">
        IERS
       </a>
       and can be accounted for. Atmospheric delay is fairly well understood, and is dominated by the pressure measurement alone. Early models had uncertainties in the 5–10 mm range for reasonable elevation angles,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
        <a href="#cite_note-18">
         [18]
        </a>
       </sup>
       though more recent efforts have produced a model claiming 3 mm accuracy down to 10 degrees above the horizon, and sub-millimeter performance above 20–30° elevation.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
        <a href="#cite_note-19">
         [19]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The weather is perhaps the biggest error source. Atmospheric loading is estimated from the barometric pressure at the telescope and the average pressure within a 1000 km radius. Ocean loading has been handled strictly by empirical models, and ground water has been largely ignored. APOLLO will probably require improvements in all these models to reach the full accuracy of the measurements.
      </p>

       <span class="mw-headline" id="Status">

       <span class="mw-editsection">

         [
        </span>
        <a href="/w/index.php?title=Apache_Point_Observatory_Lunar_Laser-ranging_Operation&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Status">
         edit
        </a>
        <span class="mw-editsection-bracket">
         ]
        </span>
       </span>
      </h2>
      <p>
       APOLLO has been up and working to various degrees since October 2005, with science-quality data beginning April 2006. By mid-2011, the status was:
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status2011_24-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-status2011-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <ul>
       <li>
        All 5 reflectors (three Apollo and two Lunokhod) ranged routinely.
       </li>
       <li>
        As many as 12 photons in a single pulse (limited by detector—might have been more).
       </li>
       <li>
        Sustained rate of about 3 photons per pulse over several minutes. This about 65 times more photons detected than previous efforts.
       </li>
       <li>
        As many as 50,000 return photons detected in a single lunation (during 5 hours total operation).
       </li>
      </ul>
      <p>
       As of mid-2011, the range precision (per session) was believed to be about 1.8–3.3 mm per reflector,
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status2011_24-2">
        <a href="#cite_note-status2011-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       while the orbit of the Moon is being determined to roughly the 15 mm level.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-status2011_24-3">
        <a href="#cite_note-status2011-24">
         [24]
        </a>
       </sup>
       The gap between the measurements and the theory could be due to systematic errors in the ranging, insufficient modeling of various conventional effects that become important at this level, or limitations of our
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theory_of_gravity" title="Theory of gravity">
        theory of gravity
       </a>
       . Although it is possible that this discrepancy is due to
       <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_physics" title="New physics">
        new physics
       </a>
       , the primary suspect is insufficient modeling, since this is known to be both complex and difficult.
      </p>
      <p>
       To enable APOLLO to improve beyond the part per trillion measurement accuracy level, in 2016 it added a cesium
       <a href="/wiki/Atomic_clock" title="Atomic clock">
        atomic clock
       </a>
       and improved calibration system.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-absolute_cal_25-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-absolute_cal-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-timing_cal_26-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-timing_cal-26">
         [26]
        </a>
       </sup>
       With the new system in place, the possible accuracy can be increased to better 2 mm.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-absolute_cal_25-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-absolute_cal-25">
         [25]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       The new system has confirmed the accuracy of previous measurements. It revealed that the previous estimate of 10 ps of error (corresponding to 1.5 mm of distance uncertainty) attributed to APOLLO's
       <a href="/wiki/Global_Positioning_System" title="Global Positioning System">
        GPS
       </a>
       -synchronized
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_oven" title="Crystal oven">
        oven
       </a>
       -controlled
       <a href="/wiki/Crystal_oscillator" title="Crystal oscillator">
        crystal oscillator
       </a>
       was too low; the true figure was actually closer to 20 ps (3 mm).
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clock_performance_27-0">
        <a href="#cite_note-clock_performance-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
       However, careful record-keeping allowed the old data to be reanalyzed in light of the new understanding of the clock's variations and most of the accuracy recovered.
       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-clock_performance_27-1">
        <a href="#cite_note-clock_performance-27">
         [27]
        </a>
       </sup>
      </p>
      <p>
       By confirming the accuracy of previous measurements, and making new even more accurate measurements, the still unresolved 15–20 mm discrepancy between theory and experiment is now placed more firmly on the theoretical models.
      </p>
